 Peter Heather (born 1960 in Northern Ireland) is a British historian specializing in late antiquity, with a focus on the Western Roman Empire's interactions with barbarian groups and the resulting successor states in Europe. As Professor of Medieval History at King's College London since 2008, he emphasizes the causal role of large-scale migrations and military pressures from Huns, Goths, and other external forces in precipitating the Empire's fifth-century collapse, drawing on archaeological evidence, Roman texts, and demographic analysis to challenge narratives of gradual internal transformation. Educated at Maidstone Grammar School and New College, Oxford, where he earned his undergraduate degree and doctorate, Heather previously held academic positions at University College London, Yale University, and Worcester College, Oxford. His major works include The Fall of the Roman Empire: A New History of Rome and the Barbarians (2005), which synthesizes multidisciplinary data to argue for the destructive impact of barbarian confederations on Roman frontiers; Empires and Barbarians (2009), exploring migration patterns and ethnogenesis; and Christendom: The Triumph of a Religion (2022), tracing Christianity's institutional development from the third to twelfth centuries amid post-Roman fragmentation. These contributions have influenced scholarly debates by privileging empirical reconstructions of invasion scales and Roman military responses over minimalist interpretations of barbarian movements.

Biography

Early Life and Education

Peter Heather was born in Northern Ireland in 1960. 
He attended Maidstone Grammar School in Kent, England. Heather then studied at New College, Oxford, where he completed his undergraduate degree, Master of Arts, and Doctor of Philosophy. His doctoral thesis, titled The Goths and the Balkans, A.D. 350-500, was submitted in 1987 and examined Gothic interactions with Roman territories during late antiquity.

Academic Career

Heather completed his DPhil at the University of Oxford in 1987, with a thesis examining the Goths and the Balkans from A.D. 350 to 500. Following his doctorate, he held teaching and research positions at University College London, Yale University, and Worcester College, Oxford.
In January 2008, Heather joined King's College London as the statutory Chair of Medieval History in the Department of History, a position he has held since. There, he has contributed to initiatives such as the Centre for Late Antique and Medieval Studies and the Medieval History Research Hub, focusing his scholarly output on the later Roman Empire and its successor states. His tenure at King's has solidified his role as a leading figure in medieval historiography, with ongoing supervision of PhD students and publications integrating historical, archaeological, and documentary evidence.

Core Research Areas

Fall of the Western Roman Empire

Peter Heather's analysis of the fall of the Western Roman Empire centers on the decisive role of large-scale barbarian invasions, triggered by Hunnic expansion, which overwhelmed the empire's military and territorial integrity between the late fourth and fifth centuries CE. In his 2005 monograph The Fall of the Roman Empire: A New History of Rome and the Barbarians, he posits that these exogenous pressuresrather than predominant internal decayaccounted for the empire's disintegration, culminating in the deposition of Romulus Augustulus in 476 CE. Heather maintains that the Western Empire retained substantial administrative and fiscal resilience into the fifth century, capable of fielding armies of 50,00060,000 men per major theater, but was undone by the cumulative impact of uncoordinated assaults that stripped away revenue-generating provinces like North Africa after the Vandal conquest in 439 CE.
Central to Heather's thesis is the Hunnic catalyst: their empire's rapid consolidation around 370375 CE displaced Gothic federates and other groups along the Danube frontier, initiating a chain of migrations. This began with approximately 100,000 Visigothspotentially up to 400,000 including dependentscrossing the Danube in 376 CE to flee Hunnic domination, only to revolt after Roman mistreatment and corruption in provisioning. The ensuing Battle of Adrianople in 378 CE resulted in the death of Emperor Valens and the near-annihilation of two-thirds of the Eastern Roman field army, exposing systemic vulnerabilities in Roman frontier defenses and diplomacy toward non-Roman peoples. Heather emphasizes that subsequent incursions, such as the Rhine crossing by Vandals, Alans, and Suebi in 406 CE and Attila's Hunnic campaigns in the 440s450s CE, involved demographically significant population transfers, not mere elite warbands, which Roman resources could not absorb or defeat piecemeal.
Heather integrates textual evidence from late Roman historians like Ammianus Marcellinus with archaeological data on settlement patterns and coin hoards to quantify invasion scales, arguing against minimalist interpretations that privilege small-scale ethnogenesis or cultural continuity. He contends that while internal factorssuch as usurpations (over 20 major ones in the fifth century) and reliance on barbarian recruitsexacerbated vulnerabilities, they were secondary to the "super-sized" external threats, which reduced imperial revenues by up to 40% through territorial losses and disrupted tax collection. This perspective contrasts with culturalist views, such as those of Peter Brown, by prioritizing military-political causation over religious or social transformations, asserting that the empire's supra-regional structure irreparably fractured under sustained barbarian hegemony in former provinces.

Barbarian Migrations and Ethnic Formations

Heather's analysis of barbarian migrations posits large-scale population movements as a primary driver of Europe's post-Roman reconfiguration, countering revisionist interpretations that minimize demographic impacts in favor of elite cultural transfers or administrative accommodations. In Empires and Barbarians (2010), he delineates four major Germanic migration phasesearly expansions, the Hunnic-era upheavals around 376454 CE, subsequent Vlkerwanderung entries into Roman territories, and later Slavic shiftsalongside steppe nomad incursions by Huns, Avars, and Magyars, arguing these involved substantial groups of warriors, freemen, families, and dependents rather than isolated warbands. This perspective integrates textual narratives, such as Ammianus Marcellinus's account of the Tervingi Goths' Danube crossing in 376 CE with approximately 100,000 individuals fleeing Hunnic advances, with archaeological indicators like the Cernjachov culture's distribution, which suggest organized, multi-ethnic coalitions with significant material continuity from Pontic steppe origins.
Central to Heather's examination of specific migrations is the Hunnic empire's catalytic role, which compressed Gothic subgroups into federate statuses within Roman borders while enabling predatory raids that eroded imperial fiscal capacity; the Huns' post-Attila fragmentation in 454 CE at the Nedao battle then unleashed secondary waves, including the Ostrogoths' consolidation under Theoderic, who led 100,000 migrants into Italy by 489 CE to establish a kingdom blending Roman administration with Gothic military dominance. Similarly, the Vandals' 429439 CE trek across Gaul and Hispania to North Africa, comprising 80,000 combatants and non-combatants, exemplifies how migration chainsinitiated by external pressuresyielded successor states that supplanted Roman provincial structures through settlement and resource extraction. Heather contends these events were not mere elite overlays but entailed verifiable population displacements, as evidenced by linguistic retentions (e.g., Gothic names and laws) and settlement patterns disrupting Roman villa economies.
Regarding ethnic formations, Heather critiques the ethnogenesis modelpopularized by scholars like Reinhard Wenskus and Herwig Wolframwhich portrays barbarian identities as situational constructs around core myths rather than stable demography, arguing instead that pre-Roman ethnic kernels, forged in peripheral interactions with imperial trade and diplomacy, underwent adaptive fusions during migrations without dissolving into purely Romanized continuities. For instance, Visigothic identity post-418 CE settlement in Aquitaine emerged from Tervingian refugees' amalgamation with Roman provincials and other barbarians, yet retained distinct military traditions and Arian Christianity, enabling kingdom-building in Gaul and Spain by 507 CE. He employs causal reasoning to link these processes to broader developmental gradients: Roman proximity accelerated barbarian state-formation via technology transfer and warfare incentives, while Hunnic hegemony temporarily homogenized steppe groups before ethnic rediversification. This framework, grounded in cross-referencing Roman historiography with barbarian sagas and artifact assemblages, underscores migrations' role in generating the medieval ethnic mosaic, though Heather acknowledges evidentiary gaps in quantifying exact scales amid archaeological debates over cultural continuity.

Rise of Christendom and Medieval Europe

Heather's analysis of the rise of Christendom emphasizes its contingent nature, rejecting teleological narratives that portray Christianity's dominance as predestined or stemming solely from inherent doctrinal superiority. In Christendom: The Triumph of a Religion, AD 300-1300 (2023), he traces the religion's expansion from the Constantinian era through the High Middle Ages, highlighting how political patronage, elite alliances, and state mechanismsrather than mass popular appealdrove its institutional entrenchment across fragmented post-Roman territories. For instance, Constantine's conversion in 312 CE marked a pivotal shift, granting Christianity imperial favor that facilitated its spread via legal privileges and resource allocation, though Heather stresses this was amplified by subsequent rulers' pragmatic adoption amid power vacuums.
The collapse of the Western Roman Empire around 476 CE profoundly disrupted Christian structures, leading to localized adaptations in barbarian successor kingdoms like the Visigoths, Ostrogoths, and Franks, where Heather argues Christianity served as a tool for legitimizing new ethnic polities rather than a seamless continuity from Roman times. In these contexts, top-down royal conversionssuch as Clovis I's baptism in 496 CEpropagated Nicene orthodoxy over Arian variants, fostering ethnic cohesion and administrative frameworks that prefigured medieval feudalism, though pagan resistances and doctrinal schisms persisted for centuries. Heather integrates archaeological and textual evidence to demonstrate how migrations and settlements reshaped demographics, enabling Christianity to embed in rural power bases by the 7th-8th centuries, as seen in the Carolingian Renaissance under Charlemagne (crowned 800 CE), which enforced uniformity through conquest and missionary efforts.
By the 11th-13th centuries, Heather contends, papal reforms under figures like Gregory VII (1073-1085 CE) centralized authority, transforming Christendom into a "religious superstate" via doctrines like the Investiture Controversy and crusades, which weaponized ideology against internal heresies and external threats like Islamic expansions. This era's enforcement mechanisms, including inquisitorial practices and fear of excommunication, consolidated dominance but also exposed Christianity's reliance on coercive power, with Heather noting failures in regions like Scandinavia, where full integration lagged until the 12th century. Complementing this, his earlier Empires and Barbarians (2009) elucidates the migratory dynamics from 400-950 CE that birthed medieval Europe's ethnic mosaic, arguing these movements supplied the human capital for Christian kingdoms, countering minimalist views of continuity by quantifying substantial population displacements via numismatic and settlement data. Overall, Heather's framework privileges causal realism, attributing Christendom's medieval ascendancy to adaptive interactions between religion, warfare, and governance amid empire's ruins.

Eastern Roman Continuity and Islamic Interactions

Peter Heather analyzes the survival of Roman imperial institutions in the East after the Western Empire's collapse in 476 AD, emphasizing administrative, fiscal, and military continuity under emperors such as Anastasius I (r. 491518) and Justinian I (r. 527565), who preserved the late Roman state's bureaucratic framework and tax systems largely intact. In his 2018 monograph Rome Resurgent: War and Empire in the Age of Justinian, Heather portrays Justinian's reign as a deliberate effort to restore the full extent of Roman imperium by reconquering lost western territories, drawing on primary sources like Procopius of Caesarea to reconstruct the era's strategic dynamics. He contends that the Vandalic War (533534 AD), involving a Roman expeditionary force of approximately 15,000 men that defeated a Vandal kingdom army numbering over 80,000, yielded immediate fiscal gains through recovered African revenues estimated at 250,000 solidi annually, bolstering the Eastern Empire's resources without net overextension.
Heather extends this assessment to the Gothic War (535554 AD), where Justinian's forces, despite high costs exceeding 200,000 troops mobilized over two decades, reincorporated Italy and its tax base into the empire, temporarily enhancing overall military capacity and ideological claims to Roman universality. These campaigns, in Heather's causal framework, demonstrated the Eastern Roman state's operational resilience and continuity as the primary heir to the classical empire, rather than a mere defensive successor state, by leveraging naval superiority and reformed field armies to project power across the Mediterranean. However, he highlights how the resultant fiscal and demographic strains including plague outbreaks from 541 AD onward that killed up to 2550 million in the empireeroded long-term sustainability, setting the stage for external threats.
Turning to Islamic interactions, Heather links the Eastern Empire's mid-sixth-century exertions to its vulnerability during the seventh-century Arab conquests, arguing that Justinian's wars exhausted reserves just as renewed Persian aggression under Khosrow II (r. 590628 AD) culminated in Heraclius I's (r. 610641 AD) pyrrhic victories, which depleted manpower and finances without restoring pre-war territorial integrity. The Rashidun Caliphate's invasions from 634 AD onward exploited this fragility, capturing key provinces like Syria after the Battle of Yarmouk (636 AD) and Egypt by 642 AD, depriving Constantinople of roughly two-thirds of its tax revenues and eastern legions. Heather interprets these losses as a pivotal rupture in Eastern Roman continuity, transforming the state into a contracted, defensively oriented entity increasingly oriented toward Anatolia and the Balkans, functioning in effect as a peripheral power paying tribute to the expanding Islamic caliphates rather than a dominant Mediterranean imperium. This shift, he reasons from logistical and economic data, compelled Byzantine rulers to adopt thematic military districts by the late seventh century, marking a departure from traditional Roman expeditionary warfare toward localized defense against sustained jihadist pressures.

Methodological Contributions

Multidisciplinary Evidence Integration

Heather's analyses of late antiquity and the early Middle Ages emphasize the synthesis of textual records with material culture to overcome the limitations of each. Contemporary Roman sources, such as Ammianus Marcellinus's Res Gestae and Priscus's fragments on the Huns, provide narrative details on military encounters and diplomatic interactions but are often provincially biased or incomplete regarding barbarian internal dynamics. Heather supplements these with archaeological data, including fortified settlements, weapon hoards, and pottery distributions, to quantify invasion scales and economic disruptions, as seen in his correlation of textual accounts of the Gothic crossing of the Danube in 376 CE with evidence of refugee influxes in the Balkans.
In Empires and Barbarians (2009), this integration extends to migration patterns, where Heather juxtaposes sparse ethnographic descriptions from Ptolemy or Tacitus with post-Roman artifact assemblagessuch as Anglo-Saxon brooches in Britain or Lombard graves in Italyto argue for substantial folk movements rather than elite transfers alone. He employs distribution mapping of trade goods and burial rites to trace ethnic formations, critiquing over-reliance on continuity models that undervalue textual indications of violence, such as the sack of Rome in 410 CE evidenced by coin hoards signaling capital flight. This method highlights causal links between external pressures and imperial fragmentation, with archaeological proxies for population density reinforcing estimates of barbarian forces exceeding 100,000 combatants by the fifth century.
Heather also incorporates numismatic evidence to track fiscal collapse, integrating coin debasement patterns from the third-century crisis onward with literary complaints of inflation, thereby linking internal administrative strains to vulnerability against Hunnic tribute demands post-434 CE. While acknowledging interpretive challenges, such as ambiguous artifact attributions, his framework prioritizes convergent evidence across disciplines to validate claims of systemic breakdown, avoiding unsubstantiated minimalism in migration historiography.

Causal Analysis of Empire Decline

Heather's methodological approach to analyzing empire decline prioritizes exogenous military and migratory pressures as primary causal drivers, integrated through a contingency-based framework that traces specific event chains rather than deterministic long-term trends. In examining the Western Roman Empire's collapse circa 376476 AD, he contends that the Hunnic expansions from the Eurasian steppes, beginning around 370 AD, acted as a catalyst by displacing Gothic and other barbarian confederations, resulting in large-scale incursions that Roman forces could not repel indefinitely. This perspective counters endogenous-focused explanations, such as chronic administrative inefficiency or cultural decadence, by demonstrating through military records that Rome maintained functional governance until repeated field army defeats eroded fiscal and territorial bases.
Central to his analysis is the quantification of barbarian group scales and Roman military responses; for instance, he estimates that the Gothic migration of 376 AD involved over 100,000 warriors and dependents, overwhelming frontier legions and leading to the pivotal Battle of Adrianople in 378 AD, where Emperor Valens lost half the Eastern field army. Heather employs causal realism by linking these shocks to downstream effects, including the settlement of foederati (barbarian allies) within Roman provinces, which fragmented authority as groups like the Visigoths under Alaric transitioned from tributaries to autonomous powers by 410 AD, sacking Rome itself. Archaeological correlates, such as destruction layers at sites like Anderitum (Richborough) and reduced coin circulation in former provinces post-400 AD, provide empirical backing for military causation over gradual economic erosion.
Heather critiques monocausal or minimized-violence interpretations, such as those portraying the era as a benign "transformation" via elite cultural continuity, by marshaling prosopographical data showing the displacement of Roman senatorial classes and the failure of central taxation systems after losses in Gaul and Africa by the 450s AD. His method integrates textual sources like Ammianus Marcellinus with material evidence to argue that empire decline manifests as a feedback loop: initial border breaches reduce revenue (e.g., African grain supplies halved after Vandal conquest in 439 AD), impairing recruitment and logistics, thereby amplifying subsequent threats until imperial coordination collapsed under Emperor Romulus Augustulus in 476 AD. This event-sequence reconstruction underscores adaptability limits in large empires facing asymmetric peripheral aggressions, a pattern Heather extends comparatively to other polities while insisting on evidentiary primacy over ideological narratives.

Reception and Influence

Scholarly Praise and Impact

Heather's The Fall of the Roman Empire: A New History of Rome and the Barbarians (2005) received acclaim for its authoritative synthesis of military and political dynamics in the Western Empire's collapse between 332 and 489 CE. Reviewers highlighted its clear, direct narrative as unmatched in English-language scholarship since J.B. Bury's work a century earlier, praising Heather's prowess as a military historian who effectively elucidates complex events. The volume's interpretive innovationlinking the Huns' diminished role post-454 CE to the empire's failure as a supra-regional structurefurther underscored its analytical depth.
Contemporary assessments lauded the book's masterful handling of intricate barbarian-Roman interactions, describing it as a "magisterial new history" that updates Edward Gibbon's Decline and Fall with modern evidentiary rigor while maintaining accessibility and narrative zest. Its inclusion of 16 precise maps was noted for enhancing textual clarity, reflecting Heather's commitment to empirical presentation.
Heather's scholarship has profoundly shaped late antique historiography by emphasizing external pressuressuch as mass barbarian migrations and Hunnic disruptionsas primary drivers of imperial disintegration, countering "transformation" models that downplay catastrophe. This "catastrophist" framework, articulated in works like Empires and Barbarians (2009), has influenced debates on ethnic formations and state collapse, with his stress on migration's scale gaining corroboration from post-2010 genetic studies affirming substantial population movements. As a leading authority on the period, Heather's integration of textual, archaeological, and quantitative evidence has set standards for causal analysis in Roman decline studies, evidenced by his tenure as Professor of Medieval History at King's College London and citations in subsequent analyses of empire resilience.

Key Criticisms and Debates

Heather's emphasis on large-scale barbarian migrations as a primary driver of the Western Roman Empire's collapse in the fifth century has drawn significant critique from historians associated with the Toronto School, including Guy Halsall and Michael Kulikowski, who argue that he overstates the demographic impact and ethnic coherence of these movements. Critics contend that archaeological evidence, such as continuity in settlement patterns and material culture across Roman provinces, indicates smaller-scale elite transfers rather than mass population replacements, challenging Heather's interpretation of literary sources as evidence for "Vlkerwanderung"-style invasions triggered by Hunnic pressures around 376410 CE. Halsall, in his review of Empires and Barbarians (2010), praises Heather's comprehensive engagement with archaeological data but faults him for insufficiently nuancing group identities, suggesting that barbarian "peoples" were often fluid constructs formed within Roman frontiers rather than pre-existing migratory hordes with fixed ethnic aims.
Heather counters these positions by integrating multidisciplinary evidence, including toponymy, burial assemblages, and Roman administrative records, to demonstrate substantial disruptionssuch as the displacement of over 100,000 Goths in 376 CE and subsequent federate settlements that eroded fiscal revenues by an estimated 2030% in key provinces like Gaul and Spain. He critiques minimalist models for underplaying the causal role of external military shocks, arguing that internal Roman factors like administrative inefficiency and civil wars were exacerbated but not primarily caused by these incursions, a view supported by comparative analysis with the Eastern Empire's survival amid similar pressures. Kulikowski, reviewing Empires and Barbarians, similarly questions Heather's reliance on traditional narratives of barbarian agency, positing instead that Roman political fragmentation enabled localized identity formations rather than empire-wide ethnic upheavals.
Debates extend to methodological preferences, with detractors accusing Heather of favoring narrative-driven literary accounts over processual archaeology that highlights cultural assimilation, potentially reviving 19th-century nationalist biases in migration historiography. In response, Heather advocates a "snowball" model of escalating migrations, where initial refugee waves coalesced into larger threats, evidenced by the rapid formation of kingdoms like the Visigothic in 418 CE, and dismisses overly constructivist approaches as evading the violent discontinuities attested in sources like Priscus and Ammianus Marcellinus. These exchanges underscore broader tensions in late antique studies between catastrophe-oriented and transformation paradigms, with Heather's framework influencing renewed emphasis on geopolitical realism over idealized continuity.
In discussions of Christianity's rise, as in Christendom (2022), Heather's portrayal of doctrinal compromises with pagan elements has elicited criticism for adopting a skeptical, arguably secular lens that downplays theological innovations' internal dynamism, though such views align with his causal focus on institutional power struggles rather than ideological purity.

Major Publications

Seminal Works on Roman Decline

Heather's seminal contribution to understanding the decline of the Western Roman Empire is The Fall of the Roman Empire: A New History of Rome and the Barbarians (2005), which synthesizes archaeological, textual, and numismatic evidence to argue that the empire's collapse stemmed from overwhelming external military pressures rather than inherent internal weaknesses. In this work, spanning the period from 332 to 489 CE, Heather contends that the arrival of the Huns in western Eurasia around 370 CE triggered mass displacements of barbarian groups, propelling them across Roman frontiers in unprecedented numbers and scales that the empire's defenses could not contain. These invasions led to repeated territorial losses, erosion of the tax base, and breakdown of centralized control, culminating in the deposition of Romulus Augustulus in 476 CE as the effective end of the western imperial structure.
Heather minimizes endogenous factors such as overtaxation, moral degeneration, or disruptive religious changes, asserting instead that the fourth-century empire remained economically viable and militarily capable until exogenous shocksexacerbated by Rome's own prior expansions, which militarized neighboring peoplesoverstretched its resources. He highlights contingent events, including the Huns' shift from direct threats to unreliable mercenaries, as pivotal in preventing Roman recovery, while emphasizing the role of barbarian polities in fragmenting the west into successor kingdoms. This "catastrophist" interpretation challenges late twentieth-century "transformation" models that portray the fall as a gradual, non-violent evolution, positioning Heather's analysis as a rebuttal favoring causal primacy of invasion dynamics.
An earlier foundational work, Goths and Romans, 332489 (1991), laid groundwork by detailing Gothic-Roman interactions as a microcosm of broader decline processes, arguing that Roman diplomatic interference and military engagements inadvertently fostered Gothic cohesion and aggression. Heather traces Gothic migrations from the Danube frontier, through defeats like Adrianople in 378 CEwhere 20,00030,000 Roman troops perishedand subsequent settlements in the Balkans and Italy, illustrating how these conflicts eroded Roman authority and enabled barbarian groups to extract concessions that weakened imperial cohesion. By integrating sparse Gothic sources with Roman accounts, the book establishes the Goths' role in dismantling western structures, prefiguring the comprehensive barbarian-centric thesis of his later volume.

Recent Books on Empires and Religion

Peter Heather's Christendom: The Triumph of a Religion, AD 3001300, published in 2023 by Knopf, provides a detailed analysis of Christianity's transformation from a peripheral sect within the Roman Empire to the dominant religious framework of medieval Europe. The book spans roughly a millennium, beginning with Emperor Constantine's conversion around 312 AD and extending to the High Middle Ages circa 1300, when papal authority had solidified amid fragmented post-Roman polities. Heather contends that this ascendancy hinged on pragmatic political endorsements rather than solely theological superiority, with imperial structures supplying the coercive and administrative mechanisms for doctrinal enforcement and institutional growth.
Central to Heather's thesis is the causal interplay between empire and faith: Constantine's policies, including the Edict of Milan in 313 AD and councils like Nicaea in 325 AD, integrated Christianity into Roman governance, leveraging state resources for missionary expansion and heresy suppression. Following the Western Empire's collapse by 476 AD, Heather describes how the Papacy exploited power vacuums, forging alliances with barbarian kingdomssuch as Clovis of the Franks' baptism in 496 ADand evolving into a transnational entity that mediated imperial ambitions, as seen in Charlemagne's coronation by Pope Leo III on Christmas Day 800 AD. In the East, the Byzantine Empire's continuity under emperors like Justinian I (r. 527565 AD) sustained Orthodox Christianity through legal codification and reconquests, though recurrent iconoclasm debates (e.g., 726843 AD) underscored tensions between imperial caesaropapism and clerical autonomy.
Heather integrates multidisciplinary evidence, including archaeological data on church constructionover 1,000 basilicas built empire-wide by 500 ADand textual sources like Eusebius's Ecclesiastical History (c. 325 AD), to argue that Christendom's cohesion emerged from adaptive responses to imperial disintegration rather than inexorable spiritual momentum. He highlights contingencies, such as the failure of alternative faiths like Arianism among Goths, which dissipated post-500 AD due to lacking sustained state backing, contrasting with Catholicism's alignment with Frankish and Anglo-Saxon rulers who converted en masse between 496 and 800 AD, enabling territorial evangelization across some 2 million square kilometers. This perspective challenges narratives of inevitable religious destiny, emphasizing instead how political realismrulers adopting Christianity for legitimacy and unitydrove its monopolization, with the Papacy amassing lands equivalent to a mid-sized kingdom by 1300 AD through donations like Pepin the Short's in 756 AD.
In parallel, Heather's co-authored Why Empires Fall: Rome, America, and the Future of the West (2023, with John Rapley; Yale University Press) extends imperial analysis but intersects religion tangentially, positing that Rome's fifth-century decline stemmed from overextension and internal fiscal strains rather than cultural or confessional decay alone. While not centering faith, it notes Christianity's role in post-imperial fragmentation, where doctrinal schisms exacerbated barbarian integrations, informing Heather's broader view that religious institutions thrive under but do not independently sustain empires. These works collectively underscore Heather's emphasis on empirical causation in religious-imperial dynamics, drawing on metrics like tax yields (Roman annona contributing to church endowments) and migration patterns to explain Christendom's endurance amid imperial flux.
