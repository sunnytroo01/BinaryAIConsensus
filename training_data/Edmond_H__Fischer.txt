Edmond H. Fischer
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Edmond H. Fischer (April 6, 1920 August 27, 2021) was a Swiss-American biochemist best known for co-discovering the role of reversible protein phosphorylation in cellular regulation, a breakthrough that earned him the 1992 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, shared with Edwin G. Krebs. His work demonstrated how enzymes add or remove phosphate groups to proteins, acting as molecular switches that control processes like glycogen breakdown, influencing fields from metabolism to signal transduction.
Born in Shanghai, China, to an Austrian father and a French motherhis father a businessman from ViennaFischer grew up in a multicultural environment before moving to Switzerland in 1920s amid family circumstances. He attended the Collge de Calvin in Geneva, earning his Maturit Fdrale in 1939, and later studied at the University of Geneva, where he obtained licences s sciences in biology and chemistry, along with an ingnieur chimiste diploma by 1947. He began his studies at the University of Geneva during World War II, earning his diplomas by 1947. For his doctoral research, he crystallized alpha-amylase from hog pancreas, sparking his interest in protein structure and function.
In 1950, Fischer emigrated to the United States on a Rockefeller Foundation fellowship, joining the University of Washington (UW) in Seattle as an assistant professor of biochemistry, where he remained for the rest of his career, rising to full professor. There, he collaborated with Krebs starting in the early 1950s, elucidating how phosphorylation activates glycogen phosphorylase in rabbit muscle, revealing a broader paradigm for enzymatic regulation that extends to hundreds of proteins. Elected to the American Academy of Arts and Sciences in 1972 and the National Academy of Sciences in 1973, Fischer's research laid the foundation for targeted therapies, including over 75 kinase-inhibiting drugs approved in the 21st century for treating cancers and inflammatory diseases.

Early life and education

Childhood and family background

Edmond H. Fischer was born on April 6, 1920, in the French Concession of the Shanghai International Settlement, China. He had two older brothers, Raoul and Georges. His parents were of European descent: his mother, Rene Tapernoux, was French-born and had arrived in Shanghai via Hanoi with her family, while his father, Oscar Fischer, was an Austrian from Vienna who held doctorates in law and business and worked in import-export trade. The family maintained strong Swiss heritage through Fischer's maternal grandfather, a Swiss national who had emigrated to become a journalist, founding the first French newspaper in China, Courrier de Chine, and contributing to the establishment of the lcole Municipale Franaise in Shanghai.
Growing up in a large, affluent household in Shanghai staffed by a gardener, cook, butler, and dressmaker, Fischer attended the lcole Municipale Franaise during his early years. The family's Swiss ties and the escalating political instability in China during the 1920s, marked by civil wars and foreign interventions, prompted the decision to send Fischer and his brothers to Switzerland for their education. Around age seven in 1927, amid rising threats from Japanese expansionism in the region, Fischer and his brothers traveled via the Trans-Siberian Railway to French-speaking Switzerland, where he was enrolled in the boarding school La Chtaigneraie near Geneva; his parents remained in Shanghai until returning to Europe in 1936.
Fischer's early years were shaped by his parents' influences, with his father's international trade work exposing him to diverse global affairs and his mother's cultured background fostering an appreciation for the arts, particularly music, which led to piano studies under Johnny Aubert at the Geneva Conservatory. These experiences, combined with family vacations to Japan, contributed to a multicultural upbringing that nurtured his intellectual curiosity. An early inspiration came from reading the biography of Louis Pasteur, which sparked his interest in science and biology.

Academic training in Switzerland

Edmond H. Fischer's formal education in Switzerland began in 1927 when, at the age of seven, he was sent from his family's home in Shanghai to attend La Chtaigneraie, an international boarding school overlooking Lake Geneva, where his interest in science first developed. This move was motivated by his family's Swiss heritage, which encouraged pursuit of scientific studies in Europe. In 1935, he transferred to the Collge de Calvin (formerly Collge de Genve) for secondary education, earning his Maturit Fdrale in 1939 just as World War II erupted.
Fischer began undergraduate studies at the University of Geneva in 1939, focusing on biology and chemistry at the School of Chemistry, though the program was interrupted by the war; Switzerland's neutrality allowed him to continue without major disruption. He obtained Licences s Sciences in both biology and chemistry, along with a Diploma of Ingnieur Chimiste, initially aspiring to microbiology but advised to build a foundation in chemistry. During the wartime years, Fischer gained early laboratory experience in Geneva, working on the isolation and purification of enzymes for analyzing polysaccharides, using rudimentary equipment like home-built electrophoresis apparatus.
In 1947, at age 27, Fischer was awarded a PhD in organic chemistry from the University of Geneva under the supervision of Kurt Heinrich Meyer, head of the Department of Organic Chemistry. His thesis examined the structure of polysaccharides such as starch and glycogen through enzymatic methods, including the purification and crystallization of -amylase from hog pancreas and other sources to demonstrate its protein nature rather than polysaccharide composition.
Parallel to his scientific training, Fischer pursued music intensively, gaining admission to the Geneva Conservatory of Music during high school and studying piano for seven years under Johnny Aubert starting at age 14. He performed publicly as a concert pianist, including works by Chopin and Mendelssohn, and continued these performances until around age 30, briefly considering a professional musical career before committing fully to science.

Professional career

Initial research positions

Following his PhD in 1947 at the University of Geneva, where his thesis focused on the structure of polysaccharides and alpha-amylases, Edmond H. Fischer remained in Geneva as a research assistant under Kurt H. Meyer, continuing investigations into polysaccharide-degrading enzymes such as alpha-amylase purification and crystallization. During this period from 1947 to 1950, he also served as a Privat Docent, delivering the university's first course in enzymology and mentoring graduate students in biochemical techniques related to carbohydrate metabolism. This role built directly on his doctoral work, emphasizing experimental approaches to enzyme isolation that prepared him for broader studies in protein biochemistry.
In 1950, prior to completing his Geneva commitments, Fischer received a Swiss National Foundation postdoctoral fellowship, arranged by Nobel laureate Paul Karrer, for a planned collaboration at the California Institute of Technology (Caltech) in Pasadena, where he aimed to extend his enzyme research to American laboratories. The departure was delayed due to ongoing work in Geneva, hastened in 1952 by the death of his mentor Kurt H. Meyer. En route to Caltech in 1953, he delivered seminars at several U.S. institutions, including offers from the University of Pittsburgh and the University of Wisconsin-Madison, but an invitation from biochemist Hans Neurath redirected him to the University of Washington in Seattle. There, he first met Edwin G. Krebs, a fellow biochemist whose work on muscle enzymes aligned with Fischer's interests, igniting a mutual fascination with protein modification mechanisms and leading to their immediate collaboration on enzyme regulation.
Fischer relocated permanently to the United States in 1953 under the Swiss National Foundation fellowship, accepting an assistant professorship in the Department of Biochemistry at the University of Washington School of Medicine in Seattle, forgoing the Caltech position due to the opportunity's appeal and the institution's emerging medical research focus. As a Swiss immigrant in the post-World War II era, he navigated early professional hurdles, including language barriershis "fractured English" often drawing amused reactions from colleagues, such as Krebs's tolerant smiles at his French-accented phrasingwhile competing for tenure-track roles amid limited funding and visa uncertainties for foreign scientists. These challenges underscored the transitional difficulties of establishing a career abroad, yet his fellowship and Neurath's endorsement facilitated a stable foothold in American academia.

Long-term role at University of Washington

In 1953, Edmond H. Fischer was appointed as an assistant professor of biochemistry at the University of Washington in Seattle, following an unexpected offer from department chair Hans Neurath upon his arrival in the United States for postdoctoral work at Caltech. This position marked the beginning of his enduring affiliation with the institution, where he conducted the bulk of his career amid a small but collaborative faculty in the newly formed medical school. By 1961, Fischer had been promoted to full professor, reflecting his growing influence in the department and his contributions to biochemical research.
Throughout his decades at the University of Washington, Fischer played a pivotal role in departmental leadership and institutional development, helping to expand the biochemistry program's scope and resources during a period of rapid growth in biomedical sciences. He mentored scores of PhD students and postdoctoral fellows, fostering a legacy of excellence in enzymology and signal transduction. Among his notable trainees was Nick Tonks, who conducted pioneering work on protein tyrosine phosphatases in Fischer's laboratory during the late 1980s. Fischer's guidance emphasized rigorous experimentation and interdisciplinary collaboration, producing a cadre of scientists who advanced the field globally.
Fischer retired as professor emeritus in 1990 but remained actively engaged with the department, attending seminars and supporting ongoing research initiatives until his death in 2021. His sustained presence helped sustain the University of Washington's reputation as a hub for biochemical innovation, bridging academic research with broader scientific communities.

Scientific contributions

Work on glycogen phosphorylase

Upon arriving at the University of Washington in 1950, Edmond H. Fischer initiated research on glycogen phosphorylase in the early 1950s, extending his doctoral investigations into the enzyme's role in starch breakdown in potatoes conducted under Kurt Meyer at the University of Geneva. This work built a foundation for exploring glycogen metabolism in animal tissues, focusing on the enzyme's function in mobilizing energy stores.
Fischer, in collaboration with Edwin G. Krebs, isolated and purified glycogen phosphorylase from rabbit skeletal muscle, achieving crystallization of the inactive form (phosphorylase b) in 1958. Their purification efforts demonstrated that the enzyme catalyzes the phosphorolytic cleavage of glycogen into glucose-1-phosphate, a critical step in glycogen breakdown to provide energy during muscle contraction. This isolation enabled detailed assays of enzymatic activity in muscle extracts, revealing dependencies on cofactors like AMP for the b form.
A key discovery was that glycogen phosphorylase exists in two interconvertible forms: an active phosphorylase a and an inactive phosphorylase b, with the conversion between them mediated by a kinase enzyme for activation and a phosphatase for deactivation. Early experiments further showed that hormones such as epinephrine rapidly enhanced phosphorylase activity in muscle preparations, stimulating the shift to the active form without initially apparent changes to the protein's primary structure.
These findings were documented in seminal publications co-authored by Fischer and Krebs during the 1950s and 1960s, including studies on muscle extract activities and conversions in 1955, the converting enzyme in 1956, and crystallization in 1958, which collectively positioned phosphorylase as a pivotal model for studying enzymatic regulation in metabolism.

Reversible protein phosphorylation mechanism

In the mid-1950s, Edmond H. Fischer and Edwin G. Krebs made a pivotal breakthrough by elucidating the mechanism underlying the interconversion of glycogen phosphorylase between its active (a) and inactive (b) forms, identifying it as a reversible phosphorylation-dephosphorylation cycle. Their work demonstrated that the conversion from phosphorylase b to the active phosphorylase a is catalyzed by an enzyme they termed phosphorylase kinase, which adds a phosphate group, while the reverse conversion from phosphorylase a to b is mediated by a phosphatase enzyme that removes the phosphate. This discovery, achieved through collaborative experiments at the University of Washington, revealed a novel regulatory process for enzyme activity in cellular metabolism.
Key experimental evidence came from in vitro assays using rabbit skeletal muscle extracts, where Fischer and Krebs observed that the activation of phosphorylase b required adenosine triphosphate (ATP) as a phosphate donor and a divalent cation such as manganese (Mn) or calcium (Ca) as a cofactor. In these assays, the addition of ATP to dialyzed muscle extracts containing phosphorylase b led to the stoichiometric incorporation of one phosphate group per phosphorylase subunit, resulting in the formation of active phosphorylase a, as measured by increased glycogen breakdown activity. The reversal was demonstrated by the action of the phosphatase (initially called the PR enzyme), which hydrolyzed the phosphate ester bond, releasing inorganic phosphate (P) and restoring the inactive form without requiring additional cofactors beyond the enzyme itself. These findings confirmed that the cycle operates as a dynamic switch, with phosphorylation activating the enzyme and dephosphorylation inactivating it.

Protein-O-PO32+H2OProtein-OH+Pi

These equations highlight the covalent modification at a specific serine residue on the protein, enabling rapid and reversible control of enzymatic function. Fischer and Krebs detailed the kinase activity in their seminal 1955 publication in the Journal of Biological Chemistry, providing the first direct evidence for ATP-dependent protein phosphorylation as a regulatory mechanism. A companion paper in the same journal described the phosphatase activity, completing the cycle's characterization.
By the 1960s, Fischer and Krebs, along with other researchers, recognized that this phosphorylation-dephosphorylation cycle extended far beyond glycogen phosphorylasethe initial model systemand represented a ubiquitous mechanism for signal transduction across diverse proteins and cellular pathways. Their work laid the foundation for understanding how kinases and phosphatases regulate processes like hormone response and cell growth, influencing thousands of subsequent studies on protein modification.

Influence on cellular signaling and disease

Fischer's discovery of reversible protein phosphorylation extended rapidly to broader cellular signaling cascades by the 1970s, where it was recognized as a key mechanism for integrating hormonal signals such as insulin and glucagon to regulate metabolism. This process involves second messengers like cyclic AMP (cAMP) activating protein kinases that phosphorylate target enzymes, amplifying extracellular signals into coordinated intracellular responses, such as glycogen breakdown in response to glucagon or glucose uptake via insulin signaling. By the mid-1970s, studies in Fischer's lab and others had mapped these cascades, showing how phosphorylation enables precise metabolic control, for instance, in liver and muscle cells balancing energy homeostasis.
The implications of dysregulated phosphorylation for disease became evident in cancer research, where aberrant kinase and phosphatase activity drives oncogenesis through uncontrolled cell proliferation. Fischer's foundational work highlighted how mutations in tyrosine kinases, such as those in the src oncogene or epidermal growth factor receptor, lead to persistent signaling that promotes tumor growth. This understanding directly influenced the development of targeted therapies, including imatinib (Gleevec), approved in 2001, which inhibits the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase in chronic myelogenous leukemia, transforming treatment outcomes for patients. Over 75 kinase inhibitors have since been approved, many building on principles from phosphorylation regulation.
In diabetes, Fischer's insights into phosphorylase activation provided critical context for glucose regulation, revealing how phosphorylation cascades govern glycogenolysis and insulin-mediated glucose transport, disruptions in which contribute to hyperglycemia. For example, phosphatase activity in these pathways modulates insulin receptor signaling, and Fischer's later identification of protein tyrosine phosphatase 1B (PTP1B) as a negative regulator of insulin action underscored its role in type 2 diabetes pathogenesis.
During the 1980s and 1990s, Fischer's research shifted to protein tyrosine phosphorylation, linking it to cell growth and immune responses through the purification of key phosphatases like CD45, which is essential for T-cell activation in immunity. Collaborating with Nicholas Tonks, he characterized over 100 tyrosine phosphatases, demonstrating their control over growth factor receptors and oncoproteins, further connecting phosphorylation imbalances to diseases like autoimmune disorders and cancer.
Fischer's legacy encompasses more than 220 publications, establishing reversible phosphorylation as the cornerstone of signal transduction and inspiring modern phosphoproteomics, which maps thousands of phosphorylation sites to decode cellular networks. This foundation remains vital in 2020s drug design, where kinase and phosphatase inhibitors continue to target signaling defects in cancer, diabetes, and beyond, with ongoing trials for novel PTP1B inhibitors in metabolic disorders.

Personal life

Marriages and family

Edmond H. Fischer married his first wife, Nelly Gagnaux, a Swiss national, in 1948. The couple had two sons, Franois and Henri. Nelly Gagnaux died in 1961.
In 1963, Fischer married Beverley Bullock, who brought a daughter, Paula, from her previous marriage, making her Fischer's stepdaughter. The marriage lasted until Beverley's death in 2006, and there were no additional children from this union.
Fischer and his first wife immigrated to the United States in 1953 as a family decision for a sabbatical year, later settling in Seattle in 1956 after visiting the city together and being drawn to its landscape reminiscent of Switzerland. The family established their life there, with Fischer's sons and stepdaughter all eventually marrying.
During the 1992 Nobel Prize announcement, Fischer received the call from Stockholm at 3:45 a.m. while his wife Beverley was ill and resting nearby; the family's subsequent trip to the Nobel ceremonies alongside collaborator Edwin G. Krebs's family fostered closer bonds, turning their groups into a supportive "confrrie." Fischer was survived by his two sons, stepdaughter, and four grandchildren.

Hobbies and later years

Fischer maintained a lifelong passion for the piano, having been admitted to the Geneva Conservatory of Music as a young man and seriously considering a professional career in music before ultimately pursuing science. He played daily for much of his life, performing sonatas by composers such as Beethoven and Mozart for friends and colleagues, and gave semi-professional recitals at scientific meetings as well as private events, including one at his grandson's wedding on July 31, 2021. His family supported these pursuits, with his wife joining him for flying lessons that complemented his musical interests.
In addition to music, Fischer developed a keen interest in aviation, learning to fly at around age 60 in the early 1980s and obtaining a private pilot's license. He owned a Cessna aircraft for about 25 years, using it for recreational flights to reach destinations like the San Juan Islands more quickly on weekends, and continued flying until approximately age 80 in the early 2000s.
Following his retirement from the University of Washington in 1990, Fischer remained engaged through occasional lab consultations and public lectures promoting science education to young audiences. He also contributed to philanthropy in the biotech and scientific fields, serving on the board of the Bert L. and N. Kuggie Vallee Foundation, which supports interdisciplinary biomedical research, and helping establish the Eddy Fischer Fellowship Fund to aid young scientists attending Nobel Laureate meetings.
Fischer remained remarkably active into his later years, continuing to play the piano regularlyhis final public performance occurring in June 2021 at a virtual Lindau Nobel Laureate meetingand managing only minor ailments despite reaching age 100. He died peacefully of natural causes on August 27, 2021, in Seattle, Washington, at the age of 101. Following his death, the University of Washington held a virtual memorial seminar highlighting his enduring legacy of mentorship and warmth toward colleagues and students, while the Nobel Foundation updated his biographical profile to note his passing, emphasizing his contributions to science and personal graciousness.

Awards and honors

Nobel Prize recognition

On October 12, 1992, the Nobel Assembly at the Karolinska Institute announced that Edmond H. Fischer and Edwin G. Krebs had been awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, shared equally, "for their discoveries concerning reversible protein phosphorylation as a biological regulatory mechanism."
The announcement came at 5 a.m. Seattle time, awakening Fischer and Krebs with phone calls from reporters; Fischer later described the experience as "a bizarre business that catches you off guard," prompting the initial question, "Why us?" Joined by family and colleagues, they celebrated the news that morning at the University of Washington.
Fischer delivered his Nobel Lecture on December 8, 1992, at the Karolinska Institutet in Stockholm, titled "Protein Phosphorylation and Cellular Regulation," in which he summarized over 40 years of collaborative research on the topic. The total prize amount was 6.5 million Swedish kronor, shared between the two laureates.
The award recognized foundational work from the 1950s on the regulation of glycogen phosphorylase, initially viewed as a niche mechanism in glycogen metabolism; broader appreciation of its role as a universal regulatory process in cellular function delayed full recognition for nearly four decades.

Other major accolades

In addition to the Nobel Prize, which stood as the pinnacle of his scientific recognition, Fischer received a series of prestigious honors that underscored his enduring impact on biochemistry and mentorship. He was elected to the American Academy of Arts and Sciences in 1972, acknowledging his early contributions to protein enzymology. The following year, in 1973, he was elected to the U.S. National Academy of Sciences, a distinction that highlighted his leadership in elucidating regulatory mechanisms in cellular processes.
Fischer's international stature was further affirmed by his election as a Foreign Member of the Royal Society in 2010, one of the final major honors of his career, celebrating his lifelong advancements in signal transduction research. From 2007 to 2014, he served as Honorary President of the World Cultural Council, a role in which he actively advocated for global science education and interdisciplinary collaboration to address societal challenges.
Among his other notable awards were the Passano Award in 1988, which recognized the broader implications of their discoveries for medical research. Fischer also received multiple honorary doctorates, including from the University of Basel in 1988, reflecting his ties to his Swiss roots and contributions to European biochemistry. In recognition of his mentorship, the University of Washington established the Edmond H. Fischer Endowed Chair in Biochemistry, supporting graduate fellowships and perpetuating his legacy in training the next generation of scientists.
