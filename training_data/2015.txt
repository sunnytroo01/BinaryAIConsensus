 2015 was a common year starting on Thursday of the Gregorian calendar, comprising 365 days from January 1 to December 31.
The year was defined by escalating global security challenges, including the territorial expansion and heightened visibility of ISIS amid ongoing conflicts in the Middle East, punctuated by major terrorist attacks such as those in Paris. Diplomatic progress included the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA), a multilateral agreement reached in July to curb Iran's nuclear program in exchange for sanctions relief. Natural disasters struck prominently with the April 25 Nepal earthquake of magnitude 7.8, which killed nearly 9,000 people, injured over 21,000, and devastated infrastructure across central Nepal and neighboring regions. Culturally, the year featured blockbuster releases like Star Wars: The Force Awakens, which premiered on December 14 and became one of the highest-grossing films ever upon its wide release.
Broader themes encompassed a surge in global migration driven by conflicts, economic recovery in parts of the world following the financial crisis, and advancements in climate talks leading toward the Paris Agreement, though these were overshadowed by persistent geopolitical instability and humanitarian crises.

Conflicts and international security

Ongoing wars

The Syrian Civil War saw significant escalation in 2015, with prolonged fighting between government forces, rebels, and jihadist groups leading to major territorial shifts. Kurdish-led forces, backed by U.S. airstrikes, resolved the Battle of Kobani in January by expelling ISIS militants after months of siege, marking a key defeat for the group in northern Syria. The conflict intensified further when Russia initiated direct military intervention on September 30, launching airstrikes to bolster the Assad regime against opposition forces and ISIS, which expanded regime control over contested areas.
ISIS made territorial advances in Iraq and Syria early in the year, capturing the Anbar provincial capital of Ramadi in May despite Iraqi government resistance, which highlighted vulnerabilities in local defenses and enabled the group's temporary expansion westward. These gains strained coalition efforts against the caliphate, though they preceded later counteroffensives.
The Yemeni Civil War erupted in March 2015 amid clashes between Houthi rebels and forces loyal to President Hadi, prompting a Saudi-led coalition of Arab states to intervene militarily on March 26 with airstrikes and ground support to restore the government, escalating the conflict into a broader regional proxy struggle. This intervention aimed to counter Houthi advances toward Aden but prolonged the fighting across multiple fronts.

Terrorist attacks

On January 37, Boko Haram militants launched attacks on the Nigerian towns of Baga and Doron Baga, overrunning a military base and targeting civilians, in what became one of the group's deadliest assaults.
Four days later, on January 7, two Islamist gunmen stormed the Paris offices of the satirical magazine Charlie Hebdo, opening fire and killing 12 people, including journalists and police, in apparent retaliation for the publication's depictions of the Prophet Muhammad.
In late October, Metrojet Flight 9268, en route from Egypt to Russia, exploded mid-air over the Sinai Peninsula due to a bomb planted by ISIS affiliates, killing all 224 passengers and crew.
On November 13, ISIS operatives conducted coordinated shootings and bombings across Paris, including at the Bataclan theater, a stadium, and cafes, resulting in 130 deaths and hundreds injured.

Politics and governance

Major elections

In the United Kingdom, the general election on May 7 resulted in a surprise victory for the Conservative Party led by David Cameron, securing an unexpected majority of 12 seats in the House of Commons and enabling the formation of a single-party government without coalition partners.
Canada's federal election on October 19 saw the Liberal Party, under Justin Trudeau, achieve a majority in the expanded 338-seat House of Commons, marking a shift from the previous Conservative government and emphasizing themes of progressive change.
Argentina's presidential election culminated in a runoff on November 22, where Mauricio Macri defeated the Peronist candidate Daniel Scioli with 51.4% of the vote, ending 12 years of leftist rule by the Kirchner administrations and signaling a move toward center-right policies focused on economic liberalization.
In Poland, the parliamentary election on October 25 delivered a decisive win for the Law and Justice party, which captured both the presidency of the Sejm and a strong majority in the Sejm itself, rejecting the incumbent Civic Platform-led coalition and ushering in conservative governance reforms.

Diplomatic agreements

The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA), a nuclear agreement between Iran and the P5+1 group (United States, United Kingdom, France, Russia, China, and Germany plus the European Union), was finalized on July 14, 2015, in Vienna, imposing restrictions on Iran's nuclear program in exchange for sanctions relief. The deal limited Iran's uranium enrichment capacity, reduced its stockpile of enriched uranium, and enhanced International Atomic Energy Agency monitoring to prevent weapon development.
In response to Greece's government-debt crisis, negotiations culminated in a third economic adjustment program agreed upon on July 12, 2015, between the Greek government and its European creditors and the International Monetary Fund, following a July 5 referendum rejecting prior austerity terms. This bailout package, totaling up to 86 billion, included structural reforms and fiscal measures to stabilize Greece's economy and avert default.
The Minsk II agreement, signed on February 12, 2015, by representatives of Ukraine, Russia, and the self-proclaimed Donetsk and Luhansk People's Republics under mediation by the Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe, aimed to implement an immediate ceasefire in eastern Ukraine's Donbas region. It outlined steps for withdrawal of heavy weapons, prisoner exchanges, and constitutional reforms granting special status to conflict areas, though implementation faced ongoing challenges.
The Paris Agreement, adopted on December 12, 2015, at the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change's COP21 conference in Paris, committed nearly 200 nations to limiting global temperature rise to well below 2C above pre-industrial levels, with efforts to cap it at 1.5C. Parties pledged nationally determined contributions for emissions reductions, adaptation measures, and financial support from developed to developing countries.

Science and technology

Space achievements

NASA's New Horizons spacecraft performed the first close flyby of Pluto on July 14, 2015, passing within 12,500 kilometers of the dwarf planet's surface and transmitting high-resolution images that unveiled a diverse terrain including vast nitrogen ice plains, rugged mountains rising up to 3,500 meters, and potential cryovolcanic features. These observations, the first detailed views of Pluto since its discovery in 1930, also captured data on its thin atmosphere and five moons, reshaping understanding of Kuiper Belt objects.
In March 2015, the Dawn spacecraft achieved orbit around Ceres on March 6, marking the first time a probe orbited two distinct solar system bodies after previously visiting Vesta, and began mapping the dwarf planet's surface. Dawn's instruments revealed intriguing bright spots in Occator Crater, later confirmed as deposits of sodium carbonate from subsurface briny water, suggesting past geological activity involving water and hydrothermal processes.
SpaceX advanced reusable rocket technology with successful first-stage landings of its Falcon 9, culminating in a historic vertical touchdown on December 21, 2015, after deploying 11 Orbcomm OG2 satellites into orbit. This followed earlier attempts, demonstrating controlled descent and propulsion for recovery, a step toward reducing launch costs through booster reuse.
China contributed to space science by launching the Dark Matter Particle Explorer (DAMPE) satellite on December 17, 2015, designed to detect high-energy cosmic rays and search for dark matter particles from low-Earth orbit. Additionally, the nation debuted the solid-fueled Long March-11 rocket on September 25, 2015, enabling rapid launches from land-based sites without complex fueling infrastructure.

Medical and environmental advances

In 2015, significant progress in gene editing was marked by the first reported use of CRISPR-Cas9 on human embryos by a Chinese research team, demonstrating the technique's potential for precise genomic modifications despite ethical concerns. Additionally, researchers introduced a smaller variant of Cas9 from Staphylococcus aureus (SaCas9), enabling more efficient delivery in therapeutic applications.
The Ebola outbreak, which began in 2014, saw containment efforts intensify in 2015 through ring vaccination strategies using the rVSV-ZEBOV vaccine candidate, which proved safe and effective in preventing cases during trials in Guinea. Vaccine trials during this period confirmed immunogenicity and contributed to the decline of new infections by late 2015.
Zika virus awareness surged in 2015 amid outbreaks in the Americas, spurring initial vaccine development efforts focused on protecting vulnerable populations like pregnant women, though no vaccine was yet available.
Environmentally, the Paris Agreement established technical commitments to limit global temperature rise to well below 2C above pre-industrial levels, with aspirations for 1.5C, through nationally determined contributions for emission reductions and enhanced transparency in reporting. It also emphasized technology transfer to build resilience against climate impacts, integrating adaptation measures into long-term low-greenhouse-gas development strategies.

Culture and entertainment

Film and media

Star Wars: The Force Awakens, directed by J.J. Abrams, was released on December 18, 2015, and quickly shattered box office records with an estimated opening weekend of over $225 million in the U.S. The film achieved the largest domestic three-day debut at $247.9 million, including Thursday previews, marking a significant revival for the franchise.
Mad Max: Fury Road, directed by George Miller, garnered widespread critical acclaim for its intense action sequences and innovative practical effects, earning nominations for Best Picture and Best Director at the Academy Awards. The film dominated the technical categories, securing six Oscar wins, the most of any movie that year, including for editing, production design, and sound.
The 88th Academy Awards, held in 2016 for 2015 films, highlighted Spotlight as the Best Picture winner, recognizing its investigative journalism narrative on the Catholic Church abuse scandal. Other notable wins included technical honors that underscored the year's emphasis on visual and auditory craftsmanship in cinema.
The rise of streaming media accelerated in 2015, with Netflix expanding its original content output, including series like Master of None and Grace & Frankie, positioning it as a legitimate TV network rivaling traditional broadcasters. This shift doubled Netflix's investment in originals for the following year, emphasizing creative freedom and broadening viewer access to premium content.

Music and arts

Adele's third studio album, 25, was released on November 20, 2015, and quickly became the year's dominant musical event, selling over 7.44 million copies in the United States alone and topping charts worldwide. The album's lead single "Hello" achieved unprecedented streaming and sales figures, reinforcing Adele's influence in pop and soul music.
Taylor Swift's 1989 World Tour, supporting her 2014 album 1989, extended through much of 2015, featuring high-production performances across multiple continents and solidifying her status as a leading pop artist with innovative stagecraft and guest appearances.
The death of blues legend B.B. King on May 14, 2015, prompted widespread tributes from musicians and fans, highlighting his enduring legacy in blues guitar and composition, with events including a processional in Memphis honoring his contributions.
In visual arts, emerging trends included provocative installations like Banksy's Dismaland bemusement park, a satirical take on consumer culture and escapism that drew global attention to street art's evolution into large-scale exhibitions. Yayoi Kusama's immersive infinity room installations at major museums further exemplified the year's focus on experiential, pattern-driven contemporary art tied to themes of perception and infinity.

Sports

Football scandals and tournaments

The 2015 FIFA corruption scandal erupted on May 27 when U.S. authorities arrested seven FIFA officials in Zurich, Switzerland, on charges including racketeering, wire fraud, and money laundering related to bribery schemes spanning over two decades. These arrests targeted corruption in the awarding of World Cup hosting rights and media and marketing deals, implicating executives from FIFA and its confederations. The fallout intensified scrutiny on FIFA's governance, culminating in President Sepp Blatter's announcement of his resignation on June 2, despite his recent re-election, amid separate investigations into his conduct by Swiss authorities and FIFA's ethics committee.
In club competitions, the UEFA Champions League final took place on June 6 at the Olympiastadion in Berlin, where Barcelona defeated Juventus 3-1, with goals from Ivan Rakiti, Luis Surez, and Neymar securing Barcelona's fifth European title. The match highlighted Barcelona's attacking prowess under Luis Enrique, completing a continental treble after their La Liga and Copa del Rey successes.
The FIFA Women's World Cup, hosted in Canada, concluded in July with the United States defeating Japan 5-2 in the final to claim their third title and first since 1999. Carli Lloyd's standout performance, including a hat-trick in the final, underscored the growing global profile of women's football.
Regionally, the CONCACAF Gold Cup was held across the United States and Canada from July 7 to 26, with Mexico defeating Jamaica 3-1 in the final to secure their seventh title. Other continental tournaments included the Africa Cup of Nations in Equatorial Guinea, won by Ivory Coast, and the Copa Amrica in Chile, claimed by the host nation.

Other global competitions

The 2015 Rugby World Cup was hosted in England, where New Zealand defeated Australia 3417 in the final on October 31 to secure their third title and become the first team to win consecutive tournaments.
At the IAAF World Championships in Athletics held in Beijing, Usain Bolt dominated the sprints by winning gold in the 100m, defeating Justin Gatlin, along with the 200m and 4x100m relay events.
Chris Froome of Team Sky claimed victory in the 2015 Tour de France, marking the first time a British rider won the race twice.
In the NBA Finals, the Golden State Warriors overcame the Cleveland Cavaliers in six games to win the championship, with Andre Iguodala earning Finals MVP honors.

Disasters and crises

Natural disasters

A magnitude 7.8 earthquake struck Nepal on April 25, centered near Gorkha district, triggering widespread landslides and the partial collapse of structures in Kathmandu, resulting in nearly 9,000 deaths and injuring over 21,000 others. The quake's shallow depth amplified its destructive force along the Himalayan front, prompting immediate international rescue efforts focused on rubble clearance and temporary shelters amid aftershocks.
In March, Severe Tropical Cyclone Pam, a Category 5 storm, made landfall in Vanuatu, unleashing winds exceeding 250 km/h that demolished homes, schools, and infrastructure across multiple islands, displacing thousands despite a relatively low death toll of around 11 due to preemptive evacuations. The cyclone's rapid intensification and direct hits on populated areas like Tanna and Erromango exacerbated food and water shortages, with recovery involving urgent aid for rebuilding resilient communities.
Monsoon rains in August triggered devastating floods in Myanmar, submerging low-lying areas and destroying over 1 million acres of rice fields, killing more than 100 people and displacing over 1 million others across the region including parts of Southeast Asia. Relief operations prioritized helicopter deliveries to isolated villages cut off by swollen rivers like the Irrawaddy.
A swarm of seismic activity culminated in a magnitude 8.3 earthquake off Chile's coast near Illapel on September 16, generating tsunami warnings that prompted the evacuation of about 1 million people and causing 15 deaths from related incidents. The event's aftershocks, numbering in the thousands, heightened structural risks in coastal areas, with responses including rapid assessments for tsunami debris and seismic reinforcements.

Human-induced incidents

On March 24, Germanwings Flight 9525, an Airbus A320 en route from Barcelona to Dsseldorf, crashed into the French Alps, killing all 144 passengers and 6 crew members aboard; investigation determined the co-pilot deliberately locked the captain out of the cockpit and initiated a controlled descent into terrain amid his untreated mental health issues. The incident prompted global aviation regulators to enhance pilot mental health monitoring protocols.
Throughout 2015, the European refugee crisis intensified with deadly migrant boat crossings in the Mediterranean, where smugglers overloaded unseaworthy vessels fleeing conflict zones; the deadliest period peaked in April, including a April 19 sinking off Libya that claimed nearly 800 lives when an overcrowded fishing boat capsized after colliding with a rescue ship. Overall, over 3,700 migrant fatalities occurred that year, driven by negligent operations and inadequate safety measures by human traffickers.
On August 12, massive explosions rocked a hazardous goods warehouse in Tianjin, China, triggered by the spontaneous combustion of overheated dry nitrocellulose containers stored improperly near ammonium nitrate; the blasts killed at least 173 people, including firefighters, and caused widespread environmental contamination. Regulatory failures in site management and chemical handling were cited as key contributors to the disaster's scale.
In September, the Volkswagen emissions scandal emerged when U.S. regulators uncovered defeat devicessoftware in about 11 million diesel vehicles worldwidethat detected testing conditions to temporarily reduce nitrogen oxide emissions, allowing real-world pollution far exceeding legal limits. The deception, aimed at meeting stringent standards while maintaining performance, led to billions in fines, recalls, and a leadership shakeup at the automaker.

Notable deaths

January to June

King Abdullah bin Abdulaziz Al Saud, the influential monarch of Saudi Arabia, died on January 23, 2015, at approximately 90 years old while hospitalized for a lung infection. His passing marked a significant transition in the kingdom's leadership, with his half-brother Salman ascending to the throne shortly thereafter.
Leonard Nimoy, renowned for portraying Spock in the Star Trek franchise, died on February 27, 2015, at age 83 from end-stage chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. Nimoy's death prompted widespread tributes from fans and the entertainment industry, highlighting his enduring cultural impact beyond acting into photography and poetry.
B.B. King, the legendary blues guitarist and singer often called the "King of the Blues," passed away on May 14, 2015, at his home in Las Vegas at age 89. King's innovative style, exemplified by his signature guitar "Lucille," influenced generations of musicians across genres, solidifying his legacy as a pivotal figure in American music.
Christopher Lee, the acclaimed actor known for iconic roles in horror films like those featuring Dracula and in fantasy epics such as the Lord of the Rings and Hobbit series, died on June 7, 2015, at age 93 in a London hospital after respiratory issues and heart failure. Lee's versatile career spanned over 200 films, earning him a reputation as one of cinema's most commanding presences.

July to December

In literature and science, E. L. Doctorow, renowned for blending historical fiction with social commentary in works like Ragtime and Billy Bathgate, died on July 21 from complications of lung cancer at age 84. Oliver Sacks, the British neurologist and author whose case studies in books such as Awakenings illuminated neurological disorders and human resilience, passed away on August 30 from cancer at age 82.
In film, Omar Sharif, the Egyptian actor celebrated for his portrayals of T. E. Lawrence's ally in Lawrence of Arabia and Yuri Zhivago in the epic adaptation, died on July 10 from a heart attack at age 83.
In sports, Yogi Berra, the New York Yankees catcher and three-time MVP who won 10 World Series championships and became known for philosophical quips like "It ain't over 'til it's over," died on September 22 of natural causes at age 90.
In technology and gaming, Satoru Iwata, Nintendo's president who oversaw the Wii and DS successes and emphasized accessible entertainment, died on July 11 from bile duct cancer at age 55.
In music, Scott Weiland, lead singer of Stone Temple Pilots whose grunge-influenced hits like "Plush" defined 1990s alternative rock, died on December 3 from an accidental overdose at age 48 while on tour. Lemmy Kilmister, Motrhead's founding bassist and vocalist whose aggressive sound shaped heavy metal with albums like Ace of Spades, succumbed to prostate cancer, cardiac arrhythmia, and heart failure on December 28 at age 70, just days after diagnosis.
