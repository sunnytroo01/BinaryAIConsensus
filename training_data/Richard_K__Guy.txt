Richard K. Guy
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Richard Kenneth Guy (30 September 1916 9 March 2020) was a British-Canadian mathematician renowned for his contributions to number theory, combinatorics, graph theory, and combinatorial game theory. Born in Nuneaton, England, to schoolteacher parents, Guy earned a degree from Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge, in 1938, without pursuing a formal doctorate, a common practice in British mathematics at the time. He later received an honorary doctorate from the University of Calgary in 1991. Over his long career, he authored more than 300 scholarly papers and over a dozen books, often emphasizing unsolved problems and accessible expositions, while collaborating with luminaries such as Paul Erds, John Horton Conway, and Elwyn Berlekamp. Guy's work extended beyond academia; he was an avid mountaineer, chess problem enthusiast, and philanthropist who supported mathematical education and environmental causes until his death at age 103.
Guy's early career was shaped by wartime service and international teaching. During World War II, he served in the Royal Air Force as a meteorologist from 1941 to 1945. After the war, he taught mathematics at secondary schools in England before joining the University of Malaya in Singapore from 1951 to 1962, where he advanced to professor. He then moved to the Indian Institute of Technology in Delhi from 1962 to 1965, fostering mathematical research in Asia. In 1965, Guy immigrated to Canada and joined the University of Calgary, where he became head of the mathematics department, helped establish the computer science department in 1975, and served as emeritus professor until his passing. He supervised several PhD students, including Roger Eggleton (1973) and Richard Nowakowski (1978), and mentored generations through problem-solving sessions and coaching for competitions like the Putnam exam.
Guy's mathematical legacy is marked by his focus on intriguing, unsolved problems and elegant expositions that bridged recreational and professional mathematics. In number theory, he compiled the influential Unsolved Problems in Number Theory (first edition 1981, third edition 2004), a compendium that has inspired countless researchers. His work in combinatorial game theory culminated in the seminal four-volume Winning Ways for your Mathematical Plays (1982, revised 20012004), co-authored with Conway and Berlekamp, which analyzed impartial games like Nim and introduced concepts such as the Sprague-Grundy theorem in an engaging, illustrated style. In graph theory, he contributed to crossing numbers and extremal problems, often collaborating with Erds (with whom he shared an Erds number of 1). Other notable books include The Book of Numbers (1996, with Conway) and Don't Try to Solve These Problems! (1993), reflecting his witty, problem-oriented approach. Guy also edited the "Unsolved Problems" column in the American Mathematical Monthly for decades, stimulating global mathematical inquiry.
Beyond mathematics, Guy's personal life intertwined with his passions. Married to Nancy Louise Thirian since 1940 until her death in 2010, he raised three childrenElizabeth, Michael, and Peterseveral of whom pursued careers in mathematics and computing, along with seven grandchildren in related fields. An enthusiastic mountaineer, he and Louise funded the Louise and Richard Guy Hut in Yoho National Park, Canada, and participated in annual ascents of the Calgary Tower for charity. His interdisciplinary interests extended to chess problems and recreational mathematics, often featured in collaborations with Martin Gardner. Guy's enduring influence is evident in tributes like the annual Louise and Richard Guy Lecture series at the University of Calgary and his role in organizing international conferences on combinatorial games.

Biography

Early life and education

Richard Kenneth Guy was born on 30 September 1916 in Nuneaton, Warwickshire, England, to schoolteachers Augusta Adeline Tanner and William Alexander Charles Guy. His parents' professions as educators profoundly influenced his early development, fostering a strong emphasis on learning and intellectual pursuits within the family environment. Although they encouraged him toward a teaching career, Guy initially resisted this path, drawn instead to the pure enjoyment of mathematics.
Guy attended Warwick School as a boarder, where he was placed in advanced classes due to his aptitude. During his school years, he developed keen interests in mathematics, spurred by the encouragement of teacher Cyril Caton, and in chess, where he both played competitively and began composing problems. These early passions laid the foundation for his lifelong engagement with recreational mathematics and chess composition.
In 1935, Guy entered Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge, on scholarships to study mathematics. He completed his B.A. in 1938 and later received his M.A. in 1941. His time at Cambridge solidified his mathematical training, preparing him for subsequent contributions to the field.

World War II service

In December 1940, shortly before the intensification of World War II, Richard K. Guy married Nancy Louise Thirian in Nottingham, England. The couple would go on to have three children during the war years: Elizabeth Anne in 1941, Michael John in 1943, and Peter Richard in 1944.
Guy trained as a meteorologist in 1941 and received an emergency commission as a flight lieutenant in the Meteorological Branch of the Royal Air Force, serving from 1941 to 1945. His duties focused on weather forecasting essential for military aviation, including support for bombers operating over Europe and transatlantic flights. He was initially posted to Prestwick in Scotland, followed by Reykjavk, Iceland, in 1942, where he forecasted conditions for Allied operations amid challenging Arctic weather. Later, in 1944, he was assigned to Bermuda, continuing meteorological support for transatlantic aviation routes until his demobilization in 1946.
This period of service interrupted Guy's early academic pursuits but immersed him in the practical demands of numerical weather prediction, requiring precise calculations and data analysis under pressureskills that aligned with his mathematical background from Cambridge.

Academic career

After graduating from Cambridge in 1938, Guy obtained a teaching diploma from the University of Birmingham and taught mathematics at Stockport Grammar School from 1939 to 1941. Following World War II, he resumed his teaching career in England, first at Stockport Grammar School from 1945 to 1947. He then moved to Goldsmiths College, University of London, where he taught mathematics at the degree level from 1947 to 1951, during which time he also served as the endings editor for the British Chess Magazine.
In 1951, Guy relocated with his wife Louise and their young family to Singapore, accepting a lectureship at the University of Malaya (now the National University of Singapore), where he remained until 1962. The position in a vibrant mathematics department allowed him to balance teaching with growing interests in research.
From 1961 to 1965, Guy took a position at the newly established Indian Institute of Technology in New Delhi, where he contributed to teaching engineering students while the family adapted to life in India. In 1965, seeking further opportunities, the family moved to Canada, and Guy joined the University of Calgary as a professor of mathematics, a role he held until his retirement in 1982.
After retirement, Guy was granted emeritus status at the University of Calgary, where he continued to work daily in his office, supervising graduate students and fostering collaborations, including notable work with Paul Erds on problems in number theory and combinatorics. He received an honorary Doctor of Laws from the University of Calgary in 1991 and the Lester R. Ford Award from the Mathematical Association of America in 1989 for his expository paper "The Strong Law of Small Numbers."

Personal life and death

Richard K. Guy married Nancy Louise Thirian on December 21, 1940, in a union that lasted 70 years until her death in 2010. Together, they raised three childrenElizabeth Anne, Michael John, and Peter Richardwhile navigating frequent relocations tied to Guy's academic career, including approximately a decade in Singapore from 1951 to 1961 at the University of Malaya, a stint in India at the Indian Institute of Technology in Delhi, and their permanent move to Canada in 1965, where they settled in Calgary and adapted to life in the Rockies amid family growth and professional demands. Their son Michael followed in his father's footsteps as a mathematician, reflecting the intellectual environment of their household.
Guy's personal interests centered on the outdoors, particularly mountaineering, hiking, and cross-country skiing, pursuits he shared with Nancy and continued vigorously after her passing. The couple climbed in the Swiss Alps, summited Mount Kinabalu in Borneo, and explored the Canadian Rockies, with Guy attending 29 General Mountaineering Camps organized by the Alpine Club of Canada, of which he became an honorary member alongside the Calgary Mountain Club. Even into advanced age, he maintained an active lifestyle, skiing the St. Elias Mountains in 1989 at 73 and reaching the Mount Assiniboine Towers at 90, while hiking trails like Wonder Pass at 100, crediting such endeavors for his enduring vitality.
Guy's longevity, reaching 103 years, was bolstered by this resilient, outdoor-oriented routine, allowing him to remain engaged as Professor Emeritus at the University of Calgary until February 2020, just weeks before his death on March 9, 2020, in Calgary, Alberta. Post-retirement in 1982, he extended his impact through philanthropy, notably co-funding the Louise and Richard Guy Hut on the Wapta Icefield with the Alpine Club of Canada in 2015 to support mountaineering access, and directing donations toward the University of Calgary's scholarship endowments and lecture series to advance mathematical education, efforts recognized by his 1991 honorary doctorate from the institution.

Mathematical work

Number theory

Richard K. Guy's contributions to number theory centered on analytic and additive properties of integers, with a particular emphasis on unsolved problems, coincidental patterns in sequences, and Diophantine equations. His seminal book Unsolved Problems in Number Theory, first published in 1981 and expanded in subsequent editions in 1994 and 2004, compiles and analyzes over 200 open questions, serving as a cornerstone reference for researchers. The work organizes problems into categories like additive bases, prime representations, and Diophantine approximations, providing historical context, partial results, and computational insights for each. For instance, it explores variants of the Bunyakovsky conjecture, which asserts that an irreducible polynomial of degree greater than 1 with integer coefficients, positive leading coefficient, and no fixed prime divisor produces infinitely many primes when evaluated at positive integers, and questions about integer partitions, such as whether every sufficiently large integer can be expressed as a sum of distinct partition numbers.
Guy introduced the "Strong Law of Small Numbers" in a 1988 essay, cautioning that apparent patterns or equalities observed in small integer values often fail to generalize because "there arent enough small numbers to meet the many demands made of them." This principle highlights pitfalls in number theory, such as mistaking finite coincidences in sequenceslike the equality of certain binomial coefficients or polygonal numbersfor universal truths. He extended this in 1990 with the "Second Strong Law of Small Numbers," observing that "when two numbers look equal, it aint necessarily so," illustrated by examples where initial matches in generating functions or recurrences diverge later. These essays underscore the importance of rigorous verification beyond small cases in additive and combinatorial number theory.
In collaboration with Paul Erds, Guy investigated additive number theory topics, including the structural properties and asymptotic behavior of integer sequences, contributing several joint papers on sums, differences, and representations of integers. Their efforts advanced understanding of additive bases and the distribution of sums in arithmetic progressions, often blending computational exploration with conjectural insights.
Guy's work on Diophantine approximation included a 1993 collaboration with Andrew Bremner and Richard J. Nowakowski, examining which positive integers nnn can be written as (x+y+z)(1x+1y+1z)(x + y + z)\left(\frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{y} + \frac{1}{z}\right)(x+y+z)(x1+y1+z1) for positive integers x,y,zx, y, zx,y,z. They reduced the problem to finding integer points on specific elliptic curves, demonstrating, for example, that n=62n = 62n=62 is representable with x=5075x = 5075x=5075, y=128050y = 128050y=128050, z=160602z = 160602z=160602, while providing bounds on solutions and linking it to irrationality measures for algebraic numbers through approximation quality. This research informs broader studies of rational approximations to irrationals, as discussed in Guy's book under sections on effective irrationality measures, where he surveys bounds like Roth's theorem and its implications for how well irrationals can be approximated by rationals.
Guy also analyzed iterative processes akin to the Collatz conjecture, focusing on aliquot sequences defined by repeatedly summing proper divisors. In a 1975 paper with John L. Selfridge, he introduced the concept of a "driver"a prime power factor that temporarily dominates the sequence's growthand proved that only finitely many such drivers exist, specifically 2, 233, 2335233 \times 52335, 25725 \times 7257, 293113129^3 \times 11 \times 312931131, and even perfect numbers. Their computations revealed long, diverging sequences, such as one exceeding 3400 terms starting from even indices up to 99225, challenging the conjecture that all aliquot sequences terminate. In a 1983 article, Guy highlighted the Collatz (3n+1) problem as particularly resistant, noting extensive computational verification up to 101810^{18}1018 without counterexamples but emphasizing its deceptive simplicity and lack of modular structure for proof. These studies exemplify Guy's blend of computation and theory in probing unsolved iterative dynamics.

Combinatorics and geometry

Richard K. Guy made significant contributions to combinatorics and geometry, focusing on discrete structures, enumerative problems, and their intersections with other mathematical areas. His research emphasized finite configurations, such as tilings and polyhedra, often exploring impossibility results and minimal examples through rigorous proofs and constructions. Over his career, Guy authored or co-authored numerous papers in combinatorial geometry and related fields.
One of Guy's notable achievements in geometric combinatorics was the discovery of a unistable polyhedron with 19 faces and 34 vertices in 1968, the smallest known example at the time of a convex body that rests stably in only one orientation when placed on a plane. This construction, detailed in a joint paper with John H. Conway, resolved an open question about the existence of such monostatic polyhedra and provided a physical model that Guy himself built. The result highlighted the interplay between stability conditions and polyhedral combinatorics, influencing later work that reduced the minimal face count to 14.
Guy's work on polyominoes advanced the enumeration and tiling properties of these edge-connected squares, including proofs of impossibility for certain coverings. In collaboration with Hallard T. Croft and Kenneth J. Falconer, he compiled key unsolved problems in polyomino tilings, such as whether specific sets of polyominoes can tile rectangles or the plane without gaps or overlaps, emphasizing parity and coloring arguments to establish non-tilability. These efforts provided foundational classifications and stimulated research into algorithmic enumeration, where fixed polyominoes up to order 28 have been cataloged, though higher orders remain computationally intensive.
In graph theory, Guy contributed to decomposition problems and Ramsey-type questions, disproving a conjecture by Paul Erds on the coarseness of complete graphs KnK_nKn, which measures the minimum number of planar subgraphs needed to cover all edges. He established that the coarseness of KnK_nKn is exactly n/2\lceil n/2 \rceiln/2 for n7n \geq 7n7, using embeddings and thickness bounds. Additionally, in brief collaboration with Erds, Guy explored generalized Ramsey numbers for graphs, determining thresholds where monochromatic subgraphs are unavoidable in edge-colored complete graphs.
Guy bridged geometry and number theory through studies involving elliptic curves, particularly over finite fields, to parametrize geometric configurations with rational or integer constraints. For instance, he investigated triangles with rational sides satisfying specific area or perimeter conditions, showing that certain Morley trisector triangles lead to elliptic curves y2=x34x+4y^2 = x^3 - 4x + 4y2=x34x+4 over finite fields, yielding infinitely many solutions modulo primes. This approach revealed deep connections, such as integer-sided triangles equidissected by altitudes.
His broader impact in combinatorial geometry included fair division problems, where he analyzed equitable partitions of convex sets, such as dividing polygons into subsets of equal area using minimal cuts, often linking to impartial games for strategic fairness. These problems, featured in his compilations of open questions, underscored applications to resource allocation and underscored the unity of combinatorial structures.

Computational and recreational mathematics

Richard K. Guy made significant contributions to computational mathematics through his early adoption of computers to enumerate complex mathematical structures, such as polyominoes, which are plane figures formed by joining equal squares edge to edge. In the 1960s, as computational resources became more accessible, Guy utilized them to explore and count polyomino configurations, building on the foundational work of Solomon Golomb and aiding in the systematic classification of these objects for puzzle and tiling problems. His efforts helped bridge manual combinatorial analysis with automated enumeration, providing data that informed later theoretical developments in tiling theory.
One of Guy's most celebrated discoveries occurred in late 1969, when he identified the glider pattern while collaborating with John Horton Conway and others on the cellular automaton known as Conway's Game of Life. Visiting his son in Cambridge, Guy observed a stable, moving configuration of five cells that translates diagonally across the grid at a speed of c/4, exhibiting glide reflection symmetry; Conway subsequently named it the "glider." This pattern proved essential as a building block for more complex structures, including glider guns that periodically emit gliders, demonstrating the Game of Life's capacity for universal computation akin to a Turing machine.
Guy's work extended into combinatorial game theory (CGT), where he independently developed key concepts for analyzing impartial games in 1947, predating widespread awareness of the Sprague-Grundy theorem. He introduced the minimum excluded value (mex) function to compute the Grundy number of a position, defined as the mex of the Grundy numbers of all reachable positions, enabling the evaluation of game states by assigning nimbers that facilitate optimal play strategies. These tools allowed the decomposition of multi-component games into sums of simpler ones, equivalent to Nim heaps, and Guy applied them to invent octal games, a class parameterized by binary digits that dictate heap-splitting rules and generate periodic Grundy sequences ripe for computational verification.
In 1982, Guy co-authored Winning Ways for your Mathematical Plays with Elwyn R. Berlekamp and John H. Conway, a seminal two-volume treatise that systematized CGT for recreational and theoretical purposes. The work analyzes impartial games such as variants of Nim, Dawson's Chess, and Kayles, using mex and Grundy numbers to derive winning strategies and introduce surreal numbers for partizan games, blending rigorous analysis with playful examples to make advanced theory accessible. Guy's contributions emphasized computational methods to compute game values, influencing subsequent research in algorithm design for game solving.
Guy popularized recreational mathematics through expository writings and editorial efforts inspired by Martin Gardner's Scientific American columns, fostering public engagement with puzzles and games. He maintained a lively correspondence with Gardner, exchanging ideas on topics like isogons and graph crossings, which enriched Gardner's columns and Guy's own publications. As editor of The Lighter Side of Mathematics (1994), Guy compiled proceedings from the Eugne Strens Memorial Conference, featuring puzzles, historical insights, and computational recreations that echoed Gardner's style while advancing scholarly interest in fun mathematical challenges.

Chess compositions

Endgame studies

Richard K. Guy composed nearly 200 original chess endgame studies between 1937 and 1954, emphasizing strategic depth and paradoxical outcomes that highlighted the interplay between chess tactics and logical reasoning. His studies often featured intricate pawn structures and king maneuvers designed to create mutual zugzwang positions, where both sides are forced into disadvantageous moves, leading to draws or unexpected wins through refusal to capture or precise timing. These compositions were recognized for their instructional value, demonstrating how minimalistic setups could yield complex solutions, and they integrated mathematical logic by requiring solvers to deduce optimal paths akin to proof-based reasoning in number theory. Guy's work earned acclaim in chess literature for pioneering themes such as stalemate traps that exploited positional paradoxes, influencing subsequent composers in the genre.
One notable example is Guy's 1942 study published in the British Chess Magazine, where White to move and draw in a pawn endgame relies on reciprocal zugzwang to prevent Black's pawn advance. This maneuver forces Black's king into a passive role, culminating in a drawn position through careful king "dancing" that mirrors combinatorial optimization problems. Another innovative study from 1946 in LEchiquier de Paris showcases an unusual pawn promotion sequence in a queen endgame, where White sacrifices a pawn to promote against Black's two-pawn advantage, enabling a swift mate and underscoring Guy's focus on counterintuitive promotions and queen-vs-queen dynamics.
Guy's most celebrated achievement was a 1944 study in Chess magazine, which set a record for the maximum number of bishop promotions in a single compositionfour in totalheld for over 30 years. The sequence involves successive captures and promotions, exemplifying his integration of mathematical precision, treating promotions as a chain of logical dependencies to achieve material dominance, and it remains a benchmark for promotion-themed endgames. A compilation of his studies, edited by John Roycroft, was published in 1996, preserving these works and highlighting their enduring impact on endgame study composition.

Editorial roles and codes

Guy served as the Endings Editor of the British Chess Magazine from 1947 to 1951, where he managed the endgame studies column and promoted problem-solving content to engage readers in analytical chess puzzles. During his tenure, he selected and published studies, including his own compositions, contributing to the magazine's reputation for high-quality chess problem material.
In collaboration with Hugh Blandford and John Roycroft, Guy co-invented the GBR code in the mid-20th century, a concise notation system for representing chessboard positions in endgame studies using at most six digits. The code sums values for major pieces1 for each white piece and 3 for each black piece, ordered as queens (Q), rooks (R), bishops (B), and knights (N)followed by a decimal point for white and black pawns, enabling quick decoding by division (quotient for black, remainder for white). This innovation standardized material classification, aiding the organization of study databases and look-up directories.
Guy's work extended to international chess problem tournaments, where he participated as a composer and earned recognition for his submissions published in British Chess Magazine.
Through his editorial and coding contributions, Guy shaped archival practices by emphasizing verification of study soundness and systematic indexing, which became foundational for preserving and accessing chess endgame compositions in global collections.

Publications

Books

Richard K. Guy was a prolific author whose books bridged advanced mathematics with broader audiences, emphasizing open problems, game theory, and the historical allure of numbers. His seminal Unsolved Problems in Number Theory, first published in 1981 by Springer-Verlag, compiles hundreds of open questions across topics like primes, divisibility, and Diophantine equations, serving as an enduring reference for researchers. The work expanded through three editionsthe second in 1994 and the third in 2004incorporating new problems and updates on progress, and it has amassed over 700 academic citations for its role in stimulating number theory investigations.
In 1982, Guy co-authored Winning Ways for Your Mathematical Plays with Elwyn R. Berlekamp and John H. Conway, a two-volume exploration of impartial games and combinatorial strategies published by Academic Press. This foundational text introduced concepts like the Sprague-Grundy theorem in an engaging, narrative style, influencing fields from computer science to puzzle design; a revised four-volume second edition appeared from A K Peters between 2001 and 2004. The series has exceeded 700 citations, underscoring its lasting impact on recreational and theoretical game analysis.
Guy's The Book of Numbers: The Secret of Numbers and How They Changed the World, co-written with John H. Conway and released by Springer in 1996, traces the evolution of numerical concepts from ancient systems to modern curiosities, blending history, patterns, and cultural anecdotes for non-specialists. Praised for its whimsical yet rigorous approach, the book has garnered around 600 citations and remains a popular introduction to numeracy's societal role.
He also produced works like Unsolved Problems in Geometry (1991, co-authored with Hallard T. Croft and Kenneth J. Falconer; reprinted 1994), extending his catalog of challenges to geometric configurations and packing problems, reflecting ongoing scholarly engagement. Other notable titles include Unsolved Problems in Combinatorial Games (2001, co-authored with Berlekamp and Richard Nowakowski), a key reference in game theory analysis.

Selected papers

Richard K. Guy authored over 300 scholarly papers across number theory, combinatorics, geometry, and related fields, many published in prestigious journals such as the American Mathematical Monthly.
One of his most influential contributions is the paper "The Strong Law of Small Numbers," published in the American Mathematical Monthly in 1988, which humorously critiques the tendency for small integers to coincidentally satisfy numerous conjectural patterns in mathematics, earning the Lester R. Ford Award from the Mathematical Association of America. This work has been widely cited for its insights into the pitfalls of pattern recognition in number theory, with over 100 citations in mathematical literature.
In "Don't Try to Solve These Problems!" (1983, American Mathematical Monthly), Guy explores impossibility proofs, demonstrating techniques to establish that certain combinatorial and number-theoretic problems lack solutions, such as specific tiling impossibilities and Diophantine equations without integer roots. The paper emphasizes methodological approaches to non-existence results, influencing subsequent work on undecidability and negative proofs in discrete mathematics.
Guy collaborated with Paul Erds on several seminal papers, including "Distinct Distances Between Lattice Points" (1970, Elemente der Mathematik), which investigates the minimal number of distinct distances realized by sets of lattice points in the plane, providing bounds relevant to geometric problems in additive combinatorics. Another key joint work, "Crossing Number Problems" (1973, American Mathematical Monthly), addresses conjectures on the minimum number of edge crossings in drawings of complete graphs, establishing asymptotic bounds and highlighting connections to extremal graph theory. These collaborations, part of Guy's four joint papers with Erds, advanced understanding of arithmetic progressions and subset structures in finite sets.
Early in his career, Guy contributed to computational enumerations through "Some Mathematical Recreations" (1960, Nabla), which includes classifications and counts of polyominoesconnected unions of unit squareslaying groundwork for enumerative combinatorics and tiling problems. This paper, with its focus on fixed polyominoes up to congruence, has informed later algorithmic approaches to polyomino generation and has been referenced in over 50 studies on recreational mathematics.
Guy's papers collectively amassed thousands of citations, reflecting their high impact.
