Antiproton
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The antiproton (denoted as p or \bar{p}) is the antiparticle counterpart to the proton, a fundamental baryon composed of two up antiquarks and one down antiquark, possessing the same mass of 938.272088(3) MeV/c, a negative electric charge of 1 elementary charge (e), spin of 1/2 , and an oppositely directed magnetic moment relative to the proton. As a stable particle in isolation, the antiproton annihilates upon contact with ordinary matter, primarily protons, converting its rest mass energy into multiple pions, gamma rays, and other particles through strong and electromagnetic interactions.
The antiproton was discovered on October 19, 1955, by physicists Emilio Segr and Owen Chamberlain, along with their collaborators Clyde Wiegand, Edward Lofgren, and Thomas Ypsilantis, at the University of California, Berkeley's Radiation Laboratory using the 6.2 GeV Bevatron accelerator. High-energy protons were collided with a copper target to produce proton-antiproton pairs via pair production, with approximately 60 antiprotons identified by measuring their momentum via magnetic deflection and velocity via time-of-flight and Cherenkov radiation detection, confirmed by annihilation tracks in photographic emulsions. This breakthrough, predicted theoretically by Paul Dirac's 1928 relativistic quantum equation implying the existence of antimatter, earned Segr and Chamberlain the 1959 Nobel Prize in Physics and confirmed the existence of antimatter beyond positrons and antineutrons.
Antiprotons are produced today in particle accelerators by smashing high-energy proton beams into fixed targets, generating them as secondary particles in high-energy collisions, followed by accumulation, cooling, and deceleration to low energies for study. At CERN's Antiproton Decelerator (AD), protons from the Proton Synchrotron are directed at an iridium target to yield antiprotons, which are then stochastically cooled and decelerated to a kinetic energy of 5.3 MeV (momentum around 100 MeV/c), with the ELENA extension further decelerating them to 0.1 MeV kinetic energy (momentum around 13.7 MeV/c) to enhance trapping efficiency by factors of 10 to 100as of 2025, ELENA routinely extracts bunches of up to 10 million antiprotons. These low-energy antiprotons enable experiments probing fundamental symmetries, such as CPT invariance, by comparing properties like charge-to-mass ratios and magnetic moments between protons and antiprotons, with measurements confirming equality to parts-per-trillion precision (as of 2025).
In antimatter research, antiprotons are combined with positrons to form antihydrogen atoms, allowing spectroscopic comparisons with hydrogen to test matter-antimatter asymmetry and gravitational behavior, as pursued by collaborations like ALPHA, AEgIS, ASACUSA, BASE, and GBAR at CERN. Recent advancements include the BASE collaboration's 2025 demonstration of proton transport from antimatter facilities for direct comparisons. Key achievements include the first production of antihydrogen in 2002, its trapping for over 1,000 seconds in 2011, and the inaugural measurement of its visible spectrum in 2012, revealing similarities to hydrogen and constraining exotic physics models. Beyond fundamental physics, antiproton beams facilitate studies of nuclear interactions, exotic atoms like antiprotonic helium, and potential applications in cancer therapy via precise energy deposition from annihilation products, though production challenges limit practical use.

Physical Properties

Fundamental Characteristics

The antiproton is the antiparticle of the proton, exhibiting identical mass but opposite electric charge, as predicted by the Dirac equation and confirmed through CPT symmetry in quantum field theory. As a baryon, it is classified within the standard model of particle physics as a hadron composed of antiquarks.

Comparison to the Proton

The antiproton and proton exhibit identical mass and spin, consistent with expectations from CPT invariance, a fundamental symmetry in quantum field theory that equates the properties of particles and their antiparticles under combined charge conjugation, parity, and time reversal transformations. The measured mass of the antiproton is 938.27208816(29) MeV/c, matching the proton's mass to within experimental precision. Similarly, both particles possess spin 1/2, reflecting their status as spin-1/2 fermions in the baryon octet of the quark model.
In contrast, the antiproton carries an opposite electric charge of -1 relative to the proton's +1 (in elementary charge units), leading to reversed interactions with electromagnetic fields. This charge reversal also manifests in their magnetic moments: the proton's magnetic moment is +2.79284734(3) nuclear magnetons, while the antiproton's is -2.7928473441(42) nuclear magnetons, confirming the expected sign flip under particle-antiparticle conjugation. Consequently, the Land g-factor, which relates the magnetic moment to spin and charge, is approximately +5.586 for the proton and -5.586 for the antiproton, with the magnitudes identical but signs opposed due to the charge difference.
These properties arise from the quark model, where the proton consists of two up quarks and one down quark (uud), while the antiproton comprises their antiquarks ( d). The up quark has charge +2/3 and the down quark -1/3, yielding the proton's net +1 charge; the antiquarks reverse these to -2/3 and +1/3, resulting in the antiproton's -1 charge and altered electromagnetic couplings, such as opposite responses to magnetic fields. This compositional difference underscores the antiproton's role as the CPT conjugate of the proton, yet it profoundly affects observable behaviors in electromagnetic environments.
The stark disparity between protons and antiprotons in the observable universewhere protons outnumber antiprotons by a factor of roughly 10highlights the baryon asymmetry, a key unsolved problem in cosmology arising from slight violations of CP symmetry in the early universe that favored matter over antimatter. This imbalance, preserved since the Big Bang, implies that antiprotons are exceedingly rare in natural settings, primarily appearing as secondaries from high-energy processes rather than primordial relics.

History and Discovery

Theoretical Prediction

The theoretical prediction of the antiproton emerged from Paul Dirac's development of relativistic quantum mechanics in 1928, where his equation for the electron incorporated both quantum mechanics and special relativity, yielding solutions with negative energy states that implied the existence of antiparticles with opposite charge but identical mass. Although initially formulated for electronspredicting the positron as a "hole" in a sea of negative-energy electronsthe Dirac equation's structure for spin-1/2 fermions naturally extended to other fundamental particles like the proton, which was then regarded as elementary, suggesting an antiproton analog with positive mass and negative charge. Dirac later clarified this interpretation in 1930, proposing the "Dirac sea" model, an infinite filled sea of negative-energy particles where absences (holes) behave as positively charged antiparticles, a concept applicable to protons as well.
In the 1930s, this framework was extended to baryons amid growing understanding of nuclear forces. Hideki Yukawa's 1935 meson theory, which posited intermediate-mass particles (mesons) to mediate the strong nuclear interaction between protons and neutrons, built on Dirac's ideas by incorporating quantum field concepts, implicitly anticipating antiparticles for both mesons and the nucleons themselves within a relativistic field description. Yukawa's approach treated protons and neutrons as Dirac fields, reinforcing the prediction of antiprotons as their charge-conjugate counterparts, essential for consistency in describing nuclear binding and short-range forces.
The role of particle-antiparticle pairs became central to quantum field theory, where the Dirac sea provided an early mechanism for pair creation and annihilation, later visualized through Richard Feynman's diagrams in the 1940s, which depicted baryons and antibaryons emerging symmetrically from vacuum fluctuations in processes like scattering. These pairs ensured charge conservation and field quantization, with antiprotons arising as necessary counterparts to protons in Feynman-Stueckelberg interpretations of relativistic invariance. However, early adoption faced skepticism; for instance, George Gamow argued in 1934 against stable negative protons, citing potential instabilities in atomic and nuclear structures due to unchecked electromagnetic attractions and rapid annihilation risks in ordinary matter.
This theoretical groundwork laid the foundation for later formalizations, such as the CPT theorem, which rigorously mandates antiparticle existence for every particle under charge conjugation, parity, and time reversal symmetries.

Experimental Confirmation

The antiproton was experimentally confirmed in 1955 at the University of California, Berkeley's Radiation Laboratory, using the Bevatron accelerator, where protons were accelerated to 6.2 GeV and directed onto stationary copper targets to produce the particles through high-energy collisions. The detection system employed a combination of scintillation counters, Cherenkov counters, and time-of-flight measurements to select negatively charged particles with a momentum of approximately 1.19 GeV/c and a velocity corresponding to 0.78, yielding a mass within 5% of the proton's value of about 938 MeV/c.
Confirmation of the antiproton's identity relied on its negative charge, proton-like mass, and behavior in matter: unlike electrons, these particles exhibited minimal ionization and stopped abruptly upon encountering protons, leading to annihilation events. Shortly after the initial detection, visual confirmation came from cloud chamber observations of antiproton-proton annihilation, revealing characteristic tracks of multiple pions produced in the process. In total, the teamEmilio Segr, Owen Chamberlain, Clyde Wiegand, Thomas Ypsilantis, and Edward Lofgrenobserved around 60 such events during runs totaling about 7 hours, corresponding to a production rate of roughly 7 antiprotons per hour at the 6.2 GeV energy.
This groundbreaking discovery provided direct empirical validation of antimatter's existence beyond positrons and electrons, profoundly impacting particle physics by confirming the symmetry predicted in Dirac's theory. For their work, Emilio Segr and Owen Chamberlain were awarded the 1959 Nobel Prize in Physics, recognizing the antiproton's observation as a pivotal advancement in understanding fundamental particles.

Natural Occurrence

Detection in Cosmic Rays

The first detection of antiprotons in cosmic rays occurred in 1979 through balloon-borne experiments that employed superconducting magnet spectrometers to measure particle trajectories and velocities. These instruments identified antiprotons by analyzing the mismatch between a particle's velocity (determined via time-of-flight and Cherenkov detectors) and its momentum-to-charge ratio (rigidity) in the magnetic field, distinguishing them from protons and other negatively charged particles like pi-mesons. The experiment reported 46 antiproton candidates in the energy range of approximately 5 to 12 GeV, confirming their presence in the galactic cosmic ray flux.
Antiprotons in cosmic rays are primarily produced as secondary particles when high-energy primary cosmic ray protons and nuclei collide with interstellar gas, primarily hydrogen and helium, generating pions that decay into antiprotons. This spallation process occurs throughout the galaxy, with the resulting antiprotons propagating to Earth while undergoing energy losses and interactions. Observations indicate that the yield leads to roughly 1 antiproton per 10,000 incoming protons at Earth, reflecting the combined effects of production efficiency and galactic propagation.
Measurements of the antiproton flux in galactic cosmic rays reveal a ratio of approximately 10410^{-4}104 antiprotons per proton, which decreases at higher energies due to reduced production cross-sections and propagation effects. Key experiments have refined these fluxes: the Balloon-Borne Experiment with a Superconducting Spectrometer (BESS), conducted in the 1990s and 2000s via Antarctic balloon flights, measured the antiproton spectrum from 0.18 to 4.20 GeV, establishing a precise low-energy baseline consistent with secondary origins. More recently, the Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer-02 (AMS-02) on the International Space Station, operational since 2011, has provided high-precision data confirming an antiproton fraction of about 0.016% (or 1.61041.6 \times 10^{-4}1.6104) in the rigidity range of 1 to 400 GV, with improved statistics over millions of events. In February 2025, AMS-02 reported the antiproton flux over a full solar cycle (20112024), further confirming consistency with secondary astrophysical production and no significant excess from primordial sources.
The antiproton-to-proton spectral ratio exhibits a peak around 10 GeV, where the flux appears slightly higher than expected from standard astrophysical models of secondary production and propagation. This feature has sparked debate, with some analyses suggesting it could indicate contributions from dark matter annihilation or decay, though alternative explanations involving astrophysical uncertainties remain viable. Ongoing AMS-02 observations continue to test these interpretations with data spanning a full solar cycle.

Terrestrial and Astrophysical Sources

In 2011, the Payload for Antimatter Matter Particle Detection and Annihilation (PAMELA) satellite experiment discovered a belt of geomagnetically trapped antiprotons encircling Earth, analogous to the Van Allen radiation belts but containing antimatter. This belt is located primarily in the inner radiation zone, with detections concentrated in the South Atlantic Anomaly region at altitudes of 350610 km. The antiproton flux in this region exceeds the galactic cosmic-ray antiproton flux by three orders of magnitude at solar minimum and is up to four orders of magnitude higher than the sub-cutoff flux outside the belts, with measurements spanning kinetic energies from 60 to 750 MeV.
These terrestrial antiprotons are produced through interactions of incoming cosmic rays with Earth's upper atmosphere, primarily via the Cosmic Ray Albedo Neutron Decay (CRAND) process, where cosmic-ray protons collide with atmospheric nuclei to generate antineutrons that subsequently decay into antiprotons. The resulting antiprotons are then captured and trapped by Earth's geomagnetic field, spiraling along field lines despite annihilation losses with residual atmospheric protons.
Trapped antiprotons in planetary magnetic fields have finite residence times of months to years, limited by gradual annihilation with atmospheric constituents and diffusion out of the trapping region. Observations of the cosmic-ray antiproton spectrum provide a lower limit on the intrinsic antiproton lifetime exceeding 1.7 million years (90% confidence level), derived from the absence of decay signatures in propagated fluxes modeled via galactic diffusion.
Beyond terrestrial environments, theoretical models predict antiprotons as potential reservoirs in various astrophysical settings, distinct from propagating cosmic-ray populations. In supernova remnants, nonlinear kinetic models of cosmic-ray acceleration forecast significant antiproton production through spallation of accelerated protons on interstellar gas, with reacceleration of preexisting interstellar antiprotons enhancing yields up to GeV energies. Near black holes, particularly rapidly rotating stellar-mass ones, gravitomagnetic interactions can accelerate protons to produce antiprotons via pair production and nuclear collisions, contributing to cosmic-ray fluxes. Models for gamma-ray bursts similarly anticipate elevated antiproton outputs from high-energy proton interactions in the burst environment, potentially yielding fluxes orders of magnitude above standard cosmic-ray secondaries during active phases.
Recent analyses of Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer-02 (AMS-02) data collected through 2023 confirm consistency with secondary astrophysical origins for observed antiprotons, showing no significant excess attributable to primordial sources such as evaporating black holes or exotic mechanisms.

Production and Facilities

Accelerator-Based Production

Antiprotons are produced in particle accelerators primarily through the collision of high-energy proton beams with fixed heavy targets, such as iridium or copper, generating proton-antiproton pairs via inelastic scattering and pair production mechanisms. The threshold kinetic energy required for pair production in the laboratory frame, for a proton colliding with a stationary proton target, is approximately 6 GeV, though practical production employs higher energies to achieve sufficient yields.
The cross-section for antiproton production in proton-proton collisions, (pp p \bar{p} X), is on the order of 10 millibarns at a proton beam energy of 26 GeV and reaches a peak value around 100 GeV, reflecting the energy dependence of secondary particle yields in high-energy interactions. Following production, the antiprotons, initially possessing energies in the GeV range and high emittance, undergo cooling and accumulation processes to prepare them for downstream experiments. Stochastic cooling reduces the beam's momentum spread by detecting and correcting statistical fluctuations in particle positions and velocities, while electron cooling involves merging the antiproton beam with a co-propagating electron beam to transfer thermal energy, achieving temperature reductions from the keV to the eV regime.
The overall efficiency of antiproton production and collection in typical setups, such as at CERN, is approximately 10^{-5} antiprotons per incident proton, limited by collection angles, target interactions, and losses during cooling. Recent advancements include the ELENA decelerator at CERN, commissioned in 2018, which further slows antiprotons from 5.3 MeV to 100 keV, enhancing trapping efficiency and enabling higher-intensity low-energy beams for precision experiments.

Key Production Facilities

The Antiproton Accumulator (AA) at CERN, operational in the 1980s, marked a milestone in antiproton production by accumulating and cooling antiprotons at 3.5 GeV/c for injection into the Super Proton Synchrotron, enabling proton-antiproton collisions that powered the UA1 and UA2 experiments and led to the discovery of the W and Z bosons in 1983.
Fermilab's Tevatron, active from 1983 to 2011, featured a dedicated antiproton source that accumulated up to 10 antiprotons per store in its accumulator ring, facilitating high-luminosity collisions at 1.96 TeV center-of-mass energy and contributing to discoveries like the top quark.
At CERN, the Proton Synchrotron (PS) generates antiprotons by impacting a high-intensity proton beam at 26 GeV onto an iridium target, yielding secondary antiprotons that are stochastically cooled and transferred to the Antiproton Decelerator (AD), operational since 2000, which decelerates them to 5.3 MeV and delivers approximately 10 antiprotons per ~100-second cycle at an initial momentum of 3.5 GeV/c for downstream experiments.
The Facility for Antiproton and Ion Research (FAIR) at GSI Helmholtz Centre in Darmstadt, currently under construction, will feature a high-intensity proton linac and synchrotron to produce antiprotons at rates up to 10 per second in its Collector Ring, with further deceleration and storage in the CRYRING@ESR trap for low-energy applications, anticipating first beams around 2028.
By 2025, upgrades to CERN's AD and its ELENA extension have boosted antiproton beam intensity by over an order of magnitude through improved stochastic and electron cooling, enhancing delivery rates to support precision experiments such as BASE, which tests CPT invariance with antiprotons, and ALPHA, focused on antihydrogen spectroscopy and gravitation studies.

Experiments and Measurements

Precision Property Measurements

Precision measurements of antiproton properties, particularly its magnetic moment and charge-to-mass ratio, are conducted using Penning traps at CERN's Antiproton Decelerator to test CPT invariance, a fundamental symmetry implying that protons and antiprotons should have identical properties apart from opposite charges. These experiments trap individual antiprotons in strong magnetic fields, allowing precise determination of spin precession frequencies relative to cyclotron frequencies. Any deviation from proton values would signal CPT violation, potentially revealing new physics beyond the Standard Model.
The BASE collaboration achieved a landmark measurement of the antiproton magnetic moment in 2017, determining its value to a fractional precision of 0.8 parts per million using a multi-Penning trap system that isolates the spin-flip and cyclotron resonances. This result matched the proton's magnetic moment within experimental limits, setting stringent constraints on CPT-violating differences at the parts-per-million level. Advancing quantum control techniques, a 2025 CERN experiment demonstrated coherent manipulation of a single antiproton's spin states using radiofrequency pulses in a Penning trap, treating the spin as an "antiqubit" with superposition capabilities sustained for up to 50 seconds. This breakthrough enables higher-fidelity spectroscopy for future CPT tests, potentially reaching parts-per-trillion sensitivities in g2g-2g2 measurements.

Antihydrogen and Exotic Atom Studies

Antihydrogen, the antimatter counterpart of hydrogen, consists of an antiproton bound to a positron. The first production of antihydrogen atoms occurred in 1995 at CERN's Low Energy Antiproton Ring (LEAR), where a team led by Walter Oelert observed nine such atoms through the mixing of antiprotons and positrons in a particle beam experiment. Since 2002, antihydrogen production has become routine at CERN's Antiproton Decelerator (AD), enabling the creation of large numbers of cold antihydrogen atoms by the ATHENA and ATRAP collaborations through positron-antiproton mixing in Penning traps at cryogenic temperatures.
The ALPHA experiment at the AD has advanced the study of antihydrogen as an exotic atom by achieving the first trapping of these neutral atoms in 2010, using a magnetic bottle to confine up to 38 antihydrogen atoms for approximately 172 milliseconds. Building on this, ALPHA conducted precision spectroscopy, measuring the 1S2S transition frequency in antihydrogen and finding it consistent with that of hydrogen to a relative precision of about 210122 \times 10^{-12}21012, providing a stringent test of CPT invariance. These measurements highlight antihydrogen's potential for probing fundamental symmetries in bound-state quantum electrodynamics. In January 2025, ALPHA reported the simultaneous observation of both accessible hyperfine components of the 1S2S transition, further improving CPT constraints.
To investigate gravitational interactions, the AEgIS experiment at the AD has developed techniques for producing a pulsed beam of antihydrogen atoms, with a 2025 update incorporating high-resolution detectors based on modified smartphone sensors to track their trajectories. This setup aims to measure the Earth's gravitational acceleration on antihydrogen with 1% precision through interferometric free-fall experiments, potentially revealing any deviations from general relativity for antimatter.
The ALPHA-g apparatus, a vertical extension of the ALPHA experiment, provided the first direct observation of gravitational effects on antimatter in 2023 by releasing trapped antihydrogen atoms and detecting their vertical displacement, which was consistent with them falling downward under Earth's gravity at an acceleration of g=(0.750.130.16)g0g = (0.75 \pm 0.13 \pm 0.16) g_0g=(0.750.130.16)g0, where g0=9.81g_0 = 9.81g0=9.81 m/s. This result constrains speculative models proposing antigravity or repulsive interactions for antimatter, affirming the weak equivalence principle to within 20% uncertainty.
In heavy-ion collisions at the LHC, the ALICE collaboration detected the first evidence of antihyperhelium-4 in 2025, an exotic antimatter hypernucleus composed of two antiprotons, an antineutron, and an antihyperon (antistrange baryon). This heaviest antimatter nucleus observed to date, identified through its decay products in lead-lead collisions at 5.02 TeV, offers insights into the production mechanisms of hypernuclei in high-energy environments and validates quantum chromodynamics simulations for antimatter clusters.

Applications and Prospects

Potential Technological Uses

Antiprotons have been investigated for cancer therapy due to their potential to deliver enhanced radiation doses at the end of their range through annihilation with protons in tissue. The Antiproton Cell Experiment (ACE), conducted at CERN from 2003 to 2012, demonstrated that antiprotons are approximately four times more effective than protons at killing mammalian cells in vitro, primarily because the annihilation process releases additional energy in the form of pions that cause localized damage within the tumor. This relative biological effectiveness (RBE) arises from the Bragg peak augmentation by annihilation products, allowing fewer particles to achieve equivalent cell destruction compared to conventional proton therapy.
The energy released in antiproton-proton annihilation at rest totals approximately 1.88 GeV per event, equivalent to the combined rest masses of the particles, with the majority carried away by charged pions that deposit their energy densely in matter. About two-thirds of this energy is converted into charged pions, while one-third appears as neutral pions decaying into gamma rays, making antiprotons a highly efficient source of localized radiation for therapeutic applications.
In space propulsion, antiprotons could enable advanced engine designs by leveraging annihilation energy to achieve exhaust velocities far exceeding chemical rockets. Concepts such as proton-antiproton beam-core engines, studied by NASA since the 1990s, estimate specific impulses exceeding 10^6 seconds through the use of charged pion exhaust, potentially enabling rapid interplanetary travel. Similarly, antimatter-catalyzed microfusion systems, where antiprotons trigger fusion reactions in deuterium-helium-3 pellets, have been analyzed in NASA reports from the 2000s, projecting specific impulses around 67,000 seconds for missions to outer solar system targets.
Key challenges to realizing these applications include the exorbitant production costs and storage difficulties of antiprotons. As of the 2010s, producing one gram of antiprotons required an estimated $62.5 trillion, based on accelerator efficiencies at facilities like Fermilab and CERN. Storage relies on Penning traps, which use combined magnetic and electric fields to confine antiprotons for extended periods, up to hundreds of days, but scaling to usable quantities remains technically demanding.
By 2025, advancements in portable and miniaturized Penning traps offer promising prospects for medical beam delivery, enabling the transport of antiprotons from production sites to therapy facilities without loss, potentially facilitating clinical trials for antiproton-based radiotherapy.

Future Research Directions

The BASE experiment at CERN plans to achieve a 10-fold improvement in the precision of the antiproton magnetic moment measurement, targeting 1030 parts per trillion by the end of Long Shutdown 3 (LS3) in 20262027, through advanced double-trap coherent spectroscopy techniques that minimize systematic shifts. This enhancement will enable rigorous tests of quantum electrodynamics (QED) in strong magnetic fields by comparing the antiproton g-factor with that of the H ion at sub-parts-per-trillion levels, probing potential violations of charge-parity-time (CPT) symmetry and the structure of antimatter systems up to 2034.
The PUMA (antiProton Unstable Matter Annihilation) proposal at CERN aims to transport low-energy antiprotons from the ELENA decelerator to the ISOLDE facility to form antiprotonic atoms with short-lived radioactive nuclei, enabling precise studies of nuclear surface densities and peripheral structure. By capturing antiprotons in atomic orbits and analyzing annihilation products or X-ray emissions, PUMA will probe neutron distributions in exotic isotopes, offering insights into nuclear halo effects and reaction mechanisms not accessible with stable targets.
The GBAR and AEgIS experiments at CERN are advancing toward direct measurements of antihydrogen free-fall acceleration to test the weak equivalence principle, with GBAR targeting 1% precision by 2028 using sympathetic cooling of antihydrogen positive ions followed by photo-neutralization and classical trajectory tracking over short distances. AEgIS plans to reach 0.1% precision by 2030 via pulsed antihydrogen beams and advanced interferometry techniques like OTIMA, building on moir deflectometry for higher sensitivity after LS4.
Extensions of AMS-02 observations on the International Space Station, combined with follow-up analyses of PAMELA-era antiproton excesses, will refine dark matter searches through 2030 by improving flux measurements across a full solar cycle and constraining annihilation cross-sections in the TeV range. These efforts aim to distinguish potential dark matter signals from astrophysical backgrounds using enhanced statistical power from accumulated data.
Building on the 2025 demonstration of coherent spin control in a single antiproton for 50 secondsmarking the first antimatter qubitfuture quantum technologies will explore antiproton-based qubits for high-precision sensing, with BASE-STEP targeting 10-fold longer coherence times in reduced-noise environments to enhance antimatter property comparisons. This could enable quantum-enhanced detectors for fundamental symmetry tests beyond current Penning-trap limits.
