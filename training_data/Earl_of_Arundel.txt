 The Earldom of Arundel is the oldest surviving earldom in the Peerage of England, originally created around 1138 for William d'Aubigny, a royal butler and companion to King Henry I, whose family held Arundel Castle following its construction shortly after the Norman Conquest. The title, confirmed by King Stephen and later by Henry II in 1155 with the Honour and Castle of Arundel, became hereditary and linked to extensive lands in Sussex, embodying Norman feudal power and loyalty to the crown through generations of military service and high office. Passing to the FitzAlan family in the 13th century via inheritance and marriage, and subsequently to the Howard family in 1580, the earldom has been subsidiary to the Dukedom of Norfolk since the 17th century, with notable holders serving as Earls Marshal of England, overseeing state ceremonies and heraldry. Today, it is held by Edward Fitzalan-Howard, 18th Duke of Norfolk, with his eldest son, Henry Fitzalan-Howard, using the style Earl of Arundel as heir apparent, continuing a lineage marked by resilience amid attainders, restorations, and pivotal roles in English history from the medieval wars to modern monarchy.

Overview of the Title

Origins and Etymology

The title Earl of Arundel originates from the feudal honour of Arundel, an administrative division known as the Rape of Arundel in Sussex, England, which encompassed extensive lands centered on Arundel Castle and was granted as dower to Adeliza of Louvain, second wife of King Henry I, shortly before his death in 1135. Following Henry I's death, Adeliza married William d'Aubigny, a Norman baron and royal butler of French origin, who supported King Stephen during the Anarchy and received the earldom as a reward for loyalty, with the creation dated to 1138. The title effectively denoted comital authority over Sussex, with Arundel as the caput (chief seat), though early earls were sometimes styled Earl of Sussex or Chichester interchangeably until the association with Arundel solidified.
The place name Arundel derives from Old English hr-hne-dell, translating to "valley of horehound," where hr-hne refers to the plant Marrubium vulgare, a hoary-leaved herb common in the region, and dell denotes a small valley; this etymology is attested in Domesday Book forms like "Harundel." A folk etymology, unsubstantiated by linguistic evidence, connects the name to Old French arondel ("little swallow"), reflected in local tradition and the depiction of swallows on Arundel town arms, but this likely arose post-Conquest as a romantic interpretation rather than the Anglo-Saxon root. The honour's Norman overlordship under d'Aubigny reinforced the title's prestige, tying it causally to control of strategic coastal estates vital for defense and revenue in medieval Sussex.

Precedence and Subsidiary Status

The Earldom of Arundel occupies the position of premier earldom in the Peerage of England, created circa 1139 and recognized for its precedence by the antiquity of its tenure, particularly through possession of Arundel Castle, which was uniquely confirmed by the Crown in 1433 as conferring earldom status. This ranking places it ahead of all other surviving earldoms, such as that of Shrewsbury created in 1442, establishing its superior standing within the third rank of the peerage hierarchy, below dukes and marquesses but above viscounts and barons, with internal ordering determined by date of creation.
Since its merger with the Dukedom of Norfolk, the earldom has been held as a subsidiary title by the duke, a status formalized on 29 December 1660 when Parliament restored the dukedom to Thomas Howard, 5th Duke of Norfolk, with the earldom descending therewith and retaining precedence dating to the original creation under John Howard, 1st Duke of Norfolk in 1483. The eldest son of the Duke of Norfolk bears the courtesy title of Earl of Arundel during his father's lifetime, without formal possession of the peerage dignity until inheritance of the primary dukedom. This arrangement underscores the earldom's integral role within the Howard family's aggregated honors, where it serves both ceremonial and hereditary functions without independent succession.

Historical Creations

First Creation: d'Aubigny Line (11381243)

The earldom of Arundel was first created by King Stephen around 11381142 for William d'Aubigny, a Norman nobleman who served as pincerna (chief butler) to Henry I and demonstrated loyalty to Stephen amid the Anarchy. William, born circa 1109, acquired Arundel Castle through his marriage to Adeliza of Louvain, the widowed queen of Henry I, around 11361139; the castle formed part of her dower lands. In 1139, William briefly sheltered Empress Matilda at Arundel before reaffirming allegiance to Stephen, who rewarded him with the earldom, sometimes styled as Earl of Sussex or Chichester due to the castle's location. He also held the earldom of Lincoln briefly from 1139 until its reassignment in 1141.
William d'Aubigny, 1st Earl of Arundel, died on 12 October 1176 and was buried at Wymondham Priory, which he had founded. He and Adeliza had at least eight children, including William, who succeeded as 2nd Earl. The 2nd Earl William (died 24 December 1193) married Matilda de Saint-Hilaire after 1173, producing two sons: William and Hugh. He remained a prominent royal servant under Henry II, retaining influence despite the dynastic shift from Stephen's line.
The 3rd Earl William (died before 30 March 1221) succeeded his father and married Mabel, daughter of Hugh of Chester, earl of Chester, with whom he had six children, including sons William and Hugh. He participated in the Fifth Crusade, dying during the campaign near Acre. His elder son, William, 4th Earl (died before 7 August 1224), briefly held the title without recorded marriage or issue before succumbing young.
Hugh d'Aubigny, 5th and final Earl of this creation (died 7 May 1243), succeeded his brother in 1224. Born circa 1215, he married Isabel de Warenne, daughter of Earl Warenne, in 1234 but produced no children. Upon Hugh's death without legitimate male heirs, the earldom reverted to the Crown, while Arundel Castle and associated estates passed to his nephew John FitzAlan through inheritance from Hugh's sisters. The title remained dormant until its recreation in 1289 for the FitzAlan line.

Second Creation: FitzAlan Line (12891580)

The second creation of the Earldom of Arundel commenced in 1289, when Richard FitzAlan (c. 12671302), son and heir of John FitzAlan, lord of the honour of Arundel, attained his majority and received a summons to Parliament directed to "Richard de Arundel" on 29 December 1289, thereby assuming the comital dignity associated with the family's longstanding holdings at Arundel Castle, inherited in 1243 following the extinction of the d'Aubigny male line. This writ marked the formal revival of the title, distinct from the earlier creation, as the FitzAlans had previously held only baronial rank despite possession of the castle and honour. Richard participated in Edward I's campaigns in Wales and Scotland, including the siege of Carlaverock in 1300, and died on 9 March 1302, leaving the earldom to his son.
Edmund FitzAlan, 2nd Earl (1 May 128517 November 1326), succeeded his father but aligned with Edward II against the Despenser faction and Isabella of France's invasion. Captured near Shrewsbury in 1326, he was beheaded without trial at Hereford on 17 November 1326 at the instigation of Roger Mortimer, with his estates forfeited; chronicles report the execution required 22 blows from a blunt sword. The earldom was restored in 1330 to his son, Richard FitzAlan, 3rd Earl (c. 131324 January 1376), who had been disinherited during the forfeiture. This Richard, through marriage to Eleanor de Lancaster (after annulment of his first union to Isabel Despenser), acquired the earldom of Surrey and extensive lands; he commanded the second division at the Battle of Crcy in 1346, fought at Poitiers in 1356, and served multiple terms as Admiral of the West, contributing to English naval dominance in the Hundred Years' War. His death in 1376 passed the titles intact to his son.
Richard FitzAlan, 4th Earl (134621 September 1397), inherited young and initially supported the Lords Appellant against Richard II's favorites, leading to temporary forfeiture in 1388 before restoration. He later reconciled with the king but was arrested in 1397 for alleged treason, tried by parliamentary process, and beheaded on Tower Hill, with his estates again attainted until Henry IV's accession in 1399 restored them to his son Thomas FitzAlan, 5th Earl (13 October 138113 October 1415). Thomas, a ward of the crown under Henry IV, fought at Agincourt in 1415 as a key commander and died shortly thereafter from disease contracted during the campaign.
The succession continued through minors and guardians amid the Wars of the Roses, with John FitzAlan, 6th Earl (14 February 140812 June 1435), dying young; Humphrey FitzAlan, 7th Earl (30 January 142924 April 1438), succeeding as an infant and predeceasing early; and William FitzAlan, 8th Earl (c. 14171487), who supported the Yorkist cause, serving as Treasurer of England from 1462 to 1466 and Lord Treasurer again under Richard III. William's son, Thomas FitzAlan, 9th Earl (145025 March 1524), remained loyal to Henry VII after Bosworth, while grandson William FitzAlan, 10th Earl (c. 147623 January 1544), held high offices including Lord Chamberlain and supported Henry VIII's regime. The line ended with Henry FitzAlan, 11th Earl (23 April 151224 February 1580), a Tudor courtier who navigated religious changes, served as Lord Chamberlain, and amassed wealth through offices and marriages, but died without surviving sons; his daughter Mary wed Thomas Howard, transferring the earldom via special remainder to the Howard line upon Philip Howard's creation in 1580.

Third Creation: Howard Line (1580present)

The Earldom of Arundel passed to the Howard family upon the death of Henry FitzAlan, 12th Earl of Arundel, on 24 February 1580, as his only daughter Mary FitzAlan's son, Philip Howard, succeeded as the 13th Earl through genealogical succession despite the title's prior association with the FitzAlan line. Born on 28 June 1557, Philip Howard, grandson of the deceased earl and son of the executed Thomas Howard, 4th Duke of Norfolk, inherited the title amid religious tensions, having converted to Catholicism around 1583.
Philip Howard's tenure was marked by imprisonment; arrested in 1585 for suspected involvement in Catholic plots and high treason against Elizabeth I, he was confined in the Tower of London, fined 10,000, and sentenced to death, though the execution was not carried out. He died there on 19 October 1595, officially from natural causes but with contemporary suspicions of poisoning, leading to the title's attainder. In recognition of his steadfast faith, Howard was beatified in 1929 and canonized by Pope Paul VI in 1970 as one of the Forty Martyrs of England and Wales.
The title remained attainted until 18 April 1604, when King James I restored it to Philip's son, Thomas Howard (born 7 July 1585, died 4 October 1646), who became the 14th Earl of Arundel and also held the Earldom of Surrey. Thomas, a prominent art collector and diplomat, was appointed to the Privy Council and Knight of the Garter in 1611; he later received the Earldom of Norfolk in 1644, linking the Arundel title more firmly to the Howard ducal line. Upon his death without surviving legitimate male issue, the earldom passed to his grandson Thomas Howard, 5th Duke of Norfolk (16271677), establishing it as a subsidiary title of the Dukedom of Norfolk, the premier non-royal dukedom in England.
Since 1677, the Earldom of Arundel has been consistently held by successive Dukes of Norfolk, with the Howard (later Fitzalan-Howard) family navigating periods of Catholic recusancy and political challenges while maintaining hereditary precedence as England's premier earldom. The title's continuity reflects the family's strategic marriages and legal restorations, avoiding further attainders after the 17th century. The current holder is Edward William Fitzalan-Howard, 18th Duke of Norfolk (born 2 December 1956), who succeeded his father in 2002 and serves as Earl Marshal, overseeing state ceremonies including coronations. His heir apparent is Henry Miles Fitzalan-Howard (born 3 December 1987), styled Earl of Arundel.

Associated Seat and Estates

Arundel Castle

Arundel Castle, situated in Arundel, West Sussex, England, originated as a motte-and-bailey fortification established on Christmas Day 1067 by Roger de Montgomery, 1st Earl of Arundel, a key ally of William the Conqueror who received the Honour of Arundel as a reward for his support at the Battle of Hastings. The motte, an artificial earthen mound exceeding 100 feet in height, remains the castle's oldest feature, while early stone elements, including parts of the shell keep, were added by King Henry II around 1155 during his campaigns to fortify royal holdings.
After Roger de Montgomery's death in 1094, the castle passed to his sons as 2nd and 3rd Earls before escheating to the Crown under Henry I, who granted it to William d'Aubigny (c. 11001176), elevating him to Earl of Arundel in 1138 and linking the title directly to possession of the castle and its honour. The d'Aubigny (Albini) line held it until 1243, when John FitzAlan (c. 12231267) acquired the property through his marriage to Isabel d'Aubigny, sole heiress of Hugh d'Aubigny, 5th Earl, initiating over three centuries of FitzAlan stewardship that solidified the castle as the principal seat of the Earls of Arundel following the title's recreation in their favor in 1289.
The FitzAlans expanded the medieval structure, with Richard FitzAlan, 8th Earl (13061376), initiating significant rebuilding in the 14th century, including the great hall and chapel, though the castle endured sieges during the Wars of the Roses. Ownership transferred to the Howard family in 1580 via Philip Howard (15571595), whose mother Mary FitzAlan was the daughter and heiress of Henry FitzAlan, 12th Earl; Philip, restored as 13th Earl posthumously, merged the Arundel estates with the Dukedom of Norfolk, which the Howards have held since 1483, with the Earldom as a subsidiary title.
Slighted and partially demolished by Parliamentary forces during the English Civil War in 1645 after surrendering to avoid total destruction, the castle lay in ruins for two centuries until restorations began under Charles Howard, 11th Duke of Norfolk (17461815), who rebuilt the gatehouse and domestic quarters in Gothic Revival style from 1787. The most extensive phase occurred under Henry Fitzalan-Howard, 15th Duke (18471917), who from 1875 to 1900 transformed it into a Victorian stately home with modern amenities like electricity, while preserving medieval elements such as the Norman keep, gatehouse, and barbican.
Today, Arundel Castle serves as the ancestral seat of the Dukes of Norfolk, currently Edward Fitzalan-Howard, 18th Duke (born 1956), who resides there with his family while maintaining it as a Grade I listed historic house open to the public, encompassing 40 acres of gardens and hosting events tied to the Earl Marshal's ceremonial duties.

Other Holdings and Developments

The Fitzalan-Howard family maintains the Angmering Park Estate in West Sussex, comprising approximately 6,750 acres of farmland, woodland, and parkland as part of their broader Sussex holdings. This estate, originally linked to the historic Norfolk properties in the region, supports agricultural activities and conservation efforts, including initiatives to restore populations of farmland birds through habitat management. Together with adjacent lands, the Arundel and Angmering estates total around 16,000 acres across the South Downs, emphasizing sustainable farming and public access trails.
In East Anglia, the family oversees the Norfolk Estate, centered on agricultural production via Norfolk Estate Farms Ltd, which manages arable and pastoral operations on retained lands including areas near Thetford and the hinterland of Great Yarmouth. These holdings trace back to early Howard inheritances but have been streamlined over centuries, with focus shifting from medieval sites like Bungay Castle to modern farming efficiencies.
The Sheffield estate in South Yorkshire represented a significant historical diversification, where from around 1800, estate agents under the Dukes of Norfolk leased land strategically to steel and cutlery manufacturers, influencing urban industrial growth through long-term lease policies that prioritized manufacturing clusters. This development transformed common lands into productive sites, though much of the estate was disposed of or transferred to local councils by the 1950s amid post-war nationalization and urban expansion.
Contemporary management includes Fitzalan Estates Ltd, which handles industrial and commercial properties derived from core holdings, alongside Arundel Services Ltd for estate administration, indicating a shift toward diversified revenue from leasing and development rather than solely agrarian pursuits. These entities support ongoing maintenance and adaptation of assets, including recent office consolidations in Sheffield to address operational efficiencies.

Notable Earls and Contributions

Medieval and Early Modern Earls

William d'Aubigny, 1st Earl of Arundel (c. 11091176), received the earldom around 1138 from King Stephen in recognition of his military support during the Anarchy, a civil war contesting the English throne between Stephen and Empress Matilda. As royal butler, he managed the king's household logistics, a position that enhanced his influence at court under both Stephen and subsequent King Henry II. In 1138, he married Adeliza of Louvain, the widowed second queen consort of Henry I, securing a politically advantageous alliance that bolstered his status and lands, including the custody of Arundel Castle. His loyalty to Stephen extended to commanding forces at key battles, contributing to the stabilization of Stephen's rule despite the conflict's inconclusive outcome.
Successive d'Aubigny earls maintained the title until Hugh d'Aubigny, 4th Earl (d. 1243), who died without male heirs, leading to the earldom's escheat to the crown. The FitzAlan family acquired Arundel Castle in 1244 through John FitzAlan's marriage to Isabel d'Aubigny, Hugh's sister and co-heiress, integrating the estate into their Shropshire-based holdings. The earldom was recreated in 1289 for Richard FitzAlan, 1st Earl of Arundel in the second creation (styled as 8th Earl in some genealogies due to continuity claims), who served Edward I in campaigns against Wales and Scotland, dying in 1302. His son, Edmund FitzAlan, 2nd Earl (12851326), opposed the Despensers' influence under Edward II, resulting in his execution by hanging at Hereford in 1326 amid the baronial revolt that facilitated Isabella and Mortimer's regency.
Richard FitzAlan, 3rd Earl (c. 13131376), emerged as a pivotal figure in Edward III's reign, amassing wealth estimated at 1,900 in coffers from northern March lordships like Clun and Oswestry. Appointed Admiral of the North in 1340 and Justice of North Wales in 1344, he led naval operations and captured Berwick-upon-Tweed in 1345 during the Second War of Scottish Independence, demonstrating tactical acumen in amphibious assaults. His second marriage to Eleanor of Lancaster in 1345 allied him with the royal family, yielding significant dowry lands and influence, though he navigated tensions with the crown over war profits and appointments. Richard's son, another Richard FitzAlan, 4th Earl (13461397), opposed Richard II's favorites, joining the Lords Appellant in 1388 to impeach and execute several royal counselors, but was himself tried and beheaded in 1397 for treason during the king's revenge.
In the early modern period, Henry FitzAlan, 12th Earl (15121580), the last FitzAlan holder, navigated Tudor politics as a conservative peer, serving as Lord Chamberlain under Mary I and briefly under Elizabeth I while amassing estates through strategic marriages. His daughter Mary married Thomas Howard, Duke of Norfolk, transferring the earldom to the Howard line upon Henry's death without surviving male heirs. Philip Howard, 13th Earl (15571595), inherited amid religious strife, imprisoned in the Tower of London from 1585 for suspected Catholic recusancy and alleged involvement in plots against Elizabeth I, dying there after ten years; he was later beatified by the Catholic Church in 1929 for his faith. Thomas Howard, 14th Earl (15851646), known as the "Collector Earl," amassed one of Europe's premier art collections, including classical sculptures and paintings, patronizing artists like Rubens and van Dyck while serving as ambassador to the Habsburgs under James I. His diplomatic efforts and cultural acquisitions elevated the earldom's prestige, though his Catholic sympathies led to periodic royal disfavor.

Tudor and Stuart Era Figures

Henry Fitzalan, 12th Earl of Arundel (15121580), served as a key courtier across multiple Tudor monarchs, holding positions such as Lord Chamberlain from 1546 and Lord High Steward during Mary I's coronation in 1553. Despite his Catholic sympathies, he navigated Elizabeth I's accession by pledging loyalty while maintaining influence, though suspicions of conspiracy led to his temporary house arrest in 1568. His marriage to Mary Arundell in 1545 produced a daughter, Mary, whose union with Thomas Howard, 4th Duke of Norfolk, linked the FitzAlan estates to the Howard family, facilitating the title's transition upon FitzAlan's death on 24 February 1580.
Philip Howard, 13th Earl of Arundel (15571595), inherited the title in 1580 as grandson of Henry FitzAlan through his mother, Mary, following the execution of his father, Thomas Howard, 4th Duke of Norfolk, for treason in 1572. A convert to Catholicism in the early 1580s, Howard faced imprisonment in the Tower of London from 1585 onward for departing England without royal permission and engaging in recusant practices, including hearing Mass; he was attainted by Parliament in 1589 on charges tied to perceived Catholic plotting, though evidence centered on religious nonconformity rather than direct sedition. He died in the Tower on 19 October 1595 after a decade of confinement, with his son Thomas later restored to the peerage in 1604.
Thomas Howard, 14th Earl of Arundel (15851646), son of Philip Howard, emerged as a leading Stuart-era patron of the arts, amassing over 700 paintings, numerous sculptures, and antiquities through European tours beginning in the 1610s, earning him the moniker "the Collector Earl." Politically active under James I and Charles I, he served as ambassador to the Habsburg court in 1636 and influenced royal collecting, though his Catholic family ties and diplomatic failures strained relations with Parliament during the 1640s lead-up to civil war. Married to Aletheia Talbot in 1606, Howard's acquisitions, including marbles from Italy, laid foundations for later British collections, but financial strains from his patronage contributed to the dispersal of much of his holdings after his death on 4 October 1646 in Padua.
Henry Frederick Howard, 15th Earl of Arundel (16081652), succeeded his father Thomas in 1646 amid the English Civil War, inheriting depleted estates but continuing the family's artistic legacy through oversight of remaining collections. As a proprietor of colonial ventures like Carolana in the 1640s, he sought economic recovery, though royalist sympathies limited his influence under the Commonwealth; he died unmarried on 17 April 1652, passing the title to his brother William Howard, Viscount Stafford, whose attainder in 1680 for alleged Popish Plot involvement temporarily disrupted the line until restoration.

Modern Holders and Achievements

Miles Francis Stapleton Fitzalan-Howard, 17th Duke of Norfolk and holder of the Earldom of Arundel, demonstrated notable military service during World War II, earning the Military Cross in April 1944 for reconnaissance under fire across mined roads during the Battle of the Sangro in Italy while serving with the Grenadier Guards. He participated in the D-Day landings and advanced to the rank of major-general, later serving as chief of the British mission to Soviet forces in Germany from 1957. As Earl Marshal from 1979 until his death in 2002, he oversaw ceremonial state events, continuing the Howard family's hereditary role in such capacities.
His successor, Edward William Fitzalan-Howard, 18th Duke of Norfolk and current Earl of Arundel since 2002, has upheld the office of Earl Marshal, coordinating the state funeral of Queen Elizabeth II on 19 September 2022 and the coronation of King Charles III on 6 May 2023 at Westminster Abbey. These efforts earned him the Freedom of the Town of Arundel in recognition of his contributions to national ceremonial life. Under his custodianship, Arundel Castle remains a publicly accessible historic site, supporting tourism and preservation of the family's ancestral seat in West Sussex.
The heir apparent, Henry Miles Fitzalan-Howard (born 3 December 1987), bears the courtesy title Earl of Arundel and engages in business endeavors, including operating an insurance firm from offices in Arundel after relocating his family to the castle in 2023. Previously a motor racing driver, he represents the continuation of the Howard lineage's involvement in contemporary economic activities while preparing for potential future responsibilities in peerage and estate management.

Family Lineage and Succession

Key Lineages and Marriages

The FitzAlan lineage originated from Alan fitz Flaad, a Breton settler in England under Henry I, whose son William fitz Alan became lord of Oswestry and Clun in Shropshire; descendants acquired Arundel Castle and associated honors through John FitzAlan's tenure as lord in the mid-13th century, leading to the formal second creation of the earldom for his son Richard FitzAlan in 1289. The family maintained the title through male succession for nearly three centuries, with intermarriages to houses like WarenneEdmund FitzAlan, 9th Earl (12851326), wed Alice de Warenne, daughter of the Earl of Surrey, securing wardship and alliances under Edward II.
A pivotal marriage linking the FitzAlans to the Howards was that of Mary FitzAlan (15411557), daughter of Henry FitzAlan, 12th Earl (15121580), to Thomas Howard, 4th Duke of Norfolk (15381572), in December 1555; Mary's dowry included prospective Arundel estates, and following her brother Henry's death without issue in 1556, she became the principal heiress, passing claims to her son Philip Howard (15571595), who inherited the earldom upon his grandfather's death in 1580. This union transferred the ancient FitzAlan holdings to the Howard family, already elevated as Dukes of Norfolk since 1483, despite Philip's subsequent attainder for recusancy in 1585.
The Howard lineage continued the earldom after restoration, with Philip's son Thomas Howard (15851646) confirmed as 14th Earl in 1604; a key marriage for the Howards was Thomas's union with Alethea Talbot (15851654), daughter and heiress of Gilbert Talbot, 7th Earl of Shrewsbury, on 25 September 1606, which augmented estates with northern properties and Shrewsbury wealth, bolstering the family's position amid Catholic sympathies and royal favor under James I. Subsequent holders adopted the hyphenated Fitzalan-Howard surname by the 18th century to preserve the Arundel maternal heritage, as seen in Bernard Howard's change confirmed in 1755 and perpetuated in ducal titles. These alliances underscored the earldom's role in consolidating marcher lordships, royal kinship, and vast landholdings across England.

Family Tree Summary

The Howard descent of the Earldom of Arundel traces to Philip Howard (15571595), summoned to Parliament as Baron Maltravers in 1580 and inheriting the earldom through his paternal grandmother's connection to the FitzAlans via his father's marriage to Mary FitzAlan (d. 1557), only surviving child of Henry FitzAlan, 12th Earl of Arundel (15121580). Philip, son of Thomas Howard, 4th Duke of Norfolk (15381572), and Margaret Audley, succeeded amid the failure of the FitzAlan male line. His sole surviving son, Thomas Howard (15851646), acceded as 14th Earl, acquiring significant Continental art and antiquities that formed the basis of the Arundel Marbles collection.
Thomas's second son, Henry Frederick Howard (16081652), succeeded as 15th Earl in 1646, holding also the Earldoms of Surrey and Norfolk; he produced no legitimate issue, leading the title's passage upon his death to his younger brother, Thomas Howard (16271677), 5th Duke of Norfolk, thereby uniting the earldom with the dukedom in the senior Howard line. Role in English and British History

Military and Political Influence

The FitzAlan earls played significant roles in medieval military campaigns, particularly during the Hundred Years' War under Edward III. Richard FitzAlan, 3rd Earl of Arundel (13131376), served as Admiral of the West and participated in naval engagements, including the Battle of Sluys in 1340, where English forces defeated a French fleet, securing control over the Channel. His successor, Richard FitzAlan (c. 13461397), known as the 4th or 11th Earl depending on numbering, commanded a fleet in 1387 that achieved a decisive victory off Margate against a larger Franco-Flemish convoy, capturing ships and disrupting enemy supply lines despite numerical inferiority. These actions demonstrated the earls' strategic importance in maritime warfare, contributing to England's defensive posture against French incursions.
In the Wars of the Roses, the title's alignment with the Yorkist cause amplified its military prominence. John Howard, elevated to Duke of Norfolk but holding the Arundel inheritance through marriage, commanded the vanguard at the Battle of Bosworth Field on 22 August 1485, where he was killed fighting for Richard III against Henry Tudor's forces, marking a pivotal defeat for Yorkists. His son, Thomas Howard (later 2nd Duke of Norfolk), continued this tradition by leading English forces to victory at the Battle of Flodden on 9 September 1513 as Earl of Surrey, routing a Scottish invasion army and killing King James IV, which secured the northern border and bolstered Tudor authority. These engagements underscored the earls' role in decisive land battles, often at the vanguard, leveraging their regional levies from Sussex and East Anglia.
Politically, the earls exerted influence through high offices and local governance, particularly after the Howards assumed the title in the late 16th century via inheritance. As hereditary Earls Marshal a position formalized for the Howards in 1672 but rooted in earlier marshalcy dutiesthe holders oversaw state ceremonies, heraldry, and arrangements for coronations and funerals, granting leverage in royal symbolism and court protocol. Henry FitzAlan, 12th Earl (15121580), navigated Tudor politics by supporting Mary I's accession in 1553 and serving as Lord Chamberlain from 1546, influencing privy council decisions amid religious shifts, though his Catholic leanings later curtailed favor under Elizabeth I. Earlier FitzAlans wielded comital authority in Sussex, shaping local administration and musters from 1330 to 1360, which extended to national councils. In the 20th century, Miles FitzAlan-Howard, 17th Duke of Norfolk and Earl of Arundel (19152002), rose to major-general in the British Army, commanding during World War II and later missions, while fulfilling Earl Marshal duties for state events. This blend of ceremonial and advisory roles sustained the title's enduring political weight, often intersecting with monarchical transitions and national defense.

Religious and Cultural Dimensions

The Earls of Arundel, particularly following the transition to the Howard family in the late 16th century, exemplified steadfast adherence to Roman Catholicism during England's post-Reformation era, often at great personal cost. Philip Howard, who succeeded as the 13th Earl in 1580, initially conformed to the Church of England but converted to Catholicism in September 1584 amid growing religious tensions under Queen Elizabeth I. His open practice of the faith led to imprisonment in the Tower of London from 1585 until his death on October 19, 1595, officially attributed to dysentery but suspected by contemporaries of poisoning due to his recusancy. Howard's imprisonment stemmed from charges including hearing Mass and attempting to flee England, reflecting the broader penal laws against Catholics; he was beatified in 1929 and canonized by Pope Paul VI on October 25, 1970, as one of the Forty Martyrs of England and Wales for dying in odium fidei. This martyrdom underscored the family's resistance to state-imposed Protestantism, with subsequent Howards facing attainders and recusancy fines for maintaining Catholic chapels and priests.
Earlier, Henry Fitzalan, the 12th Earl (15111580), navigated Tudor religious shifts with outward conformity but retained Catholic sympathies, supporting Queen Mary I's restoration of Catholicism in 1553 and amassing liturgical manuscripts that preserved pre-Reformation traditions. His library at Nonsuch Palace included over 300 volumes of religious texts, many acquired in the 1560s, which later influenced Catholic intellectual continuity through inheritance to John Lumley and the Arundel collections. The Howard Earls' recusancy persisted into the 17th century, contributing to the title's intermittent forfeiture, yet the family's Catholic identity endured, enabling their role as hereditary Earls Marshal in arranging state ceremonies despite Emancipation Act restrictions until 1829.
Culturally, the Earls advanced Renaissance humanism and artistic patronage, bridging religious heritage with secular collecting. Henry Fitzalan's library formed the nucleus of one of England's earliest major private collections, encompassing illuminated manuscripts and classical works that sustained scholarly access to Catholic-era scholarship amid iconoclastic destruction. His daughter Mary inherited and expanded it, passing elements to the Howards. Thomas Howard, 14th Earl (15851646), dubbed the "Collector Earl," elevated this tradition through grand tours in Italy and the Low Countries, acquiring over 200 ancient marbles, busts, and inscriptionsknown as the Arundel Marblesthat introduced systematic antiquarianism to England and formed the basis for the British Museum's classical holdings after dispersal in 1667. Howard patronized artists like Peter Paul Rubens and Anthony van Dyck, commissioning portraits and fostering Flemish influences that shaped Stuart court aesthetics, while his wife Alathea's acquisitions included Venetian paintings, emphasizing connoisseurship over mere accumulation. These efforts positioned the Earls as cultural intermediaries, preserving artifacts that countered Puritan iconoclasm and informed neoclassical revival.

Attainders, Restorations, and Disputes

Major Attainders and Executions

Edmund FitzAlan, 9th Earl of Arundel, faced attainder and execution on 17 November 1326 for his steadfast loyalty to King Edward II amid the invasion led by Queen Isabella and Roger Mortimer. Captured after the king's deposition, he was summarily tried and beheaded at Hereford without the formalities of a full parliamentary process, resulting in the immediate forfeiture of his estates and titles to the crown. His execution exemplified the rapid purges of Edward II's supporters, with his head displayed publicly to deter opposition.
Richard FitzAlan, 11th Earl of Arundel, was attainted by Parliament on 21 September 1397 during King Richard II's purge of the Lords Appellant, who had previously challenged royal authority in 1388. Accused of treason for opposing the king's policies, including the peace with France, FitzAlan was condemned without defense and beheaded the same day on Cheapside in London, his body quartered and displayed as a warning. The attainder stripped his son Thomas of inheritance, though partial restoration occurred under Henry IV in 1399, reflecting the title's vulnerability to monarchical retribution. FitzAlan's defiance stemmed from longstanding grievances over royal favoritism toward figures like Robert de Vere, underscoring the earl's role in restraining perceived abuses of power.
Philip Howard, 13th Earl of Arundel, inherited the title in 1580 but encountered attainder in 1585 amid Elizabethan suspicions of Catholic recusancy and potential treason. Imprisoned in the Tower of London from that year, he was convicted in 1589 for allegedly praying for the Spanish Armada's success and maintaining covert Catholic practices, leading to a death sentence commuted to lifelong confinement. Howard died in captivity on 19 October 1595, officially of dysentery, though contemporaries alleged neglect or poisoning; his attainder barred his son Thomas from immediate succession until reversal in 1604 under James I. This case highlighted the intersection of religious conformity and noble privilege, with Howard's Howard family ties amplifying scrutiny despite his nominal Protestant upbringing.
These incidents, spanning medieval depositions to Tudor religious conflicts, repeatedly disrupted the Arundel lineage, with attainders often reversed upon regime changes to legitimize new rulers' claims to continuity. No further major attainders or executions affected holders after the 16th century, as the title's integration into the Howard ducal line stabilized its fortunes.

Succession Claims and Legal Resolutions

The execution of Edmund FitzAlan, 9th Earl of Arundel, on 17 November 1326 for his support of Hugh Despenser the Younger resulted in his attainder, with the earldom temporarily granted to Edmund of Woodstock, Earl of Kent. Following Kent's own execution in March 1330, Richard FitzAlan, Edmund's son, petitioned for reinstatement later that year; he was restored in blood and to the earldom, along with most estates, by early 1331 through royal grant under Edward III.
Richard FitzAlan's son, Richard, 11th Earl of Arundel, was executed on 21 September 1397 amid Richard II's purge of opposition, leading to attainder and forfeiture of titles and lands. His grandson Thomas FitzAlan, born in 1381 and previously imprisoned, succeeded as heir but faced delayed recognition until Henry IV's accession; the attainder was reversed by late 1399 or early 1400, restoring Thomas to the earldoms of Arundel and Surrey, along with associated honors and estates.
In the Tudor era, Philip Howard inherited the earldom in 1580 as grandson of Henry FitzAlan, 12th Earl, but his recusant Catholicism led to imprisonment in 1585 and trial for treason; on 14 April 1589, he was attainted, his titles forfeited, and sentenced to death, though commuted to lifelong confinement in the Tower of London, where he died on 19 October 1595. His son, Thomas Howard, born in 1585, benefited from the Stuart accession; on 18 April 1604, James I restored him in blood and recreated the earldoms of Arundel and Surrey, returning select family estates while others passed to relatives. These restorations, granted via royal prerogative without parliamentary contest, resolved claims by affirming male-line descent post-attainder, solidifying the title's continuity under the Howard dukes of Norfolk thereafter.

Current Status and Legacy

Present Holder and Heir

The Earldom of Arundel is held by Edward William Fitzalan-Howard, 18th Duke of Norfolk (born 2 December 1956), who succeeded to the title as a subsidiary of the dukedom upon the death of his father, Miles Francis Stapleton Fitzalan-Howard, 17th Duke of Norfolk, in 2002. As the premier duke in the Peerage of England and hereditary Earl Marshal, the duke oversees state ceremonies, including the 2023 coronation of King Charles III.
The heir apparent is the duke's eldest son, Henry Miles Fitzalan-Howard (born 3 December 1987), who uses the courtesy title Earl of Arundel. Henry, who resides at Arundel Castle with his family, married Cecilia Mary Elizabeth del Mar Aylmer on 10 June 2016; the couple has three daughters born between 2018 and 2024. In the event of Henry's death without male issue, the succession would pass to his younger brothers, Lord Thomas Fitzalan-Howard (born 1989) or Lord Philip Fitzalan-Howard (born 1996), per the patent of creation limited to heirs male.

Contemporary Role and Preservation Efforts

Henry Miles Fitzalan-Howard, born 3 December 1987, holds the courtesy title of Earl of Arundel as heir apparent to his father, Edward Fitzalan-Howard, 18th Duke of Norfolk. In contemporary Britain, the title carries ceremonial significance within the peerage, reflecting the Howard family's longstanding position as leading Catholic nobility and hereditary Earls Marshal of England, though the Earl Marshal office is vested in the Duke. The Earl resides at Arundel Castle, the family's principal seat since the 11th century, where he manages aspects of estate operations alongside his role as Managing Director of Noble Insurance Group, a firm specializing in high-net-worth insurance. 
Preservation efforts at Arundel Castle emphasize maintaining its medieval origins alongside Victorian Gothic restorations initiated by the 11th Duke of Norfolk in 1791 and substantially completed by the 15th Duke in 1900 following a major fire in 1895. The current Duke and his family have continued these works, including structural repairs and enhancements to gardens and interiors, while opening the castle and its 40-acre gardens to over 100,000 public visitors annually from April to October, generating funds for upkeep through admissions, events, and tours. These initiatives preserve architectural features like the 11th-century motte, 13th-century barbican, and Howard family art collections, ensuring the site's role as a living heritage asset amid modern tourism demands. The Earl's involvement supports this stewardship, aligning with the family's commitment to safeguarding the castle's historical integrity against weathering and usage wear.
