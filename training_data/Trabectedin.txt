Trabectedin
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Trabectedin, marketed under the brand name Yondelis, is a marine-derived alkylating antineoplastic agent. It is approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of unresectable or metastatic liposarcoma or leiomyosarcoma (subtypes of soft tissue sarcoma) that received prior anthracycline-based chemotherapy. The European Medicines Agency (EMA) has approved it for advanced soft tissue sarcoma and, in combination with pegylated liposomal doxorubicin, for relapsed platinum-sensitive ovarian cancer. It functions by binding covalently to the minor groove of DNA, particularly at guanine residues, forming adducts that bend and unwind the DNA helix, thereby interfering with DNA repair, transcription, and cell cycle progression to induce cancer cell death. Originally isolated from the Caribbean sea squirt Ecteinascidia turbinata, trabectedin represents an early approved marine-derived natural product for cancer therapy, with its complex structure produced semisynthetically for clinical use.
Trabectedin's development began in the 1990s from marine natural products research, leading to EMA approval in 2007 for advanced soft tissue sarcoma (expanded in 2009 for ovarian cancer in combination with pegylated liposomal doxorubicin) and FDA approval in 2015 specifically for unresectable or metastatic liposarcoma or leiomyosarcoma following anthracycline-based chemotherapy. Its unique mechanism distinguishes it from traditional alkylating agents, as it not only damages DNA but also modulates tumor microenvironment factors, such as reducing inflammatory cytokines and inhibiting monocyte-to-macrophage differentiation in the tumor stroma.
Clinically, trabectedin is administered intravenously as a 24-hour infusion at a dose of 1.5 mg/m every three weeks, typically via a central venous catheter, with premedication using dexamethasone to mitigate nausea and hepatotoxicity. Dose adjustments are required for hepatic impairment or severe neutropenia and thrombocytopenia. Common adverse effects include myelosuppression (e.g., neutropenia in up to 50% of patients), fatigue, nausea, vomiting, and elevated liver enzymes, with serious risks encompassing rhabdomyolysis, cardiomyopathy, and capillary leak syndrome necessitating regular monitoring of blood counts, creatine phosphokinase, liver function tests, and cardiac ejection fraction. Trabectedin is metabolized primarily by CYP3A4, leading to potential interactions with strong inhibitors or inducers of this enzyme, and it carries warnings for embryo-fetal toxicity, requiring contraception during and after treatment.

Medical uses

Soft tissue sarcoma

Trabectedin received approval from the European Medicines Agency (EMA) in 2007 for the treatment of adult patients with advanced soft tissue sarcoma after failure of anthracyclines and ifosfamide, or for those unsuitable for these agents, with efficacy data primarily supporting its use in liposarcoma and leiomyosarcoma subtypes. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved trabectedin in 2015 specifically for unresectable or metastatic liposarcoma or leiomyosarcoma that has progressed following prior anthracycline-containing chemotherapy. This approval was based on the phase III ET743-SAR-3007 trial, a randomized, open-label study comparing trabectedin to dacarbazine in 518 patients with advanced liposarcoma or leiomyosarcoma; the trial demonstrated a significant progression-free survival (PFS) benefit with trabectedin, with a median PFS of 4.2 months versus 1.5 months for dacarbazine (hazard ratio 0.55; 95% confidence interval 0.44-0.70; P < 0.001).
The standard dosing regimen for trabectedin in soft tissue sarcoma is 1.5 mg/m administered as an intravenous infusion over 24 hours, repeated every 3 weeks, with premedication using dexamethasone to mitigate hepatic toxicity. Efficacy in this setting includes an overall response rate of approximately 10% and a disease control rate (complete response + partial response + stable disease) of 51-60%, with particularly notable disease stabilization in liposarcoma subtypes where rates can reach up to 70% in translocation-related cases such as myxoid liposarcoma. These outcomes reflect trabectedin's role in providing prolonged disease control through DNA damage in sarcoma cells, rather than high rates of tumor shrinkage.
In frontline settings, a 2024 phase III trial (LMS-04) evaluated trabectedin combined with doxorubicin followed by trabectedin maintenance versus doxorubicin alone in 150 patients with advanced leiomyosarcoma, showing improved overall survival with the combination (median 33 months versus 24 months; HR 0.65; 95% CI 0.44-0.95). This regimen also extended median PFS to 12 months compared to 6 months with doxorubicin monotherapy (HR 0.37; 95% CI 0.26-0.53), establishing trabectedin as a valuable component in combination therapy for specific sarcoma subtypes.

Ovarian cancer

Trabectedin, in combination with pegylated liposomal doxorubicin (PLD), received approval from the European Medicines Agency (EMA) in 2009 for the treatment of relapsed platinum-sensitive ovarian cancer in patients who have failed prior platinum- and taxane-based chemotherapy. This indication targets patients with a platinum-free interval of at least 6 months, addressing a critical need in second-line therapy for this disease, where options are limited after initial treatment failure.
The approval was supported by the OVA-301 phase III randomized trial, which enrolled 672 patients with recurrent ovarian cancer and compared the combination of trabectedin plus PLD against PLD monotherapy. In this study, the combination demonstrated a significant progression-free survival (PFS) benefit, with a median PFS of 7.3 months compared to 5.8 months for PLD alone (hazard ratio 0.79; 95% CI, 0.68-0.93; P=0.0050). Additionally, the objective response rate (ORR) was higher with the combination at 28% versus 19% for PLD alone (P=0.0005), indicating improved tumor control. Subgroup analyses, particularly in the partially platinum-sensitive population (platinum-free interval 6-12 months), showed further OS improvements, with median OS of 23.0 months for trabectedin plus PLD versus 17.1 months for PLD (HR 0.59; 95% CI, 0.43-0.82; P=0.0015).
The standard dosing regimen for this ovarian cancer indication involves intravenous administration of PLD at 30 mg/m followed immediately by trabectedin at 1.1 mg/m infused over 3 hours, repeated every 3 weeks until disease progression or unacceptable toxicity. This schedule was used in the OVA-301 trial and balances efficacy with manageable toxicity, including premedication with dexamethasone to mitigate hypersensitivity and hepatic effects.
As of 2025, trabectedin has not received approval from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for ovarian cancer, remaining approved solely for advanced soft tissue sarcoma; however, it has been utilized off-label in select cases within the United States and other regions lacking formal approval.

Investigational uses

Trabectedin is being investigated in phase III trials for advanced leiomyosarcoma, where combination with doxorubicin has demonstrated improved survival outcomes compared to doxorubicin monotherapy. In a 2024 phase III trial reported by the National Cancer Institute, patients receiving doxorubicin plus trabectedin followed by trabectedin maintenance achieved a median overall survival of 33 months, versus 24 months with doxorubicin alone, alongside a median progression-free survival of 12 months versus 6 months. A separate phase III trial (NCT04979442) compared milademetan to trabectedin in patients with dedifferentiated liposarcoma, finding similar progression-free survival outcomes with no significant advantage for milademetan, highlighting trabectedin's established role as a benchmark in pretreated sarcoma.
Ongoing combination studies are exploring trabectedin with novel agents in advanced sarcomas. In a phase I expansion trial (SB101), SON-1010 combined with trabectedin in patients with advanced leiomyosarcoma or liposarcoma completed a positive safety review in March 2025, with no unexpected toxicities observed and topline efficacy data anticipated in the second half of 2025. Additionally, the phase III INVINCIBLE-3 trial (NCT06263231) is evaluating intratumoral INT230-6 against standard-of-care options including trabectedin in adults with recurrent or metastatic soft tissue sarcoma, aiming to assess efficacy and safety in this setting.
Investigational applications extend to other malignancies, including breast, prostate, and pediatric sarcomas. Preclinical and early-phase studies have examined trabectedin in triple-negative breast cancer, where it enhances antitumor effects when combined with IL-12 by modulating immune responses in murine models. In prostate cancer, prior phase II trials assessed trabectedin in metastatic castration-resistant cases, though recent activity focuses on broader solid tumor explorations without new prostate-specific advancements reported in 2024-2025. For pediatric sarcomas, the SARC037 phase II trial (2024 results) evaluated a 1-hour infusion of trabectedin with low-dose irinotecan in relapsed/refractory Ewing sarcoma, showing meaningful clinical benefit and supporting further study in this population. A 2025 retrospective analysis of relative dose intensity in advanced leiomyosarcoma found no significant correlation between higher trabectedin dose intensity and improved progression-free or overall survival, informing optimal dosing strategies in investigational contexts.
Off-label applications in rare sarcomas, such as uterine leiomyosarcoma, are under review for expanded access. The Canadian Drug Agency's 2025 assessment recommends reimbursing trabectedin in combination with doxorubicin for advanced unresectable or metastatic uterine or soft-tissue leiomyosarcoma, based on indirect treatment comparisons demonstrating favorable efficacy relative to alternatives like pazopanib.

Adverse effects

Common side effects

The most frequently reported adverse reactions to trabectedin are hematologic toxicities, including neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, and anemia. In the pivotal ET743-SAR-3007 phase 3 trial for advanced liposarcoma or leiomyosarcoma, grade 3-4 neutropenia occurred in 43% of patients receiving trabectedin monotherapy, while thrombocytopenia and anemia were reported at grade 3-4 rates of 21% and 19%, respectively. In the OVA-301 phase 3 trial evaluating trabectedin plus pegylated liposomal doxorubicin for relapsed ovarian cancer, neutropenia affected 92% of patients overall (73% grade 3-4), thrombocytopenia 68% (17% grade 3-4), and anemia was commonly observed, though specific rates varied by subset. These effects stem from trabectedin's DNA-binding mechanism, which contributes to myelosuppression.
Gastrointestinal disturbances and fatigue are also prevalent. Nausea occurred in 75% of patients in ET743-SAR-3007 (7% grade 3-4) and 74% in the OVA-301 trabectedin combination arm (11% grade 3-4), with vomiting reported in 46% and 47% of patients, respectively (6% and 9% grade 3-4). Fatigue was noted in 69% of ET743-SAR-3007 participants (8% grade 3-4) and 48% in OVA-301 (9% grade 3-4). Elevated liver enzymes, particularly ALT (90% any grade, 31% grade 3-4) and AST (84% any grade, 17% grade 3-4), were common in the sarcoma trial, often transient and resolving post-treatment.
Myelosuppression frequently necessitates dose interruptions or reductions, occurring in approximately 40% of patients in ET743-SAR-3007 and leading to delays in 20-30% of treatment cycles across studies. Standard premedication with dexamethasone (20 mg IV, 30 minutes prior to infusion) is recommended to mitigate potential hypersensitivity reactions and hepatotoxicity. Routine monitoring of complete blood counts and liver function is essential for managing these effects, with most resolving after supportive care or dose adjustments.

Serious adverse effects

Trabectedin is associated with several serious adverse effects, primarily hepatotoxicity, rhabdomyolysis, and cardiotoxicity, which require careful monitoring and dose adjustments to mitigate risks.
Hepatotoxicity is a prominent concern, manifesting as severe elevations in transaminases and other liver function tests. In clinical trials, grade 3-4 elevations in alanine aminotransferase (ALT) or aspartate aminotransferase (AST) occurred in approximately 35% of patients, with a median time to onset of 29 days and resolution within 13 days; drug-induced liver injury was reported in 1.3% of cases, and rare instances of fulminant hepatitis or hepatic failure have led to fatalities. Baseline liver function tests are required prior to initiating therapy, with weekly monitoring during the first two cycles and before each subsequent dose; doses should be withheld or reduced if transaminases exceed 2.5 times the upper limit of normal (ULN) in monotherapy or 5 times ULN in combination regimens, and permanently discontinued in cases of severe hepatic impairment (Child-Pugh class C). Trabectedin is contraindicated in patients with active hepatitis or severe preexisting hepatic impairment due to heightened risk of life-threatening liver toxicity.
Rhabdomyolysis, though rare, represents a severe musculoskeletal toxicity linked to elevated creatine phosphokinase (CPK) levels. Grade 3-4 CPK elevations occurred in 6% of patients, rhabdomyolysis occurred in approximately 1.1% of patients across pooled clinical data, with fatal outcomes in 0.8% of the ET743-SAR-3007 trial participants; renal failure occurred in 2.9% of patients with grade 3-4 CPK elevations. these events have been noted particularly with concurrent use of strong CYP3A4 inhibitors. Monitoring includes baseline and pre-dose CPK assessments, with immediate discontinuation recommended upon confirmation of rhabdomyolysis.
Cardiotoxicity, including cardiomyopathy and left ventricular dysfunction, is uncommon but can be serious, especially in combination therapies or patients with risk factors such as prior anthracycline exposure. In trials, cardiomyopathy developed in 6% of patients, with grade 3-4 events in 4% and one fatal case (0.3%); median onset for severe cases was 5.3 months. Cardiac function, assessed via left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF), should be evaluated before treatment initiation and every 2-3 months thereafter, with discontinuation if LVEF falls below the lower limit of normal or grade 3-4 toxicity occurs.
Capillary leak syndrome (CLS), characterized by hypotension, edema, and hypoalbuminemia, has been reported with trabectedin, including serious cases resulting in death. In clinical trials, CLS occurred in 0.7% of patients (3/424 in pooled data), all serious with one fatal. Monitor for symptoms such as sudden weight gain, new-onset edema, or hypotension, and permanently discontinue if CLS is confirmed.
Contraindications include hypersensitivity to trabectedin, and the drug carries warnings against use in pregnancy, where it can cause fetal harm when administered to a pregnant woman, based on its mechanism of action and findings in animal studies, necessitating effective contraception and discontinuation of breastfeeding. While trabectedin lacks specific black box warnings, the FDA prescribing information emphasizes the risks of hepatotoxicity and myelosuppression, which can escalate from common side effects like fatigue or nausea if not monitored.

Pharmacology

Mechanism of action

Trabectedin exerts its antitumor effects primarily through covalent binding to DNA, where it alkylates the N2 position of guanine residues in the minor groove, forming monoadducts that bend the DNA helix. This binding is facilitated by a reactive carbinolamine intermediate on the drug molecule, which undergoes nucleophilic attack by the exocyclic amino group of guanine, resulting in a stable, albeit reversible, covalent adduct. These adducts distort the DNA structure, interfering with the progression of RNA polymerase II and stalling transcription, particularly of actively transcribed genes.
The drug inhibits the activity of specific transcription factors, such as NF-Y, by preventing their binding to promoter regions and disrupting the assembly of transcription initiation complexes. This selective blockade of activated transcription, without significantly affecting constitutive transcription, leads to the downregulation of genes involved in cell proliferation and survival. Additionally, trabectedin hampers nucleotide excision repair (NER) pathways by trapping repair proteins like XPG at the adduct sites, which converts stalled transcription complexes into cytotoxic DNA lesions, including single-strand breaks that progress to double-strand breaks during replication. These double-strand breaks trigger cell cycle arrest and apoptosis through activation of p53-dependent and -independent pathways.
Trabectedin's cytotoxicity is notably selective for tumor cells with defects in homologous recombination (HR) repair, such as those harboring BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutations, where sensitivity can be up to 100-fold higher compared to HR-proficient cells due to their inability to resolve the induced DNA damage. Beyond direct genotoxic effects, trabectedin modulates the tumor microenvironment by inducing apoptosis in tumor-associated macrophages, particularly the immunosuppressive M2 subtype, via caspase-8 activation through TRAIL receptors, thereby reducing pro-tumorigenic cytokine production (e.g., IL-6 and CCL2) and enhancing antitumor immune responses. This multifaceted action contributes to its efficacy in cancers with complex stromal interactions.

Pharmacokinetics

Trabectedin is administered exclusively by intravenous infusion, resulting in nearly complete bioavailability of approximately 100%. Peak plasma concentrations are achieved at the end of the infusion, typically over a 24-hour period, with dose-proportional systemic exposure up to 1.8 mg/m.
The drug exhibits extensive distribution, with a steady-state volume of distribution exceeding 5,000 L, indicating widespread tissue penetration. Trabectedin is highly bound to plasma proteins (9498%), primarily to albumin at site I. Tumor penetration occurs primarily through passive diffusion due to its lipophilic nature and large distribution volume.
Metabolism occurs predominantly in the liver via the cytochrome P450 3A4 enzyme, leading to multiple inactive metabolites. The terminal elimination half-life is approximately 180 hours, supporting sustained plasma levels that contribute to its mechanism of action.
Excretion is primarily via the biliary route into feces (approximately 58%), with minimal renal elimination (<6% of the dose, less than 1% as unchanged drug). Plasma clearance is around 31 L/h. In patients with moderate hepatic impairment (bilirubin 1.53 times the upper limit of normal and AST/ALT less than 8 times the upper limit), exposure increases by about 97%, necessitating dose adjustments.
Trabectedin is a substrate of CYP3A4, and co-administration with strong inhibitors such as ketoconazole can increase systemic exposure by up to 66%, while inducers like rifampin may decrease it by 31%; monitoring and dose modifications are recommended.

Chemistry

Chemical structure

Trabectedin, also known as ecteinascidin 743 (ET-743), has the molecular formula C39H43N3O11S and a molecular weight of 761.84 g/mol.
It is a complex tetrahydroisoquinoline alkaloid featuring a pentacyclic structure composed of three tetrahydroisoquinoline subunits (A, B, and C rings) fused via a central phenoxydiazine core and connected by an 11-membered macrocyclic lactone bridge, along with a spiro-thioacetal linkage. The molecule includes a labile carbinolamine (hemiaminal) functional group at the C-21 position within the C subunit, which contributes to its structural instability under certain conditions.
Key structural features encompass multiple hydroxy groups at positions such as C-11 and C-12, methoxy groups at C-6 and C-7', an acetate ester at C-22, and a sulfide atom integrated into the spiro-thioacetal system that bridges the B and C subunits, imparting rigidity to the overall scaffold. These elements define its bridged ecteinascidin skeleton, originally isolated from the Caribbean tunicate Ecteinascidia turbinata.
Trabectedin's stereochemistry is intricate, with seven chiral centers that dictate its three-dimensional conformation, as evidenced by the specified configurations in its IUPAC name: (1R,2R,3R,11S,12S,14R,26R)-5,6',12-trihydroxy-6,7'-dimethoxy-7,21,30-trimethyl-27-oxo-3',4'-dihydro-2'H-17,19,28-trioxa-24-thia-13,30-diazaspirotriacontane-26,1'-isoquinoline]-4,6,8,15,20,22-hexaen-22-yl acetate. This natural product arises from microbial precursors associated with its tunicate host, highlighting its biosynthetic origins.

Biosynthesis

Trabectedin, also known as ecteinascidin 743 (ET-743), was first isolated in 1985 from the Caribbean sea squirt Ecteinascidia turbinata, a marine tunicate found in mangrove ecosystems. The compound occurs naturally at extremely low concentrations, approximately 0.0001% by dry weight in the tunicate, necessitating the harvesting of vast quantities of biomassup to one tonto obtain milligram amounts suitable for early research. This reliance on wild harvesting led to significant supply constraints and ecological concerns, including overharvesting that depleted local populations of E. turbinata and prompted efforts toward sustainable aquaculture to mitigate environmental impact.
The biosynthesis of trabectedin is attributed to a specialized bacterial endosymbiont within E. turbinata, identified as the -proteobacterium Candidatus Endoecteinascidia frumentensis. This symbiont's compact 631 kb genome encodes a non-ribosomal peptide synthetase (NRPS) pathway responsible for assembling the complex tetrahydroisoquinoline structure of trabectedin, with no evidence of polyketide synthase (PKS) involvement in the core assembly. The biosynthetic gene cluster is unusually dispersed across three genomic regions spanning 173 kb, likely resulting from genome reduction in this obligate symbiont, and includes 25 genes encoding NRPS modules, tailoring enzymes, and accessory proteins.
Key precursors in the pathway are derivatives of tyrosine, cysteine, and methionine, which provide the aromatic, sulfur-containing, and methyl units essential to trabectedin's scaffold. The NRPS machinery, exemplified by the multidomain enzyme EtuA1, facilitates the stepwise condensation and cyclization of these amino acid-derived subunits: tyrosine contributes to the tetrahydroisoquinoline rings via Pictet-Spengler-like mechanisms, while cysteine introduces the thioether bridge, and methionine supplies methyl groups for N-methylation. Post-assembly tailoring follows, involving oxidation by FAD-dependent monooxygenases (e.g., EtuO1) to introduce hydroxyl groups and form the carbinolamine moiety, alongside acetylation (EtuY) and additional N-methylations (EtuM4) for structural refinement. Sulfation occurs as a late-stage modification, likely mediated by a sulfotransferase within the cluster, to yield the active sulfate ester on the exocyclic nitrogen.
Despite these insights, challenges persist in replicating the natural pathway ex vivo. Attempts to culture C. Endoecteinascidia frumentensis have yielded only nanograms per liter due to its obligate symbiotic lifestyle and dependence on host interactions for full pathway functionality. To address supply limitations, PharmaMar developed a semi-synthetic production method in the 1990s, starting from cyanosafracin B (safracin B)a structurally related alkaloid produced by fermentation of the bacterium Pseudomonas fluorescensfollowed by chemical modifications to convert it to trabectedin. This approach has enabled scalable manufacturing while preserving the molecule's natural-inspired complexity. Recent metabolic engineering of P. fluorescens has improved precursor yields, achieving up to 0.5% dry cell weight of related compounds as of 2023.

Total synthesis

The first total synthesis of trabectedin was achieved by E. J. Corey's group at Harvard University in 1996, comprising 36 steps from commercially available materials and affording an overall yield of 0.72%. This pioneering effort highlighted the challenges of constructing the molecule's pentacyclic core, particularly the stability of the sensitive C21 carbinolamine functionality, which was addressed through a late-stage intermolecular Strecker reaction followed by intramolecular cyclization to form the C-S bond and sulfation.
In 2002, Tohru Fukuyama's group reported a total synthesis in 25 steps with an overall yield of approximately 0.56%, employing a Ugi four-component condensation and an intramolecular Heck reaction for ring assembly. A further advancement from Fukuyama's group completed a total synthesis in 27 steps and 0.45% overall yield, incorporating an intramolecular cascade Heck reaction to efficiently build the diazabicyclononane core with improved stereocontrol. In 2006, Jieping Zhu's group developed a convergent total synthesis from five building blocks, requiring 23 steps from a key intermediate (overall ~40 steps) and achieving a 1.1% yield from L-glutamic acid; this route featured a Pictet-Spengler cyclization and an acid-induced sulfide contraction, drawing inspiration from biosynthetic pathways for cascade ring formations. Samuel J. Danishefsky's group contributed a stereospecific formal total synthesis in 2006.
For commercial production, PharmaMar established a semi-synthetic process starting from the microbially derived precursor cyanosafracin B, enabling gram-scale synthesis since 2007 to meet clinical demand after regulatory approval. This approach involves 8-10 steps, including selective demethylation, sulfation, and macrolactonization, with overall yields around 10-15% and avoids the inefficiencies of de novo total synthesis.
Key synthetic challenges include achieving stereocontrol at the tetrahydroisoquinoline units (particularly C12-C13 and C18-C19), often via asymmetric Pictet-Spengler reactions or chiral auxiliaries, and managing the labile sulfate group, which is typically introduced late to prevent elimination or hydrolysis. These total syntheses have facilitated analog development, such as lurbinectedin (PM01183), where the exocyclic sulfate is replaced by a sulfamide for enhanced stability and potency; lurbinectedin was FDA-approved in 2020 for metastatic small cell lung cancer, enabling structure-activity relationship studies and second-generation therapeutics.

History

Discovery and development

Trabectedin, originally known as ecteinascidin 743 (ET-743), was first identified through the U.S. National Cancer Institute's (NCI) marine natural products screening program initiated in the 1950s and 1960s, during which extracts from the Caribbean tunicate Ecteinascidia turbinata demonstrated antitumor activity in preclinical assays. The compound was isolated and its structure elucidated in 1984 by Kenneth L. Rinehart at the University of Illinois, Urbana-Champaign, following collections of the tunicate in the early 1980s. In the early 1990s, the Spanish biopharmaceutical company PharmaMar licensed the rights to ET-743 from the University of Illinois and pursued its development, purifying the active agent from large quantities of the tunicate; notably, 1 gram of ET-743 was obtained from processing 1 metric ton of E. turbinata, highlighting the significant supply constraints inherent to natural extraction.
Preclinical studies in the 1990s further characterized trabectedin's potential, revealing potent antitumor activity in murine xenograft models of soft tissue sarcoma and ovarian cancer, where it exhibited cytotoxicity at low nanomolar concentrations against tumor cell lines. By 2000, investigations had elucidated its primary mechanism of action as covalent binding to the minor groove of DNA, leading to helix distortion and inhibition of transcription, which contributed to its selective activity in these models. These findings supported advancement to clinical evaluation, with early efforts focused on overcoming production limitations through exploration of the compound's biosynthesis in the tunicate, which initially aided in understanding yield variability but proved insufficient for scaling.
Clinical development commenced with Phase I trials in 1996, evaluating safety, dosing, and pharmacokinetics in patients with advanced solid tumors, which identified a 24-hour infusion schedule as optimal while noting challenges such as hepatotoxicity and supply shortages due to reliance on natural sourcing. To address these supply issues, PharmaMar entered a collaboration with Ortho Biotech in 2001 for co-development and commercialization, enabling expanded clinical testing. Key milestones included the granting of orphan drug designation by the European Medicines Agency in 2001 for soft tissue sarcoma and by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration in 2003 for the same indication, facilitating prioritized review and incentives for rare cancer research. By 2005, PharmaMar had scaled production through a semisynthetic process starting from the microbial precursor saframycin B, resolving earlier bottlenecks and supporting ongoing trials.

Regulatory approvals

Trabectedin, marketed as Yondelis, received its initial regulatory approval from the European Medicines Agency (EMA) on September 17, 2007, for the treatment of adult patients with advanced soft tissue sarcoma after failure of anthracyclines and ifosfamide, or those unsuitable for such therapy. This approval was based on phase II clinical data demonstrating antitumor activity in this setting. In 2009, the EMA expanded the indication to include combination therapy with pegylated liposomal doxorubicin (PLD) for platinum-sensitive relapsed ovarian cancer, following results from the phase III OVA-301 trial showing improved progression-free survival.
The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved trabectedin on October 23, 2015, under the accelerated approval pathway for the treatment of patients with unresectable or metastatic liposarcoma or leiomyosarcoma who received a prior anthracycline-containing regimen. This decision was supported by the phase III ET743-SAR-3007 trial, which demonstrated a significant improvement in progression-free survival compared to dacarbazine. Unlike in Europe, the FDA did not approve trabectedin for ovarian cancer, following an advisory committee's rejection in 2009 due to insufficient evidence of overall survival benefit.
Approvals in other regions followed the European timeline, often tied to orphan drug designations for rare cancers like soft tissue sarcoma. Trabectedin was approved in Russia in 2007 for advanced soft tissue sarcoma. In South Korea, approval occurred in the early 2010s for similar indications. Japan granted approval in 2015 for soft tissue sarcoma, with orphan status facilitating the process. These conditional approvals emphasized its role in orphan indications, limiting initial use to post-anthracycline failure cases.
As of 2025, no new indications have been added globally, but post-approval updates have reinforced safety labeling, particularly for hepatic monitoring due to the risk of hepatotoxicity. The EMA authorized a generic version, Trabectedin Accord, on April 25, 2025, maintaining the existing indications and requiring liver function tests prior to each cycle and dose adjustments for elevations in transaminases. This aligns with ongoing pharmacovigilance emphasizing proactive hepatic assessment to mitigate severe adverse effects.

Society and culture

Legal status

Trabectedin is not classified as a controlled substance under the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) schedules, with a designated schedule of none. As a cytotoxic alkylating agent, it is available only by prescription due to its significant toxicity profile, requiring administration by qualified healthcare professionals.
The European Commission granted orphan drug designation to trabectedin for the treatment of soft tissue sarcoma on May 30, 2001, and for ovarian cancer on October 17, 2003, providing 10 years of market exclusivity upon approval to encourage development for rare conditions. Similarly, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) designated it as an orphan drug for soft tissue sarcoma on September 30, 2004, and for ovarian cancer on March 29, 2005, conferring 7 years of market exclusivity to support its use in these low-prevalence cancers.
Access to trabectedin is restricted in many countries to hospital or specialized oncology centers due to the need for intravenous infusion over 24 hours via a central venous line and close monitoring for adverse effects. Prior to its regulatory approvals around 2010, compassionate use programs enabled access in non-approved regions, such as expanded access initiatives in Europe starting in 2003 for patients with advanced soft tissue sarcoma who had exhausted standard therapies.
As of 2025, trabectedin's legal status remains unchanged, continuing as a prescription-only medication without controlled substance classification, though a generic version was authorized in the European Union in April 2025 following the expiration of prior market exclusivities.

Brand names and availability

Trabectedin is primarily marketed under the brand name Yondelis by Janssen Pharmaceuticals in collaboration with PharmaMar S.A., the originator developer, in the United States and the European Union. It is formulated as a lyophilized powder for intravenous injection, available in single-dose vials containing 0.25 mg or 1 mg of trabectedin.
In certain markets following the expiration of exclusivity periods, trabectedin is available under its generic name ecteinascidin or as branded generics. For instance, in India, Natco Pharma has offered a generic version marketed as Trabec since 2022.
PharmaMar serves as the primary manufacturer, having developed a semisynthetic production process to overcome initial supply constraints from natural extraction, while Janssen handles marketing and distribution in the US. This approach stabilized the global supply chain after regulatory approvals, ensuring reliable production for commercial use.
Yondelis is widely accessible in Europe and North America through standard healthcare channels for its approved indications in soft tissue sarcoma and ovarian cancer. However, availability remains limited in low- and middle-income countries due to the high cost, approximately $6,000 per typical dose.
