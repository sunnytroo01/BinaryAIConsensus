 The Red Arrows, officially the Royal Air Force Aerobatic Team, is the aerobatics display team of the Royal Air Force, based at RAF Waddington in Lincolnshire. Formed in 1964 with its first flights in 1965, the team executes precision aerial maneuvers using nine BAE Systems Hawk T1 fast jets to demonstrate the RAF's speed, agility, and operational excellence.
The Red Arrows serve multiple roles, including recruiting new personnel, acting as ambassadors for the United Kingdom, and advancing defence diplomacy while promoting British aerospace industry capabilities. By early 2024, the team had completed nearly 5,000 displays across 57 countries, with notable tours such as the 2016 Asia-Pacific and Middle East expedition covering 20,000 miles and reaching a global audience of one billion, and the 2019 North American tour viewed by hundreds of millions. Initially equipped with the Folland Gnat trainer, which enabled 1,292 displays until 1979 and the debut of the signature Diamond Nine formation in 1968, the squadron transitioned to the Hawk T1 in 1980 for enhanced performance in complex routines. Comprising pilots and support staff drawn from frontline RAF units, the team maintains rigorous standards to embody professional aviation proficiency.

History

Predecessors and Formation

Prior to the Red Arrows, the Royal Air Force operated several aerobatic display teams, including the Black Arrows of No. 111 Squadron and the Yellowjacks of the Central Flying School. The Black Arrows, established in 1956 with Hawker Hunter F.6 jets, gained international acclaim for executing a world-record loop with 22 aircraft at the Farnborough Air Show on 6 September 1958, demonstrating exceptional formation flying capabilities with supersonic fighters. The Yellowjacks, formed in 1964 using five yellow-painted Folland Gnat T.1 trainers, showcased precise maneuvers and directly supplied the Gnat aircraft that became the Red Arrows' initial fleet, forming the technical and operational foundation for the unified team. Other predecessors, such as the Red Pelicans Jet Provost team, contributed to the naming convention by blending "red" schemes with arrow-themed precision.
In late 1964, amid resource rationalization, the RAF disbanded these command-sponsored unofficial teams and formed a single official aerobatic unit, the Royal Air Force Aerobatic Team, designated as the premier display squadron. Named the Red Arrows to evoke the legacy of prior squads like the Black Arrows, the team commenced operations in 1965 at RAF Little Rissington, Gloucestershire, with seven red-liveried Folland Gnat T.1 jets selected for their agility and suitability for tight formations. Under initial command of Flight Lieutenant Lee Jones, a Black Arrows veteran, the squadron performed its first public demonstration on 6 May 1965 at Kemble Airfield, followed by 65 displays that season across the UK and Western Europe, including France, Italy, the Netherlands, Belgium, and West Germany. The formation expanded to nine aircraft in 1966, enabling the development of the iconic Diamond Nine maneuver that defined subsequent routines.

Aerobatic Displays and Formation Flying

The Red Arrows execute aerobatic displays emphasizing synchronized formation flying and high-precision maneuvers with nine Hawk T1 jets, typically lasting 25-30 minutes under optimal conditions. Displays adapt to weather constraints: full routines incorporate loops above a 5,500-foot cloud base, rolling displays feature wing-overs and rolls between 2,500 and 5,500 feet, and flat displays limit to flypasts and steep turns below 2,500 feet. These routines highlight close-formation techniques, with aircraft maintaining separations as tight as 18-24 inches wingtip-to-wingtip during standard positions, achieved through rigorous pilot training exceeding 1,500 fast-jet hours and real-time visual referencing. 
The first half focuses on coordinated aerobatics split between the "Enid" (Reds 1-5) and "Hanna" (Reds 6-9) sections, performing loops, rolls, and shape changes in formations like the trademark Diamond Ninea nine-aircraft diamond configuration symbolizing team unity and precision since its introduction in 1966. Smoke trails in red, white, and blue, generated by dye systems, trace these paths for visual enhancement and audience comprehension, with dedicated ground teams managing dye production to ensure consistent output during maneuvers. The second half shifts to dynamic elements, including opposition passes by the Synchro Pair (Reds 6 and 7), such as the Mirror Rollsimultaneous rolls maintaining formation integrityand the Boomerang, a 2021-introduced finale involving high-speed pull-ups and reversals.
Additional maneuvers underscore formation discipline, like the Slalom, where Reds 2-5 interchange positions mid-flight without disrupting overall alignment, and the Magnum, a nine-jet breakaway to landing emphasizing sequential separation for safety. Pilots endure up to 5g forces in loops and rolls, relying on leader-initiated calls and mutual visual scanning to mitigate collision risks in zero-visibility smoke or high-speed symmetry breaks. Red 10, a non-displaying pilot, oversees from a chase position, providing radio coordination and post-flight analysis to refine techniques. Operations and Displays

Routine Structure and Signature Maneuvers

The Red Arrows' aerobatic displays are adapted to meteorological conditions, categorized into full, rolling, and flat variants. Full displays, performed when the cloud base exceeds 5,500 feet, incorporate loops and high-altitude maneuvers. Rolling displays, suitable for cloud bases between 2,500 and 5,500 feet, feature wing-overs and rolls. Flat displays, restricted to cloud bases below 2,500 feet, consist primarily of flypasts and steep turns to ensure safety.
A standard full routine commences with the team's entry in tight formation, progressing through synchronized aerobatics in the initial phase, utilizing the Enid formation (Reds 15) for front elements and Hanna formation (Reds 69) for rear synchronization, including the Synchro Pair (Reds 6 and 7). The sequence builds in complexity, transitioning to opposition maneuvers in the latter half, emphasizing dynamic passes and breaks. Routines conclude with the Magnum, a coordinated nine-aircraft break-to-land procedure. Displays are redesigned annually to introduce novel challenges, as seen in the 2025 season's enhanced formations, loops, and rolls.
Signature maneuvers highlight the team's precision and include:

Vixen Break: A high-speed head-on pass culminating in a explosive split of the formation, trailing red, white, and blue smoke, often generating forces up to five times gravity.

Diamond Nine: The emblematic tight formation of all nine aircraft in a diamond shape, symbolizing unity and executed during entry and flypasts.

Mirror Roll: Performed by the Synchro Pair rolling in unison to demonstrate mirrored opposition flying.

Slalom: Involves Reds 25 weaving and swapping positions in a serpentine pattern to showcase positional accuracy.

Boomerang: A opposition maneuver by Reds 6 and 7, arcing outward before converging, introduced as a 2021 finale element.

These elements, supervised by Red 10 from the ground, underscore the routine's emphasis on formation integrity and pilot synchronization, with separations as close as a few feet at speeds exceeding 400 mph.

Innovations in Displays and Fuel Usage

The Red Arrows have periodically innovated their aerobatic routines by reintroducing historical maneuvers and adapting formations to enhance visual impact and precision, such as the 2024 season's revival of the Short Diamond formation, a 5-4 split, and a modified Python sequence incorporating a 360-degree turn, elements not performed for decades. These updates maintain the team's signature diamond-nine configuration while optimizing for contemporary audience engagement and safety margins during high-speed passes.
In parallel, the team has advanced fuel usage through the integration of sustainable alternatives, marking a shift toward reduced environmental impact without compromising performance. On June 14, 2025, the Red Arrows executed a world-first flypast over London for the King's Birthday Parade using a blend of sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) in their BAE Systems Hawk T1 engines and hydrotreated vegetable oil (HVO) in smoke pods to generate colored trails, replacing traditional diesel. SAF, derived from renewable feedstocks, achieves lifecycle carbon emission reductions of up to 80% compared to conventional jet fuel, though actual blends in this event were partial to ensure compatibility with existing aircraft systems.
This initiative extended to full aerobatic displays at the Royal International Air Tattoo (RIAT) in July 2025, where the team conducted their first public routine powered by SAF at a 35% blend, alongside HVO-based biofuel for smoke generation, demonstrating operational viability under dynamic maneuvers like loops and rolls. These adaptations align with broader RAF efforts to trial drop-in fuels that require no hardware modifications, potentially lowering dependence on fossil fuels while preserving the precision required for formations maintaining separations as close as 18 inches at speeds exceeding 400 mph. Earlier explorations, such as 2021 considerations for "green" smoke alternatives, underscore an ongoing emphasis on minimizing trail emissions during displays.

Aircraft and Technology

Evolution from Gnats to Hawk T1

The Red Arrows commenced operations using the Folland Gnat T.1, a lightweight jet trainer noted for its agility and compact design, which facilitated tight formations and dynamic aerobatics. The team's inaugural display occurred on 6 May 1965 at RAF Little Rissington, marking the unification of RAF aerobatic units under a single banner. Over the subsequent 14 years, the squadron executed 1,292 formation displays with the Gnat, from 6 May 1965 to 15 September 1979, establishing a reputation for precision flying that drew international acclaim.
The transition to the British Aerospace Hawk T1 was driven by the RAF's adoption of the Hawk as its primary jet trainer, entering service in 1976 to replace aging aircraft like the Gnat across training squadrons. The Red Arrows received their first Hawk in August 1979, initiating a phased conversion that culminated in full operational use for the 1980 display season. This shift aligned the team's mount with frontline training assets, enhancing logistical compatibility and enabling displays that showcased the Hawk's superior thrust-to-weight ratio and structural robustness for sustained high-G maneuvers.
The Hawk T1's Adour engine provided greater power output compared to the Gnat's Viper, allowing for expanded maneuver repertoires and higher performance envelopes, while its design incorporated advanced safety features like zero-zero ejection seats. Post-transition, the team relocated from RAF Kemble to RAF Scampton, supporting intensified training and maintenance regimes tailored to the new aircraft. This evolution ensured continuity in display excellence while adapting to technological advancements in RAF aviation doctrine.

Personnel and Team Structure

Pilots: Selection, Roles, and Leadership

The Red Arrows' pilots are selected exclusively from serving Royal Air Force fast-jet aviators who possess extensive operational experience, typically having flown aircraft such as the Typhoon or F-35 in combat or frontline roles. Candidates must accumulate at least 1,500 flying hours and demonstrate exceptional proficiency, often rated above average in evaluations. The selection process involves shortlisting up to nine candidates followed by a rigorous week-long assessment camp that includes peer evaluations, interviews, and demonstrations of teamwork and precision flying skills to ensure compatibility with the team's high-stakes environment.
The display team comprises ten pilots designated as Red 1 through Red 10, who operate nine Hawk T1 aircraft during performances, with positions fixed for the entire season to foster synchronization. Specific roles vary by position: inner wingmen like Red 4 and Red 5 maintain formation stability during tight maneuvers, while outer positions such as Red 7, 8, and 9 execute high-risk elements like the Apache loop or Big Battle formation breaks. Red 10 primarily serves as the synchronization leader, flying opposing passes to calibrate timing and symmetry, and may rotate into the diamond nine for larger displays.
Leadership is embodied by Red 1, the Team Leader or "Boss," a squadron leader responsible for choreographing routines, overseeing training from October to March, and commanding the formation during shows; this role entails a three-year tenure to ensure continuity. The overall squadron falls under the Officer Commanding, a wing commander who manages administrative and logistical aspects beyond flying operations. For the 2025 season, Squadron Leader Jon Bond serves as Red 1, having assumed the position in 2024 after prior operational service. This structure emphasizes merit-based hierarchy, where leadership emerges from proven aerial expertise rather than tenure alone.

Support Crew: The Blues and Ground Operations

The Blues, comprising the Red Arrows' engineering and support personnel, number approximately 120 members who wear royal blue flying suits during the display season to distinguish them from the pilots. This team is led by a Senior Engineering Officer, supported by two Junior Engineering Officers, an Engineering Support Flight officer, a Warrant Officer, and two Flight Sergeants, ensuring comprehensive oversight of maintenance and operational readiness.
Engineering roles form the core of the Blues, with around two-thirdsapproximately 80 mechanical techniciansresponsible for servicing the Hawk T1 aircraft, including engines, hydraulics, and structural components. Avionics technicians, totaling 14, manage electrical systems and instrumentation, while a smaller cadre of weapons technicians maintains critical safety elements such as ejection seats and pyrotechnic devices. Survival equipment technicians, numbering three, handle pilots' protective gear, including helmets and parachutes.
Ground operations encompass logistics, transport, and display support, coordinated to enable global deployments and rapid turnaround. A five-member logistics team oversees spare parts inventory and loading, while eight drivers operate a fleet of 18 vehicles, ranging from 38-tonne trucks to vans, including tasks like refueling and supplying diesel for smoke systems. The operations team plans flights and missions, integrating with engineering for aircraft servicing and safety protocols.
Specialized sub-units enhance display functionality: three RAF photographers capture aerial footage and images, two dye teams maintain smoke pods for colored vapor trails, and the Engineering Support Flight conducts quality assurance and training using regular and reserve personnel. The Circus, a mobile detachment of 10 aircraft engineering technicians and one photographer, provides on-site technical support during tours, with members allocated to specific pilots and often flying in the rear seats of Hawk aircraft for immediate response.

Training Regimen and Team Dynamics

The selection of pilots for the Red Arrows requires candidates to be serving fast-jet pilots from frontline RAF squadrons, possessing a minimum of 1,500 flying hours, having completed an operational tour, and holding an above-average flying assessment. A shortlist of up to nine applicants is then evaluated during a dedicated selection week, which includes intensive flying tests, formal interviews, media handling assessments, and peer evaluations, culminating in the annual appointment of up to three new pilots to three-year display tours. The team leader (Red 1) undergoes a separate selection process reserved for candidates with prior experience on a full three-year pilot tour.
Training for the display season commences in October or November at RAF Waddington, immediately after the prior year's commitments conclude, initially focusing on small formations of three or four aircraft to rebuild basic skills. Each pilot conducts three sorties daily across five days per week, with every flighttypically lasting 30 minutesembedded within a cycle of roughly two hours dedicated to pre-flight briefings, post-flight debriefs, and team discussions to refine maneuvers and address discrepancies. The regimen escalates progressively to larger formations and complex sequences, incorporating up to 15 flights per pilot weekly depending on conditions, before transitioning to overseas training under Exercise Springhawk in mid-March or early April for optimal weather and extended hours. Final evaluation by senior RAF officers during Springhawk grants Public Display Authority, authorizing the switch to red flying suits for pilots and royal blue coveralls for the ground crew.
Team dynamics prioritize unwavering trust, precision, and cohesion, essential for formations where aircraft operate in close proximity at high speeds, supported by the integration of nine display pilots, one reserve, and approximately 120 engineering technicians known as "the Blues." Peer assessments during selection explicitly evaluate interpersonal compatibility and collective performance potential, ensuring recruits align with the team's emphasis on mutual accountability and shared professionalism. This structure fosters a high-performance environment where pilots and support staff collaborate seamlessly, with engineering teams maintaining aircraft standards through rigorous quality assurance and safety protocols, reinforcing overall operational reliability.

Incidents, Controversies, and Responses

Aviation Accidents and Safety Record

The Red Arrows maintain rigorous safety protocols amid the inherent risks of high-performance aerobatic maneuvers, having completed over 5,000 displays worldwide since their formation in 1965. Despite this, the team has experienced 10 pilot fatalities over its first 50 years, with the majority occurring in training or practice sessions rather than public displays. Accidents underscore the physical and mechanical challenges of formation flying at speeds exceeding 700 mph and g-forces up to 7g, though official inquiries have led to procedural and equipment improvements.
Key incidents include the team's first fatality on 11 September 1969, when a pilot struck trees during a practice flight at RAF Cranswell. On 7 May 1971, four pilots perished in a mid-air collision approximately 100 feet above the runway during a rehearsal at RAF Kemble, marking the deadliest event in the team's history. Flight Lieutenant Neil Duncan MacLachlan died in a 1988 crash, the last pilot fatality for over two decades until a cluster in 2011.
In 2011, two pilots lost their lives in separate accidents. On 20 August, Flight Lieutenant Jon Egging, flying Red 4 (Hawk T1 XX179), crashed into a field near Bournemouth Airport after a display at the Bournemouth Air Festival; a Service Inquiry attributed the incident to g-induced loss of consciousness, preventing ejection. On 8 November, Flight Lieutenant Sean Cunningham died at RAF Scampton when his ejection seat activated inadvertently during low-level training, with investigations citing potential faults in the seat's firing handle or parachute deployment.
The most recent fatality occurred on 20 March 2018 at RAF Valley, where Hawk T1 XX204 stalled during a simulated engine failure exercise and crashed on the runway; the pilot, Flight Lieutenant David Baden, ejected safely, but rear-seat engineer Corporal Jonathan Bayliss, aged 41, did not and was killed. A coroner ruled the death preventable, citing inadequate risk assessment for the maneuver. Non-fatal incidents, such as a 2010 mid-air collision during practice in Cretewhere Red 6's pilot ejected with injuries and Red 7 diverted safelyhave prompted enhanced collision avoidance training.

Misconduct Allegations and Inquiries

In 2022, the Royal Air Force initiated a Non-Statutory Inquiry (NSI) into the RAF Aerobatic Team (RAFAT), known as the Red Arrows, and its base at RAF Scampton, prompted by reports of unacceptable behaviors. The inquiry examined the period from 2017 to 2021 and identified widespread and normalized predatory conduct, including sexual harassment such as unwanted physical contact, sexual text messages, and verbal comments on women's appearance. Specific incidents documented exposure of genitals on two occasions, which were not met with offense and reflected a normalized tolerance for such acts within the team's military subculture.
The NSI report detailed a culture of male sexual entitlement, where female personnel and other ranks were particularly vulnerable to targeting, often facilitated by an alcohol-focused environment that exacerbated risks to flight safety and operational effectiveness. Bullying, defined under JSP 763 standards, occurred across ranks and branches, compounded by a bystander culture that failed to challenge misconduct and leadership shortcomings in information flow and response to visible issues. One formal Service Police complaint resulted in a Court Martial conviction for battery, highlighting disciplinary outcomes from the allegations.
A companion Command, Leadership, and Management (CLM) report corroborated the NSI findings, attributing persistent problems to historical tendencies to handle matters internally and inadequate accountability structures. On November 1, 2023, Air Chief Marshal Sir Richard Knighton, Chief of the Air Staff, publicly acknowledged the issues, issuing an unreserved apology to affected individuals, particularly three named women, and confirming that investigations had led to dismissals and other actions. The inquiries emphasized extra-marital relationships and unchecked behaviors as undermining team cohesion, with no evidence of systemic cover-ups but clear failures in proactive oversight.

Reforms, Accountability, and Recovery Measures

In response to the Non-Statutory Inquiry (NSI) and Command, Leadership, and Management reports published on 1 November 2023, which documented widespread and normalized unacceptable behaviors including sexual harassment, bullying, and a bystander culture within the RAF Aerobatic Team (RAFAT) from 2017 to 2021, the RAF accepted all recommendations and initiated accountability measures. Chief of the Air Staff Air Chief Marshal Sir Richard Knighton issued an unreserved apology to affected personnel, particularly three women who had raised formal complaints, acknowledging that such conduct had no place in the service. Individual sanctions included the dismissal of at least two pilots for sexual misconduct and bullying, with one case involving Flight Lieutenant Will Cambridge in 2022, reflecting a commitment to enforce the Ministry of Defence's zero-tolerance policy on harmful behaviors.
The team was placed under "special measures" on 14 November 2023, entailing enhanced oversight, leadership changes, and close monitoring of the 130-strong squadron at RAF Waddington to prevent recurrence of the toxic culture identified in the inquiries. Reforms emphasized rebuilding trust through significant personnel turnover in leadership and crew roles, alongside reinforced reporting mechanisms via command chains and confidential helplines to encourage whistleblowing without fear of reprisal. Defence Minister Andrew Murrison stressed that no special treatment would be afforded, ensuring alignment with broader RAF standards on conduct.
Recovery efforts progressed into 2024, with the reconstituted team completing a six-month training regimen and securing Public Display Authority (PDA) approval in May for its 60th anniversary season, demonstrating operational resumption under scrutiny. By April 2024, squadron representatives stated the team had "moved on" from the historical allegations, focusing on cultural safeguards and performance, though ongoing monitoring persisted to embed long-term behavioral changes. These measures aimed to restore the unit's effectiveness while prioritizing ethical standards, with no major new misconduct reports emerging through the 2025 display season preparations.

Recent Developments and Outlook

2024-2025 Season Highlights

The 2024 season commemorated the Red Arrows' 60th anniversary, with the team returning to full nine-aircraft displays after previous limitations due to aircraft availability. Key events included a looping arrival and full performance at the Royal International Air Tattoo (RIAT) at RAF Fairford on July 19-21, 2024, showcasing maneuvers like the Spaghetti Break. International highlights featured a Niagara Falls flypast in Canada and a joint display with the Royal Canadian Air Force Snowbirds, alongside participation in the Battle of Crete commemorations in Greece on May 21. By the start of the season, the team had accumulated nearly 5,000 displays across 57 countries since its formation.
The 2025 season featured a demanding schedule of 56 displays across 12 countries, drawing millions of spectators and emphasizing European engagements with visits to 12 European nations. The team introduced a new display sequence with enhanced challenging formations, loops, and rolls, earning public display authority for over 60 public appearances including airshows and flypasts. Prominent UK events encompassed performances at the Clacton Airshow on August 21, Blackpool Airshow, and RIAT 2025 at RAF Fairford, while overseas highlights included the San Javier Airshow in Spain honoring the King. The season concluded on October 17 at RAF Waddington, Lincolnshire, with final sorties marking the end of tours for pilots such as Squadron Leader Tom Hansford (Red 6) and Squadron Leader Graeme Muscat (Red 10).

Sustainability Initiatives and Future Plans

The Red Arrows have pioneered the integration of sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) into their operations to mitigate environmental impact from jet propulsion and smoke generation. On June 14, 2025, the team executed a world-first flypast over London powered by a blend of SAF for engines and hydrotreated vegetable oil (HVO) for colored smoke trails, marking the inaugural public ceremonial use of these alternatives. SAF reduces lifecycle carbon emissions by up to 80% relative to conventional jet fuel, while HVO, a renewable diesel from vegetable oils, supports similar decarbonization for non-propulsion uses. These trials align with broader RAF objectives to decrease dependence on global fossil fuel chains and validate fuels for operational scalability.
Subsequent applications expanded in 2025, including a debut aerobatic display at the Royal International Air Tattoo (RIAT) from July 1820, where 35% of fuel comprised sustainable sources like HVO. At the International Ayr Show in August, a 15% SAF blend powered performances, further testing compatibility with Hawk T1 aircraft. By September 2025, additional innovations enabled SAF and HVO to generate the team's signature colored trails, advancing eco-friendly visual effects.
Future plans emphasize aircraft modernization amid sustainability goals. The Ministry of Defence initiated a competition in July 2025 to procure replacements for the Hawk T1 and T2 fleet, as projections indicate potential service life limitations beyond the mid-2030s, prompting evaluation of advanced trainers compatible with low-carbon operations. A September 2025 agreement elevates Ayrshire, Scotland, as a leading candidate for manufacturing these next-generation jets, potentially enhancing domestic supply chains for sustainable aviation technologies. Display preparations for 2026, including refined formations, resumed training on October 7, 2025, at RAF Waddington, with ongoing fuel trials informing long-term decarbonization.
