Cancer vaccine
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 A cancer vaccine is a type of immunotherapy that either prevents the development of certain cancers by targeting infectious agents linked to oncogenesis or treats existing malignancies by stimulating the immune system to recognize and eliminate tumor cells expressing specific antigens. These vaccines work by introducing antigenssuch as viral proteins or tumor-specific neoantigensto prime immune cells like T cells and antibodies, thereby enhancing the body's natural defenses against cancer. Unlike traditional vaccines for infectious diseases, cancer vaccines address the challenge of tumor immune evasion, where cancers suppress immune responses through mechanisms like antigen loss or checkpoint inhibition.
Preventive cancer vaccines focus on blocking the initial infection by oncogenic viruses, significantly reducing the incidence of associated cancers. The human papillomavirus (HPV) vaccines, including Gardasil 9, Cervarix, and earlier versions of Gardasil, target high-risk HPV types responsible for approximately 70% of cervical cancers and other HPV-related malignancies such as anal, oropharyngeal, and vulvar cancers. Licensed by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for individuals aged 945, these vaccines are nearly 100% effective in preventing infections and precancerous lesions from targeted HPV strains when administered before exposure, with protection lasting at least 1012 years based on clinical follow-up data. Similarly, the hepatitis B virus (HBV) vaccine prevents chronic HBV infection, which is a major risk factor for hepatocellular carcinoma, and has been routinely recommended since the 1980s, contributing to substantial declines in liver cancer rates in vaccinated populations. These prophylactic approaches have demonstrated population-level impact, with HPV vaccination programs projected to avert up to 90% of cervical cancers globally if widely adopted.
Therapeutic cancer vaccines, in contrast, aim to elicit immune responses against established tumors and are typically personalized or antigen-specific to overcome the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. The FDA has approved a limited number of such vaccines, including sipuleucel-T (Provenge), a dendritic cell-based vaccine for metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer, which extends median survival by about 4 months in clinical trials. Another is talimogene laherparepvec (T-VEC), an oncolytic virus therapy injected into melanoma lesions to selectively replicate in and lyse cancer cells while expressing granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor to boost local immune activation, showing improved durable response rates in advanced cases. Bacillus Calmette-Gurin (BCG), derived from attenuated Mycobacterium bovis, is also FDA-approved for intravesical treatment of non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer, inducing local inflammation and immune-mediated tumor destruction. Despite these successes, therapeutic vaccines often face limitations such as modest response rates (typically 1020% in monotherapy) due to tumor heterogeneity and immune exhaustion, necessitating combinations with checkpoint inhibitors like PD-1 blockers to enhance efficacy.
Ongoing research emphasizes innovative platforms to improve therapeutic outcomes, including mRNA-based vaccines that encode neoantigensunique mutations in patient tumorsfor personalized immunization, as demonstrated in early-phase trials for pancreatic, kidney, and glioblastoma where they induced robust T-cell responses, with prolonged progression-free survival observed in pancreatic and kidney cancers. Viral vector and peptide vaccines targeting shared tumor antigens are also advancing, with over 2,300 therapeutic cancer vaccine clinical trials registered as of September 2025, many exploring combinations with other immunotherapies. These developments hold promise for broader applicability across solid and hematologic cancers, though challenges like antigen identification and manufacturing scalability persist.

Fundamentals

Definition and History

A cancer vaccine is a type of immunotherapy designed to stimulate the body's immune system to recognize and attack cancer cells, either by targeting established tumors in therapeutic applications or by preventing infections from oncogenic viruses that can lead to cancer. Unlike conventional vaccines, which primarily prevent infectious diseases before exposure, cancer vaccines address either pre-existing malignancies or high-risk viral infections, such as those caused by human papillomavirus (HPV) or hepatitis B virus (HBV).
A pivotal milestone occurred in the 1890s with William Coley's work, where he administered bacterial toxinsknown as Coley's toxinsto patients with inoperable sarcomas, achieving tumor regression in some cases and establishing the first deliberate attempt at bacterial-based cancer immunotherapy. Progress accelerated in the 1970s with the identification of tumor-specific antigens in chemically induced animal tumors, providing evidence that unique molecular targets on cancer cells could elicit immune responses. By the 1990s, researchers shifted toward molecularly defined vaccines, cloning human tumor antigens such as MAGE-1, which enabled more precise immune targeting.
Key advancements in the 2000s included the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval of Sipuleucel-T in 2010, the first therapeutic cancer vaccine for metastatic prostate cancer, which demonstrated prolonged survival in clinical trials. Concurrently, preventive cancer vaccines emerged: the hepatitis B vaccine, approved in 1981, reduced HBV-related liver cancer incidence by preventing chronic infections, while the HPV vaccine, approved in 2006, targeted viruses responsible for cervical and other cancers. The 2020s brought innovations in mRNA-based vaccines, accelerated by rapid developments during the COVID-19 pandemic, with personalized neoantigen mRNA vaccines entering clinical trials to elicit tailored T-cell responses against patient-specific tumors. As of 2025, several mRNA-based personalized neoantigen vaccines have advanced to phase 3 trials, demonstrating robust T-cell responses and improved progression-free survival in cancers such as melanoma and pancreatic cancer.

Prevention versus Treatment

Preventive cancer vaccines are designed to induce immunity against oncogenic viruses or environmental risk factors prior to the onset of malignancy, thereby blocking pathways such as viral integration into host DNA or chronic inflammation that drive cellular transformation. These vaccines target healthy individuals at risk, focusing on population-level strategies to avert cancer development altogether. In contrast, therapeutic cancer vaccines aim to stimulate immune responses against established tumors in patients with diagnosed malignancies, often employing personalized approaches that identify and target tumor-specific neoantigens to promote tumor regression or slow disease progression. This distinction underscores a shift from preemptive protection to reactive intervention, where preventive vaccines leverage the immune system's naive state, while therapeutic ones must contend with an altered tumor microenvironment.
Strategically, preventive vaccines typically utilize viral vector or protein-based platforms to elicit broad, long-lasting humoral and cellular immunity against infectious agents like human papillomavirus (HPV) or hepatitis B virus (HBV), administered in early life or before exposure. Therapeutic vaccines, however, often incorporate dendritic cell or mRNA technologies tailored to individual tumor profiles, seeking to overcome established immune tolerance and activate effector T cells directly at the tumor site. These approaches reflect differing priorities: preventive strategies emphasize widespread vaccination campaigns for herd immunity and incidence reduction, whereas therapeutic ones prioritize patient-specific customization to address tumor heterogeneity and evasion mechanisms.
Outcomes for preventive vaccines demonstrate high efficacy, with HPV vaccines achieving over 90% reduction in vaccine-type-related cervical precancers and a substantial decrease in cervical cancer incidence among vaccinated populations. Similarly, HBV vaccination has led to reductions in hepatocellular carcinoma incidence in high-risk groups, such as a 74% reduction in children aged 6-9 years and 68% in those aged 10-14 years in Taiwan, through long-term cohort studies tracking vaccinated versus unvaccinated individuals. Therapeutic vaccines, by comparison, exhibit low response rates, typically around 4% objective clinical responses in early-phase monotherapy trials, hampered by tumor-induced immunosuppression and immune exhaustion in advanced disease settings. While preventive vaccines yield population-wide impacts by curbing cancer onset, therapeutic ones focus on extending individual survival, though challenges like regulatory T cell dominance in tumors limit their broader applicability.

Types

Preventive Vaccines

Preventive cancer vaccines aim to immunize individuals against oncogenic viruses prior to infection, thereby blocking the initial steps in viral carcinogenesis and averting the development of associated malignancies. These vaccines primarily target viruses such as human papillomavirus (HPV) and hepatitis B virus (HBV), which are responsible for approximately 15-20% of all global cancer cases. By inducing neutralizing antibodies and cellular immunity against viral entry or persistence, these vaccines prevent chronic infections that lead to cellular transformation and tumor formation.
Key examples include HPV vaccines like Gardasil-9, approved by the FDA in 2014, which protects against nine HPV typesincluding seven high-risk strains responsible for about 90% of cervical cancers. Similarly, HBV vaccines such as Engerix-B, a recombinant subunit vaccine using hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg), prevent chronic HBV infection, which is a major precursor to hepatocellular carcinoma. These vaccines have demonstrated high efficacy in clinical and real-world settings; for instance, HPV vaccines using virus-like particles (VLPs)non-infectious structures mimicking the viral capsid assembled from HPV L1 major capsid proteinsachieve near-complete protection against persistent infection with targeted strains.
Vaccination schedules typically involve 2-3 doses depending on age and immune status: for HPV vaccines like Gardasil-9, adolescents aged 9-14 years receive 2 doses 6-12 months apart, while those 15 years and older require 3 doses at 0, 2, and 6 months. HBV vaccines like Engerix-B follow a standard 3-dose regimen at 0, 1, and 6 months to ensure long-term seroprotection. Up to 2025, population-level data from vaccinated cohorts show 80-90% reductions in infections with vaccine-targeted HPV types, alongside substantial declines in precancerous lesions and cervical cancer incidence. For HBV, widespread vaccination has led to over 90% prevention of chronic carriage in infants and a marked decrease in hepatocellular carcinoma rates in vaccinated populations.
An emerging example of a preventive cancer vaccine targeting non-viral factors, such as those in high-risk individuals for lung cancer, is LungVax, developed by researchers at the University of Oxford, the Francis Crick Institute, and University College London. LungVax employs viral vector technology, where DNA sequences are inserted into a harmless chimpanzee adenovirus (ChAdOx2) as a carrier to deliver genetic instructions into human cells. These cells then produce lung cancer-related neoantigensabnormal proteins resulting from DNA mutations on precancerous cellsprompting the immune system to recognize these as red flags and attack the precancerous cells to prevent tumor development. The vaccine is designed for high-risk groups, such as current or former smokers aged 55-74. Funding of 1.7 million from Cancer Research UK and the CRIS Cancer Foundation supports its development, with a phase 1 clinical trial expected to begin in summer 2026 to assess safety, optimal dosing, and immune response in individuals at high risk of lung cancer recurrence or onset.
Despite their success, preventive vaccines have limitations: they do not address cancers caused by non-viral factors, such as those driven by genetic mutations or environmental exposures, and achieving herd immunity requires vaccination coverage exceeding 80% in target populations to interrupt viral transmission effectively.

Therapeutic Vaccines

Therapeutic cancer vaccines aim to treat established cancers by eliciting or enhancing immune responses against tumor cells, distinguishing them from preventive vaccines that target viral causes to avert oncogenesis. These vaccines focus on activating T cells to recognize and destroy malignant cells, often addressing the challenge of immune evasion in advanced disease. Unlike prophylactic approaches, therapeutic vaccines must overcome the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment to generate durable anti-tumor immunity.
A core design principle of therapeutic cancer vaccines is to break immune tolerance to tumor-associated antigens (TAAs), which are self-like proteins overexpressed in cancer cells, by presenting them in an immunogenic context that activates antigen-specific CD8+ and CD4+ T cells. To achieve this, vaccines are frequently formulated with adjuvants, such as Toll-like receptor agonists, to stimulate dendritic cells and promote cytokine release for stronger T-cell priming. Additionally, combination strategies with immune checkpoint inhibitors, like anti-PD-1 antibodies, are employed to counteract T-cell exhaustion and amplify vaccine-induced responses in the tumor microenvironment.
Therapeutic cancer vaccines are categorized based on their delivery platforms and antigen presentation methods. Peptide-based vaccines use synthetic peptides derived from TAAs to directly stimulate T-cell responses, often requiring adjuvants to enhance immunogenicity and target shared antigens across patients. Dendritic cell vaccines involve harvesting patient-derived dendritic cells, loading them ex vivo with tumor antigens, and reinfusing them to initiate anti-tumor immunity; a prominent example is Sipuleucel-T, an autologous dendritic cell product pulsed with prostatic acid phosphatase (PAP) peptides, which extended median overall survival by 4.1 months (from 21.7 to 25.8 months) in men with metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer in a phase III trial. Recent advances in dendritic cell-based vaccines focus on neoantigen-loaded approaches for personalized immunotherapy. For example, a phase 2 trial in newly diagnosed glioblastoma patients demonstrated improved survival outcomes with personal dendritic cell vaccines targeting tumor-specific neoantigens. Ongoing clinical trials are exploring neoantigen-based dendritic cell vaccines in solid tumors, showing potential in eliciting durable tumor-specific immune responses. Viral vector vaccines employ modified viruses, including oncolytic types like adenoviruses or poxviruses, to deliver and express tumor antigens while lysing cancer cells to release additional antigens and provoke inflammation. mRNA-based vaccines encode neoantigenspatient-specific mutations unique to tumorsfor personalized therapy, instructing cells to produce antigens that trigger tailored T-cell responses; ongoing 2025 trials have demonstrated feasibility in advanced melanoma, with objective response rates up to 40% when combined with checkpoint blockade, and in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, where mRNA-lipoplex formulations induced long-lived CD8+ T cells against somatic mutations.
Clinical outcomes for therapeutic vaccines vary by cancer type, with prostate cancer trials showing median survival extensions of 4-6 months using dendritic cell approaches like Sipuleucel-T, though broader efficacy remains limited. In solid tumors, challenges arise from intratumoral heterogeneity, which leads to antigen escape and variable T-cell infiltration, resulting in response rates often below 20% without combinatorial therapies.

Mechanisms and Development

Mechanism of Action

Cancer vaccines function by delivering tumor-specific or tumor-associated antigens to the immune system, primarily through antigen-presenting cells (APCs) such as dendritic cells, which process these antigens and present them on major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules to T cells. This presentation occurs via MHC class I molecules for cytotoxic CD8+ T cells, which recognize intracellular antigens and directly target tumor cells, and MHC class II molecules for helper CD4+ T cells, which provide support for CD8+ T cell expansion and facilitate antibody production by B cells. The core process aims to prime and expand antigen-specific T cells capable of mounting a targeted antitumor response.
The activation sequence begins with the vaccine introducing antigens, often tumor-associated antigens like those derived from mutated proteins, to APCs in lymphoid tissues. APCs internalize, process, and load these antigens onto MHC class I or II molecules; for exogenous antigens, cross-presentation allows MHC class I loading to activate CD8+ T cells. Nave T cells recognizing the peptide-MHC complex via their T cell receptor (TCR) require a second signal for full activation: co-stimulation through the CD28 receptor on T cells binding to B7 molecules (CD80/CD86) on APCs, preventing T cell anergy and promoting proliferation and differentiation into effector cells. Effector CD8+ T cells then traffic to the tumor microenvironment, where they induce tumor cell apoptosis primarily through the perforin/granzyme pathwayperforin forms pores in the target cell membrane to allow granzyme entry and caspase activationor the Fas-FasL pathway, which triggers extrinsic apoptosis signaling.
A key outcome of this process is the generation of long-lived memory T cells, including central memory and tissue-resident memory subsets, which persist after initial tumor clearance to provide sustained immune surveillance and rapid response to potential recurrence. These memory cells maintain antitumor specificity and contribute to durable protection by proliferating upon re-exposure to tumor antigens.
To enhance these mechanisms, adjuvants such as Toll-like receptor (TLR) agonists are incorporated into cancer vaccines to mimic pathogen-associated molecular patterns, thereby activating APCs, promoting cytokine release, and providing danger signals that counteract tumor-induced immune suppression, including T cell anergy. TLR agonists, like those targeting TLR4 or TLR9, mature dendritic cells and amplify both innate and adaptive responses, improving antigen presentation and T cell priming.

Methods of Vaccine Design

Cancer vaccines are engineered using diverse platforms to deliver tumor-associated antigens effectively while stimulating robust immune responses. Peptide and protein-based vaccines typically involve synthetic peptides or recombinant proteins mixed with adjuvants to enhance immunogenicity. Short peptides, often less than 15 amino acids, are economical and biocompatible but require adjuvants like poly-ICLC to provide co-stimulation and activate T cells. Longer peptides (15-25 amino acids) incorporate multiple epitopes to engage both CD8+ and CD4+ T cells, with stability improved through lipid modifications or self-assembling formulations.
Nucleic acid-based platforms, including DNA plasmids and mRNA, encode antigens for in vivo expression by host cells. DNA vaccines offer long-term stability and persistent antigen presentation but exhibit lower immunogenicity, often necessitating electroporation or viral vectors for delivery. mRNA vaccines, produced via in vitro transcription, enable rapid cytoplasmic translation and avoid nuclear integration risks; nucleoside modifications such as pseudouridine enhance stability and reduce innate immune activation. Cell-based approaches utilize autologous or allogeneic dendritic cells pulsed with antigens or whole tumor cells engineered to express immune stimulants like GM-CSF, providing broad antigen coverage but facing challenges in specificity.
Viral vectors, such as adenoviruses or modified vaccinia Ankara, deliver antigens with inherent adjuvant properties to promote strong T-cell responses, while non-viral vectors like liposomes or electroporation systems offer safer alternatives for gene transfer. For instance, in the design of preventive vaccines like LungVax, DNA sequences encoding lung cancer-related neoantigens are inserted into a harmless chimpanzee adenovirus as a carrier to deliver genetic instructions into human cells; these cells then produce the neoantigensabnormal proteins arising from DNA mutations in precancerous cellsallowing the immune system to recognize and attack such cells to prevent tumor development. Design considerations prioritize stability through encapsulation techniques, immunogenicity via adjuvants or targeting of antigen-presenting cells, and scalability for clinical production. Personalization in therapeutic vaccines relies on next-generation sequencing (NGS) to identify patient-specific neoantigens, enabling tailored platforms like mRNA encoding multiple epitopes for improved tumor targeting. Advances in the 2020s, inspired by COVID-19 vaccine technologies, include mRNA platforms from Moderna and BioNTech using lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) for lymph node-targeted delivery, enhancing uptake and reducing dosing requirements. Nanoparticle encapsulation, such as LNPs or polymer-based systems, further improves antigen stability and immunogenicity across platforms. Production adheres to good manufacturing practice (GMP) standards, with mRNA synthesis via GMP-compliant in vitro transcription and purification ensuring scalability for personalized vaccines.

Antigens and Targets

Antigen Candidates

Antigen candidates for cancer vaccines are primarily derived from three categories: tumor-associated antigens (TAAs), tumor-specific antigens (neoantigens), and viral antigens. TAAs are proteins overexpressed in tumor cells compared to normal tissues, making them potential targets despite their presence in healthy cells, which can lead to off-target immune responses. Examples include HER2, a receptor tyrosine kinase overexpressed in approximately 20-30% of breast cancers, and MAGE-A3, a cancer-testis antigen expressed in melanoma and other solid tumors but silenced in most normal tissues except the testis.
Tumor-specific antigens, often referred to as neoantigens, arise from somatic mutations unique to the tumor genome, offering high specificity without tolerance in the host immune system. These are particularly abundant in cancers with high tumor mutational burden (TMB), such as melanoma, where whole-exome sequencing identifies patient-specific neoantigens for personalized vaccines; for instance, studies have targeted up to 20 neoantigens per patient derived from sequencing tumor and normal tissue pairs. WT1, a transcription factor overexpressed in acute myeloid leukemia and other hematologic malignancies, serves as another example of a TAA.
Recent advancements as of 2025 include AI and machine learning tools for more accurate neoantigen prediction from sequencing data.
Viral antigens target virus-associated cancers, exploiting oncogenic viral proteins that are foreign to the human immune system. In human papillomavirus (HPV)-related cervical and head-and-neck cancers, the E6 and E7 oncoproteins are prime candidates, as they inactivate tumor suppressors p53 and Rb, respectively, and have been incorporated into preventive and therapeutic vaccines like those based on virus-like particles.
The selection of antigen candidates involves a multi-step process beginning with bioinformatics tools to predict peptides that bind to major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules for presentation to T cells. Algorithms assess binding affinity, stability, and immunogenicity potential by analyzing tumor sequencing data, prioritizing epitopes likely to elicit cytotoxic T-lymphocyte responses; tumors with high TMB, like melanoma, yield more candidates due to increased neoantigen diversity. Validation follows through in vitro and ex vivo immunogenicity assays, such as ELISPOT, which quantifies interferon-gamma secretion by T cells in response to candidate peptides, confirming their ability to stimulate antigen-specific immunity.
Despite these advances, challenges persist in antigen selection and application. Tumor heterogeneity results in variable antigen expression across primary and metastatic lesions, complicating the choice of broadly effective targets and necessitating multi-antigen approaches. Additionally, tumors can evade immunity through antigen downregulation or loss, reducing vaccine efficacy; for example, MHC class I downregulation occurs in up to 90% of some tumor types, limiting neoantigen presentation.

Desirable Characteristics

Cancer vaccines should ideally possess a combination of properties that ensure strong therapeutic impact while minimizing risks and enabling widespread clinical application. These include high immunogenicity to mount effective anti-tumor immune responses, robust safety profiles to protect patient health, and practical features for feasible production and delivery. Additionally, optimal vaccines demonstrate synergy with complementary therapies, long-term durability, and adaptability to dynamic tumor landscapes, all evaluated through standardized preclinical and immune correlates.
Immunogenicity is a cornerstone, requiring vaccines to induce potent, broad T-cell responses, including both CD8+ cytotoxic and CD4+ helper T cells, with minimal off-target effects to focus immunity on tumor cells. This encompasses the ability to elicit both humoral (antibody-mediated) and cellular immunity, often enhanced by adjuvants like TLR agonists or GM-CSF, which promote dendritic cell maturation and T-cell activation. For instance, neoantigen-based vaccines have demonstrated strong immunogenicity by targeting tumor-specific mutations, leading to durable memory T-cell formation that persists for 24.5 years post-vaccination. Such responses are critical for overcoming tumor immune evasion and achieving clinical efficacy.
Safety profiles must feature low toxicity and negligible risk of autoimmunity, avoiding cross-reactivity with healthy tissues through precise antigen selection. Vaccines should be well-tolerated, even in frail or elderly patients, with common side effects limited to mild flu-like symptoms such as fatigue or fever, as observed in mRNA and peptide platforms. Non-integrating delivery methods, like mRNA-lipid nanoparticles, further reduce risks compared to DNA-based approaches, ensuring no genomic alterations in host cells.
Practicality demands efficient manufacturing and distribution to support broad accessibility. Off-the-shelf vaccines using shared antigens enable immediate availability and lower costs, while personalized options should limit production to under 8 weeks to align with patient treatment timelines. Thermostability is essential for global use, with peptide vaccines offering high stability at room temperature and mRNA platforms advancing toward scalable, cost-effective production through miniaturized lines. These attributes facilitate integration into routine oncology care without excessive logistical burdens.
Beyond core properties, desirable vaccines exhibit synergy with existing therapies, such as immune checkpoint inhibitors (e.g., pembrolizumab or nivolumab), to counteract the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment and amplify T-cell infiltration. Durability of response is vital, aiming for years-long immunity via sustained T-cell memory, as seen in synthetic long peptide vaccines capable of maintaining anti-tumor effects for several years. Adaptability to tumor evolution, through multi-epitope targeting or revaccination, ensures ongoing relevance against heterogeneous or mutating cancers.
Evaluation of these characteristics relies on preclinical models, such as mouse tumor challenge assays, that measure immune correlates like IFN- production via ELISpot or intracellular cytokine staining, alongside T-cell receptor sequencing to confirm response breadth and persistence. These metrics guide optimization, prioritizing vaccines that correlate immune activation with tumor regression in vivo.

Clinical Progress

Approved Cancer Vaccines

Approved cancer vaccines encompass both preventive and therapeutic types that have received regulatory approval from agencies such as the FDA and EMA. Preventive vaccines target oncogenic viruses to avert cancer development, while therapeutic vaccines stimulate immune responses against established tumors. As of 2025, these vaccines have demonstrated significant public health impact, particularly in reducing virus-associated malignancies, though therapeutic options remain limited to specific indications with incremental benefits.
Preventive cancer vaccines include those against human papillomavirus (HPV) and hepatitis B virus (HBV), which are major causes of cervical, anal, oropharyngeal, and hepatocellular carcinomas. The HPV vaccine Gardasil-9, a 9-valent recombinant vaccine, was approved by the FDA in 2014 for individuals aged 9 through 45 to prevent HPV types 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, and 58-associated diseases, including cervical, anal, oropharyngeal, and other head and neck cancers; EMA approval followed in 2015. It elicits neutralizing antibodies against viral capsid proteins, preventing persistent infection and subsequent oncogenesis. Similarly, the HBV vaccine Recombivax HB, a recombinant vaccine containing hepatitis B surface antigen, was approved by the FDA in 1986 for immunization against HBV infection in all age groups, with EMA approval in 1987; widespread use has reduced hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) incidence by approximately 70% in vaccinated cohorts. Together, HPV and HBV vaccines are projected to avert over 1 million cancer cases worldwide annually by preventing viral infections that drive oncogenesis.
Therapeutic cancer vaccines, designed to treat existing malignancies, include several FDA- and EMA-approved products for niche indications. Sipuleucel-T (Provenge), an autologous cellular immunotherapy, was approved by the FDA in 2010 and EMA in 2013 for asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer; it involves leukapheresis to isolate antigen-presenting cells, which are activated ex vivo with prostatic acid phosphatase fused to granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) before reinfusion, extending median overall survival by 4.1 months compared to placebo. Talimogene laherparepvec (T-VEC, Imlygic), an oncolytic herpes simplex virus type 1 engineered to express GM-CSF, was approved by the FDA in 2015 and EMA in 2015 for unresectable cutaneous, subcutaneous, and nodal lesions in patients with advanced melanoma recurrent after initial surgery; it selectively replicates in tumor cells, lysing them and recruiting immune effectors via GM-CSF to enhance systemic antitumor responses. Bacillus Calmette-Gurin (BCG), a live attenuated strain of Mycobacterium bovis, was approved by the FDA in 1990 and EMA in 1995 for carcinoma in situ and high-risk non-muscle invasive bladder cancer; administered intravesically, it induces local inflammation and T-cell activation against tumor-associated antigens. Nadofaragene firadenovec-vncg (Adstiladrin), a non-replicating adenovirus vector delivering the interferon alpha-2b gene, was approved by the FDA in 2022 for BCG-unresponsive non-muscle invasive bladder cancer with carcinoma in situ with or without papillary tumors; EMA approval followed in 2023; it promotes interferon production to stimulate innate and adaptive immunity directly in the bladder mucosa. These therapeutic vaccines are limited to specific cancers, offering modest survival benefits in targeted populations without broad applicability. No new therapeutic cancer vaccines gained approval in 2024 or 2025, though some received expanded indications in combination regimens.

Clinical Trials and Recent Developments

Clinical trials for cancer vaccines have accelerated in the 2020s, with a focus on RNA-based and personalized neoantigen approaches to assess safety, immunogenicity, and preliminary efficacy across various solid tumors. Phase I and II trials have predominantly evaluated safety and dosing, often involving mRNA vaccines administered to over 100 patients per study cohort, demonstrating tolerable adverse events and induction of antigen-specific T-cell responses. For instance, phase III trials have begun testing efficacy endpoints, such as progression-free survival in personalized neoantigen vaccines for high-mutational-burden cancers like glioblastoma, where early data suggest potential delays in tumor recurrence.
In 2025, notable advancements include the phase I success of an off-the-shelf mRNA vaccine (ELI-002 2P) in a UCLA-led trial, which elicited strong T-cell responses against KRAS mutations in patients with pancreatic cancer, with no dose-limiting toxicities observed in the initial 20 participants. Similarly, Dana-Farber's modified NeoVax (NeoVax MI), incorporating multi-adjuvants, showed robust vaccine-specific immunity in a phase I trial for advanced melanoma, with enhanced CD8+ T-cell activation in all treated patients compared to prior formulations. At Mount Sinai, a multi-peptide personalized vaccine demonstrated immune responses in diverse cancers during phase I testing, including elevated interferon-gamma production in 70% of evaluable cases across breast, lung, and colorectal tumors. ESMO 2025 presentations highlighted mRNA COVID-19 vaccines' cross-training effects, where prior vaccination doubled the likelihood of tumor elimination when combined with checkpoint inhibitors in metastatic settings.
Combination strategies have emerged as a key focus, pairing vaccines with PD-1 inhibitors to overcome immunosuppressive tumor microenvironments. In KEYNOTE-series trials, such as KEYNOTE-942 evaluating mRNA-4157 with pembrolizumab, adjuvant therapy in resected melanoma yielded recurrence-free survival rates up to 74.8% at 2.5 years, with objective response rates reaching approximately 40% in responsive subgroups. RNA vaccines are under investigation in over 120 global trials targeting ovarian and lung cancers, often in combination regimens that amplify neoantigen presentation and checkpoint blockade.
Trial outcomes have reported objective response rates of 15-50% among responders, particularly in tumors with high tumor mutational burden, which serves as a biomarker predicting vaccine efficacy by correlating with neoantigen load. Progression-free survival extensions of 3-12 months have been observed in phase II settings, such as with PDC*lung01 vaccine plus anti-PD-1 therapy in non-small cell lung cancer, where median PFS reached 9 months. As of 2025, more than 120 RNA-based cancer vaccine trials are active worldwide, registered on ClinicalTrials.gov, with the United States and China leading in trial initiation and enrollment.
Dendritic cell (DC)-based vaccines continue to advance in clinical trials, with over 100 active studies worldwide as of 2025. Recent phase II trials have demonstrated improved survival outcomes in glioblastoma patients receiving personalized DC vaccines alongside standard therapy, such as extended overall survival compared to historical controls. Neoantigen-based DC vaccines have shown feasibility, safety, and preliminary efficacy in various solid tumors, including response rates up to 40% when combined with checkpoint inhibitors in melanoma. For instance, a double-blind randomized phase II trial of AV-OVA-1, an autologous DC vaccine loaded with tumor antigens, reported enhanced immune responses and progression-free survival benefits in glioblastoma.

Abandoned or Failed Approaches

Early efforts in cancer vaccine development included whole-cell vaccines using autologous tumor cells, particularly in trials during the 1990s, which were largely abandoned due to poor standardization of antigen presentation and immunosuppressive factors in the tumor lysates, resulting in low immune response rates often below 10%. For instance, Canvaxin, an allogeneic whole-cell melanoma vaccine, failed in phase III trials (NCT00052156) because of inconsistent efficacy and inability to demonstrate survival benefits in advanced patients. Similarly, GVAX pancreas, involving irradiated autologous tumor cells combined with GM-CSF, was discontinued after phase III failure (NCT00052130) owing to inadequate clinical outcomes and manufacturing inconsistencies.
Peptide-based vaccines also faced significant setbacks, exemplified by GlaxoSmithKline's MAGE-A3 immunotherapeutic for non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). The MAGRIT phase III trial (NCT00480025), reported in 2016 but concluding data from 2015, randomized 2,312 patients post-resection to receive MAGE-A3 with AS15 adjuvant or placebo, yet showed no improvement in disease-free survival (median 60.5 vs. 57.9 months; HR 1.02, p=0.74), leading to program termination. This failure highlighted limitations in targeting shared tumor-associated antigens like MAGE-A3, which failed to elicit durable antitumor responses in the adjuvant setting.
Pre-2010 dendritic cell (DC) vaccine trials, akin to those preceding sipuleucel-T's approval, were often discontinued due to manufacturing complexity in autologous DC production, including variability in cell maturation and antigen loading that compromised scalability and reproducibility. A phase III trial of autologous peptide-pulsed DCs in metastatic melanoma (reported 2006) demonstrated no significant survival benefit, contributing to abandonment of similar personalized approaches amid high costs and logistical challenges. Viral vector strategies in the 2000s, such as adenovirus-based vaccines for prostate cancer, encountered safety hurdles; early trials like those using Ad5-PSA vectors reported dose-limiting adverse events including fever and coagulopathy at high doses (610^12 vp), prompting halts in escalation and shifts away from unmodified vectors.31660-4)
Common reasons for these failures included inadequate adjuvants, such as alum or early GM-CSF formulations, which provided weak immune stimulation and failed to overcome tumor-induced tolerance. Tumor immune evasion mechanisms, notably adaptive PD-L1 upregulation in response to vaccine-induced IFN from T cells, dampened effector responses in the tumor microenvironment, as observed in preclinical models where vaccines like TEGVAX slowed but did not regress tumors without checkpoint blockade. Patient selection errors, particularly enrolling those with advanced disease stages, exacerbated outcomes by introducing heavy immunosuppression and reduced T-cell functionality in the tumor bed.
These historical setbacks prompted a strategic shift post-2015 toward combination therapies integrating vaccines with checkpoint inhibitors and a focus on neoantigens for greater specificity and immunogenicity. In the 2020s mRNA vaccine era, no major programs have been abandoned, though ongoing monitoring addresses personalization scalability issues like rapid production and stability for individualized neoantigen formulations.

Challenges and Future Directions

Hypothesized Problems

The tumor microenvironment (TME) presents significant biological barriers to cancer vaccine efficacy by fostering an immunosuppressive milieu that dampens vaccine-induced immune responses. Regulatory T cells (Tregs) and myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) are key contributors, as they inhibit T cell activation and proliferation through mechanisms such as cytokine secretion and arginase activity, thereby blunting antitumor immunity elicited by vaccines. For instance, MDSCs accumulate in the TME and suppress CD8+ T cell function, reducing the potential for vaccines to generate durable cytotoxic responses.
Another biological hurdle is the emergence of antigen loss variants, where tumor cells downregulate or mutate targeted antigens, allowing immune escape from vaccine-induced T cells. This phenomenon is particularly problematic in heterogeneous tumors, as selective pressure from vaccination can favor the survival of antigen-negative subclones, leading to relapse. In "cold" tumors like pancreatic cancer, which exhibit low neoantigen loads due to minimal mutational burden, vaccines struggle to prime sufficient T cell responses, as there are fewer immunogenic targets to exploit.
Additionally, central tolerance to self-antigens hampers vaccine efficacy, as the immune system deletes or anergizes high-affinity T cells reactive to tumor-associated self-antigens during thymic development, resulting in weak or absent responses to vaccines targeting these epitopes.
Practical challenges further complicate cancer vaccine implementation, including tumor heterogeneity, which necessitates multi-antigen cocktails to cover diverse antigenic profiles across tumor subclones and prevent escape. Personalized vaccines, tailored to individual neoantigens, face manufacturing delays due to the need for tumor sequencing, antigen prediction, and custom production, often spanning several weeks to months. High costs associated with these bespoke processes, including advanced sequencing and GMP-compliant synthesis, restrict access in low-resource settings, exacerbating global disparities in cancer immunotherapy.
As of 2025, clinical data indicate that cancer vaccines achieve durable responses in only 20-30% of patients, influenced by factors such as gut microbiome composition, which modulates systemic immunity and vaccine responsiveness, and patient comorbidities that impair immune vigor.

Recommendations and Ongoing Research

The National Cancer Institute (NCI) and Food and Drug Administration (FDA) recommend incorporating immune monitoring endpoints, such as T-cell response assays and cytokine profiling, into cancer vaccine clinical trial designs to better assess immunogenicity and correlate it with clinical outcomes. These guidelines emphasize prioritizing high-burden cancers like lung and breast cancer in trial enrollment, given their global incidence rates exceeding 2.2 million and 2.3 million new cases annually, respectively, to maximize public health impact. Additionally, integrating artificial intelligence (AI) for antigen prediction is advised to accelerate personalization, as AI models can analyze multi-omics data to improve neoantigen identification in low-tumor-mutation-burden cancers like pancreatic, with ongoing advancements in accuracy.
Key strategies for advancing cancer vaccines include combining them with immunotherapies, such as pairing neoantigen vaccines with chimeric antigen receptor T-cell (CAR-T) therapy to enhance T-cell persistence and broaden epitope coverage in solid tumors. Universal vaccines targeting shared neoantigens from recurrent driver mutations (e.g., KRAS in ~20-40% of colorectal and some gastric cancers) offer an off-the-shelf alternative, though coverage remains limited compared to personalized approaches. To promote global equity, scalable mRNA platforms are recommended, leveraging expanded manufacturing capacities that have increased significantly since 2020for instance, Moderna's U.S. onshoring of drug product capabilities announced in November 2025enabling low-cost distribution to low- and middle-income countries where cancer mortality is disproportionately high.
Looking toward 2025-2030, research priorities include AI-driven vaccine designs that predict patient responses by integrating genomic and proteomic data, with models like imNEO achieving superior neoantigen prioritization over traditional methods. Prophylactic neoantigen vaccines for high-risk individuals, such as smokers for lung cancer via initiatives like LungVaxwhich entered phase 1 trials in late 2025 aiming to intercept precancerous lesions by inducing preemptive immune surveillancehold promise. LungVax employs viral vector vaccine technology, where DNA sequences are inserted into a harmless chimpanzee adenovirus as a carrier to deliver genetic instructions into human cells; these cells then produce lung cancer-related neoantigens, which are abnormal proteins arising from DNA mutations in precancerous cells, enabling the immune system to recognize these as red flags and attack the precancerous cells to prevent tumor development. Longitudinal studies following post-2025 trials are essential to evaluate response durability, with early data indicating sustained T-cell memory beyond five years in melanoma patients receiving mRNA vaccines.
Ongoing research in dendritic cell (DC)-based cancer vaccines emphasizes refinements such as targeting specific DC subsets, including conventional dendritic cells type 1 (cDC1), to enhance antigen cross-presentation, and integrating DC vaccines with other immunotherapies like checkpoint inhibitors to overcome tumor immunosuppression. A 2025 review underscores the promise of these strategies for improving clinical outcomes in various solid tumors. For instance, a phase 2 trial published in September 2025 reported improved survival rates in patients with newly diagnosed glioblastoma when personalized DC vaccines were added to standard therapy.
Expert consensus from the American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) in 2025 emphasizes increasing enrollment from diverse populations in cancer clinical trials to address disparities, as lower vaccination adherence in underrepresented groups correlates with socioeconomic factors. Increased funding is urged for off-the-shelf vaccine options, with projections estimating market growth to $14 billion by 2030, supported by grants prioritizing accessible platforms.
