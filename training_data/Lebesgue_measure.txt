 The Lebesgue measure is a mathematical construct that assigns a non-negative real number, representing a generalized notion of length, area, or volume, to certain subsets of Euclidean space Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, providing a rigorous foundation for integration that extends beyond the limitations of the Riemann integral. Introduced by French mathematician Henri Lebesgue in his 1902 doctoral thesis "Intgrale, longueur, aire," it addresses key shortcomings in earlier integration theories by focusing on the range of functions rather than their domain partitions, enabling the integration of a broader class of functions, including those that are discontinuous on sets of positive measure. Lebesgue measure is defined initially on rectangles as the product of their side lengths, m(i=1n)=i=1n(biai)m(\prod_{i=1}^n ) = \prod_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)m(i=1n)=i=1n(biai), and extended to more general sets via the Lebesgue outer measure, which is the infimum of the total lengths of countable coverings by open intervals.
A set ERnE \subseteq \mathbb{R}^nERn is Lebesgue measurable if it satisfies Carathodory's criterion: for any set AAA, the outer measure of AAA equals the sum of the outer measures of AEA \cap EAE and AEcA \cap E^cAEc. The Lebesgue measure on measurable sets is then the restriction of this outer measure, forming a complete, translation-invariant, (/page/Sigma)(/page/Sigma)(/page/Sigma)-additive measure on the (/page/Sigma)(/page/Sigma)(/page/Sigma)-algebra of Lebesgue measurable sets, which includes all Borel sets and many non-Borel sets as well. Key properties include assigning measure zero to points, countable sets, and the Cantor set, while ensuring that the measure of the unit interval is 1, aligning with intuitive notions of length. This framework underpins modern real analysis, probability theory, and functional analysis, facilitating theorems like the monotone convergence theorem and dominated convergence theorem that fail for Riemann integration.
Lebesgue's innovation arose from early 20th-century challenges in Fourier analysis and the study of pathological functions, such as those arising from Dirichlet's examples of non-Riemann-integrable functions on dense sets. In his 1902 thesis publication, Lebesgue proved equivalences like his criterion for Riemann integrability: a bounded function on is Riemann integrable if and only if its set of discontinuities has Lebesgue measure zero. Introduction and Intuition

Historical Context

The development of the Lebesgue measure emerged in the early 20th century as part of Henri Lebesgue's efforts to extend the scope of integration beyond the limitations of existing theories. In his 1902 doctoral thesis, titled Intgrale, longueur, aire, Lebesgue introduced a new framework for measuring sets and integrating functions, defining measure in terms of coverings and establishing the foundations for what would become known as Lebesgue measure. This work, completed when Lebesgue was 27 years old, built upon set-theoretic ideas to assign measures to a broader class of subsets of the real line, addressing gaps in prior geometric and analytic approaches.
Lebesgue's innovations were heavily influenced by earlier mathematicians grappling with the shortcomings of Riemann integration, particularly its struggles with discontinuous functions. Bernhard Riemann's 1854 definition of the integral, while effective for continuous functions, failed to handle many discontinuous cases adequately, prompting refinements by others. Giuseppe Peano and Camille Jordan developed the Peano-Jordan measure in the late 19th century, providing a notion of content for bounded sets through inner and outer approximations, but it remained finitely additive and limited to Jordan-measurable sets without pathological features. Additionally, mile Borel's 1898 work on measure theory introduced countable additivity for certain sets, influencing Lebesgue to adopt this property while extending it to more general domains.
Lebesgue aimed to create a measure capable of handling unbounded functions and sets exhibiting pathological properties, such as those arising from Cantor's set theory, which defied intuitive notions of length or area. He first outlined key ideas in a 1901 paper, with the full theory detailed in his 1902 thesis published in Annali di Matematica Pura ed Applicata. A subsequent book, Leons sur l'intgration et la recherche des fonctions primitives (1904), further disseminated his ideas. By the 1920s, Lebesgue measure had gained widespread adoption in functional analysis, underpinning developments like Frigyes Riesz's work on Hilbert spaces and Stefan Banach's abstract integration theories, solidifying its role in modern mathematics.

Motivations from Riemann Integration

The Riemann integral, introduced by Bernhard Riemann in the mid-19th century, is fundamentally defined for bounded functions on closed and bounded intervals, relying on partitions of the domain into subintervals and approximating the area under the curve via upper and lower sums. This approach excels for continuous functions, where uniform continuity ensures the upper and lower sums converge to the same value, but it encounters significant limitations when dealing with functions exhibiting substantial discontinuities. Specifically, a function is Riemann integrable only if the set of its discontinuities has measure zero in a suitable sense, a criterion later formalized by Lebesgue, but the Riemann framework lacks a prior notion of measure to handle pathological cases effectively.
A prime example illustrating these shortcomings is the Dirichlet function, defined on as f(x)=1f(x) = 1f(x)=1 if xxx is rational and f(x)=0f(x) = 0f(x)=0 if xxx is irrational. This function is discontinuous at every point in because rational and irrational numbers are dense in the reals, making it impossible for the function to approach a limit anywhere. Consequently, for any partition of , the upper Riemann sum equals 1 (as each subinterval contains rationals) while the lower Riemann sum equals 0 (as each contains irrationals), so the upper and lower integrals differ, rendering the function non-Riemann integrable. The Dirichlet function's characteristic highlights the need to assign a "length" or measure to sets like the rationals in , which are dense yet countable and intuitively possess zero length, but whose indicator function defies Riemann integration due to the integral's dependence on interval-based approximations rather than set properties.
This motivates extending the concept of length beyond simple intervals to arbitrary subsets of the real line, using outer approximationssuch as covering sets with countable unions of intervals whose total length can be made arbitrarily smallto define a measure that captures the "size" of sets independently of their topological density. For instance, the rational numbers in can be enumerated and covered by disjoint intervals around each point with total length less than any >0\epsilon > 0>0, suggesting a measure of zero, which allows integration over such sets in a way Riemann cannot. Another illustrative case is Thomae's function (or the popcorn function), defined on as t(x)=1/qt(x) = 1/qt(x)=1/q if x=p/qx = p/qx=p/q in lowest terms with q>0q > 0q>0, and t(x)=0t(x) = 0t(x)=0 if xxx is irrational. While discontinuous precisely at the rationals (a countable set), Thomae's function is Riemann integrable with integral 0, as the discontinuities form a set of "zero content" in the Riemann sense, where upper sums can be controlled by excluding small intervals around rationals. However, this ad hoc handling underscores the limitations of Riemann integration, prompting the development of a systematic measure theory to precisely quantify such sets of zero measure and enable integration for a broader class of functions.

Consider the real numbers modulo the rationals, where two reals xxx and yyy are equivalent if xyQx - y \in \mathbb{Q}xyQ. This partitions R\mathbb{R}R into uncountably many equivalence classes. Using the axiom of choice, select one representative from each equivalence class that intersects the interval [0,1)[0, 1)[0,1); the resulting set V[0,1)V \subset [0, 1)V[0,1) is the Vitali set.

The construction of the Vitali set relies on the axiom of choice to select representatives from the uncountable family of equivalence classes. Without the axiom of choice, it is consistent that every set of reals is Lebesgue measurable. Specifically, in Solovay's model of ZF set theory plus the axiom of dependent choices (assuming the existence of an inaccessible cardinal), every set of reals has a Lebesgue measure. This shows that the existence of non-measurable sets is equivalent to a weak form of the axiom of choice over ZF.
In higher dimensions, the Banach-Tarski paradox provides another illustration of non-measurable sets. Using the axiom of choice, the unit ball in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 can be partitioned into finitely many non-measurable pieces that can be rigidly reassembled into two unit balls, implying the existence of non-measurable subsets whose "volumes" defy additivity. This phenomenon extends to Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn for n3n \geq 3n3, highlighting limitations of measure in dimensions where free groups act non-trivially.

Relations to Other Concepts

Comparison with Jordan Measure

The Jordan measure, also known as Jordan content, provides a notion of size for bounded subsets of Euclidean space Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn by approximating them with finite unions of rectangles. Specifically, for a bounded set ARnA \subset \mathbb{R}^nARn, the outer Jordan measure mJ(A)m_J^*(A)mJ(A) is defined as the infimum of the total volume of finite unions of closed rectangles that cover AAA, while the inner Jordan measure mJ(A)m_J(A)mJ(A) is the supremum of the total volume of finite unions of closed rectangles contained within AAA. A set AAA is Jordan measurable if mJ(A)=mJ(A)m_J^*(A) = m_J(A)mJ(A)=mJ(A), in which case its Jordan measure is this common value.
Despite its intuitive appeal, the Jordan measure has significant limitations that restrict its utility in advanced analysis. It is only finitely additive, meaning that for a finite disjoint union of Jordan measurable sets, the measure of the union equals the sum of the measures, but it fails to be countably additive over countable disjoint unions. For instance, the set of rational numbers in has outer Jordan measure 1 (as it can be covered by the interval itself) but inner Jordan measure 0 (since no finite union of rectangles with positive volume can fit inside without including irrationals), rendering it non-Jordan measurable. Similarly, sets with boundaries of positive Lebesgue measure, such as certain modifications of the Cantor set, may not be Jordan measurable, highlighting how the reliance on finite approximations misses pathological sets with intricate frontiers.
In contrast, the Lebesgue measure addresses these shortcomings by generalizing the Jordan approach through countable coverings and outer measure constructions, achieving countable additivity and applicability to unbounded sets. Every Jordan measurable set is Lebesgue measurable, and the two measures coincide on such sets, establishing the Lebesgue measure as a proper extension of the Jordan measure. This inclusion ensures that all sets amenable to finite rectangular approximations retain their assigned sizes under the broader Lebesgue framework, while enabling the measurement of a vastly larger class of sets, including those requiring infinite decompositions.
Historically, the Jordan measure emerged from Camille Jordan's work in the late 19th century, predating Lebesgue's development but confined to "regular" sets with well-behaved boundaries due to its finite additivity. Introduced in Jordan's Cours d'analyse de l'cole Polytechnique first published between 1882 and 1887, it represented an early attempt to rigorize notions of content inspired by classical geometry, yet its restrictions underscored the need for a more robust theory to handle the complexities arising in modern analysis.

The Lebesgue integral is constructed using the Lebesgue measure to integrate measurable functions over measurable sets. For general non-negative measurable functions fff, the integral fd\int f \, d\mufd is the supremum of integrals of simple functions less than or equal to fff.
