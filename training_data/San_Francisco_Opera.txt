 The San Francisco Opera is a professional opera company founded in 1923 by Italian conductor Gaetano Merola in San Francisco, California, making it one of the oldest continuously operating opera organizations in the United States. It stages its primary performances at the War Memorial Opera House, a 3,146-seat venue inaugurated in 1932 during the Great Depression through community donations, marking the first such opera house in America funded entirely by public contributions. As the second-largest opera company in North America by budget and scale, it has presented full seasons of grand opera, emphasizing both classic repertoire and contemporary works.
Under Merola's three-decade leadership until his death in 1953, the company established itself as the West Coast's premier opera presenter, conducting annual tours and building ties with local philanthropists and Italian-American communities to sustain operations amid economic challenges. Successors like Kurt Herbert Adler expanded the season's length and artistic scope, introducing the Merola Opera Program in 1957 to train emerging artistsa initiative that remains one of the world's most prestigious young artist development effortsand staging ambitious cycles such as Wagner's Der Ring des Nibelungen in 1935. The company has commissioned and premiered numerous contemporary operas, including Jake Heggie's Dead Man Walking in 2000, which has since become one of the most performed new American operas globally.
Innovations such as free outdoor simulcasts since 2006 have broadened public access, drawing tens of thousands annually to events like Opera in the Park, while the affiliated San Francisco Opera Center has nurtured talent since 1982. Recent efforts include world premieres like the forthcoming The Monkey King in 2025, reflecting ongoing commitment to new compositions amid financial pressures facing nonprofit arts institutions.

History

Founding and Gaetano Merola Era (19231953)

The San Francisco Opera was established in 1923 by Gaetano Merola (18811953), a Neapolitan conductor who had visited the city since 1906 with touring companies and recognized the potential for a permanent resident ensemble. Merola noted that San Francisco had spent $425,000 on visiting opera troupes in the preceding year, prompting him to advocate for a locally funded company independent of transient performers. He cultivated support from philanthropists, the Italian-American community, and business leaders to finance operations, marking a shift toward community-driven opera production on the West Coast.
The company's debut occurred on September 26, 1923, at the Civic Auditorium with Giacomo Puccini's La bohme, conducted by Merola and starring soprano Queena Mario as Mim and tenor Giovanni Martinelli as Rodolfo. Productions relied on singers' personal costumes, community-donated props, an orchestra assembled from the San Francisco Symphony, and a chorus trained by Merola. The inaugural season compressed ten operas into thirteen days, emphasizing Italian repertory staples like Verdi's Aida and Rigoletto alongside works by Puccini and Bizet.
In 1932, the Opera relocated to the War Memorial Opera House, which opened on October 15 with Puccini's Tosca; constructed as a World War I veterans' memorial amid the Great Depression, the venue was financed solely through public donations following an eight-year campaign led by Merola. His leadership introduced annual tours starting with Los Angeles in 1924continuing there until 1965and extending to San Diego and Seattle, positioning the company as the West Coast's dominant opera force. Seasons expanded in length and scope, attracting international artists and culminating in the 1935 staging of Richard Wagner's Der Ring des Nibelungen cycle, which bolstered the organization's national reputation.
Merola, who conducted extensively and prioritized Italian opera, guided the company through adversities including the 1906 earthquake, the Spanish Flu, both world wars, and economic downturns until his death on August 30, 1953, during a Madama Butterfly performance at the Stern Grove Festival. Over three decades, his efforts transformed San Francisco Opera into a cornerstone of American musical institutions, emphasizing fiscal prudence, artistic ambition, and regional outreach without reliance on government subsidies.

Kurt Herbert Adler Era (19531981)

Kurt Herbert Adler, an Austrian-born conductor who had joined the San Francisco Opera as chorus master in 1943, was appointed artistic director upon Gaetano Merola's death in 1953 and elevated to general director in 1957, a position he held until his retirement in 1981. Under Adler's autocratic yet visionary leadership, the company evolved from a regional fall-season ensemble into a major international opera house, emphasizing high artistic standards, innovative productions, and the cultivation of emerging talent.
Adler expanded the opera's programming by doubling the season's length, introducing a Spring Opera series in 1971 for chamber works and experimental repertoire, and fostering collaborations with renowned directors such as Jean-Pierre Ponnelle, whose bold stagings redefined traditional operas. The company achieved financial growth, with the annual budget reaching $7 million by 1978 amid consistently sold-out performances, reflecting increased audience engagement and institutional prestige. Notable artistic milestones included U.S. debuts of luminaries like sopranos Leontyne Price, Elisabeth Schwarzkopf, and Birgit Nilsson, as well as tenor Mario del Monaco, which elevated the company's global profile. Key premieres encompassed the U.S. premiere of Richard Strauss's Die Frau ohne Schatten in 1977, alongside other American and world premieres that broadened the repertoire beyond standard works.
In 1957, Adler established the Merola Opera Program, the nation's first comprehensive training initiative for young professional opera singers, conductors, and coaches, which continues to nurture talent through performances, masterclasses, and mentorship. He also founded the San Francisco Opera Center and the Adler Fellowship Program to support advanced artist development, ensuring a pipeline of performers for mainstage productions. Adler's emphasis on discipline and excellence, often enforced through rigorous auditions and demanding rehearsals, solidified the opera's reputation, though his imperious style occasionally drew internal tensions among staff and board members. By 1981, these initiatives had positioned the San Francisco Opera as a leader in American lyric theater, capable of attracting top international artists and mounting ambitious new works.

Terence McEwen and Lotfi Mansouri Eras (19822001)

Terence McEwen, a Canadian-born executive previously serving as vice president of London Records, was appointed general director of the San Francisco Opera in 1982 as the hand-picked successor to Kurt Herbert Adler. His tenure emphasized vocal excellence and artist development amid economic challenges, leading to the creation of the San Francisco Opera Center in 1982 to consolidate and administer the company's education and training programs, including the Merola Opera Program and Adler Fellowship. A major artistic milestone was the 1985 premiere of a new production of Wagner's Der Ring des Nibelungen, directed by Nikolaus Lehnhoff with sets by John Conklin, which ran over four seasons and featured conductors such as Donald Runnicles. McEwen stepped down in 1988 after six years, during which the company maintained its focus on core operatic repertoire while navigating fiscal constraints.
Lotfi Mansouri, an Iranian-born stage director who had debuted at the San Francisco Opera in 1963 and previously led the Canadian Opera Company, succeeded McEwen as general director from 1988 to 2001. Under his leadership, the company broadened its programming to include more contemporary and lesser-performed works, such as the 2000 world premiere of Jake Heggie's Dead Man Walking, which drew significant attention for its adaptation of Sister Helen Prejean's memoir and starred Patricia Racette and Susan Graham. Mansouri championed the use of supertitles to enhance audience accessibility, building on earlier implementations to make performances more inclusive for non-native speakers and newcomers. His era also saw infrastructural advancements, including oversight of renovations to the War Memorial Opera House, and initiatives like the Pacific Visions program to foster new operatic works. 
The combined McEwen-Mansouri period marked a transition from Adler's autocratic style to more collaborative management, with sustained emphasis on high-caliber international castssuch as Plcido Domingo and Leontyne Price in key revivalswhile addressing operational efficiencies amid fluctuating budgets and attendance. By 2001, the company had solidified its reputation for innovative stagings and talent cultivation, setting the stage for further evolution despite periodic financial strains reported in arts coverage of the time.

Pamela Rosenberg and David Gockley Eras (20012016)

Pamela Rosenberg served as general director of the San Francisco Opera from 2001 to 2006, becoming the company's first female leader in that role. Her tenure emphasized ambitious and innovative programming influenced by her European experience, particularly from directing the Stuttgart Opera. Key highlights included the world premiere of John Adams' Doctor Atomic in 2005 and the first North American production of Olivier Messiaen's Saint Franois d'Assise. However, her era faced significant financial challenges, with a reported $7.7 million operating deficit in fiscal year 2002 on a budget of approximately $60 million, followed by projected shortfalls leading to reduced performances and budgetary cuts. These issues contributed to her relatively short tenure, described by observers as dramatic and the briefest in the company's history up to that point. 
David Gockley succeeded Rosenberg as general director on January 1, 2006, serving until 2016 after a prior 33-year leadership at Houston Grand Opera. Under Gockley, the company stabilized financially following the prior deficits and implemented technological innovations to expand accessibility, including free live simulcasts starting in 2006 at Civic Center Plaza (drawing 6,000 attendees initially) and later at Oracle Park (over 30,000). He established the Koret-Taube Media Suite, the first permanent high-definition video production facility in an American opera house, supporting simulcasts, cinema broadcasts, and recordings. Gockley appointed Nicola Luisotti as music director and prioritized new works, commissioning or co-commissioning 11 operas and presenting nine world premieres during his decade, such as Marco Tutino's Two Women (2015). His programming facilitated 282 artist role debuts, blending core repertoire with contemporary American compositions to build audiences. Gockley's leadership culminated in the company's transition to Matthew Shilvock on August 1, 2016, with Gockley receiving the San Francisco Opera Medal upon retirement. 

Matthew Shilvock Era (2016present)

Matthew Shilvock assumed the role of Tad and Dianne Taube General Director of the San Francisco Opera on August 1, 2016, succeeding his mentor David Gockley after serving as associate general director since 2010. In this capacity, Shilvock oversees all artistic programming, business operations, and initiatives such as the company's repertory season and young artist training programs. His early priorities included revitalizing core repertory with new productions of Verdi and Wagner operas, reflecting a commitment to mounting ambitious stagings of large-scale works amid rising operational costs.
Under Shilvock's leadership, the company has emphasized innovative programming while navigating financial pressures. Notable efforts include the 202526 season's new production of Wagner's Parsifal, marking the opera's return to the stage after 25 years and fulfilling one of Shilvock's inaugural goals; this season, the company's 103rd, opens on September 5, 2025, with Richard Strauss's Elektra under music director Eun Sun Kim and features the 25th anniversary celebration of Jake Heggie's Dead Man Walking. Shilvock has also prioritized audience diversification through new stories and voices, including commissions and co-productions aimed at broader appeal, though specific world premieres during his tenure remain limited compared to prior eras.
Financially, the era has been marked by challenges, with ticket sales comprising only 16% of revenue in recent yearsdown from 60% six decades priorprompting heavy reliance on philanthropy, including targeted outreach to Asian American donors and board members. The annual budget for an eight-opera season hovers in the mid-$90 million range, supported by endowments and contributions, as evidenced by audited results showing operational deficits offset by fundraising. In September 2025, performers secured a two-year union contract with wage increases, enhanced benefits, and workplace protections, averting potential labor disruptions amid post-pandemic recovery. Shilvock's tenure coincides with broader industry trends of declining earned income, necessitating structural adaptations like reduced matinee performances and intensified donor engagement.

Organization and Operations

Venue and Facilities

The San Francisco Opera's primary venue is the War Memorial Opera House, located at 301 Van Ness Avenue in San Francisco, California. Opened on October 15, 1932, as a memorial to World War I veterans, the Beaux-Arts structure was designed by architect Arthur Brown Jr. and features a 3,146-seat auditorium. The opera house has served as the company's home since its inception, hosting performances with a proscenium opening measuring 52 feet wide and a stage depth accommodating standard operatic productions.
The venue underwent significant seismic retrofitting following the 1989 Loma Prieta earthquake, which included upgrades to structural integrity, acoustics, and audience comfort. Further renovations completed in 2021 enhanced seating with increased legroom, higher cushions, narrower armrests for a wider feel, and integrated cupholders, while preserving the historic interior. These improvements integrate modern technology, such as advanced lighting and sound systems, with the original Old World theater ambiance, supporting the opera's technical requirements for elaborate sets and orchestras.
Facilities include a grand foyer, multiple levels of seating (orchestra, boxes, dress circle, and balcony), and orchestra pit configurations adjustable for different productions. Backstage areas support comprehensive operations, with a remote 25,000-square-foot costume shop for fabrication and storage. The venue's design ensures optimal sightlines and acoustics, contributing to its status as a California Historical Landmark.

Orchestra, Chorus, and Training Programs

The San Francisco Opera Orchestra serves as the resident ensemble for all mainstage productions at the War Memorial Opera House, providing instrumental accompaniment under the direction of the company's Music Director. Eun Sun Kim, appointed Music Director in fall 2021 as the first woman to hold the position at a major American opera company, leads the orchestra, which includes principal players such as Concertmaster Kay Stern, Associate Concertmaster Maya Cohon, and section principals like Viola Principal CarlaMaria Rodrigues. The musicians, represented by their own organization, perform during the opera's season of approximately nine productions, with recent contract negotiations addressing wage increases through July 2026 amid operational challenges.
The San Francisco Opera Chorus, directed by John Keene since January 2022, comprises a regular ensemble of 48 singers26 men and 22 womensupplemented by an extra chorus that expands to over 100 members for productions requiring larger forces, such as grand operas with extensive crowd scenes. Keene, formerly head of music and chorus master at Seattle Opera, oversees preparation, blending vocal precision with dramatic integration in collaboration with the Music Director and production staff; an associate chorus master, Fabrizio Corona, provides additional support. Auditions for chorus positions emphasize at least four years of classical voice training, strong sight-reading in music and languages, and rapid memorization, with compensation structured by rehearsal and performance rates for extras alongside weekly salaries for regulars.
San Francisco Opera invests in artist development through two flagship training programs: the Merola Opera Program and the Adler Fellowship Program, both focused on cultivating professional skills for opera's next generation. The Merola Opera Program, operational since the company's early history and widely recognized as a leading incubator, offers a competitive summer residency for approximately 20-30 young singers, coaches, accompanists, and stage directors, featuring intensive masterclasses, language coaching, staged scenes, and public performances to build technical and interpretive proficiency.
Established in 1977 initially as the San Francisco Opera/Affiliate Artists program, the Adler Fellowship provides a two-year, performance-oriented residency for 10-12 advanced artists, often Merola alumni, emphasizing individualized training in vocal technique, diction across multiple languages, acting, movement, and career preparation. Fellows participate in mainstage roles, Opera Center recitals, and community engagements, with recent cohorts announced in November 2024 receiving stipends and grants to support transition to international careers; the program prioritizes empirical skill-building over ideological framing, drawing from auditions and prior achievements.

Programming and Repertoire

Core Repertoire and Premieres

The San Francisco Opera's core repertoire draws heavily from the 19th-century Italian operatic canon, particularly works by Giuseppe Verdi and Giacomo Puccini, which have anchored seasons since the company's inception in 1923. These staples, including Rigoletto (Verdi, 1851), La Bohme (Puccini, 1896), and Tosca (Puccini, 1900), recur frequently due to their melodic accessibility, dramatic intensity, and proven audience appeal, with La Bohme staged in multiple productions across nearly every decade and cited as one of the most enduringly popular operas globally. French grand opra, such as Bizet's Carmen (1875), and German Romantic works by Richard Wagner and Richard Strauss, like Die Walkre and Elektra, also feature prominently, balancing vocal virtuosity with orchestral depth. This emphasis reflects founder Gaetano Merola's Italian roots and the company's aim to sustain high attendance through familiar masterworks amid experimental programming.
Complementing these classics, the San Francisco Opera has championed innovation through commissions and premieres, presenting over 20 world premieres since 1961 and numerous U.S. debuts of contemporary and rediscovered scores. Early efforts under Kurt Herbert Adler introduced American audiences to post-war European works, such as Poulenc's Dialogues des Carmlites (U.S. premiere, September 20, 1957) and Strauss's Die Frau ohne Schatten (U.S. premiere, 1959).

Financial Status and Challenges

Budget, Funding, and Economic Pressures

The San Francisco Opera Association maintains an operating budget with expenses of $87.5 million for the fiscal year ended July 31, 2024, down slightly from $92.9 million the prior year. Operating revenues totaled $36.3 million, including $15.4 million from ticket sales and $1.9 million from education program fees, while contributed income and endowment transfers provided critical support exceeding $73 million in gross contributions before expenses. Private donations from individuals, corporations, foundations, and bequests form the backbone of funding, accounting for approximately 75% of the operating budget, supplemented by a donor-restricted endowment valued at $309 million as of July 31, 2024. Government grants, such as those from the National Endowment for the Arts and San Francisco's Grants for the Arts program, contribute modestly but are not primary sources.
Structural operating deficits remain a persistent feature, reaching $51.2 million in fiscal 2024 after endowment investment transfers of $16.9 million, with similar gaps of $53.4 million in 2023 covered through draws on long-term funds and one-time gifts. These shortfalls reflect a model where earned income from performances covers only about 18% of costs, necessitating reliance on philanthropic support amid high fixed expenses for productions, orchestra, and facilities at the War Memorial Opera House.
Economic pressures have intensified post-pandemic, with ticket sales dropping to 116,719 for the 2023-24 season from 128,437 in the pre-COVID full season, signaling audience contraction amid inflation, elevated San Francisco living costs, and donor fatigue in a competitive arts landscape. Seasonal cash flow strains prompt use of a $15 million line of credit maturing July 31, 2025, while broader industry trendssuch as rising labor and production costs under union contractsexacerbate vulnerabilities, prompting budget reductions to $87 million for 2025-26. The company's total assets of $362 million provide a buffer, but sustained deficits risk eroding endowment principal if philanthropic inflows weaken further.

Labor Relations and Operational Adjustments

In response to post-pandemic financial strains, the San Francisco Opera negotiated modifications to its collective bargaining agreements with musicians during the COVID-19 shutdown, including a 50% salary reduction for the remaining term to preserve operations amid canceled seasons. These adjustments allowed the company to retain its orchestra without layoffs, prioritizing long-term viability over immediate compensation demands, though they drew criticism from unions for eroding pre-crisis wage structures.
Tensions resurfaced in late 2024 and early 2025 as contracts with the American Federation of Musicians (AFM) Local 6 and the American Guild of Musical Artists (AGMA) approached expiration, with musicians advocating for restored pay and improved benefits amid rising living costs in San Francisco. Negotiations with AFM, covering the orchestra, extended over six months before ratification on June 26, 2025, of a two-year deal effective August 1, 2024, through July 31, 2026, featuring annual wage increases averaging 3-4%, enhanced retirement contributions, and revised health care provisions to address affordability gaps. 
AGMA talks, representing chorus, principal artists, and stage personnel, spanned nine months and concluded with ratification on August 25, 2025, of a two-year contract retroactive to March 1, 2025, and extending to February 28, 2027. The agreement incorporated pay raises totaling approximately 7% over the term, new wellness benefits, fortified anti-harassment protocols, and operational flexibilities such as adjusted audition formats and scheduling to reduce artist burnout while enabling cost controls for management. Unlike concurrent disputes at the San Francisco Symphony, which authorized strikes, these pacts averted work stoppages at the Opera, reflecting management's emphasis on collaborative bargaining to sustain a full 2025-2026 season of nine mainstage productions. 
Earlier labor frictions, such as a 2023 federal lawsuit by former employee Leighland Hooks alleging violations of labor practices, and a 2004 union claim of breached grievance procedures, highlight recurring challenges in balancing artistic labor rights with fiscal discipline, though resolutions have consistently prioritized continuity over confrontation. These adjustments underscore the Opera's strategy of incremental reformsfavoring data-driven cost modeling over expansive concessionsto navigate economic pressures from declining ticket sales and endowment dependencies without compromising core programming.

Reception, Achievements, and Criticisms

Artistic Accomplishments and Influence

Under Kurt Herbert Adler's general directorship from 1953 to 1981, the San Francisco Opera achieved international prominence through the introduction of major artists to American audiences, including the U.S. debuts of sopranos Leontyne Price, Elisabeth Schwarzkopf, and Birgit Nilsson, as well as tenor Mario del Monaco. Adler's tenure also featured two world premieres and 18 American premieres, expanding the company's repertoire beyond standard European works to include innovative stagings, such as Jean-Pierre Ponnelle's production of Madama Butterfly in 1963. In 1957, Adler established the Merola Opera Program, the first comprehensive pre-professional training initiative for opera singers, conductors, and directors in the United States, which has since launched careers of over 200 artists performing worldwide.
The company has commissioned and premiered numerous contemporary operas, influencing the development of American opera by prioritizing works with literary and social depth. Notable world premieres include Jake Heggie's Dead Man Walking in 2000, which has seen over 85 productions globally and is the most-performed American opera of its era; Andr Previn's A Streetcar Named Desire in 1998; and John Adams's Antony and Cleopatra in 2022, drawing on Shakespeare's tragedy for a modern operatic lens. Other significant debuts encompass American premieres like Kaija Saariaho's Innocence and co-commissions such as Philip Glass's Appomattox, reflecting a commitment to blending historical mastery with new compositions that address contemporary themes.
The San Francisco Opera's influence extends to recognizing artistic excellence via the San Francisco Opera Medal, instituted by Adler in 1970 as the company's highest honor for contributions to opera; recipients include composer Jake Heggie in 2025 for works premiered at the company and longtime artists like mezzo-soprano Catherine Cook in 2022. By fostering premieres of operas by American composers like Heggie and Adams, the company has shaped the national repertoire, emphasizing resilience and adaptation in transplanting European traditions to a distinctly American context of innovation and community engagement.

Controversies in Productions and Management

In 2001, the San Francisco Opera faced criticism over the use of glycol-based theatrical fog in productions such as Arshak II and Samson et Dalila, after at least seven chorus members reported health sensitivities including respiratory issues, prompting their excusal from rehearsals. Management, led by General Director Pamela Rosenberg, agreed to suspend the Rosco-brand glycol fog and switch to a mineral oil-based alternative like DF 50, citing safety studies and ventilation assessments, though some singers expressed ongoing concerns about the substitute. The dispute escalated by 2002, with chorus members alleging illnesses such as vomiting, dizziness, and vocal strain from both glycol and mineral oil fogsaffecting 39% of surveyed chorus in a 2000 state health studywhile production officials maintained the effects were safe based on expert reviews and denied negligence. California OSHA issued a $750 fine after reducing a more serious citation, and individual lawsuits claimed fraud and endangerment, highlighting tensions between artistic needs and performer health amid limited job alternatives for singers.
In November 2018, the opera removed countertenor David Daniels from its June production of Handel's Orlando, where he was cast as Medoro, following sexual assault allegations from two former students, including a lawsuit and police investigation. Daniels, who had debuted with the company in 1998 and appeared in five prior productions, denied the claims alongside his husband; the opera cited "business and professional reasons" tied to the unresolved probes as preventing his onstage participation. No replacement was immediately named, reflecting broader industry scrutiny of performer conduct amid #MeToo-era accountability.
Management has encountered repeated labor conflicts with unions representing singers, dancers, and orchestra members. In November 2004, performers filed a federal lawsuit alleging the company forced unpaid participation in a gala honoring music director Donald Runnicleswhose salary had risen 42% to $513,000while bypassing required arbitration and grievance processes, amid broader wage freezes for lower-paid chorus members earning $45,000$55,000 annually. The American Guild of Musical Artists (AGMA) pursued another federal suit in late 2019, claiming 25 contract breaches including underpayments, insufficient hiring of principals and dancers, inadequate costumes, and unpaid soloist expenses, with management accused of bad-faith bargaining despite a $5 million budget cut and favoritism toward executive pay raises. More recently, in September 2024, the orchestracontractless since July 31refused to perform the season-opening Un ballo in maschera until securing a temporary one-month extension, as management invoked post-pandemic financial strains including a $90 million budget gap and prior revenue losses from 532 income-free days. These episodes underscore persistent frictions over compensation, working conditions, and fiscal priorities in a high-cost opera environment.
