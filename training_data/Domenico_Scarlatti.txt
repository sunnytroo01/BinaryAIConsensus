 Giuseppe Domenico Scarlatti (26 October 1685 23 July 1757) was an Italian Baroque composer and harpsichordist, best known for his 555 innovative keyboard sonatas that blended Italian precision with Spanish folk influences and foreshadowed classical forms. Born in Naples to the renowned opera composer Alessandro Scarlatti, he received his primary musical education from his father and family members, developing early skills in composition and performance. By his late teens, Scarlatti was already active in Rome, where he served as choirmaster to the Queen of Poland and later at St. Peter's Basilica, while also composing operas and meeting contemporaries like George Frideric Handel in a famous 1709 keyboard competition.
In 1719, Scarlatti moved to Lisbon as court harpsichordist and music teacher to the Portuguese princess Maria Brbara de Bragana, whom he followed to Madrid in 1729 after her marriage to the Spanish crown prince (later King Ferdinand VI). He spent the remainder of his career in Spain, retiring around 1754, during which time he composed the majority of his keyboard works amid the cultural milieu of the Bourbon monarchy, incorporating elements of Spanish guitar techniques, dance rhythms like the fandango, and national folk tunes. His sonatas, often single-movement pieces in binary form lasting under five minutes, featured technical innovations such as hand-crossing, rapid leaps, arpeggios, and chromatic passages, reflecting influences from his travels and royal patrons.
Beyond his sonatas, Scarlatti's oeuvre includes 14 operas, sacred vocal works like the Stabat Mater for 10 voices, cantatas, concerti grossi, and sinfonias, though much of his early Italian output remains less performed today. His compositions expanded the harpsichord's expressive and virtuosic potential, influencing later figures such as Mozart and Haydn in the development of the sonata form, and his works continue to be celebrated for bridging Baroque and Classical styles while embodying Iberian musical identity.

Biography

Early life

Giuseppe Domenico Scarlatti was born on October 26, 1685, in Naples, Kingdom of Naples, to the renowned composer Alessandro Scarlatti and his wife, Antonia Ansaloni. As the sixth of ten children in a family deeply immersed in music, Scarlatti grew up surrounded by artistic influences; his older brother Pietro Filippo was also a composer, contributing to the family's legacy in Baroque music. The Scarlattis' prominence in Neapolitan musical circles provided young Domenico with an environment rich in performance and composition from an early age.
Scarlatti's formal musical education began under the guidance of his father, Alessandro, who trained him rigorously in keyboard techniques, composition, and counterpoint. This paternal instruction laid the foundation for Domenico's technical proficiency and stylistic development. By age sixteen, in 1701, he achieved his first notable public engagement, serving as organist at the royal chapel in Naples, where he demonstrated exceptional skill under his father's oversight as maestro di cappella.
During these formative years, around 1701 to 1703, Scarlatti produced his initial compositional works, including cantatas and sinfonias that reflected his emerging voice in vocal and instrumental genres. In 1703, the family's relocation to Romeprompted by Alessandro's pursuit of new professional opportunitiesimmersed Domenico in the city's dynamic musical networks, broadening his exposure to diverse influences and collaborations.

Italian period

In 1709, at the age of 23, Domenico Scarlatti was appointed maestro di cappella to Queen Maria Casimira Sobieska, the exiled Polish queen in Rome, temporarily succeeding his father Alessandro in that role. This position involved composing both sacred and secular music for her private chapel and theater, marking his entry into professional musical service in the Roman musical scene. From 1714 to 1719, Scarlatti served as maestro di cappella at the Cappella Giulia in St. Peter's Basilica, gaining exposure to the city's vibrant ecclesiastical and courtly traditions.
Scarlatti's compositional output in Italy began with operas, including his debut works L'Ottavia restituita al trono (1703, revised and performed in Rome around 1707) and Il Giustino (1703, with a later production in Naples in 1710). These operas, staged in Roman and Neapolitan theaters, showcased his early mastery of dramatic vocal writing and orchestration, influenced by the prevailing Italian operatic styles of the period. Concurrently, Scarlatti engaged with prominent musicians in Rome, including Arcangelo Corelli, whose violin concertos he encountered at Cardinal Pietro Ottoboni's academies, and George Frideric Handel, with whom he participated in a renowned keyboard competition around 17081709 at Ottoboni's palace. In this event, both composers demonstrated exceptional harpsichord and organ skills, with accounts noting Handel's edge on the organ and Scarlatti's superiority on the harpsichord.
From 1714 to 1719, Scarlatti served as maestro di cappella to the Portuguese ambassador to the Holy See in Rome, a role that included composing sacred music such as his Stabat Mater for ten voices (c. 17141715). This polychoral work, blending contrapuntal rigor with expressive text setting, was performed in Roman churches and reflected his deepening involvement in sacred composition. These initial sonatas emphasized technical innovation and idiomatic keyboard writing, laying the groundwork for his extensive catalog of over 550 sonatas. By 1719, Scarlatti departed Italy for Lisbon to join the Portuguese court full-time.

Portuguese service

In November 1719, Domenico Scarlatti arrived in Lisbon, where he had been appointed mestre de capela to King Joo V of Portugal, a position secured through the Portuguese ambassador in Rome and reflecting the king's ambition to elevate the court's musical standards with Italian expertise. His duties encompassed directing the royal chapel, composing music for court occasions, and providing instruction to the royal family, particularly as harpsichord teacher to the young Princess Maria Brbara de Bragana, who was nine years old at the time. Scarlatti's annual salary was set at 600 cruzados, along with accommodations and travel allowances, underscoring the prestige of the role amid Portugal's growing importation of Italian musical talent. The isolation of the Lisbon court, compounded by Portugal's political and geographical distance from major European centers, restricted extensive travel but allowed Scarlatti to focus intensively on keyboard instruction and composition tailored to the court's needs.
During his decade in Portugal, Scarlatti's compositional output included operas and sacred vocal works performed at the royal court, such as the opera Narciso (premiered in a revised version around 1722) and other dramatic pieces that adapted his earlier Italian experiences to Portuguese tastes. He also produced sacred music, notably the Miserere mei, Deus in E minor, a polychoral setting that highlighted his skill in blending Italian contrapuntal techniques with the court's liturgical demands. These works, along with motets and psalms like Laetatus sum, were created for chapel services and royal festivities, contributing to the Italianization of Portuguese sacred music under Joo V's patronage. Scarlatti's close collaboration with Princess Maria Brbara began early in his tenure; he composed her first keyboard sonatas during lessons, fostering her exceptional talent and laying the foundation for their lifelong artistic partnership.
In 1728, during a leave to Rome, Scarlatti married Maria Caterina Gentili, a 16-year-old Roman singer, on May 15; the couple returned to Lisbon later that year. They had five children, all of whom were raised in the royal court's orbit during the family's initial years in Portugal. This personal milestone coincided with Scarlatti's deepening role at court, where his teaching and compositions for Maria Brbaraover 30 early sonatas by 1729demonstrated his innovative approach to keyboard music, influenced by the princess's emerging virtuosity.

Spanish court

In 1729, Domenico Scarlatti relocated to Madrid following the marriage of his pupil, Princess Maria Barbara of Portugal, to Ferdinand, Prince of Asturias (later King Ferdinand VI of Spain). This move marked the beginning of his extended tenure at the Spanish court, where he continued serving as Maria Barbara's music master and composer.
Scarlatti's position was formalized in 1738 with his appointment as maestro de cmara to the royal household, entailing responsibilities for composing music for the royal chapel and theater performances. This role brought salary increases and the granting of noble status, elevating his standing within the court. He also taught the royal children, maintaining a close instructional relationship with the family.
During his time in Madrid, Scarlatti produced over 500 keyboard sonatas, collectively known as the Essercizi, dedicated to Maria Barbara and reflecting her skill as a performer; many survive in manuscripts notated in her own hand. These works were composed primarily for private court use, underscoring his role in advancing keyboard music under royal patronage.
Scarlatti also contributed to the court's theatrical life with operas such as La Gracia de amor (possibly from 1754, though its attribution and date remain debated) and intermezzos performed for royal entertainment. Due to the court's emphasis on privacy, these pieces received limited public performances, circulating mainly within the royal circle.
In his later years, Scarlatti's health declined, with gout impairing his mobility, though he persisted in teaching the royal children until his death in 1757.

Death and personal life

Domenico Scarlatti died on 23 July 1757 in his residence at Calle de Leganitos in Madrid, at the age of 71, after receiving the last rites from the parish of San Martn. He was secretly buried in the Convent of San Norberto in Madrid, but the exact site of the burial was suppressed at the time, and no trace of the grave remains today following the convent's dissolution in 1845.
Scarlatti had drafted his final will in 1749, a document that reveals his personal concerns amid standard 18th-century testamentary formulas. He bequeathed all his property to his second wife, Anastasia Ximenes (with whom he had four children), except for specific legacies to his children from both marriages; the estate was modest, consisting primarily of household goods, clothing, and a small amount of money. One of his sons from the second marriage, Alexandro Joseph Nicolas, received particular mention in inheritance proceedings, though details of his later career as a courtier are sparse.
Following Scarlatti's death, his family dispersed. Of his nine children (five from his first wife, Maria Caterina Gentili, who died in 1739, and four from Ximenes), only his daughter Maria Ana remained in Spain; the others returned to Italy.
Contemporary records on Scarlatti's personal life are limited, offering glimpses into habits that reflected his integration into Iberian culture. He was known for his passion for gambling, particularly card games, which reportedly led to financial strains. Anecdotal accounts also suggest he enjoyed attending bullfights, a popular spectacle at the Spanish court. No undisputed portrait of Scarlatti from life survives, though a 1738 painting by Domingo Antonio de Velasco, depicting him in the mantle of the Order of Santiago, is widely accepted as authentic. Obituaries and notices, including one in the San Martn parish register and brief mentions in Spanish periodicals like the Gaceta de Madrid, praised his unparalleled mastery of the keyboard.

Compositions

Keyboard sonatas

Domenico Scarlatti's primary compositional legacy consists of 555 single-movement keyboard sonatas, the vast majority of which are structured in binary form and were composed mainly between 1729 and 1757 for the harpsichord. These works represent a pinnacle of Baroque keyboard writing, emphasizing virtuosic display and idiomatic keyboard techniques developed during Scarlatti's service at the Spanish court. The first 30 sonatas, designated K. 130 in modern catalogs, were published in London as Essercizi per gravicembalo in 1738, dedicated to King John V of Portugal and marking Scarlatti's only printed collection during his lifetime.
The sonatas are cataloged using two primary systems: the numbering devised by Alessandro Longo in his 1937 edition of the complete works (L. 1555), which organizes them into thematic suites, and the more widely adopted chronological catalog by Ralph Kirkpatrick from his 1953 monograph (K. 1555), which prioritizes estimated composition dates based on manuscript evidence. Kirkpatrick's system remains the standard for scholarly and performance references, often grouping sonatas thematically by mood, such as pastoral or dance-like, or by technical focus, like repeated notes or arpeggios, to highlight Scarlatti's exploratory approach.
A defining feature of the sonatas is their incorporation of Spanish folk elements, particularly guitar-derived idioms such as rasgueado strumming patterns and syncopated rhythms, evident in works like the Sonata in B minor, K. 27, and the Sonata in D minor, K. 141, which evoke flamenco and bolero dances through percussive effects and idiomatic hand positions mimicking string plucking. Technical demands further distinguish the repertoire, including intricate hand-crossing passages in the Sonata in F major, K. 107, where the hands interchange positions rapidly, and virtuosic rapid scales that span the keyboard, challenging performers with speed and precision akin to contemporary guitar techniques.
The primary manuscript sources for the sonatas are two parallel sets of 15 volumes each, copied around 17421756 likely under Scarlatti's supervision for his patron, Queen Maria Barbara of Spain, with the Venice set (Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana) containing 496 sonatas in a richly bound format and the Parma set (Biblioteca Palatina) offering similar content in plainer bindings. These manuscripts preserve the majority of the known sonatas.
Originally conceived for the harpsichord, the sonatas exploit its bright timbre and crisp articulation to convey rhythmic vitality and ornamental flourishes, though they adapt well to the modern piano, where dynamic gradations enhance their expressive range without altering the core structure. In concert performance, sonatas are frequently paired for contrast, such as K. 1 in D major (a lively allegro) with K. 87 in B minor (a more introspective grave), to balance tempos and keys while showcasing the repertoire's diversity.

Operas and vocal works

Domenico Scarlatti composed approximately 15 operas during his early career, primarily in the Italian opera seria style characterized by da capo arias and dramatic narratives drawn from classical or historical subjects, of which only about 8 survive. Notable examples include Amor d'un ombra e gelosia d'un'aura (1714), a pastoral drama with mythological elements performed in Rome for private audiences, and Tetide in Sciro (1712), staged in Rome during his service to the Queen of Poland. These works were presented in major centers such as Rome, Naples, and Lisbon, reflecting Scarlatti's connections to aristocratic patrons and royal chapels.
In addition to operas, Scarlatti produced over 200 chamber cantatas, typically scored for soprano and basso continuo, which blend pastoral themes with dramatic expression to evoke emotions of love, jealousy, and nature. Series such as those featuring characters like Clori, Dorina, and Fileno exemplify this genre, often structured in recitatives and arias that highlight virtuosic vocal lines and intimate emotional depth, intended for small courtly or private performances.
Scarlatti's sacred vocal output includes motets, masses, and oratorios. His Stabat Mater for two voices and continuo (1714) demonstrates refined contrapuntal writing within a concise, devotional framework, performed in chapels associated with his Italian patrons.
Following his move to Portugal in 1719 and later to Spain, Scarlatti's vocal composition shifted from the elaborate structures of Italian opera to simpler, more functional pieces suited to court liturgies and private devotions, often incorporating local influences. Surviving manuscripts, including cantatas and sacred works, are preserved in collections such as the Biblioteca Nacional de Espaa.

Chamber and orchestral music

Scarlatti's chamber music includes a small number of sonatas for violin and harpsichord or trio sonatas in the Neapolitan tradition, composed during his early career. These works feature concise movements that blend lyrical melodies with contrapuntal elements, reflecting influences from Roman composers like Corelli.
His orchestral output remains limited, consisting primarily of sinfonias and overtures for strings and continuo, often serving as introductions to theatrical works or standalone pieces from the early 1700s. A representative example is the Sinfonia in C major, structured in three movementsPresto, Adagio, and Allegrissimothat showcase energetic rhythms and graceful phrasing typical of Neapolitan orchestral style. Similar pieces include the Sinfonia a 3 in G major and the Sinfonia in A minor, both emphasizing string textures and binary forms without extensive development.
Few original concertos by Scarlatti survive, with his instrumental ensemble writing focused more on chamber scales than large-scale orchestral forms; much of the orchestral material appears as incidental music for his operas. Adaptations of his keyboard sonatas into concertos, such as the 12 Concerti Grossi in seven parts arranged by Charles Avison (including one in A minor), highlight the adaptability of Scarlatti's themes to concerto grosso formats influenced by Corelli.
Manuscripts of these works are scattered across libraries in Italy and Spain, such as those in the Biblioteca Palatina in Parma and the Royal Library in Madrid, with modern editions remaining sparse compared to his keyboard sonatas due to their lesser prominence in his oeuvre.

Musical style

Technical innovations

Domenico Scarlatti's keyboard sonatas are predominantly structured in binary form, characterized by an AABB layout where the first section typically modulates to the dominant or relative major, and the second returns to the tonic, often without indicated repeats to maintain continuous momentum. This form dominates his output of approximately 555 sonatas, with later works showing expansions that introduce sonata-like developments, such as thematic reworkings or extended contrasts in the second half, as seen in K. 193 where a vamp-like passage builds structural climax over 8699 bars. These expansions deviate from strict symmetry, incorporating irregular phrase lengths and accelerated second halves for heightened dramatic effect, evident in sonatas like K. 305 and K. 214.
Scarlatti's writing is highly idiomatic to the keyboard, emphasizing virtuosic techniques that exploit the instrument's capabilities, including wide leaps, hand crossings, and rapid repeated notes that evoke guitar strumming, such as the rasgueado effect in K. 141 through left-hand chordal repetitions. Hand independence is a hallmark, with rhythmic contrasts between hands and frequent crossings, as in K. 27 where arpeggios swap roles starting at bar 11 to enhance technical display and texture. Other innovations include glissandi marked for single-finger execution on white keys in K. 379, cluster chords creating dissonant textures in K. 119, and inter-manual opposition in contrary motion scales in K. 222, all designed to push performers toward greater dexterity and precision.
Ornamentation in Scarlatti's sonatas is extensive and integral to the structure, featuring mordents, appoggiaturas, and trills that not only add expressivity but also serve technical purposes, such as syncopated trills in K. 141 that challenge finger control. These elements are often notated inconsistently across manuscripts, with trills varying between sources in K. 515 at bar 54, and appoggiaturas holding structural weight in cadences, as in K. 409 at bar 44. Dance-derived rhythms, like dotted patterns and syncopations, are woven into the ornamental fabric without dominating the form, as exemplified in K. 380's bolero-like trills.
Many sonatas reflect a pedagogical intent, functioning as exercises in virtuosity composed primarily for Princess Maria Barbara of Portugal (later Queen of Spain), Scarlatti's pupil, to develop advanced keyboard skills. The first 30 sonatas, published as Essercizi per gravicembalo in 1738, progressively increase in difficulty, focusing on finger independence and rational use of all ten fingers, as noted in the preface to K. 9. Techniques like hand crossings in K. 65 simulate multi-hand play, serving as training tools that influenced teaching methods for elite performers. This approach extended Scarlatti's innovations beyond mere composition, making his works staples for technical instruction.
Compared to contemporaries, Scarlatti's techniques surpass the complexity of Handel's keyboard works or Couperin's ordered suites in virtuosic demand and dramatic single-movement focus, with guitar-mimicking effects and hand independence setting them apart from the more contrapuntal challenges in Bach's fugues. His expansions in binary form anticipate elements of Classical sonata structure, prioritizing keyboard-specific physicality and innovation over multi-movement forms prevalent among his peers.

Harmonic and rhythmic elements

Scarlatti's harmonic language exhibits notable boldness, characterized by frequent chromaticism that infuses his keyboard sonatas with expressive tension and coloristic variety. In works such as the Sonata in A minor, K. 3, descending chromatic scales in the bass (measures 10-15) create a sense of instability, while arpeggiated diminished seventh chords (measures 15-16 and 19-20) heighten dramatic intensity. Similarly, the Sonata in E major, K. 20, features adventurous chromaticism around bar 55, where nested subdominant cadences employ modal shifts from major to minor, replacing expected voice-leading resolutions with abrupt chord changes for heightened sonority. Pedal points, both explicit and implied, further anchor these chromatic explorations, providing stability amid flux, as seen in various sonatas where sustained bass notes support layered dissonances.
This harmonic daring extends to modal mixtures drawn from Iberian traditions, incorporating elements like the Andalusian "Doric" tetrachord in cadential progressions. For instance, in the Sonata in G major, K. 6 (measures 18-25), flamenco-inspired cadences blend Phrygian inflections with diatonic frameworks, evoking folkloric flavors while maintaining structural coherence. Scarlatti's use of diminished sevenths often deviates from strict functional roles typical of contemporaries like Bach, treating them more as coloristic devices than preparatory dominants, which aligns with his empirical approach to keyboard sonority. Tonal exploration underscores this innovation, with sonatas spanning over 40 distinct keys across the corpus of 555 works, favoring sharp keys over flat ones, possibly reflecting the brighter timbre suited to the Spanish harpsichord. Cycles of fifths frequently drive modulations, as in the Sonata in A major, K. 184, where a varied sequence progresses chromatically from A-flat major to C minor, enhancing thematic connectivity in paired sonatas.
Rhythmic vitality animates Scarlatti's music, employing hemiola, syncopation, and asymmetric meters to evoke dance-like propulsion. Hemiola appears in Iberian-influenced pieces, shifting accents across triple and duple groupings for rhythmic ambiguity, particularly in sonatas echoing flamenco rhythms. Syncopation permeates works like the Sonata in E major, K. 380, where off-beat accents in measures 12-18 disrupt expected pulses, fostering a lively, improvisatory feel. Asymmetric meters, such as 3/8, dominate fandango-based sonatas like K. 492, where the compound triple meter captures the dance's irregular lilt, with acceleration techniquesshortening note values or compressing phrasesbuilding momentum toward cadences.
Dissonance resolution in Scarlatti's sonatas adheres to late Baroque principles of tension and release but often incorporates unresolved appoggiaturas for sustained emotional depth. These accented dissonances, struck simultaneously with the principal note, linger without full resolution, as in trills forming appoggiatura dissonances that emphasize expressive delay. This approach builds on the Neapolitan school's emphasis on affective harmony inherited from his father Alessandro, yet integrates empirical Spanish elements like modal ambiguities, creating a unique synthesis of structured tension and folk-inflected spontaneity. Within the binary form framework, these elements unfold across reprises, where harmonic cycles reinforce rhythmic drive without overt thematic repetition.

Influences from Italian and Iberian traditions

Domenico Scarlatti's early musical formation was profoundly shaped by Italian Baroque traditions, particularly through his father Alessandro Scarlatti, a leading figure in Neapolitan opera whose dramatic expressiveness and melodic invention permeated Domenico's initial compositions. In Rome, where he served as maestro di cappella at St. Peter's Basilica from 1715 to 1719, Scarlatti encountered the contrapuntal rigor of Girolamo Frescobaldi's keyboard works, the structural clarity of Arcangelo Corelli's concertos, and the fugal complexity of George Frideric Handel's style during their famed 1709 keyboard competition. These influences fostered a foundation of virtuosic Italian keyboard idioms, evident in his early sonatas' operatic flourishes and polyphonic textures.
Upon relocating to the Iberian Peninsula in 1719, Scarlatti's style absorbed elements from Portuguese and Spanish vernacular music, including courtly dances and guitar-derived techniques such as rasgueado strumming patterns. In Spain, particularly after 1729, he integrated folk motifs like the Folas variations in Sonata K. 518, which echo traditional Spanish ground basses, and flamenco-inspired rhythms characterized by syncopated accents and hemiolas in later works such as K. 490. These Iberian infusions contrasted with his avoidance of the ornate French harpsichord suite style, favoring instead the direct, idiomatic expressiveness of local traditions over the Couperin-esque ornamentation.
Scarlatti's genius lay in synthesizing these disparate sources into a cohesive cross-cultural idiom, adopting modal scales from Iberian folk musicsuch as Phrygian inflections evoking cante jondoand percussive effects mimicking castanets through rapid repeated notes and hand-crossing gestures, as in K. 193 and K. 434. This fusion paralleled the stylistic blends in works by his contemporary Antonio Soler, a Spanish monk whose keyboard sonatas similarly merged Italian structures with native rhythms, though Soler drew direct inspiration from Scarlatti's example during the latter's Madrid tenure. By the 1730s, Scarlatti's early purely Italianate sonatas had evolved into a mature "Scarlatti idiom," balancing Baroque counterpoint with Iberian exoticism in a uniquely personal voice that prioritized rhythmic vitality and thematic invention over conventional forms.

Legacy

Historical editions and manuscripts

The primary manuscripts of Domenico Scarlatti's keyboard sonatas, none of which are autographs, are preserved in key European libraries and form the foundation of the corpus. The most significant collection is the fifteen-volume set in the Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana in Venice, containing 496 sonatas dated between 1742 and 1757, likely copied by court musicians including those associated with Queen Maria Brbara of Spain. Complementing this are the fifteen volumes in the Biblioteca Palatina in Parma, holding 463 sonatas compiled concurrently with the later Venice volumes, with substantial overlap in content but some unique pieces. These manuscripts, along with smaller collections in locations such as Mnster and Vienna, account for the majority of the known sonatas and highlight the irregular circulation of Scarlatti's music during his lifetime.
The transmission of Scarlatti's works began with limited 18th-century prints, primarily focused on his keyboard output. The earliest publication was the 1738 London edition of 30 Essercizi per gravicembalo, issued by Thomas Roseingrave, which included sonatas not fully represented in the major manuscripts and helped disseminate the music in England. Scattered librettos for his operas, such as Telemaco (1718) and Griselda (1721), were printed in Italy and Portugal, providing textual evidence of performances but rarely with full scores. No comprehensive printed collection of the sonatas appeared until the 19th century, during a period of relative neglect for Scarlatti's music outside specialist circles.
In the 19th century, editions remained sparse until publishers like Breitkopf & Hrtel issued selections in the 1830s, drawing from available manuscripts and early prints to revive interest among pianists. Alessandro Longo's thematic catalog from his complete edition project (19031910), organized over 500 sonatas but included attribution errors and chronological inaccuracies, which were systematically corrected in Ralph Kirkpatrick's authoritative 1953 catalog identifying 555 authentic sonatas. Kirkpatrick's work, based on philological analysis of the Venice and Parma sources, established the standard K. numbering and emphasized the manuscripts' reliability while noting variants in ornamentation and text.
20th-century scholarship advanced through urtext editions, such as Longo's Opere complete per clavicembalo (Ricordi, 19031910, revised post-1937) and Carlo Pestalozza's selections for Ricordi, which aimed for fidelity to the sources by minimizing editorial interventions. Facsimiles of the Parma and Venice manuscripts became available, enabling direct study, while digital scans on platforms like IMSLP and national library archives (e.g., the Biblioteca Palatina's online repository) have democratized access. Challenges in transmission persist, including attribution disputes for sonatas like K. 556569, which lack strong manuscript support and are often considered spurious or by contemporaries such as Antonio Soler.

20th- and 21st-century revivals

The revival of Domenico Scarlatti's music in the 20th century began with pioneering performances on period instruments, notably through harpsichordist Wanda Landowska's recordings of his keyboard sonatas in the 1930s. Landowska, a key figure in the early music movement, captured 40 sonatas for HMV between 1934 and 1940, emphasizing the composer's idiomatic writing for the harpsichord and bringing his works to a broader audience via commercial discs. These efforts helped shift perceptions from Scarlatti's neglect in the Romantic era toward a renewed appreciation for his technical brilliance and stylistic innovations.
In the mid-20th century, Italian pianist Arturo Benedetti Michelangeli advanced Scarlatti's popularity through his precise and introspective piano interpretations, particularly in recitals during the 1950s. Michelangeli included sonatas like K. 322 in programs, such as his 1952 performance in Arezzo, where his crystalline tone and rhythmic vitality highlighted the music's Iberian flair on modern piano. Following World War II, the surge in recordings amplified this momentum; Canadian harpsichordist Scott Ross completed the first integral cycle of all 555 keyboard sonatas in the 1980s, releasing the set on Erato in 1988 after a 15-month recording project that showcased the full scope of Scarlatti's output. Complementing this comprehensiveness, Vladimir Horowitz featured select sonatas in his piano recitals, including a 1981 Metropolitan Opera House program of five works that dazzled with his virtuosic flair and dramatic phrasing.
Entering the 21st century, performers increasingly turned to period instruments for authenticity, with French harpsichordist Pierre Hanta leading a revival through his ongoing cycle of sonatas on historical harpsichords, such as his 2005 recordings that capture the music's rhythmic vitality and ornamental finesse. This approach aligns with broader trends in historically informed performance, including fortepiano interpretations that underscore Scarlatti's textural nuances. Crossover experiments have also emerged, blending Scarlatti's motifs with jazz; for instance, pianist Donal Fox's 2008 Scarlatti Jazz Suite reimagines sonatas like K. 87 in improvisational styles, fusing Baroque structure with swing rhythms.
Scarlatti's music has permeated film soundtracks, enhancing dramatic narratives with its evocative character; the Sonata in E major, K. 380, appears in Ingmar Bergman's 1960 film The Devil's Eye, where its playful yet introspective quality underscores scenes of temptation and illusion. In piano pedagogy, Scarlatti's sonatas play a vital role in technical development, as evidenced by their inclusion in intermediate curricula for cultivating hand independence, leaps, and rhythmic precisionFrdric Chopin himself advocated teaching them to students for their formative value. Recent scholarship has invigorated interest through events like the 2025 International Conference "Scarlatti's World: Artists, Sovereigns, and Diplomats" in Lisbon, which examined the family's Iberian ties and cultural exchanges. Complementing this, new editions such as the Ricordi's critical series in the 2010s provide performers with updated, source-based texts that reflect ongoing research into Scarlatti's Portuguese and Spanish contexts.

Influence on later composers

Domenico Scarlatti's keyboard sonatas exerted a direct influence on his pupil Antonio Soler, whose own compositions echoed Scarlatti's technical innovations and stylistic vitality. Soler, who studied under Scarlatti in Madrid from around 1752 until his death in 1757, produced over 150 harpsichord sonatas that mirrored the master's exploitation of keyboard devices, such as rapid repeated notes and hand-crossing figurations, while incorporating similar binary forms and rhythmic drive. This apprenticeship not only preserved Scarlatti's Iberian-infused techniques but also disseminated them within Spanish musical circles, as evidenced by Soler's works blending Scarlatti's gallant spirit with local traditions.
In the late 18th century, Scarlatti's binary-form sonatas contributed to the evolution of keyboard writing, influencing composers like Joseph Haydn through structural parallels and idiomatic phrasing. Haydn's early piano sonatas, such as those in Hob. XVI:20-26, exhibit similarities in their concise binary layouts, staccato articulations, and arpeggiated textures, reflecting Scarlatti's expansion of dance-derived forms into more expressive vehicles. This connection underscores Scarlatti's role in bridging Baroque keyboard practices toward Classical clarity, paving the way for Haydn's innovations in sonata design.
During the Romantic era, Scarlatti's sonatas inspired adaptations by virtuosic pianists, notably Franz Liszt and Johannes Brahms, who integrated his technical demands into their repertoires. Liszt frequently performed Scarlatti's works in recitals, drawing on their rhythmic energy and idiomatic passages to inform his own transcendental etudes and sonata transcriptions. Similarly, Brahms incorporated Scarlattian elements, such as crossed-hand patterns and harmonic surprises, into his piano variations and intermezzos, viewing the sonatas as models of concise, characterful expression. Muzio Clementi, a pivotal figure in piano pedagogy, also drew upon Scarlatti's technical challenges in his Gradus ad Parnassum studies, which emphasized finger independence and dynamic contrasts akin to those in Scarlatti's essercizi.
Scarlatti's 20th-century impact extended to neoclassical and modernist composers, particularly Bla Bartk, whose folk integrations paralleled Scarlatti's fusion of Iberian elements. Introduced to Scarlatti around 1910, Bartk admired the ironic detachment and motivic economy in sonatas like K. 162, which influenced his structural approaches in works such as the Third String Quartet, where rhythmic vitality and modal inflections echo Scarlatti's Phrygian-tinged dances. This affinity highlighted Scarlatti's role as a precursor to Bartk's synthesis of national idioms with abstract forms.
Scarlatti's legacy bolstered national schools, especially Spain's early 20th-century revival, as seen in Manuel de Falla's Harpsichord Concerto (1923-1926), which evoked Scarlatti's ornamental flourishes and dance rhythms amid neoclassical restraint. Falla, embracing Stravinsky's neoclassicism, referenced Scarlatti's style to reclaim Iberian heritage, blending guitar-like strums and modal harmonies in a manner that revitalized Spanish keyboard music. While direct citations from modern minimalists like Philip Glass remain anecdotal, his repetitive rhythmic patterns in piano etudes share conceptual ties to Scarlatti's ostinato-driven vitality, though unverified in primary sources.
Broader aspects of Scarlatti's oeuvre facilitated the sonata's evolution from operatic roots to instrumental prominence, influencing Ludwig van Beethoven's multi-movement structures. Scarlatti's single-movement binaries, with their thematic development and tonal drama, prefigured Beethoven's expansions in sonatas like Op. 13, shifting focus from vocal expression to keyboard autonomy and paving the path for Romantic instrumental depth.
