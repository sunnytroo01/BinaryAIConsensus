Landsgemeinde
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Landsgemeinde is a traditional open-air assembly of citizens in select Swiss cantons, serving as a direct democratic institution where eligible voters convene annually to deliberate and decide by show of hands on cantonal legislation, taxation, budgets, and elections. Originating in the late Middle Ages as a sovereign body in rural cantons, it embodies a pre-modern form of participatory governance that predates modern representative systems and persists as a public, non-secret ballot mechanism.
Historically practiced in multiple cantons including Uri, Schwyz, and Obwalden, the Landsgemeinde has been abolished in most over time in favor of secret ballots and parliamentary procedures, leaving only Glarus and Appenzell Innerrhoden as active practitioners as of 2025. In Appenzell Innerrhoden, the assembly has convened on the last Sunday in April since 1403, integrating folk traditions with political decision-making on issues ranging from infrastructure to constitutional amendments. Glarus's version, restored in 1837 after periods of suspension, functions as an exemplary model of direct democracy, where visible participation fosters accountability though it has faced critiques for potential social pressures inherent in open voting.
This institution underscores Switzerland's commitment to direct democracy, contrasting with global trends toward indirect representation, and highlights tensions between transparency and individual voting privacy, with low modern attendance in some cases reflecting shifts in civic engagement yet preserving a tangible link to medieval self-governance.

Definition and Core Features

Legal and Historical Basis

The Landsgemeinde traces its origins to the late medieval period in rural Swiss cantons, where assemblies of free male citizens gathered to deliberate on matters of governance, taxation, and defense. These gatherings emerged as a practical mechanism for collective decision-making in decentralized alpine communities, predating the formal Old Swiss Confederacy. The earliest documented Landsgemeinde occurred in 1294, three years after the Rtli oath that allied the founding cantons of Uri, Schwyz, and Unterwalden in 1291.
In the canton of Glarus, a pivotal assembly on March 11, 1387, produced the first written articles of association, establishing a foundational charter for self-governance that emphasized direct participation over feudal hierarchies. This event marked an early codification of customary practices into a more structured form, reflecting the canton's autonomy amid the growing confederation. Similar assemblies developed in other rural cantons, such as Appenzell and Obwalden, during the 13th and 14th centuries, serving as sovereign bodies for electing officials and enacting laws by acclamation or show of hands.
Legally, the Landsgemeinde gained formal status through cantonal constitutions, particularly during the regenerative period of liberal reforms in the 1830s, when Switzerland transitioned toward modern democratic frameworks. In Glarus, the assembly of May 29, 1836, adopted a new constitution that abolished prior divisions and entrenched the Landsgemeinde as the canton's supreme legislative authority, open to all eligible male citizens. Cantonal constitutions in places like Appenzell Innerrhoden similarly mandated annual open-air meetings as the primary venue for legislation, judicial elections, and constitutional amendments, distinguishing them from representative parliaments in urban cantons. These provisions aligned with Switzerland's federal structure, where cantons retain sovereignty over internal organization, provided it conforms to the federal constitution of 1848.

Procedural Mechanics

The Landsgemeinde convenes annually on the first Sunday in May in Glarus and the last Sunday in April in Appenzell Innerrhoden, gathering eligible voters outdoors in the cantonal capital's main square under the chairmanship of the Landammann, the cantonal president who wields a ceremonial sword as a symbol of authority. Proceedings commence around 9:30 a.m. following an oath of office or formal opening, with participantsSwiss citizens resident in the canton aged 16 and above in Glarus or 18 and above in Appenzell Innerrhodenforming a ring around the podium.
The agenda, comprising 10 to 12 pre-submitted items from the cantonal parliament such as elections for the seven-member executive council, judges, tax rates, and constitutional amendments, is outlined by the Landammann, who explains each motion before declaring "the word is free" to invite public debate. Any eligible voter may address the assembly, proposing amendments, rejections, or alternatives on the spot a flexibility particularly emphasized in Glarus, where electors can modify provisions directly rather than merely approving or rejecting prepared texts. In Appenzell Innerrhoden, male voters traditionally carry swords during the event, underscoring historical customs, while Glarus encourages children to observe within the voter ring to foster civic education.
Decisions proceed by simple majority via public show of hands, with voters in Glarus raising orange cards for visibility and silence often signifying unanimous acceptance without a formal vote. The Landammann, assisted by government members or experienced observers, visually estimates the majority; in cases of doubt or closeness, a recount or exact headcount is ordered, eschewing electronic aids as rejected by Glarus voters in 2016. Elections for positions like Landammann require separate hand-votes, and the assembly handles major expenditures exceeding thresholds such as CHF 1 million in Glarus. Sessions typically extend from morning into the afternoon, concluding after all items are resolved, with extraordinary assemblies possible upon petition by sufficient electors.

Scope of Authority and Decisions

In the cantons of Glarus and Appenzell Innerrhoden, where the Landsgemeinde persists as of 2025, it functions as the paramount cantonal body for legislation, elections, and fiscal approvals, embodying direct popular sovereignty over matters reserved to the canton under Swiss federalism. This authority encompasses constitutional amendments, lawmaking, executive and judicial appointments, tax determinations, and budget ratifications, with decisions binding unless overridden by subsequent assemblies or federal law. Unlike parliamentary systems in other cantons, the Landsgemeinde's open assembly ensures immediate citizen veto or endorsement of proposals prepared by executive or preparatory bodies.
In Glarus, the Landsgemeinde enacts and amends the cantonal constitution and statutes, sets the annual tax multiplier, approves the budget and audited state accounts, and resolves significant policy issues such as infrastructure or administrative reforms. It elects the Landammann (chief executive), Landesstatthalter (deputy), and cantonal judges, while the Kantonsrata 60-member preparatory legislaturedebates initiatives, verifies accounts, and forwards items for final disposition, ensuring the assembly retains ultimate decisional power without delegation of core sovereignty.
Appenzell Innerrhoden operates without a separate cantonal parliament, vesting comprehensive legislative competence directly in the Landsgemeinde, which adopts the constitution, legislates on cantonal affairs, and passes resolutions on fiscal and administrative matters via majority hand-vote. Elections include the regierender and stillstehender Landammann, five Standeskommission members (executive council), Cantonal Court president and judges, and the Stnderat delegate every four years; it also ratifies state accounts and addresses federal-cantonal intersections, with individual vote counts available for close decisions to enhance accuracy.
These powers exclude federal competencies like foreign policy or defense, per Article 3 of the Swiss Federal Constitution, and are exercised annuallyfirst Sunday in May for Glarus, last Sunday in April for Appenzell Innerrhodenprioritizing substantive consensus over procedural delegation. Limitations arise from quorum requirements (typically implicit via attendance) and the non-secret ballot's transparency, which deters absenteeism but invites social pressures verifiable in small populations of approximately 40,000 (Glarus) and 16,000 (Appenzell Innerrhoden).

Historical Development

Medieval Origins

The Landsgemeinde emerged in the late 13th century as a form of communal assembly in the rural cantons of central Switzerland, rooted in the traditions of self-governing alpine communities. The earliest documented instance occurred in Schwyz on November 1, 1294, when free male citizens convened outdoors to address matters of alliance and defense shortly after the formation of the Old Swiss Confederacy via the Federal Charter of 1291 between Uri, Schwyz, and Unterwalden. These gatherings, known as Tag or Versammlung, allowed participants to deliberate and decide by acclamation or show of hands on issues such as taxation, military levies, and pacts with neighboring regions, reflecting a decentralized governance model suited to sparsely populated, agrarian Waldsttten (forest cantons).
In contrast to the representative councils of urban centers like Zurich or Bern, the Landsgemeinde emphasized direct participation by all eligible freemen, excluding serfs and clergy, and convened periodicallyoften annually or as neededin open fields or marketplaces to symbolize collective sovereignty. This practice drew from pre-existing Germanic tribal assemblies (things) adapted to the feudal fragmentation of the Holy Roman Empire, where local valleys asserted autonomy against Habsburg overlords. Historical records indicate that by the early 14th century, similar assemblies solidified in Uri and Unterwalden, enabling rapid consensus on confederative oaths, such as the 1315 pact before the Battle of Morgarten.
The institution's medieval character was marked by its integration with militia systems and oath-based alliances, fostering resilience against external threats while limiting executive power to elected Landammann (cantonal leaders) accountable to the assembly. Expansion occurred as allied territories adopted the model; for instance, Glarus incorporated Landsgemeinde practices by the mid-14th century following its 1352 entry into the confederacy, with roots traceable to 13th-century communal decisions under monastic influence. Such assemblies numbered participants in the hundreds, voting on up to a dozen items per session, and persisted as the primary legislative body until the 19th century despite growing populations.

Expansion and Institutionalization

The Landsgemeinde expanded from its origins in the central Alpine forest cantonsUri, Schwyz, and Unterwalden (later divided into Obwalden and Nidwalden)to other rural territories associating with the Old Swiss Confederacy in the 14th century. The first documented assembly occurred in Schwyz in 1294, shortly after the Federal Charter of 1291 formalized alliances among these valleys, establishing the practice as a mechanism for communal decision-making on alliances, taxes, and military matters. As the Confederacy grew through defensive pacts against Habsburg expansion, newly allied rural cantons adopted similar assemblies to assert sovereignty, distinguishing them from urban cantons like Lucerne and Zurich, which relied on patrician councils. Glarus, allying in 1352, recorded its initial Landsgemeinde in 1387, using the gathering to ratify pacts and elect officials.
Zug, also joining in 1352, institutionalized the Landsgemeinde as its primary legislative body by the late 14th century, while Appenzell, separating from the Abbey of St. Gallen around 1403, formalized annual open-air assemblies on the last Sunday in April to deliberate on governance and disputes. This spread reinforced the rural cantons' (Lnderorte) identity within the Confederacy, where assemblies functioned as sovereign forums for majority-rule votes by show of hands, often numbering hundreds of armed freemen. By the 15th century, amid wars like the Burgundian conflicts (14741477), the practice had solidified in eight cantons: Appenzell (later split into Innerrhoden and Ausserrhoden), Glarus, Obwalden, Nidwalden, Schwyz, Uri, and Zug, enabling rapid consensus on federal alliances without centralized authority.
Institutionalization deepened in the 16th century as cantonal constitutions and traditions codified the Landsgemeinde's role, shifting emphasis toward electing executives and judges while reserving legislative votes for assemblies, though ad hoc referenda handled some policies. In Glarus, for instance, the assembly evolved into a biannual event by 1535, balancing Protestant and Catholic factions post-Reformation. This structure persisted through the Ancien Rgime, embedding direct accountability in rural governance and influencing the Confederacy's decentralized federalism, even as urban cantons favored representative councils. The practice's endurance highlighted its adaptation to local demographics, with participation limited to adult male citizens bearing arms, ensuring decisions reflected communal stakes rather than abstract representation.

Modern Adaptations and Declines

By the mid-20th century, the Landsgemeinde had largely declined across Switzerland, with most cantons transitioning to secret ballot systems to address concerns over voter anonymity and potential social coercion in public hand-raising votes. This shift prioritized individual voting privacy, reflecting broader modern democratic norms that view open voting as susceptible to peer pressure and intimidation. As of 2025, only two cantonsAppenzell Innerrhoden and Glarusretain the traditional open-air assembly for key decisions, down from at least eight historical practitioners originating in the medieval period.
In Appenzell Innerrhoden, the institution persisted following the 1991 federal court-mandated introduction of women's suffrage, which had been locally rejected at the Landsgemeinde until overridden nationally; the canton maintained hand-raising voting despite debates on secrecy, emphasizing communal transparency. Glarus adapted by voting at its 2007 Landsgemeinde to lower the cantonal voting age to 16, the youngest in Switzerland, aiming to boost youth engagement in direct democracy while preserving the assembly format. These adaptations underscore efforts to sustain the practice amid logistical challenges, such as weather dependency and declining attendance in larger populations, which have fueled ongoing discussions about full replacement with ballot-based referendums.
The decline stems from practical inefficiencies and evolving preferences for scalable, private voting mechanisms that accommodate urban growth and diverse electorates, as evidenced by the abolition in cantons like Obwalden in 1998, where voters approved secret ballots to mitigate public scrutiny. Proponents of retention argue it fosters accountability through visible participation, yet empirical trends show sustained erosion, with no new adoptions and persistent calls for reform in remaining venues due to risks of demagoguery in open forums. This trajectory aligns with Switzerland's broader hybridization of direct democracy, blending assembly traditions with mandatory and optional referendums for wider accessibility.

Implementation and Operations

Participating Cantons

The Landsgemeinde remains active as of 2025 exclusively in two Swiss cantons: Glarus and Appenzell Innerrhoden, where it functions as the highest cantonal authority for legislation, taxation, and elections through public hand-raising votes held annually on the last Sunday in April. In Glarus, the assembly convenes in the main square of the cantonal capital, accommodating decisions on budgetary matters, constitutional amendments, and candidate selections for government positions. Appenzell Innerrhoden's gathering, similarly traditional, emphasizes communal participation in a rural setting, with eligible citizens aged 16 and older in Glaruslowered by Landsgemeinde vote in 2007exercising direct sovereignty.
Historically, the Landsgemeinde originated in the three founding cantons of the Swiss ConfederacyUri, Schwyz, and Unterwalden (subdivided into Obwalden and Nidwalden)with the earliest documented assembly occurring in 1294 following the Federal Charter of 1291. It expanded to other central cantons, including Glarus from 1387, Zug, and Appenzell (divided into Innerrhoden and Ausserrhoden in 1597), serving as a core mechanism of direct democracy in these rural, German-speaking regions. These assemblies embodied the confederation's emphasis on communal consensus over representative intermediaries, though interruptions occurred during the Napoleonic era (17981815), when French-imposed structures temporarily suppressed them.
Over the 19th and 20th centuries, most cantons phased out the Landsgemeinde in favor of secret ballot systems to address logistical challenges such as increasing population sizes, inclement weather, and demands for voter anonymity. Uri discontinued its assembly prior to 1945, while Schwyz followed suit in the early 20th century; Zug transitioned earlier in the 1840s. In the late 1990s, the practice persisted in five cantons until successive abolitions: Nidwalden in 1996, Appenzell Ausserrhoden in 1997, and Obwalden in 1998, leaving only Glarus and Appenzell Innerrhoden committed to the tradition amid debates over its viability in modern governance.

Voting and Assembly Processes

The Landsgemeinde assembly convenes annually in an open-air setting on the main town square of the cantonal capital, gathering eligible citizens to deliberate and vote on legislative proposals, budgets, elections, and constitutional matters by simple majority. In Glarus, proceedings begin on the first Sunday in May at approximately 9:30 a.m. with an oath of office for officials, followed by a parade and assembly; the Landammann, or cantonal president, opens the session, declares debate open ("the word is free"), and presents the agenda, typically comprising 10-12 items such as tax adjustments or policy amendments. Citizens may propose spontaneous amendments if endorsed by at least 10 members of the cantonal parliament, leading to immediate discussion.
Voting occurs via a public show of hands, using orange cards for visibility in Glarus, where the Landammann visually estimates the majority without electronic aidsa 2016 proposal for counting devices was rejected to preserve tradition. Uncontested items pass in silence; contested ones prompt short speeches adhering to structured oratory rules (e.g., clear points, no interruptions), followed by hand-raising, with recounts for close margins until a clear majority emerges. This non-secret method ensures transparency but exposes voters to peer observation. Participation includes all Swiss citizens resident in the canton aged 16 and older in Glarus (lowered by assembly vote in 2007), with women enfranchised since 1971; turnout hovers around 10-20% of the ~41,000 eligible.
In Appenzell Innerrhoden, the assembly follows a parallel format on the last Sunday in April, electing the seven-member cantonal government (Standeskommission), judges, and addressing legislative and fiscal issues through hand-raising votes counted by officials. Debate allows citizen interventions, emphasizing majority consensus in a public forum; eligibility requires Swiss citizenship and residency, with voting age at 18, and historical male traditions like carrying swords now largely symbolic. Both cantons prioritize verbal and visual immediacy over secret ballots, fostering direct accountability amid low attendance influenced by weather and convenience.

Logistical and Cultural Elements

The Landsgemeinde in Glarus convenes annually on the first Sunday in May in the canton's central arena, featuring a visitors' grandstand, market stalls, and space for orators alongside a marching band procession. Free public transport facilitates attendance, with activities commencing around 8:00 AM via vendors and brass bands outside the town hall, followed by formal proceedings at 9:30 AM led by the Landammann's oath-taking. Voting occurs through hand-raising on agenda items such as taxes and legislative proposals, with majorities estimated visually by officials; electronic voting aids were rejected in a 2016 referendum to preserve tradition. In Appenzell Innerrhoden, the assembly gathers on the last Sunday in April in the town square, preceded by a procession from St. Mauritius Church.
Logistically, these open-air events emphasize accessibility and simplicity, accommodating turnout rates around 10% of eligible voters in Glarus despite capacities for thousands, with proceedings conducted outdoors irrespective of weather conditions like rain or snow. Discussions allow open participation, though decorum prohibits applause or heckling, and exact hand counts are taken only if estimates prove inconclusive. Participants must present identification for voting eligibility, historically verified by sidearms in some cantons, underscoring the public, non-secret nature of the ballot.
Culturally, the Landsgemeinde functions as a communal festival blending politics with tradition, offering local fare such as veal sausages with onion sauce in Glarus and fostering intergenerational learning, as children in the canton observe within the voter ring. Attendees in Glarus don broad-brimmed hats, while Appenzell men carry ceremonial swords, evoking medieval origins dating to the 14th century and symbolizing direct self-governance. This atmosphere promotes consensus-seeking over confrontation, reflecting Swiss values of pragmatic unity, though participation has waned amid modern alternatives like secret ballots.

Empirical Advantages

Promotion of Direct Accountability

The Landsgemeinde promotes direct accountability through its mechanism of public assembly, where eligible citizens gather outdoors to vote by show of hands on legislative proposals, budgets, and executive elections, ensuring that officials face immediate, personal scrutiny from the electorate. This format compels representatives to justify decisions in real-time before a physically present populace, fostering transparency and deterring opaque governance practices inherent in mediated representative systems. In cantons such as Glarus and Appenzell Innerrhoden, where the practice persists as of 2025, annual assemblies allow voters to directly approve or reject fiscal plans, with Glarus's 2025 Landsgemeinde addressing cantonal budgets exceeding 1 billion Swiss francs.
Empirical analyses of Swiss direct democracy, including Landsgemeinde-influenced systems, reveal enhanced fiscal restraint, as cantons with strong participatory elements maintain lower per capita public debtaveraging 20-30% below national figures in direct-democratic jurisdictionsdue to voters' ability to veto excessive spending. This voter oversight correlates with Switzerland's sustained low corruption levels, with the country scoring 82 out of 100 on the 2023 Corruption Perceptions Index, outperforming most representative democracies and attributing part of this edge to direct mechanisms that align incentives between governors and governed.
Field experiments further substantiate increased accountability, showing that politicians in direct-democratic cantons respond more promptly and substantively to citizen inquiriesup to 25% higher engagement ratesanticipating public backlash via assembly votes or referenda. Such responsiveness mitigates principal-agent problems, where representatives might otherwise prioritize personal or elite interests, as evidenced by consistent policy adherence to majority preferences in Landsgemeinde cantons, evidenced by rejection rates of executive proposals exceeding 10% annually in Glarus assemblies. 
The militia-based political structure intertwined with Landsgemeinde reinforces this by drawing officials from civilian ranks, who return to ordinary life post-term, heightening personal stakes in accountable performance and reducing careerist detachment observed in full-time legislatures. Overall, these elements cultivate a governance model where accountability derives from continuous, unfiltered citizen involvement rather than periodic elections.

Evidence from Swiss Governance Metrics

Empirical analyses of Swiss cantonal fiscal data reveal that stronger direct democratic institutions, including those embodied in Landsgemeinde practices, are associated with lower public debt and restrained government spending. A study examining cantonal debt levels from the late 20th century found that direct democracy mechanisms significantly reduce debt accumulation, exerting a dampening effect on fiscal expansion even after controlling for other institutional factors like debt brakes. This aligns with broader evidence from Swiss municipalities and cantons, where fiscal referenda and citizen veto powers correlate with decreased public borrowing and more conservative budgeting, as voters prioritize fiscal sustainability over expansive programs.
Cantons historically reliant on Landsgemeinde, such as Appenzell Innerrhoden, exemplify these patterns through notably sound public finances, including one of the lowest debt ratios among the 26 cantons and minimal reliance on equalization transfers. Economic indicators further underscore this: Appenzell Innerrhoden's labor force participation rate for ages 15-64 stood at 86.2% in recent data, exceeding the national average of 80.3%, indicative of governance structures supporting high employment and resource efficiency. Competitiveness assessments highlight its gains in resource potential, driven by low-tax policies and direct oversight that curb wasteful expenditure.
These outcomes reflect causal links from direct accountability to fiscal prudence, as evidenced by longitudinal cantonal comparisons showing proactive direct democracy usage enhances sustainability without compromising service delivery. While small cantonal sizes and rural demographics contribute, the consistent correlation across direct democratic variants supports Landsgemeinde's role in fostering voter-driven restraint, contrasting with higher debt in less participatory systems.

Criticisms and Risks

Potential for Ochlocracy and Demagoguery

The visible, non-secret voting by show of hands in Landsgemeinde assemblies facilitates potential social pressures that can mimic ochlocratic tendencies, as participants may conform to apparent majorities to avoid isolation or ostracism, prioritizing group consensus over independent evaluation. Empirical analysis of collective voting mechanisms, including Swiss open-air assemblies, identifies pitfalls such as herding behavior and reduced expression of dissenting views due to the transparency of votes, which can distort outcomes toward short-term emotional appeals rather than substantive deliberation. This dynamic echoes classical concerns about direct assemblies, where the immediacy of crowd interaction heightens vulnerability to transient passions over institutional safeguards.
Demagoguery poses another inherent risk, as the format allows influential speakers to directly address and potentially manipulate the assembled populace through unfiltered rhetoric, bypassing the filtering effects of representative deliberation or secret balloting. In historical Swiss contexts, several cantons transitioned from Landsgemeinde voting to secret ballots partly to curb such influences, with Uri's 1847 abolition explicitly citing risks of demagogic sway in open assemblies. While no large-scale ochlocratic breakdowns have been documented in modern practiceowing to small-scale settings and cultural norms of restraintthe structure theoretically amplifies the capacity for charismatic figures to exploit visible majorities, as evidenced by broader critiques of assembly-based direct democracy favoring ballot secrecy to insulate decisions from performative oratory.
These potentials are mitigated in persisting cantons like Appenzell Innerrhoden, where turnout hovers around 10-20% of eligible voters and decisions often reflect longstanding local consensus rather than volatile mobs, yet the open format retains intrinsic exposure to majority tyranny absent anonymity. Critics argue this setup can entrench de facto rule by vocal or organized subgroups, underscoring a trade-off between participatory immediacy and protections against unreflective collectivism.

Practical and Efficiency Drawbacks

The Landsgemeinde's reliance on open-air gatherings in fixed public squares creates inherent logistical constraints, particularly in accommodating growing populations, as venues often prove insufficient for all eligible voters. This scalability issue has historically driven abolitions in several cantons; for instance, Nidwalden ended its cantonal Landsgemeinde on December 1, 1996, by a large majority vote to adopt secret ballots, citing practical modernization needs, while Obwalden followed suit in 1998 for similar reasons of feasibility and broader participation.
Weather dependency compounds these challenges, as assemblies occur outdoors regardless of conditions, leading to reduced turnout during inclement weather. Empirical analysis of Swiss Landsgemeinden demonstrates that rainfall negatively impacts participation rates, with each millimeter of rain correlating to fewer attendees due to discomfort and accessibility barriers, thereby distorting the democratic representativeness of outcomes.
Operationally, the process proves inefficient for timely decision-making, as sessions can extend for hours amid verbal debates, hand-raising votes, and manual tallies prone to human error in sizable crowds exceeding several thousand. Declining attendancesuch as the 11% turnout recorded in Appenzell Innerrhoden in 2015, the lowest since tracking began in 1970stems from the format's demands on participants' time and physical presence, clashing with contemporary schedules and preferences for convenient alternatives like postal or electronic voting.

Comparative Shortcomings Versus Representative Systems

The Landsgemeinde's reliance on physical assembly inherently limits its applicability to small populations, rendering it infeasible for larger jurisdictions characteristic of most modern states. In the cantons of Appenzell Innerrhoden, with a population of approximately 16,500 as of 2023, and Glarus, with around 42,000 residents, the system functions due to manageable crowd sizes that allow for open-air hand-raising votes. However, scaling this model to representative systems in larger entities, such as Switzerland's federal parliament or national legislatures elsewhere, would encounter insurmountable logistical barriers, including venue capacity, travel demands, and coordination costs, which representative assemblies mitigate through delegated proxy voting by elected officials.
Unlike representative systems, where legislators specialize and deliberate complex policies through committees and expert consultations, the Landsgemeinde exposes decisions to immediate majority impulses without structured expertise or extended debate, potentially yielding outcomes driven by incomplete information or short-term sentiments. Participants, often numbering in the low thousands for attendance despite eligible voter bases exceeding 10,000 in Glarus, must vote on diverse cantonal mattersfrom budgets to judicial electionsin a single annual session lasting several hours, precluding the in-depth analysis afforded by professional parliaments. This format contrasts with representative bodies, which distribute workload across full-time lawmakers, enabling evidence-based scrutiny that direct assemblies, constrained by time and participant knowledge, cannot replicate at equivalent depth.
Practical inefficiencies further underscore the Landsgemeinde's disadvantages relative to representative governance, including vulnerability to external factors like weather, which can deter attendance and skew participation toward more committed or locally proximate voters, and the absence of secret ballots, fostering social pressures absent in confidential representative elections. Turnout in Landsgemeinde events averages 10-15% lower than in secret-ballot referenda within the same cantons, as physical presence requirements exclude those unable to attend due to work, health, or distance. Moreover, hand-counting votes lacks the precision and verifiability of electronic or paper-based systems in representative contexts, raising risks of disputes or errors without recourse to recounts. Representative systems, by contrast, streamline decision-making through institutionalized processes, reducing disruption to citizens' routines and enhancing overall administrative efficiency.

Contemporary Status

Current Practice in Remaining Cantons

The Landsgemeinde endures exclusively in the cantons of Glarus and Appenzell Innerrhoden, functioning as the highest cantonal legislative and electoral body through direct, public participation. These assemblies embody a form of pure direct democracy, where decisions on key matters are made by majority vote among attending eligible citizens, contrasting with secret ballots used elsewhere in Switzerland.
In Glarus, the assembly convenes annually on the first Sunday in May in the town of Glarus, chaired by the Landammann who delivers an opening address and administers oaths to the gathering. Eligible participants include Swiss citizens aged 16 and older residing in the canton, a voting age lowered by Landsgemeinde decision in 2007. Voting proceeds openly via show of hands, with the chair estimating majorities to determine outcomes on elections, constitutional amendments, and approvals for major expendituressuch as one-off costs exceeding CHF 1,000,000 or recurring ones over CHF 200,000and land acquisitions above CHF 5,000,000. Extraordinary assemblies may be called if requested by 2,000 electors or the cantonal parliament.
In Appenzell Innerrhoden, the Landsgemeinde assembles on the last Sunday in April on the Landsgemeindeplatz in Appenzell, where eligible votersSwiss citizens aged 18 and older domiciled in the cantongather to elect or confirm the seven-member executive Standeskommission and cantonal judges, deliberate constitutional and legislative proposals, and address financial issues including budgets. Every four years, it selects the canton's representative to the federal Council of States. Decisions are reached by show of hands, with counts overseen by officials; voters may speak from the podium, propose amendments, or submit initiatives during the proceedings, fostering interactive debate. The 2025 assembly occurred on April 27.

Recent Events and Reforms

In response to the COVID-19 pandemic, the Landsgemeinde in Appenzell Innerrhoden was cancelled in 2020, marking the first suspension of the assembly since its establishment, due to health risks associated with large gatherings. The event resumed in 2021 with mandatory mask-wearing and other precautions, resulting in significantly reduced attendance compared to pre-pandemic levels, attributed to ongoing public health concerns.
Glarus continued its Landsgemeinde through the pandemic period without full cancellation, adapting to restrictions while maintaining the traditional hand-raising vote format. In April 2025, the Glarus assembly addressed key agenda items including budgetary approvals and local infrastructure proposals, drawing hundreds of participants to the open-air event despite modern critiques of its secrecy limitations. 
In Appenzell Innerrhoden, the April 2025 Landsgemeinde elected Angela Koller as the first female Landammann, succeeding Roland Inauen in the role equivalent to cantonal mayor and head of the education directorate, reflecting gradual shifts in gender representation within the conservative canton's leadership structure. No major structural reforms to the Landsgemeinde format have been enacted in either canton since the early 2000s, with Glarus having lowered the voting age to 16 in 2007 via assembly vote, a change unique among Swiss cantons that has persisted without reversal.
