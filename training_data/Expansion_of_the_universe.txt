Expansion of the universe

1x
 The expansion of the universe is the ongoing increase in the scale of space, causing distant galaxies to recede from one another at speeds proportional to their distance, as quantified by Hubble's law (v = Hd, where v is recession velocity, d is distance, and H is the Hubble constant). This relationship is observed through the redshift of light from distant galaxies, where light stretches to longer wavelengths due to the expansion of space itself, which occurs uniformly in all directions with no central point. This phenomenon, a cornerstone of modern cosmology, originated from the Big Bang approximately 13.8 billion years ago and is evidenced by the cosmological redshift of light from remote objects, where expanding space stretches wavelengths toward the red end of the spectrum. Initially, the expansion decelerated due to gravitational attraction from matter, but around 9 billion years after the Big Bang, it began accelerating, driven by the mysterious force known as dark energy, which comprises about 68-70% of the universe's total energy content.
Edwin Hubble's groundbreaking observations in 1929 provided the first empirical evidence for this expansion, using Cepheid variable stars to measure distances to "nebulae" (now known as galaxies) and spectroscopy to detect their redshifts, demonstrating that the universe is not static but dynamically enlarging. Earlier theoretical foundations were laid by Alexander Friedmann in 1922 and Georges Lematre in 1927, who derived expanding universe models from Einstein's general relativity, with Lematre proposing a "primeval atom" akin to the Big Bang. The current expansion rate, or Hubble constant, is estimated at around 70 km/s/Mpc based on data from missions like the Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe (WMAP) and the Hubble Space Telescope, with values ranging from about 67 to 73 km/s/Mpc due to the ongoing Hubble tension between early-universe and local measurements, though precise resolution remains a topic of active refinement.
The acceleration of expansion was discovered in 1998 through observations of Type Ia supernovae by teams led by Saul Perlmutter, Brian Schmidt, and Adam Riess, who found these "standard candle" explosions appearing fainter and more distant than expected in a decelerating universe, earning them the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics. This revelation implies that dark energy dominates the universe's fate, potentially leading to an ever-accelerating "Big Rip" or continued dilution, though its exact naturepossibly vacuum energy or a dynamic field called quintessenceremains unknown. Early in cosmic history, a brief phase of rapid expansion called inflation occurred fractions of a second after the Big Bang, stretching the universe exponentially and smoothing out irregularities to explain its observed flatness and uniformity on large scales. Ongoing missions such as the James Webb Space Telescope, Euclid, and the Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope continue to probe these dynamics, refining our understanding of the universe's composition (roughly 5% ordinary matter, 25% dark matter, and 70% dark energy) and evolution.

Historical Development

Early Observations and Theories

In the 18th century, philosophical and early astronomical speculations laid groundwork for questioning the static, finite nature of the cosmos. Immanuel Kant, in his 1755 work Universal Natural History and Theory of the Heavens, proposed the "island universe" hypothesis, suggesting that the Milky Way was merely one of many distant, self-contained stellar systems or galaxies, implying a vast, potentially infinite universe composed of such isolated entities. This idea challenged the prevailing view of a single, bounded universe centered on the Milky Way. Complementing such notions, Heinrich Wilhelm Olbers articulated a paradox in 1823 regarding an infinite, static universe uniformly filled with stars: if the cosmos extended eternally without expansion or absorption, the night sky should appear as bright as the surface of a star due to overlapping light from all directions, yet observations showed darkness, hinting at unresolved inconsistencies in static models.
Early 20th-century spectroscopic observations provided empirical hints of motion among distant objects. Between 1912 and 1917, American astronomer Vesto Slipher at Lowell Observatory measured radial velocities for 25 spiral nebulae using a 24-inch telescope equipped with advanced spectrographs. His first measurement, on September 17, 1912, targeted the Andromeda nebula (M31), revealing a blueshift corresponding to an approach velocity of approximately 300 km/s, but subsequent spectra of other spirals like NGC 4565 and Messier 81 predominantly showed redshifts up to 1,800 km/s, interpreted as Doppler effects indicating recession from the Milky Way. Slipher's work demonstrated that these "nebulae" were not mere gas clouds within our galaxy but likely independent systems with systematic velocities, though their extragalactic nature remained debated.
Theoretical advancements in general relativity soon intersected with these observations. In 1917, Albert Einstein published a cosmological model derived from his field equations, positing a static, finite universe to align with the prevailing astronomical consensus; to prevent gravitational collapse, he introduced the cosmological constant term \Lambda, a repulsive factor balancing matter's attraction. That same year, Dutch astronomer Willem de Sitter explored an alternative solution: an empty universe with positive \Lambda but no matter, resulting in hyperbolic expansion where test particles recede from each other, producing redshift-like effects without actual motion through space. De Sitter's model, though devoid of observable matter, suggested a dynamic cosmos compatible with Slipher's redshifts as gravitational frequency shifts.
Edwin Hubble's 1929 observations at Mount Wilson Observatory provided decisive evidence for expansion. By resolving Cepheid variable stars in several nebulae, including Andromeda, Hubble confirmed their extragalactic distancesup to about 2 million light-yearsand correlated these with Slipher's radial velocities, revealing a linear relation v=H0dv = H_0 dv=H0d, where vvv is the recession velocity, ddd is distance, and the proportionality constant (later termed the Hubble constant) was initially estimated at H0500H_0 \approx 500H0500 km/s/Mpc. This velocity-distance law indicated a uniformly expanding universe, building on theoretical foundations like Alexander Friedmann's 1922 dynamic solutions to Einstein's equations.

Formulation of the Big Bang Model

In 1922, Russian mathematician and physicist Alexander Friedmann derived solutions to Einstein's field equations of general relativity, demonstrating that a homogeneous and isotropic universe could expand or contract over time, challenging the prevailing static model. Friedmann assumed a uniform distribution of matter and derived dynamic scale factors that described the universe's radius as a function of time, allowing for both open and closed geometries depending on the density parameter. His work provided the first theoretical framework for an evolving cosmos, though it initially received limited attention and was even critiqued by Einstein as an error before being acknowledged.
Building on Friedmann's solutions, Belgian physicist and priest Georges Lematre independently developed a similar model in 1927, proposing an expanding universe originating from a hot, dense state that he later termed the "primeval atom" in subsequent elaborations. Lematre linked theoretical predictions to emerging astronomical data by deriving a linear relation between the recession velocity of galaxies and their distancenow known as Hubble's lawwithout prior knowledge of Friedmann's paper, and estimated the expansion rate at approximately 625 km/s/Mpc based on available redshift measurements. This synthesis positioned the expansion as evidence for a finite-age universe evolving from a singular, high-energy origin, marking a pivotal step toward the Big Bang framework.
In the 1930s, American physicist Richard C. Tolman advanced the theoretical underpinnings by exploring the compatibility of cosmic expansion with thermodynamics, particularly how the second law implies an arrow of time directed toward increasing entropy, consistent with an origin from a low-entropy singularity. Tolman's analysis in his 1934 monograph showed that an expanding universe naturally aligns with the observed directionality of thermodynamic processes, as contraction would reverse entropy gradients, supporting the unidirectionality from a dense initial state. Concurrently, he proposed observational tests, including the surface brightness dimming of distant objects due to expansion, where the flux per unit solid angle decreases as (1 + z)^4 for redshift z, serving as an early precursor to verifying relic radiation signatures like the cosmic microwave background.

Key Discoveries and Milestones

In 1948, George Gamow, Ralph Alpher, and Robert Herman predicted the existence of relic radiation from the hot Big Bang as a uniform blackbody background with a temperature of approximately 5 K, arising from the thermal equilibrium of the early universe as it expanded and cooled. This prediction stemmed from their work on Big Bang nucleosynthesis, where the universe's initial high temperature would leave a detectable microwave glow after photons decoupled from matter.
The cosmic microwave background (CMB) was serendipitously discovered in 1965 by Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson at Bell Laboratories, who observed an isotropic excess antenna temperature of about 3.5 K at 4080 MHz using a horn antenna, later refined to 2.7 K as the temperature of a thermal blackbody spectrum. This uniform radiation across the sky confirmed the hot Big Bang model's expectation of relic thermal equilibrium, providing direct evidence for the universe's expansion from a dense, hot state and ruling out steady-state alternatives. The finding earned Penzias and Wilson the 1978 Nobel Prize in Physics.
Building on Edwin Hubble's foundational 1929 observation that galaxies recede with velocities proportional to distance, late-1990s observations of Type Ia supernovae revealed an accelerating expansion. In 1998, teams led by Saul Perlmutter (Supernova Cosmology Project) and independently by Adam Riess and Brian Schmidt (High-Z Supernova Search Team) analyzed distant supernovae as standard candles, finding their dimmer-than-expected brightness indicated faster-than-constant recession, implying a dominant repulsive componentlater termed dark energydriving the universe's speedup. These results, published in 19981999, revolutionized cosmology and earned Perlmutter, Riess, and Schmidt the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics.
The Planck satellite, launched by the European Space Agency in 2009, delivered precision CMB measurements from 2013 to 2018, mapping temperature and polarization anisotropies with unprecedented angular resolution and sensitivity. The 2013 results refined early universe parameters, estimating the Hubble constant at 67.4 0.5 km/s/Mpc, matter density _m 0.315, and dark energy density _ 0.685, confirming the CDM model's consistency with expansion history. Final 2018 data further tightened constraints, measuring the CMB temperature at 2.7255 0.0006 K and spectral index n_s = 0.9649 0.0042, providing robust evidence for inflationary expansion in the universe's first instants.
Observations from NASA's James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) in the early 2020s have probed high-redshift galaxies (z > 10), revealing unexpectedly massive and mature systems forming just 300500 million years after the Big Bang, challenging models of galaxy assembly timelines but affirming the overall expansion framework through confirmed large lookback times. For instance, JWST's 20222024 deep-field surveys identified galaxies like JADES-GS-z14-0 at z 14.3, with stellar masses up to 10^9 solar masses, indicating rapid early growth consistent with an expanding universe but requiring refinements to star formation efficiency. These findings, while prompting reevaluations of reionization and dark matter roles, bolster the metric expansion paradigm by extending redshift-distance relations to the cosmic dawn.
In November 2025, a study led by Young-Wook Lee at Yonsei University, published in Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society, analyzed Type Ia supernovae data and suggested that the universe's expansion may have already transitioned to a decelerating phase at the present epoch. Accounting for progenitor age biases in supernova luminosities and aligning with baryon acoustic oscillation measurements from the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI), the results imply that dark energy is evolving over time rather than constant, potentially marking a paradigm shift in cosmology if confirmed by further observations.

Hubble's Law and the Scale Factor

Hubble's law, discovered by Edwin Hubble in 1929, states that the recessional velocity (v) of a galaxy is directly proportional to its distance (d) from Earth, expressed as $ v = H_0 d $, where $ H_0 $ is the Hubble constant, currently estimated around 70 km/s/Mpc. This relationship is observed through the redshift of light from distant galaxies, where light stretches to longer wavelengths due to the expansion of space, indicating that galaxies are moving away faster the farther they are. In cosmology, Hubble's law provides key evidence for an expanding universe, supporting the Big Bang model in which space itself expands uniformly in all directions with no central point. This is illustrated by the raisin bread analogy: galaxies are like raisins in rising dough, moving apart as the "space" (dough) expands, without the raisins themselves growing (see Metric Expansion of Space). The law applies on large scales and helps estimate the universe's age (around 13.8 billion years) and history, though the exact value of $ H_0 $ remains debated due to the "Hubble tension" between different measurement methods.
The Hubble parameter $ H(t) $ is defined as the fractional rate of change of the cosmic scale factor $ a(t) $, given by $ H(t) = \dot{a}/a $, where $ \dot{a} = da/dt $. In this framework, the scale factor $ a(t) $ parameterizes the relative size of the universe at different times, with distances between comoving objects scaling as $ a(t) $. The law arises from the metric expansion of space, where the apparent recession is a kinematic effect rather than actual motion through space. In decelerating phases of cosmic evolution, dominated by matter and radiation, $ H(t) $ decreases over time as the expansion rate slows due to gravitational attraction.
By convention, the scale factor is normalized such that $ a(t_0) = 1 $ at the present epoch $ t_0 $, providing a reference for measuring expansion history. For light emitted at earlier time $ t_e $ and observed today, the scale factor relates to the cosmological redshift $ z $ via $ a(t_e) = 1/(1 + z) $, linking the law directly to spectroscopic observations of distant galaxies.
On small scales, Hubble's law exhibits local deviations due to peculiar velocitiesrandom motions of galaxies relative to the Hubble flow, influenced by gravitational interactions within clusters and superclusters. These perturbations become negligible compared to recession velocities for distances greater than approximately 10 Mpc, where the linear relation holds robustly.

Shape and Curvature of the Universe

The shape and curvature of the universe are fundamental aspects of its global geometry, as encoded in the FriedmannLematreRobertsonWalker (FLRW) metric, which assumes homogeneity and isotropy on large scales. The spatial curvature is parameterized by the constant $ k $, which can be $ k = +1 $ for a closed universe with positive curvature (analogous to the surface of a sphere, finite in volume but without boundaries), $ k = 0 $ for a flat universe (Euclidean geometry, infinite extent), or $ k = -1 $ for an open universe with negative curvature (hyperbolic geometry, also infinite). This parameter influences the overall topology and fate of the universe's expansion.
The curvature's impact on cosmology is quantified through the density parameter $ \Omega_k = -\frac{k c^2}{H^2 a^2} $, where $ c $ is the speed of light, $ H $ is the Hubble parameter, and $ a $ is the scale factor (normalized to 1 at the present epoch). A value of $ \Omega_k = 0 $ corresponds to a flat universe, while $ \Omega_k > 0 $ indicates negative curvature (open) and $ \Omega_k < 0 $ positive curvature (closed). Observations of the cosmic microwave background (CMB) anisotropies provide the most precise constraints on this parameter. Data from the Planck satellite indicate $ \Omega_k = 0.0007 \pm 0.0019 $ at 68% confidence level, consistent with a spatially flat universe where the total energy density $ \Omega_\total \approx 1 $.
In a closed universe with $ k = +1 $ and without significant dark energy, the Friedmann equation suggests that gravitational attraction could eventually overcome expansion, leading to a "Big Crunch" recollapse. However, in the standard \LambdaCDM model with dark energy, even closed universes continue to expand forever due to the dominance of the cosmological constant at late times. The near-zero value of $ \Omega_k $ from CMB measurements strongly favors a flat, infinite universe, ruling out recollapse on cosmological timescales under the standard \LambdaCDM model. The comoving distances in such geometries are modified by the curvature term, affecting measurements of angular scales in the CMB.
The curvature also manifests in the paths of light propagating through space, as photons follow null geodesics determined by the metric. Cosmological Horizons

Observational Consequences

Redshift and Recession Velocities

The expansion of the universe causes photons emitted from distant sources to experience a stretching of their wavelengths as space itself expands, resulting in a cosmological redshift. This effect is quantified by the redshift parameter $ z $, defined as $ z = \frac{\Delta \lambda}{\lambda} $, where $ \Delta \lambda $ is the change in wavelength and $ \lambda $ is the emitted wavelength. In the framework of the FriedmannLematreRobertsonWalker (FLRW) metric, the cosmological redshift arises because the wavelength of light scales with the cosmic scale factor $ a(t) $, such that $ 1 + z = \frac{a(t_\text{now})}{a(t_\text{emit})} $, where $ t_\text{now} $ is the time of observation and $ t_\text{emit} $ is the time of emission.
This redshift is interpreted as an indicator of recession velocities, particularly for nearby galaxies where the approximation $ v \approx c z $ holds, with $ c $ the speed of light; this linear relation, known as Hubble's law, connects the redshift to the expansion rate $ H_0 d $, where $ d $ is the distance. For higher redshifts, a more precise relativistic expression for the recession velocity is used, derived from the special relativistic Doppler formula: $ v = c \frac{(1 + z)^2 - 1}{(1 + z)^2 + 1} $. This formula provides an effective velocity interpretation but does not imply actual superluminal motion, as the cosmological expansion exceeds the speed of light for distant objects without violating relativity.
Redshifts observed in astronomy include three main types: cosmological redshift due to the uniform expansion of space, Doppler redshift from peculiar motions of objects relative to the Hubble flow (such as local galaxy velocities), and gravitational redshift from differences in gravitational potential, which is typically negligible for extragalactic sources compared to the other effects. For distant galaxies, the dominant contribution is cosmological, while Doppler effects account for small deviations in nearby systems.
Observationally, cosmological redshifts are measured spectroscopically by identifying the displacement of characteristic absorption or emission linessuch as the calcium H and K lines or hydrogen Balmer seriesto longer (redder) wavelengths in the spectra of distant galaxies. These shifts, first systematically noted in early 20th-century observations, provide direct evidence of recession and allow mapping of the universe's expansion history.

Effects on Cosmic Temperature

The expansion of the universe leads to a progressive cooling of the cosmic microwave background (CMB), the relic thermal radiation from the early hot phase of cosmic evolution. In the standard cosmological model, the temperature $ T $ of the CMB scales inversely with the scale factor $ a(t) $ of the universe, such that $ T \propto 1/a(t) $. This relation arises because the wavelengths of photons are stretched by the metric expansion, maintaining the blackbody spectrum but shifting it to lower energies and temperatures. Initially, during the hot plasma era shortly after the Big Bang, the CMB temperature was on the order of thousands of Kelvin, enabling processes like nucleosynthesis and recombination; today, it has cooled to $ T = 2.72548 \pm 0.00057 $ K as measured by the Planck satellite.
The number density of CMB photons $ n_\gamma $ evolves as $ n_\gamma \propto T^3 \propto 1/a^3 $, reflecting the conservation of photon number in the absence of significant creation or annihilation processes after recombination. For a blackbody distribution, this scaling follows directly from the thermodynamic properties of photon gas, where the number density is given by $ n_\gamma = \frac{2 \zeta(3)}{\pi^2} \left( \frac{k_B T}{\hbar c} \right)^3 $, with the current value approximately 411 photons per cubic centimeter. Similarly, the energy density of the radiation $ u $ scales as $ u \propto T^4 \propto 1/a^4 $, combining the $ 1/a^3 $ dilution of photon number with an additional $ 1/a $ factor from the redshift-induced decrease in individual photon energy $ E \propto 1/a $. This steeper decline distinguishes radiation from non-relativistic matter, whose energy density evolves only as $ 1/a^3 $.
Observationally, the expansion affects the perceived brightness of distant cosmic sources, particularly through surface brightness dimming. For extended sources like galaxies or the CMB itself at high redshifts, the observed bolometric surface brightness diminishes by a factor of $ (1+z)^4 $, where $ z $ is the redshift ($ 1+z = 1/a $). This factor incorporates two contributions from photon energy loss due to redshift, one from the reduced rate of photon arrival caused by time dilation, and an additional effect from the angular size distance in the luminosity distance formula, where flux $ F \propto 1/d_L^2 $ and $ d_L = (1+z)^2 d_A $ for flat universes. The redshift stretches photon wavelengths, effectively cooling the observed spectrum and reducing flux accordingly.
In the future, as the universe enters a dark energy-dominated era with exponential expansion ($ a(t) \propto e^{H t} $, where $ H $ is the constant Hubble parameter), the CMB temperature will continue to decrease asymptotically toward zero. This cooling will render the CMB an increasingly dilute and cold radiation field, with photon energies dropping exponentially over cosmic timescales, though the blackbody form persists.

Impact on Density and Structure Formation

The expansion of the universe causes the energy densities of its constituents to evolve differently, profoundly affecting the distribution and clustering of matter. In the standard CDM model, the energy density of non-relativistic matter, including baryons and cold dark matter, dilutes as ma3\rho_m \propto a^{-3}ma3, where aaa is the scale factor, due to the increasing volume occupied by a fixed number of particles. The energy density of radiation, comprising photons and relativistic species, scales more rapidly as ra4\rho_r \propto a^{-4}ra4, incorporating both volume dilution and the redshift-induced loss of individual particle energies. In contrast, dark energy, often represented by a cosmological constant, maintains a constant density \rho_\Lambda, unaffected by expansion. These scalings determine the relative dominance of components over cosmic time, with radiation initially prevailing, followed by matter, and eventually dark energy driving accelerated expansion.
This differential dilution influences the growth of density perturbations, the seeds of cosmic structure. During the radiation-dominated epoch, the rapid expansion suppresses the amplification of small-scale fluctuations below the particle horizon, where relativistic pressure prevents gravitational collapse; perturbations on these sub-horizon scales remain nearly frozen or oscillate without significant growth. The Jeans length, the characteristic scale separating pressure-supported and gravity-dominated regimes, aligns closely with the horizon size in this era, inhibiting the formation of bound structures until the transition to matter domination around redshift z3400z \approx 3400z3400. Only then does the slower expansion allow non-relativistic matter perturbations to grow linearly with aaa, enabling the coalescence into galaxies and clusters.
A key relic of the early universe's plasma dynamics is baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO), which manifest as frozen comoving scales imprinted by sound waves propagating in the tightly coupled baryon-photon fluid before recombination. These acoustic modes, with a characteristic wavelength set by the sound horizon at decoupling (approximately 150 Mpc today), decouple after the universe becomes neutral, leaving density enhancements that persist as preferred separation scales in the galaxy distribution. Expansion stretches these comoving features without altering their relative sizes, making BAO a standard ruler for probing cosmic distances and the expansion history.
On supergalactic scales, the interplay of expansion and gravity shapes the observed large-scale structure, characterized by a filamentary cosmic web. Overdense regions experience gravitational infall along one or two dimensions, forming elongated filaments and sheets, while underdense voids expand faster than the mean Hubble flow due to reduced self-gravity, amplifying their emptiness and pushing surrounding matter outward. This differential expansion separates overdensities, with filaments connecting massive clusters at nodes and enclosing vast voids that occupy over 80% of the cosmic volume, as revealed by surveys like the Sloan Digital Sky Survey.

Matter-Dominated Epoch

The matter-dominated epoch began at the time of matter-radiation equality, approximately 50,000 years after the Big Bang at a redshift of $ z \approx 3400 $, when the energy density of matter surpassed that of radiation due to the faster dilution of radiation density with expansion. The current accepted value for the Hubble constant, H0H_0H0, which quantifies the present-day expansion rate of the universe, lies in a consensus range of approximately 67 to 74 km s1^{-1}1 Mpc1^{-1}1, reflecting significant discrepancies between different measurement approaches. The origins of the Hubble tension remain debated, with potential explanations including systematic errors in observations or indications of new physics beyond the standard \LambdaCDM model. Systematic uncertainties may arise from Cepheid calibration issues, such as metallicity effects or crowding in stellar fields, or from gravitational lensing magnification biases in supernova distances. Alternatively, the tension could signal modifications to \LambdaCDM, such as evolving dark energy with a time-varying equation of state, early-universe physics like additional relativistic degrees of freedom, or non-standard recombination processes that alter CMB interpretations.

If unresolved, the Hubble tension poses profound challenges to the \LambdaCDM model, potentially requiring revisions to our understanding of cosmic evolution. A higher H0H_0H0 implies a younger universe for fixed matter and dark energy densities, conflicting with the CMB-derived age of approximately 13.8 billion years and affecting calculations of the observable horizon size and particle horizon distance. This could necessitate incorporating new components, such as interacting dark energy or primordial non-Gaussianities, to reconcile datasets and maintain cosmological consistency.

Observational Probes

The cosmic microwave background (CMB) provides a key observational probe of the universe's expansion through its angular power spectrum, where the positions and amplitudes of acoustic peaks align precisely with predictions from the CDM model, incorporating the effects of early universe expansion during recombination. The integrated Sachs-Wolfe (ISW) effect, arising from the time-varying gravitational potentials due to ongoing expansion, contributes a detectable late-time signal that further corroborates the model's expansion history, with cross-correlations between CMB maps and large-scale structure yielding detections at over 3 significance.
Type Ia supernovae serve as standardized candles to trace the expansion via their light curves and the magnitude-redshift relation, revealing an accelerating phase at redshifts z < 1 that deviates from a decelerating, matter-dominated expansion. Observations of these events, calibrated against nearby samples, show that their peak luminosities dim with redshift in a manner consistent with dark energy driving the acceleration, providing evidence for a transition from deceleration to acceleration around z 0.6.
Large-scale galaxy redshift surveys, such as those from the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS), probe expansion through the two-point correlation function of galaxy distributions and the properties of cosmic voids, which evolve in size and density contrast according to the time-dependent matter density parameter _m(z). Measurements indicate that voids expand faster than the mean Hubble flow in an accelerating universe, with void-galaxy cross-correlations exhibiting redshift-space distortions that match expectations for evolving _m(z) from z 0 to 1, supporting the CDM framework's integrated expansion history.
High-redshift quasars and gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) extend the Hubble diagram to z > 6, using quasar UV/X-ray luminosity relations and GRB afterglow correlations as distance indicators to confirm a smooth expansion without abrupt changes. Quasar samples up to z 7.5 align with CDM predictions for the luminosity distance, while GRBs at z > 6 provide complementary probes, together validating the model's consistency across cosmic epochs without evidence for deviations in the expansion rate.

Addressing Misconceptions

Nature of Spatial Expansion

The expansion of the universe refers to the metric expansion of space itself, whereby the distances between gravitationally unbound objects increase over time according to general relativity. This process stretches the fabric of spacetime uniformly on large scales, rather than objects moving through a fixed space.
Cosmic expansion primarily affects systems on large intergalactic scales beyond galaxy clusters (several Mpc), where gravitational binding is insufficient to counteract the stretching of space. In contrast, bound systemssuch as atoms, the solar system, planets, stars, and galaxiesare held together by stronger forces like electromagnetism and gravity, which overpower the expansion and maintain fixed proper distances within them. For instance, the diameter of the Milky Way galaxy remains stable at about 100,000 light-years, unaffected by the overall cosmic flow due to its gravitational cohesion.
A common misconception portrays the Big Bang as an explosion from a central point, with matter flying outward into empty space. This "explosion analogy" conveys the idea of rapid outward expansion from a hot, dense state but is misleading because it implies a central explosion point (the universe has no center) and expansion into pre-existing space (space itself expands; there is no "outside").
A preferred analogy represents galaxies as dots on the surface of an inflating balloon. As the balloon expands, the dots move apart uniformly with no preferred center; every observer sees all others receding. This illustrates homogeneous expansion, demonstrates that space itself stretches rather than objects moving through fixed space, and shows the metric expansion of space. However, the analogy is two-dimensional (the surface of a three-dimensional balloon), which can lead to confusion about a higher-dimensional "outside" or interior (though only the surface is relevant), and the balloon has a finite boundary, unlike the possibly infinite universe.
The balloon analogy must not be misinterpreted to suggest that the universe is an expanding bubble that will "pop" like a soap bubble. The universe's expansion is the uniform stretching of space itself, with no edge, center, or pre-existing exterior space into which it expands; there is no "outside" that could cause it to burst. Unlike a literal bubble, there is no boundary that can rupture, and the analogy only illustrates the separation of points due to metric expansion without implying any catastrophic popping event.
This misconception is distinct from the unrelated speculative theory of false vacuum decay, a quantum process in which a bubble of lower-energy vacuum could nucleate and expand near the speed of light, potentially altering fundamental physics catastrophically within the affected region. Such decay is extremely improbable, with some estimates placing the probability as low as 1 in 10^{868}.
Similar analogies, such as raisins in expanding dough (raisin-bread model), provide a three-dimensional illustration of the same principles. Cosmologists generally prefer the balloon or raisin-bread analogies over the explosion analogy because they better avoid implying a center or expansion into external space.
From any vantage point, distant galaxies appear to recede isotropically, much like points on the surface of an inflating two-dimensional balloon moving apart without a central origin on that surfacethe analogy illustrates how three-dimensional space can expand without boundaries or an external "outside."

Superluminal Recession and Relativity

In the standard model of cosmology, the recession velocity of distant galaxies is given by $ v_{\text{rec}} = H d $, where $ H $ is the Hubble parameter and $ d $ is the proper distance. For sufficiently large distances $ d > c/H $ (approximately 14 billion light-years with the current value of $ H_0 \approx 70 $ km/s/Mpc), this recession velocity exceeds the speed of light $ c $. However, this superluminal recession does not violate special relativity, as no physical object or information travels faster than $ c $ through local space; the apparent velocity arises from the cumulative expansion of space itself between distant points.
The compatibility with relativity stems from the Friedmann-Lematre-Robertson-Walker (FLRW) metric, which describes the expanding universe in general relativity. In this framework, the superluminal recession velocity is a coordinate-dependent quantity representing the rate at which distances increase due to expansion, not a proper motion of galaxies through space. The metric's causal structure preserves the light-speed limit for local signals, ensuring that the spacetime geometry remains consistent with relativistic principles.
A helpful analogy illustrates this distinction: imagine ants crawling on the surface of an inflating balloon, where the ants represent galaxies and the balloon's surface expansion mimics the universe's metric expansion. Each ant moves locally at sub-luminal speeds relative to nearby ants, but distant ants recede faster than light due to the growing separation on the expanding surfaceno ant exceeds the local speed limit, yet the overall separation can become superluminal.
Galaxies beyond the Hubble horizon (where $ v_{\text{rec}} > c $) are observable today because light emitted from them in the past has reached us, limited instead by the particle horizonthe maximum distance from which light could have traveled since the Big Bang. This allows us to detect the cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation from a redshift of $ z \approx 1100 $, marking the epoch of recombination when the universe became transparent.

Expansion on Local and Galactic Scales

On local and galactic scales, the expansion of the universe becomes negligible because gravitational binding energies within these structures far exceed the effects of cosmic expansion, preventing any measurable stretching of space within them. In bound systems such as planetary orbits, atoms, or galaxies, local gravitational forces dominate, maintaining stable configurations over cosmic timescales. For instance, the Earth's orbit around the Sun has remained effectively unchanged despite the universe's expansion, with the cumulative effect amounting to a fractional increase of only about one part in 10^{24} over the age of the solar system. This stability arises because the tidal forces from expansion are orders of magnitude weaker than the gravitational binding, as demonstrated by general relativistic calculations showing that such systems follow geodesic motion unaffected by the large-scale metric expansion.
Peculiar motions, driven by local gravitational interactions, overwhelmingly dominate velocities on these scales, dwarfing the signal from the Hubble flow. These peculiar velocities typically range from 300 to 1000 km/s, arising from attractions like the Local Group's infall toward the Virgo Cluster, where measurements indicate an infall velocity of approximately 250 km/s. In contrast, the expected recession due to cosmic expansion follows Hubble's law, $ v = H_0 d $, with the current Hubble constant $ H_0 \approx 70 $ km/s/Mpc yielding velocities of only tens of km/s for nearby structures within a few Mpc. This disparity means that on scales up to several megaparsecs, gravitational perturbations obscure the smooth Hubble flow almost entirely.
At galactic scales, peculiar motions can even reverse the direction expected from expansion. The Andromeda Galaxy (M31), despite being about 0.78 Mpc from the Milky Way, is approaching rather than receding, with a relative radial velocity of approximately -110 km/s due to mutual gravitational attraction overriding the weak expansive effect. This peculiar motion highlights how local dynamics govern interactions within the Local Group, a gravitationally bound ensemble of galaxies spanning roughly 1 Mpc.
The Local Group itself remains gravitationally bound against the cosmic expansion, ensuring its future cohesion and eventual internal mergers. Simulations predict that the Milky Way and Andromeda will collide and merge in approximately 4 billion years, forming a single elliptical galaxy often dubbed "Milkdromeda," while other group members like the Triangulum Galaxy may also integrate over subsequent billions of years.
