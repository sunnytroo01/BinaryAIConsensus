Ecological succession
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Ecological succession is the process by which the species composition and structure of an ecological community changes over time, often progressing through a series of stages toward a relatively stable climax community that persists until disrupted by a new disturbance. This natural phenomenon involves the replacement of one community by another as pioneer species modify the environment, facilitating the arrival and establishment of subsequent species. Succession can occur in two primary forms: primary succession, which begins on newly exposed, barren substrates lacking soil or biotic legacy, such as after glacial retreat or volcanic eruptions, starting with hardy pioneer organisms like lichens and mosses that gradually build soil; and secondary succession, which follows a disturbance like fire, logging, or farming on sites with existing soil and seed banks, allowing faster recovery through herbaceous plants, shrubs, and eventually trees.
Key mechanisms driving succession include facilitation, where early species improve conditions for later ones by adding organic matter or reducing harsh abiotic factors; tolerance, in which later species establish independently as they compete for resources without strong dependence on predecessors; and inhibition, where early colonizers suppress competitors until external forces like disease or further disturbance remove them. These processes unfold over timescales ranging from decades in secondary successionsuch as the regrowth of grasslands to pine forests in abandoned fields within 2030 yearsto centuries in primary succession, as seen in the development of mature oak-hickory forests from bare sand dunes over 100200 years. Notable examples include the Indiana Dunes, where Henry Chandler Cowles documented succession from beach grasses to black oak savannas in the late 19th century, and post-fire recovery in North American forests, where grasses and shrubs give way to conifers and hardwoods.
The concept of succession highlights the dynamic nature of ecosystems, influenced by factors like climate, soil development, species interactions, and disturbance frequency, ultimately shaping biodiversity and ecosystem function. While traditional views emphasized a deterministic path to a single climax type determined by regional climate, modern understanding recognizes variability due to stochastic events and multiple possible endpoints.

Definition and Fundamentals

Definition and Scope

Ecological succession refers to the predictable and directional change in the species composition and relative abundances of organisms within a community, leading to alterations in community structure over ecological timescales, typically following a disturbance or the initial colonization of a barren habitat. This process involves the sequential replacement of one community by another as environmental conditions and biotic interactions evolve, resulting in increasingly complex ecosystems.
The scope of ecological succession extends beyond vascular plants to include animal, fungal, and microbial communities, where similar patterns of species turnover and functional shifts occur in response to changing habitats. It is fundamentally distinct from evolutionary changes, which operate on genetic and phylogenetic timescales spanning generations or millennia, and from short-term population fluctuations driven by transient factors like seasonal weather variations or predation pulses. Instead, succession emphasizes community-level dynamics on timescales of years to centuries, governed by ecological processes rather than genetic adaptation.
Central to understanding succession are the distinctions between autogenic and allogenic drivers. Autogenic processes are internally mediated by the organisms themselves, such as through soil nutrient enrichment from litter decomposition or shading that alters light availability, thereby facilitating the establishment of later-arriving species. In contrast, allogenic processes are externally imposed by abiotic factors, including climatic shifts, erosion, or catastrophic events like floods, which reshape the physical environment independently of the biota.
Community assembly rules describe the non-random patterns by which species are filtered into or excluded from developing communities during succession, often reflecting trait-based compatibilities and competitive hierarchies that promote coexistence or dominance. For instance, pioneer speciesthose that initiate succession in harsh, resource-poor environmentstypically exhibit traits such as rapid growth rates, efficient seed dispersal, short lifespans, and high tolerance to extremes in temperature, desiccation, or nutrient scarcity, enabling them to colonize and stabilize initially unstable substrates.

Historical Context and Importance

The concept of ecological succession emerged from observations by 19th-century naturalists who documented progressive changes in vegetation following disturbances, such as those on newly exposed soils or after fires, laying the groundwork for understanding community development over time.
A pivotal milestone came in 1899 with Henry Chandler Cowles' studies on the sand dunes of Lake Michigan, where he described how plant communities sequentially colonized and stabilized shifting sands, introducing the idea of succession as a directional process driven by environmental modification. Building on this, Frederic E. Clements formalized the theory in his 1916 monograph Plant Succession, proposing that communities develop predictably toward a stable "climax" state analogous to organismal growth, influenced by climate and site factors. In response, Henry A. Gleason articulated the individualistic concept in 1926, arguing that plant associations arise from the independent responses of species to environmental gradients rather than integrated community units, shifting emphasis toward probabilistic and species-specific dynamics.
Ecological succession holds critical importance in informing biodiversity conservation by revealing how communities recover post-disturbance, thereby guiding restoration efforts to enhance species diversity and habitat connectivity. It underpins land management practices, such as reforestation and agroecosystem design, by illustrating pathways to ecosystem resilience against perturbations like climate variability or human impacts. Furthermore, succession drives key ecosystem services, including soil formation through organic matter accumulation and carbon sequestration, where advancing seral stages increase soil organic carbon stocks, mitigating atmospheric CO levels.
Since the early 20th century, succession has solidified as a core paradigm in ecology, providing a framework for predicting long-term community trajectories and integrating disturbance into ecosystem models. This recognition extends to global conservation initiatives, influencing the management of UNESCO biosphere reserves, where succession principles inform zoning and restoration to balance human activities with natural recovery processes.

Processes of Succession

Stages and Seral Communities

Ecological succession unfolds through a sequence of developmental stages, collectively termed seral stages, which represent progressive changes in community structure and composition over time. The initial pioneer stage features low species diversity dominated by rapid colonizers, such as lichens and opportunistic plants, that tolerate extreme conditions and initiate ecosystem development. Intermediate seral stages follow, marked by rising complexity as more species integrate into the community, fostering layered structures and enhanced interactions among organisms. These phases build toward greater stability, with each stage altering the habitat to support the next in a directional trajectory.
Seral communities embody these transient assemblages, functioning as temporary ecosystems that drive succession forward through environmental modifications. Under the facilitation model, early seral species ameliorate site conditionssuch as by improving soil fertility or reducing exposureenabling the invasion and dominance of later-arriving species, which in turn displace their predecessors. This relay-like progression underscores the non-random nature of seral dynamics, where each community serves as a stepping stone in the overall sere, the complete continuum from initiation to maturity.
Key characteristics of seral progression include steady increases in biomass accumulation, canopy height, and the intricacy of species interactions, reflecting a shift from simple to more integrated food webs. Species composition evolves as well, with a decline in the prevalence of r-selected speciescharacterized by high reproductive rates and fast growth but low competitive abilityin favor of K-selected species that emphasize longevity, efficiency, and resource competition in denser environments.
Representative examples illustrate these patterns, such as in barren rock or glacial forelands where pioneer lichens and mosses colonize exposed surfaces, breaking down substrates to create soil for herbaceous plants and shrubs in subsequent seral stages, eventually giving way to tree-dominated communities. This lichen-moss to shrub-forest sequence highlights how seral communities progressively enhance habitat suitability, culminating in a balanced state akin to a climax configuration.

Mechanisms Driving Change

Ecological succession is propelled by a combination of autogenic and allogenic mechanisms that alter the environment and species composition over time. Autogenic mechanisms arise from biotic interactions within the community, such as competition, predation, and facilitation, which drive changes through organismal activities. In contrast, allogenic mechanisms stem from external abiotic processes that modify the habitat independently of the biota.
Autogenic mechanisms often involve species interactions that reshape resource availability and habitat suitability. Competition occurs when early-successional species deplete resources like light, water, or nutrients, creating conditions less favorable for their persistence but allowing more efficient later species to dominate. Predation and herbivory can accelerate turnover by selectively reducing populations of dominant early species, thereby opening niches for successors. Facilitation, a key process, happens when pioneer species ameliorate harsh conditions for subsequent colonizers; for instance, nitrogen-fixing plants such as legumes or alder enrich infertile soils by converting atmospheric nitrogen into bioavailable forms, enabling the establishment of nutrient-demanding species in primary succession sites like glacial retreats.
The tolerance model describes how later-arriving species succeed through superior resource use efficiency, tolerating and gradually outcompeting earlier ones without requiring environmental modification. Under this model, succession proceeds as species with broader tolerances or higher efficiencies invade and persist, even in the presence of incumbents, leading to a shift toward more competitive assemblages. Evidence for tolerance is limited but observed in systems where resource partitioning allows coexistence before dominance shifts.
The inhibitory model posits that early species actively suppress potential successors through resource preemption or chemical inhibition, maintaining dominance until disturbances or senescence release resources. In this scenario, pioneers occupy space and inhibit invaders via allelopathy or intense competition, halting succession until external factors remove them, after which rapid replacement occurs. This model is well-supported in many communities, particularly where long-lived species create stable but temporary barriers to change.
Allogenic mechanisms contribute by altering the physical environment through processes external to the biota. Soil development, driven by weathering, erosion, and deposition, progressively improves substrate fertility and structure, favoring complex vegetation in barren landscapes. Microclimate shifts, such as changes in temperature or humidity due to regional climate variations, can redirect successional trajectories by altering suitability for species assemblages. Hydrological changes, including alterations in water flow or groundwater levels from geomorphic activity, influence moisture regimes and nutrient cycling, often accelerating or diverting succession in riparian or wetland systems.

Climax Community and Theories

The climax community represents the mature, self-sustaining endpoint of ecological succession, characterized by a stable equilibrium between the biota and prevailing environmental conditions. In this stage, the community maintains itself through internal processes without significant external inputs, achieving a dynamic balance where species composition remains relatively constant over time.
Key characteristics of climax communities include high species diversity, which supports complex food webs and trophic interactions; closed nutrient cycles, where organic matter decomposition and uptake efficiently recycle resources within the system; and resistance to small-scale disturbances, allowing recovery without major shifts in structure. For instance, in old-growth forests, shade-tolerant species regenerate in the understory, sustaining biodiversity and nutrient retention over centuries. These features enhance overall ecosystem stability, though large disturbances can reset succession.
Theoretical frameworks for the climax community diverge on its predictability and formation. Frederic Clements's monoclimax theory posits a single, predictable climax per climatic region, viewing the community as a holistic superorganism where species interactions drive orderly development toward this endpoint. In contrast, Arthur Tansley's polyclimax theory recognizes multiple stable climaxes within a region, influenced by local factors like soil type and topography, creating a mosaic of edaphic or topographic climaxes alongside the dominant climatic one.
Clements's holistic perspective treats the community as an integrated entity akin to a living organism, with facilitation among species leading to the climax. Henry Gleason's individualistic theory, however, emphasizes species' independent responses to environmental gradients, portraying communities as chance assemblages rather than unified wholes, which undermines the predictability of a singular climax.
Regarding successional mechanisms, the relay floristics model describes discrete stages where incoming species displace predecessors through invasion and competition, aligning with Clements's ordered progression. Alternatively, the initial floristic composition model highlights the role of early-arriving species from seed banks or propagules, whose relative dominance shifts over time without major replacements, particularly in secondary succession. These models, proposed by Frank Egler, illustrate how initial conditions can shape the path to stability.
Criticisms of the traditional climax concept argue that such communities are rare in dynamic landscapes, where frequent disturbances prevent attainment of equilibrium. Furthermore, the alternative stable states theory posits that ecosystems can persist in multiple self-reinforcing configurations under similar conditions, with thresholds separating them; crossing these via disturbance or consumer activity can lead to abrupt shifts, challenging the idea of a unique, inevitable climax. For example, shallow lakes may alternate between clear-water and turbid states depending on nutrient levels and grazing pressure.

Types of Succession

Primary Succession

Primary succession refers to the sequential development of biological communities on newly exposed, barren substrates that lack preexisting soil, organic matter, or biological legacies, such as lava flows from volcanic eruptions, glacial tills from retreating ice, or shifting sand dunes. This process marks the initial colonization of lifeless environments, where extremophile organismscapable of tolerating extreme conditionsinitiate ecosystem assembly without interference from prior inhabitants.
The progression is characteristically slow, beginning with pioneer species such as lichens, mosses, algae, and nitrogen-fixing bacteria that colonize the sterile surface through long-distance dispersal of spores or propagules. These pioneers facilitate soil formation by secreting acids that weather rock substrates and by contributing organic inputs through growth and decay, gradually building a thin layer of soil that supports subsequent seral stages. Key challenges include acute nutrient scarcityparticularly limitations in nitrogen and phosphorusand the scarcity of propagules, as isolated sites depend on rare dispersal events from distant sources, often delaying colonization by decades.
Timelines for primary succession typically span decades to centuries, with soil depth and community complexity increasing gradually as environmental conditions ameliorate. On Surtsey Island, formed by submarine eruption in 1963 off Iceland's coast, pioneer lichens and mosses arrived within years, leading to 59 vascular plant species present by 2013, of which 39 had established viable populations, though seabird activity has since driven nutrient enrichment and grassland dominance amid erosion challenges; as of 2025, 78 vascular plant species had been recorded on the island. In Alaska's Glacier Bay, glacial retreat over approximately 200 years has enabled a chronosequence from bare till colonized by mosses and lichens, through nitrogen-fixing Dryas shrubs, to mature spruce-hemlock forests, with persistent phosphorus limitation constraining later stages. Unlike secondary succession, which accelerates on disturbed sites with residual soil and seeds, primary succession demands full de novo assembly from abiotic foundations.

Secondary Succession

Secondary succession is the sequential replacement of species in an ecosystem following a disturbance that removes vegetation but leaves the soil and some biological legacies, such as seed banks, root systems, and surviving organisms, intact. This process commonly occurs after events like wildfires, logging, hurricanes, or the abandonment of agricultural fields, enabling faster recovery compared to primary succession due to the pre-existing nutrient-rich soil and propagule sources. The trajectory of secondary succession is shaped by the severity and type of disturbance; for instance, a low-intensity surface fire may spare understory plants and seeds, promoting quicker regrowth from remnants, whereas a high-intensity crown fire that kills most above-ground biomass can delay recovery but still leverages subsurface structures.
The process typically initiates with rapid colonization by r-selected, disturbance-adapted pioneer species, such as annual herbs and grasses, which exploit open niches and stabilize the soil through facilitationaltering microhabitats to benefit subsequent invaders. As succession advances, competitive interactions and tolerance mechanisms allow mid-successional species like shrubs and perennial forbs to dominate, eventually giving way to late-successional trees and perennials that form more complex communities. In post-fire scenarios, such as in oak-hickory forests, surviving root sprouts and buried seeds enable herbaceous regrowth within the first year, followed by shrub establishment in 510 years and canopy closure by trees in 2050 years, depending on site conditions.
Timelines for secondary succession generally span years to decades, contrasting with the centuries often required for primary succession, as demonstrated in classic studies of old-field recovery in the eastern United States. For example, in abandoned agricultural fields in North Carolina, Catherine Keever documented a progression starting with horseweed (Erigeron canadensis) dominance in the first year, shifting to asters (Aster pilosus) in the second, and broomsedge (Andropogon virginicus) by the third, with pine invasion leading to woodland development over subsequent decades. This accelerated pace is attributed to the soil seed bank, which can contain thousands of viable propagules per square meter from pre-disturbance vegetation.
Variations in secondary succession depend heavily on the composition and abundance of remnant species, which can steer the community toward alternative stable states through priority effects and biotic feedbacks. Persistent disturbances, such as ongoing grazing, can arrest succession at an early herbaceous stage by limiting woody recruitment and favoring graze-tolerant grasses, preventing progression to shrubland or forest. In such cases, the ecosystem may stabilize in a deflected trajectory, maintaining lower biodiversity until the inhibiting factor is removed.

Cyclic and Seasonal Succession

Cyclic succession refers to recurring, non-directional changes in community structure driven by internal processes that create closed-loop patterns, often maintaining ecosystem stability without progressing to a single climax state. This form of succession contrasts with linear trajectories by emphasizing spatial and temporal heterogeneity, where disturbances like treefalls generate patches that regenerate in a cyclical manner. In forest ecosystems, gap dynamics exemplify this process, as canopy openings from fallen trees allow light-dependent species to establish, leading to eventual canopy closure and new gap formation elsewhere. Seminal work by Watt (1947) described these patterns in plant communities, highlighting how "upgrade" phases of colonization alternate with "downgrade" phases of decay, fostering a mosaic of seral stages across the landscape.
Herbivore-induced cycles further illustrate cyclic succession, particularly in coniferous forests where outbreaks of insects like the spruce budworm (Choristoneura fumiferana) cause periodic defoliation and mortality, resetting community composition. These outbreaks, occurring every 3040 years in eastern North American boreal forests, promote regeneration of early-successional species such as balsam fir (Abies balsamea), which then become susceptible to future epidemics, perpetuating the cycle. Mathematical models, such as those developed by Ludwig et al. (1978), demonstrate how predator-prey interactions and resource depletion drive these oscillations, with budworm populations surging when host trees mature and crashing due to starvation and parasitism. In savannas, fire cycles maintain grass-tree balance by recurrently killing woody seedlings while sparing established trees, preventing woodland encroachment; for instance, in African mesic savannas, fires every 25 years fueled by grass biomass limit tree recruitment to fire-protected niches. Bond and Keeley (2005) quantified this dynamic, showing that fire frequency directly correlates with grass dominance, as intense burns limit tree recruitment and reduce woody cover, with fire exclusion experiments showing substantial increases in tree density.
Seasonal succession involves predictable annual shifts in species dominance tied to environmental fluctuations, often resulting in transient community compositions that reset each year. In aquatic systems like temperate lakes, phytoplankton communities undergo succession from diatoms in spring to green algae in summer and cyanobacteria blooms in late summer, driven by nutrient availability, temperature, and grazing pressure. The Plankton Ecology Group (PEG) model by Sommer et al. (1986) outlines this sequence, where silicon depletion favors non-diatom taxa, leading to algal blooms that can dominate biomass for weeks. In terrestrial grasslands, phenological sequences dictate community dynamics, with cool-season grasses emerging first in spring, followed by warm-season forbs and graminoids peaking in summer; this temporal partitioning reduces competition and supports biodiversity. Ettinger et al. (2018) analyzed these patterns across North American prairies, finding that early-season events like leaf-out constrain later flowering by 1020 days on average, synchronizing with pollinator availability. Vernal pools in Mediterranean climates exemplify compressed seasonal cycles, transitioning from aquatic algae and amphibians in wet winters to emergent herbs and insects in spring, before drying to seed banks in summer; Keeley and Zedler (1998) documented this in California pools, where hydroperiod length determines species turnover, with short inundation favoring annuals over perennials.
These patterns arise from endogenous rhythms, such as mast seeding in trees, where synchronized reproduction every 210 years depletes resources and triggers off-years, influencing herbivore populations and regeneration. Sork et al. (1993) reviewed oak (Quercus spp.) masting, attributing it to hormonal cues that synchronize flowering across populations for predator satiation. Exogenous cycles, like seasonal flooding in riparian zones, impose rhythmic disturbances that scour channels and deposit sediments, favoring pioneer species in low-elevation floodplains while allowing succession to shrubs upstream. Lytle and Poff (2004) showed that flood magnitude and timing control these shifts, with high-flow regimes maintaining herbaceous dominance by resetting woody encroachment. Disruptions, such as altered flood frequencies from climate change, can desynchronize these cycles, amplifying variability in community resilience.

Influencing Factors

Biotic Interactions and Feedback Loops

Biotic interactions among organisms play a pivotal role in shaping the trajectories of ecological succession by generating feedback loops that either promote or inhibit community development. Positive feedback loops, often manifested through facilitation, occur when established species enhance the establishment and survival of later-arriving species, thereby accelerating progression to more complex seral stages. For instance, nurse plants in dryland ecosystems provide shade, reduce soil evaporation, and improve nutrient availability, leading to higher seedling survival ratesup to 13% greater under pioneer nurse species compared to open areas. These facilitative effects are particularly pronounced in early successional stages, where pioneer shrubs like Pityrocarpa moniliformis outperform late-successional trees in supporting target species such as Anadenanthera colubrina.
In contrast, negative feedback loops arise from competitive interactions that suppress subordinate species, maintaining diversity or slowing succession. Allelopathy exemplifies this, where plants release chemical compounds that inhibit neighbors' growth, creating bare zones and altering community composition. In Mediterranean forests, allelopathic extracts from pioneer Pinus halepensis reduce height growth in late-successional Quercus pubescens by up to 54% under combined competition and chemical stress, potentially delaying oak regeneration and prolonging pine dominance. Such negative feedbacks promote intransitive competition networks, where no single species dominates, fostering coexistence across successional phases.
Predation, herbivory, and mutualism further mediate these dynamics as key biotic interactions. Herbivory by large mammals, such as African elephants (Loxodonta africana), acts as a keystone process in savannas by browsing and toppling trees, which reduces woody encroachment and maintains open grasslands, thereby influencing successional shifts toward herbaceous communities. This creates habitat heterogeneity without broadly diminishing tree diversity, though it decreases overall tree abundance, with neutral effects on herbaceous cover, while increasing herbaceous diversity. Mutualistic interactions, conversely, reinforce positive feedbacks; for example, mycorrhizal networks connect tree roots, enabling carbon, nitrogen, and water transfer from mature individuals to seedlings, which boosts photosynthetic rates and survival in regenerating forests. In Douglas-fir (Pseudotsuga menziesii) stands, these networks facilitate interspecific resource sharing with species like paper birch (Betula papyrifera), enhancing establishment during secondary succession.
These interactions collectively influence biodiversity by regulating species coexistence. The Janzen-Connell effects, driven by host-specific pathogens and herbivores, impose distance- and density-dependent mortality on conspecific seedlings, preventing monopoly by early colonizers and promoting diverse recruitment patterns. In grasslands and forests, such negative feedbacks are widespread across functional groups, stabilizing fitness differences and maintaining high plant diversity throughout succession.

Abiotic and Stochastic Factors

Abiotic factors, including climate, topography, and edaphic conditions, play a pivotal role in shaping the trajectory and pace of ecological succession by determining habitat suitability and species establishment. Climate influences the overall direction of succession toward climax communities adapted to prevailing temperature, precipitation, and seasonal patterns, as seen in how regional climatic regimes dictate the composition of mature forest types. Topography affects microhabitats through variations in slope, aspect, and elevation, which alter exposure to sunlight, water retention, and erosion rates; for instance, south-facing slopes in temperate regions often accelerate succession due to warmer conditions favoring early colonizers. Edaphic conditions, such as soil pH, nutrient availability, and texture, filter species arrival and persistence, with acidic soils limiting calcicole plants while promoting acid-tolerant species like certain conifers during early seral stages.
Stochastic events introduce unpredictability by abruptly altering successional paths through rare, high-impact disturbances such as landslides or disease epidemics. Landslides, often triggered by intense rainfall or seismic activity, create barren patches that reset succession to primary stages, with the random nature of their occurrence and spatial extent leading to heterogeneous recovery patterns across landscapes. Disease epidemics, like the chestnut blight caused by the fungus Cryphonectria parasitica, can eliminate dominant canopy species, as occurred in eastern U.S. forests where American chestnut (Castanea dentata) comprised up to 45% of the biomass before its functional extirpation between 1900 and 1950, shifting succession from oak-chestnut to oak-hickory dominated systems. These events disrupt predictable progression, favoring opportunistic species and altering community structure for decades.
Such stochastic disturbances leave legacy effects that influence long-term trajectories, often resulting in hysteresis where recovery to pre-disturbance states becomes difficult or impossible due to reinforced alternative stable states. For example, post-fire shifts in boreal forests from spruce to deciduous dominance create legacies of altered soil chemistry and seed banks that resist reversion, even under changing conditions. In coral reefs, El Nio events exacerbate thermal stress leading to widespread bleaching, which disrupts succession by reducing competitive corals and allowing algal overgrowth, with legacy effects hindering reef recovery for years. Random dispersal events, such as wind-blown propagules arriving by chance, can similarly introduce founder effects that bias seral community development toward atypical compositions.

Dispersal and Environmental Filtering

Dispersal limitation refers to the constraints on the movement and arrival of propagules (such as seeds, spores, or larvae) to suitable habitats, which can significantly influence the rate and trajectory of ecological succession. In isolated environments like oceanic islands or fragmented landscapes, physical barriers such as distance, topography, or habitat discontinuity prevent species from reaching potential colonization sites, leading to delayed or incomplete community assembly during early successional stages. Dispersal vectors, including wind for lightweight seeds, animals for endozoochory (e.g., birds ingesting and excreting seeds), and water currents for hydrochory, determine which species can overcome these barriers; for instance, in fragmented tropical forests, bird-dispersed seeds often fail to reach isolated patches more than 100 meters from source populations, slowing secondary succession.
Environmental filtering acts as an abiotic sieve that selects for species with physiological tolerances matching local conditions, shaping community composition independent of dispersal arrival. In arid zones, prolonged drought filters out species lacking deep roots or efficient water-use strategies, favoring drought-tolerant shrubs and succulents that dominate early seral stages in desert succession. Similarly, in coastal salt marshes, salinity gradients impose strong filtering, where high-salinity low-marsh zones support only halophytic species like Spartina alterniflora capable of osmoregulation, while fresher high-marsh areas allow broader colonization by less salt-tolerant grasses during primary succession.
The interplay between dispersal and environmental filtering is illuminated by source-sink dynamics within metacommunity frameworks, where source patches (high-quality habitats producing excess propagules) subsidize sink patches (marginal habitats) through ongoing dispersal, maintaining diversity across heterogeneous landscapes during succession. In metacommunities, this linkage between local patches via dispersal counters filtering effects by introducing propagules that might otherwise be excluded, as seen in estuarine systems where tidal flows connect salinity-variable sites, enabling gradual community turnover. Stochastic events, such as rare long-distance dispersal, can occasionally bypass limitations but remain secondary to these core processes.

Succession Across Ecosystems

Terrestrial Habitats

In terrestrial habitats, ecological succession is prominently driven by vegetation dynamics, where plant communities progressively stabilize and diversify in response to disturbances and environmental conditions. Forests, grasslands, and shrublands exemplify these patterns, with primary and secondary succession shaping community structure over decades to centuries. Vegetation plays a central role in modifying soil, microclimate, and nutrient availability, facilitating the establishment of later seral stages.
Forest succession often proceeds through gap-phase replacement, a process where localized canopy openings from tree mortalitysuch as windthrow or diseasecreate opportunities for regeneration. These gaps, typically 1001,000 m in size, allow light-demanding pioneer species to establish initially, followed by shade-tolerant trees that fill the space and contribute to canopy closure. This mechanism maintains diversity in uneven-aged stands and contrasts with large-scale disturbances that reset entire areas. In the Pacific Northwest, old-growth conifer forests illustrate cyclic succession, featuring multi-layered canopies with dominant Douglas-fir (Pseudotsuga menziesii) transitioning to western hemlock (Tsuga heterophylla) and Sitka spruce (Picea sitchensis) over 200500 years. These ecosystems are characterized by large-diameter trees (>100 cm), high structural complexity including snags and downed logs, and enhanced nutrient retention through slow decomposition.
Grassland succession emphasizes rapid recovery mechanisms, particularly after fire, which is a frequent disturbance in these systems. Post-fire, perennial bunchgrasses like Andropogon gerardii in North American tallgrass prairies resprout from underground rhizomes within weeks, restoring cover and preventing erosion while suppressing weed invasion. This secondary succession favors fire-adapted herbaceous species, leading to a return to pre-fire composition within 15 years in mesic grasslands. Grazing by large herbivores, such as bison, further maintains prairie dynamics by selectively consuming woody seedlings and forbs, inhibiting transition to shrubland or forest states and preserving graminoid dominance. In the absence of these disturbances, succession can shift toward alternative stable states with increased shrub encroachment.
In desert and shrubland habitats, primary succession on unstable substrates like sand dunes progresses slowly due to extreme aridity and nutrient scarcity, often spanning centuries. Pioneer colonizers, including lichens and ephemeral grasses such as Aristida spp., stabilize shifting sands by trapping aeolian deposits and initiating soil formation through organic matter accumulation. Over time, this facilitates the establishment of drought-tolerant shrubs like creosote bush (Larrea tridentata), forming a stable seral stage. However, invasions by exotic annual grasses, such as Bromus tectorum (cheatgrass), disrupt this trajectory by accelerating fine-fuel loads and fire frequency, converting native perennial shrublands to invasive-dominated grasslands and altering long-term community assembly.
Across terrestrial ecosystems, succession culminates in climax communities that exhibit zonation patterns influenced by elevation and latitude, reflecting climatic gradients in temperature and precipitation. At higher elevations or latitudes, cooler conditions favor coniferous forests or tundra-like vegetation as climax states, while lower elevations or latitudes support deciduous broadleaf or shrub-dominated communities. These zonal shifts, such as the transition from montane forests to alpine meadows along elevational gradients, underscore how macroclimatic factors constrain potential climax types and vegetation dynamics.

Aquatic and Wetland Systems

Ecological succession in aquatic systems often follows a trajectory from oligotrophic conditions, characterized by low nutrient levels and clear water dominated by phytoplankton, to eutrophic states with high nutrient enrichment, increased algal blooms, and reduced water clarity. This progression, known as lake aging, involves gradual sediment and organic matter accumulation that shallows the water body, transitioning from plankton-dominated phases to communities featuring submerged aquatic plants such as macrophytes. In ponds, this process culminates in infilling, where emergent vegetation overtakes open water, leading to marsh formation and eventual conversion to meadow through ongoing sedimentation and plant colonization.
In wetland systems, succession is prominently shaped by peatland development, beginning with minerotrophic fens reliant on groundwater nutrients and progressing to ombrotrophic bogs fed solely by precipitation, which foster acidic, nutrient-poor conditions and specialized plant communities like sphagnum mosses. Vegetation shifts occur along a chronosequence, with early phases showing high nitrogen accumulation and diverse herbaceous species, followed by intermediate stages of peak carbon sequestration through peat buildup, and later dominance by stress-tolerant bog plants that deepen the water table. Tidal marshes exemplify accretion-driven succession, where pioneer species such as Spartina maritima colonize intertidal flats, trapping sediments to elevate the substrate at rates up to 29 mm per year, enabling transitions to higher-elevation communities like Sarcocornia perennis and Atriplex portulacoides over decades.
Hydrological drivers profoundly influence these successions, with flooding regimes acting as an environmental sieve that filters species based on tolerance to inundation duration and depth, promoting cyclic or directional changes in community structure. Salinity fluctuations, often intensified by sea-level rise or altered freshwater inputs, induce shifts toward salt-tolerant vegetation, potentially accelerating infilling in coastal wetlands or drainage in inland systems through altered water balances. In the Florida Everglades, reduced flooding has facilitated sawgrass (Cladium jamaicense) expansion into previously slough-dominated areas along hydrologic gradients, altering plant community boundaries and illustrating how drier conditions drive successional dynamics in subtropical wetlands. Overall, these fluid-mediated processes distinguish aquatic and wetland succession from terrestrial patterns, emphasizing submergent and emergent adaptations over soil stabilization.

Human-Altered and Restored Ecosystems

Human-altered ecosystems, such as mining scars and urban brownfields, often initiate secondary succession following severe disturbances that remove vegetation and topsoil, leading to the colonization by pioneer species adapted to nutrient-poor or contaminated substrates. In post-mining sites, vegetation development progresses through stages where early herbaceous colonizers stabilize soil, followed by shrubs and trees, as observed in a nine-year study of sandy quarry sites where technical reclamation accelerated cover but spontaneous succession fostered higher diversity. Urban brownfields, characterized by compacted soils and residual industrial debris, similarly support ruderal plant communities that transition toward woodland if unmanaged, though succession can be uneven due to fragmented habitats. These sites exemplify how anthropogenic legacies alter successional trajectories compared to natural disturbances.
Invasives frequently accelerate secondary succession in these disturbed areas by outcompeting natives in the absence of biotic resistance, particularly in urban brownfields where altered microclimates favor ruderal exotics. Urbanization enhances the invasive potential of alien species through increased propagule pressure and reduced competition, leading to dominance in early successional stages and hindering native recovery. For instance, in derelict urban lots, invasive grasses and forbs can rapidly establish, altering soil conditions and slowing progression to later seral stages.
Restoration efforts in human-altered ecosystems often employ assisted succession to guide trajectories toward desired endpoints, such as seeding native species to bypass slow natural colonization in post-mining landscapes. Active revegetation using native topsoil and seeds improves soil quality and promotes diverse plant establishment, as demonstrated in arid mining sites where assisted measures like tillage and amendments accelerated woody plant recruitment. Trajectory control involves targeted interventions, such as periodic mowing in brownfields, to maintain early-successional habitats or direct toward climax communities resembling pre-disturbance states.
Novel ecosystems emerge in human-modified sites as hybrid communities blending native and non-native species under altered abiotic conditions, such as urban forests that integrate introduced trees with local understory in post-industrial areas. These systems, influenced by ongoing human activities like climate shifts and land-use changes, deviate from historical baselines but can provide ecosystem services like carbon sequestration. A conceptual framework distinguishes hybridsamenable to restoration toward historical statesfrom fully novel ones requiring new management paradigms, with urban forests exemplifying the former through adaptive designs that leverage succession for biodiversity.
Challenges in these ecosystems include persistent invasions that disrupt succession and legacy pollutants that inhibit plant establishment and soil processes. Invasive species control is essential in restoration, as they reduce native seedling survival and alter nutrient cycling, necessitating integrated management like repeated removals post-seeding. Legacy pollutants, such as heavy metals from mining, constrain community assembly by imposing abiotic filters that favor tolerant species and slow microbial recovery, even decades after remediation. In a 2020s example, post-2019 Australian bushfire reforestation efforts in fire-prone eucalypt forests reevaluated tactics to incorporate assisted planting amid invasive grass proliferation and drought legacies, promoting rapid native regeneration to counter shifting successional pathways; as of 2025, initiatives like the Bushfire Recovery Program have planted over one million trees to support these efforts.

Biodiversity Dynamics

Changes in Plant Communities

During ecological succession, plant communities undergo predictable transformations in species composition, diversity, and functional traits, driven primarily by interactions among plants and changing resource availability. These shifts typically progress from sparse, opportunistic assemblages in early stages to more complex, stable structures in later phases, reflecting adaptations to increasing biotic pressures and environmental stabilization.
Alpha diversity, or species richness within a local patch, often increases rapidly in early succession as pioneer species colonize disturbed sites, peaks during intermediate stages due to habitat heterogeneity and niche opportunities, and may stabilize or decline toward climax communities dominated by fewer, competitive species. Beta diversity, measuring compositional turnover across sites or along successional chronosequences, tends to rise over time as environmental filtering and dispersal shape distinct community variants, enhancing regional heterogeneity.
Functional shifts in plant communities mirror these diversity patterns, transitioning from dominance by r-strategistsfast-growing, short-lived pioneers with high reproductive output and efficient resource acquisitionto K-strategists in later stages, which are slower-growing, longer-lived species emphasizing competitive persistence and stress tolerance. This progression aligns with trait syndromes, such as the leaf economics spectrum, where early-successional plants exhibit high specific leaf area and rapid nutrient turnover for quick exploitation of open resources, while late-successional dominants favor tougher leaves and conservative nutrient use for shaded, resource-limited conditions.
The primary causes of these plant community changes are competition hierarchies and resource partitioning, which intensify as biomass accumulates and alter resource supply ratios. In early succession, r-strategists outcompete others for light and nutrients in open environments through rapid growth; as canopies close, superior competitors for belowground resources (e.g., nitrogen or water) emerge, leading to hierarchical replacement where species with lower resource requirements (R**) displace those with higher thresholds. Resource partitioning further enables coexistence in mid-to-late stages by allowing species to specialize on different proportions of limiting essentials, such as light versus soil nutrients, reducing overlap and stabilizing communities.
A representative example is old-field succession in temperate regions, where herbaceous perennials like goldenrods (Solidago spp.) initially dominate nutrient-rich, sunlit soils post-abandonment, but woody shrubs and trees (e.g., pines or hardwoods) gradually invade and supplant them over decades, driven by shading and shifts in nitrogen availability. This herbaceous-to-woody transition accelerates in warmer climates, underscoring temperature's role in modulating competition dynamics.

Shifts in Animal Populations

Animal communities in ecological succession typically exhibit colonization patterns that begin with highly mobile generalist species adapted to disturbed or pioneer habitats, such as insects and small mammals that exploit open ground and early vegetation. For instance, in post-glacial landscapes, early colonizers like montane shrews and deer mice arrive rapidly due to their dispersal abilities, while forest-associated specialists, including red squirrels and porcupines, establish later as vegetation matures. This progression reflects a shift from opportunistic taxa tolerant of harsh conditions to those requiring structural complexity, with dispersal limitations delaying the arrival of less mobile species.
Trophic interactions further shape animal responses and influences on succession, with herbivores often modifying plant trajectories by selectively grazing pioneer species, thereby promoting diversity in intermediate stages. Predators, in turn, stabilize these dynamics by controlling herbivore densities, preventing overexploitation and facilitating the transition to more complex food webs. In aquatic systems undergoing restoration, such trophic cascades lag behind plant establishment, as herbivorous invertebrates respond more slowly to increasing algal and macrophyte biomass.
Diversity in animal communities undergoes high initial turnover during early succession, driven by environmental filtering and stochastic colonization, before stabilizing in later stages approaching climax conditions. Metazoan succession generally lags plant community development, with species richness increasing nonlinearlypeaking in mid-succession before plateauingas habitat complexity builds. Beta diversity remains elevated for poor dispersers, reflecting replacement rather than nested subsets.
Representative examples illustrate these shifts: in coastal dune heathlands, butterfly communities transition from open-habitat specialists like the grayling (Hipparchia semele) and silver-studded blue (Plebeius argus) in early grey dune stages to generalists such as the ringlet (Aphantopus hyperantus) as shrub encroachment and forestation reduce suitable open areas, leading to declines in threatened species. Similarly, in recovering tropical forests, small mammal assemblages show a decline in generalist abundance and richness as arthropod food resources diminish, with limited recovery of forest specialists despite structural maturation, highlighting trophic dependencies.

Microsuccession and Soil Processes

Microsuccession refers to the fine-scale temporal changes in microbial communities, particularly bacteria and fungi, that occur during ecological succession and drive early soil formation and nutrient dynamics. In initial stages of primary succession, bacterial communities dominate due to their rapid colonization and ability to fix nitrogen and weather minerals, facilitating the establishment of pioneer plants. As organic matter accumulates, fungal communities increase in abundance, often shifting the balance from bacterial to fungal dominance; this transition enhances decomposition of complex organic compounds, accelerating nutrient cycling and releasing bioavailable nutrients like phosphorus and carbon for higher trophic levels. For instance, studies on glacier forelands show that bacterial-fungal ratios shift from high bacterial dominance in young soils (within 5-10 years post-disturbance) to balanced or fungal-dominated communities after 20-50 years, correlating with increased litter input and reduced soil pH. These shifts are crucial for stabilizing soil aggregates and promoting pedogenesis.
Soil processes during microsuccession involve the gradual development of soil horizons, accumulation of organic matter, and modulation of enzyme activities that underpin ecosystem stability. Pioneer microbes contribute to A-horizon formation by producing extracellular polymeric substances that bind mineral particles, initiating topsoil layering within decades. Organic matter accumulation accelerates as fungal hyphae and bacterial biofilms incorporate plant residues, leading to humus formation and increased soil carbon stocks; in successional chronosequences, soil organic carbon can double from early to mid-stages (10-50 years) due to microbial necromass contributions. Enzyme activities, such as cellulase for carbon breakdown and phosphatase for phosphorus mobilization, rise with community maturation, reflecting microbial adaptation to resource availability; for example, -glucosidase activity often peaks in intermediate succession stages, enhancing decomposition rates by 20-50% compared to barren substrates.
Key interactions in microsuccession include rhizosphere dynamics, where plant roots recruit specific microbial consortia, and earthworm engineering, which bioturbates early soils to foster microbial diversity. In the rhizosphere, root exudates stimulate bacterial growth initially, followed by mycorrhizal fungal colonization that extends nutrient foraging; this succession improves plant phosphorus uptake by up to 30% in nutrient-poor soils. Earthworms, as ecosystem engineers, ingest surface litter and excrete nutrient-rich casts, promoting fungal proliferation and enzyme activity in nascent soils; their activity in early succession (e.g., post-mining sites) can increase soil aeration and microbial biomass by 1.5-2 times within 5-10 years, accelerating horizon differentiation.
Representative examples illustrate these processes. Post-glacial microbial mats in alpine forelands, such as those in the European Alps, form cyanobacterial-bacterial communities within 1-4 years of ice retreat, trapping sediments and initiating organic matter buildup that supports fungal ingress over decades. Similarly, mycorrhizal succession in rhizospheres during forest regrowth shows arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi replacing ectomycorrhizal types as tree species shift from pioneers to late-succession dominants, enhancing soil enzyme diversity and carbon sequestration in the process.

Modern Perspectives and Applications

Quantitative Models and Predictions

Quantitative models in ecological succession provide mathematical frameworks to predict community dynamics, transition probabilities, and long-term outcomes based on empirical data and mechanistic processes. These approaches range from probabilistic models that simulate stage transitions to differential equation-based systems that capture interspecies interactions, enabling forecasts of succession trajectories under varying conditions. Such models are essential for understanding how initial conditions, disturbances, and environmental factors influence the convergence toward climax communities or alternative stable states.
Markov chain models represent succession as a series of probabilistic transitions between discrete community stages, assuming time-homogeneity and ergodicity to predict stationary distributions of species compositions. In these models, transition probabilities are estimated from data on stage durations and transition likelihoods, allowing simulation of long-term successional pathways in forests and other ecosystems. For instance, in mixed boreal forests, Markov chains have been used to forecast the probability of reaching mature stages, revealing that non-Markovian effects, such as age-dependent transitions, can alter predicted outcomes.
Extensions of the Lotka-Volterra competition equations incorporate successional dynamics by modeling pairwise species interactions within changing environmental contexts, such as seasonal variations or disturbances that prevent stable equilibria. The basic two-species competition model is given by:

Recent advances include comprehensive state-and-transition frameworks for vegetation succession (Walker et al., 2024) and improved convergence in forest carbon models to better predict responses to climate change (Dymond et al., 2025).
Individual-based models (IBMs), such as SORTIE, simulate forest succession by tracking the growth, reproduction, and mortality of individual trees in spatially explicit environments, emphasizing resource competition like light availability. SORTIE parameterizes submodels for fecundity, growth, and survival from field measurements, enabling predictions of canopy dynamics and species coexistence over centuries. Chronosequence analysis complements these by substituting spatial gradients for time, inferring successional patterns from sites of varying ages along stable environmental gradients, though it assumes minimal divergence in trajectories.
These models generate predictions of trajectory divergence under scenarios like altered disturbance frequencies or initial compositions, often validated against long-term observational data. For example, at the Hubbard Brook Experimental Forest, simulations using the PnET-SOM model have been calibrated to multi-decadal plot data, confirming nitrogen retention dynamics during secondary succession and highlighting how early mining of soil nitrogen supports later biomass accumulation. Such validations demonstrate the models' utility in forecasting resilience to perturbations, with error analyses showing robust predictions for community-level metrics like basal area and species diversity.

Impacts of Climate Change and Anthropogenic Disturbances

Climate change is profoundly altering the trajectories of ecological succession by shifting environmental conditions that influence species composition and community assembly. In boreal forests, warming temperatures and prolonged droughts are driving transitions from tree-dominated systems to shrublands, as increased mortality of mature trees prevents re-establishment of coniferous canopies and favors resilient shrub species. These shifts disrupt traditional successional pathways, reducing carbon storage and altering nutrient cycling in affected ecosystems. Additionally, phenological mismatches arise when climate-driven changes in timingsuch as earlier spring onsetaffect interacting species at different rates, leading to asynchronies in trophic interactions that hinder community stabilization during succession.
Anthropogenic disturbances exacerbate these climate effects by fragmenting habitats and introducing legacy pollutants that impede recovery processes. Habitat fragmentation, often from land-use changes like urbanization and agriculture, accelerates invasive species establishment by creating edge effects and dispersal corridors, which alter competitive dynamics and slow native community reassembly in secondary succession. For instance, acid rain from industrial emissions has depleted soil calcium in eastern U.S. forests, causing long-term declines in tree growth, with recovery projected to span centuries even after emission reductions.
Recent studies from the 2020s highlight how intensifying disturbances interact with climate change to reset successional clocks. In permafrost regions, thawing induced by rising temperatures transitions microbial communities from stochastic to deterministic assembly, initially promoting diverse, transient taxa before favoring environmentally filtered dominants; this alters soil carbon dynamics and influences overlying plant succession toward wetland-like states. Similarly, increased fire frequency in fire-prone ecosystems, such as Australian eucalypt forests, reduces unburnt mature habitat by over 30% since 1980, repeatedly resetting succession to early seral stages and preventing progression to late-successional forests.
Predictions indicate that these pressures may lead to the loss of traditional climax states, where stable, mature communities fail to establish under rapidly changing conditions. Emerging no-analog communitiesnovel assemblages without modern precedentsare anticipated as species respond individualistically to novel climates, particularly in subtropical regions by 2100, resulting in unpredictable interactions and reduced ecosystem resilience during succession.

Restoration Ecology and Management

Restoration ecology applies principles of ecological succession to rehabilitate degraded ecosystems, aiming to accelerate recovery toward self-sustaining states by emulating natural processes. A core principle involves mimicking natural succession trajectories, where interventions replicate disturbance and recovery dynamics to foster community assembly and functional attributes like nutrient cycling and habitat provision. This approach draws from the Society for Ecological Restoration's (SER) international standards, which emphasize recovering ecosystem structure, function, and biodiversity through process-based methods that align with local ecological contexts.
Reference ecosystems serve as benchmarks in restoration planning, providing models for target species composition, community structure, and dynamics to guide project goals. These referencesoften intact nearby habitatsinform the selection of native species and expected successional stages, ensuring restored sites develop resilience and adaptability. For instance, SER standards recommend using reference models to accommodate predicted environmental changes while prioritizing native biota, thereby avoiding maladaptive outcomes in variable conditions. Monitoring is integral, with adaptive adjustments based on observed trajectories to maintain alignment with reference attributes.
Key techniques in restoration ecology leverage successional mechanisms to enhance recovery efficiency. Nucleation planting establishes small clusters of pioneer or facilitator species to create microhabitats that attract seed dispersers, such as birds, thereby initiating woody plant colonization and expanding forest cover more rapidly than passive methods. This strategy, tested in experiments spanning 213 years, yields higher densities and diversity of native recruits compared to unplanted controls, while being cost-effective for large-scale applications in deforested landscapes. Soil amendments, particularly organic inputs like compost or sewage sludge, restore degraded substrates by boosting microbial biomass and enzyme activities essential for nutrient cycling, which supports early successional plant establishment. In semiarid mine sites, such amendments increased plant cover from 42% to 8185% over 10 years, shifting microbial communities toward copiotrophic groups like Proteobacteria that accelerate organic matter decomposition and soil fertility.
Successional indicators guide monitoring to evaluate restoration progress across temporal scales. In high-diversity forests, such as Brazil's Atlantic Forest, key metrics include survival rates and soil fertility in initial stages (23 years), native species richness and basal area in short-to-medium terms (350 years), and ecological processes like pollination and seed dispersal for long-term assessment (>50 years). These indicators detect deviations from desired trajectories, enabling timely interventions to promote biodiversity and ecosystem services like water regulation.
Case studies illustrate the application of these principles and techniques. In China's Loess Plateau, coal mine reclamation utilized pioneer trees like Robinia pseudoacacia and Pinus tabuliformis to trigger succession, achieving a self-sustaining ecosystem state after 2023 years with high carbon sequestration and nutrient accumulation, though diverging from surrounding natural forests in composition. Post-hurricane mangrove restoration in the Caribbean employs ecological mangrove restoration frameworks, focusing on hydrological assessments and stressor removal (e.g., debris and barriers) before propagule planting, which facilitates natural regeneration and enhances resilience across fragmented islands affected by storms like Irma.
Challenges in restoration ecology include addressing climate uncertainty through adaptive management, which builds site-specific resilience by assessing threats over timescales (e.g., 30100 years) and fostering diverse, self-regulating systems via polycentric governance and ongoing monitoring. This approach counters variability in temperature and precipitation by promoting ecological feedbacks and social participation, as demonstrated in Rwanda's Albertine Rift where tailored activities mitigate habitat shifts. Policy integration, such as the EU Nature Restoration Regulation, complements these efforts by setting binding targetsrestoring 20% of degraded land and sea by 2030while enhancing the Natura 2000 network's coherence through habitat improvements and species recovery, ensuring alignment with broader biodiversity and climate objectives.
