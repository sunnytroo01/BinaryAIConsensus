The telescope is the instrument that transformed humanity's understanding of its place in the cosmos. Before the telescope, the universe appeared to be a small, cozy sphere with Earth at the center and the stars fixed to a distant crystal dome. After the telescope, the universe revealed itself to be vast beyond comprehension, filled with billions of galaxies each containing billions of stars, extending in every direction for billions of light-years. The telescope did not just extend our vision. It shattered our illusions about the scale of reality and our significance within it.

The first practical telescope was built by Hans Lippershey, a Dutch spectacle maker, in 1608. He discovered that placing two lenses in a tube could magnify distant objects by a factor of about three. The invention spread rapidly across Europe, and in 1609, Galileo Galilei built his own telescope and pointed it at the sky. What he saw changed the world forever.

Galileo discovered four moons orbiting Jupiter, proving that not everything in the sky orbits Earth. He observed the phases of Venus, which could only be explained if Venus orbited the Sun rather than Earth. He saw mountains and craters on the Moon, proving that celestial bodies were not the perfect, unblemished spheres that Aristotelian philosophy demanded. He observed that the Milky Way, which appeared as a faint cloudy band to the naked eye, was actually composed of countless individual stars too faint and close together to be distinguished without magnification. Each of these observations provided evidence for the heliocentric model proposed by Copernicus and undermined the geocentric model that had been accepted for nearly two thousand years.

The refracting telescope, which uses lenses to focus light, was the dominant design for over two centuries. Isaac Newton built the first reflecting telescope in 1668, which uses a curved mirror instead of a lens to collect and focus light. Reflecting telescopes have several advantages over refractors: mirrors do not suffer from chromatic aberration, the color fringing caused by different wavelengths of light bending at slightly different angles through a lens; mirrors can be supported from behind, allowing them to be made larger and lighter than lenses, which can only be supported at their edges and sag under their own weight at large sizes.

The evolution of telescope technology has been driven by a simple imperative: bigger is better. A larger telescope collects more light, which allows it to see fainter objects and resolve finer details. The largest optical telescopes in the world now have primary mirrors or mirror arrays with effective diameters of ten meters or more. The Extremely Large Telescope, currently under construction by the European Southern Observatory in Chile, will have a primary mirror thirty-nine meters in diameter, composed of seven hundred and ninety-eight hexagonal segments. It will be able to see objects in the universe that are too faint for any existing telescope, and it will be able to directly image planets around nearby stars.

Space-based telescopes have transformed astronomy by observing from above Earth's atmosphere, which absorbs, scatters, and distorts light in ways that limit ground-based observations. The Hubble Space Telescope, launched in 1990, has been one of the most productive scientific instruments in history. Despite its initially flawed primary mirror, which was corrected by a servicing mission in 1993, Hubble has produced over one and a half million observations, contributed to more than eighteen thousand scientific papers, and produced some of the most iconic images in the history of science, including the Hubble Deep Field, a ten-day exposure of a tiny, apparently empty patch of sky that revealed approximately three thousand galaxies, each containing billions of stars.

The James Webb Space Telescope, launched on Christmas Day 2021, is the most powerful and most expensive space telescope ever built. Its primary mirror, 6.5 meters in diameter and composed of eighteen hexagonal gold-coated beryllium segments, is more than six times the light-collecting area of Hubble. JWST observes primarily in the infrared, which allows it to see through clouds of dust that obscure visible light, detect the most distant galaxies in the universe whose light has been stretched to infrared wavelengths by the expansion of space, and study the atmospheres of exoplanets for potential biosignatures.

Radio telescopes detect radio waves emitted by astronomical objects, revealing aspects of the universe that are invisible to optical telescopes. Karl Jansky accidentally discovered cosmic radio emission in 1932 while investigating sources of static interfering with transatlantic telephone communications. Radio astronomy has since produced some of the most important discoveries in astrophysics, including pulsars, which are rapidly rotating neutron stars that emit beams of radio waves like cosmic lighthouses; quasars, which are supermassive black holes at the centers of distant galaxies that emit enormous amounts of energy as they consume surrounding matter; and the Cosmic Microwave Background, the afterglow of the Big Bang.

The Very Large Array in New Mexico consists of twenty-seven radio antennas, each eighty-two feet in diameter, arranged in a Y-shaped configuration that can be spread across a maximum baseline of twenty-two miles. By combining the signals from all twenty-seven antennas, the VLA achieves the angular resolution of a single antenna twenty-two miles in diameter, far exceeding the resolution of any single radio dish. The Atacama Large Millimeter Array in Chile operates at millimeter and submillimeter wavelengths and was instrumental in creating the first image of a black hole's shadow.

X-ray telescopes, such as the Chandra X-ray Observatory, detect high-energy radiation emitted by some of the most violent phenomena in the universe: supernova remnants, black holes consuming matter, galaxy clusters merging, and neutron stars with surface temperatures of millions of degrees. Because Earth's atmosphere absorbs X-rays, X-ray telescopes must operate in space.

Gravitational wave detectors represent an entirely new way of observing the universe. LIGO, the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory, uses two L-shaped detectors, each with arms two and a half miles long, to measure the minuscule stretching and compressing of spacetime caused by passing gravitational waves. The stretching is incredibly small: LIGO can detect changes in the length of its arms of less than one ten-thousandth the diameter of a proton. This precision allows it to detect gravitational waves produced by colliding black holes and neutron stars thousands or millions of light-years away. Gravitational wave astronomy has opened a completely new window on the universe, allowing us to observe events that produce no light at all.

The history of astronomy is a history of expanding horizons. Each new type of telescope, each improvement in resolution, sensitivity, or wavelength coverage, has revealed aspects of the universe that were previously unknown and often completely unexpected. Nobody predicted pulsars before they were discovered. Nobody predicted the acceleration of the universe's expansion before it was observed. Nobody predicted the detailed structure of the Cosmic Microwave Background before it was mapped. The universe is consistently more complex, more beautiful, and more surprising than our theories predict.

The next generation of telescopes will continue this tradition of discovery. Ground-based optical telescopes with mirrors forty meters across will image exoplanet atmospheres. Space-based infrared telescopes will study the formation of the first galaxies. Radio telescope arrays spanning entire continents will map the distribution of hydrogen gas across the cosmos. Gravitational wave detectors in space will detect signals at frequencies inaccessible from the ground. And neutrino detectors buried deep underground or submerged in the ocean will observe the universe through particles that pass through matter almost without interacting.

The telescope began as a simple tube with two lenses. It has become a global network of instruments that observes the universe across the entire electromagnetic spectrum and beyond. It has shown us that the universe is thirteen point eight billion years old, contains two trillion galaxies, and is expanding at an accelerating rate. It has revealed the life cycle of stars, the structure of galaxies, the existence of black holes, the remnants of the Big Bang, and the possibility of other habitable worlds. And every new telescope, every new instrument, every new technique of observation has the same message: there is more to discover. We have only just begun to see.
