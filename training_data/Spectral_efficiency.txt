Spectral efficiency

 Spectral efficiency is a fundamental metric in telecommunications that quantifies the capacity of a communication system to transmit information using a given amount of radio-frequency spectrum, typically measured in bits per second per hertz (bit/s/Hz). It represents the average number of bits of data that can be reliably conveyed per unit of bandwidth, serving as a key indicator of how effectively spectrum resources are utilized in wireless networks.
In broader terms, as defined by the International Telecommunication Union (ITU), spectral efficiencyalso known as spectrum utilization efficiency (SUE)is the ratio of the useful effect achieved by a radio system (such as traffic volume or transmission rate multiplied by service area) to the spectrum utilization factor, which incorporates bandwidth, spatial extent, and time denied to other users due to interference or occupancy. This multidimensional approach accounts for real-world constraints like spectrum sharing and geographical coverage, enabling comparisons between systems through relative spectral efficiency (RSE = SUE of the system under study / SUE of a reference system). Measurement can be theoretical, based on system parameters, or empirical, using monitored values for bandwidth, space, and time to compute actual efficiency (e.g., 54.53% for a specific frequency band like 1860-1875 MHz).
The pursuit of higher spectral efficiency is driven by the finite nature of the radio spectrum, a scarce resource essential for supporting growing data demands in mobile networks, broadcasting, and satellite communications. In digital systems, it is fundamentally bounded by Shannon's capacity theorem, expressed as $ C = B \log_2(1 + \text{SNR}) $, where $ C $ is the capacity in bit/s, $ B $ is bandwidth in Hz, and SNR is the signal-to-noise ratio, yielding spectral efficiency as $ C/B $ in bit/s/Hz. Key factors influencing it include modulation schemes (e.g., higher-order QAM for denser constellations), error-correction coding, multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) antenna configurations, and interference management techniques like beamforming. For instance, massive MIMO systems can achieve sum spectral efficiencies exceeding hundreds of bit/s/Hz per cell by leveraging spatial multiplexing to serve multiple users simultaneously, with improvements scaling linearly with the number of antennas under favorable conditions.
Advancements in spectral efficiency have been pivotal in evolving cellular standards, from 4G LTE's typical 1-3 bit/s/Hz to 5G's potential of 10-30 bit/s/Hz through enhanced MIMO and carrier aggregation, addressing capacity needs for billions of connected devices. However, tradeoffs exist with energy efficiency, as higher spectral efficiency often requires more complex processing and power, prompting research into balanced designs for sustainable networks. Looking toward 6G, emerging paradigms like faster-than-Nyquist signaling and AI-driven resource allocation aim to push efficiencies beyond 100 bit/s/Hz while integrating terahertz bands and non-terrestrial networks.

Fundamentals

Definition

Spectral efficiency is a fundamental metric in communication systems that measures the capacity to transmit information using a given bandwidth resource. It quantifies how much data can be conveyed per unit of spectrum, expressed as the ratio of the data rate $ R $ in bits per second (bps) to the bandwidth $ B $ in hertz (Hz), yielding units of bits per second per hertz (bit/s/Hz). This metric is essential for evaluating the performance of modulation and coding schemes in utilizing limited spectral resources effectively.
The basic formula for spectral efficiency $ \eta $ is

where $ \eta $ represents the spectral efficiency in bit/s/Hz, $ R $ is the achievable data rate, and $ B $ is the occupied bandwidth. This formulation highlights the trade-off between increasing data rates and conserving bandwidth, a core challenge in designing efficient communication links.
Unlike related metrics such as power efficiency, which assesses the energy consumption per bit transmitted (typically in bits per joule), or throughput, which denotes the gross data rate without normalizing for bandwidth, spectral efficiency specifically targets the optimization of spectrum usage amid finite availability. Power efficiency prioritizes low-energy operation, often at the expense of spectral utilization, while throughput emphasizes total volume but ignores per-unit-spectrum cost.

Units and Notation

Spectral efficiency is primarily quantified in units of bits per second per hertz (bit/s/Hz or b/s/Hz), representing the data rate achievable per unit of bandwidth. This unit is standard across wireless communication systems, where it measures how effectively the spectrum is utilized to transmit information. Alternative expressions include bits per symbol, particularly in the context of digital modulation schemes, where the number of bits encoded per transmitted symbol indicates efficiency before accounting for bandwidth occupancy. Another variant is symbols per second per hertz, which normalizes the symbol rate to bandwidth and is useful for evaluating uncoded transmission rates.
Common notation conventions employ the Greek letter to symbolize spectral efficiency in engineering literature. In information theory contexts, it is often denoted as C/B, where C represents the channel capacity in bits per second and B is the bandwidth in hertz, directly linking to fundamental limits on information transmission.
While the core unit of bit/s/Hz applies uniformly, variations arise between domains such as wireless and optical communications. In wireless systems, it focuses on radio frequency spectrum allocation, whereas in fiber optics, spectral efficiency is measured in bits/s/Hz, often in the context of wavelength-division multiplexed (WDM) channels, tying performance to channel spacing and capacity. For instance, optical systems may achieve efficiencies like 5.33 bit/s/Hz per wavelength channel in high-capacity transmissions.
To relate spectral efficiency to other metrics, such as area spectral efficiency, the unit extends to bits per second per hertz per square meter (bit/s/Hz/m), which incorporates spatial dimensions for network-wide assessments. This conversion typically multiplies the link-level spectral efficiency (in bit/s/Hz) by the spatial density of users or cells (in users per square meter), yielding the aggregate throughput normalized by bandwidth and area.

Link Spectral Efficiency

Calculation Methods

Spectral efficiency for individual communication links in single-user scenarios is typically calculated as the ratio of the information bit rate to the effective bandwidth occupied by the signal. In uncoded systems, where no forward error correction is applied, the spectral efficiency is given by = log(M) bits/s/Hz, assuming ideal Nyquist-rate signaling without excess bandwidth, where M represents the number of distinct symbols in the modulation constellation. For example, in quadrature amplitude modulation (QAM), larger constellations such as 16-QAM (M=16) yield = 4 bits/s/Hz under these ideal conditions.
When forward error correction coding is employed, the spectral efficiency accounts for the coding rate R, defined as the ratio of information bits to total coded bits (0 < R 1), resulting in = R log(M) bits/s/Hz. This adjustment reflects the overhead introduced by redundancy, which reduces the net information throughput relative to the uncoded case while improving error performance. For instance, applying a rate-1/2 convolutional code to 16-QAM modulation halves the efficiency to 2 bits/s/Hz compared to the uncoded baseline.
In practical implementations, the effective bandwidth B exceeds the minimum Nyquist bandwidth due to pulse-shaping filters and allocated guard bands to mitigate inter-channel interference. For signals shaped with a raised-cosine filter, the occupied bandwidth is B = (1 + ) R_s / 2 Hz in the baseband equivalent model, where R_s is the symbol rate and is the roll-off factor (typically 0 1), leading to a practical spectral efficiency of = / (1 + ) bits/s/Hz for coded systems (or log(M) / (1 + ) for uncoded). Guard bands further increase B beyond the signal's spectral occupancy, reducing proportionally; for example, a 10% guard band allocation on each side effectively enlarges B by 20% relative to the pulse-shaped bandwidth.
A representative derivation for binary phase-shift keying (BPSK), an uncoded scheme with M=2, illustrates the process. BPSK encodes 1 bit per symbol, so log(2) = 1 bit/symbol. The symbol rate R_s equals the bit rate R_b, as each symbol carries one bit. Under ideal Nyquist signaling (=0), the minimum double-sided bandwidth is B = R_s Hz for the passband signal, yielding = R_b / B = 1 bit/s/Hz. With a roll-off factor =0.25, B increases to approximately 1.25 R_s Hz, reducing to 1 / 1.25 = 0.8 bits/s/Hz.

Examples in Digital Modulation

Binary Phase Shift Keying (BPSK) and Quadrature Phase Shift Keying (QPSK) serve as foundational examples of phase-shift keying modulations, illustrating entry-level spectral efficiency in point-to-point digital links. BPSK modulates the carrier phase to either 0 or 180 to encode one bit per symbol, resulting in a spectral efficiency of 1 bit/s/Hz under ideal Nyquist signaling conditions. Its constellation diagram features two antipodal points on the in-phase axis, typically at +E and -E, where E represents symbol energy, providing robustness against phase noise but limiting throughput. QPSK extends this by using four phase states0, 90, 180, and 270to convey two bits per symbol, doubling the spectral efficiency to 2 bit/s/Hz. The QPSK constellation forms a square in the complex plane with points at ((E/2), (E/2)), ((E/2), -(E/2)), (-(E/2), (E/2)), and (-(E/2), -(E/2)), maintaining similar power efficiency to BPSK while enhancing bandwidth utilization.
Higher-order Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM) schemes build on these principles to achieve greater spectral efficiency, trading off increased susceptibility to noise and higher bit error rates (BER) for more bits per symbol. In 16-QAM, 16 constellation points arranged in a 44 square grid encode 4 bits per symbol, delivering 4 bit/s/Hz; the points are positioned at coordinates like (1, 1), (1, 3), (3, 1), and (3, 3), scaled by symbol energy. This denser packing reduces the Euclidean distance between symbols compared to QPSK, necessitating about 4 dB higher signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) to maintain a BER of 10^{-5}. Similarly, 64-QAM employs a 88 grid of 64 points to encode 6 bits per symbol, yielding 6 bit/s/Hz, but requires roughly 8.4 dB more SNR than QPSK for equivalent error performance due to even smaller inter-symbol separations. These trade-offs make higher-order QAM suitable for high-SNR environments, such as line-of-sight links, where the spectral gains outweigh the error rate penalties.
Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing (OFDM) integrates these modulation techniques across multiple subcarriers to boost link spectral efficiency in broadband systems like Wi-Fi and LTE, with subcarrier spacing playing a key role in overall performance. Each subcarrier can independently apply QPSK, 16-QAM, or 64-QAM, allowing adaptive modulation to approach the efficiency of single-carrier schemes while mitigating frequency-selective fading. In LTE, the standard 15 kHz subcarrier spacing yields longer symbol durations (66.7 s useful time), minimizing cyclic prefix (CP) overhead to about 4.77.1% depending on CP length, which preserves high efficiency by reducing the fraction of time spent on ISI protection. Wi-Fi (e.g., 802.11a/g) uses a larger 312.5 kHz spacing for 20 MHz channels, shortening symbol times to 3.2 s and enabling more subcarriers (52 total), but increasing relative CP overhead to around 16% in multipath scenarios; this design enhances robustness to Doppler shifts in mobile use at the minor cost of efficiency. The choice of spacing thus optimizes link efficiency by balancing overhead, interference, and channel coherence.
A practical illustration of these concepts appears in 4G LTE links, where under ideal conditionshigh SNR, minimal interference, and 64-QAM modulationspectral efficiency reaches 6 bit/s/Hz for single-antenna configurations, scaling to 78 bit/s/Hz with 22 MIMO by spatially multiplexing streams without additional bandwidth. This performance aligns with the general formula for spectral efficiency, = log(M) bits/s/Hz for M-ary modulation in noise-free scenarios, as outlined in prior calculations.

System Spectral Efficiency

Core Concepts

System spectral efficiency serves as an aggregate performance metric for wireless communication systems, quantifying the overall capacity achieved across multiple users and cells relative to the allocated spectrum resources. It is defined as the total data rate supported by the system divided by the total bandwidth allocated, thereby accounting for the collective throughput in multi-user, multi-cell environments. This metric extends beyond isolated connections to evaluate how effectively the spectrum is utilized at the network level, enabling comparisons of system designs in terms of scalability and resource optimization.
In contrast to link spectral efficiency, which measures the performance of a single transmitter-receiver pair in isolation, system spectral efficiency incorporates broader network dynamics including frequency reuse patterns, inter-cell interference management, and overhead from multiplexing multiple users. These factors can significantly degrade or enhance the effective use of bandwidth, as interference from adjacent cells reduces achievable rates, while efficient reuse allows the same spectrum to serve more users without proportional bandwidth increases. For instance, in cellular deployments, multiplexing overhead arises from resource allocation schemes that divide time, frequency, or code resources among users, impacting the net system throughput.

This formulation yields units of bits per second per hertz (bit/s/Hz) per cell, reflecting the average efficiency normalized across the network.
Frequency reuse patterns play a pivotal role in determining system spectral efficiency by dictating how spectrum is partitioned across cells to mitigate co-channel interference. In conventional cellular systems, a reuse factor of 1/3common in sectorized deploymentsassigns distinct frequency bands to adjacent sectors or cells, limiting each to one-third of the total available bandwidth and thereby capping the overall efficiency at approximately one-third of the link-level potential under ideal conditions. Modern systems increasingly adopt a reuse factor of 1 through advanced interference coordination, maximizing efficiency by allowing full spectrum utilization per cell while relying on techniques like beamforming to control interference.

Area Spectral Efficiency

Area spectral efficiency quantifies the performance of wireless systems in terms of throughput delivered per unit bandwidth and per unit geographical area, typically expressed in bit/s/Hz/km. This metric is essential for deployment planning, as it accounts for how efficiently spectrum is utilized across spatial extents, enabling comparisons between different network configurations and environments.
The formula for area spectral efficiency is given by _area = _sys / A, where _sys represents the system spectral efficiency (throughput per unit bandwidth) and A denotes the effective coverage area of a cell or sector. This derivation normalizes the bandwidth-normalized throughput by the spatial footprint, highlighting the impact of network geometry on overall capacity. Smaller values of A, as in dense deployments, can amplify _area by concentrating resources, while larger A reduces it due to extended coverage demands.
Factors unique to area spectral efficiency include cell size, which affects frequency reuse and interference; user density, which influences load distribution and multiplexing gains; and propagation models, such as path loss exponents that model signal attenuation over distance. For instance, urban environments with cell sizes on the order of hundreds of meters and high user densities (up to thousands per km) yield higher _area compared to rural settings with cell sizes exceeding kilometers and sparse users (tens per km), due to reduced interference and better resource utilization in compact layouts.
In 5G networks, particularly those employing massive MIMO, projected area spectral efficiency values from 2016 studies range from 27 to 31 bit/s/Hz/km, depending on the propagation modelsuch as the modified COST231 Hata model for urban scenarios (yielding around 28 bit/s/Hz/km at SNR of -13 dB) versus distance-based models more suited to rural areas (up to 31 bit/s/Hz/km under similar conditions). These model-based values, assuming comparable cell geometries, underscore the metric's sensitivity to environmental factors. In practice, urban deployments achieve significantly higher _area than rural onesoften 3-10 times or morethrough densification and advanced beamforming that enable smaller effective coverage areas. As of 2025, real-world 5G standalone networks in dense urban areas with sub-6 GHz and mmWave integration have reported values exceeding 50 bit/s/Hz/km in optimized cases.

Theoretical Foundations

Shannon Capacity Limit

The Shannon capacity limit represents the fundamental upper bound on the reliable transmission rate over a noisy communication channel, as established by Claude Shannon in his seminal 1948 paper "A Mathematical Theory of Communication," which introduced the noisy-channel coding theorem. This theorem demonstrates that, provided the transmission rate does not exceed the channel capacity, arbitrarily reliable communication is possible using sufficiently long codewords, even in the presence of noise.
The Shannon-Hartley theorem quantifies this limit for a band-limited channel subject to additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN). It states that the channel capacity CCC in bits per second is given by

where BBB is the channel bandwidth in hertz and SNR\mathrm{SNR}SNR is the signal-to-noise ratio. Consequently, the maximum spectral efficiency \eta, defined as the capacity per unit bandwidth, is =log2(1+SNR)\eta = \log_2 (1 + \mathrm{SNR})=log2(1+SNR) bit/s/Hz.
The derivation of this capacity formula assumes an AWGN channel model, where the noise is Gaussian distributed with zero mean and uniform power spectral density across the bandwidth BBB, and considers the asymptotic case of infinite block length for error-free decoding. Capacity is achieved by maximizing the mutual information between the channel input and output, with the optimal input distribution being Gaussian, leading to the logarithmic expression that reflects the exponential increase in distinguishable signal levels as SNR grows.
In the high-SNR regime, the spectral efficiency simplifies asymptotically to log2(SNR)\eta \approx \log_2 (\mathrm{SNR})log2(SNR) bit/s/Hz, highlighting a linear growth in bits per hertz with the logarithm of power relative to noise. However, practical implementations fall short of this limit due to finite block lengths, which introduce a non-zero error probability even below capacity, creating a performance gap that depends on the desired reliability and code complexity.

Practical Factors

Influencing Variables

Spectral efficiency in wireless communication systems is fundamentally influenced by the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), which quantifies the strength of the desired signal relative to background noise. The achievable spectral efficiency \eta is directly proportional to log2(1+SNR)\log_2(1 + \text{SNR})log2(1+SNR) bits per second per hertz, as derived from the Shannon capacity formula, establishing an upper bound on the information rate over a given bandwidth. Higher SNR values enable more efficient modulation schemes and higher-order constellations, thereby increasing the number of bits transmitted per symbol without expanding the bandwidth. However, in practical deployments, achieving high SNR is challenging due to environmental factors, limiting the realization of theoretical maxima.
Fading and multipath effects further degrade the effective SNR, introducing variability in signal amplitude and phase that reduces the average spectral efficiency. In multipath environments, signals arrive via multiple propagation paths, causing constructive and destructive interference that leads to deep fades, particularly in frequency-selective channels. Studies on cellular systems demonstrate that fading models such as Nakagami-m can reduce area spectral efficiency compared to additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) scenarios, depending on the fading severity parameter. Lognormal shadowing compounds these effects by adding large-scale signal variations, necessitating robust coding and diversity techniques to mitigate the impact on overall throughput efficiency, though the core degradation stems from the diminished instantaneous SNR during fades.
Interference from other users or systems significantly impacts spectral efficiency by lowering the signal-to-interference-plus-noise ratio (SINR), which serves as a more comprehensive metric than SNR in multi-user environments. Co-channel interference occurs when the same frequency is reused in nearby cells, while adjacent-channel interference arises from imperfect filtering in neighboring bands, both reducing the SINR and thereby capping the achievable rate via a modified capacity expression log2(1+SINR)\log_2(1 + \text{SINR})log2(1+SINR). In dense deployments, such as cellular networks, co-channel interference can substantially reduce spectral efficiency if reuse factors are not optimized, as evidenced in analyses of multi-antenna systems where SINR scaling directly correlates with throughput gains. Quantifying and managing SINR is critical, as even low levels of interference can force lower modulation orders, diminishing the bits-per-hertz metric across the network.
Protocol overheads, including control signaling, packet headers, and synchronization preambles, impose losses on effective spectral efficiency by consuming a portion of the available bandwidth without carrying user data. In modern systems like LTE, control channel overhead reduces the net data rate and thus the efficiency from the gross symbol rate. Synchronization signals for timing and frequency alignment further erode efficiency, particularly in time-division duplex setups where pilot overheads scale with the number of antennas. These non-payload elements are essential for reliable operation but systematically lower the payload-to-bandwidth ratio, with notable impacts in massive MIMO contexts at high user densities.
Bandwidth fragmentation, arising from guard bands and non-contiguous spectrum allocations, further diminishes spectral efficiency by leaving unused portions of the spectrum that cannot support data transmission. Guard bands, typically 5-10% of the total bandwidth, prevent adjacent-channel interference but represent wasted resources, especially in fragmented allocations where spectrum is licensed in disjoint blocks. Fragmentation can reduce utilization, as smaller contiguous slots limit the deployment of wider channels that offer higher efficiency. Non-contiguous allocations exacerbate this in cognitive radio scenarios, where dynamic access leads to multiple guard bands proportional to the number of fragments, directly lowering the effective bandwidth available for modulation.

Enhancement Techniques

Advanced coding techniques have significantly improved spectral efficiency by enabling reliable data transmission at rates closer to the theoretical Shannon limit. Turbo codes, introduced in 1993, achieve performance close to the Shannon limit for a bit error rate of 10510^{-5}105, using parallel concatenation of convolutional codes and iterative decoding. Low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes, particularly irregular variants, deliver near-Shannon-limit performance in additive white Gaussian noise channels through sparse parity-check matrices and belief propagation decoding. Polar codes, proven to achieve channel capacity asymptotically, approach the Shannon limit for binary-input symmetric channels in finite-length implementations via successive cancellation decoding. These codes enhance spectral efficiency in practical systems like wireless standards.
Beamforming and massive multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) systems boost spectral efficiency by exploiting spatial dimensions to serve multiple users simultaneously. Beamforming directs signals toward specific users, improving signal-to-interference ratios and achieving multiplexing gains that increase throughput without additional spectrum. In massive MIMO configurations with hundreds of antennas at base stations, spectral efficiency can improve substantially in dense urban networks through spatial multiplexing and interference suppression. These techniques mitigate interferencea key limiting factorby precoding signals to null unwanted directions, enabling higher order modulation and denser user packing. Recent advancements include AI-driven beam management to further optimize efficiency in dynamic environments.
Carrier aggregation combines multiple spectrum bands into a wider effective bandwidth, increasing overall data rates while preserving per-Hertz efficiency. In 5G networks, it aggregates carriers across low-, mid-, and high-frequency bands, supporting up to 16 component carriers to expand bandwidth from 20 MHz to over 1 GHz without proportionally increasing overhead. This maintains spectral efficiency \eta by scaling capacity linearly with aggregated bandwidth BBB, as =R/B\eta = R / B=R/B remains consistent across bands.
In 5G New Radio (NR), these enhancements culminate in peak spectral efficiencies of 30 bit/s/Hz downlink through higher-order modulation up to 256-QAM and multi-layer transmission with up to 8 spatial layers in massive MIMO. Carrier aggregation further amplifies this by combining bands for effective bandwidths exceeding 100 MHz, achieving the targeted efficiency under ideal conditions like low mobility and sufficient antennas.

Technological Comparisons

Efficiency Across Standards

Spectral efficiency in communication standards has evolved dramatically, driven by innovations in modulation schemes, multiple access techniques, and signal processing. Early wireless standards like GSM relied on basic time-division multiple access (TDMA) and Gaussian minimum shift keying (GMSK), yielding low efficiencies suitable primarily for voice services. Subsequent generations, such as 3G UMTS with wideband code-division multiple access (W-CDMA), improved throughput for data while maintaining compatibility with existing spectrum allocations. Fourth-generation LTE introduced orthogonal frequency-division multiple access (OFDMA) and multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) systems, enabling higher peak and average efficiencies. Fifth-generation NR further advances this with massive MIMO, higher-order modulation up to 256-QAM, and flexible numerology, achieving peak values over 30 bit/s/Hz in downlink under ideal conditions. In optical communications, dual-polarization quadrature phase-shift keying (DP-QPSK) in coherent systems leverages polarization multiplexing and digital signal processing to attain 2 bit/s/Hz, supporting terabit-scale capacities over fiber.

Additionally, real-world signal-to-noise ratios are lower than idealized assumptions because of fading channels and power backoff to manage peak-to-average ratios, preventing full exploitation of high-order modulations. These gaps highlight ongoing research into low-overhead waveforms and advanced error-correcting codes to close the divide further.

Historical Developments

The concept of spectral efficiency in wireless communications traces its roots to the early 20th century, when analog frequency modulation (FM) radio systems achieved approximately 0.1 bit/s/Hz, limited by the need for wide bandwidths to carry audio signals without distortion. These early systems prioritized signal quality over data throughput, with FM broadcast occupying channels up to 200 kHz wide for 15 kHz audio, resulting in low efficiency due to guard bands and simple modulation. The introduction of digital techniques in the 1980s marked a significant shift, exemplified by the IS-54 standard for U.S. digital cellular, which delivered around 1.6 bit/s/Hz through time-division multiple access (TDMA), tripling capacity over analog predecessors by compressing voice and reusing spectrum more effectively.
In the 1990s and 2000s, the adoption of code-division multiple access (CDMA) in standards like wideband CDMA (WCDMA) further enhanced efficiency, with WCDMA achieving up to ~2 bit/s/Hz in enhanced modes like HSDPA by employing spreading codes that allowed multiple users to share the same frequency band, reducing interference and enabling higher data rates for emerging mobile internet services. This era was driven by growing demand for wireless data, underscored by the U.S. Federal Communications Commission's (FCC) inaugural spectrum auctions in 1994, which allocated personal communications service (PCS) licenses and highlighted the scarcity of radio spectrum, spurring innovations in efficient utilization. Concurrently, International Telecommunication Union (ITU) standards, such as those for International Mobile Telecommunications (IMT-2000), established global benchmarks that incentivized efficiency gains through harmonized frequency bands and performance criteria.
From the 2010s onward, long-term evolution advanced (LTE-Advanced) and 5G new radio (NR) leveraged multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) technologies to push spectral efficiency beyond 10 bit/s/Hz in practical deployments, enabling gigabit speeds and massive connectivity by exploiting spatial multiplexing and beamforming. These advancements built on Claude Shannon's 1948 foundational work on channel capacity, which theoretically bounded efficiency but inspired practical approaches to approach those limits. Projections for 6G, as outlined in ongoing ITU IMT-2030 efforts (as of 2025), anticipate efficiencies reaching 100 bit/s/Hz through integrated sensing, AI-optimized resource allocation, and terahertz bands, reflecting continued evolution amid exponential data growth.
