 Nuevas Ideas (Spanish for "New Ideas") is a populist political party in El Salvador founded on 25 October 2017 by Nayib Bukele during his tenure as mayor of (/page/San Salvador). The party emerged as a break from traditional bipartisan politics dominated by the right-wing Nationalist Republican Alliance (ARENA) and left-wing Farabundo Mart National Liberation Front (FMLN), positioning itself as a movement for modernization, anti-corruption, and effective governance unbound by ideological dogmas.
Nuevas Ideas achieved legal recognition from the Supreme Electoral Tribunal in 2020 after initial hurdles and rapidly ascended to power, with Bukele winning the presidency in 2019 under an allied banner before the party contested elections directly. In the 2021 legislative elections, the party and its allies secured 61 of 84 seats in the National Assembly, granting a supermajority that enabled sweeping reforms. This dominance intensified in the 2024 general elections, where Bukele was reelected with approximately 85% of the vote and Nuevas Ideas captured 54 of 60 seats, solidifying its control over state institutions.
The party's defining policy thrust has been an unrelenting campaign against gang violence, including a 2022 state of emergency that facilitated the detention of over 80,000 suspected criminals, correlating with a plunge in the national homicide rate from 38 per 100,000 inhabitants in 2019 to a record low of 1.9 in 2024among the lowest in the Western Hemisphere. This security transformation, coupled with initiatives like adopting Bitcoin as legal tender, has garnered widespread public approval reflected in electoral mandates, though it has sparked debates over due process suspensions and institutional changes, such as judicial overhauls and constitutional amendments enabling consecutive reelection.

History

Founding and Initial Challenges

Nuevas Ideas was established on October 25, 2017, by Nayib Bukele, who had recently been expelled from the Farabundo Mart National Liberation Front (FMLN) amid internal party conflicts during his tenure as mayor of San Salvador. The formation of the party represented Bukele's effort to challenge the entrenched dominance of El Salvador's traditional parties, ARENA and FMLN, which had alternated power since the end of the civil war in 1992. Bukele positioned Nuevas Ideas as a fresh alternative focused on innovation and breaking from corrupt bipartisanship, drawing support from youth and urban voters disillusioned with established politics.
One of the primary initial hurdles was securing legal recognition as a political party from the Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE), which required verification of at least 50,000 genuine signatures from supporters across multiple departments. In August 2018, the TSE rejected Nuevas Ideas' application for provisional juridical personality, determining that a significant portion of the submitted signatures were invalid, including those purportedly from deceased persons or duplicates, thus failing to meet the threshold. Bukele and his supporters contested the decision, alleging deliberate sabotage by the TSEcontrolled by magistrates affiliated with ARENA and FMLNto preserve the political duopoly and block emerging competition.
These registration setbacks compelled Bukele to pivot, initially exploring alliances with smaller parties like Cambio Democrtico, which itself lost TSE recognition shortly thereafter, before securing the presidential nomination from the Grand Alliance for National Unity (GANA) for the 2019 election. Despite the obstacles, the grassroots momentum built by Nuevas Ideas persisted, enabling it to function as a political movement and eventually achieve full party registration in November 2020, paving the way for participation in subsequent elections. The early struggles highlighted institutional barriers favoring incumbents, as evidenced by the TSE's history of stringent scrutiny on new entrants while established parties faced less rigorous enforcement.

Registration and Pre-Election Maneuvers

Nuevas Ideas underwent the formal recognition process with the Tribunal Supremo Electoral (TSE) in mid-2018, amid scrutiny over its organizational structure and compliance with legal requirements for new parties. On August 20, 2018, the TSE announced it would decide on the party's status shortly thereafter, following public hearings where opponents raised concerns about potential irregularities in membership verification. The TSE ultimately granted official recognition later that month, enabling Nuevas Ideas to operate as a legal political entity capable of fielding candidates.
Despite this milestone, the party faced strategic limitations for the 2019 presidential election, as its recent formation precluded seamless participation; Bukele instead secured the nomination under the established Grand Alliance for National Unity (GANA) to bypass potential delays and opposition tactics. For the February 2021 legislative elections, registration efforts intensified, but the TSEdominated by magistrates aligned with traditional parties like ARENA and FMLNimposed hurdles on candidate slates (planillas). In late 2020, reports emerged of the TSE blocking or threatening to annul inscriptions in departments such as Morazn, citing incomplete documentation or verification failures. By January 2021, specific attempts to nullify slates, including one for the Mejicanos municipality, were labeled illegal by party leaders, who argued the actions stemmed from fabricated technicalities aimed at excluding Nuevas Ideas.
Nuevas Ideas countered these maneuvers through rapid legal appeals, internal audits to correct alleged deficiencies, and aggressive public advocacy. Bukele and party president Xavier Zablah Bukele accused the TSE of direct sabotage, framing it as a defensive ploy by legacy elites to preserve their influence amid the party's rising popularity. Leveraging Bukele's social media reach, the party mobilized supporters to protest perceived bias in the TSE, which was viewed skeptically due to its historical ties to opposition factions resistant to Bukele's outsider challenge. These efforts pressured the electoral body to allow corrective submissions, ensuring full participation; Nuevas Ideas ultimately registered candidates across all 14 departments, paving the way for its electoral dominance.

2021 Legislative Elections and Supermajority

Legislative elections in El Salvador were held on February 28, 2021, to elect all 84 deputies to the unicameral Legislative Assembly, alongside municipal and Central American Parliament contests. Nuevas Ideas, having secured legal recognition as a political party in November 2020 after prior hurdles, fielded candidates for the first time in national elections, aligning closely with President Nayib Bukele's agenda of anti-corruption, security enhancements, and economic innovation. The party's campaign capitalized on Bukele's approval ratings, bolstered by his administration's COVID-19 vaccination rollout and early efforts to curb gang violence, contrasting with the established parties ARENA and FMLN's long-standing dominance and perceived inefficacy.
Official results certified by the Tribunal Supremo Electoral (TSE) on March 19, 2021, showed Nuevas Ideas obtaining 56 seats, exactly the two-thirds threshold (56 of 84) required for a supermajority to enact constitutional reforms without needing opposition support. This outcome represented a seismic shift, as traditional parties collapsed: ARENA secured 14 seats, FMLN only 4, while Bukele-aligned GANA gained 5, pushing the pro-government bloc to 61 seats overall. Voter turnout stood at approximately 52.6%, with Nuevas Ideas capturing over 50% of valid votes for legislative positions.
The supermajority took effect when the new assembly convened on May 1, 2021, enabling swift legislative actions, such as the dismissal of Supreme Court justices and the attorney general on May 1, moves justified by the party as necessary to dismantle entrenched corruption but criticized by opposition and international observers for undermining judicial independence. While some civil society groups alleged campaign irregularities, including misuse of state resources, the TSE's validation and Bukele's sustained public supportrooted in tangible reductions in homicide rates from prior yearsaffirmed the electoral mandate's legitimacy under El Salvador's proportional representation system. This dominance facilitated subsequent policies, including Bitcoin's adoption as legal tender earlier in 2021, unhindered by veto threats.

Integration with Bukele's Presidency

Nuevas Ideas, founded by Nayib Bukele in 2017 and legalized by the Supreme Electoral Court on August 24, 2018, has functioned as the primary political vehicle for advancing his presidential agenda since securing a legislative supermajority. In the February 28, 2021, elections, the party captured 56 of the 84 seats in the Legislative Assembly, enabling unified governance between the executive and legislative branches. This alignment allowed Bukele's administration to bypass previous opposition from the assembly, where his party previously held minority influence after his 2019 election under the GANA banner.
The integration deepened through key legislative actions that reinforced executive authority. On May 1, 2021, the Nuevas Ideas majority voted to remove the attorney general and the five magistrates of the Supreme Court's Constitutional Chamber, replacing them with appointees aligned with Bukele's priorities, such as combating entrenched corruption and facilitating policy implementation. This move, occurring shortly after the supermajority was seated, exemplified the party's role in restructuring institutions to support Bukele's security-focused reforms, including the March 2022 state of emergency declaration, which the assembly has extended over 30 times to enable mass arrests targeting gang members.
Further consolidation occurred via constitutional amendments backed by Nuevas Ideas. In 2023, the party reduced assembly seats from 84 to 60 and altered allocation formulas, measures ratified ahead of the 2024 elections that bolstered its dominance. On July 31, 2025, with a 57-3 vote, the assembly approved reforms eliminating indefinite presidential re-election bans, directly enabling Bukele's potential continued tenure and underscoring the party's instrumental role in perpetuating his leadership model. These steps reflect Nuevas Ideas' operational fusion with the presidency, prioritizing pragmatic governance over traditional checks, amid high public approval for resulting security gains.

2024 General Elections

The 2024 general elections in El Salvador occurred on February 4, 2024, selecting the president, vice president, and all 60 members of the unicameral Legislative Assembly for five-year terms. Nayib Bukele, the incumbent president from Nuevas Ideas, sought re-election alongside Vice President Flix Ulloa, despite Article 152 of the constitution prohibiting consecutive presidential terms. In September 2021, El Salvador's Constitutional Chamberwhose magistrates had been replaced by the legislature earlier that yearruled that the ban did not apply to Bukele's case, citing prior non-consecutive service in other roles and international precedents. Critics, including human rights organizations, argued this decision undermined constitutional checks, as the court's composition favored the ruling party. Bukele formally registered his candidacy on October 26, 2023, with the Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE).
The campaign emphasized Bukele's security policies, particularly the ongoing state of emergency since March 2022, which led to over 75,000 gang-related arrests and a sharp decline in homicide rates from 38 per 100,000 in 2019 to 2.4 in 2023. Nuevas Ideas positioned itself as the vehicle for continued anti-crime measures and economic initiatives like Bitcoin adoption, contrasting with opposition parties such as the FMLN and ARENA, which criticized authoritarian tendencies and human rights concerns in prisons. Voter turnout was approximately 43%, lower than the 52.9% in 2019, amid reports of technical glitches in electronic voting systems and restrictions on polling station access.
Bukele secured a landslide victory with 84.6% of the valid votes (about 2.7 million), far surpassing runner-up Joel Snchez of the Partido de Concertacin Nacional (9.4%). In the legislative race, Nuevas Ideas captured 54 seats, expanding its supermajority from 56 in the prior assembly and enabling unchecked passage of reforms. A second round on March 3 allocated remaining seats via proportional representation.
The Organization of American States (OAS) Electoral Observation Mission noted peaceful voting without coercion but highlighted deficiencies in vote tallying transparency and TSE impartiality. Opposition parties alleged irregularities, including overcounted votes for Nuevas Ideas due to software errors inflating legislative tallies by up to 20% in some departments, prompting calls for recounts that the TSE rejected. Despite these claims, the results stood, reflecting strong public support for Bukele's governance amid improved security metrics.

Post-Election Reforms and Developments

Following the 2024 general elections, in which Nuevas Ideas secured 54 of 60 seats in the Legislative Assembly, the party leveraged its supermajority to enact significant constitutional reforms. On July 31, 2025, the Assembly approved amendments eliminating presidential term limits, thereby permitting indefinite re-election for the presidency. These changes also extended the presidential term from five to six years, effective for future administrations starting after 2029, and applied similar provisions to mayors and deputies.
The reforms passed with near-unanimous support from Nuevas Ideas deputies, reflecting the party's consolidated control over legislative processes amid Bukele's high approval ratings tied to prior reductions in violent crime. Critics, including human rights organizations, argued the changes further entrenched executive dominance, potentially undermining democratic checks, as the prior constitutional ban on consecutive re-elections had been circumvented during Bukele's 2024 re-election itself. However, supporters within Nuevas Ideas framed the measures as necessary for policy continuity, citing empirical drops in homicide ratesfrom 38 per 100,000 in 2019 to under 2 per 100,000 by 2024attributable to ongoing territorial control plan and state of emergency extensions.
Beyond constitutional alterations, the post-election period saw sustained implementation of security-focused legislation, including multiple extensions of the regime of exception initiated in 2022, which by mid-2025 had led to over 80,000 gang-related arrests. Nuevas Ideas also advanced fiscal measures reinforcing Bitcoin's status as legal tender, with legislative backing for digital asset infrastructure amid reported increases in tourism and foreign investment. These developments occurred against a backdrop of limited opposition influence, as traditional parties held only six Assembly seats combined.

Ideology and Political Positions

Populist Foundations and Pragmatism

Nuevas Ideas originated as a populist movement in response to public disillusionment with El Salvador's post-civil war political duopoly of the Nationalist Republican Alliance (ARENA) and the Farabundo Mart National Liberation Front (FMLN), which had alternated power since 1989 but failed to curb rampant corruption, gang violence, and economic stagnation. Founded by Nayib Bukele after his ouster from the FMLN in 2017, the party positioned itself as an outsider force championing "new ideas" to restore governance to the people, emphasizing direct communication via social media platforms to rally support against elite capture of institutions. This anti-establishment rhetoric resonated widely, framing traditional parties as obsolete and self-serving, thereby cultivating a base through appeals to national renewal and accountability rather than partisan loyalty.
The populist core of Nuevas Ideas manifests in its rejection of mediated politics in favor of leader-centric mobilization, with Bukele leveraging personal branding and viral campaigns to embody the vox populi, particularly among millennials and urban youth who viewed legacy parties as complicit in the country's homicide rates exceeding 50 per 100,000 inhabitants in prior years. By portraying itself as the vehicle for salvaging sovereignty from corrupt intermediaries, the party fostered a narrative of empowerment through decisive action, unencumbered by the ideological baggage of predecessorsARENA's market-oriented conservatism and FMLN's socialismboth of which empirical data showed yielded persistent insecurity and underdevelopment.
Underpinning this populism is a pragmatic ethos that prioritizes measurable outcomes over doctrinal adherence, with Bukele repeatedly stating that the party adheres to neither left- nor right-wing labels but to solutions reflecting everyday Salvadorans' needs. This approach, described as a "third way," enables flexible policy experimentation, such as adopting Bitcoin as legal tender in 2021 to harness remittances comprising over 20% of GDP, bypassing ideological debates in favor of economic utility. Critics from human rights-oriented outlets often frame such pragmatism as authoritarian opportunism, yet the party's electoral dominancesecuring over 80% in 2024 presidential and legislative racesdemonstrates causal efficacy in addressing voter priorities like security, where homicide rates plummeted post-2019 interventions.
This results-driven pragmatism extends to governance, where Nuevas Ideas evaluates initiatives by their impact on stability and prosperity, diverging from academia and media narratives that privilege procedural norms amid biased critiques of executive overreach. Empirical validation, including a 90% approval rating for Bukele by 2023 surveys, underscores the viability of bypassing rigid ideologies for adaptive realism, though it invites scrutiny for potentially eroding checks amid supermajorities achieved in 2021 and 2024 elections.

Security and Law Enforcement Stance

Nuevas Ideas advocates a hardline approach to public security, emphasizing aggressive law enforcement against gangs such as MS-13 and Barrio 18, which have historically dominated territories and extorted communities. The party supports suspending certain constitutional protections during states of emergency to enable mass arrests and military deployment, viewing these as essential to dismantle gang structures that prior administrations failed to eradicate through negotiations or softer measures. This stance aligns with President Nayib Bukele's Territorial Control Plan, initiated in 2019 and reinforced by Nuevas Ideas' legislative supermajority after the 2021 elections, which facilitated repeated extensions of the state of exception declared in March 2022 following a surge in gang killings.
Key measures backed by the party include the construction of the Center for Confinement of Terrorism (CECot), a mega-prison opened in 2023 with capacity for 40,000 inmates, and policies allowing security forces to conduct warrantless detentions and searches in gang-suspected areas. By mid-2025, over 80,000 individuals had been arrested under the regime, with the party arguing that isolating gang leaders in high-security facilities disrupts command hierarchies and prevents recidivism, contrasting with earlier gang truces that temporarily reduced violence but empowered criminals. Nuevas Ideas legislators have passed complementary laws, such as those enhancing penalties for gang affiliation and authorizing broader intelligence gathering, positioning security as a prerequisite for economic and social stability.
These policies have correlated with a sharp decline in violence: El Salvador's homicide rate fell from 38 per 100,000 inhabitants in 2019 to 1.9 in 2024, with total homicides dropping to 114 for the year, the lowest on record. Gang-controlled extortion and territorial disputes, which previously claimed thousands of lives annually, have diminished, enabling residents to reclaim public spaces without fear, as evidenced by increased nighttime activity and reduced migration driven by violence.
Critics, including organizations like Human Rights Watch, allege widespread arbitrary detentions, with estimates of thousands of innocents arrested based on tattoos or proximity to gang areas, and reports of abuse in custody; the government has acknowledged and released around 8,000 such cases by late 2024. Nuevas Ideas counters that such measures, while imperfect, address root causes of gang power through deterrence and incapacitation, yielding empirically verifiable safety gains that outweigh procedural lapses in a context where gangs previously operated with impunity due to institutional weaknesses and prior failed policies. The party's legislative actions, such as reforming judicial processes for mass trials of gang members, reflect a prioritization of collective security over individual due process in high-threat scenarios, though international observers with histories of critiquing strongman governance in Latin America question the long-term sustainability without judicial reforms.

Economic and Fiscal Policies

Nuevas Ideas' economic policies emphasize pragmatic innovation, fiscal discipline, and leveraging improved security to attract investment and foster growth, reflecting President Nayib Bukele's administration priorities since the party's legislative supermajority in 2021. The approach seeks to reduce reliance on external debt, promote technological advancement, and address structural challenges like low productivity, though outcomes have been modest amid persistent macroeconomic imbalances. Real GDP growth averaged approximately 2.5% annually from 2021 to 2024, reaching 2.6% in 2024 despite floods and fiscal adjustments, lagging behind regional peers like Guatemala and Nicaragua.
A cornerstone policy was the June 2021 adoption of Bitcoin as legal tender alongside the U.S. dollar, aimed at enhancing financial inclusion for the unbanked, reducing remittance costs (which constitute over 20% of GDP), and positioning El Salvador as a crypto hub to draw foreign direct investment. The government launched the Chivo wallet for digital transactions and invested public funds in Bitcoin, holding about 15% of reserves in the cryptocurrency by 2025. However, adoption remained low, with minimal usage for everyday transactions and no significant boost to financial inclusion, as evidenced by IMF assessments citing unfavorable cost-benefit ratios and volatility risks. Critics, including the IMF, highlighted fiscal costs and urged policy reversals, leading to concessions in the 2025 IMF agreement to limit Bitcoin's monetary role.
On fiscal policy, Nuevas Ideas has pursued debt sustainability amid a public debt-to-GDP ratio exceeding 80% inherited from prior administrations, implementing liability management operations and revenue-based financing. In June 2025, the legislative supermajority passed a fiscal sustainability law to curb spending, prioritize debt reduction, and align with IMF requirements for a $1.4 billion Extended Fund Facility approved in February 2025. The 2025 national budget, announced in September 2024, marked a shift to zero planned deficit, fully funded by domestic revenues without new debt for current expenditures, following a $338 million deficit in 2024. This included three debt operations in 2024 to extend maturities and reduce immediate pressures, though high interest rates (around 7%) on dollar-denominated obligations persist.
These measures aim to stabilize finances and enable infrastructure and tech investments, with security gains indirectly supporting tourism and business confidence, though direct economic diversification remains limited and poverty reduction uneven. IMF projections anticipate 2.5% GDP growth in 2025, contingent on continued consolidation and external factors like U.S. remittances.

Social and Governance Views

Nuevas Ideas upholds El Salvador's total ban on abortion, with President Nayib Bukele explicitly ruling out any reforms to the constitutional right to life from conception and opposing legalization under any circumstances. The party has maintained prosecutions for obstetric emergencies misinterpreted as abortions, though Bukele's administration released several women convicted in such cases in December 2021, framing it as correcting judicial errors rather than policy shifts.
On family and gender issues, Nuevas Ideas promotes traditional structures, rejecting same-sex marriage and gender ideology in public policy. Bukele has publicly attacked "gender theory" as ideological imposition, directing its removal from school curricula in March 2024 and banning inclusive language in public education to preserve Spanish grammar and cultural norms. This aligns with a socially conservative stance emphasizing protection of children and mental health over progressive sexuality education, which the party views as divisive.
In education policy, the party prioritizes discipline and anti-gang measures, implementing military-style regulations in schools starting August 2025, including mandatory haircuts, uniforms, and grooming standards to prevent recruitment and restore order. These reforms, led by a military officer as education minister, reflect a pragmatic focus on security as foundational to learning environments, building on empirical reductions in school violence post-gang crackdowns.
Governance views center on reforming entrenched institutions to prioritize efficiency and anti-corruption over procedural democratic norms, rejecting traditional left-right dichotomies in favor of pragmatic "third way" solutions. The party has pursued supermajority-driven changes, including the 2021 dismissal of Supreme Court justices and the attorney general to address perceived corruption in prior administrations, followed by constitutional amendments in July 2025 eliminating presidential term limits to enable indefinite re-election. Critics, including human rights organizations with documented ideological leanings toward progressive causes, label these as authoritarian backsliding, but supporters cite causal links to improved governance outcomes, such as halved homicide rates since 2019, as evidence of necessity against legacy elite capture.
Nuevas Ideas advocates centralized executive authority for decisive action, enacting laws like the May 2025 Foreign Agents Law to regulate NGO funding transparency amid concerns over foreign influence in domestic affairs. This reflects a realist approach to state sovereignty, prioritizing empirical security gainsevidenced by El Salvador's transformation from one of the world's highest murder rates to near-zero in urban areasover institutional checks that previously enabled impunity. The party's internal discipline and Bukele's 85% approval ratings in 2024 polls underscore voter endorsement of governance emphasizing results over pluralism.

Organization

Leadership and Internal Structure

Nuevas Ideas operates with a centralized leadership structure dominated by founder Nayib Bukele and his close relatives, functioning more as a personalist movement than a traditional bureaucratic party. The party's formal president is Xavier Zablah Bukele, a cousin of Nayib Bukele, who was elected in internal party primaries on March 1, 2020, and has held the role since. Zablah Bukele, an entrepreneur with prior business ties to government officials, oversees party operations alongside a national executive committee composed largely of Bukele family members, friends, and appointees from the presidential administration. 
Vice President Flix Ulloa leads the party's parliamentary group in the Legislative Assembly, coordinating the 54 Nuevas Ideas deputies elected in 2024 and ensuring alignment with Bukele's policy priorities. Despite statutory claims of being a democratic and decentralized organization, the party's decision-making remains top-down, with Bukele exerting de facto control over candidate selection, strategy, and public messaging, often bypassing formal bodies. Key roles, including regional coordinators and financial overseers, are filled by loyalists from Bukele's inner circle, reflecting a structure built on personal networks rather than broad membership bases or competitive primaries.
The party has announced plans for nationwide internal elections in 2025 to select its dirigencia, or leadership cadre, marking a potential shift toward formalized internal democracy amid criticisms of opacity in prior selections. Political advisor Silvio Aquino indicated in October 2024 that discussions are underway to design this process, though details on eligibility, voting mechanisms, and oversight remain unspecified. This follows the 2020 primaries, which were limited in scope and won overwhelmingly by family-linked candidates, underscoring the party's evolution from an ad hoc grouping of Bukele supporters into a dominant electoral machine.

Symbols and Branding

The visual identity of Nuevas Ideas centers on the color cyan, a bright greenish-blue shade that distinguishes the party and permeates its branding across political materials and public infrastructure. This color palette reflects a modern, dynamic aesthetic aligned with Nayib Bukele's millennial-oriented political style, appearing in legislative contexts as the "Bancada Cyan," the official name for the party's bloc in the Asamblea Legislativa.
The party's primary logo features the words "Nuevas Ideas" in uppercase letters rendered in cyan, emphasizing simplicity and readability for digital and print media. Official representations registered with El Salvador's Tribunal Supremo Electoral include this textual logo alongside a flag consisting of a solid cyan field bearing a prominent white capital "N" at the center, symbolizing the party's name through minimalist design. The "N" motif extends into grassroots branding, with the letter frequently painted in cyan on curbs, sidewalks, and building facades in areas under party influence, fostering visual ubiquity and association with Bukele's governance.
Municipal administrations controlled by Nuevas Ideas have incorporated cyan elements into local escudos and signage, standardizing aesthetics to align with national party imagery and Bukele's administrative priorities. This cohesive branding strategy supports the party's populist appeal, leveraging consistent visual cues to project renewal and unity without reliance on traditional ideological symbols.

Electoral Performance

Presidential Elections

Nuevas Ideas did not contest the 2019 Salvadoran presidential election, during which its founder Nayib Bukele ran as the candidate of the Grand Alliance for National Unity (GANA) and won with 53.1 percent of the valid votes in the first round on February 3, 2019.
In July 2023, the party selected Bukele as its presidential candidate for the 2024 election, despite constitutional prohibitions on consecutive re-election, which had been circumvented through prior judicial reforms.
Bukele, alongside vice presidential running mate Flix Ulloa, formally registered their candidacy with the Supreme Electoral Tribunal on October 26, 2023.
The 2024 presidential election occurred on February 4, 2024, concurrently with legislative elections, under a state of emergency that had been in place since 2022.
Bukele secured a landslide victory, receiving 82.66 percent of the votes as officially declared by the Supreme Electoral Tribunal, far surpassing opponents including Manuel Flores of the Farabundo Mart National Liberation Front (6.4 percent) and Joel Snchez of the Nationalist Republican Alliance (5.5 percent).
This result reflected strong public support for Bukele's security policies, which had drastically reduced homicide rates, though critics highlighted electoral irregularities and the erosion of institutional checks.

Legislative Assembly Elections

In the 2021 Salvadoran legislative election, held on February 28, Nuevas Ideas participated for the first time after its official registration and secured 56 of the 84 seats in the Legislative Assembly, marking a shift from opposition dominance to a pro-Bukele majority. This outcome, confirmed by the Tribunal Supremo Electoral (TSE) following final scrutiny on March 19, reflected strong public support amid Bukele's early presidential tenure, with the party outperforming traditional parties like ARENA (14 seats) and FMLN (4 seats). The victory enabled passage of key security and fiscal measures previously stalled.
Prior to 2021, Nuevas Ideas had not contested legislative elections; its founder Nayib Bukele had run for president in 2019 under the allied GANA banner due to registration delays, while the 2018 legislative vote predated the party's full electoral debut.
The 2024 Salvadoran legislative election, concurrent with the presidential vote on February 4, followed a 2023 constitutional reform reducing seats from 84 to 60 to streamline representation. Nuevas Ideas won 54 seats, confirmed by TSE on February 19 after a manual recount amid opposition claims of technical glitches and irregularities in electronic voting. This supermajority, leaving only 2 seats for Vamos and 4 for other minor parties, solidified legislative alignment with Bukele's administration, though critics from outlets like El Faro alleged procedural biases favoring the ruling party without overturning official tallies.

Municipal Elections

In the municipal elections held on February 28, 2021, Nuevas Ideas contested 262 mayoral races across El Salvador's then-existing municipalities and secured victory in 150 of them, marking a substantial breakthrough for the nascent party against established competitors like ARENA and the FMLN, which had dominated local governance for decades. This outcome reflected growing public support for the party's association with President Nayib Bukele's administration, particularly amid early security reforms, though turnout was approximately 52%.
Prior to the 2024 elections, El Salvador's Legislative Assembly, controlled by Nuevas Ideas and allies, approved a restructuring on June 14, 2023, consolidating the 262 municipalities into 44 larger ones, each encompassing multiple distritos (former municipalities) to streamline administration and resource allocation. Critics, including opposition parties, alleged the redistricting favored urban and Bukele-supportive areas, potentially diluting rural opposition strongholds, while proponents cited efficiency gains in service delivery.
The subsequent municipal elections occurred on March 3, 2024, with voters selecting 44 mayors amid notably low turnout of around 40-50%, attributed partly to voter fatigue following the February presidential vote and logistical challenges. Nuevas Ideas emerged as the leading victor, capturing a majority of the positions, while President Bukele announced that his party and pro-government allies (including GANA and PCN) collectively won 43 out of 44, consolidating local control aligned with national policies on security and infrastructure. Official Tribunal Supremo Electoral scrutiny finalized these results by early March, confirming Nuevas Ideas' dominance despite isolated opposition gains in reconfigured districts.

PARLACEN Elections

In the February 28, 2021, elections for the Central American Parliament (PARLACEN), held concurrently with legislative and municipal contests, Nuevas Ideas obtained 12 of El Salvador's 20 allocated deputy seats, establishing a majority representation. This outcome reflected the party's broader electoral dominance, driven by voter support for President Nayib Bukele's security-focused agenda amid high turnout of approximately 52.4 percent.
The subsequent PARLACEN elections occurred on March 3, 2024, separate from the presidential and legislative vote earlier that month, with El Salvador again electing 20 deputies via proportional representation. Nuevas Ideas secured 13 seats, surpassing its 2021 tally and outperforming traditional parties like ARENA (which received the remainder alongside minor allocations to FMLN, PDC, and PCN), based on final scrutiny of 1.4 million votes by the Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE). Voter turnout was around 37 percent, lower than in national races, amid criticisms of PARLACEN's limited efficacy and high per-deputy costs exceeding $100,000 annually.
Despite these gains, Nuevas Ideas-led initiatives culminated in El Salvador's approved exit from PARLACEN on July 31, 2025, when the Legislative Assembly passed a constitutional reform with 57 votes from its supermajority, effective after ratification processes. Proponents, including Nuevas Ideas deputies, argued the body represented redundant expendituretotaling millions yearlywithout substantive regional impact, prioritizing national sovereignty over supranational participation. This move nullified the 2024-elected deputies' terms prematurely, aligning with the party's emphasis on fiscal efficiency and skepticism toward multilateral institutions perceived as inefficient.

Policy Achievements

Crime Reduction and Gang Crackdown

The Legislative Assembly, controlled by Nuevas Ideas following its 2021 supermajority victory, approved a state of emergency on March 27, 2022, after gangs perpetrated 87 homicides over the prior weekend, enabling suspension of habeas corpus, search warrants, and other due process protections to facilitate mass arrests of suspected MS-13 and Barrio 18 members. By September 2024, authorities had detained over 80,000 individuals under this regime, with Nuevas Ideas legislators repeatedly extending the emergencymore than 30 times by mid-2025while enacting laws to classify gang membership as a terrorism offense and impose sentences of 20 to 45 years.
These measures dismantled gang operational capacity through territorial sweeps, intelligence-led operations, and confinement in facilities like the Center for the Confinement of Terrorism (CECOT), a 40,000-capacity mega-prison opened in 2023 to isolate high-risk inmates and prevent coordination. Homicide rates, which stood at approximately 18 per 100,000 inhabitants in 2021, declined to 7.8 in 2022 (a 56.8% drop from 1,147 to 496 murders), 2.4 in 2023, and 1.9 in 2024among the lowest in Latin Americacorrelating directly with the incarceration surge exceeding 1.6% of the population.
Extortion revenues, previously estimated at $408 million annually from businesses and residents, collapsed as gang hierarchies fragmented, contributing to a broader security transformation that reduced overall violent crime by over 80% since the emergency's onset, per National Civil Police data. Nuevas Ideas framed this "iron fist" strategy as a rejection of prior failed truce-and-release approaches, prioritizing empirical deterrence over rehabilitation, with sustained low daily homicide averages (0.31 per day through October 2025) validating the causal link between mass detention and territorial control.

Economic Initiatives and Bitcoin Adoption

In June 2021, President Nayib Bukele announced plans to adopt Bitcoin as legal tender, a policy swiftly approved by the Legislative Assembly under Nuevas Ideas control and implemented on September 7, 2021, marking El Salvador as the first nation to do so alongside the US dollar. The measure sought to enhance financial inclusion for the estimated two-thirds of adults without bank accounts, reduce remittance costs (which constitute about 25% of GDP), and foster economic sovereignty by diversifying from dollarization. Supporting infrastructure included the state-backed Chivo digital wallet, which offered initial $30 Bitcoin incentives to users, and plans for geothermal-powered Bitcoin mining using volcanic energy.
Additional economic components involved proposals for Bitcoin City, a tax-free zone funded by $1 billion in volcano bonds to attract cryptocurrency-related investment, though construction has not advanced as of 2025. The government accumulated Bitcoin holdings, reaching 6,102 BTC by early 2025, valued at approximately $550 million including $250 million in unrealized gains from periodic purchases. However, empirical data indicate limited domestic adoption: by 2024, only 8% of Salvadorans used Bitcoin for payments, with user penetration projected at 5.77% in 2025, and no significant shift in remittance flows or broad financial inclusion gains observed.
Bitcoin's volatility and low uptake contributed to international friction, including IMF resistance that delayed financing until a December 2024 agreement for a $1.4 billion loan, requiring concessions such as making Bitcoin acceptance voluntary for private entities, limiting new government purchases, and enhancing fiscal risk management. These steps reflected a partial retreat from mandatory adoption, with IMF assessments noting no discernible macroeconomic benefits from the policy after over three years. Broader economic efforts under the Nuevas Ideas government included establishing the Investment and Export Promotion Agency (INVEST) in June 2023 to attract foreign direct investment and a 2025 budget emphasizing fiscal discipline by avoiding new public debt issuance. GDP growth averaged around 3% annually from 2022 to 2024 (2.8% in 2022, 3.5% in 2023, 2.6% in 2024), driven more by post-pandemic recovery and security improvements than cryptocurrency integration, amid persistent challenges like a 70% informal economy share.

Infrastructure and Public Welfare Gains

The Bukele administration, supported by the Nuevas Ideas-dominated legislature, has prioritized large-scale road construction to improve national connectivity, with a $1.4248 billion commitment allocated to eastern El Salvador infrastructure development as of March 2025. This includes the completion of the Gerardo Barrios Ring Road in August 2025, which connects key eastern municipalities and promotes economic sustainability by reducing travel times and facilitating goods transport. Additionally, on December 2, 2024, President Bukele inaugurated a major road project representing one of the largest such investments in the country's history, costing $160 million and spanning extensive highway expansions to alleviate congestion and support commerce.
In education, the government initiated a nationwide school rebuilding program, constructing or renovating two public schools daily as part of a strategy to modernize facilities and enhance access, reported in July 2025. This effort addresses longstanding deficiencies in infrastructure, aiming to improve learning environments amid broader public investment increases. Healthcare infrastructure has also seen targeted upgrades, including the June 2023 groundbreaking for a $61.2 million reconstruction of the Rosales Hospital in San Salvador, focusing on expanded capacity and modern equipment to serve urban populations.
Public welfare initiatives under Nuevas Ideas include a 2022 pension reform that increased benefits by 30% for retirees, funded through legislative adjustments to social security allocations. Market modernization projects, announced in August 2025, have transformed traditional public markets into cleaner, safer venues with improved hygiene standards and vendor support, fostering competitive local economies. Tourism-related welfare gains, such as Surf City developments in coastal areas, have incorporated infrastructure like beachfront amenities and access roads, contributing to over $65 million in capital investments across 65 projects by September 2024. These measures, while emphasizing physical upgrades, have been critiqued for relying on state-led funding amid fiscal constraints, though they correlate with reported enhancements in daily living standards.

Controversies

Constitutional Amendments and Re-Election

Following the 2021 legislative elections, in which Nuevas Ideas secured a supermajority of 56 seats in the 84-seat Legislative Assembly, the party moved to alter judicial interpretations barring consecutive presidential terms. On May 1, 2021, assembly members from Nuevas Ideas and allies voted 64-35 to dismiss all five magistrates of the Supreme Court's Constitutional Chamber, whose terms had three years remaining, citing alleged neglect of duties without individual hearings or evidence presentation. The Inter-American Commission on Human Rights condemned the dismissals as lacking due process.
The newly appointed magistrates, selected by the Nuevas Ideas-controlled assembly on June 28, 2021, without transparent deliberation, issued a ruling on September 3, 2021, interpreting Article 154 of the constitution to permit immediate re-election for incumbents under certain conditions, overriding prior rulings that prohibited consecutive terms. This decision enabled President Nayib Bukele, Nuevas Ideas' leader, to register as a candidate for the 2024 election. On November 3, 2023, the Supreme Electoral Tribunal, aligned with the ruling bloc, approved his candidacy despite ongoing constitutional debates. Bukele won re-election on February 4, 2024, with 84.7% of the vote.
In a further reform, on July 31, 2025, the Nuevas Ideas-dominated assembly, holding 57 seats, approved and ratified constitutional amendments by a 57-3 vote, eliminating presidential term limits to allow indefinite re-election, extending terms from five to six years, and removing second-round runoffs. These changes, effective after ratification, drew criticism from organizations like Human Rights Watch for eroding democratic checks, though supporters argued they reflected popular mandate. The reforms bypassed traditional multi-legislature approval processes via a new fast-track mechanism established earlier in 2025.

State of Emergency Measures

On March 27, 2022, following a surge of 87 homicides over the preceding weekendincluding 62 killings on March 26 alonePresident Nayib Bukele requested, and the Legislative Assembly dominated by Nuevas Ideas approved, a state of exception under Article 29 of El Salvador's Constitution. This measure suspended several constitutional guarantees, including the rights to habeas corpus, legal defense during initial detention, presumption of innocence, and protection from warrantless arrests and searches, enabling security forces to detain suspects without immediate judicial oversight.
The regime facilitated rapid mass detentions targeting suspected gang members, primarily from MS-13 and Barrio 18, with over 80,000 individuals arrested by mid-2024, representing approximately 1.5% of the population. Nuevas Ideas legislators, holding a supermajority of 54 out of 60 seats since the 2021 elections, have extended the state of exception nearly every 30 daysover 50 times as of October 2025often with minimal debate, framing it as essential for sustained public security. Accompanying policies included constructing the Center for Confinement of Terrorism (CECOT), a mega-prison holding up to 40,000 inmates under strict conditions with limited family access or rehabilitation programs.
Empirical outcomes include a sharp decline in violent crime: El Salvador's homicide rate fell from 18 per 100,000 inhabitants in 2021 to 2.4 in 2023, with official data showing zero murders in certain months and gang extortion reports dropping by over 90%. Government sources attribute this to the disruption of gang command structures, corroborated by reduced gang activity in U.S. State Department assessments, though some analysts question potential underreporting of homicides reclassified as "other deaths."
Critics, including Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International, document widespread arbitrary detentionsestimated at 20-30% of arrestees lacking gang tiesalong with reports of torture, enforced disappearances, and at least 260 deaths in custody by late 2023, often attributing these to inadequate due process under the regime. These organizations, while providing case-specific evidence from detainees and families, have faced accusations of overlooking pre-regime violence levels that exceeded 100 homicides monthly; nonetheless, the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights has urged proportionality and judicial review to mitigate violations. Nuevas Ideas defends the measures as a necessary response to decades of gang impunity, with public approval polls exceeding 80% and electoral gains reflecting voter prioritization of security over procedural concerns.

Human Rights Allegations

Human Rights Watch has documented widespread arbitrary detentions under El Salvador's state of emergency, declared on March 27, 2022, in response to 87 gang-related homicides over a single weekend, with over 81,000 individuals arrested by mid-2024, often on the basis of physical appearance, anonymous tips, or social media activity rather than concrete evidence. The suspension of constitutional protections, including the right to a defense attorney and presumption of innocence, has resulted in mass hearings processing up to 500 detainees at once, limited access to legal representation, and indefinite pretrial detention for those charged with gang affiliation under aggregated laws like "unlawful association."
Allegations of torture and ill-treatment in custody include beatings, waterboarding, sexual violence, and degrading conditions in overcrowded facilities, where the prison population surged from 39,000 to over 108,000 by 2024, exceeding capacity by more than 38,000 inmates. Cristosal, a Salvadoran human rights organization, reported 261 deaths in state custody by July 2024, attributing many to torture, medical neglect, or unhygienic conditions such as detainees sleeping on floors without adequate food or healthcare; authorities have closed most investigations into these deaths without charges against security forces. Enforced disappearances have also been alleged, with families denied information on detainees' locations for days or weeks.
Amnesty International has highlighted patterns of state-sponsored abuse replacing gang violence, including the arbitrary arrest of over 3,000 children and the targeting of human rights defenders, such as the September 2024 detention of two activists protesting mass evictions on baseless charges. The Inter-American Commission on Human Rights urged an end to the emergency regime in September 2024, citing its systematic impact on due process and vulnerable populations. While the government maintains that releases of approximately 7,000 individuals cleared of wrongdoing demonstrate due diligence, critics from organizations like Human Rights Watch argue that inadequate investigations and impunity for officials perpetuate a cycle of unaddressed violations.

Claims of Electoral Irregularities and Authoritarianism

Opposition parties in El Salvador, including the Farabundo Mart National Liberation Front (FMLN) and Nationalist Republican Alliance (ARENA), alleged electoral irregularities during the February 4, 2024, general elections, particularly in the legislative race where Nuevas Ideas secured a supermajority of 54 out of 60 seats. Claims included over 60 documented anomalies such as unauthorized handling of ballots, prohibited electoral propaganda inside voting centers, and discrepancies between electronic tallies and manual recounts. The Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE) acknowledged technical failures in the electronic transmission system, which collapsed and delayed results, prompting partial recounts in affected centers, though it ultimately validated the outcomes without annulling votes. 
International observers, including the Organization of American States (OAS) Electoral Observation Mission, reported numerous complaints about delays in result transmission, presence of unauthorized party agents, and irregularities in vote counting procedures, but did not conclude evidence of widespread fraud sufficient to alter the presidential results where Nayib Bukele won 84.65% of the vote. Human rights organizations like WOLA and Cristosal echoed concerns over militarization of polling stations and suppression of opposition campaigning under the ongoing state of emergency, arguing these factors undermined free choice, though turnout was officially reported at 52.6% without independent verification. Supporters of Nuevas Ideas dismissed these as sour grapes from defeated parties, pointing to pre-election polls showing Bukele's approval ratings above 80% and attributing technical glitches to outdated infrastructure rather than manipulation. No independent audits confirmed systematic ballot stuffing or hacking favoring the ruling party.
Accusations of authoritarianism against Bukele and Nuevas Ideas center on the party's legislative actions post-2021 elections, where it dismissed five Constitutional Chamber magistrates and the attorney general on May 1, 2021, replacing them with loyalists, enabling subsequent reforms like indefinite re-election. Critics, including U.S. congressional reports and outlets like The New Yorker, label this judicial capture as a consolidation of executive power, violating separation of powers and allowing the state of emergencyprolonged over 30 times since March 2022to suspend habeas corpus and due process for mass arrests exceeding 75,000 suspected gang members. In August 2024, the Nuevas Ideas-dominated assembly approved constitutional amendments permitting non-consecutive re-election, which opponents argue circumvents the 1983 ban despite a 2021 court ruling in Bukele's favor. These moves, per human rights groups like CIVICUS, reflect "popular authoritarianism" through institutional dismantling, though empirical data shows public support driven by homicide rates dropping from 38 per 100,000 in 2019 to 2.4 in 2023.
Such claims often emanate from opposition-aligned NGOs and Western media, which have historically critiqued Bukele's security model despite its causal link to reduced violence via territorial control and incarceration, raising questions about source motivations amid El Salvador's shift from gang-dominated governance. Bukele has countered by framing reforms as anti-corruption necessities against entrenched elites, with no successful legal challenges overturning them domestically. International bodies like the U.S. State Department have noted restrictions on media and judiciary independence but stopped short of deeming the 2024 elections invalid.
