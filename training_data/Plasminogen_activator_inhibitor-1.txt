Plasminogen activator inhibitor-1
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1), encoded by the SERPINE1 gene on chromosome 7q21.3-q22, is the primary physiological inhibitor of tissue-type plasminogen activator (tPA) and urokinase-type plasminogen activator (uPA), thereby serving as a key regulator of the plasminogen-plasmin system and fibrinolysis. As a member of the serpin superfamily, PAI-1 is a single-chain glycoprotein with a molecular weight of approximately 45-50 kDa, comprising 379 or 381 amino acids, and features a reactive center loop (RCL) that facilitates its inhibitory mechanism by forming stable complexes with target proteases.
Beyond its central role in inhibiting plasminogen activation to prevent excessive fibrin degradation and maintain hemostatic balance, PAI-1 exhibits multifaceted functions in cellular processes, including the modulation of cell migration, adhesion, proliferation, and apoptosis through interactions with extracellular matrix proteins like vitronectin and receptors such as those in the low-density lipoprotein receptor (LDLR) family. Its expression and activity are tightly regulated by various factors, including cytokines (e.g., IL-6 and TNF-), growth factors, metabolic signals like insulin and glucose, hormones, and genetic polymorphisms such as the 4G/5G promoter polymorphism (rs1799768) in SERPINE1, which is associated with higher plasma PAI-1 levels in carriers of the 4G allele (4G/4G or 4G/5G genotypes), influences circadian rhythms with a morning peak, and is linked to worse outcomes in sepsis, including increased risk of multiple organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS) and septic shock.
Elevated PAI-1 levels are associated with numerous pathological conditions, reflecting its "double-edged sword" nature: in thrombosis and cardiovascular disease, it contributes to impaired fibrinolysis and increased risk of myocardial infarction and stroke; in cancer, paradoxically high expression promotes tumor progression, angiogenesis, and metastasis despite its inhibitory role, serving as a prognostic biomarker in malignancies like breast, lung, and colorectal cancers; and in metabolic disorders, it links obesity, insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, and fibrosis, with adipose tissue as a major source; in sepsis, the 4G/5G polymorphism contributes to higher PAI-1 levels and increased risk of multiple organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS) and septic shock; it has also been implicated in severe COVID-19. These associations underscore PAI-1's potential as a therapeutic target, though its complex biology necessitates careful consideration in drug development, with inhibitors like TM5614 currently in phase II clinical trials for cancer and other conditions as of 2025.

Molecular Biology

Gene and Genetics

The SERPINE1 gene, which encodes plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1), is located on the long arm of human chromosome 7 at the cytogenetic band 7q21.3-q22. This gene spans approximately 12.3 kilobases and consists of 9 exons, with the coding sequence distributed across 8 of them, producing a mature mRNA transcript of about 2.2 kilobases. The genomic organization reflects the broader serpin superfamily, where introns interrupt the coding regions to facilitate alternative splicing, though the primary isoform is predominant in most tissues.
A common functional polymorphism in the promoter region of SERPINE1 is the 4G/5G insertion/deletion variant (rs1799768) located 675 base pairs upstream of the transcription start site (c.-675_676delG or c.-817dupG relative to the ATG start codon). The 4G allele, lacking one guanine residue compared to the 5G allele, creates a stronger binding site for transcription factors, resulting in higher transcriptional activity and elevated PAI-1 protein levels in plasma, particularly under basal conditions. This polymorphism accounts for up to 25% of the variability in PAI-1 antigen levels among individuals and has been extensively studied for its influence on thrombotic risk, though its direct impact on disease is modulated by environmental factors. Moreover, this polymorphism has been associated with worse outcomes in sepsis. Carriers of the 4G allele (4G/4G or 4G/5G genotypes) have higher PAI-1 levels, leading to impaired fibrinolysis and increased risk of multiple organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS) and septic shock. In a prospective study of Caucasian patients with severe sepsis due to pneumonia, carriers of the 4G allele had a 2.74-fold higher risk of MODS (OR 2.74, 95% CI 1.335-5.604, p=0.006) and a 2.57-fold higher risk of septic shock (OR 2.57, 95% CI 1.180-5.615, p=0.018) compared to 5G/5G carriers. In another cohort of patients with severe sepsis, the 4G/4G genotype was associated with higher 30-day mortality (OR 1.95, 95% CI 1.063-3.561, p=0.03). This polymorphism is typically detected by PCR-RFLP or other PCR-based genotyping methods.
Rare loss-of-function mutations in SERPINE1 can lead to PAI-1 deficiency, an autosomal recessive disorder characterized by hemorrhagic diathesis due to unchecked fibrinolysis. Complete deficiency, often caused by homozygous null mutations such as frameshifts or nonsense variants (e.g., a duplication in exon 4 leading to premature termination), results in undetectable PAI-1 activity and antigen levels, manifesting as severe bleeding episodes including prolonged menorrhagia, hemarthroses, and excessive posttraumatic hemorrhage. Partial deficiencies from heterozygous missense mutations, such as A15T in the signal peptide, may cause milder bleeding tendencies, with antigen levels below 50% of normal. These mutations highlight PAI-1's essential role in hemostasis, with over 20 pathogenic variants documented, primarily affecting the reactive center loop or signal sequence.
The SERPINE1 gene exhibits high evolutionary conservation across vertebrates, reflecting its fundamental role in protease inhibition and tissue homeostasis. Orthologs are present in a wide range of species, including mice (Serpine1 on chromosome 5), rats, zebrafish, and chickens, with 185 identified orthologs in Ensembl databases showing sequence identities often exceeding 70% in the core serpin domain. This conservation extends to non-mammalian species like Xenopus, underscoring PAI-1's ancient origin within the serpin superfamily, which arose before the divergence of chordates.

Protein Structure

Plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) is a 45 kDa glycoprotein belonging to the serpin superfamily of serine protease inhibitors, consisting of 379 amino acids in its mature form and featuring a characteristic fold with three -sheets (AC) and nine -helices (hAhI). This superfamily includes over 1,500 members that function through a unique conformational change mechanism to inhibit target proteases. PAI-1's structure enables it to serve as the principal physiological inhibitor of tissue-type plasminogen activator (tPA) and urokinase-type plasminogen activator (uPA), regulating fibrinolysis and other processes.
Central to PAI-1's inhibitory function is its reactive center loop (RCL), a flexible 26-residue segment (P16P10) that protrudes from the protein's surface and mimics the substrate of target proteases. The RCL contains the critical P1P1 Arg346Met347 bond, which acts as a bait for cleavage by the active site serine of tPA or uPA, facilitating substrate mimicry and subsequent inhibition. This bond's positioning allows PAI-1 to engage proteases with high specificity and association rate constants on the order of 1010 M s.
PAI-1 exists in multiple conformational states that dictate its activity: the active (metastable) form, where the RCL is exposed for protease inhibition; the latent (autoinhibited) form, in which the RCL inserts into -sheet A, rendering PAI-1 inactive; and the substrate form, where the RCL is cleaved but does not fully translocate the protease. The transition from active to latent occurs spontaneously with a half-life of approximately 2 hours at 37C, driven by thermodynamic instability unique among serpins, which limits PAI-1's functional duration in vivo.
PAI-1 undergoes N-linked glycosylation at three potential sitesAsn165, Asn209, and Asn329with tissue-specific patterns that influence conformational stability and latency transition rates but are not essential for its core inhibitory activity against tPA or uPA. Glycosylation at these sites promotes heterogeneous forms that enhance overall protein stability, potentially modulating the balance between active and latent conformations in physiological contexts.
Crystal structures of PAI-1 have elucidated these features, with the active conformation captured in complexes such as PDB entry 1OC0 (2.3 resolution), which shows PAI-1 bound to the somatomedin B domain of vitronectin, highlighting the RCL's exposure and helical regions' role in stability. Other structures, including PDB 1DB2 for the stabilized active form and PDB 4AQH for the latent state, confirm the -sheet insertion mechanism underlying latency.
The serpin inhibition mechanism of PAI-1 involves rapid formation of a non-covalent Michaelis complex with tPA or uPA, followed by nucleophilic attack at the P1P1 bond to form a covalent acyl-enzyme intermediate. Subsequent RCL insertion into -sheet A translocates and distorts the protease's active site triad (Ser-His-Asp), rendering it catalytically inactive and trapping it in a stable, irreversible complex. This distortion prevents further substrate turnover, distinguishing serpins from classical inhibitors and underscoring PAI-1's role in precise proteolytic regulation.

Biosynthesis and Regulation

Sites of Synthesis

Plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) is primarily synthesized in endothelial cells lining the vascular system, where it contributes to basal regulation of fibrinolysis. In the liver, hepatocytes serve as a key site of PAI-1 production, particularly under conditions influencing lipid metabolism and insulin sensitivity. Adipose tissue represents another major source, with expression occurring in mature adipocytes as well as pre-adipocytes. Within adipose depots, stromal cells, including those with macrophage-like characteristics, are the predominant producers of PAI-1, outpacing adipocytes in contribution to local and circulating levels. Megakaryocytes synthesize PAI-1 mRNA, which is stored in platelets; upon activation, platelets produce active PAI-1 de novo, accounting for the majority (up to 80%) of circulating PAI-1.
PAI-1 expression exhibits both constitutive and inducible patterns across these sites. In endothelial cells, synthesis is largely constitutive at low basal levels but can be rapidly upregulated by various stimuli. In contrast, adipose tissue displays high inducible expression that correlates strongly with obesity; levels rise in visceral fat depots, which express PAI-1 up to fivefold more than subcutaneous fat in obese individuals. This obesity-associated increase is driven by factors such as insulin and inflammatory cytokines, briefly referencing signals that enhance production without altering core biosynthetic sites.
During developmental processes like wound healing, PAI-1 expression is upregulated in fibroblasts, facilitating extracellular matrix stabilization and tissue repair. In fetal skin, PAI-1 levels in fibroblasts remain low early in gestation (e.g., E14.5 in mice) to support scarless healing but increase later (e.g., E18), aligning with heightened scarring potential.
Species differences influence PAI-1 expression patterns, notably in adipose tissue. Humans exhibit higher overall PAI-1 production from visceral adipose compared to rodents, where expression increases predominantly in visceral depots during obesity but remains lower in subcutaneous fat across conditions.

Regulatory Mechanisms

The expression of plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) is tightly controlled at the transcriptional level by various growth factors and cytokines. Transforming growth factor- (TGF-) potently induces PAI-1 transcription through Smad signaling, where ligand-bound TGF- receptors phosphorylate Smad2 and Smad3, leading to their association with Smad4 and subsequent binding to CAGA boxes in the PAI-1 promoter. This pathway is essential for PAI-1 upregulation in response to TGF- in diverse cell types, including fibroblasts and endothelial cells. Inflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor- (TNF-) and interleukin-1 (IL-1) also stimulate PAI-1 transcription via activation of nuclear factor-B (NF-B) and activator protein-1 (AP-1) pathways, resulting in increased PAI-1 mRNA and protein synthesis in endothelial and adipose cells.
Hormonal factors further modulate PAI-1 levels through transcriptional and potentially other mechanisms. Estrogen reduces PAI-1 expression and circulating levels, as demonstrated by hormone-replacement therapy with conjugated estrogens, which lowered plasma PAI-1 by up to 50% in postmenopausal women, particularly those with elevated baseline levels. This effect is mediated by estrogen receptor-dependent inhibition of cytokine-induced PAI-1 promoter activity. In contrast, glucocorticoids such as dexamethasone and corticosterone enhance PAI-1 transcription and production, often synergizing with TGF- to amplify expression in adipose tissue and other sites.
Post-transcriptional mechanisms, including regulation of mRNA stability, provide an additional layer of control over PAI-1 expression. PAI-1 mRNA contains AU-rich elements in its 3' untranslated region that promote rapid degradation, but factors like epidermal growth factor (EGF) and TGF- can stabilize the transcript, thereby prolonging PAI-1 protein synthesis. The 4G/5G polymorphism in the PAI-1 promoter influences mRNA abundance and stability, with the 4G allele associated with higher steady-state mRNA levels due to enhanced transcriptional efficiency and reduced degradation susceptibility.
Circadian rhythms regulate PAI-1 expression through clock gene components such as CLOCK and BMAL1, which bind the PAI-1 promoter to drive oscillatory transcription, peaking in the early morning and contributing to diurnal variations in plasma PAI-1. Metabolic cues, particularly in states of insulin resistance, elevate PAI-1 via insulin-mediated signaling that stabilizes mRNA and enhances synthesis, a process prominent in metabolic syndrome where PAI-1 overexpression links adiposity to impaired fibrinolysis. This metabolic regulation intersects with obesity-related PAI-1 production in adipose tissue.

Physiological Functions

Role in Fibrinolysis

Plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) functions as the principal physiological inhibitor of the fibrinolytic system by rapidly and irreversibly binding to tissue-type plasminogen activator (tPA) and urokinase-type plasminogen activator (uPA). This interaction forms stable, covalent complexes that prevent these serine proteases from cleaving plasminogen into the active enzyme plasmin, thereby blocking the degradation of fibrin within blood clots. The inhibitory mechanism relies on PAI-1's serpin fold, which undergoes a conformational change upon target engagement, inactivating the plasminogen activators and halting fibrinolysis.
By modulating the activity of tPA and uPA, PAI-1 maintains hemostatic balance in the vasculature, where elevated PAI-1 levels enhance clot stability to mitigate bleeding risks during injury, while lower PAI-1 levels promote efficient fibrinolysis to ensure timely clot resolution. This regulatory role is critical for preventing both excessive thrombosis and hemorrhage, as PAI-1 accounts for the majority of tPA inhibition in circulating plasma, with concentrations typically ranging from 5 to 50 ng/mL in healthy individuals.
In vivo studies using PAI-1 knockout mice demonstrate enhanced fibrinolytic capacity, with blood clots lysing more rapidly compared to wild-type controls, underscoring PAI-1's essential role in restraining fibrinolysis under physiological conditions. These models also reveal a mild baseline increase in fibrinolytic activity and reduced propensity for thrombus persistence, confirming PAI-1's dominance in modulating the plasminogen activator/plasmin pathway.

Extracellular Matrix Remodeling

Plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) plays a critical role in extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling by modulating the urokinase-type plasminogen activator (uPA)/uPA receptor (uPAR) system, which facilitates pericellular proteolysis essential for cell migration and tissue restructuring. By inhibiting uPA activity, PAI-1 limits excessive plasmin generation at the cell surface, thereby regulating the localized degradation of ECM components such as fibronectin and laminin that would otherwise promote uncontrolled cell invasion. Additionally, in its matrix-bound form, PAI-1 promotes cell migration by stabilizing vitronectin-uPAR interactions, facilitating directed motility in physiological remodeling. This balanced inhibition ensures precise control over migratory processes in non-vascular contexts, preventing aberrant tissue architecture.
In embryogenesis, PAI-1's modulation of the uPA/uPAR axis supports orchestrated cell migration during organ formation, where transient uPA activation drives morphogenetic movements while PAI-1 curbs over-degradation of provisional matrices. For instance, in developing neural tissues, PAI-1 interacts with vascular-derived factors to fine-tune interneuron tangential migration, ensuring proper layering without excessive ECM breakdown. Similarly, during wound healing, matrix-bound PAI-1 suppresses uPA-uPAR interactions to restrain pericellular proteolysis, promoting orderly re-epithelialization and granulation tissue formation; studies in PAI-1-deficient models demonstrate accelerated but disorganized healing with increased matrix turnover. This dual soluble and ECM-anchored function of PAI-1 highlights its importance in maintaining spatial fidelity of repair processes.
PAI-1 indirectly regulates matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) by inhibiting plasmin, which otherwise activates pro-MMPs like MMP-2 and MMP-9, thereby controlling ECM turnover in tissue maintenance and repair. This inhibitory cascade prevents hyperactive collagenolysis and elastin degradation, preserving structural integrity during dynamic remodeling events. In stromal contexts, PAI-1's suppression of plasmin-mediated MMP activation maintains a balanced proteolytic environment conducive to fibrosis resolution rather than pathological scarring.
In angiogenesis, PAI-1 limits excessive vessel sprouting by constraining protease-driven ECM invasion at endothelial tips, ensuring controlled neovascularization without chaotic outgrowth. Ex vivo assays of aortic rings from PAI-1 knockout mice reveal heightened capillary sprouting in collagen matrices, underscoring PAI-1's role in stabilizing nascent vessels through moderated uPA/plasmin activity. This regulatory function is evident in tissue-specific models, such as placental development, where PAI-1 deficiency causes transient reductions in vascularization at embryonic day 10.5, impairing labyrinthine layer formation due to dysregulated ECM remodeling and fetal-maternal interface integrity.

Pathological Roles

Cardiovascular Diseases

Elevated levels of plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) are associated with an increased risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE), as higher plasma PAI-1 concentrations predict future incident VTE events independently of other risk factors. Similarly, elevated PAI-1 is linked to a higher incidence of myocardial infarction (MI), where impaired fibrinolysis due to increased PAI-1 contributes to thrombotic complications in coronary artery disease. In patients with thrombotic disorders, PAI-1 elevation is a common finding that disrupts the balance of the fibrinolytic system, promoting clot persistence.
The 4G/5G polymorphism in the PAI-1 gene promoter, which influences PAI-1 expression levels, further exacerbates cardiovascular risk; the 4G/4G genotype is associated with approximately 2-fold increased odds of MI compared to the 5G/5G genotype, as evidenced by adjusted odds ratios around 2.0 in population studies. This genetic variant leads to higher baseline PAI-1 production, amplifying hypofibrinolysis in susceptible individuals.
In atherosclerosis, PAI-1 contributes to disease progression by impairing fibrinolysis and promoting thrombosis, while its inhibition of matrix metalloproteinases may influence plaque remodeling, though the net effect heightens the risk of acute events. This imbalance contributes to the progression of atherothrombosis, where PAI-1 overexpression in vascular tissues fosters chronic vessel remodeling and acute occlusion.
PAI-1 levels are also correlated with hypertension and metabolic syndrome, where elevated concentrations align with insulin resistance, a core feature of these conditions that exacerbates cardiovascular pathology. In metabolic syndrome, PAI-1 acts as a prothrombotic mediator linking obesity, dyslipidemia, and vascular inflammation to heightened CVD risk.
As a biomarker in acute coronary syndrome (ACS), elevated PAI-1 antigen levels are independently associated with major adverse cardiovascular events, serving as an indicator of poor prognosis and thrombotic burden in affected patients. Increased PAI-1 in ACS reflects ongoing endothelial dysfunction and fibrinolysis impairment, aiding risk stratification beyond traditional markers.

Cancer and Metastasis

Plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) exhibits a paradoxical role in tumor invasion and metastasis, stemming from its inhibition of urokinase-type plasminogen activator (uPA). While uPA promotes extracellular matrix (ECM) degradation to facilitate cancer cell migration, PAI-1's inhibition of uPA was initially expected to suppress invasion; however, PAI-1 ultimately enhances tumor progression through context-dependent mechanisms. At low levels, PAI-1 permits sufficient uPA activity to drive ECM proteolysis, enabling early tumor cell detachment and invasion. In contrast, high PAI-1 levels later stabilize the tumor microenvironment by limiting excessive proteolysis, promoting cell survival signals via interactions with low-density lipoprotein receptor-related protein 1 (LRP-1), and facilitating epithelial-mesenchymal transition, thereby supporting metastatic spread in breast and other cancers.
PAI-1 also contributes to angiogenesis, a critical process for tumor growth and metastasis, by modulating vascular endothelial cell behavior. In PAI-1 knockout mouse models, implanted malignant keratinocytes formed smaller tumors with significantly reduced vascularization, as evidenced by decreased microvessel density in vivo. This reduction arises from PAI-1's role in promoting endothelial cell migration and survival through vitronectin binding and inhibition of apoptosis, with exogenous PAI-1 restoration normalizing vascular outgrowth in aortic ring assays.
Recent research has uncovered PAI-1's involvement in immune evasion, particularly by upregulating programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) expression on tumor cells. In 2024 studies using breast cancer and melanoma models, PAI-1 activated the JAK/STAT signaling pathway via LRP1/uPA/uPAR complexes, inducing PD-L1 on cancer cells and surrounding stromal elements like tumor-associated macrophages, which suppressed CD8+ T-cell activation and infiltration. Pharmacological inhibition of PAI-1 with TM5614 reduced PD-L1 levels, decreased immunosuppressive cell abundance, and enhanced anti-tumor immunity in mouse xenografts, suggesting PAI-1 blockade could synergize with PD-1 inhibitors.
Clinically, elevated serum PAI-1 levels serve as a prognostic marker for metastasis in various cancers. In melanoma patients, high PAI-1 concentrations (>2.86 ng/mL baseline) predict advanced disease and resistance to immunotherapy, correlating with increased metastatic risk through enhanced tumor cell adhesion modulation. Similarly, in advanced non-small cell lung cancer, patients with high PAI-1 levels have poorer prognosis, reflecting its role in promoting vascular remodeling and invasion.

Aging and Senescence

Plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) plays a pivotal role in cellular senescence, where it is upregulated in response to stress signals via the p53 pathway, acting as a downstream effector that enforces cell cycle arrest and contributes to the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP). PAI-1 overexpression alone can induce senescence in fibroblasts by disrupting extracellular matrix (ECM) homeostasis and promoting the release of pro-inflammatory SASP factors such as IL-6 and TGF-. Concurrently, the TGF- pathway amplifies PAI-1 expression, which in turn enhances TGF- signaling to sustain senescence through ECM remodeling and secretion of profibrotic mediators like MMP-2 and MMP-9. This bidirectional interaction creates a feedback loop that perpetuates the senescent state across various cell types, including endothelial and epithelial cells.
In the context of organismal aging, elevated PAI-1 levels drive pathological changes in multiple tissues, notably contributing to vascular stiffness by inhibiting endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) and reducing nitric oxide bioavailability, which promotes arterial hardening and collagen deposition. PAI-1 also accelerates stem cell exhaustion, particularly in mesenchymal and hematopoietic stem cells, by blocking urokinase plasminogen activator (uPA)/tissue plasminogen activator (tPA)-mediated insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1) signaling, leading to impaired stem cell proliferation and niche dysfunction. These mechanisms link PAI-1 to age-related diseases, including cardiovascular pathologies where it exacerbates senescence in vascular smooth muscle cells, as highlighted in a 2025 review on PAI-1's contributions to cardiovascular aging.
Experimental evidence from mouse models underscores PAI-1's causal role in aging; genetic deletion of PAI-1 in Klotho-deficient (kl/kl) mice, which exhibit accelerated aging, prolongs median lifespan up to 4.2-fold (from 58 days to 246 days) and reduces senescence markers such as p16^INK4a by 92%, IL-6 by 83%, and restores telomere length in multiple organs. This intervention also preserves organ function and attenuates ECM alterations, demonstrating PAI-1's mediation of senescence-driven decline.
Recent research from 2023 to 2025 has further implicated PAI-1 in neurodegenerative aging, particularly Alzheimer's disease (AD), where elevated PAI-1 in the hippocampus and cortex of late-onset AD patients correlates with p53/p21 activation and promotes neuronal senescence. PAI-1 secreted by senescent astrocytes induces neuron apoptosis and impairs amyloid- (A) clearance, increasing A burden in AD models; PAI-1 inhibition reduces brain A load and mitigates neuropathology. These findings position PAI-1 as a key modulator of neuronal senescence in AD progression.

Interactions

Protein-Protein Interactions

Plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) interacts with vitronectin, a multifunctional glycoprotein in the extracellular matrix (ECM), to stabilize its active conformation and prevent rapid conversion to the latent form. This binding occurs primarily through the somatomedin B domain of vitronectin and the helix F region of PAI-1, with a high-affinity dissociation constant (Kd) of approximately 0.3 nM, enabling efficient association under physiological conditions. By anchoring PAI-1 to the ECM, vitronectin localizes the inhibitor to sites of potential fibrinolysis, thereby enhancing its regulatory role in tissue remodeling.
PAI-1 also forms a complex with 1-acid glycoprotein (orosomucoid, ORM1), an acute-phase reactant elevated during inflammation, which stabilizes PAI-1's inhibitory activity against plasminogen activators. This interaction potentiates PAI-1's anti-fibrinolytic effects in inflammatory states, such as sepsis, by prolonging the functional lifespan of active PAI-1 and contributing to a pro-thrombotic environment.
Furthermore, PAI-1 engages in ternary complex formation with urokinase-type plasminogen activator (uPA) and its receptor (uPAR), which subsequently modulates interactions with integrins, particularly v3 and 51, on cell surfaces. Upon binding uPA to uPAR, PAI-1 inhibits the protease and disrupts vitronectin-mediated cell adhesion by altering integrin affinity, thereby regulating cell migration and detachment in processes like wound healing. This modulation underscores PAI-1's broader influence on ECM dynamics, as detailed in its role in extracellular matrix remodeling.
PAI-1 interacts with low-density lipoprotein receptor-related protein 1 (LRP1), a member of the LDL receptor family, to facilitate the endocytosis and degradation of uPA-PAI-1 complexes. This binding, with higher affinity for the complex than free PAI-1, regulates pericellular proteolysis and influences intracellular signaling pathways involved in cell migration, adhesion, and survival.

Genetic and Pathway Interactions

Plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1), encoded by the SERPINE1 gene, exhibits epistatic interactions with variants in the angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) gene that modulate thrombosis risk. Specifically, the combination of the PAI-1 4G allele and the ACE D/D genotype has been associated with an increased risk of myocardial infarction, with an odds ratio of 1.5 (95% CI: 1.042.17) in a cohort of matched case-control pairs from the Physicians' Health Study, highlighting a gene-gene interaction that elevates PAI-1 levels and impairs fibrinolysis. This epistasis underscores how SERPINE1 polymorphisms can amplify cardiovascular susceptibility when co-occurring with ACE I/D variants, independent of individual effects. The promoter 4G/5G polymorphism in SERPINE1, which influences basal PAI-1 expression, contributes to this interaction by enhancing transcriptional activity in the presence of ACE D alleles.
PAI-1 integrates into the transforming growth factor- (TGF-)/Smad signaling pathway, where it acts as a downstream effector promoting fibrosis in organs such as the liver and kidney. TGF- induces PAI-1 transcription via Smad3 binding to the SERPINE1 promoter, leading to extracellular matrix accumulation and fibrotic remodeling in renal tubulointerstitial diseases. In hepatic fibrosis models, this pathway activation upregulates PAI-1, which in turn stabilizes matrix proteins and perpetuates Smad-mediated profibrotic signaling, as evidenced by reduced fibrosis in PAI-1-deficient mice exposed to TGF-. These interactions position PAI-1 as a key mediator linking TGF-/Smad to chronic fibrotic pathologies.
A 2024 signaling pathway map delineates PAI-1's role in cellular senescence, revealing crosstalk with the Wnt/-catenin pathway that influences aging-related processes, including those implicated in Alzheimer's disease. PAI-1 serves as a transcriptional target of canonical Wnt/-catenin signaling, where -catenin/TCF complexes bind the SERPINE1 promoter to upregulate expression, thereby reinforcing senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) components in senescent cells. In Alzheimer's contexts, dysregulated Wnt/-catenin signaling intersects with elevated PAI-1 levels in neuronal and glial cells, contributing to amyloid--induced senescence and neurodegeneration, as PAI-1 inhibition mitigates -catenin-mediated fibrotic and inflammatory responses. This network highlights PAI-1's integrative function across senescence pathways.
Genome-wide association studies have identified variants near PPARG that regulate circulating PAI-1 levels independent of body mass index. In obese populations, elevated PAI-1 expression links to insulin resistance and obesity progression.

Pharmacology and Therapeutics

PAI-1 Inhibitors

Plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) inhibitors have emerged as promising therapeutic agents to counteract hypofibrinolysis by blocking PAI-1's inhibition of plasminogen activators, thereby enhancing fibrinolysis and potentially mitigating thrombotic and fibrotic conditions. Small-molecule inhibitors represent a key class, with tiplaxtinin (PAI-039) serving as a prototypical example. This orally bioavailable compound selectively inhibits PAI-1 by stabilizing its latent, inactive conformation, preventing interaction with tissue-type plasminogen activator (tPA) and urokinase-type plasminogen activator (uPA), with an IC50 of 2.7 M in biochemical assays. Preclinical studies demonstrated that tiplaxtinin reduced thrombus formation in rat models of arterial and venous thrombosis, increasing time to occlusion and promoting thrombus resolution without affecting platelet aggregation. Although advanced to phase I clinical trials for antithrombotic potential, development was halted due to an unfavorable risk-benefit profile, including bleeding risks.
Biologic inhibitors, particularly monoclonal antibodies targeting PAI-1, offer high specificity and have shown utility in oncology. For instance, an anti-PAI-1 monoclonal antibody that binds the reactive center loop (RCL) of PAI-1 inhibits its serpin activity, thereby disrupting PAI-1-mediated extracellular matrix remodeling and tumor invasion. In preclinical models of esophageal squamous cell carcinoma, this antibody significantly suppressed metastasis and tumor growth by reducing PAI-1-induced uPA inhibition and downstream signaling for cell migration. Such antibodies provide a targeted approach for cancer applications, where elevated PAI-1 promotes tumor progression, though clinical translation remains limited by immunogenicity concerns.
Natural compounds also modulate PAI-1 activity, with epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG), a polyphenol abundant in green tea, inhibiting PAI-1 expression and function. EGCG suppresses TNF--induced PAI-1 production in human umbilical vein endothelial cells via inhibition of ERK1/2 phosphorylation, reducing PAI-1 mRNA and protein levels in a dose-dependent manner. This inhibition may contribute to EGCG's cardiovascular protective effects observed in epidemiological studies of green tea consumption.
In preclinical models of atherosclerosis, PAI-1 inhibitors have demonstrated substantial efficacy in reducing plaque burden. For example, chronic administration of tiplaxtinin in LDL receptor-deficient mice on a high-fat diet significantly decreased atherosclerotic lesion area in the aortic root, arch, and thoracic aorta by up to 50%, alongside reduced macrophage infiltration and vascular smooth muscle cell senescence. These findings underscore the therapeutic potential of PAI-1 inhibition in hypofibrinolytic states associated with cardiovascular disease, though human trials are needed to confirm translatability. As of 2025, PAI-1 inhibitors like TM5614 are in Phase II and III clinical trials for various conditions, including SARS-CoV-2 pneumonia, systemic sclerosis-associated interstitial lung disease, malignant melanoma (in combination with nivolumab), and chronic myelogenous leukemia.

Emerging Therapies

Recent advancements in nucleic acid therapeutics have introduced innovative strategies to address PAI-1 dysregulation. One promising approach involves mRNA lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) for PAI-1 replacement therapy in deficiency syndromes, where PAI-1 levels are critically low, leading to bleeding disorders and cardiac fibrosis. A 2025 study demonstrated that repeated administration of murine PAI-1 mRNA encapsulated in LNPs effectively restored circulating PAI-1 activity in PAI-1-deficient mouse models, establishing a foundation for this modality as a preventative treatment without the limitations of protein-based therapies due to PAI-1's short half-life.
Antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) and related RNA interference technologies targeting SERPINE1 mRNA offer potential for reducing PAI-1 expression in conditions like fibrosis and aging, where elevated PAI-1 contributes to extracellular matrix accumulation and cellular senescence. For instance, knockdown of SERPINE1 using small interfering RNA (siRNA), a mechanism analogous to ASOs, has been shown to enhance matrix metalloproteinase activity and reduce fibrotic adhesions in injured flexor tendon models, highlighting PAI-1's role in suppressing tissue remodeling. In aging contexts, PAI-1 upregulation is linked to senescence-associated secretory phenotype, and ASO-mediated targeting of related pro-fibrotic pathways, such as TGF- signaling, has demonstrated antifibrotic effects that could extend to PAI-1 modulation.
Gene editing technologies, including CRISPR/Cas9, are being explored in preclinical settings to correct PAI-1-related genetic variants, such as the 4G/5G promoter polymorphism associated with increased thrombosis risk due to higher PAI-1 expression. While specific trials targeting this polymorphism remain early-stage, broader applications of CRISPR for congenital coagulopathies underscore its potential to modulate PAI-1 levels and prevent thrombotic events by editing SERPINE1 regulatory elements in hematopoietic cells.
In oncology, PAI-1-neutralizing nanobodies represent a cutting-edge biologic for enhancing antitumor immunity, particularly by disrupting PAI-1's promotion of immune evasion in the tumor microenvironment. Structural studies from 2020-2022 revealed that nanobodies like Nb42 and Nb64 inhibit PAI-1 activity by stabilizing its latent conformation or blocking plasminogen activator interactions, providing a scaffold for engineering cancer-specific variants. Recent preclinical evidence indicates that PAI-1 inhibition can improve T-cell infiltration and synergize with checkpoint inhibitors, addressing PAI-1's role in suppressing immune responses in solid tumors by facilitating PD-L1 expression.
