 Radford University is a public comprehensive university located in Radford, Virginia, founded in 1910 as a women's normal school to train teachers. With a total enrollment of 8,817 students for fall 2025including 7,395 undergraduates and 1,422 graduatesthe institution offers 75 bachelor's degrees, 27 master's programs, and 5 doctoral degrees across seven colleges, emphasizing hands-on, career-focused education in fields such as nursing, business, education, and the sciences. As an NCAA Division I member in the Big South Conference, Radford supports 16 varsity sports teams and has been recognized as the 2025 Top Adventure College in the Southeast and Mid-Atlantic region for its vibrant outdoor recreation opportunities.
Originally established as the State Normal and Industrial School for Women at Radford, the university underwent significant evolution, renaming to Radford College in 1943, admitting men and transitioning to coeducation in 1964, and achieving full university status in 1979 amid expansions in academic programs and infrastructure. Today, it maintains a 90% acceptance rate and a 49% six-year graduation rate, fostering close student-faculty relationships through small class sizes and personalized advising on its 211-acre main campus, complemented by a satellite campus in Roanoke via Radford University Carilion. The university's commitment to accessibility is highlighted by initiatives like the Radford Tuition Promise, which covers tuition for qualifying Virginia residents, contributing to consecutive years of record enrollment growth.
In national rankings, Radford is placed #329 among National Universities and #178 among Top Public Schools by U.S. News & World Report for 2026, while earning a #6 spot for Best Value Colleges in Virginia and a STARS Silver rating for sustainability efforts. Its academic strengths include highly regarded programs in nursingthe largest undergraduate cohort ever at 1,295 students in 2025and education, alongside interdisciplinary opportunities in criminal justice, psychology, and performing arts. Beyond academics, Radford promotes a dynamic campus life with over 200 student organizations, cultural events, and community engagement, preparing graduates to become responsible citizens and leaders in Virginia and beyond.

History

Founding and early years

Radford University traces its origins to March 10, 1910, when the Virginia General Assembly chartered the institution as the State Normal and Industrial School for Women at Radford, a two-year normal school dedicated to training teachers for rural schools in Virginia. Located in the rural New River Valley of Southwest Virginia, the school aimed to address the shortage of qualified educators in the state's underserved areas, emphasizing practical instruction in teaching methods, domestic arts, and industrial skills for women. Initial enrollment stood at approximately 150 students, reflecting the institution's modest beginnings as a regional response to educational needs.
Classes commenced on September 17, 1913, following the dedication of the Administration Buildinglater known as Founders Hallon August 9, 1913, which served as the campus's first major structure housing classrooms, administrative offices, a library, and an auditorium. The first commencement occurred from June 6 to 9, 1914, awarding diplomas to five graduates and marking the school's early success in producing educators. Campus development proceeded gradually, with Tyler Hall opening as the first official residence hall in 1915 to accommodate the growing student body of women in this isolated setting.
In 1924, the institution was renamed Radford State Teachers College and expanded to a four-year program, enabling it to grant bachelor's degrees and broadening its curriculum beyond initial teacher training. Enrollment grew steadily, reaching over 500 students by 1940, supported by additional facilities and a focus on women's higher education. During the 1940s, amid statewide consolidation efforts, the college affiliated with Virginia Polytechnic Institute in 1944, operating as its women's division and sharing administrative and academic resources to enhance offerings. This period solidified Radford's role as a key center for female scholarship in rural Virginia up to the mid-20th century.

Transition to university status

In 1964, Radford College achieved a significant milestone by becoming an independent four-year institution, granting its first bachelor's degrees and separating from its prior affiliation as the women's division of Virginia Polytechnic Institute. This transition marked the beginning of expanded academic offerings beyond teacher training, with enrollment reaching 2,460 students that fall.
The institution underwent further transformation in 1972 under the leadership of President Donald N. Dedmon, who assumed office that year and spearheaded its integration into Virginia's public higher education system as a co-educational college. On June 8, 1972, Dedmon announced the admission of male students starting in the fall, ending the college's women-only tradition and aligning it with broader state educational reforms. This shift boosted enrollment to 3,670 by fall 1972, reflecting increased accessibility and appeal.
By 1979, following sustained growth in programs and student numbers, the Virginia General Assembly elevated Radford College to university status, renaming it Radford University in its fifth official name change. This recognition formalized its evolution into a comprehensive public institution offering diverse undergraduate and emerging graduate degrees.
The transition period saw substantial campus expansions to accommodate rising demand, including the acquisition of new properties along the New River and construction of academic buildings such as those supporting expanded liberal arts and professional programs. Enrollment surged from approximately 3,600 in 1972 to over 5,700 by 1980, necessitating these developments under Dedmon's administration.
Radford University's growth during this era played a pivotal role in the economic and demographic expansion of Radford city, fostering an education-driven economy that attracted faculty, staff, and related services to the region. The influx of students and institutional investments stimulated local commerce and positioned the university as a cornerstone of community development in the New River Valley.

Recent developments

In the 1990s and 2000s, Radford University expanded its academic portfolio with the addition of advanced graduate programs, including the Psy.D. in Counseling Psychology launched in 2008 to meet growing demand for professional training in mental health services. The institution also transitioned to NCAA Division I athletics in 1984, joining the Big South Conference and elevating its competitive profile across multiple sports. These developments supported broader institutional growth, with enrollment rising steadily and new facilities enhancing campus life.
The 2010s marked further expansions in infrastructure and accessibility. In 2014, the university opened the 115,000-square-foot Student Recreation and Wellness Center, a $32 million facility funded by student fees to promote health, fitness, and community engagement among undergraduates and graduates. Concurrently, Radford emphasized online and continuing education, launching its first fully online doctoral programthe Doctor of Nursing Practicein fall 2010 to serve working professionals and extend reach beyond the main campus.
Recent years have seen robust enrollment gains and strategic initiatives amid post-COVID recovery. Fall 2024 enrollment reached a record 7,686 students, a increase from the prior year, driven by a 28% rise in freshmen and 39% in new transfers, reflecting enhanced recruitment and affordability efforts. This momentum continued into fall 2025 with 7,837 total students and a record 796 new graduate enrollees, up 8% from 2024. In response to the pandemic, the university bolstered hybrid learning options through the Center for Innovative Teaching and Learning, integrating flexible modalities to support student persistence and access.
To promote affordability, Radford implemented a tuition rebate in 2024-2025, refunding the approved 1.5% increase for in-state undergraduates in traditional programs, benefiting over 5,000 students. In July 2024, the university partnered with Virginia Tech to create a fast-track pathway for Radford undergraduates in agricultural and applied economics to complete a master's degree in one additional year, addressing workforce needs in agribusiness. In May 2025, the university restructured its athletics program to grow its NCAA Division I offerings, including adding men's and women's indoor and outdoor track and field teams. November 2025 brought the launch of five new donor societies, revitalizing philanthropy to fund scholarships and programs under the ongoing "For the Commonwealth" campaign. These efforts align with updates to the strategic plan, "Shaping Tomorrow Together" (2026-2031), which prioritizes affordability, outreach, and inclusive excellence following board approval in late 2025.

Campus

Location and layout

Radford University is situated in the city of Radford, Virginia, within the New River Valley region of the Roanoke Valley, directly along the banks of the New River. The main campus encompasses 211 acres of scenic terrain, offering proximity to the Appalachian Mountains and serving as an integral part of the local landscape. Additional sites, such as the 380-acre Selu Conservancy along the nearby Little River, extend the university's footprint and provide resources for environmental and educational activities just minutes from the main campus.
The campus layout centers around a historic main quadrangle featuring early red-brick buildings that anchor the academic core, with surrounding areas divided into residential zones, athletic facilities, and administrative spaces for a cohesive, mixed-use environment. Pedestrian-friendly pathways connect these zones, weaving through abundant green spaces that enhance accessibility and promote an inviting atmosphere across the approximately 55 buildings on site. The architectural style blends Georgian Revival elements in the original structurescharacterized by symmetrical facades, stone accents, and brick constructionwith contemporary designs in newer additions, reflecting the university's evolution while maintaining visual harmony.
Nestled in a region of natural beauty, the campus integrates with the surrounding community through its shared riverfront along the New River, fostering recreational and ecological connections. Accessibility is supported by Interstate 81, which runs adjacent to the campus via Exit 109, and proximity to Amtrak services in Roanoke, approximately 36 miles southwest. Sustainability initiatives include campus-wide efforts to increase renewable energy, such as evaluations for photovoltaic solar panels, alongside water conservation measures protecting the New River watershed.

Key facilities

Radford University's academic facilities include several key buildings dedicated to administration, teaching, and research. Martin Hall serves as the primary administrative hub, housing offices for the president, provost, institutional research, and marketing and communication, and was dedicated in 1996 in honor of former president Charles K. Martin. Peters Hall, originally constructed in 1953 as a physical education facility, now primarily supports the College of Education and Human Development, including the Teaching Resources Center and a climbing wall for recreational use. The College of Humanities and Behavioral Sciences operates from Hemphill Hall, a 143,600-square-foot LEED Gold-certified building completed in 2016 that features professional-grade labs, a moot courtroom, broadcasting studios, and collaborative spaces for disciplines like history, psychology, and media studies. McConnell Library anchors the academic infrastructure with extensive collections, including special archives on regional history, university records, and materials from local historians, supporting research across disciplines.
Support infrastructure at Radford University is managed by Facilities Management, which oversees maintenance, operations, and development to ensure clean, safe, and functional buildings and grounds for students, faculty, and visitors. A notable facility is the Hurlburt Student Center, affectionately known as "The Bonnie," a 45,000-square-foot hub opened in 2005 that functions as a central gathering place with lounges, a food court, computer labs, meeting rooms, a game room, and a 250-seat auditorium; it underwent a $1 million renovation and reopened in October 2025 with added amenities like new restaurants and expanded gaming areas. Conference services facilitate events through various venues, including technology-equipped rooms in buildings like McConnell Library, Peters Hall, and Russell Hall, along with lodging options during academic breaks.
Utility and technology services enhance campus accessibility and functionality. High-speed Wi-Fi is available campus-wide via the eduroam network, enabling seamless connectivity in academic buildings, residence halls, and outdoor areas. Vending machines, kitchens, and laundry facilities are provided in residence halls to support daily student needs.
Health and safety features include the on-campus Student Health Services center in Moffett Hall, staffed by Carilion Clinic providers offering medical care, immunizations, and wellness support. Emergency services are coordinated through the Office of Emergency Management, University Police, and the student-run Radford University EMS, which provides 24/7 response using an all-hazards approach. Many facilities incorporate ADA-compliant designs, with the Center for Accessibility Services ensuring accommodations like accessible housing and technology for students with disabilities.
In the 2020s, expansion projects have focused on modernizing infrastructure for enhanced learning. Reed Hall and Curie Hall, housing biology and other science programs, underwent complete renovations in 2020 to include state-of-the-art teaching and research labs. IT upgrades, including expanded cybersecurity training and online platforms through the Center for Innovative Teaching and Learning, have supported hybrid and competency-based education initiatives.

Academics

Structure and rankings

Radford University enrolls 7,837 students for fall 2025, with undergraduates accounting for 79% of the total, or 6,206 individuals. The institution maintains a student-faculty ratio of 14:1, which supports an average class size of 19 and promotes interactive, hands-on learning experiences.
The academic calendar operates on a semester system, featuring fall and spring terms of 15 weeks each, supplemented by summer sessions and accelerated options like 7-week fast-track courses to accommodate diverse student needs. This structure emphasizes practical, experiential education through small classes and collaborative opportunities across disciplines.
In national rankings, Radford University placed #329 among National Universities in the 2026 U.S. News & World Report edition, reflecting its transition from regional to broader recognition, and #178 among Top Public Schools. It has earned high marks for social mobility, including designation as a Top Performer in the Regional Universities South category in prior assessments, underscoring its role in advancing access and outcomes for underserved students. Additionally, the university was voted the Top Adventure College in the Southeast and Mid-Atlantic region for 2025 by Blue Ridge Outdoors magazine, highlighting its integration of outdoor activities with academics.
Under the 2025 Carnegie Classification, Radford is classified as a Professions-focused Undergraduate/Graduate-Master's Large/Medium institution and an Opportunity College and University for higher access and earnings potential, focusing on professional and applied scholarship, including doctoral programs, rather than high-volume R1 research output. Key initiatives include the digital archives and special collections at McConnell Library, which preserve university history, local manuscripts, and cultural materials to support scholarly inquiry and community engagement. Academic excellence extends to athletics, where 85.5% of the 269 eligible student-athletes achieved a GPA of 3.2 or higher, earning spots on the 2023-2024 Big South Presidential Honor Rollthe highest rate in the conference.
Diversity and inclusion form a core aspect of Radford's academic environment, with minority students comprising about 34% of the enrollment. The university advances equity through targeted initiatives for first-generation college attendees, including enhanced advising and financial support, earning it the 2025 Carnegie Classification as an Opportunity College and University for higher access and earnings potential.

Colleges and programs

Radford University organizes its academic offerings across seven colleges: the Artis College of Science and Technology, the Davis College of Business and Economics, the College of Education and Human Development, the College of Humanities and Behavioral Sciences, the College of Visual and Performing Arts, the Waldron College of Health and Human Services, and the College of Graduate and Professional Studies. Each college houses departments that deliver specialized curricula, fostering hands-on learning in fields ranging from STEM to the arts and health professions.
The university provides more than 70 undergraduate majors, alongside over 100 minors and certificates, spanning diverse disciplines. Popular programs include nursing (B.S.N.), business administration (B.B.A.), psychology (B.A./B.S.), and criminal justice (B.S.), which emphasize practical skills and career preparation. Interdisciplinary options, such as multi- and interdisciplinary studies (B.A./B.S.), allow students to customize their education across fields.
At the graduate level, Radford offers over 40 programs, including 27 master's degrees in 23 disciplines, six doctoral programs, and various certificates. Notable offerings encompass the Master of Business Administration (M.B.A.), Master of Science in Nursing (M.S.N.), Education Specialist (Ed.S.), and Doctor of Psychology (Psy.D.) in counseling psychology. Continuing education and online options through Radford University Online support working professionals with flexible formats for degrees like the M.B.A. and R.N. to B.S.N.
Unique programs highlight the university's strengths in creative and applied fields, such as interior design (B.F.A.), dance (B.A./B.F.A.), and forensic science (interdisciplinary minor). Students can enhance their studies through honors programs and study abroad opportunities, promoting global perspectives and academic excellence.
Radford maintains a 90% acceptance rate for admissions, reflecting its accessibility, while approximately 50% of students graduate within six years.

Student life

Housing and residence

Radford University provides on-campus housing through 15 residence halls and university-operated apartments, offering a range of living options for undergraduate students. These facilities accommodate a substantial portion of the student body, with individual halls varying in size; for instance, Muse Hall, the largest, houses approximately 950 residents, while Moffett Hall accommodates about 350 and Jefferson Hall around 185. Room configurations include traditional double-occupancy setups and suites with attached private bathrooms, alongside specialized options like honors housing integrated into living-learning programs.
All rooms come fully furnished with beds, desks, and wardrobes, and include essential amenities such as utilities, cable television, high-speed Wi-Fi, and access to laundry facilities within each building. Themed residential communities enhance the living experience by grouping students with shared interests; examples include wellness-focused housing that promotes holistic well-being through connections to health resources and leadership halls that emphasize personal development and service activities. These initiatives are part of the university's Living-Learning Programs, which integrate academic and co-curricular elements to support student growth.
Dining services operate multiple on-campus venues featuring diverse cuisines to accommodate various dietary needs and preferences, with a strong emphasis on nutritious, quick-service meals and incorporation of locally sourced ingredients where feasible. Flexible meal plans, such as the default Highlander Residential option combining unlimited access to dining halls with flexible dining dollars for retail locations, allow students to tailor their eating habits while promoting balanced nutrition. Allergen-friendly options are also available across locations to ensure inclusivity.
University policies mandate on-campus living for incoming freshmen and first-year students for at least two academic years (four semesters) to facilitate their transition and community integration. Resident Assistants (RAs), upper-level students trained to serve as peer mentors, reside in the halls to offer guidance, enforce policies, and coordinate community-building events like hall programs and social gatherings. The Residence Hall Association further supports resident voices through advocacy and campus-wide programming. Recent facility enhancements prioritize accessibility, including elevator modernizations in Muse Hall, ADA-compliant renovations in projects like Dalton Hall, and broader upgrades to mechanical systems and entryways for improved mobility.
Upperclassmen and transfer students have access to university-operated apartments located adjacent to campus, providing greater independence while maintaining proximity to academic and recreational resources. The university has expanded these options by acquiring and renovating off-campus apartment buildings, ensuring comparable quality to on-campus residences through partnerships with local property management.

Extracurricular activities

Radford University supports over 250 registered student clubs and organizations, encompassing a wide range of interests to foster community and personal development among its students. These groups include cultural organizations such as the African Student Association, which builds a supportive community for African students while promoting cultural awareness on campus. Service-oriented clubs feature the Radford University Campus Ministers Association (RUCMA), a council of advisors and ministers overseeing religious student organizations and coordinating faith-based activities. Special interest groups, like the Asian Pop Culture Club established in 2025, focus on exploring Asian media, including movies, music, and trends, through events and discussions.
The university's Fraternity and Sorority Life community comprises more than 18 chapters, emphasizing leadership development, lifelong friendships, and personal growth. These organizations prioritize philanthropy and community service, with members engaging in fundraising and volunteer initiatives that support local and national causes, such as health awareness and education programs.
Student media and arts provide creative outlets through Highlander Student Media, which includes The Tartan, the student-run bi-weekly newspaper covering campus news and events since 1921. Radio Free Radford offers programming on music, talk shows, and community issues, accessible online and on campus. The Department of Theatre and Cinema produces annual mainstage shows and student-directed works, such as original plays and musicals performed at the Pridemore Playhouse. Campus events like Bonnie Bash, an annual welcome celebration with games, food, music, and competitions, kick off the academic year and draw hundreds of participants.
University traditions reflect its heritage as a state normal school founded in 1910 to train teachers, instilling a strong emphasis on service and community involvement from its early days. Homecoming features alumni reunions, parades, and pride events that unite the campus community each fall. The Highlanders Festival, held annually, celebrates Scottish heritage with heavyweight Highland Games competitions like caber toss and stone put, alongside live music, vendors, and family activities.
The Office of Student Involvement oversees club registration, event planning, and resources like the annual Club Fair, facilitating student-led initiatives and promoting participation. Recent growth in international student enrollment, including a 45% increase in freshman deposits for fall 2025, has led to expanded international student groups that enhance cultural exchange and support for global perspectives.

Athletics

Teams and conference

Radford University's athletic teams compete as the Highlanders in the NCAA Division I, fielding 16 varsity sports across men's and women's programs as members of the Big South Conference. The university has maintained its affiliation with the Big South since becoming a charter member in 1983, when it transitioned to Division I competition. This longstanding membership provides a regional competitive framework, emphasizing rivalries within the conference's footprint in the southeastern United States.
The Highlanders sponsor seven men's varsity teams: baseball, basketball, cross country, golf, soccer, and track and field. The nine women's varsity teams include basketball, cross country, flag football, golf, lacrosse, soccer, softball, track and field, and volleyball. In May 2025, Radford announced a restructuring of its athletic programs, discontinuing both men's and women's tennis effective for the 2025-26 academic year while adding men's indoor and outdoor track and field and women's flag football to enhance program diversity and competitiveness. These changes aim to align with evolving NCAA trends, such as the growing inclusion of flag football, while restoring previously sponsored sports like men's track and field, which had been discontinued in 2014.
Coaching stability has been a hallmark of Radford's athletic programs, particularly in men's basketball, where long-term leadership has fostered consistent development. Dr. Ron Bradley served as head coach from 1991 to 2002, compiling a 193-124 overall record and a .699 winning percentage in Big South regular-season play, which contributed to the program's early success and multiple conference titles. Current head coach Zach Chu, appointed in 2025, continues this tradition by prioritizing player growth and team culture.
Radford places a strong emphasis on recruiting student-athletes who balance academic and athletic commitments, reflected in its high academic achievement rates. In 2024, 85.50% of eligible Highlanders earned spots on the Big South Presidential Honor Roll, surpassing conference averages and underscoring the program's focus on holistic development. Notable alumni like Javonte Green, a 2015 graduate and former standout forward who set multiple career records in scoring, rebounding, and steals, exemplify the impact of this approach; Green transitioned to professional basketball, including stints in the NBA with the Chicago Bulls and Boston Celtics, before his 2025 induction into the Radford Athletics Hall of Fame.
Beyond varsity competition, Radford supports non-varsity options through its Student Recreation and Wellness Center, which oversees approximately 22 club sports teamssuch as rugby, ultimate frisbee, and equestrianand a robust intramural program offering leagues in sports like basketball, flag football, soccer, and volleyball. These opportunities engage over 450 participants annually, promoting inclusivity and skill-building in a less competitive environment.

Achievements and facilities

Radford University's athletics program has achieved notable success in women's basketball, particularly under the leadership of Charlene Curtis, who coached the team to four Big South Conference regular-season titles and three tournament championships during her tenure from 1984 to 1990. In November 2025, Curtis, a 1976 Radford alumna, was awarded the NCAA Gerald R. Ford Award for her trailblazing contributions as a coach and advocate, marking the first such honor for a Radford affiliate, while former baseball player and current head coach Alex Guerra (class of 2011) received the NCAA Award of Valor for his heroic river rescue. In October 2025, the women's soccer team claimed its seventh Big South regular-season title.
The Radford Athletics Hall of Fame, established in 1995, has inducted 76 members as of 2024, recognizing outstanding contributors across sports, with the 2025 classincluding men's basketball standout Javonte Green and women's basketball legend Yvette "Pebbles" Smithannounced in July 2025 and celebrated during the fall induction ceremony. In the 2023-24 season, the men's basketball team posted a 16-17 overall record, achieving a free throw percentage of 73.9% and averaging 10.8 turnovers per game, contributing to efficient play in Big South competition. During the university's transition to NCAA Division I in the mid-1980s, the cross country program experienced key successes, including the women's team's first national qualification in 1980 after winning the Region II Division II championship and multiple athletes earning Big South all-decade honors for the 1980s.
Athletes' academic performance underscores the program's holistic excellence, with 85.5% of eligible student-athletes named to the Big South Presidential Honor Roll in 2024, the highest rate among conference institutions. The Highlander Club, the official booster organization, provides essential funding and support for athletics through memberships, events, and philanthropy to enhance facilities and programs.
Key facilities include the Dedmon Center, a versatile multi-sport arena seating 3,200 that hosts basketball and volleyball games, features a recently renovated court, weight room, and adjacent natatorium with an eight-lane Olympic-size pool and diving well completed in 1987. Outdoor training occurs at Tom Lillard Field at Cupp Stadium, the largest soccer and track facility in the Big South with capacity for 5,000 spectators and supporting soccer, lacrosse, and track and field events. The Student Recreation and Wellness Center complements athletic preparation with its 115,000-square-foot space, including an indoor jogging track, gymnasiums, racquetball courts, and extensive fitness equipment for strength and conditioning.

Notable people

Alumni

Radford University alumni have made significant contributions across diverse fields, reflecting the institution's emphasis on practical education and community engagement. With over 94,000 living alumni, approximately 60% reside in Virginia, while the remaining 40% are distributed across the United States. The university's global reach is evident in its alumni network, which spans all 50 states through regional chapters and representatives.
In sports, several graduates have achieved prominence at professional and collegiate levels. Frank Beamer, who earned a master's degree in guidance and counseling from Radford in 1972, became a legendary college football coach at Virginia Tech, leading the team for 29 seasons and amassing 280 wins. Javonte Green, a 2016 graduate, transitioned from Radford's men's basketball team to the NBA, playing for teams including the Chicago Bulls and Boston Celtics after his jersey was retired by the university in 2021. Carlik Jones, class of 2019, also excelled in basketball, earning multiple Big South Player of the Week honors before competing professionally overseas and representing South Sudan in the 2024 Paris Olympics. Lynne Agee, who received her master's degree in educational administration from Radford in 1981, built a distinguished coaching career in women's basketball, retiring in 2011 with 562 career wins, including 335 at UNC Greensboro.
Graduates in entertainment and the arts have also gained national recognition. Jayma Mays, a 2000 theater alumna, is known for her roles in television series such as Glee and films like Red Eye. Kiera Cass, who graduated with a B.S. in history, authored the bestselling The Selection series, a dystopian young adult franchise adapted into a film.
Other alumni have excelled in business, academia, and advocacy. Randal J. Kirk, a 1976 graduate with a B.A., founded biotech firms including Intrexon and served as the university's commencement speaker in 2019, highlighting his entrepreneurial impact. Steven Salaita, who earned his B.A. in political science and M.A. in English from Radford, became a noted scholar in Native American and indigenous studies, authoring several books on literature and politics, though he gained wider attention for a 2014 controversy involving the rescission of a job offer at the University of Illinois due to his social media activity.
The Radford University Alumni Association honors these achievements through annual awards, recognizing contributions since the first commencement in June 1914. Recent recipients include Charlesiah McLean, class of 1999, an advocate for inclusive leadership who advanced to executive roles at Novartis, emphasizing diversity and mentorship. Similarly, Cindy Chace, a 1986 graduate and former varsity basketball player, has been spotlighted for her civic-minded leadership in business and philanthropy, including service on the RU Foundation board.

Faculty and administrators

Radford University has been led by eight presidents since its founding in 1910 as a women's college. The first president, John Preston McConnell, served from 1911 to 1937 and played a key role in establishing the institution's early academic foundation, including the construction of its initial campus buildings. David Wilbur Peters succeeded him, leading from 1938 to 1951 and overseeing the school's integration as the Women's Division of Virginia Polytechnic Institute during World War II. Charles Knox Martin Jr. served from 1952 to 1972, during which the institution separated from VPI, expanded its student body significantly, and added numerous academic programs and facilities.
Donald Newton Dedmon, president from 1972 to 1994, is noted for his long tenure and contributions to elevating Radford to university status in 1979, alongside substantial campus growth and a focus on faculty development in teaching excellence. Douglas Covington led from 1995 to 2005, launching the university's first capital campaign and advancing strategic planning, including enhancements to arts programs that resulted in the naming of the Covington Center for the Arts. Penelope Ward Kyle, the first woman president, served from 2005 to 2016 and secured over $330 million for infrastructure projects while introducing the university's first doctoral programs. Brian O. Hemphill held the position from 2016 to 2021, establishing the Radford University Carilion partnership for health sciences and investing in facilities like the $101 million Artis Center for Communication and Technology. The current president, Bret S. Danilowicz, assumed office in July 2022 and emphasizes institutional priorities centered on people, purpose, place, and pride to foster student success and community engagement.
Notable faculty at Radford University include recipients of the Virginia Outstanding Faculty Awards, the Commonwealth's highest honor for higher education instructors. For instance, Jeremy Wojdak, a professor of biology, received the award in 2020 for his innovative teaching and research in ecology and disease dynamics. In history, Sharon Roger Hepburn, department chair and professor, was named a Dalton Eminent Scholar in 2022 and earned the Modern Language Association's Book Prize in 2024 for her work on Japanese American internment during World War II. Earlier honorees include Grace Toney Edwards, who received the Virginia award in 1990 for her contributions to Appalachian studies and folklore through teaching and scholarship. These faculty exemplify Radford's emphasis on teaching awards and disciplinary expertise.
Among administrators, Charlene Curtis (class of 1976) stands out as a pioneering figure in athletics. As head women's basketball coach from 1984 to 1990, she compiled a 121-53 record and became the first African American woman to lead a Division I women's basketball program at Radford; she later served in leadership roles, including on the university's Board of Visitors, and advanced diversity initiatives across collegiate sports until her passing in 2022.
Radford's faculty reflects a commitment to diversity, with approximately 87% identifying as White, 5% Black or African American, 3% Asian, and smaller percentages from multi-ethnic and other groups, supporting an inclusive academic environment (as of 2023). Faculty recognition extends to university-specific honors, such as the Alumni Association's new 2025 awards for outstanding faculty and staff contributions to alumni outreach. Additionally, in 2025, Radford launched five new donor societies, including the Society of 1910, to honor philanthropic leaders among faculty, staff, and supporters who advance the university's mission through sustained giving.
