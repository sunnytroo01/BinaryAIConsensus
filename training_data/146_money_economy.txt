Money is one of humanity's most important inventions, yet most people who use it every day have only a vague understanding of what it actually is. Money is not wealth. Wealth is the goods and services that improve human life: food, shelter, clothing, healthcare, education, transportation, entertainment. Money is a tool for measuring, storing, and exchanging wealth. It is a language of value, a shared agreement that allows strangers to trade with each other efficiently even when they have never met and may never meet again.

Before money, trade was conducted through barter: the direct exchange of goods and services. I give you a chicken, you give me a basket of wheat. Barter works in small, close-knit communities but fails at scale because it requires a double coincidence of wants, meaning both parties must have exactly what the other wants at exactly the same time. If you have chickens and want wheat, you need to find someone who has wheat and wants chickens. If the wheat farmer wants pottery instead of chickens, the trade cannot happen. Money solves this problem by providing a universal medium of exchange that everyone accepts.

The first forms of money were commodity money: objects that had inherent value and were widely accepted as payment. Cattle, grain, salt, shells, beads, and precious metals have all served as money in various cultures and times. Gold and silver became the dominant forms of commodity money because they are durable, divisible, portable, scarce, and universally desired. Coins made of gold and silver, stamped with the seal of a king or government to guarantee their weight and purity, were first minted in Lydia, in present-day Turkey, around 600 BC.

Paper money was invented in China during the Tang Dynasty, around the seventh century AD, and became widespread during the Song Dynasty. Paper money is not valuable in itself. It is valuable because the government that issues it guarantees that it can be exchanged for goods and services and used to pay taxes. This is fiat money: money that has value because a government declares it has value and because people trust that declaration. All modern currencies are fiat money. The United States dollar has not been backed by gold since 1971, when President Nixon ended the gold standard.

Banks create money through a process called fractional reserve banking. When you deposit one hundred dollars in a bank, the bank does not simply store your money in a vault. It keeps a fraction, say ten dollars, as a reserve and lends the remaining ninety dollars to someone else. That borrower might deposit the ninety dollars in another bank, which keeps nine dollars as a reserve and lends eighty-one dollars. This process repeats, and through this chain of deposits and loans, the original one hundred dollars can create approximately one thousand dollars of money in the economy. This money creation process is how the money supply grows in a modern economy, and it is regulated by central banks that set reserve requirements and interest rates.

Inflation occurs when the general level of prices in an economy rises over time, reducing the purchasing power of money. If a loaf of bread cost one dollar last year and costs one dollar and three cents this year, inflation is three percent. Moderate inflation, around two to three percent per year, is considered healthy because it encourages spending and investment. High inflation, above ten percent, is destructive because it erodes savings, creates uncertainty, and can trigger a wage-price spiral in which workers demand higher wages to keep up with rising prices, which forces businesses to raise prices further, which triggers more wage demands.

Deflation, a sustained decrease in the general price level, might seem like a good thing because your money buys more over time, but it is actually dangerous for an economy. If consumers expect prices to fall, they delay purchases, which reduces demand, which causes businesses to cut production and lay off workers, which reduces income, which further reduces demand, creating a downward spiral that can be very difficult to escape. The Great Depression of the 1930s was exacerbated by severe deflation.

Interest rates are the price of borrowing money. When you take out a mortgage, you pay interest to the bank for the privilege of using its money to buy a house. When a bank deposits money at the central bank, it earns interest. Central banks use interest rates as their primary tool for managing the economy. When the economy is growing too slowly, the central bank lowers interest rates, making borrowing cheaper and encouraging spending and investment. When the economy is growing too fast and inflation is rising, the central bank raises interest rates, making borrowing more expensive and cooling demand.

The stock market is a system for buying and selling ownership shares in companies. When a company sells shares to the public for the first time in an initial public offering, it raises capital that it can use to grow the business. After the IPO, shares trade on exchanges like the New York Stock Exchange, where their prices fluctuate based on supply and demand, which in turn reflect investors' expectations about the company's future profits. Stock prices rise when investors believe a company will become more profitable and fall when they believe it will become less profitable.

The global economy is interconnected through trade, investment, and financial markets. A financial crisis in one country can spread rapidly to others through these connections, as the 2008 global financial crisis demonstrated. The crisis began in the United States housing market but quickly spread to financial institutions around the world that had invested in mortgage-backed securities, triggering a global recession that was the most severe economic downturn since the Great Depression.

Understanding money and economics is essential for making good personal financial decisions: budgeting, saving, investing, managing debt, and planning for retirement. It is equally essential for understanding the political and social issues that shape the world: taxation, government spending, trade policy, inequality, poverty, and the debate over the proper role of government in the economy. Money is not everything, but understanding how it works is the foundation of financial literacy, and financial literacy is the foundation of economic security and independence.
