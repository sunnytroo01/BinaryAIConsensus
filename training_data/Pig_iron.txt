Pig iron
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Pig iron is a high-carbon intermediate product of iron smelting, obtained by reducing iron ore in a blast furnace, and is characterized by its brittle nature due to a carbon content typically ranging from 3 to 4.5%, along with impurities such as 0.5-4% silicon, 0.15-2.5% manganese, up to 0.2% sulfur, and 0.025-2.5% phosphorus.
In the production process, iron oreprimarily hematite or magnetiteis charged into a blast furnace along with coke as the reducing agent and fuel, and limestone as a flux to remove impurities as slag; preheated air is blasted through tuyeres to reach temperatures sufficient to melt the reduced iron, which is then tapped as molten metal and cast into molds. The name "pig iron" derives from the historical practice of casting the molten iron into sand molds arranged in a main channel (the "sow") with branching depressions (the "pigs") resembling a sow nursing her piglets.
Due to its high carbon content and brittleness, pig iron is unsuitable for direct structural use but serves as the primary feedstock for steel production, where it is refined in basic oxygen converters or electric arc furnaces to lower carbon levels and add alloying elements like manganese and chromium. Portions of pig iron are also used in foundries to produce cast iron products, such as pipes and engine blocks, by adjusting its composition for improved castability. Globally, pig iron production is integral to the iron and steel industry, with blast furnaces operating continuously for campaigns typically lasting 15 to 20 years to supply hot metal for downstream processing.

Definition and Etymology

Definition

Pig iron is a high-carbon iron alloy produced by smelting iron ore, primarily hematite (FeO) or magnetite (FeO), in a blast furnace using coke as the reducing agent and limestone as a flux to remove impurities. This process yields a molten material that is cast into ingots, forming the basis for further metallurgical refinement.
The alloy typically contains 3.5% to 4.5% carbon by weight, along with other elements such as silicon, manganese, sulfur, and phosphorus, which contribute to its characteristic properties. This elevated carbon content results in a brittle microstructure dominated by cementite (FeC), making pig iron unsuitable for direct structural or fabrication applications without additional processing.
Unlike finished iron products, pig iron occupies an intermediate status in metallurgy, serving as the primary precursor for producing steel (with carbon reduced below 2%) and cast iron (through remelting and alloying). It contrasts sharply with wrought iron, which is nearly pure iron with minimal carbon and fibrous slag inclusions for malleability, or low-carbon steels designed for ductility and strength.

Etymology

The term "pig iron" originates from the distinctive shape of the sand molds used in its traditional casting process. Molten iron from the blast furnace was poured into a central runner channel, known as the "sow," which fed into multiple branching molds arranged perpendicularly, resembling a sow nursing a litter of piglets; the individual ingots formed in these branches were thus called "pigs." This nomenclature arose in the context of early modern ironworking practices in England, where the visual analogy to animal husbandry was applied to the solidified metal forms.
The earliest documented use of the term "pig iron" appears in English in 1665, in the treatise Metallum Martis by ironmaster Dud Dudley, who described innovations in iron production using coal rather than charcoal. Prior to this, the material was commonly referred to as "crude iron" in metallurgical texts, emphasizing its unrefined state directly from the furnace, while "cast iron" served as a broader descriptor for the poured product in foundry operations. In early European foundries, regional variations included terms like "raw iron" in German-speaking areas (e.g., Roheisen) and "fer brut" in French contexts, reflecting similar notions of its primitive, unprocessed nature.
The influence of mold shapes on the terminology persisted through the 18th and 19th centuries, with "pig beds"shallow sand trenches arranged in the sow-and-piglets configurationremaining standard in many ironworks for cooling and solidifying the metal into transportable bars. These pig beds, often laid out in open yards near furnaces, allowed workers to break off the pigs for reheating and further processing, reinforcing the porcine metaphor in trade and technical literature across Britain and continental Europe.

History

Ancient and Medieval Origins

The earliest evidence of iron smelting dates to around 2000 BCE in Anatolia, where bloomery processes were developed to extract iron from ore, producing spongy blooms of low-carbon iron that served as precursors to later high-carbon forms like pig iron. These rudimentary furnaces, fueled by charcoal and operated with bellows, yielded wrought iron blooms that required further forging to remove impurities, marking the initial transition from bronze to iron in the region.
The production of true cast iron, akin to pig iron, first emerged in China as early as the 8th century BCE, with significant development during the Warring States period (5th3rd centuries BCE), enabling the casting of molten high-carbon iron into molds for weapons, tools, and agricultural implements. This innovation relied on advanced blast furnaces that achieved temperatures sufficient to fully liquefy the iron, resulting in pig iron-like products with carbon contents typically exceeding 2%, which were then annealed or refined for practical use. By the Han Dynasty (206 BCE220 CE), these techniques had evolved with larger, more efficient furnaces at sites like Xuxiebian in Sichuan Province, where pig iron ingots were systematically produced and converted to wrought iron, supporting widespread applications in warfare and infrastructure.
In Europe, ironworking spread from Anatolian origins through Greek and Roman influences, relying predominantly on bloomery furnaces that occasionally yielded high-carbon iron but lacked the systematic casting of pig iron seen in China. Roman production focused on wrought iron for tools and armaments, with high-phosphorus or high-carbon variants emerging sporadically in medieval bloomeries, yet true pig iron casting remained rare until the 12th century, as evidenced by the Lapphyttan site in Sweden, Europe's earliest known blast furnace. Adoption in Europe was limited and regionally varied until the 14th century, constrained by technological and fuel limitations compared to Asian advancements.

Industrial Revolution Advancements

The advent of the Industrial Revolution marked a pivotal shift in pig iron production, driven by innovations that transitioned from labor-intensive, charcoal-based methods to more efficient, scalable processes. In 1709, Abraham Darby I successfully adapted the blast furnace at Coalbrookdale, England, to use cokea byproduct of coal distillationinstead of charcoal for smelting iron ore, enabling the continuous production of pig iron on a commercial scale. This breakthrough addressed the limitations of charcoal, which was scarce and costly due to extensive woodland depletion, and laid the foundation for higher-volume output suitable for casting into goods like pots and kettles. Darby's method not only reduced fuel dependency on forests but also produced pig iron with properties amenable to further industrial applications, accelerating the mechanization of ironworks.
Complementing these advancements were refinements in refining pig iron into wrought iron, which expanded its utility beyond casting. The finery forge process, an earlier technique involving reheating pig iron in a charcoal-fired hearth to remove excess carbon, had been the primary method for conversion but was inefficient and tied to charcoal supplies. In 1784, Henry Cort patented an improved puddling process using a reverberatory furnace fueled by coke, where pig iron was stirred in an oxidizing atmosphere to produce malleable wrought iron without direct fuel contact, superseding the finery method and enabling large-scale production. This innovation, often combined with rolling mills for shaping, dramatically increased output efficiency and reduced costs, transforming pig iron from a brittle intermediate into a versatile material for structural uses.
These technological shifts fueled the British Industrial Revolution's economic expansion, powering the growth of factories, machinery, and infrastructure like railways. Pig iron production in Britain surged from approximately 70,000 tons in 1788 to over 2 million tons by 1850, supporting the rapid industrialization of sectors such as textiles and transportation. By 1870, output had reached nearly 6 million tons, an eightfold increase from 1830 levels, underscoring the scale of this transformation and Britain's dominance in global iron markets. However, early reliance on charcoal for pig iron smelting contributed to significant environmental degradation, with an estimated 5 to 6 million acres of U.S. forests cleared in the 1800s alone for fuel production, exacerbating deforestation before coke's widespread adoption.
Regionally, these advancements spread to the United States, particularly in Pennsylvania, where abundant iron ore, coal, and forests facilitated adoption. By the late 18th century, furnaces like Hopewell in Berks County began producing pig iron using charcoal blast methods from 1771, evolving to incorporate coke and anthracite innovations that mirrored British progress and supported emerging American industries. Pennsylvania's Juniata Valley became a hub, with pioneer works producing high-quality pig iron that supplied coastal manufacturers and contributed to the U.S. industrial buildup, though initial charcoal use intensified local deforestation pressures.

Production

Blast Furnace Process

The blast furnace process is the dominant industrial method for producing pig iron through the smelting and reduction of iron ore. It involves a countercurrent flow of descending solid charge and ascending hot gases in a vertical shaft furnace, where iron oxides are reduced to metallic iron using carbon from coke.
The process begins with charging the furnace from the top with alternating layers of iron ore, coke, and limestone. Iron ore, primarily hematite (FeO) in the form of pellets, sinter, or lumps, supplies the iron content; coke provides carbon for reduction and combustion; and limestone (CaCO) serves as a flux to bind impurities. The charge descends through the furnace while preheated gases rise, facilitating progressive heating and reactions.
Preheated air, known as the hot blast and reaching temperatures up to 1200C, is injected horizontally through tuyereswater-cooled nozzlespositioned around the lower part of the furnace. This blast combusts the coke near the tuyeres, generating heat and carbon dioxide, which then reacts further to form carbon monoxide, the primary reducing agent.
The blast furnace structure is a refractory-lined steel shell typically up to 40 m in height, divided into distinct zones: the stack (upper section) for preheating and initial reduction; the bosh (converging middle section) for intense combustion and reduction; and the hearth (lower conical base) where molten pig iron accumulates. Tuyeres encircle the upper hearth or lower bosh, enabling the air injection that sustains the process.
As the charge descends, key reduction reactions occur in the upper and middle zones, primarily mediated by carbon monoxide:

This indirect reduction converts iron oxides to metallic iron, with direct reduction by carbon also contributing at higher temperatures. Simultaneously, slag forms from the flux and impurities: limestone decomposes to lime (CaO), which combines with silica (SiO) and other gangue to produce molten slag, such as calcium silicate (CaSiO), separating from the denser iron.
Coke serves dual roles as the primary fuel, providing the exothermic energy for melting through combustion (C + O CO), and as the reductant via the generated CO, with typical consumption around 450 kg per tonne of pig iron produced. In modern blast furnaces, pulverized coal injection (PCI) or other reductants can reduce coke consumption to 300400 kg per tonne.
In modern operations, large blast furnaces achieve typical outputs of 4,00010,000 metric tons of molten pig iron per day, tapped periodically from the hearth.

Casting and Refining Steps

Once the smelting process in the blast furnace is complete, the molten pig iron, at a temperature of 14201480C, is tapped from the furnace hearth through a taphole into refractory-lined ladles for transfer to the casting area. This tapping occurs periodically, typically every 26 hours (612 times per day) in modern blast furnaces depending on furnace size and production rate, with the molten iron flowing at a velocity of about 5 m/s through a 70 mm diameter hole.
In traditional casting, the ladle of molten iron is positioned over a prepared pig bed on the cast house floor, where the metal is poured into a network of sand molds. The pig bed layout features a central main channel, known as the sow, from which branching side channels extend to form individual pig molds, resembling a sow nursing her pigletshence the nomenclature. These molds, typically made of compacted sand, shape the iron into ingots or "pigs" weighing 1050 kg each, allowing for efficient solidification and easy separation once cooled. In modern operations, pig casting machines (PCMs) have largely replaced manual pig beds; these conveyor-based systems pour the molten iron into a series of moving molds, producing uniform pigs of 1045 kg that are air-cooled to a plastic state and then quenched with water sprays for rapid solidification.
Solidification in sand molds requires several hours of cooling, often 38 hours, to ensure the pigs fully harden without defects, as the sand provides gradual heat extraction to prevent cracking. During this time, the pigs contract slightly as they cool from liquid to solid form, and workers break the solidified runners to collect the ingots.
If the sulfur content exceeds specifications for downstream processing, initial desulfurization may be performed on the molten pig iron prior to or during casting by injecting reagents such as calcium carbide-based mixtures into the ladle, reducing sulfur levels through slag formation and gas evolution. This step is optional and depends on the iron's intended use, but it enhances compatibility with steelmaking. Degassing, if required, involves similar ladle treatments to remove dissolved gases.
The solidified pigs are then stockpiled and transported to steel mills, often by rail cars or ships for long distances, while hot metal can be moved in insulated torpedo ladles (holding 90150 tons) via overhead cranes or rail-mounted transfer cars to minimize reheating needs.
Throughout the process, slaga byproduct floating on the molten ironis skimmed off during tapping using gates or runners and separated into dedicated channels. This blast furnace slag is quenched and granulated for reuse in construction applications, such as cement production, road bases, and aggregates, promoting resource efficiency and reducing landfill use.

Composition and Properties

Chemical Composition

Pig iron is an iron-carbon alloy with iron comprising the majority of its composition, typically ranging from 92% to 94% by weight. The primary alloying element is carbon, which constitutes 3.5% to 4.5% and originates from the coke used in the blast furnace reduction process. Silicon, present at 0.5% to 3%, derives from the silica in the iron ore and slag, while manganese accounts for 0.5% to 1%, also introduced via the ore.
Impurities in pig iron include phosphorus, which can reach up to 1.5% and contributes to material brittleness, and sulfur at levels of 0.02% to 0.15%. Trace elements such as aluminum and titanium are also present in minor amounts, typically below 0.1% each, stemming from the raw materials and furnace conditions.
The chemical composition varies depending on the type of pig iron and the source ore. Basic pig iron, intended for steelmaking, features low phosphorus (less than 0.12%) and silicon (under 1.5%) to facilitate further refining. In contrast, foundry pig iron has higher silicon content (1.5% to 3.5%) to enhance castability in iron castings.
Industry standards, such as those outlined in ASTM specifications for merchant pig iron (e.g., No. 1 Foundry Pig Iron), define acceptable ranges for these elements to ensure quality and suitability for downstream applications.

Physical and Mechanical Properties

Pig iron exhibits a density ranging from 7.1 to 7.4 g/cm, which is slightly lower than that of pure iron due to its high carbon content and impurities. Its melting point is between 1150C and 1200C, significantly reduced compared to pure iron's 1538C primarily because of the dissolved carbon that depresses the melting temperature. Visually, pig iron appears as a grayish, brittle solid with a rough, uneven surface resulting from rapid cooling during casting into pig shapes.
Mechanically, pig iron is characterized by high hardness, typically in the Brinell range of 200 to 300, making it resistant to wear but also highly brittle. It demonstrates poor ductility, with elongation under tensile stress less than 1%, leading to sudden fracture without significant plastic deformation. This brittleness arises from its eutectic microstructure, consisting primarily of pearlite (a lamellar mixture of ferrite and cementite) and free cementite (FeC), where the hard, brittle cementite phases dominate and inhibit dislocation movement.
Thermally, pig iron has a high specific heat capacity of approximately 0.54 kJ/kgK, allowing it to absorb substantial heat before temperature rise. This, combined with its low melting point, contributes to excellent castability, enabling it to flow readily into molds despite its brittleness in the solid state.

Uses and Applications

Steelmaking Processes

Pig iron, characterized by its high carbon content of 3.54.5%, is the principal raw material for converting into steel through decarburization and impurity removal. These processes lower the carbon level to 0.051.5%, yielding steel suitable for diverse applications while generating byproducts like slag.
The Bessemer process, pioneered in the 1850s by Henry Bessemer, marked the advent of large-scale steel production from molten pig iron. In this method, air is blown through the molten pig iron in a pear-shaped converter, oxidizing excess carbon and other elements like silicon and manganese into gases and slag. The process was revolutionary for its speed and low cost, enabling the mass production of steel and fueling the Industrial Revolution's infrastructure boom. However, it became obsolete by the early 20th century due to difficulties in dephosphorization and inconsistent steel quality, paving the way for more advanced techniques.
The Basic Oxygen Process (BOP), developed in the mid-20th century, now dominates integrated steelmaking. It involves charging a converter with molten pig iron (typically 7090% of the charge) and scrap steel, then lancing high-purity oxygen downward to burn off carbon as carbon monoxide and dioxide. This oxidation, combined with lime additions to form basic slag, removes impurities like phosphorus and sulfur, reducing carbon content to steel-grade levels in about 2040 minutes. BOP's efficiency stems from its exothermic reactions, which minimize external energy needs and achieve high productivity, accounting for roughly 70% of global steel output.
In parallel, the Electric Arc Furnace (EAF) process remelts pig iron with scrap in a refractory-lined vessel using electric arcs from graphite electrodes. The arcs generate temperatures up to 1,800C, melting the charge and enabling alloying with elements like chromium or nickel for specialized steels. While EAF primarily relies on scrap (often 75100% of input), incorporating pig iron enhances melt fluidity and composition control, particularly in mini-mills. This method offers flexibility for recycling and lower emissions compared to integrated routes, comprising about 30% of worldwide steel production.
Across these steelmaking routes, metal yields from pig iron typically exceed 90%, reflecting efficient conversion with minimal losses to slag or gases. Byproducts, notably basic oxygen or electric arc slag (produced at 100150 kg per ton of steel), are valorized in cement manufacturing as a hydraulic binder, substituting clinker and reducing CO emissions.

Cast Iron and Other Uses

Pig iron serves as a primary raw material in foundries, where it is remelted in electric induction or cupola furnaces to produce molten metal for casting various types of iron alloys. This process typically involves blending pig iron with steel scrap and returns at varying proportions, typically 20-75% of the charge depending on the desired composition and to control impurities like sulfur and phosphorus. The resulting melts are used to create gray cast iron, characterized by flake graphite for improved machinability and thermal conductivity; ductile (nodular) cast iron, with spheroidal graphite for enhanced ductility and strength; and white cast iron, featuring cementite for superior hardness and wear resistance.
These cast irons find extensive applications in structural and functional components. Gray cast iron, often derived from pig iron melts, is commonly used for water pipes, manhole covers, and machine bases due to its excellent fluidity and ability to dampen vibrations. Ductile cast iron components, produced by treating pig iron-based melts with nodulizers like magnesium, are employed in engine blocks, crankshafts, and gears for their high tensile strength (up to 400 MPa) and impact resistance. White cast iron, achieved through rapid cooling of pig iron remelts, is applied in abrasion-resistant parts such as grinding balls and liners.
During remelting, alloying additions are made to tailor the microstructure. Silicon, typically added as ferrosilicon (FeSi75) at 1-3% of the charge, promotes graphitization by stabilizing graphite formation and preventing carbide precipitation, which is essential for the ductility and machinability of gray and ductile irons. In ductile iron production, silicon content is adjusted to 2.0-2.8% to enhance spheroid growth and overall mechanical properties without excessive brittleness. Ornamental ironwork, such as railings and decorative fittings, continues to utilize pig iron-derived cast irons for their aesthetic surface finishes and corrosion resistance when alloyed appropriately.
Beyond primary casting, pig iron has minor but specialized applications. It acts as a raw material in ferroalloys production, where it is smelted in oxygen reactors with coke and coal to yield alloys like ferromanganese and ferrochrome, reducing energy consumption by up to 70% compared to traditional methods. Granulated pig iron is also processed into steel shot and grit, serving as metallic abrasives for sandblasting, peening, and surface preparation in manufacturing. In modern foundries, recycling rates for iron scrap and returns, including pig iron remnants, exceed 80%, with overall metal recovery approaching 90% through efficient melting and slag management practices.
Emerging uses include niche roles in advanced manufacturing and heavy equipment. Additionally, remelted pig iron forms cast iron counterweights and ballast blocks for heavy machinery, such as excavators and cranes, providing the necessary density (up to 7.2 g/cm) for stability during load handling. The inherent brittleness of pig iron limits its direct structural use, necessitating remelting for these applications.
