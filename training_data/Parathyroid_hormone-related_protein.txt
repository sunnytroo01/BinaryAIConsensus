Parathyroid hormone-related protein
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP) is a multifunctional protein hormone encoded by the PTHLH gene on chromosome 12, consisting of 139 to 173 amino acids depending on isoform, and sharing significant N-terminal sequence homology with parathyroid hormone (PTH), particularly in the first eight of the initial 13 amino acids that confer receptor-binding activity. Discovered in 1987 as the primary mediator of humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy (HHM) in patients with certain cancers, PTHrP is produced by a wide array of normal and neoplastic tissues, where it undergoes extensive post-translational processing, including alternative splicing, proteolytic cleavage, and glycosylation, to generate diverse bioactive fragments.
Unlike PTH, which functions as a systemic endocrine hormone primarily secreted by the parathyroid glands to maintain serum calcium levels, PTHrP predominantly acts in paracrine and autocrine fashions to influence local cellular processes across multiple organ systems. It binds to the same G-protein-coupled receptor (PTH1R) as PTH, activating signaling pathways such as adenylate cyclase and protein kinase C to exert effects on bone resorption, renal calcium reabsorption, and phosphate excretion, though its physiological roles extend far beyond calcium homeostasis. In normal physiology, PTHrP is essential for fetal skeletal development by regulating chondrocyte differentiation and endochondral bone formation, facilitates transplacental calcium transport during gestation, and supports mammary gland development and lactation by promoting epithelial calcium secretion. Additionally, it modulates smooth muscle tone in vascular, uterine, and gastrointestinal tissues, contributes to skin and hair follicle cycling, and influences tooth eruption and neuronal survival.
In pathological contexts, PTHrP overexpression by tumors, particularly squamous cell carcinomas and breast cancers, drives HHM in up to 80% of solid tumor-associated hypercalcemia cases by mimicking PTH's hypercalcemic actions on bone and kidney, independent of skeletal metastases. Beyond malignancy, dysregulated PTHrP signaling is implicated in conditions such as osteoporosis, where it may inhibit bone resorption via C-terminal fragments like osteostatin (amino acids 107111), and in cardiovascular diseases through its effects on vascular smooth muscle proliferation. Recent research highlights PTHrP's intracrine roles, with nuclear localization signals enabling direct gene regulation within target cells, underscoring its versatility as both an extracellular signaling molecule and an intracellular modulator of growth and survival.

Structure and Genetics

Protein Structure

Parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP) is initially synthesized as a preprohormone precursor, consisting of a signal peptide (residues 136), a prohormone sequence (residues 3742), and a mature protein that varies in length due to alternative splicing of the PTHLH gene, producing three principal isoforms of 139, 141, or 173 amino acids. The precursor undergoes post-translational processing, including cleavage of the signal peptide in the endoplasmic reticulum to form pro-PTHrP, followed by further proteolytic maturation in the Golgi and secretory vesicles to generate the active secreted forms and intracellular fragments. This processing yields a family of bioactive peptides from distinct regions of the protein, enabling diverse functions through both extracellular secretion and intracellular retention.
The amino acid sequence of mature PTHrP exhibits significant N-terminal homology to parathyroid hormone (PTH), particularly in the first 13 residues, where eight amino acids are identical, allowing PTHrP to bind and activate the shared PTH1 receptor (PTH1R). This homology is characterized by 8 identical amino acids in the first 13 residues, with overall sequence identity of approximately 32% in the first 34 residues, conferring structural similarity that supports receptor recognition and signaling. Beyond residue 13, the sequences diverge substantially, with PTHrP possessing unique extensions that distinguish its broader biological roles from those of PTH.
PTHrP features three principal structural domains: the N-terminal domain (residues 136), which encompasses the PTH-like signaling region responsible for receptor activation; the mid-region (approximately residues 38111), associated with developmental signaling; and the C-terminal domain (residues 112139/141/173, varying by isoform), which includes sequences for nuclear targeting and anti-apoptotic activities. The mid-region contains a bipartite nuclear localization signal (NLS) spanning residues 88107, characterized by a polybasic motif (KRKK at 9497) that facilitates importin-mediated nuclear translocation. This NLS overlaps with functional epitopes in the mid- and C-terminal regions, enabling PTHrP fragments to exert intracrine effects within the nucleus.
Key residues within the N-terminal domain are critical for PTH1R activation, including histidine at position 5, which influences receptor selectivity and signaling duration compared to PTH, and hydrophobic residues such as leucine 24 and phenylalanine 23 in the core-binding helix (residues 1731), which stabilize ligand-receptor interactions. Additionally, the mid- and C-terminal domains harbor motifs implicated in regulatory interactions, including potential calcium-modulating sequences like the osteostatin region (residues 107111 or 109141 in longer isoforms), which features serine and tyrosine residues that may contribute to calcium-related binding affinities in cellular contexts.

Gene and Expression

The PTHLH gene, which encodes parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP), is located on the short arm of human chromosome 12 at position 12p11.22 and spans approximately 15 kb of genomic DNA. This gene consists of seven exons, with the first two primarily encoding untranslated regions and alternative promoters, while the subsequent exons contribute to the coding sequence and 3'-untranslated regions. The genomic organization supports complex post-transcriptional processing, enabling the production of multiple mRNA transcripts.
Alternative splicing of PTHLH pre-mRNA generates three principal isoforms: PTHrP(1-141), PTHrP(1-173), and PTHrP(1-139), each differing in their C-terminal sequences due to the inclusion or exclusion of specific exons. These isoforms arise from the utilization of distinct polyadenylation sites and splicing patterns, particularly involving exons 4 through 6, which encode variable C-terminal domains. This mechanism allows for tissue-specific expression of isoforms with potentially divergent functions, though the core N-terminal signaling domain remains conserved across variants.
Transcriptional regulation of the PTHLH gene is mediated by multiple factors binding to promoter regions, including the AP-1 transcription factor complex, which activates expression in response to growth signals in various cell types. Similarly, NF-B pathways enhance PTHLH transcription, particularly in inflammatory or oncogenic contexts such as adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma cells. Hormonal regulators also play key roles; for instance, estrogen modulates PTHLH expression through estrogen receptor-mediated mechanisms in pituitary and breast cells, often exerting inhibitory effects. Vitamin D, via its receptor and interaction with elements like Ku antigen, typically represses PTHLH transcription, contributing to feedback control in calcium homeostasis.
PTHLH exhibits ubiquitous expression across tissues, with particularly high levels in skin and hair follicles, where it supports developmental processes. Elevated expression is also observed in lactating mammary glands, aiding epithelial maintenance and milk production. In fetal tissues, PTHLH is widely distributed, including in skeletal, renal, and placental structures, reflecting its roles in organogenesis. In contrast, circulating PTHrP levels in adult blood remain low under normal conditions, typically below detectable thresholds in healthy individuals.

Physiological Functions

Calcium Homeostasis and Bone Regulation

Parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP) plays a critical physiological role in maintaining calcium homeostasis through both systemic and local actions, primarily by binding to the same G protein-coupled receptor as PTH, the type 1 parathyroid hormone receptor (PTH1R), to enable similar signaling effects. Unlike circulating PTH, PTHrP predominantly acts in a paracrine manner within tissues such as bone and kidney, fine-tuning calcium levels without inducing sustained hypercalcemia. This localized regulation supports bone remodeling and mineral ion balance, ensuring adequate calcium availability for skeletal integrity and other essential functions.
In bone, PTHrP exerts paracrine effects that balance resorption and formation to regulate local calcium dynamics. Produced by osteoblasts and other bone cells, PTHrP stimulates osteoclast activity indirectly by upregulating receptor activator of nuclear factor kappa-B ligand (RANKL) expression in osteoblasts, which promotes osteoclast differentiation and bone resorption to release calcium stores. Concurrently, PTHrP exhibits anabolic properties by inhibiting osteoblast apoptosis and enhancing osteoblast recruitment and differentiation, thereby supporting bone formation and preventing excessive resorption. These dual actions maintain bone mass and contribute to calcium mobilization during physiological demands, as evidenced in osteoblast-specific PTHrP overexpression models that demonstrate increased bone formation without net loss.
PTHrP also influences renal calcium handling, mimicking PTH to promote systemic calcium conservation. In the distal tubules of the kidney, PTHrP increases calcium reabsorption through activation of PTH1R, enhancing transepithelial calcium transport via channels like TRPV5. Additionally, it inhibits phosphate reabsorption in the proximal tubule, leading to phosphaturia and preventing ectopic calcification, which helps sustain serum calcium-phosphate balance. These renal effects are supported by studies showing that PTHrP infusion elevates urinary cyclic AMP and alters ion excretion patterns similarly to PTH, underscoring its role in fine-tuning mineral homeostasis.
Locally within the growth plate, PTHrP regulates chondrocyte function to support endochondral ossification and mineralization without systemic calcium perturbations. Expressed by perichondrial cells and chondrocytes in the resting and proliferative zones, PTHrP delays terminal differentiation and promotes proliferation in the proliferative zone, ensuring coordinated cartilage maturation and subsequent bone deposition. This action facilitates calcium incorporation into the mineralizing matrix, as disruptions in PTHrP signaling in murine models lead to accelerated chondrocyte hypertrophy and impaired skeletal growth.
In the fetal context, PTHrP contributes to calcium homeostasis by facilitating transplacental transport and modulating bone resorption to prioritize skeletal mineralization. The mid-region fragment of PTHrP, secreted by the fetus, stimulates active calcium uptake across the placenta, elevating fetal serum calcium levels essential for bone development. Furthermore, fetal PTHrP inhibits excessive bone resorption, preserving maternal-fetal calcium gradients; in PTHrP-deficient mice, this results in reduced placental calcium transfer and hypocalcemia, highlighting its protective role against fetal skeletal demineralization.

Developmental and Tissue-Specific Roles

Parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP) plays diverse paracrine roles in embryonic development and adult tissue maintenance, distinct from its systemic effects on calcium homeostasis. Expressed in numerous fetal tissues, PTHrP influences cell proliferation, differentiation, and tissue remodeling across multiple organ systems. In embryonic contexts, it coordinates growth processes such as chondrocyte maturation in skeletal elements and facilitates nutrient transfer across the placenta. These functions highlight PTHrP's importance as a local signaling molecule that supports organogenesis and specialized physiological adaptations in adulthood.
In tooth development, PTHrP is essential for eruption by regulating remodeling in the dental follicle and activating osteoclasts to resorb overlying bone and root tissues. Studies in PTHrP-null mice demonstrate that its absence leads to failed tooth eruption due to disrupted osteoclast formation and impaired pathway creation beneath the tooth. PTHrP acts via the PTH/PTHrP receptor (PTH1R) on dental mesenchymal progenitors, promoting their differentiation into supportive structures like cementum while ensuring proper root elongation. This localized signaling prevents ankylosis and maintains periodontal integrity during eruption.
Within mammary glands, PTHrP drives epithelial proliferation and secretory differentiation during pregnancy and lactation, supporting alveolar expansion and milk production. It activates STAT5 signaling in mammary epithelial cells, enhancing their growth and functional maturation without inducing dedifferentiation. PTHrP also facilitates calcium transport into milk by regulating transepithelial flux in the gland, contributing to neonatal nutrition while maintaining maternal homeostasis. Additionally, it inhibits adipocyte differentiation in the mammary stroma, preserving space for glandular tissue expansion during development.
PTHrP modulates hair follicle cycling by promoting entry into the anagen (growth) phase and influencing keratinocyte differentiation in the follicle matrix. Expressed in the outer root sheath and hair bulb, it stimulates proliferation of follicular keratinocytes, extending the duration of active hair growth. Antagonists of PTHrP accelerate the transition to catagen (regression), underscoring its role in maintaining cyclic progression; transgenic overexpression in mice disrupts normal follicle development, leading to altered cycling. These effects occur through PTH1R-mediated pathways that balance growth and regression signals in the dermal papilla.
During fetal development, PTHrP regulates chondrocyte differentiation in growth plates, delaying terminal maturation to allow proper endochondral ossification and skeletal elongation. In PTHrP-deficient models, chondrocytes prematurely undergo hypertrophic differentiation, resulting in shortened limbs and accelerated bone formation. It also enhances placental calcium transfer from mother to fetus, acting on trophoblast cells to increase active transport and support fetal mineralization independent of the classical PTH1R. Furthermore, PTHrP relaxes uterine smooth muscle, preventing premature contractions and aiding in gestation maintenance by modulating myometrial tone; similar paracrine actions relax vascular and gastrointestinal smooth muscle to regulate tone and motility.
Beyond these primary sites, PTHrP supports keratinocyte growth and differentiation in the skin epidermis, promoting barrier formation and wound healing through autocrine/paracrine loops. In the pancreas, it stimulates beta-cell proliferation, enhancing insulin secretion and islet mass expansion in response to physiological demands, as evidenced by increased replication in human beta-cells treated with PTHrP. In the central nervous system, PTHrP acts as a survival factor for neurons, protecting against excitotoxic damage through PTH1R-dependent and intracrine mechanisms that promote differentiation and inhibit apoptosis. These roles underscore PTHrP's broad contributions to tissue-specific adaptation and development.

Pathological Roles

Humoral Hypercalcemia of Malignancy

Humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy (HHM) is a paraneoplastic syndrome characterized by tumor secretion of parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP), leading to systemic hypercalcemia through PTH-like actions on bone and kidney. PTHrP, produced ectopically by various malignant cells, circulates at elevated levels and binds to the parathyroid hormone 1 receptor (PTH1R), mimicking the effects of parathyroid hormone (PTH) but in the absence of parathyroid gland involvement. This condition accounts for approximately 80% of all cases of hypercalcemia associated with malignancy, particularly in patients without evident bone metastases.
PTHrP secretion is most commonly observed in solid tumors such as squamous cell carcinomas (e.g., of the lung, head, neck, or esophagus), breast cancer, and renal cell carcinoma, though it can also occur in lymphomas and other malignancies. These tumors produce full-length or processed fragments of PTHrP that enter the bloodstream, elevating circulating levels and driving hypercalcemia. The mechanism involves PTHrP-mediated activation of PTH1R in target organs: in the kidney, it promotes phosphaturia by inhibiting phosphate reabsorption in the proximal tubule and enhances calcium reabsorption in the distal tubule, though the net effect often results in hypercalciuria due to the overwhelming filtered calcium load from hypercalcemia; in bone, it stimulates osteoblast production of RANKL, which activates osteoclasts to increase bone resorption and release calcium into the circulation.
Clinically, HHM presents with severe hypercalcemia, typically serum calcium levels exceeding 14 mg/dL (3.5 mmol/L), accompanied by symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, constipation, polyuria, dehydration, and altered mental status; parathyroid hormone levels are suppressed (<20 pg/mL), and renal impairment is common due to calcium-mediated nephrotoxicity. Diagnosis relies on measuring elevated circulating PTHrP levels, generally >2 pmol/L (with normal <4.2 pmol/L depending on the assay), alongside low PTH and evidence of malignancy. Treatment focuses on hydration, loop diuretics for calciuresis, and antiresorptive agents such as bisphosphonates (e.g., zoledronic acid) to inhibit osteoclast activity or denosumab to block RANKL; emerging PTHrP-targeted therapies, like monoclonal antibodies, are under investigation for refractory cases.

Involvement in Cancer Progression and Other Disorders

Parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP) exerts significant influence on cancer progression through autocrine and paracrine mechanisms that stimulate tumor cell proliferation. In breast cancer, PTHrP activates the PTH1R receptor to upregulate cyclin D1 and Ki67 expression, driving cell cycle progression and tumor expansion. Similarly, in prostate cancer, PTHrP promotes proliferation and survival by inducing IL-8 production, fostering an autocrine loop that enhances tumor growth. Nuclear localization of PTHrP further contributes to these effects; in breast cancer cells, it regulates G2/M phase enrichment, while in prostate cancer, full-length PTHrP (1-173) exacerbates bone lesion severity compared to truncated isoforms.
PTHrP also facilitates metastasis by enhancing tumor invasion, angiogenesis, and osteolytic bone destruction. It upregulates integrins such as 1, 5, 6, and 4 in breast and prostate cancer cells, promoting adhesion and migration essential for metastatic spread. Angiogenesis is stimulated through increased VEGF and IL-8 expression, supporting vascularization of metastatic sites. In bone metastases, PTHrP drives osteoclastogenesis via RANKL activation, leading to osteolytic lesions prevalent in approximately 70% of fatal breast cancers and nearly all poorly differentiated prostate tumors. The mid-region fragment (38-94) of PTHrP specifically aids epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) in breast cancer, facilitating invasion and dissemination.
Beyond cancer, PTHrP dysregulation contributes to various non-malignant disorders. In inactivating PTH/PTHrP signaling disorders (iPPSDs), defects in the PTH-PTHrP pathway mimic pseudohypoparathyroidism-like syndromes, resulting in end-organ resistance, hypocalcemia, and hyperphosphatemia due to impaired cAMP-mediated signaling. In fibrosis, PTHrP promotes hepatic fibrosis by activating stellate cells through the PTH1R/PKC/Hedgehog axis, increasing -SMA, collagen I, and TGF-1 expression. Conversely, in pulmonary contexts, PTHrP can inhibit lipofibroblast-to-myofibroblast transdifferentiation via PPAR upregulation, offering protection against certain fibrotic insults like nicotine-induced injury. PTHrP influences vascular tone and renal function, with dysregulation implicated in hypertension through modulation of inflammation and apoptosis in mesangial cells. In hair follicle physiology, PTH/PTHrP signaling induces premature catagen-telogen transition, and its dysregulation contributes to alopecia by disrupting the hair cycle.
Recent studies highlight PTHrP as a biomarker for poor prognosis in lung cancer, particularly in PTHrP-producing adenocarcinomas, where elevated levels correlate with aggressive disease and reduced survival. Preclinical models demonstrate therapeutic potential, with PTHrP-neutralizing antibodies inhibiting pancreatic cancer growth and metastasis in vivo. Genetic associations include PTHLH polymorphisms linked to increased breast cancer risk. Certain PTHLH variants also correlate with altered bone mineral density, influencing osteoporosis susceptibility in non-cancer contexts.

Discovery and Molecular Interactions

Historical Discovery

Parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP) was first identified in 1987 through efforts to elucidate the cause of humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy (HHM), a paraneoplastic syndrome common in certain cancers such as lung carcinomas. Researchers led by L.J. Suva purified the protein from the human lung cancer cell line BEN, which secretes factors inducing hypercalcemia, and performed peptide sequencing on its N-terminal and internal fragments to design oligonucleotide probes. This enabled the isolation of full-length cDNA clones, revealing a prepropeptide with a 36-amino-acid signal sequence followed by a mature 141-amino-acid protein exhibiting significant homology to parathyroid hormone (PTH), particularly in the first 13 residues critical for receptor binding. The cloned gene was expressed in mammalian cells, confirming its biological activity in stimulating cyclic AMP production and calcium mobilization, thus establishing PTHrP as the primary mediator of HHM.
In 1989, further genomic characterization of the PTHLH gene, encoding PTHrP, was achieved by isolating and sequencing clones from a human placental library, which demonstrated that the gene spans approximately 13 kilobases and contains seven exons with organizational similarity to the PTH gene. This work highlighted the evolutionary conservation and distinct identity of PTHrP as a separate entity from PTH, while underscoring its potential for regulated expression. Early studies in the 1990s confirmed the existence of multiple PTHrP isoforms arising from alternative splicing of the primary transcript, including variants encoding 139-, 141-, and 173-amino-acid mature proteins, which were detected in various tumor and normal cell types. Concurrently, investigations revealed widespread local expression of PTHrP mRNA and protein in non-malignant tissues, such as skin, lactating mammary glands, and fetal tissues, shifting focus from a tumor-specific factor to a paracrine regulator.
A pivotal milestone in the 1990s came from genetic studies elucidating PTHrP's developmental roles. In 1994, A.C. Karaplis and colleagues generated PTHrP knockout mice via targeted disruption of the gene in embryonic stem cells, resulting in homozygous null mutants that exhibited lethal chondrodysplasia characterized by accelerated chondrocyte differentiation, shortened limbs, and premature mineralization of the growth plate, leading to postnatal death from respiratory failure due to a hypoplastic rib cage. These findings demonstrated PTHrP's essential function in endochondral bone development and epithelial-mesenchymal interactions during embryogenesis. By the early 2000s, accumulating evidence from transgenic models and tissue-specific expression analyses solidified PTHrP's role as a ubiquitous cytokine-like regulator of cell growth, differentiation, and calcium homeostasis across multiple organ systems, far beyond its initial association with malignancy.

Receptor Binding and Signaling Pathways

Parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP) primarily exerts its effects through high-affinity binding to the parathyroid hormone type 1 receptor (PTH1R), a class B G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR). The N-terminal domain of PTHrP, particularly residues 1-34, engages PTH1R via a two-site binding mechanism: the C-terminal portion of this fragment interacts with the receptor's extracellular N-terminal domain, while the N-terminal residues bind to the transmembrane helices and extracellular loops. This binding activates multiple downstream signaling pathways, with PTHrP also showing low-affinity interaction with the PTH2 receptor (PTH2R), though this is less characterized and primarily responsive to PTH.
Upon binding to PTH1R, PTHrP triggers G protein-dependent and independent signaling cascades. The primary pathway involves Gs protein coupling, which stimulates adenylyl cyclase to increase cyclic AMP (cAMP) levels, activating protein kinase A (PKA) and influencing calcium and phosphate regulation. Additionally, Gq/11 coupling activates phospholipase C (PLC), leading to the production of inositol trisphosphate (IP3) and diacylglycerol (DAG), which elevate intracellular calcium and activate protein kinase C (PKC), promoting cell proliferation. Beta-arrestin recruitment facilitates receptor internalization and sustains mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK)/extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK) signaling, particularly important for developmental processes.
Beyond cell surface signaling, PTHrP exhibits intracrine actions through nuclear localization. The mid-region nuclear localization signal (NLS) and C-terminal fragments enable translocation to the nucleus, where PTHrP modulates gene expression by interacting with the retinoblastoma protein (pRb), promoting its dephosphorylation and inducing G1 cell cycle arrest. This nuclear activity upregulates genes such as Runx2 and osteocalcin, distinct from classical GPCR pathways.
In contrast to parathyroid hormone (PTH), which circulates systemically from the parathyroid glands, PTHrP acts predominantly as a paracrine/autocrine factor due to local secretion in diverse tissues, enabling broader physiological roles. Multiple PTHrP isoforms, generated by alternative splicing (e.g., 139, 141, 173 amino acids), further diversify its signaling compared to the single PTH isoform. PTHrP also interacts with integrins, such as 5, 6, and 4, upregulating their expression via intracrine mechanisms to enhance cell adhesion and survival. Through PTH1R signaling, it modulates sodium-phosphate cotransporters (e.g., NaPi-IIa) and participates in feedback loops with vitamin D and fibroblast growth factor 23 (FGF23), where PTHrP stimulates FGF23 production while FGF23 suppresses PTHrP-mediated phosphate reabsorption.
