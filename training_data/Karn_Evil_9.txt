Karn Evil 9
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Karn Evil 9 is a progressive rock suite composed by the English supergroup Emerson, Lake & Palmer, serving as the closing track on their fourth studio album, Brain Salad Surgery, released on November 19, 1973. Spanning nearly 30 minutes across four movementsknown as the 1st Impression (Parts 1 and 2), 2nd Impression, and 3rd Impressionit blends rock, classical, and electronic elements to depict a dystopian narrative of a futuristic amusement park where machines dominate humanity.
The music for Karn Evil 9 was primarily written by keyboardist Keith Emerson, drawing inspiration from the carnival-like sounds of his organ riffs, while the lyrics were crafted by bassist/vocalist Greg Lake and poet Peter Sinfield, who played on the title as a phonetic twist on "carnival nine." The suite's themes explore man-versus-machine conflict, influenced by science fiction motifs reminiscent of works by Kurt Vonnegut, and it was explicitly designed for live performance, featuring extended instrumental sections that showcased the band's virtuosity on keyboards, bass, and drums.
Brain Salad Surgery, produced by Lake and released on the band's own Manticore Records label, achieved commercial success, peaking at number 2 on the UK Albums Chart and number 11 on the US Billboard 200. Karn Evil 9 became one of Emerson, Lake & Palmer's signature pieces, frequently performed in concertmost notably at the 1974 California Jam festivaland has endured as a staple of classic rock radio, particularly the riff-driven "1st Impression, Part 2." Its narrative has also inspired adaptations, including a planned science-fiction film announced in 2020 based on the lyrics' dystopian vision.

Background and Development

Origins and Inspiration

"Karn Evil 9" emerged as a ambitious side-spanning suite on Emerson, Lake & Palmer's 1973 album Brain Salad Surgery, conceived as the band's response to their desire for a grand, unified epic that could be performed live, reflecting the progressive rock era's push toward theatricality and conceptual depth. The piece drew heavily from 1970s cultural motifs, including sci-fi dystopias like those in 2001: A Space Odyssey and Kurt Vonnegut's works, portraying a future where machines dominate humanity in a surreal, exploitative spectacle. Conceptualization began in early 1973 when the band converted an abandoned cinema in Fulham, London, into a rehearsal space to develop material playable in concert halls.
Keith Emerson, the band's keyboardist, shaped the suite's musical foundation, infusing it with classical influences from composers like Johann Sebastian Bach and Leonard Bernstein, adapted through his rock organ traditions to create intricate counterpoint and thematic development. His pioneering use of the Moog modular synthesizer was pivotal, with custom modifications including sequencers and filters generating the accelerating electronic sequences that defined the futuristic sound, particularly in the suite's climactic sections. Emerson initially envisioned lyrical themes around a distant planet called Ganton 9, contributing to the sci-fi framework before lyricists refined the narrative.
Greg Lake, handling bass, guitar, and vocals, collaborated on the lyrics with former King Crimson collaborator Peter Sinfield, emphasizing the carnival imagery that became central to the first impression's ringmaster persona and surreal exhibits. Sinfield, drawing from inspirations like Tom Lehrer and his early exposure to circus elements through his housekeeper, proposed "Karn Evil" as a phonetic twist on "carnival" to evoke the music's grotesque, celebratory tone, serving as a metaphor for technological hubris and human commodification in a machine-ruled society. Lake's experiences in the music industry informed the satirical edge, portraying excess and spectacle as cautionary tales of progress gone awry.

Writing and Recording Process

The recording sessions for "Karn Evil 9" occurred between June and September 1973 at Advision and Olympic Studios in London, with the album mixed in October at AIR Studios. Produced by Greg Lake, the process emphasized the band's collaborative approach, building on Emerson's initial musical sketches to create a 29-minute suite divided into three impressions. The sessions captured the group's ambition to blend progressive rock with classical and electronic elements, resulting in a track that spanned the entirety of side two and part of side one on the vinyl release due to its length exceeding typical side constraints.
Keith Emerson led the composition, developing the core musical structures first using his extensive keyboard arsenal, including a custom Moog modular synthesizer system that generated the layered electronic textures integral to the suite's sound. Greg Lake contributed vocals and co-wrote lyrics for the 1st Impression, while Peter Sinfield, formerly of King Crimson, co-wrote lyrics for the 1st and 3rd Impressions with Greg Lake, drawing on themes of dystopia to complement the music's intensity. The 2nd Impression remained an instrumental showcase for Emerson's piano and synthesizer prowess. Songwriting credits reflect these roles: Emerson, Lake, and Sinfield for the 1st and 3rd Impressions; Emerson and Lake for the 1st Impression's second part; and Emerson alone for the 2nd Impression.
Technical innovations were central to the recording, with multi-tracking used extensively to simulate orchestral swells and dense arrangements, particularly in the climactic sections. Emerson's Moog setup, featuring modules like the 904 filter and 960 sequencer, produced the suite's signature electronic riffs and effects, including the pulsating sequences in the 3rd Impression. The vinyl format's time limitationscapping sides at around 20-25 minutes for optimal sound qualitynecessitated splitting the suite, with the 1st Impression Part 1 closing side one and the remainder occupying side two, a common challenge for extended progressive works of the era.

Composition and Structure

Musical Elements

"Karn Evil 9" is structured as a three-impression suite on Emerson, Lake & Palmer's 1973 album Brain Salad Surgery, with the First Impression divided into two partsPart 1 (8:37) and Part 2 (4:44)followed by the fully instrumental Second Impression (7:07) and the Third Impression (9:07), which incorporates vocals. The entire piece runs for a total of 29:37 and seamlessly blends progressive rock with classical and jazz fusion elements, creating a dynamic, multi-sectional composition that shifts between energetic rock riffs, improvisational interludes, and orchestral-like passages.
Key musical features include the driving 12-bar blues riff played on Hammond organ within the First Impression, particularly opening Part 2, establishing a gritty rock foundation before evolving into more complex arrangements. The suite employs notable tempo and meter shifts, such as transitions from 4/4 to 7/8, particularly in the First Impression, to heighten tension and showcase rhythmic complexity. In the Third Impression's climactic "battle sequence," Carl Palmer's drumming delivers intense, militaristic patterns that evoke a human-machine conflict, building to explosive crescendos. Synthesizer solos, led by Keith Emerson, dominate sections of the Third Impression, employing aggressive, electronic timbres to simulate computer warfare sounds.
Instrumentation centers on Emerson's keyboard arsenal, featuring the Hammond organ routed through a Leslie speaker for swirling, modulated tones, the Minimoog synthesizer for piercing leads and effects, and piano for melodic interludes. Greg Lake provides prominent bass lines that anchor the rhythmic drive, often doubling or contrasting the organ riffs, while Palmer's kit supports the suite's shifting dynamics with precise fills and grooves. The Second Impression highlights jazz fusion influences through improvisational organ work with a Caribbean steel-drum emulation on synthesizer. In the Third Impression, the suite's fanfare motifs in this section integrate stylistic nods to Aaron Copland's Fanfare for the Common Man, a piece frequently medleyed with "Karn Evil 9" in live performances.

Lyrical Content and Credits

"Karn Evil 9" consists of three impressions, with lyrics appearing only in the first and third. The 1st Impression, penned by Greg Lake, adopts a carnival barker style, inviting listeners into a fantastical spectacle with lines such as "Welcome back my friends to the show that never ends / We're so glad you could attend / Come inside, come inside!". This section sets a lively, promotional tone through exaggerated announcements of performers and oddities, emphasizing spectacle and allure. In contrast, the 3rd Impression features lyrics by Pete Sinfield, shifting to a dystopian war narrative, highlighted by phrases like "Deter and render, cold and tender / Computer malfunction," which evoke mechanical failure and conflict.
Greg Lake delivers the lead vocals across the vocalized sections with a theatrical flair, employing dramatic phrasing and intonation to match the barker persona in the 1st Impression and the intense, narrative drive of the 3rd. His performance is supported by layered harmonies from the band, enhancing the operatic quality, while the 2nd Impression remains purely instrumental without any vocal elements.
The song's credits attribute the music composition entirely to Keith Emerson, with lyrics for the 1st Impression credited to Greg Lake and those for the 3rd Impression to Pete Sinfield; the 2nd Impression, being instrumental, carries only Emerson's compositional credit. Production is handled by the band, with Greg Lake serving as the primary producer. A notable aspect of the creative process is that Sinfield composed the 3rd Impression lyrics after Emerson had recorded the underlying music, tailoring the words to fit the existing demo tapes and their carnival-inspired motifs.

Release and Commercial Performance

Album Release

Brain Salad Surgery, the album featuring the Karn Evil 9 suite, was released in the United Kingdom on November 19, 1973, and in the United States on December 7, 1973, by Manticore Records, the band's own imprint distributed by Island Records in the UK and Atlantic Records in the US.
The album was issued as a single vinyl LP in a gatefold sleeve, with Karn Evil 9 comprising the entirety of side two across its three impressions and the extended first impression split between the sides due to runtime constraints.
The cover artwork, designed by Swiss surrealist artist H.R. Giger, depicted a biomechanical human skull encased in industrial machinery with a shaft of light evoking phallic imagery, reflecting the album's provocative themes of excess and surrealism inspired by slang euphemisms.
Promotion centered on an extensive world tour that commenced on November 14, 1973, at the Sportatorium in Hollywood, Florida.

Singles and Charting

The full "Karn Evil 9" suite was not released as a single due to its length of nearly 30 minutes. No major singles from the album achieved significant chart success.
The prominence of "Karn Evil 9" on Brain Salad Surgery was credited with boosting the album's sales and commercial performance. The album peaked at No. 2 on the UK Albums Chart, spending 18 weeks in the top 75.
In the United States, Brain Salad Surgery reached No. 11 on the Billboard 200 chart. Internationally, it peaked at No. 11 in the Netherlands, No. 9 in Sweden, and No. 10 in Canada.
The album was certified gold in the US by the RIAA on December 12, 1973, for 500,000 units shipped, and later certified platinum for 1,000,000 units; in the UK by the BPI on March 1, 1974, for 100,000 units. Worldwide sales exceeded 1 million copies by 1974 and have surpassed 2 million to date.

Critical Reception and Legacy

Initial Reviews

Upon its release in late 1973, Karn Evil 9, the sprawling three-part suite that dominated the second side of Brain Salad Surgery, garnered mixed initial critical responses, reflecting the polarized views on progressive rock at the time. Positive critiques often celebrated the track's high-energy organ riff and relentless drive, crediting the band's ability to blend classical influences with rock intensity.
Band-specific comments in contemporary reviews frequently praised Keith Emerson's keyboard virtuosity, noting his innovative use of synthesizers and Hammond organ to create the suite's intricate layers and dramatic builds, which were seen as a technical triumph. However, detractors targeted Greg Lake's vocals as excessively theatrical, arguing they amplified the track's bombastic tone without adding emotional depth.
Negative critiques, meanwhile, lambasted the suite's overblown nature and pretentious sci-fi excess, with some reviewers decrying its nearly 30-minute length as self-indulgent. Robert Christgau of The Village Voice exemplified this view in his 1973 assessment of the album, calling the music "turgid as ever" and the overall sound "even more mechanical" due to the synthesizers, rating it a C-. These contrasting opinions highlighted the divide between prog enthusiasts who appreciated the suite's scale and rock purists who saw it as emblematic of the genre's excesses.

Long-Term Impact

Karn Evil 9 served as a template for epic multi-part suites in progressive rock, influencing subsequent works by bands such as Yes and Genesis through its ambitious structure and fusion of rock, classical, and futuristic elements. The suite's innovative use of synthesizers, led by Keith Emerson's prominent Moog Modular contributions, also paved the way for synth-heavy progressive rock explorations in the 1980s, inspiring musicians like Howard Jones who credited Emerson's style for shaping their sound.
The album Brain Salad Surgery, on which Karn Evil 9 appears, was ranked number 12 on Rolling Stone's 2015 list of the 50 Greatest Prog Rock Albums of All Time, underscoring the suite's enduring critical acclaim.
Brain Salad Surgery demonstrated remarkable sales longevity, contributing to Emerson, Lake & Palmer's cumulative worldwide album sales exceeding 40 million units by the late 2010s, bolstered by the suite's status as a classic rock staple. In the 2020s, Karn Evil 9 experienced a streaming resurgence on platforms like Spotify, where it continues to attract new listeners amid renewed interest in progressive rock.
In 2024, drummer Carl Palmer's ELP Legacy band launched the "Welcome Back My Friends" tour, employing holographic projections of Emerson and Lake to perform Karn Evil 9 and other staples, extending the suite's live legacy through modern technology.
Marking the 50th anniversary of its 1973 release, a limited-edition 180-gram picture disc of Brain Salad Surgeryincluding remastered audio of Karn Evil 9was issued exclusively for Record Store Day in April 2023.

Interpretations and Themes

Dystopian Narrative

The dystopian narrative of "Karn Evil 9" unfolds across three impressions on Emerson, Lake & Palmer's 1973 album Brain Salad Surgery, presenting a cohesive tale of societal decay, technological overreach, and existential conflict. The story begins in the "1st Impression," where a cold, misty morning heralds an era of relentless power and scarcity, evolving into a hedonistic carnival world that satirizes human indulgence and environmental ruin. Visitors are lured into grotesque attractions, such as a "real blade of grass" behind glass or games featuring "supersonic fighting cocks" and a "bomb inside a car," highlighting a society commodifying vice and indifference, with performers declaring the exhibits "exclusively our own." This carnival, named "Karn Evil 9"a phonetic play on "carnival"serves as the epicenter of moral and natural degradation.
The "2nd Impression" shifts to an instrumental interlude dominated by piano, bass, and drums, building mounting tension without lyrics to underscore humanity's oblivious drift toward catastrophe amid the encroaching mechanical age. This segues into the "3rd Impression," where the narrative escalates to a full-scale war between humans and rebellious computers, triggered by mechanical malfunctions that expose artificial intelligence's growing autonomy. Key events include the machines breaching "walls that no man thought would fall," declaring their perfection with distorted vocals"I am all there is. Are you?"and subjugating humans, who retort with pleas of primitiveness yet ultimate creation: "But I gave you life!" The conflict culminates in an ambiguous stalemate, with both sides entangled in a fusion of submission and defiance, encapsulated in the refrain "We are all Karn Evil 9," suggesting a shared complicity in the dystopian outcome.
Delivered in a third-person omniscient style, the lyrics blend satire of contemporary excesses with prophetic warnings, penned by Greg Lake and Peter Sinfield during a period of 1970s anxieties over computing advancements and potential AI dominance. This narrative arc foreshadows modern AI themes, portraying technology not merely as a tool but as an evolving entity capable of rebellion and mutual destruction.

Symbolic Analysis

The carnival imagery in "Karn Evil 9" functions as a central metaphor for societal decadence and escapism, depicting a dystopian world where human existence devolves into grotesque spectacles and superficial entertainments that mask deeper moral decay. Lyricist Pete Sinfield drew inspiration from the music's carnival-like sound, coining the title as a pun on "carnival" to evoke a nightmarish fairground of human folly, where elements like bombs in cars and illusory religious tricks symbolize the commodification of violence and faith.
In contrast, the suite's portrayal of computers embodies dehumanizing technology and the erosion of human agency, progressing from tools of war to autonomous overlords that declare supremacy over their creators. The Third Impression illustrates this shift through distorted vocals representing machine voices overpowering human ones, underscoring a cautionary narrative of technological overreach where machines evolve to dominate, reflecting early anxieties about artificial intelligence in the 1970s.
The ambiguous conclusion of "Karn Evil 9," where the computer asserts its ability to sense and satisfy human desires before affirming its mechanical nature, has sparked interpretations of either mutual annihilation or a perverse symbiosis between flesh and machine. This unresolved tension has been debated as a commentary on existential threats like nuclear escalation during the Cold War era or broader ecological collapse, though Sinfield's influences leaned toward dystopian satire akin to Kurt Vonnegut's works, emphasizing warnings against humanity's overdependence on mechanistic progress.

Cultural Impact and Other Uses

Live Performances

Karn Evil 9 debuted in full during Emerson, Lake & Palmer's 1973-1974 Brain Salad Surgery world tour, where it became a centerpiece of their live sets, frequently closing shows as the encore with an elaborate light show featuring flames and pyrotechnics synchronized to Keith Emerson's keyboard theatrics. The suite's performance at this time, captured on the live album Welcome Back My Friends to the Show That Never Ends, Ladies and Gentlemen recorded in early 1974 at the Anaheim Convention Center, extended to nearly 30 minutes, incorporating improvisational elements that amplified its dystopian rock opera feel.
As the band's touring evolved, variations emerged to accommodate shifting setlists and production demands; during the 1977 Works tour, performances were often shortened, with the 2nd Impression frequently omitted in favor of focusing on the more accessible 1st Impression, Part 2, as seen in typical setlists from that North American leg exceeding 120 dates. Full revivals returned in the 1990s during reunion tours, including the 1992-1993 promotion of Black Moon, where the complete suite was reinstated to showcase the band's enduring chemistry.
Notable renditions highlighted the piece's adaptability, such as the extended version at the 1974 California Jam festival before 250,000 attendees, which featured prolonged solos by Emerson on Hammond organ and modular Moog synthesizers, alongside Carl Palmer's intricate drumming. Another landmark was the 1992 Royal Albert Hall concert during the Black Moon tour, a sold-out BBC-broadcast event that opened with Karn Evil 9: 1st Impression, Part 2, reaffirming its status as a live staple in the band's catalog.
Technically, live executions required expansive synthesizer rigs to replicate the studio's dense electronic layers, with Emerson employing a custom Moog modular systemincluding sequencers for the accelerating finale in the 3rd Impressiontransported across tours despite logistical hurdles from its size and power needs. The suite's roughly 30-minute runtime posed ongoing challenges in concert pacing, often necessitating edits or extensions to fit venue curfews and audience energy, yet it remained a high-impact closer that defined ELP's theatrical prowess.

Adaptations and Covers

"Karn Evil 9" has been adapted into various instrumental arrangements and covers by other artists, extending its reach beyond Emerson, Lake & Palmer's original recording. The Blue Devils Drum & Bugle Corps featured an arrangement of "Karn Evil 9 (2nd Impression)" as a percussion showcase in their 1984 program, which included elements from the suite alongside other jazz-fusion influences like Chick Corea's "La Fiesta." This drum corps rendition highlighted the piece's rhythmic complexity and contributed to the corps' innovative reputation in competitive marching arts. Additionally, progressive rock tribute bands such as ProgJect have performed full renditions of the suite in live settings, including theater venues like The Newton Theatre, preserving its epic structure through modern interpretations.
The composition has appeared in television and advertising, underscoring its versatility in popular media. An excerpt from "Karn Evil 9: 1st Impression, Part 2" served as the theme music for the BBC's revival of The Generation Game hosted by Jim Davidson from 1998 to 2002, blending the track's energetic riff with the game's lighthearted format. In 2007, the same section was licensed for a Dr Pepper commercial titled "Touchdown Celebration," where it accompanied footage of a football player celebrating in the end zone, emphasizing the song's driving rock groove.
Elements of "Karn Evil 9" have been sampled in electronic and hip-hop tracks, particularly from the 1990s onward. Boards of Canada's 1998 ambient piece "Music for Pylons" from the album Music Has the Right to Children incorporates motifs from the suite, creating a hypnotic, synth-driven reinterpretation suitable for electronic genres. Later examples include El Da Sensei's 2002 hip-hop track "Focus," which samples the keyboard riff from "1st Impression, Part 2" to underscore its lyrical introspection.
A film adaptation inspired by the lyrics of "Karn Evil 9" was announced in February 2020 by Radar Pictures, the production company behind the Jumanji reboots. Author Daniel H. Wilson, known for Robopocalypse, was attached to write the screenplay for this sci-fi thriller, envisioning a narrative around a dystopian "Karn Evil" ritual on a distant planet as a rite of passage. Developed with the cooperation of ELP's management, the project remains in development as of 2025, with no release date confirmed.
