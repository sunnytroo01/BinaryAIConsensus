Regularity

Regular Outer Measures

A regular outer measure on a set XXX is defined as an outer measure \mu^* satisfying the property that for every subset AXA \subseteq XAX,
(A)=inf{(E):EA,Eis-measurable}.
\mu^*(A) = \inf \{ \mu^*(E) : E \supseteq A, \, E \text{ is } \mu^*\text{-measurable} \}.
(A)=inf{(E):EA,Eis-measurable}.
This condition ensures that every set AAA can be approximated from above by measurable sets whose outer measures are arbitrarily close to (A)\mu^*(A)(A), providing a form of outer regularity that enhances the analytical utility of the measure space.
While some formulations of regularity also incorporate inner approximation by compact or closed sets (i.e., (A)=sup{(K):KA,Kcompact}\mu^*(A) = \sup \{ \mu^*(K) : K \subseteq A, \, K \text{ compact} \}(A)=sup{(K):KA,Kcompact}), the standard definition for outer measures emphasizes the outer approximation via measurable supersets, aligning with the Carathodory measurability criterion where measurable sets form a \sigma-algebra.
Regular outer measures exhibit several key properties: the restriction of \mu^* to its measurable sets yields a complete measure, meaning that all subsets of null sets are measurable; moreover, the collection of measurable sets is dense in the power set of XXX with respect to outer measure approximation, allowing for tight control in limits and integrals.
A canonical example is the Lebesgue outer measure on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, which is regular because every Borel set (and more generally, every Lebesgue measurable set) can approximate any set from above in measure.
Constructions of outer measures, such as those arising from premeasures on algebras, yield regular outer measures under \sigma-finiteness conditions on the premeasure, ensuring the approximation property holds without pathological counterexamples in infinite spaces.

Regularization Process

The regularization process provides a method to construct a regular outer measure from any given outer measure, thereby enlarging the class of measurable sets to facilitate approximations. Given an outer measure \mu^* on a set XXX, the regularized outer measure \nu^* is defined for every subset AXA \subseteq XAX by
(A)=inf{(E):EA,Eis-measurable},
\nu^*(A) = \inf \left\{ \mu^*(E) : E \supseteq A, \, E \text{ is } \mu^*\text{-measurable} \right\},
(A)=inf{(E):EA,Eis-measurable},
where the infimum is taken to be \infty if no such measurable superset exists. This construction ensures that \nu^* is itself an outer measure on XXX.
It can be verified that \nu^* extends \mu^* in the sense that (E)=(E)\nu^*(E) = \mu^*(E)(E)=(E) for every \mu^*-measurable set EXE \subseteq XEX. Indeed, since EEE is \mu^*-measurable and contains itself, the infimum is at most (E)\mu^*(E)(E); conversely, by the monotonicity of \mu^*, it is at least (E)\mu^*(E)(E). Moreover, \nu^* satisfies the regularity condition by design: for any AXA \subseteq XAX, (A)\nu^*(A)(A) equals the infimum of (F)\nu^*(F)(F) over all \nu^*-measurable sets FAF \supseteq AFA. To see this, note that any \nu^*-measurable superset FFF of AAA admits a \mu^*-measurable superset EEE with (E)=(F)\mu^*(E) = \nu^*(F)(E)=(F), so the infima coincide. Thus, \nu^* is a regular outer measure.
The \sigma-algebra of \nu^*-measurable sets coincides with the \sigma-algebra of \mu^*-measurable sets. The measure \nu induced by \nu^* on its measurable sets coincides with the measure \mu induced by \mu^* on the \mu^*-measurable sets.
As an illustrative example, consider an outer measure \mu^* that lacks regularity, such as one arising from a non-standard covering construction without built-in approximations. Applying the regularization yields \nu^* with Lebesgue-like regularity properties, enabling outer approximations by measurable supersets even where \mu^* does not. This process is particularly useful for outer measures derived from premeasures on algebras that do not initially produce regular extensions, transforming them into tools suitable for advanced applications in analysis.
The primary advantage of regularization lies in its ability to impose approximability on non-regular outer measures, allowing sets to be sandwiched between measurable subsets without altering values on the original measurable class. This facilitates proofs involving limits, integrals, and extensions in measure theory, even starting from arbitrary outer measures.

Borel and Geometric Connections

In metric spaces, metric outer measures possess the property that all Borel sets are measurable with respect to the induced Carathodory -algebra. This follows from the metric outer measure condition, which ensures additivity for sets separated by positive distance, allowing the measurability of open and closed sets.
A proof sketch begins by verifying that every closed set $ F $ is measurable: for any test set $ A $, one constructs a sequence of sets $ G_n $ approximating the complement of $ F $ using the metric separation, showing $ \mu^(A) = \mu^(A \cap F) + \mu^(A \setminus F) $ via the subadditivity and monotonicity of $ \mu^ $. Since the Borel -algebra is generated by the closed sets and the collection of measurable sets forms a -algebra containing them, all Borel sets inherit measurability.
In geometric measure theory, outer measures provide the foundation for defining Hausdorff and packing measures, which extend classical volume notions to fractal sets of arbitrary dimension. The s-dimensional Hausdorff outer measure of a set $ A $ in a metric space is obtained by taking the infimum over countable covers by sets of diameter less than , summing the s-powers of diameters, normalizing appropriately, and letting approach zero; this yields a measure that is Borel-regular and coincides with Lebesgue measure for integer dimensions. Packing measures, constructed similarly but using disjoint balls to maximize coverage, complement Hausdorff measures by addressing lower bounds on set sizes and are particularly useful for analyzing the dimension and structure of fractals, such as self-similar sets where the Hausdorff dimension determines the critical scaling exponent.
Outer measures also play a central role in potential theory through capacities, which approximate the topological size of sets via infima over potentials or covers. For instance, the outer capacity of a set equals the infimum of inner capacities of containing open sets, forming an outer measure where only capacity-zero sets and their complements are measurable, thus providing a topological approximation by excluding negligible subsets.
Recent post-2000 developments extend these constructions beyond metric spaces to more general T0-topologies, where continuous valuations on open sets can be prolonged to Borel measures on sober or coherent spaces, preserving outer regularity and countably additivity. In conformal geometry, outer measures inform capacities for quasicircles, identifying subsets of outer capacity zero as topologically insignificant in quasiconformal mappings.
