Nucleic acid

1x
 Nucleic acids are biopolymers essential to all known forms of life, serving as the primary molecules for storing, transmitting, and expressing genetic information within cells. They consist of two major types: deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA), which encodes the complete genetic blueprint of an organism, and ribonucleic acid (RNA), which functions as a versatile intermediary in gene expression. Discovered in 1869 by Friedrich Miescher, nucleic acids were later recognized for their double-helical structure in DNA, proposed by James Watson and Francis Crick in 1953, marking a cornerstone of modern biology.
At the molecular level, nucleic acids are chains of nucleotide monomers, each comprising a five-carbon sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base. In DNA, the sugar is deoxyribose, and the bases are adenine (A), thymine (T), cytosine (C), and guanine (G); RNA uses ribose as the sugar and uracil (U) instead of thymine, with the same A, C, and G bases. Nucleotides link via phosphodiester bonds between the sugar of one and the phosphate of the next, forming long polynucleotide strands with directionality from a 5' phosphate end to a 3' hydroxyl end. This backbone provides structural stability, while the bases project inward to enable specific interactions.
DNA typically adopts a double-helical conformation, with two antiparallel polynucleotide strands twisted into a right-handed spiral of 2 nm diameter, completing one full turn every 10 base pairs. The strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary basesA pairing with T via two bonds, and G with C via threeallowing the sequence on one strand to dictate the other. In contrast, RNA is generally single-stranded but can fold into complex secondary structures like hairpins through intramolecular base pairing, facilitating diverse roles beyond simple messaging.
The functions of nucleic acids center on heredity and cellular processes. DNA stores genetic information in its nucleotide sequence, serving as a template for semi-conservative replication to transmit traits across generations, as seen in the human genome's approximately 3 billion base pairs. During gene expression, segments of DNA are transcribed into messenger RNA (mRNA), which carries the code to ribosomes for translation into proteins via transfer RNA (tRNA). Regulatory RNAs, such as microRNA (miRNA) and small interfering RNA (siRNA), modulate gene expression by binding target mRNAs to inhibit translation or promote degradation, while long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs) influence chromatin structure and transcription. Some RNAs, known as ribozymes, even catalyze biochemical reactions, underscoring nucleic acids' catalytic potential.

Fundamentals

Definition and Properties

Nucleic acids are linear biopolymers composed of repeating nucleotide monomers, each consisting of a nitrogenous base, a five-carbon pentose sugar, and one or more phosphate groups linked together via phosphodiester bonds. These molecules serve as the primary carriers of genetic information in living organisms, enabling the storage, transmission, and expression of hereditary traits.
Key physical and chemical properties of nucleic acids include their polyanionic character, arising from the negatively charged phosphate groups in the backbone, which imparts a high negative charge density under physiological conditions. This polyanionic nature contributes to their hydrophilicity, allowing strong interactions with water molecules through the polar phosphate and sugar moieties, which enhances solubility in aqueous environments. Additionally, the nitrogenous bases enable the formation of specific hydrogen bonds, facilitating interactions between complementary sequences, while the aromatic ring structures of the bases result in strong ultraviolet absorbance at 260 nm, a property widely used for their quantification in biochemical assays via the Beer-Lambert law.
Unlike other biopolymers such as proteins, which primarily function in catalysis and structural roles, or carbohydrates, which serve in energy storage and cell signaling, nucleic acids are uniquely specialized for the storage and propagation of sequence-specific genetic information through templated polymerization. They are broadly classified into two types based on the sugar component: deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA), which contains 2'-deoxyribose, and ribonucleic acid (RNA), which contains ribose, with the latter featuring a hydroxyl group at the 2' position that influences stability and reactivity.

Occurrence and Nomenclature

Nucleic acids are ubiquitous biomolecules found in all living cells, serving as the primary carriers of genetic information. In eukaryotic cells, deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) is primarily located within the nucleus, where it is organized into chromosomes. In prokaryotic cells, which lack a nucleus, DNA resides in a region called the nucleoid, often attached to the plasma membrane. Ribonucleic acid (RNA), in contrast, is predominantly synthesized in the nucleus of eukaryotes but functions mainly in the cytoplasm, where it participates in protein synthesis. RNA is also present in cellular organelles such as mitochondria and chloroplasts, supporting localized processes like energy production.
Beyond intact cells, nucleic acids occur in viruses, which possess either DNA or RNA as their genetic material, enabling replication within host cells. In eukaryotic organelles, mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) and chloroplast DNA (cpDNA) form compact genomes that encode essential components for respiration and photosynthesis, respectively. Extracellularly, cell-free DNA (cfDNA) circulates in bodily fluids such as blood plasma, originating from apoptotic or necrotic cells and serving as a biomarker for various conditions.
The nomenclature of nucleic acids follows standardized rules established by the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) and the International Union of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology (IUBMB). The four primary nucleobases are adenine (A), guanine (G), cytosine (C), and thymine (T) in DNA, with uracil (U) replacing thymine in RNA. These are represented by one-letter symbols in sequence notation, facilitating concise description of genetic information. Polymers are named using the "poly" prefix followed by the base abbreviation in parentheses, such as poly(A) for a chain of adenine nucleotides, commonly seen in the poly(A) tail of messenger RNA that enhances stability.
Nucleic acids are also detectable in environmental samples, providing insights into biodiversity without direct organism isolation. Environmental DNA (eDNA) and RNA are extracted from soil, water, and sediments, revealing microbial and macroorganism communities through sequencing techniques. In ancient contexts, preserved nucleic acids from permafrost, ice cores, or archaeological sediments enable reconstruction of past ecosystems and extinct species genomes.

History

Early Discoveries

In 1869, Swiss physician and biochemist Friedrich Miescher isolated a novel phosphorus-rich substance from the nuclei of white blood cells, which he obtained from pus on discarded surgical bandages, and named it "nuclein." Miescher's extraction involved treating the cells with pepsin to remove proteins and then using dilute alkali to precipitate the material, revealing its high phosphorus contentuncharacteristic of known proteinsand its acidic properties. This marked the first recognition of nucleic acids as distinct cellular components, though Miescher initially viewed nuclein as potentially involved in heredity without fully characterizing it.
By 1889, Miescher's student Richard Altmann improved isolation methods using enzymatic digestion with pepsin and alkaline hydrolysis to separate nuclein from contaminating proteins, renaming the purified, acidic core "nucleic acid." Altmann's techniques confirmed the substance's acidic nature and began distinguishing between animal-derived forms (from sources like thymus glands) and plant-derived forms (from yeast). These early extractions from thymus glands yielded what was termed thymonucleic acid, rich in nuclei, while yeast provided yeast nucleic acid, both recognized as acidic polymers.
In the early 1900s, Russian-American biochemist Phoebus Levene advanced nucleic acid characterization at the Rockefeller Institute by identifying and isolating the four constituent nucleotidesadenine, guanine, cytosine, and either thymine or uracilthrough hydrolysis of nucleic acids from thymus and yeast sources between 1909 and the 1910s. Levene's analyses, using techniques like acid and enzymatic hydrolysis, led him to propose the tetranucleotide hypothesis in 1909, positing that nucleic acids were simple, repeating polymers of these four nucleotides in equal proportions, a model that influenced research for decades despite its later refutation.

Key Structural Models and Advances

In 1944, Oswald Avery, Colin MacLeod, and Maclyn McCarty demonstrated that DNA is the genetic material through experiments on pneumococcal bacteria, where purified DNA from virulent strains transformed non-virulent strains, conferring heritable traits resistant to enzymatic degradation of proteins or RNA. This finding shifted the understanding of heredity from proteins to nucleic acids, providing the first direct evidence of DNA's role in genetic transformation.
Building on this, James Watson and Francis Crick proposed the double helix model of DNA in 1953, integrating X-ray diffraction data from Rosalind Franklin and Maurice Wilkins that revealed DNA's helical structure with a 3.4 nm repeat distance and 2 nm diameter. Their model depicted two antiparallel polynucleotide chains wound around a common axis, stabilized by hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs (adenine-thymine and guanine-cytosine), explaining DNA's ability to replicate semiconservatively and store genetic information. Franklin's Photo 51 image was pivotal, showing the X-pattern indicative of a helix, though her contributions were initially underrecognized.
The 1960s saw major advances in RNA's roles in gene expression. Franois Jacob and Jacques Monod proposed the existence of messenger RNA (mRNA) in 1961 as an unstable intermediary carrying genetic information from DNA to ribosomes for protein synthesis, based on bacterial operon studies showing rapid enzyme induction without corresponding changes in stable RNA pools. Concurrently, Sydney Brenner, Jacob, and Matthew Meselson experimentally isolated mRNA in 1961 using pulse-labeling techniques in E. coli, confirming its short half-life and role in directing translation. During this decade, the functions of transfer RNA (tRNA) and ribosomal RNA (rRNA) were also clarified: tRNA was identified as the adaptor molecule linking amino acids to mRNA codons, as shown by Mahlon Hoagland and Paul Zamecnik in 1958 through cell-free systems where soluble RNA intermediates transferred activated amino acids to ribosomal protein. rRNA's structural roles in forming the ribosome were elucidated through reconstitution experiments in the 1960s, contributing to the understanding of the central dogma of information flow from DNA to RNA to protein, as proposed by Francis Crick in 1958. The catalytic role of rRNA in the ribosome's peptidyl transferase center was later confirmed in the early 2000s through high-resolution structural studies, recognizing the ribosome as a ribozyme; this work was awarded the 2009 Nobel Prize in Chemistry to Venkatraman Ramakrishnan, Thomas A. Steitz, and Ada E. Yonath.
Advancing structural analysis, the 1970s introduced practical DNA sequencing methods. Frederick Sanger developed chain-termination sequencing in 1977, using DNA polymerase and dideoxynucleotides to generate fragments of varying lengths separated by gel electrophoresis, enabling the sequencing of the phiX174 phage genome (5,386 bases). Independently, Allan Maxam and Walter Gilbert introduced chemical cleavage sequencing in 1977, employing base-specific reactions (e.g., dimethyl sulfate for guanine) on end-labeled DNA to produce ladders readable on polyacrylamide gels, which facilitated early gene mapping. These techniques revolutionized genomics by allowing direct readout of nucleotide sequences, underpinning subsequent large-scale projects.
The completion of the Human Genome Project in 2003 marked a milestone, providing a reference sequence of approximately 3 billion base pairs covering over 99% of the euchromatic human genome through hierarchical shotgun sequencing coordinated by international consortia. This effort, initiated in 1990, identified about 20,000-25,000 protein-coding genes and revealed that non-coding DNA constitutes over 98% of the genome, transforming structural understanding and enabling comparative genomics.
The discovery of non-coding RNAs expanded nucleic acid functions beyond protein-coding. In 1993, Rosalind C. Lee, Rhonda L. Feinbaum, and Victor Ambros identified the first microRNA (miRNA), lin-4, in C. elegans as a small (~22-nucleotide) RNA that regulated developmental timing by base-pairing with lin-14 mRNA to inhibit translation. This revealed a novel post-transcriptional regulatory layer, with subsequent studies identifying thousands of miRNAs across species modulating gene expression via RNA-induced silencing complex (RISC). In 2024, Victor Ambros and Gary Ruvkun were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for the discovery of microRNA and its role in gene regulation.
More recently, the 2012 elucidation of CRISPR-Cas9 as a programmable DNA endonuclease by Martin Jinek, Krzysztof Chylinski, Ines Fonfara, Michael Hauer, Jennifer Doudna, and Emmanuelle Charpentier demonstrated how the Cas9 protein, guided by a dual crRNA-tracrRNA chimera (sgRNA), cleaves target DNA sequences in bacteria for adaptive immunity. This system, derived from bacterial defense against phages, enabled precise genome editing in eukaryotes, advancing structural models of nucleic acid-protein interactions and applications in functional genomics. In 2020, Emmanuelle Charpentier and Jennifer Doudna received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for the development of CRISPR-Cas9 as a genome-editing tool.

Chemical Composition

Nucleotide Components

Nucleotides serve as the fundamental building blocks of nucleic acids, each comprising three key components: a nitrogenous base, a pentose sugar, and a phosphate group. The nitrogenous base is attached to the 1' carbon of the sugar via a -N-glycosidic bond, and the phosphate is esterified to the 5' carbon of the sugar, forming a nucleoside monophosphate when a single phosphate is present. Nucleotides can contain one to three phosphate groups, with the triphosphate forms playing critical roles in energy transfer and polymerization reactions.
The nitrogenous bases fall into two classes: purines and pyrimidines. Purines, which have a double-ring structure, include adenine (C5H5N5C_5H_5N_5C5H5N5) and guanine (C5H5N5OC_5H_5N_5OC5H5N5O), while pyrimidines, featuring a single six-membered ring, consist of cytosine (C4H5N3OC_4H_5N_3OC4H5N3O) in both DNA and RNA, thymine (C5H6N2O2C_5H_6N_2O_2C5H6N2O2) in DNA, and uracil (C4H4N2O2C_4H_4N_2O_2C4H4N2O2) in RNA. These bases enable specific pairing in nucleic acid structures: adenine forms two hydrogen bonds with thymine (or uracil), and guanine forms three hydrogen bonds with cytosine, contributing to the stability of double helices. The pentose sugars differ between nucleic acid typesD-ribose (C5H10O5C_5H_{10}O_5C5H10O5) in RNA and 2-deoxy-D-ribose (C5H10O4C_5H_{10}O_4C5H10O4) in DNAwith the latter lacking a hydroxyl group at the 2' position, influencing structural flexibility and stability. The phosphate group (PO43PO_4^{3-}PO43) provides the negatively charged backbone when nucleotides polymerize.
While the canonical bases predominate, nucleic acids contain various modified bases that expand functional diversity. In DNA, 5-methylcytosine arises from enzymatic methylation of cytosine at the 5-position, playing a key role in epigenetic regulation and gene silencing. In RNA, pseudouridinean isomer of uridine where the uracil base attaches via a C-glycosidic rather than N-glycosidic bondenhances structural stability and is prevalent in transfer RNA and ribosomal RNA. Other modifications, such as N6-methyladenosine, further modulate RNA processing and translation efficiency.
Cells synthesize nucleotides through two primary pathways: de novo synthesis, which assembles purine and pyrimidine rings from simple precursors like amino acids, CO, and folate derivatives in a multi-step, energy-demanding process, and the salvage pathway, which recycles preformed bases or nucleosides via enzymes like hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyltransferase for purines. De novo pathways are essential for rapidly dividing cells to meet high nucleotide demands, whereas salvage pathways predominate in non-proliferating tissues for efficient recycling and conservation of resources. These pathways ensure a steady supply of nucleotides for nucleic acid biosynthesis, repair, and signaling.

Polymer Backbone and Linkages

Nucleic acids are linear polymers formed by the covalent linkage of nucleotides through phosphodiester bonds. In this linkage, the 5' phosphate group of one nucleotide reacts with the 3' hydroxyl group of an adjacent nucleotide, releasing a water molecule and creating a directional polarity from the 5' end to the 3' end of the chain. This 5'-3' orientation is universal in both DNA and RNA, ensuring consistent directionality during synthesis and processing.
The polymer backbone consists of repeating units of phosphate and sugar, denoted as -PO-sugar-, where the sugar is deoxyribose in DNA or ribose in RNA. The nitrogenous bases project from the sugar as side chains, not part of the backbone, allowing them to interact without disrupting the chain's structural integrity. This alternating sugar-phosphate framework provides rigidity and a negatively charged surface due to the ionized phosphates at physiological pH.
Polymerization occurs enzymatically, with polymerases such as DNA polymerase catalyzing the addition of nucleotides to the 3' end of the growing chain. The reaction uses nucleoside triphosphates (NTPs or dNTPs) as substrates, where the energy for bond formation is derived from the hydrolysis of the triphosphate to a monophosphate, releasing pyrophosphate. In vivo, DNA polymerase III in bacteria achieves rates of approximately 1000 nucleotides per second during replication.
Nucleic acid chains vary greatly in length; for example, the human genome comprises about 3 billion base pairs in double-stranded DNA. The average molecular weight of double-stranded DNA is roughly 660 daltons per base pair, enabling estimation of total mass for large genomes, such as approximately 4 10^{12} daltons for the total DNA in the human diploid genome.

Physical Structure and Topology

Conformations and Forms

Nucleic acids adopt specific three-dimensional conformations that are crucial for their stability, function, and interactions. In DNA, the most prevalent form under physiological conditions is B-DNA, a right-handed double helix with approximately 10.5 base pairs per turn and a helical pitch of 3.4 nm. This structure features a narrow minor groove and a wide major groove, allowing access for proteins involved in replication and transcription. The B-form is stabilized by hydration, with water molecules bridging the phosphate backbone and bases.
Under dehydrating conditions, DNA transitions to the A-form, another right-handed helix but shorter and wider, with about 11 base pairs per turn and a helical pitch of 2.8 nm. In the A-DNA, the base pairs are tilted relative to the helix axis, and the sugar adopts a C3'-endo pucker, resulting in a deep, narrow minor groove that is largely inaccessible. This conformation is less hydrated than B-DNA and is observed in fibers at low relative humidity. A third form, Z-DNA, is a left-handed double helix with 12 base pairs per turn and a pitch of approximately 4.5 nm, characterized by a zigzag phosphate backbone. Z-DNA typically forms in alternating purine-pyrimidine sequences, especially GC-rich regions, and has a flattened minor groove and elongated major groove.
RNA molecules, being single-stranded, form complex secondary structures with double-helical regions that predominantly adopt the A-form due to the presence of the 2'-hydroxyl group on the ribose sugar, which favors the C3'-endo sugar pucker and restricts flexibility. These helical segments often include stem-loop motifs, where paired regions create A-like helices with 11 base pairs per turn and a pitch of 2.8 nm, similar to DNA's A-form. The 2'-OH group enhances hydrogen bonding within the structure but also makes RNA more prone to hydrolysis compared to DNA.
The conformation of nucleic acids is influenced by environmental factors and intrinsic properties. Relative humidity affects DNA, with high humidity (above 92%) favoring the hydrated B-form, while dehydration below 75% relative humidity promotes the compact A-form. Salt concentration modulates ionic shielding of the phosphate backbone; moderate to high salt levels (e.g., 0.1-1 M NaCl) stabilize B-DNA, whereas low salt can facilitate transitions to A- or Z-forms in susceptible sequences. Sequence composition further dictates preferences, as AT-rich regions stabilize B-DNA through better hydration, while alternating GC sequences under high salt conditions adopt the Z-form.
These conformations are experimentally determined using techniques that probe atomic-level arrangements. X-ray crystallography and fiber diffraction have been pivotal, revealing the B-form through early fiber studies and the A- and Z-forms via crystal structures of oligonucleotides. Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy complements these by providing insights into solution-state conformations and dynamic transitions influenced by solvent and ions.

Topology and Supercoiling

The topology of double-stranded nucleic acids, especially DNA, is quantitatively described by three interrelated parameters: the linking number (Lk), twist (Tw), and writhe (Wr). The linking number represents the total number of times one strand winds around the other in a closed circular molecule, while twist measures the helical turns along the axis and writhe captures the coiling of the axis itself. These are related by White's theorem, which states that for a closed ribbon-like structure such as DNA, the linking number is the sum of twist and writhe:
Lk=Tw+Wr
Lk = Tw + Wr
Lk=Tw+Wr
This equation holds for oriented closed curves and provides a topological invariant for DNA. In closed circular DNA, the linking number remains conserved during processes that do not involve strand breakage, such as twisting or bending, ensuring topological stability unless enzymes intervene.
Supercoiling arises when the linking number deviates from the relaxed state (Lk), introducing torsional stress in the molecule. Negative supercoiling, characterized by underwinding (Lk < Lk), predominates in vivo and helps relieve strain from processes like transcription by promoting DNA unwinding and accessibility. Positive supercoiling, involving overwinding (Lk > Lk), typically forms ahead of unwinding forks during replication or transcription, compacting the DNA but potentially inhibiting further separation of strands. To manage these stresses, cells employ topoisomerases, enzymes classified into types I and II based on their strand-breaking mechanism. Type IA topoisomerases, such as bacterial topoisomerase I, relax negative supercoils through single-strand nicks; type IB enzymes, like human topoisomerase I, swivel the DNA to relieve both positive and negative supercoils; type IIA topoisomerases, including DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV, introduce negative supercoils or decatenate via double-strand breaks; and type IIB topoisomerases multiplex strands for specific relaxation.
Circular topologies are prevalent in bacterial plasmids and many viral genomes, where the closed loops enable compact storage and replication but introduce topological challenges like catenation. During replication of circular DNA, daughter molecules often interlink as catenanesmultiply wrapped ringsdue to unreleased intertwining from fork rotation. Decatenation, the separation of these catenanes into individual circles, is primarily mediated by type IIA topoisomerases like topoisomerase IV in bacteria, preventing segregation errors during cell division. In contrast, linear topologies in eukaryotic chromosomes avoid inherent supercoiling conservation but still experience transient superhelical stresses resolved by topoisomerases.
RNA molecules exhibit more diverse topologies due to their single-stranded nature and lack of rigid double helices, forming intricate structures like pseudoknots and kissing loops, particularly in viral genomes. A pseudoknot occurs when nucleotides in a loop base-pair with a complementary sequence outside the stem-loop, creating a compact, stable motif that often regulates translation or replication in viruses such as coronaviruses and arteriviruses. Kissing loops involve base-pairing between complementary loops of two hairpins, facilitating long-range interactions that cyclize viral RNA or enhance ribosomal recruitment, as seen in flaviviruses and picornaviruses. These RNA topological elements enable dynamic switching between conformations to control gene expression without relying on enzymatic supercoil resolution.

Sequence and Information Encoding

Primary Sequence Representation

Nucleic acid sequences are represented using a standardized notation that specifies the order of nucleotides from the 5' to 3' direction, reflecting the polarity of the phosphodiester backbone. The four canonical bases are denoted by single-letter codes: A for adenine, C for cytosine, G for guanine, and T for thymine in DNA, with U substituting for T in RNA. This convention, established by international standards, ensures unambiguous communication of primary structure and facilitates computational analysis. For double-stranded DNA, sequences are written for one strand (typically the coding or reference strand) in the 5' to 3' direction, while the complementary antiparallel strand follows base-pairing rules, such that a sequence like 5'-ATGC-3' pairs with 3'-TACG-5'.
Chargaff's rules describe the base composition in double-stranded DNA, stating that the proportion of adenine (A) equals thymine (T), and guanine (G) equals cytosine (C), a finding derived from empirical measurements of DNA from various organisms in the late 1940s. These rules arise from the complementary base pairing that stabilizes the double helix. Additionally, the GC contentthe percentage of guanine and cytosine basesvaries between species and influences DNA stability; higher GC content correlates with elevated melting temperature (Tm), as G-C pairs form three hydrogen bonds compared to two in A-T pairs, requiring more thermal energy to denature the helix.
The determination of primary sequences has evolved through key technologies. The Sanger dideoxy chain-termination method, introduced in 1977, relies on chain-terminating dideoxynucleotides to generate fragments of varying lengths, which are separated by electrophoresis to read the sequence. This approach enabled the first complete genome sequences and remains a gold standard for targeted validation. Modern high-throughput methods include Illumina's sequencing by synthesis, which uses reversible terminator nucleotides and fluorescent detection on clustered amplicons for massively parallel short-read sequencing, achieving billions of reads per run. Complementary long-read technologies, such as Oxford Nanopore sequencing, pass DNA through protein nanopores to measure ionic current disruptions, producing reads up to megabases in length for resolving complex regions.
Public repositories archive these sequences for global access and analysis. GenBank, maintained by the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI), stores annotated nucleotide sequences from diverse sources, encompassing trillions of base pairs. The European Nucleotide Archive (ENA), part of the European Molecular Biology Laboratory's European Bioinformatics Institute (EMBL-EBI), collaborates with GenBank and Japan's DDBJ in the International Nucleotide Sequence Database Collaboration to ensure synchronized, non-redundant data. Bioinformatics tools like BLAST (Basic Local Alignment Search Tool), developed by NCBI, enable rapid comparison of query sequences against these databases by identifying local similarities, supporting applications from homology detection to functional annotation.

Encoding Genetic Information

Nucleic acids encode genetic information through sequences of nucleotides that specify the synthesis of proteins and regulate cellular processes. The genetic code operates as a triplet system, where groups of three consecutive nucleotides, known as codons, determine the incorporation of specific amino acids during protein translation. With four possible nucleotides (A, C, G, T in DNA or U in RNA), there are 64 possible codons, which specify 20 standard amino acids and three stop signals (UAA, UAG, UGA) that terminate translation. This code exhibits degeneracy, meaning multiple codons can encode the same amino acid; for example, the amino acid serine is specified by six codons (UCU, UCC, UCA, UCG, AGU, AGC). The genetic code is nearly universal across all organisms, from bacteria to humans, but exceptions exist, such as in mammalian mitochondria where AUA codes for methionine instead of isoleucine and UGA serves as a codon for tryptophan rather than a stop signal.
Within nucleic acid sequences, open reading frames (ORFs) define the translatable regions that encode proteins. An ORF begins with the start codon AUG (coding for methionine) and extends continuously until a stop codon is encountered, without intervening stop codons. In prokaryotes, ORFs are typically uninterrupted, but in eukaryotic genes, they are segmented into exonscoding sequencesand introns, non-coding sequences that are transcribed but removed during RNA splicing to form mature mRNA containing only the joined exons. This exon-intron structure allows for alternative splicing, increasing protein diversity from a single gene, though the core ORF remains the sequence from start to stop codon in the processed transcript.
The information density of DNA is approximately 2 bits per base pair, as each position can represent one of four nucleotides, equivalent to log(4) = 2 bits of information. During replication, DNA polymerases achieve high fidelity through base-pairing selectivity and proofreading mechanisms, resulting in error rates as low as 10 to 10 substitutions per base pair per replication cycle in eukaryotes. These low error rates ensure the stable transmission of genetic information across generations, with additional mismatch repair systems further reducing mutations.
Much of the genome consists of non-coding sequences that do not directly encode proteins but play crucial roles in gene regulation. Promoters are DNA regions immediately upstream of genes where RNA polymerase binds to initiate transcription, while enhancers are distal sequences that increase transcription rates by looping to interact with promoters and recruit transcription factors. Repetitive elements, such as Alu sequencesshort interspersed nuclear elements (SINEs) derived from 7SL RNAcomprise about 10% of the human genome and can influence gene expression, chromatin structure, and genome evolution through insertions or recombination events.

Types

Deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA)

Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) serves as the primary molecule for long-term genetic storage in most organisms, distinguished by its double-stranded helical structure that enables stable information preservation and replication. This structure, proposed by James Watson and Francis Crick in 1953, consists of two antiparallel polynucleotide chains twisted into a right-handed helix, with the sugar-phosphate backbones forming the outer rails and nitrogenous bases projecting inward to form hydrogen-bonded pairs: adenine (A) with thymine (T) via two bonds, and guanine (G) with cytosine (C) via three bonds. The use of thymine in DNA, rather than uracil as in RNA, provides an additional methyl group that enhances base-pairing specificity and aids in damage detection during repair. The helical diameter measures approximately 2 nanometers, with a pitch of 3.4 nanometers encompassing about 10 base pairs per turn, allowing for efficient packaging of genetic information.
In terms of genome organization, DNA is arranged into chromosomes in eukaryotic cells, where linear molecules are housed within the nucleus and associated with proteins for compaction, while prokaryotes typically feature a single circular chromosome in the nucleoid region. Many prokaryotes, such as Escherichia coli, also harbor smaller, independently replicating circular DNA molecules called plasmids, which often carry accessory genes for traits like antibiotic resistance and can transfer between cells via horizontal gene exchange. In eukaryotes, the nuclear genome is divided among multiple linear chromosomeshumans have 23 pairstotaling about 3.055 gigabase pairs (Gb) of DNA, vastly larger than the prokaryotic counterpart, such as the E. coli genome at approximately 4.6 megabase pairs (Mb). This organizational difference reflects evolutionary adaptations: compact circular forms suit rapid prokaryotic reproduction, while compartmentalized linear chromosomes support complex regulation in eukaryotes.
DNA's chemical stability underpins its role as a durable genetic archive, primarily due to the deoxyribose sugar in its backbone, which lacks a hydroxyl group at the 2' carbon position present in RNA's ribose, thereby reducing susceptibility to hydrolytic cleavage by ribonucleases and alkaline conditions. This structural modification minimizes spontaneous degradation, with DNA's half-life in neutral aqueous solution exceeding that of RNA by orders of magnitude. Complementing this inherent stability are sophisticated repair mechanisms, such as base excision repair (BER), which identifies and removes damaged baseslike those oxidized by reactive oxygen species or alkylated by environmental mutagensvia glycosylases that initiate lesion-specific excision, followed by polymerase filling and ligation to restore the original sequence. BER handles the majority of single-base lesions with minimal distortion to the double helix, ensuring genome fidelity across cell divisions.
Notable variations in DNA structure occur in specific cellular compartments, such as mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA), which in humans forms a compact, circular double-stranded molecule of 16,569 base pairs encoding 13 essential proteins for oxidative phosphorylation, along with tRNAs and rRNAs. Unlike nuclear DNA, mtDNA lacks extensive histone association and resides in the mitochondrial matrix, replicating independently with its own polymerase. In eukaryotic nuclei, however, DNA is tightly packaged with histonespositively charged proteins forming octamers around which DNA wraps 1.65 times to create nucleosomes, the basic unit of chromatin that condenses the genome up to 10,000-fold for fitting within the cell nucleus. This histone-mediated packaging not only compacts DNA but also regulates access for transcription and replication through modifications like acetylation.
Evolutionarily, DNA's core architecture and function as a genetic repository are highly conserved across domains of life, yet genome sizes diverge dramatically to accommodate varying complexity: prokaryotic genomes like E. coli's 4.6 Mb support essential metabolic and reproductive functions in a single chromosome, whereas eukaryotic genomes, exemplified by the human 3.055 Gb, incorporate vast non-coding regions, introns, and regulatory elements packaged into multiple chromosomes for sophisticated multicellularity. This scaling reflects selective pressures for efficiency in simple organisms versus regulatory depth in complex ones, with DNA's double-stranded form enabling semi-conservative replication as a universal mechanism.

Ribonucleic Acid (RNA)

Ribonucleic acid (RNA) is a single-stranded nucleic acid polymer composed of nucleotides containing the sugar ribose, the bases adenine, guanine, cytosine, and uracil, and phosphate groups linked by phosphodiester bonds. Unlike DNA, RNA incorporates uracil instead of thymine, which arises because uracil is energetically less costly to produce during transcription and pairs effectively with adenine without the need for methylation seen in thymine. The presence of a hydroxyl group at the 2' position of the ribose sugar distinguishes RNA from DNA's deoxyribose, conferring greater chemical reactivity but also increased susceptibility to hydrolysis, leading to shorter half-lives typically ranging from minutes to hours in cells. This instability facilitates rapid turnover of genetic information, allowing cells to respond dynamically to environmental changes.
RNA exhibits remarkable structural versatility, enabling diverse functions beyond mere information transfer. The 2' hydroxyl group not only contributes to RNA's flexibility in folding into complex three-dimensional structures but also enables catalytic activity, as demonstrated by ribozymesRNA molecules that function as enzymes. The discovery of ribozymes, such as the self-splicing intron in Tetrahymena ribosomal RNA by Thomas Cech in 1982, revealed that RNA can catalyze phosphodiester bond formation and cleavage without protein assistance. This catalytic potential underpins RNA's role in key biological processes and supports the RNA world hypothesis, proposed by Walter Gilbert in 1986, which posits that primordial RNA molecules served dual roles as both genetic information carriers and catalysts in prebiotic evolution, preceding the emergence of DNA and proteins.
Major types of RNA include messenger RNA (mRNA), transfer RNA (tRNA), ribosomal RNA (rRNA), and various non-coding RNAs. mRNA carries genetic instructions from DNA to ribosomes for protein synthesis, typically comprising 1-2% of total cellular RNA and undergoing extensive processing to ensure functionality. tRNA acts as an adaptor molecule, recognizing mRNA codons via its anticodon loop and delivering specific amino acids; its secondary structure forms a characteristic cloverleaf with four stem-loops stabilized by base pairing, folding into an L-shaped tertiary structure approximately 70-90 nucleotides long. rRNA, the most abundant RNA type (up to 80% of cellular RNA), forms the core of ribosomes, with prokaryotic ribosomes sedimenting at 70S (composed of 30S and 50S subunits containing 16S, 23S, and 5S rRNAs) and eukaryotic at 80S (40S and 60S subunits with 18S, 28S, 5.8S, and 5S rRNAs), providing the scaffold for protein synthesis. Non-coding RNAs, which do not translate into proteins, include small nuclear RNAs (snRNAs) that form the spliceosome for intron removal, small nucleolar RNAs (snoRNAs) that guide rRNA modifications, and microRNAs (miRNAs) that regulate gene expression by binding target mRNAs to inhibit translation or promote degradation.
In eukaryotes, nascent pre-mRNA undergoes co-transcriptional processing to mature into functional mRNA. This includes 5' capping, where a 7-methylguanosine cap is added to the 5' end shortly after transcription initiation by RNA polymerase II, protecting against exonucleases and aiding export and translation. At the 3' end, a poly(A) tail of 200-250 adenine residues is added via cleavage and polyadenylation, enhancing stability and facilitating nuclear export. Splicing removes introns and joins exons using the spliceosome, a complex of snRNAs (U1, U2, U4, U5, U6) and proteins, which recognizes conserved splice sites (GU at 5' and AG at 3' introns) to catalyze two transesterification reactions. Additionally, RNA editing, such as adenosine-to-inosine (A-to-I) deamination by ADAR enzymes, alters specific bases post-transcriptionally, expanding the proteome and fine-tuning gene expression in processes like neurotransmitter receptor function.

Artificial and Synthetic Nucleic Acids

Artificial and synthetic nucleic acids are laboratory-created molecules that mimic or extend the structural and functional properties of natural DNA and RNA, often with modified backbones, sugars, or bases to enhance stability, binding affinity, or compatibility with non-natural chemistries. These analogs and de novo designs enable applications in research and biotechnology by overcoming limitations of natural nucleic acids, such as susceptibility to nucleases or restricted genetic encoding. Key examples include peptide nucleic acid (PNA), locked nucleic acid (LNA), and morpholinos, which alter the backbone for improved properties, while xeno-nucleic acids (XNAs) and expanded genetic codes introduce entirely novel scaffolds or base pairs.
Nucleic acid analogs like PNA replace the deoxyribose-phosphate backbone of DNA with a neutral pseudo-peptide polymer of N-(2-aminoethyl)glycine units linked to nucleobases, resulting in uncharged molecules that bind complementary DNA or RNA with high affinity and specificity without electrostatic repulsion. Introduced in 1991, PNAs can form stable triplexes or duplexes with targets, invading double-stranded DNA to displace one strand and form D-loops, a property exploited for sequence-specific modulation. LNA incorporates a methylene bridge constraining the ribose ring in a 3'-endo conformation between the 2'-oxygen and 4'-carbon, increasing thermal stability of hybrids with RNA or DNA by up to 10C per modification and enabling shorter oligonucleotides for equivalent binding strength. First synthesized in 1998, LNA's rigid structure enhances nuclease resistance and specificity, making it suitable for precise targeting. Morpholinos are charge-neutral oligomers where the ribose and phosphodiester linkages are replaced by morpholine rings connected via phosphorodiamidate bonds, providing exceptional stability against degradation and low toxicity while sterically blocking RNA processing or translation through sequence-specific binding. Developed in the 1980s and refined by 2000, morpholinos typically range from 20-30 units and do not cleave targets, distinguishing them from RNase H-dependent mechanisms.
In synthetic biology, xeno-nucleic acids (XNAs) represent a class of non-natural genetic polymers with alternative sugar backbones, such as threose nucleic acid (TNA), which uses a four-carbon threofuranose sugar instead of ribose or deoxyribose, forming stable antiparallel duplexes capable of base pairing with DNA and RNA. TNA, first reported in 2000, resists nuclease degradation and can serve as a template for enzymatic synthesis by polymerases, enabling evolution of functional XNA enzymes in vitro. Expanded genetic codes incorporate unnatural base pairs (UBPs) to increase the information capacity beyond the four natural bases, as exemplified by the d5SICS-dNaM pair, which forms stable hydrogen bonds and is replicated by engineered polymerases with fidelities approaching natural pairs. Pioneered in the 2000s, such UBPs allow site-specific insertion of additional components into DNA and proteins, with replication efficiencies improved to over 99% in E. coli by 2014.
Artificial nucleic acids are primarily created through chemical synthesis using the phosphoramidite method, a solid-phase approach where protected nucleoside phosphoramidite monomers are sequentially coupled to a growing chain on controlled-pore glass support, with steps including detritylation, coupling, oxidation, and capping to yield oligonucleotides up to 200 nucleotides long with high purity after deprotection. Developed in 1981, this method proceeds in the 3' to 5' direction and accommodates modified bases or backbones like those in PNA or LNA. Enzymatic assembly complements chemical methods by employing template-independent polymerases, such as terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase, to add nucleotides to primers, enabling scalable production of defined sequences with fewer chemical steps and higher fidelity for longer strands. PCR amplification, using thermostable polymerases like Taq, further scales synthetic nucleic acids by exponentially copying templates, often incorporating analogs during cycles for modified outputs.
Representative examples include aptamers, short single-stranded nucleic acids selected for high-affinity binding to targets via SELEX (systematic evolution of ligands by exponential enrichment), an iterative process starting with a random oligonucleotide library (10^14-10^15 variants) incubated with immobilized targets, followed by partitioning, elution, and PCR amplification to enrich binders over 8-15 rounds, yielding sequences with dissociation constants in the nanomolar range. First demonstrated in 1990, SELEX has produced aptamers from DNA, RNA, or modified backbones like 2'-fluoro pyrimidines for enhanced stability. Antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs), typically 15-25 nucleotides long with phosphorothioate backbones, are chemically synthesized to hybridize to complementary RNA sequences, modulating expression by steric blockade or recruitment of cellular RNases, and can incorporate LNA or morpholino modifications for improved pharmacokinetics.
Recent advances as of 2024 include expanded use of synthetic nucleic acids in therapeutics, such as siRNA-based drugs like patisiran (approved 2018) and emerging mRNA therapeutics beyond vaccines, with ongoing developments in stereopure oligonucleotides for enhanced efficacy. Additionally, in response to biosecurity concerns, a U.S. framework for screening synthetic nucleic acid sequences was established in 2023, requiring providers to implement scalable risk assessments for potential dual-use sequences by 2025.

Biological Functions

Genetic Storage and Replication

Nucleic acids, particularly DNA, serve as the primary molecules for storing genetic information in most organisms, with replication ensuring the faithful duplication of this information prior to cell division. The semi-conservative mechanism of DNA replication, where each parental strand acts as a template for a new complementary strand, was experimentally demonstrated in 1958 using density-labeled DNA in Escherichia coli. This process begins at specific origins of replication, such as the oriC site in bacterial chromosomes, where initiator proteins bind to unwind the double helix and form bidirectional replication forks that proceed in opposite directions around the circular genome. Key enzymes facilitate this unwinding and synthesis: DNA helicase separates the strands by breaking hydrogen bonds, creating single-stranded templates; primase synthesizes short RNA primers to initiate DNA synthesis since DNA polymerases cannot start de novo; and DNA ligase seals nicks in the phosphodiester backbone after Okazaki fragment joining on the lagging strand.
To maintain high fidelity, DNA replication incorporates multiple error-correction mechanisms, achieving an overall error rate of approximately 10^{-9} to 10^{-10} errors per base pair incorporated. During synthesis, DNA polymerases possess 3'5' exonuclease activity for proofreading, which removes mismatched nucleotides immediately after incorporation, improving accuracy by 10^2- to 10^3-fold. Post-replication, mismatch repair systems scan for and excise errors missed by proofreading, further reducing mutation rates by correcting about 99.9% of remaining mismatches. In contrast, RNA serves as genetic material in certain viruses, where replication occurs via RNA-dependent RNA polymerases (RdRPs) that synthesize complementary strands without DNA intermediates; for example, positive-sense single-stranded RNA viruses like poliovirus use their genomic RNA as a template to produce negative-sense intermediates, which then generate more positive-sense genomes.
Telomere maintenance is crucial for preserving chromosome integrity during repeated DNA replications, as the linear nature of eukaryotic chromosomes leads to progressive shortening of ends due to the inability of DNA polymerase to fully replicate the lagging strand terminus, known as the end-replication problem. The enzyme telomerase, a ribonucleoprotein complex, counteracts this by adding telomeric repeat sequences (e.g., TTAGGG in humans) to 3' overhangs using its RNA component as a template, thereby extending telomeres and preventing replicative senescence. This activity is particularly active in stem cells, germ cells, and most cancer cells to sustain indefinite proliferation.

Gene Expression and Protein Synthesis

Gene expression and protein synthesis represent the core processes by which genetic information encoded in nucleic acids is converted into functional proteins, following the central dogma of molecular biology, which posits that genetic information flows from DNA to RNA to protein. This unidirectional transfer ensures the accurate transmission of genetic instructions from the genome to the proteome, with DNA serving as the stable repository and RNA as the intermediary messenger. While the central dogma holds as the foundational principle, exceptions exist, such as reverse transcription in retroviruses, where RNA is used as a template to synthesize DNA via the enzyme reverse transcriptase.
Transcription initiates gene expression by synthesizing RNA from a DNA template, primarily mediated by RNA polymerase II in eukaryotes. This enzyme assembles at promoter regions, often featuring the TATA boxa conserved AT-rich sequence approximately 25-35 base pairs upstream of the transcription start sitethat recruits the TATA-binding protein to form the pre-initiation complex. Enhancers, distal DNA elements, further modulate transcription by looping to interact with promoters, facilitating the recruitment of additional transcription factors and co-activators to enhance RNA polymerase II activity. In prokaryotes, transcription produces polycistronic mRNAs, which encode multiple proteins from a single transcript, allowing coordinated expression of gene clusters such as those in operons.
Following transcription, eukaryotic pre-mRNA undergoes extensive processing to mature into export-competent mRNA. Splicing removes non-coding introns and joins coding exons via the spliceosome, a complex of small nuclear RNAs and proteins that catalyzes precise exon ligation. Additional modifications include 5' capping and 3' polyadenylation, which stabilize the transcript and facilitate nuclear export through interactions with export factors like the TREX complex, ensuring only properly processed mRNAs reach the cytoplasm for translation.
Translation decodes the mRNA into a polypeptide chain using ribosomes, which assemble from small and large subunits to form the functional complex. Initiation in eukaryotes involves eukaryotic initiation factors (eIFs), such as eIF2 and eIF4F, which recruit the initiator methionyl-tRNA to the start codon (AUG) in a cap-dependent manner, scanning the mRNA 5' untranslated region to position the ribosome accurately. During elongation, aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases charge transfer RNAs (tRNAs) with their cognate amino acids, enabling elongation factors like eEF1A to deliver these charged tRNAs to the ribosome's A site, where peptide bonds form sequentially according to the genetic code. Termination occurs upon recognition of stop codons (UAA, UAG, or UGA) by release factors, such as eRF1 and eRF3, which trigger hydrolysis of the peptidyl-tRNA bond in the P site, releasing the completed polypeptide and dissociating the ribosome.

Regulatory and Catalytic Roles

Nucleic acids play crucial regulatory roles in gene expression through various non-coding RNA species. MicroRNAs (miRNAs) and small interfering RNAs (siRNAs) mediate post-transcriptional gene silencing by incorporating into the RNA-induced silencing complex (RISC), where they guide the complex to target mRNAs for cleavage or translational repression, thereby fine-tuning gene expression levels. This mechanism was first demonstrated in seminal experiments showing potent gene interference by double-stranded RNA in C. elegans, highlighting RISC's role in sequence-specific mRNA degradation. Long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs) contribute to chromatin modulation by interacting with chromatin-modifying complexes, such as those involving histone acetyltransferases or methyltransferases, to influence nucleosome positioning, chromosome looping, and transcriptional activation or repression at specific loci. For instance, lncRNAs like Xist facilitate X-chromosome inactivation by recruiting repressive complexes to alter chromatin structure. PIWI-interacting RNAs (piRNAs) provide germline protection by silencing transposable elements, preventing genomic instability during gametogenesis; they associate with PIWI proteins to form complexes that cleave transposon transcripts and maintain heterochromatin in germ cells.
Catalytic nucleic acids, or ribozymes, exhibit enzymatic activity independent of proteins, expanding the functional repertoire of RNA. Self-splicing introns, such as group I introns in Tetrahymena ribosomal RNA, catalyze their own excision through two transesterification reactions, demonstrating RNA's ability to perform phosphodiester bond cleavages. The peptidyl transferase center of the ribosome, composed of ribosomal RNA, catalyzes peptide bond formation during protein synthesis, as evidenced by structural studies revealing RNA residues directly involved in the reaction. In vitro, deoxyribozymes (DNAzymes) have been evolved to perform similar catalytic functions, such as RNA cleavage; the first DNAzyme, selected in 1994, cleaves single-stranded RNA at a specific phosphodiester bond, showcasing DNA's latent enzymatic potential under non-physiological conditions.
Epigenetic modifications involving nucleic acids regulate chromatin accessibility without altering the DNA sequence. DNA methylation, primarily at CpG islands in promoter regions, represses transcription by recruiting methyl-binding proteins that compact chromatin and inhibit transcription factor access, a process essential for developmental gene silencing and imprinting. Histone modifications, including acetylation and methylation, dynamically alter chromatin structure; for example, histone H3 lysine 27 trimethylation (H3K27me3) promotes heterochromatin formation and gene repression, while H3K4 methylation enhances accessibility at active promoters, influencing overall chromatin openness.
Viral nucleic acids exploit host regulatory mechanisms for persistence and evasion. In HIV-1, the viral RNA reverse-transcribes into DNA, which integrates as a provirus into the host genome via the viral integrase enzyme, preferentially targeting active transcription units to ensure long-term latency and reactivation. Additionally, RNA interference (RNAi) serves as an antiviral defense where host siRNAs target viral RNAs for degradation, a pathway first evidenced in plants and insects but also functional in mammals to limit viral replication.

Modern Applications

Biotechnology and Diagnostics

Nucleic acids, particularly DNA, are central to biotechnology through techniques like the polymerase chain reaction (PCR), which amplifies specific DNA segments exponentially for applications in cloning and forensics. Invented by Kary Mullis in 1983, PCR was first demonstrated in a seminal 1985 study using enzymatic amplification of -globin genomic sequences to diagnose sickle cell anemia, enabling the production of millions of copies from minute starting material. In cloning, PCR facilitates the insertion of amplified genes into vectors for protein production, while in forensics, it amplifies short tandem repeats from degraded samples to generate DNA profiles for identification.
Variants of PCR enhance its utility in diagnostics and quantification. Quantitative PCR (qPCR), introduced by Higuchi et al. in 1993, monitors DNA amplification in real-time using fluorescent dyes or probes, allowing precise measurement of gene copy numbers for applications like viral load assessment. Reverse transcription PCR (RT-PCR) combines reverse transcriptase to convert RNA into complementary DNA followed by PCR amplification, enabling the study of gene expression from RNA templates, such as in detecting RNA viruses.
Next-generation sequencing (NGS) technologies have revolutionized nucleic acid analysis by enabling high-throughput genomic profiling. Pioneered by the 454 platform in a 2005 study that sequenced bacterial genomes using picoliter-scale pyrosequencing reactors, NGS now supports massive parallel reads for identifying cancer mutations and metagenomic diversity. In cancer genomics, NGS detects somatic mutations like those in TP53, guiding personalized diagnostics, while in metagenomics, it characterizes microbial communities from environmental samples without cultivation.
Nucleic acid probes and hybridization techniques provide spatial and high-throughput insights into gene localization and expression. Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH), building on early in situ hybridization methods from 1969, uses fluorescently labeled DNA probes to visualize specific sequences on chromosomes, aiding in detecting chromosomal abnormalities like translocations in leukemia. DNA microarrays, advanced in a 1995 study for quantitative gene expression monitoring, immobilize thousands of nucleic acid probes on a chip to hybridize with labeled targets, profiling expression patterns across genomes for disease biomarker discovery.
CRISPR-based diagnostics leverage nucleic acid detection for rapid pathogen identification. The SHERLOCK system, developed in 2017, uses Cas13 to cleave reporter molecules upon binding target RNA or DNA after isothermal amplification, producing a visible signal on paper strips for point-of-care testing of viruses like Zika with attomolar sensitivity.

Therapeutics and Synthetic Biology

Nucleic acids play a central role in therapeutics through gene therapy, which involves delivering functional genetic material to correct or compensate for diseased genes. Viral vectors, such as adeno-associated virus (AAV), are commonly used for targeted delivery due to their ability to efficiently transduce non-dividing cells like neurons and muscle cells. For instance, Zolgensma (onasemnogene abeparvovec), an AAV9-based therapy, was approved by the FDA in 2019 for treating spinal muscular atrophy (SMA) in children under two years old, delivering a functional copy of the SMN1 gene to restore motor neuron survival. More recently, CRISPR-Cas9 genome editing has revolutionized gene therapy by enabling precise modifications to DNA sequences. Casgevy (exagamglogene autotemcel), the first CRISPR/Cas9-based therapy, received FDA approval in 2023 for sickle cell disease in patients 12 years and older with recurrent vaso-occlusive crises, using the system to edit the BCL11A gene in hematopoietic stem cells to boost fetal hemoglobin production.
RNA therapeutics harness nucleic acids directly for modulating gene expression without altering the genome, offering rapid deployment for acute conditions. Messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccines encode antigens to stimulate immune responses; the Pfizer-BioNTech COVID-19 vaccine, authorized for emergency use by the FDA on December 11, 2020, demonstrated over 95% efficacy in preventing symptomatic SARS-CoV-2 infection in clinical trials, marking a breakthrough in nucleic acid-based immunization. Small interfering RNA (siRNA) drugs silence specific mRNAs via RNA interference; Patisiran (Onpattro), approved by the FDA in 2018 for polyneuropathy in hereditary transthyretin-mediated amyloidosis, uses lipid nanoparticles to deliver siRNA that degrades mutant TTR mRNA, reducing amyloid deposits and improving neuropathy scores in phase 3 trials. In 2024, the FDA approved imetelstat (Rytelo), an oligonucleotide telomerase inhibitor for adult patients with low- to intermediate-1 risk myelodysplastic syndromes with transfusion-dependent anemia, and olezarsen (Tryngolza), an antisense oligonucleotide as an adjunct to diet to reduce triglycerides in adults with familial chylomicronemia syndrome. In 2025, fitusiran (Qfitlia), an siRNA for prophylaxis in hemophilia A or B with or without inhibitors, and plozasiran (Redemplo), an siRNA to reduce triglycerides in adults with familial chylomicronemia syndrome, received FDA approval.
In synthetic biology, nucleic acids are engineered to construct novel biological systems, expanding beyond natural DNA and RNA. The synthesis of entire genomes enables the creation of minimal cells; in 2010, researchers at the J. Craig Venter Institute chemically synthesized and transplanted a 1.08-megabase Mycoplasma mycoides genome into a recipient cell, producing a self-replicating synthetic bacterium that exhibited controlled gene expression and basic metabolic functions. Extending this, xenonucleic acids (XNAs), artificial analogs with alternative sugar-phosphate backbones, have been developed to support genetic information storage and evolution in vitro, potentially forming the basis for orthogonal life forms resistant to natural enzymes and viruses. Recent advances include enzymatic DNA synthesis techniques that enable the production of longer custom DNA sequences, doubling the size of made-to-order genes as reported in 2025.
Despite these advances, nucleic acid therapeutics and synthetic biology face significant challenges. Off-target effects in CRISPR editing can lead to unintended DNA cleavages at similar sequences, potentially causing genomic instability or oncogenic mutations, as detected in various cell types through methods like GUIDE-seq. Delivery remains a hurdle, particularly for extracellular nucleic acids, where lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) encapsulate RNA to protect it from degradation and facilitate endosomal escape, though issues like liver tropism and immune activation limit tissue specificity. Ethical concerns are prominent in germline editing, where heritable changes could introduce irreversible alterations across generations, raising issues of consent, equity, and unintended societal impacts like eugenics.
