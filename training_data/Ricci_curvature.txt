Ricci curvature

1x
 Ricci curvature, more precisely the Ricci curvature tensor, is a second-order tensor in differential geometry derived as a contraction of the Riemann curvature tensor on a Riemannian manifold, quantifying the average curvature in different directions at a point and influencing the volume growth of geodesic balls. Named after the Italian mathematician Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro, who introduced it alongside Tullio Levi-Civita as part of absolute differential calculus (tensor analysis), the tensor is defined mathematically as $ R_{\mu\nu} = R^\lambda_{\ \mu\lambda\nu} $, where $ R^\lambda_{\ \mu\lambda\nu} $ denotes the components of the Riemann tensor. This object captures intrinsic geometric properties independent of embedding and serves as a trace of the full curvature information, with its scalar trace known as the Ricci scalar.
Developed through Ricci-Curbastro's foundational work on differential forms and curvature starting in the 1880s, the Ricci tensor was systematically presented in the landmark 1900 publication Mthodes de calcul diffrentiel absolu et leurs applications, co-authored with Levi-Civita at the request of Felix Klein, establishing the framework for modern tensor calculus. In general relativity, formulated by Albert Einstein in 1915, the Ricci tensor forms the core of the Einstein field equations, $ G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2} R g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu} $, linking spacetime curvature to matter and energy distribution, where $ G_{\mu\nu} $ is the Einstein tensor. Its vanishing in vacuum solutions describes Ricci-flat spacetimes, such as those in asymptotically flat gravitational wave metrics.
Beyond relativity, the Ricci tensor drives geometric evolution in the Ricci flow, introduced by Richard Hamilton in 1982 as the partial differential equation $ \frac{\partial}{\partial t} g_{ij} = -2 R_{ij} $ for a time-dependent metric $ g_{ij} $, which homogenizes curvature and has applications in topology, notably in Perelman's proof of the Poincar conjecture. Positive Ricci curvature implies volume comparisons akin to spheres, while negative values suggest hyperbolic behavior, aiding classifications of manifolds via Myers' and Bishop-Gromov theorems.

Contraction from Riemann tensor

The Ricci tensor arises as a contraction of the Riemann curvature tensor, which encodes the full information about the curvature of a Riemannian manifold. In a local coordinate basis, the Riemann tensor is denoted by $ R^\rho_{\ \sigma\mu\nu} $, where the indices follow the standard convention for the curvature operator acting on vectors. The Ricci tensor is then defined by contracting the first (upper) index \rho with the third (lower) index \mu:

\mathrm{Ric}_{\mu\nu} = R^\rho_{\ \mu\rho\nu}.

To express the Ricci tensor in fully covariant form, the metric tensor $ g^{\rho\lambda} $ is used to raise the first index of the fully covariant Riemann tensor $ R_{\lambda\mu\rho\nu} = g_{\lambda\sigma} R^\sigma_{\ \mu\rho\nu} $, resulting in
Ric=gR.
\mathrm{Ric}_{\mu\nu} = g^{\rho\lambda} R_{\lambda\mu\rho\nu}.

Here, the contraction pairs the first and third indices of the covariant Riemann tensor, preserving the symmetry Ric=Ric\mathrm{Ric}_{\mu\nu} = \mathrm{Ric}_{\nu\mu}Ric=Ric due to the properties of the Riemann tensor.
This specific contraction was introduced by Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro in 1903 as part of his development of absolute differential calculus, building on earlier work with Tullio Levi-Civita, to obtain a tensor that summarizes key geometric deviations from flat space. The choice reflects both historical and computational motivations: due to the antisymmetry of the Riemann tensor in its last two indices and other symmetries, alternative contractions (such as the first and second indices) vanish identically, making this the unique non-trivial way to produce a symmetric second-order tensor from the Riemann tensor. Computationally, it simplifies calculations in coordinate systems and facilitates links to physical applications, such as in general relativity.
The contraction also connects naturally to the study of submanifolds via the Gauss-Codazzi equations, where contracting the Gauss equation expresses the intrinsic Ricci tensor of a hypersurface in terms of the ambient Riemann tensor's projection and the second fundamental form, highlighting its role in relating extrinsic and intrinsic geometry. This formulation is dimension-independent, applying uniformly to manifolds of any dimension $ n \geq 2 $, as the index contraction does not rely on specific dimensional assumptions beyond the existence of the Riemann tensor.

Relation to scalar curvature

The scalar curvature, denoted $ \mathrm{Scal} $, is obtained by taking the trace of the Ricci tensor with respect to the metric tensor, given by $ \mathrm{Scal} = g^{ij} \mathrm{Ric}_{ij} = \mathrm{trace}_g(\mathrm{Ric}) $. This contraction yields a scalar quantity that summarizes the overall curvature of the manifold at a point, aggregating the directional information encoded in the Ricci tensor.
In an orthonormal frame $ {e_i} $, the scalar curvature simplifies to the sum $ \mathrm{Scal} = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathrm{Ric}(e_i, e_i) $, reflecting the normalization where the metric components are $ g_{ij} = \delta_{ij} $. This expression highlights the scalar curvature as the average Ricci curvature over an orthonormal basis, providing a coordinate-independent measure.
For a hypersurface in a Riemannian manifold, the Gauss equation relates the scalar curvature of the hypersurface to the ambient scalar curvature, the Ricci curvature in the normal direction, the mean curvature $ H $, and the norm of the second fundamental form $ |A|^2 $, specifically $ \mathrm{Scal} = \bar{\mathrm{Scal}} - 2 \bar{\mathrm{Ric}}(\nu, \nu) + H^2 - |A|^2 $, where $ \nu $ is the unit normal. In the special case of a hypersurface in Euclidean space, where the ambient scalar and Ricci curvatures vanish, this reduces to $ \mathrm{Scal} = H^2 - |A|^2 $.
In two-dimensional Riemannian manifolds, the scalar curvature is twice the Gaussian curvature $ K $, so $ \mathrm{Scal} = 2K $, as the Ricci tensor is proportional to the metric and the single independent component of the curvature tensor aligns with the Gaussian curvature. This relation underscores the equivalence of scalar and Gaussian curvatures up to a factor in surfaces, fully determining the intrinsic geometry.

Comparison to sectional curvature

The sectional curvature $ K(\pi) $ at a point $ p $ in a Riemannian manifold measures the intrinsic curvature of the two-dimensional submanifold tangent to a plane $ \pi $ in the tangent space $ T_p M $, generalizing the Gaussian curvature of surfaces. In contrast, the Ricci curvature $ \mathrm{Ric}(X, X) $ for a unit tangent vector $ X \in T_p M $ provides an averaged measure of sectional curvatures over all planes containing the direction of $ X $. Specifically, if $ {e_i}_{i=1}^n $ is an orthonormal basis of $ T_p M $ with $ e_1 = X $, then

This expression shows that the Ricci curvature in the direction $ X $ is the sum (or, equivalently, $ (n-1) $ times the average) of the sectional curvatures of the $ n-1 $ orthogonal planes spanned by $ X $ and the remaining basis vectors, capturing a directional trace of the full Riemann curvature tensor rather than pointwise plane-specific values.
This averaging nature implies specific relationships between uniform sectional curvature and Ricci properties. For instance, if the sectional curvature is constant and equal to $ k $ throughout the manifold, then $ \mathrm{Ric} = (n-1) k , g $, where $ g $ is the metric tensor, making the manifold Einstein with constant Ricci eigenvalue $ (n-1)k $. Conversely, Ricci-flat manifolds (where $ \mathrm{Ric} = 0 $) need not have vanishing sectional curvatures; the zero average allows for compensating positive and negative sectional curvatures in different planes. A prominent example is provided by Calabi-Yau manifolds, which are compact Khler manifolds admitting Ricci-flat metrics by Yau's solution to the Calabi conjecture, yet these metrics generally exhibit non-zero and varying sectional curvatures, including regions of negative curvature that stabilize certain geodesics.
The Ricci curvature thus inherits bounds from sectional curvature magnitudes. For a unit vector $ X $, the inequality $ |\mathrm{Ric}(X, X)| \leq (n-1) \max |K| $ holds, as the absolute value of the sum cannot exceed the number of terms times the maximum absolute sectional curvature. This bound underscores the Ricci tensor's role as a coarser, averaged invariant compared to the more detailed sectional curvature, which can vary independently across planes while still constraining the overall directional averages.

Ricci flow and evolution

The Ricci flow is a partial differential equation that evolves the metric tensor ggg of a Riemannian manifold according to the equation

Applications in Physics

Einstein field equations

In general relativity, the Einstein field equations provide the fundamental relation between the geometry of spacetime and the distribution of matter and energy within it. The Ricci tensor plays a central role in these equations through the construction of the Einstein tensor $ G_{\mu\nu} $, defined as

In the absence of matter, where $ T_{\mu\nu} = 0 $, the equations simplify to $ G_{\mu\nu} = 0 $, which implies $ \mathrm{Ric}_{\mu\nu} = 0 $ and thus Ricci-flat spacetimes. The structure of the Einstein tensor also inherits properties from the geometry of the Ricci tensor via the twice-contracted second Bianchi identity, $ \nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = 0 $, which holds identically for any metric. Substituting the field equations yields the covariant conservation law $ \nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} = 0 $, ensuring consistency between local matter dynamics and spacetime curvature without additional constraints.
Historically, Albert Einstein arrived at these equations in a series of papers presented to the Prussian Academy of Sciences in November 1915, culminating in the final form on November 25, where he explicitly introduced the Ricci tensor-based structure to achieve general covariance. Independently, David Hilbert derived an equivalent variational formulation from the Einstein-Hilbert action in a communication to the Gttingen Royal Society on November 20, 1915, emphasizing the role of the Ricci scalar in the Lagrangian.

Positive Ricci curvature rigidity

Positive Ricci curvature rigidity encompasses several fundamental theorems in Riemannian geometry that characterize complete manifolds achieving extremal conditions under a positive lower bound on the Ricci curvature tensor. These results often imply that the manifold is isometric to a standard space of constant curvature, such as the sphere, highlighting the "rigid" nature of such geometries. As a prerequisite, Myers' theorem establishes that a complete n-dimensional Riemannian manifold with Ricci curvature bounded below by Ric (n-1) has diameter at most .
Cheng's maximal diameter theorem provides a sharp rigidity result for the diameter bound. Specifically, if a complete n-dimensional Riemannian manifold M satisfies Ric_M (n-1) and diam(M) = , then M is isometric to the standard round sphere S^n of constant sectional curvature 1. This theorem, proved using eigenvalue comparison techniques, shows that the sphere is the unique manifold attaining the maximal diameter under the given curvature condition.
Obata's theorem offers another rigidity characterization, this time involving the spectrum of the Laplace-Beltrami operator. If a complete n-dimensional Riemannian manifold M has Ric_M = (n-1)g exactly (where g is the metric tensor) and admits a non-constant function f such that Hess(f) = -f g (the Obata equation), then M is isometric to the standard sphere S^n. This condition on the Hessian equates to f being the first eigenfunction of the Laplacian with eigenvalue n, underscoring the sphere's uniqueness among Einstein manifolds with this normalized Ricci tensor. The theorem relies on the Bochner identity and maximum principle applied to the function.
In the broader context of sequences of manifolds with Ricci curvature bounded below, Gromov-Hausdorff convergence reveals rigidity phenomena, particularly under non-collapsing assumptions. For sequences of n-dimensional Riemannian manifolds with Ric -(n-1) and injectivity radius bounded below (ensuring non-collapsing), the Gromov-Hausdorff limits inherit Ricci curvature bounds and are themselves Riemannian manifolds or Alexandrov spaces with similar properties; when the lower bound is positive and the diameter approaches the Myers upper bound , the limits are isometric to spheres, extending the rigidity of Cheng's and Obata's theorems to limiting cases. This convergence theory, developed through volume and entropy comparisons, prevents pathological collapses and preserves geometric structure.
Beyond the sphere, other symmetric spaces exhibit positive Ricci curvature without achieving full sectional constancy. For instance, the complex projective space \mathbb{CP}^n equipped with the Fubini-Study metric has Ricci curvature Ric = (n+1) g (normalized so that the holomorphic sectional curvature is between 1 and 4), making it Einstein with positive Ricci tensor; similar positivity holds for quaternionic and octonionic projective planes, providing non-trivial examples of rigid positively curved spaces that are quotients of spheres.

Positive values indicate clustering akin to positive continuous Ricci curvature, while negative values suggest spreading.
A simplified variant for graphs, known as the Lin-Lu-Yau curvature, modifies Ollivier's definition to avoid full optimal transport computations by taking the limit as the laziness parameter approaches 1. Specifically, for vertices xxx and yyy,

This provides an explicit, computable formula in terms of graph degrees and neighborhoods, facilitating analysis on large networks. It has been shown to bound the classical Ollivier-Ricci from below and to converge to sectional curvature in the manifold limit.
Curvature bounds in these discrete settings yield implications for network structure, such as expansion properties and connectivity. For instance, graphs with nonnegative Ollivier-Ricci curvature exhibit controlled volume growth and prevent bottlenecks, implying strong connectivity in random geometric graphs approximating manifolds. In applications to complex networks, positive curvature on edges identifies community structures by highlighting intra-cluster clustering versus inter-cluster expansion, as demonstrated in detection algorithms on social and biological networks. Similarly, lower bounds on Lin-Lu-Yau curvature ensure diameter bounds and spectral gaps in the Laplacian, promoting robustness in communication networks.
These discrete curvatures connect to the Bakry-mery theory, which provides Ricci bounds via curvature-dimension inequalities for diffusion semigroups. In the graph context, Lin and Yau established that the Bakry-mery Ricci curvature is bounded below for locally finite graphs, and the Lin-Lu-Yau variant recovers this in the infinitesimal limit, bridging discrete approximations to continuous tensor bounds and enabling limit theorems for graph sequences converging to manifolds.
