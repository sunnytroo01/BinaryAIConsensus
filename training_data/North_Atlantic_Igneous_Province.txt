North Atlantic Igneous Province
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) is a large igneous province (LIP) comprising extensive flood basalts, intrusive complexes, and volcanic sequences emplaced primarily during the Paleocene and Eocene epochs, coinciding with the rifting and continental breakup that formed the North Atlantic Ocean. It represents one of Earth's most voluminous magmatic events, with an estimated erupted volume of 12 million km of basaltic magma, potentially reaching 510 million km when including subsurface intrusions and underplating. The province's formation is closely linked to the separation of Greenland from Eurasia and North America, beginning around 62 Ma and peaking between 6055 Ma, with ongoing activity manifesting today at the Iceland hotspot.
Geographically, the NAIP extends across a broad region from Baffin Island and western Greenland in the northwest, through Iceland and the Faroe Islands, to the British Isles, Norway's continental margin, and the conjugate margins of East Greenland. Key geological features include thick sequences of subaerial and subaqueous flood basalts, such as those in the East Greenland Volcanic Basin (up to 12 km thick), seaward-dipping reflector sequences (SDRs) on the rifted margins, and major dike swarms radiating from central plutonic centers like the Skaergaard Intrusion in East Greenland. These elements are underlain by anomalously thick oceanic crust, reaching 3040 km in places like the Greenland-Iceland-Faroe Ridge, far exceeding typical oceanic crust of 67 km. The province's magmatism transitioned from pre-rift alkaline volcanism to syn-rift tholeiitic basalts and picrites, reflecting evolving mantle dynamics during continental extension.
The NAIP's origin is most commonly attributed to the impingement of a deep-seated mantle plume beneath the lithosphere, initiating ridge-plume interaction that facilitated rapid seafloor spreading rates of up to 4 cm/year post-breakup at ~53 Ma. This model is supported by geochemical signatures, including high-temperature picritic melts and elevated helium isotope ratios (He/He up to 40 Ra) indicative of primordial mantle sources. Alternative hypotheses, such as edge-driven convection or lithospheric delamination, have been proposed but face challenges in explaining the province's volume and timing. Environmentally, the NAIP's massive gas emissions, including CO and SO, are implicated in the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (~55.5 Ma), a period of rapid global warming and biotic turnover, though the exact causal links remain debated. The province also holds economic significance, with its volcanic rocks overlying hydrocarbon-bearing Mesozoic sediments in basins like the Faroe-Shetland region.

Introduction and Extent

Definition and Characteristics

The North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) is classified as a large igneous province (LIP), defined as a regional accumulation of igneous rocks exceeding 100,000 km in volume, primarily mafic in composition, and emplaced over relatively short geological timescales, generally within 1 to 5 million years. The NAIP specifically represents one of the most voluminous LIPs on Earth, with an estimated total magmatic volume of 510 million km, dominated by extrusive and intrusive basaltic rocks associated with rifting processes. This province exemplifies the hallmark LIP trait of high magma production rates, often linked to deep mantle sources, distinguishing it from smaller volcanic systems.
Key characteristics of the NAIP include its strong association with continental breakup along rifted margins, where voluminous magmatism facilitated lithospheric extension and the initiation of seafloor spreading. The province is marked by the dominance of tholeiitic flood basalts, which form thick, laterally extensive plateaus through repeated effusive eruptions, alongside significant intrusive components such as sills and dikes. Eruptions occurred in both subaerial and submarine environments, reflecting the transition from continental to oceanic settings during rifting, with magmas derived predominantly from partial melting of the mantle plume or asthenospheric upwelling.
Within the global context of LIPs, the NAIP shares eruption styles with other continental flood basalt provinces, such as the Deccan Traps, featuring non-explosive, high-volume lava flows that cover vast areas, but it is uniquely tied to volcanic passive margins rather than interior cratonic settings. Its scalecomparable to or exceeding the Deccan Traps' ~12 million km volumeunderscores its role as a type example of plume-rift interactions in LIP formation.

Geographical Coverage

The North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) spans a vast transatlantic region, encompassing both continental and oceanic domains across the northeastern Atlantic margins. Its distribution reflects the initial stages of North Atlantic rifting and seafloor spreading during the Paleogene, with igneous activity linking conjugate margins from Greenland to Europe. The province's extent is marked by a series of volcanic and intrusive complexes that straddle the present-day mid-ocean ridge system, demonstrating its role in facilitating continental breakup.
Onshore exposures of the NAIP are primarily concentrated in East Greenland, the British Isles, the Faroe Islands, and Iceland, which serves as the central hub of ongoing activity. In East Greenland, the main volcanic sequences outcrop from Scoresby Sund northward to Jameson Land, covering approximately 27,500 km of tholeiitic lavas and associated intrusions within the Jameson Land Basin. In the British Isles, significant exposures occur in Scotland's Inner Hebrides, including the islands of Skye and Mull where lava fields and central complexes dominate, and in Northern Ireland's Antrim region, featuring the Antrim Lava Group over approximately 3,900 km. The Faroe Islands host a thick sequence of basaltic lavas forming a subaerial plateau, representing a key link in the province's central axis. Iceland, as the emergent portion of the NAIP, continues this volcanic legacy with active rifting and subaerial eruptions, underscoring the province's dynamic evolution.
Submarine extensions broaden the NAIP's footprint into offshore basins and ridges, including the Vring and Mre marginal highs along the Norwegian margin, where seismic profiles reveal extensive seaward-dipping reflector sequences indicative of voluminous extrusive and intrusive activity. The Rockall Trough further delineates the eastern boundary, with buried volcanic layers and sills extending the province's influence into deeper Atlantic waters. Centrally, the Greenland-Iceland-Faroe Ridge complex forms a prominent submarine feature, spanning 1,150 km and covering 480,000 km of thickened oceanic crust influenced by mantle-derived magmatism. The NAIP flood basalts cover an estimated total area of 1.3 10 km across onshore, nearshore, and offshore domains, highlighting its scale as one of Earth's major large igneous provinces.

Formation and Chronology

Timing of Volcanic Activity

The volcanic activity associated with the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) unfolded over an overall duration of approximately 8-10 million years during the Paleocene and Eocene epochs, characterized by episodic bursts of magmatism rather than continuous eruption. Geochronological studies, primarily utilizing Ar/Ar incremental heating on whole rocks and minerals, alongside U-Pb dating of zircon and baddeleyite, have established this timeline with high precision. These methods reveal that the main eruptive phases spanned from about 62 Ma to 54 Ma, with radiometric ages clustering around key intervals that align with the Paleocene-Eocene boundary at approximately 56 Ma.
The initial pulse of volcanism occurred between ~62 and 58 Ma, predating the onset of seafloor spreading and representing pre-breakup activity across a broad region from Baffin Island to the British Isles. This phase involved widespread extrusion of dominantly basaltic lavas from beneath thick continental lithosphere, with representative ages including 61.9 1.0 Ma for the Vaigat Formation in West Greenland (Ar/Ar) and 60.8 0.5 Ma for the Lower Series Basalts in the Faroe Islands (Ar/Ar). A relative hiatus followed around ~57 Ma, marked by diminished province-wide activity, though localized intrusions persisted in some areas.
The main phase of volcanism intensified between ~56 and 54 Ma, coinciding with the initiation of seafloor spreading and a dramatic increase in melt production ratesrising from less than 200 km/km of rift per million years to over 3000 km/km. This period encompassed the peak activity at 56.1 0.5 Ma, directly correlated with continental breakup between Greenland and Eurasia around 55-56 Ma, as evidenced by magnetic anomaly chron C24r (dated to ~55.9 Ma). U-Pb ages, such as 61.32 0.05 Ma for the Antrim Tardree rhyolite in Northern Ireland, further refine the syn-rift timing, confirming the linkage to the Paleocene-Eocene boundary. By ~50 Ma, eruption rates had declined sharply, with activity becoming restricted primarily to the East Greenland margin.

Proposed Mechanisms

The primary hypothesis for the formation of the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) invokes the impingement of a deep-seated mantle plume, often associated with the Iceland hotspot, on the base of the lithosphere. This model posits that a large plume head, approximately 1,200 km in radius and 100200C hotter than ambient mantle, arrived beneath the lithosphere around 6255 Ma, causing extensive decompression melting as the hot material rose and thinned the overlying lithosphere from ~75 km to ~60 km thickness. The resulting massive melt volumes, estimated at peak production rates of ~2.2 km/yr during the main phase, led to widespread flood basaltic volcanism and facilitated continental breakup. Seismic tomography provides supporting evidence through low-velocity anomalies (e.g., -10% shear wave velocity perturbations at 150 km depth) beneath Iceland and the Greenland-Iceland-Faroe Ridge, indicating persistent hot asthenospheric material consistent with ongoing plume activity. This plume track extends southward to present-day Iceland, sustaining long-term magmatism.
Alternative models focus on lithospheric instabilities without requiring a deep plume. Edge-driven convection proposes that rifting along the proto-North Atlantic margins created thermal contrasts at the edge of thick cratonic lithosphere (e.g., Baltica), driving small-scale convective upwelling of asthenospheric mantle and localized melting. This mechanism explains the NAIP's proximity to cratonic margins and elongated volcanic distribution but struggles to account for the sustained, high-volume melt production observed at Iceland over millions of years. Delamination involves the gravitational instability and sinking of dense lower lithospheric material into the asthenosphere, triggering compensatory upwelling and partial melting; however, this model lacks robust support from NAIP uplift patterns, which show pre-rift doming more consistent with thermal perturbations than delamination-induced subsidence. Both approaches predict lower melt rates (~0.10.5 km/yr) than observed during NAIP peaks and are challenged by geochemical evidence of enriched mantle sources not easily explained by ambient asthenosphere.
Other theories include rift-induced decompression melting, where passive extension alone allows hot asthenosphere to rise and melt without anomalous temperatures, and impact-related triggering, such as the debated influence of the Chicxulub impact ~66 Ma potentially initiating distant plume activity or localized melting. Rift models align with observed uplift timing during extension but underestimate the NAIP's total volume (~6.6 10^6 km) and fail to explain off-axis volcanism. Impact hypotheses, while linking to ejecta-like spherules in Greenland, are considered minor contributors due to energetic constraints and the NAIP's prolonged activity, which extends well beyond any single impact event. Overall, the mantle plume model best integrates the province's scale, geodynamic setting, and persistence, though hybrid scenarios incorporating edge effects remain under discussion.

Petrological and Geochemical Features

Dominant Rock Types

The North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) is dominated by tholeiitic basalts, which form the vast majority of the igneous volume, exceeding 90% in many sequences. These basalts include aphyric varieties with fine-grained, holocrystalline textures and porphyritic types featuring phenocrysts of olivine, plagioclase, or clinopyroxene set in a groundmass of plagioclase, pyroxene, and glass. Subordinate lithologies comprise picrites, which are olivine-rich basalts with high MgO contents (typically 15-25 wt%) resulting from accumulation of mantle-derived olivine, and hawaiites, mildly alkaline basalts transitional between tholeiites and more evolved alkali basalts.
The primary stratigraphic units are extensive flood basalt sequences, attaining thicknesses of up to 10 km, as observed in onshore exposures. These sequences consist of stacked phoehoe and aa lava flows, interbedded with pyroclastic tuffs from explosive eruptions and hyaloclastites formed during subaqueous or ice-contact volcanism. Rare rhyolites and other silicic volcanics constitute less than 5% of the total volume, appearing as localized flows, domes, or tuffs within the predominantly mafic pile.
Texturally, the extrusive basalts display vesicular structures from gas exsolution during eruption, with vesicle sizes varying from fine amygdules to large cavities filled by secondary minerals. Columnar jointing is prevalent in thicker flows, forming polygonal prisms due to contraction during cooling. Intrusively, gabbroic rocks are widespread, including coarse-grained gabbros and layered intrusions that represent crystallized magma chambers or sills emplaced into the crust.
Geochemical variations among these rock types reflect differences in mantle melting depths and degrees tied to plume influence.

Isotopic and Trace Element Signatures

The isotopic compositions of rocks from the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP), particularly basalts and picrites, reveal signatures consistent with derivation from an ocean island basalt (OIB)-like mantle plume source incorporating recycled enriched mantle 1 (EM1)-type components, such as ancient altered oceanic crust. Initial 87Sr/86Sr ratios typically range from 0.703 to 0.705, reflecting moderate enrichment relative to depleted mid-ocean ridge basalt (MORB) sources (0.70250.7030), while 143Nd/144Nd ratios vary between 0.5128 and 0.5129 (corresponding to Nd values of +5 to +8). These ratios indicate a heterogeneous plume mantle with EM1 influences, as evidenced by correlations between elevated 87Sr/86Sr and slightly lower 143Nd/144Nd in enriched-type picrites from Baffin Island and West Greenland, suggesting mixing of depleted and recycled lithospheric materials within the proto-Iceland hotspot. Additional Os and O isotope data support this, with 187Os/188Os ratios of 0.1260.132 in picrites indicating low-radiogenic, primitive upper mantle contributions, and 18O values around 5 in olivines showing minimal supracrustal alteration.
Trace element patterns in NAIP magmas further highlight plume-derived origins, with high TiO2 contents exceeding 2 wt% in many tholeiitic basalts and picrites, distinguishing them from low-Ti continental flood basalts and pointing to elevated mantle temperatures facilitating garnet-poor melting. Normalized multi-element diagrams exhibit positive Nb-Ta anomalies relative to La and Th, a hallmark of non-subduction-related plume sources lacking the negative high field strength element (HFSE) depletions typical of arc magmas; for instance, Nb/La ratios often exceed 1 in uncontaminated samples from the Faroe Islands and East Greenland. Platinum-group element (PGE) abundances in picrites display fractionated patterns with IPGE (Os, Ir, Ru) enrichment over PPGE (Pt, Pd, Rh), consistent with high-degree melting of sulfide-undersaturated mantle and minimal crustal sulfide assimilation, alongside low 187Os/188Os ratios underscoring the primitive nature of the source.
Geochemical modeling of these signatures supports high-degree partial melting (2030%) of the mantle plume at relatively shallow depths (50100 km, within the spinel stability field), as inferred from elevated MgO (>15 wt%) in picrites and low incompatible trace element abundances indicating efficient melt extraction. This melting regime aligns with plume upwelling beneath thinning lithosphere during continental breakup. However, upper sequences in volcanic piles, such as those in the Vring Plateau and British Tertiary Igneous Province, show increasing crustal contamination, evidenced by elevated 87Sr/86Sr (>0.710) and negative Nb-Ta anomalies from assimilation of Precambrian granulites and amphibolites, which overprint the primary plume signals in evolved lavas.

Regional Geology

East Greenland

The East Greenland segment constitutes the largest onshore exposure of the North Atlantic Igneous Province, spanning from approximately 68N to 70N along the continental margin and featuring voluminous flood basalts, intrusive complexes, and associated structures formed during Paleogene rifting. This region preserves thick sequences of volcanic rocks that record early plume-related magmatism prior to continental breakup.
Key exposures include the Scoresby Sund basalts, a vast plateau of Lower Tertiary lavas covering about 40,000 km with an average thickness of 1.5 km, though locally exceeding 67 km in subaerial sections. Further south, the Blosseville Kyst region hosts significant intrusive bodies, including layered gabbros and syenites, emplaced coeval with the extrusive volcanism and contributing to the magmatic underplating observed in seismic profiles. The total igneous volume in East Greenland is substantial, with syn-breakup lavas alone estimated at around 250,000 km, underscoring the segment's role as a major contributor to the NAIP's erupted material.
Stratigraphically, the basalts are divided into lower and upper formations separated by a hiatus marked by interbasaltic red beds, lignite horizons, and paleosols indicative of subaerial weathering and vegetation development during volcanic quiescence. The lower formations, including the Magga Dan, Milne Land, and Geikie Plateau Formations, represent an initial pulse of tholeiitic flood volcanism erupted from northwest-sourced fissures. The upper formations, such as the Rmer Fjord and Skrnterne Formations, overlie these with a renewed pulse of lavas sourced from southeast, near the proto-Atlantic margin. Radiometric dating constrains the overall activity to 6253 Ma, with two main pulses: an early pre-breakup phase around 6258 Ma and a later syn-breakup phase from 5653 Ma coinciding with seafloor spreading initiation.
Unique features include the Kangerlussuaq Alkaline Complex, a ~1,000 km syenitic center emplaced over ~8 million years from ~55 to 45 Ma as a series of satellite intrusions and the main body, reflecting prolonged post-breakup plume influence along the Iceland hotspot track. Additionally, a prominent coast-parallel dyke swarm, extending ~250 km along the outermost margin south of Scoresby Sund, fed the flood basalts and records subvertical magma ascent during rifting, with dykes up to several meters thick and en echelon arrangements. These elements highlight East Greenland's high-latitude continental margin volcanism as a critical archive of NAIP evolution.

British Isles

The manifestations of the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) in the British Isles form the British Paleogene Igneous Province (BPIP), encompassing extensive Paleogene volcanic and intrusive rocks across western Scotland and northeastern Ireland. This region records a pulse of magmatism that produced thick basalt sequences and associated intrusions, reflecting plume-related melting during early North Atlantic rifting.
In Scotland, the Inner Hebrides preserve significant lava piles, including the Skye Lava Group on the Isle of Skye and the Mull Lava Group on the Isle of Mull, with the latter reaching a preserved thickness of approximately 1.8 km. These dominantly basaltic successions, comprising compound pahoehoe and aa flows, erupted from fissure vents and were accompanied by pyroclastic deposits in places. Radiometric dating places the main volcanic phase at 6158 Ma, based on ArAr and UPb analyses of lavas and associated intrusives. Further inland, the Midland Valley features widespread sills, such as the widespread Palaeogene sill complex, which intruded into Carboniferous sedimentary rocks and contributed substantially to the subsurface igneous load.
Central complexes like Ardnamurchan exemplify the intrusive style, with nested ring dykes, cone sheets, and gabbroic plugs forming the eroded roots of stratovolcanoes active around 6055 Ma. These structures highlight a progression from flood basalt extrusion to more localized, subvolcanic plutonism.
In northeastern Ireland, the Antrim Plateau hosts the Antrim Lava Group, a basalt succession up to 0.8 km thick that blankets Mesozoic and older strata over an area of roughly 3,500 km. The group divides into lower and upper formations, with the lower dated to 6264 Ma and the upper to about 60 Ma via ArAr and UPb methods. Flows here exhibit columnar jointing, as seen at the Giant's Causeway, and were fed by NESW-trending dykes. The Mourne Mountains contain granitic plutons intruded at 5651 Ma, representing a later, more evolved phase with associated ring complexes and minor acidic volcanics.
Overall, the BPIP is dominated by mafic intrusions such as dykes, sills, and layered gabbros, alongside the extrusive basalts and subordinate alkaline magmas in central complexes like Skye. This activity temporally aligns with continental breakup along the North Atlantic margins.

Faroe Islands

The Faroe Islands form a prominent subaerial exposure of the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP), where the volcanic succession, termed the Faroe Islands Basalt Group (FIBG), records extensive Paleogene flood basalt activity associated with the initial rifting of the North Atlantic. This sequence, predominantly composed of tholeiitic basalts, reaches a total thickness of approximately 5-6 km onshore and is characterized by a progression from lower plateau basalts through interbedded sedimentary units to upper lava flows, reflecting episodic volcanism interspersed with periods of erosion and sedimentation.
The stratigraphy begins with the Beinisvr Formation, comprising thick sequences of subaerial compound and simple basalt flows up to 3.25 km thick, representing the initial phase of voluminous eruptions. Overlying this is the Malinstindur Formation, a 1.05-1.35 km thick unit of subaerial lava flows interbedded with tuffs and coal-bearing sediments from the adjacent Prestfjall Formation, marking a transitional phase with volcaniclastic deposits. The upper part consists of the Enni Formation, exceeding 900 m in thickness, dominated by compound and simple flows that cap the exposed sequence, with partial erosion at the top. The overall depositional environment shifts from shallow marine influences in the lowermost units to predominantly subaerial conditions, evidenced by flow morphologies and sedimentary interbeds indicating emergence during rifting.
Radiometric dating constrains the main volcanic activity to 59-54 Ma, spanning the late Paleocene to early Eocene, with Ar-Ar and U-Pb ages confirming the timing of major eruptions and brief hiatuses. A notable feature is the Prestfjall Formation's thick coal seams, up to several meters, which developed on paleosols during eruptive pauses, preserving evidence of lush Paleogene vegetation and soil formation in a warm, humid climate. Additionally, extensive sill complexes of dolerite intrude throughout the FIBG, particularly in the lower sections, forming layered intrusions that enhance the province's magmatic plumbing system and contribute to the region's structural complexity.

Iceland

Iceland represents the modern, active core of the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP), where ongoing mantle plume activity intersects the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, sustaining subaerial volcanism and crustal thickening in an oceanic setting. Exposures in Iceland reveal extensive Tertiary basalts, primarily tholeiitic flood lavas from the Paleogene, that underlie younger Quaternary volcanic sequences, forming the foundational stratigraphic pile of the island. These basalts achieve a total thickness exceeding 10 km, as estimated from seismic and field observations, with additional seaward-dipping reflectors (SDRs) in the surrounding oceanic crust representing subaerially deposited flows that extend the magmatic record offshore.
The main phase of NAIP volcanism in Iceland occurred between approximately 55 and 52 Ma, coinciding with continental breakup and initial seafloor spreading, as evidenced by radiometric dating of basalt flows and associated intrusions. This intense Paleogene activity transitioned into persistent hotspot-driven magmatism, which continues to the present day and accounts for Iceland's emergence as a subaerial plateau above sea level.
Unique to Iceland within the NAIP is its position at a triple junction where spreading ridges interact with the plume, facilitating ridge-centered volcanism characterized by subglacial and fissure eruptions that build hyaloclastite ridges and extensive lava fields. Current volume addition from this activity is approximately 0.01 km/yr, reflecting the sustained but reduced magmatic flux compared to the initial NAIP pulse. This plume influence drives the excess melt production observed in Iceland's thickened crust.

Igneous Landforms and Structures

Extrusive Features

The extrusive features of the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) are dominated by extensive flood basalt sequences that form vast plateaus and stepped lava fields, interspersed with localized pyroclastic deposits and vent structures associated with fissure and central volcanism during the Paleogene period. These surface manifestations reflect high-volume effusive eruptions primarily between 62 and 54 Ma, covering areas exceeding 1 million km across Greenland, the British Isles, the Faroe Islands, and Iceland. The basaltic lavas, often tholeiitic, accumulated in thick piles that preserve evidence of prolonged subaerial and submarine activity, with regional variations in flow morphology and depositional environments.
Flood basalt plateaus constitute the most prominent extrusive landforms, characterized by stacked, laterally extensive lava flows that create stepped terrains. In East Greenland, the province encompasses approximately 65,000 km of basalts exceeding 6 km in thickness near the coast, with the main series (erupted 5654 Ma) divided into formations such as the Geikie Plateau, featuring evolved tholeiites and occasional picrites that form broad, plateau-like expanses. Similar vast fields occur in the Antrim region of Northern Ireland, where Paleogene lavas build stepped plateaus with compound and simple flows, while the Faroe Islands host the Faroe Island Basalt Group, up to 6.6 km thick over ~120,000 km. Flow morphologies include pahoehoe (smooth, ropy surfaces) and 'a' (blocky, rubbly) types, as seen in the Mull lava field of the British Isles, where compound pahoehoe lobes and banded flows indicate Hawaiian-style effusive activity from low-viscosity magmas. These plateaus often exhibit entablature and colonnade jointing, reflecting cooling in thick flow lobes up to tens of meters high.
Pyroclastic deposits, though subordinate to effusive products, occur as intercalated tuffs and localized cones, primarily from phreatomagmatic interactions in early volcanic phases. In East Greenland, tuff beds 14 m thick, totaling ~50 m, contain vitric basaltic clasts, armoured lapilli, and accidental lithics from underlying sediments, indicating explosive eruptions influenced by groundwater or shallow water. Tuff cones, such as the 60 m-high Carrickarade in Antrim, Ireland, surround diatreme-like vents up to 275 m across, formed by steam-driven explosions. Hyaloclastite ridges and deltas, prominent in submarine settings, include wedges up to 300 m thick in East Greenland's lower volcanics, comprising fragmented pillow lavas and breccias from quench fragmentation, while similar features up to 778 m thick appear in the Faroe Islands' subsurface. Ignimbrites are rare but noted in silicic centers, with overall pyroclastic volumes suggesting limited but impactful explosive events that released volatiles into the atmosphere.
Vent structures primarily comprise fissure swarms and subordinate central volcanoes, channeling magma to the surface during peak activity. Fissure swarms, often aligned with regional faults, fed extensive flows; for example, in Mull's British Paleogene Igneous Province, a >5 km-long NESW trending system includes dykes exploiting Caledonian weaknesses, producing spatter ramparts >5 m thick with airfall tephra, bombs, and agglutinates near vents like Calgary Bay. Central volcanoes, though more evolved in Iceland's Neogene extensions, have Paleogene precursors such as the Mull Central Complex, where nested structures indicate focused magmatism, but fissure-dominated eruptions prevailed across the province. These vents, typically 15 km across, preserve proximal deposits like scoria and spatter, highlighting the transition from linear to point-source volcanism.

Intrusive Complexes

The intrusive complexes of the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) form a critical subsurface framework, comprising extensive networks of dykes, sills, and plutonic bodies that facilitated magma transport and storage during the province's Paleogene development. These intrusions, primarily mafic to felsic in composition, are distributed across onshore and offshore regions including East Greenland, the British Isles, the Faroe Islands, and surrounding basins, with total igneous volumes estimated at 510 10^6 km. They are closely associated with continental rifting that preceded North Atlantic opening.
Dyke swarms represent regional-scale feeder systems that channeled magma from depth to surface eruptions, often oriented parallel to the rift margins. A prominent example is the East Greenland coast-parallel dyke swarm, exposed along approximately 250 km of the southeastern Greenland coast south of the Iceland hotspot track, with individual dykes up to several meters wide and dated to around 5557 Ma. These swarms exhibit bilateral symmetry relative to the rift axis and played a key role in accommodating extension during breakup. In the British-Irish Paleogene Igneous Province, curvilinear dyke swarms radiate from central complexes like those on Skye and Mull, spanning tens of kilometers and intruded between 60 and 50 Ma.
Plutonic bodies, including layered gabbros and associated felsic intrusions, constitute large subsurface reservoirs where magma crystallized in place, with individual complexes reaching volumes of several hundred to thousands of cubic kilometers. The Skaergaard intrusion in East Greenland exemplifies layered gabbros, a ~280 km body emplaced at ~55 Ma into basaltic lavas, featuring rhythmic layering from fractional crystallization and exposed by uplift and erosion. Other significant plutons include the Rum layered intrusion in the Inner Hebrides, Scotland, comprising ultramafic to gabbroic units intruded around 60 Ma, and granitic bodies such as the Kangerlussuaq syenite-granite complex in East Greenland, dated to 4751 Ma. These structures often exhibit cryptic and modal layering, reflecting density-driven settling of crystals in magma chambers.
Sill complexes dominate the intrusive architecture in sedimentary basins, forming thick, tabular sheets that exploited stratigraphic weaknesses and induced widespread contact metamorphism. The Faroe-Shetland Sill Complex offshore northwest Britain covers a minimum area of ~22,500 km within Mesozoic and Cenozoic strata, with sills up to hundreds of meters thick intruded during 6255 Ma, leading to thermal alteration of host rocks including kerogen maturation. Similar complexes occur in the Rockall Basin and West Greenland margins, where sills totaling thousands of km contributed to basin uplift and hydrocarbon system modification through heating.

Environmental Impacts

Climate and Ocean Effects

The emplacement of sills within the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) is proposed to have triggered substantial thermogenic degassing of carbon, primarily as methane and CO, from organic-rich sedimentary rocks, with estimates indicating a release of 10,00013,000 Gt of carbon overall. This massive carbon flux, occurring through hydrothermal vent systems associated with sill intrusions, is calculated to have peaked at 0.20.5 Gt C yr during the PaleoceneEocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), potentially contributing to rapid global warming of 45C, consistent with broader PETM temperature excursions of 58C. NAIP-related carbon emissions, whether primarily from volcanic degassing (C around -11) or thermogenic sources (more variable, potentially lighter), have been linked to the negative carbon isotope excursion (CIE) observed at the PETM onset, though the dominant source remains debated.
These emissions profoundly altered ocean chemistry, driving widespread surface ocean acidification through elevated pCO levels that reduced carbonate ion concentrations and promoted deep-sea carbonate dissolution. In the North Atlantic and North Sea regions, NAIP activity led to rapid onset of anoxic and euxinic conditions within approximately 300 years of the CIE, as evidenced by elevated molybdenum-to-titanium ratios, heavy molybdenum isotope values (/Mo of 1.271.99), and small pyrite framboid sizes (35 m). Concurrent eutrophication, marked by increased total organic carbon and productivity, further exacerbated oxygen depletion in these semi-restricted basins, restricted by NAIP-related tectonic uplift and flood basalt extrusion. The C excursions in marine records directly reflect this carbon perturbation, with NAIP-derived carbon signatures helping distinguish volcanic contributions from lighter sedimentary sources, though interpretations vary.
Geochemical modeling simulations suggest that NAIP emissions were sufficient to contribute to PETM climate change initiation, with Monte Carlo approaches predicting cumulative releases of ~2,750 Gt C over several millennia, closely matching observed warming and CIE patterns. Recent studies (as of 2025) indicate millennial-scale thermogenic CO release from NAIP intrusions preceded the PETM onset, potentially priming the system for the hyperthermal. A key negative feedback mechanism involved enhanced silicate weathering of exposed NAIP basalts and volcanic ash, which increased carbon sequestration through carbonate formation and organic matter burial, drawing down atmospheric CO and contributing to PETM termination over ~140 kyr. Lithium isotope records from post-PETM sediments confirm this intensified weathering, with detrital Li shifts indicating a persistent carbon sink that stabilized early Eocene climate.

Biological Consequences

The emplacement of the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) is widely linked to the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), a rapid global warming event approximately 56 million years ago that triggered significant biological disruptions, including the end-Paleocene benthic extinction event (BEE). This event resulted in the loss of approximately 50% of benthic foraminiferal species worldwide, primarily due to deep-sea acidification, hypoxia, and temperature increases exceeding 5C, as evidenced by foraminiferal assemblage analyses from deep-sea cores. NAIP emissions are hypothesized to have intensified these conditions, though the exact causal rolewhether as trigger or prolongerremains debated, contributing to a selective extinction favoring opportunistic, infaunal species while decimating diverse, specialized taxa.
On land, NAIP-related warming and environmental perturbations drove notable floral shifts in Arctic regions, including Greenland, where palynological records document a transition from conifer-dominated forests to angiosperm-rich vegetation with increased fire activity. In nearby Arctic Canada, post-eruptive spore spikesreaching up to 70% of the palynoflora from ferns, lycophytes, and bryophytesindicate rapid opportunistic recolonization following habitat disturbance by lava flows and ash deposition. These changes reflect heightened seasonality and precipitation variability, promoting wetter, more fire-prone ecosystems that altered carbon cycling and supported evolutionary innovations in plant diversity. Faunal responses included intercontinental migrations, such as the influx of Eurasian mammals into North America, which spurred taxonomic turnover and body size dwarfing as adaptations to hyperthermal stress.
In marine environments, NAIP-induced ocean warming prompted planktonic blooms dominated by stress-tolerant dinoflagellates, such as Apectodinium augustum, which expanded poleward as subtropical taxa migrated to avoid equatorial heat. Calcareous plankton, including coccolithophores and planktonic foraminifera, experienced notable declines in diversity and abundance due to carbonate undersaturation and thermal stress, disrupting primary productivity and food webs. Recovery patterns post-PETM were protracted, spanning 100,000-200,000 years, with assemblages shifting toward acidification-resistant species and gradual restoration of pre-event diversity levels in deeper waters.

Research History

Initial Observations

In the early 19th century, geological surveys in Scotland began to systematically document the extensive basalt formations associated with the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP). Robert Jameson, during his explorations of the Shetland Islands in 17981799, described the columnar basalts and associated volcanic features in detail, contributing to the growing recognition of these rocks as products of igneous activity rather than sedimentary deposits. Similarly, in Ireland, Edward Hull, as director of the Geological Survey of Ireland, conducted surveys in the 1870s and 1880s that mapped the Antrim plateau basalts, including the iconic Giant's Causeway, and affirmed their volcanic origin through observations of lava flows and intrusive structures. These efforts resolved lingering debates from the basalt controversy, establishing the plateau basalts as evidence of ancient flood volcanism across the British Isles.
Explorations in Greenland during the 1820s marked initial Danish efforts to map the island's volcanic terrains. Expeditions led by figures such as Wilhelm Graah in 18281830 along the southeastern coast noted extensive plateau-like distributions amid fjords and mountains, though systematic geological analysis remained limited at the time. By the mid-20th century, Danish geologist Asger Noe-Nygaard advanced these observations through fieldwork in the 1960s and 1970s, identifying vast sequences of flood basalts in East Greenland, particularly between Shannon and Scoresby Sund, where layered lava flows and sills formed thick volcanic piles indicative of large-scale Paleogene eruptions.
Key milestones in the 1920s and 1930s connected these regional features into a broader framework. L.R. Wager, during the 1930 British Arctic Air Route Expedition (BAARE) and subsequent expeditions in the 1930s, correlated basalt sequences across East Greenland with those in the British Isles and Iceland, highlighting similarities in composition and stratigraphy that suggested a unified igneous event. These descriptive phases laid the groundwork for understanding NAIP landforms, such as expansive basalt plateaus and dyke swarms, without yet employing modern analytical methods.

Contemporary Investigations

Contemporary investigations into the North Atlantic Igneous Province (NAIP) have advanced significantly since the 1970s, building on foundational plume hypotheses to incorporate geophysical imaging, precise geochronology, and modeling of volcanic impacts. The plume model gained prominence with W. Jason Morgan's 1971 proposal of mantle convection plumes originating from the lower mantle as the source of hotspot volcanism, which was subsequently applied to explain the voluminous Early Paleogene magmatism of the NAIP as resulting from a plume interacting with thinning lithosphere during continental breakup. This framework posits that the Iceland hotspot, linked to the NAIP, represents a persistent plume conduit, influencing both continental and oceanic volcanism. Seismic tomography studies in the 2000s and 2010s have further imaged the deep roots of this plume, revealing low-velocity anomalies extending from the lower mantle beneath Iceland and the surrounding NAIP region, supporting a multi-resolution model of two deep-mantle sources contributing to Paleocene doming and volcanism. For instance, waveform tomography of the Arctic and North Atlantic has delineated upper-mantle structures with unprecedented detail, tracing plume-related thermal anomalies that align with the province's magmatic extent.
Geochronological efforts from the 1980s through the 2000s refined the timing and phases of NAIP volcanism using Ar/Ar and U-Pb dating methods, establishing a main eruptive period from approximately 62 Ma to 54 Ma with distinct pulses tied to rifting stages. These techniques, applied to lavas and plutons across Greenland, the British Isles, and the Faroe Islands, revealed rapid eruption sequences, such as the Skye lavas dated precisely to resolve short-lived events within the broader province. Compilations of these data highlight three primary phases: pre-breakup sills and dikes around 62 Ma, peak flood basalt extrusion near 58-56 Ma, and post-breakup activity tapering to 54 Ma, providing constraints on plume-lithosphere interactions. Drilling projects in the 2010s, including those targeting the Faroe Islands Basalt Group and adjacent margins, recovered core samples that corroborated these ages and enabled geochemical analysis of magmatic evolution, though continental efforts like the International Continental Scientific Drilling Program (ICDP) focused more broadly on NAIP-related sites such as the Vring Plateau via affiliated ocean drilling.
In the 2020s, research has emphasized basin-scale modeling to quantify NAIP volcanic emissions and their climatic role, alongside satellite gravity data to delineate offshore extents. Numerical basin models integrate seismic and geochemical data to estimate gas release, suggesting that subaerial and submarine eruptions contributed significant CO and SO fluxes during the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum, with pulsed emissions amplifying global warming. Gravity anomaly mapping using GOCE satellite data has revealed crustal thickness variations and mantle density structures in the northeast Atlantic, extending the NAIP's inferred footprint beyond exposed regions to include hidden volcanic margins up to 3-4 km thick. These findings fuel ongoing debates between traditional plume models and alternatives like edge-driven convection, where lateral lithospheric contrasts at craton edges trigger small-scale upwellings to explain asymmetric magmatism without invoking deep plumes. Proponents of edge-driven convection argue it better accounts for the NAIP's prolonged, episodic nature, while plume advocates cite tomographic evidence for deep sourcing.
