 Montauban is a commune in southwestern France and the prefecture of the Tarn-et-Garonne department in the Occitanie region. Founded in October 1144 by Count Alphonse Jourdain of Toulouse as one of the earliest bastide towns, it was established to consolidate defenses and attract settlers with charters of liberties. With a population of 61,919 inhabitants as of 2021, the city features a historic center characterized by distinctive pink brick architecture, arcaded squares like the Place Nationale, and landmarks such as the Notre-Dame Cathedral and the Ingres Museum, which preserves works by the renowned local painter Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres. During the 16th and 17th centuries, Montauban emerged as a Protestant stronghold, resisting royal forces until its surrender following a prolonged siege in 1629 under Cardinal Richelieu's command. Today, it serves as an administrative and cultural hub in an agricultural hinterland, noted for its urban harmony and heritage sites that reflect medieval planning principles.

Geography

Location and topography

Montauban is located in the Tarn-et-Garonne department of the Occitanie region in southwestern France, where it functions as the prefecture. The city occupies the confluence of the Tarn and Tescou rivers, which deposit sediments forming the local alluvial soils. It sits approximately 51 kilometers north of Toulouse as measured by straight-line distance.
The topography features a flat alluvial plain, with city elevations ranging from 70 to 120 meters above sea level and an average around 100 meters. This low-lying terrain, shaped by river sedimentation, supports extensive agriculture in the surrounding areas, including fruit orchards and grain fields.
The urban layout centers on a rectilinear grid pattern characteristic of bastide planning, with straight streets radiating from the arcaded Place Nationale marketplace. Beyond the built-up area, the countryside transitions to open farmlands, while northeastward proximity to the Aveyron gorges introduces steeper, incised valley features within Tarn-et-Garonne.

Climate

Montauban experiences a humid subtropical climate (Kppen Cfa), characterized by mild winters, hot summers, and precipitation distributed unevenly throughout the year with Atlantic influences moderating extremes. The annual mean temperature is approximately 13.8C, with January averages around 5.9C and July reaching 22.4C based on long-term records from the local Mto-France station. 
Winter lows typically fall to 2C in January, supporting habitability without severe frost risks, while summer highs average 28C in July, conducive to outdoor activities but occasionally exacerbating drought stress on agriculture such as local viticulture. Annual precipitation totals about 749 mm, concentrated in autumn and spring months, with July as the driest at around 34 mm, reflecting a Mediterranean influence that limits summer convective storms.

History

Founding and medieval development

Montauban was established in October 1144 by Alphonse Jourdain, Count of Toulouse, as one of the earliest bastides in southern France, designed as a fortified market town to bolster regional control and economic activity. The initiative drew settlers primarily from the nearby village of Montauriol, which had developed around an abbey but faced constraints under abbatial rule. Alphonse Jourdain issued a charter granting inhabitants extensive freedoms, including rights to trade, self-governance, and protection from arbitrary feudal dues, which incentivized rapid settlement and commerce. This planned settlement adopted a rectangular grid layout centered on a public square, typical of bastides, to optimize defense, taxation, and market functions.
The town's name originates from the Latin Mons Albanus, denoting a "white mountain" or hill, likely alluding to the light-colored soil or the silvery foliage of willows prevalent on the site along the Tarn River. Positioned at a strategic river crossing, Montauban facilitated trade in agricultural goods, wine, and early crafts, leveraging its location to connect Toulouse's hinterlands with broader Languedoc networks. The charter's emphasis on fair markets and low tolls spurred economic vitality, with the bastide's walls and gates providing security amid feudal rivalries.
Medieval expansion included robust fortifications enclosing the core settlement, while the Pont-Vieux, a key defensive and transport link, was erected between 1311 and 1355 across the Tarn on 14 arches with flanking towers. This bridge, rising 23 meters above the river, integrated military architecture with infrastructure, underscoring the town's role in controlling riverine access during the 14th century. By the late Middle Ages, Montauban had evolved into a prosperous hub, its bastide design enabling efficient administration and growth under comital oversight.

Wars of Religion and sieges

During the French Wars of Religion (15621598), Montauban developed into a prominent Huguenot stronghold in southern France, where Calvinism attracted widespread adherence among the urban population and elites by the 1560s, driven by the spread of Reformed theology and local resistance to Catholic ecclesiastical authority. The city's commercial networks and position along trade routes facilitated Protestant consolidation, positioning it as a regional center for Reformed worship and governance amid intermittent civil strife.
Under the Edict of Nantes (1598), Montauban secured designation as one of eight Protestant places de sret, fortified enclaves granted to Huguenots for self-defense and worship. Renewed conflict erupted in 1621 when Huguenot leaders, led by the Duke of Rohan, rebelled against royal policies perceived as eroding these guarantees, prompting Louis XIII to besiege Montauban from late August to early November. Royal forces, numbering around 25,000 under Constable Charles de Luynes, conducted a three-month artillery bombardment but encountered fierce resistance, compounded by autumn rains, supply shortages, and epidemics that decimated the besiegers. The siege collapsed following Luynes's death from fever on 15 December 1621, with the army withdrawing amid substantial losses, preserving Montauban's Protestant control temporarily.
The strategic isolation of Huguenot holdouts intensified after the royal capture of La Rochelle in October 1628, exhausting Montauban's resources and alliances. On 21 August 1629, the city capitulated to advancing Catholic troops without a prolonged second siege, as defenders recognized the futility of further opposition. Surrender conditions, aligned with the broader Peace of Als (September 1629), mandated demolition of ramparts and Protestant temples, abolition of military privileges, and royal oversight of governance, though private worship persisted under residual Edict protections. These outcomes inflicted severe demographic strain, with war-related mortality, disease, famine, and emigration reducing the population and disrupting trade-dependent economy; remnants of pre-surrender fortifications persist as archaeological traces of the era's defenses. The erosion of autonomous status accelerated Catholic demographic recovery, culminating in Protestant subordination after the Edict's revocation in 1685.

Enlightenment to Industrial Revolution

In the 18th century, Montauban's economy drew prosperity from regional agricultural trade, including grain and wine exports, alongside a longstanding textile sector that processed local fibers into cloth. This commerce supported a population of around 27,000 inhabitants by the late 1700s, reflecting steady but unremarkable growth amid southern France's agrarian base. Intellectual activity emerged with the establishment of a provincial academy in 1753, fostering discussions on sciences and arts in line with broader Enlightenment currents, though the city produced no major philosophes.
During the French Revolution, Montauban became a significant Jacobin stronghold, where local clubs advocated radical reforms and resisted federalist sentiments, contributing to the national push against monarchy through petitions and alignments with Parisian Montagnards. The painter Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres was born there on August 29, 1780, to a family of artisans, embodying the era's blend of classical influences and revolutionary upheaval.
The early 19th century saw infrastructural advances with the Canal de Montech's completion in 1844, linking Montauban to Toulouse and facilitating barge traffic for goods like timber and foodstuffs over its 11-kilometer length with five locks. This complemented the later Canal latral la Garonne, operational from 1856, which extended navigable routes to the Atlantic and spurred trade volumes. Industrial development stayed limited, centered on small-scale textile mills weaving wool and cotton alongside tanneries processing hides from local livestock, with mechanization lagging due to abundant cheap labor and poor coal access; population figures hovered near 30,000 until these networks reversed stagnation.

World Wars and occupation

During World War I, the department of Tarn-et-Garonne, with Montauban as its prefecture, experienced severe demographic and economic impacts from mobilization and casualties. Approximately 5,223 men from the department were killed, equating to roughly 3% of its pre-war population of 182,500. This loss rate exceeded the national average, consistent with higher mortality in southwestern France where one in six mobilized men perished, compared to the French overall rate of about 16.7%. Agricultural production, a cornerstone of the local economy, faced strain from labor shortages due to mobilization and state requisitions of foodstuffs and resources to support the war effort.
In World War II, Montauban, located in the unoccupied Vichy zone until November 1942, initially saw local authorities comply with the regime's policies, including anti-Semitic measures enacted from October 1940 onward. Following the German occupation of the zone libre in November 1942, resistance activities intensified, with the formation of unified resistance movements (M.U.R.) occurring in Montauban in early August 1943, linking disparate groups for sabotage and intelligence operations. Deportations of Jews from the region took place under Vichy collaboration and subsequent direct Nazi control, though specific local figures for Montauban remain limited in documentation; broader Midi toulousain records indicate over 6,000 Jewish deportations from the Toulouse military region, including Tarn-et-Garonne. Resistance efforts shifted toward armed action from 1942 to 1944, involving political opposition evolving into military engagements against occupiers.
The city was liberated on August 19, 1944, primarily by local resistance fighters who confronted retreating German forces, resulting in skirmishes but limited structural damage to Montauban itself. Economic recovery was delayed by wartime disruptions, including occupation-era requisitions and the human costs of resistance and deportations, though the absence of major destruction facilitated quicker postwar stabilization compared to heavily bombed areas.

Post-1945 reconstruction and modern era

Following the liberation of Montauban on August 19, 1944, the city faced postwar economic challenges, including a rural exodus that depopulated surrounding agricultural areas as workers sought opportunities in larger urban centers like Toulouse, contributing to delayed industrialization and relative stagnation through the 1950s. This exodus reflected broader French trends of mechanization reducing farm labor needs, leaving Montauban as a diminished commercial hub overshadowed by Toulouse's expansion in aviation and manufacturing.
From the 1960s to the 1980s, proximity to Toulouse's burgeoning aerospace sector provided indirect stimulus, with some spillover employment and infrastructure links, while local agri-food processingtied to Tarn-et-Garonne's fruit and vegetable productionsupported modest recovery, though growth remained uneven compared to metropolitan France.
On March 15, 2012, Mohammed Merah, a 23-year-old of Algerian descent raised in a troubled, low-integration family environment, executed three French soldiers in separate shootings near a military barracks in Montauban, using a scooter and handgun; these acts preceded attacks in Toulouse that killed four at a Jewish school, totaling seven deaths and exposing jihadist radicalization risks fueled by Islamist ideologies and socioeconomic isolation in immigrant communities. Merah, who pledged allegiance to al-Qaeda and cited grievances over French Afghan policy and Jewish targets, was killed by police after a siege, prompting scrutiny of intelligence failures and unassimilated enclaves from North African migration waves.
By 2022, Montauban's population had reached 62,487, reflecting steady urban inflow and agglomeration expansion to over 81,000, amid efforts to modernize housing and transport via national and regional investments, though specific EU cohesion funds for Occitanie have aided peripheral renewal without reversing structural dependencies on Toulouse.

Administration and politics

Local government structure

Montauban functions as the prefecture of the Tarn-et-Garonne department, hosting the prefect who oversees state services and coordination with central government policies. The commune's local governance centers on a municipal council, comprising 53 elected members serving six-year terms, which in turn elects the mayor responsible for executive functions including policy implementation and administration. Following the 2020 municipal elections, the council exhibited a center-right orientation, with Les Rpublicains securing the mayoralty under Brigitte Barges, who was re-elected with 54.45% in the second round before transitioning leadership to Marie-Claude Berly from the same majority in August 2024.
The city integrates into the Grand Montauban agglomeration community, encompassing 12 communes and serving about 81,400 residents across 281 km as of 2022, with an extension planned for 2025 adding further territory. This intercommunal entity assumes responsibilities for shared services like waste collection, water supply, and economic promotion, allowing for economies of scale beyond individual commune capacities. The municipal council handles core local powers such as zoning via the local urban plan (PLU), public utilities management, and cultural facilities, while the agglomeration coordinates broader infrastructure.
The 2024 city budget reached 139 million euros, with 81 million allocated to operations and 58 million to investments, primarily financed through local taxes (e.g., property and residence taxes), state grants, and user fees. Following the 1982 decentralization laws, which devolved competencies in urbanism, transport, and social aid to communes, Montauban's framework has emphasized localized control, though fiscal constraints from national equalization mechanisms limit full autonomy. Empirical indicators of governance efficiency include waste management, where the agglomeration's initiativessuch as buried container systems and biodchet collectionhave reduced household waste by targeted percentages and secured a 2024 award for exemplary practices, demonstrating effective resource allocation amid decentralization's emphasis on service proximity.

Economy

Historical economic foundations

Montauban, founded in 1144 as a bastide town by Count Alphonse Jourdain of Toulouse, emerged as a hub for regional commerce, leveraging its strategic location to host trade fairs and markets that facilitated the exchange of agricultural goods and textiles. These early economic activities were rooted in the surrounding fertile lands, supporting wool production and processing typical of medieval southern France, with the town's grid layout designed to accommodate merchants and periodic fairs. By the 14th century, commercial prosperity peaked, as evidenced by the Bonis brothers, a prominent merchant-banking family whose operationsdocumented in surviving ledgers from 13451359 and account books from 13471359spanned loans, trade in commodities, and finance on a scale comparable to larger urban centers, underscoring Montauban's role in broader European networks despite its provincial status.
From the 17th to 19th centuries, Montauban's economy centered on agricultural exports, particularly wine from local vineyards including Cabernet Franc and Tannat varieties, and grain, with the city serving as a key depot in regional grain trade networks. The Tarn River provided critical navigation for these goods, enabling downstream transport to the Garonne and beyond, with historical mills along its banks harnessing water power for processing and prosperous riverine commerce sustaining recovery after disruptions like the 1629 siege during the Huguenot wars.
This agricultural foundation demonstrated resilience, transitioning in the 19th century toward mechanized farming practices amid broader French innovations, maintaining the primary sector as the enduring economic backbone through wars and market shifts rather than reliance on transient speculative ventures. Archival records highlight consistent contributions from farming, with river infrastructure aiding export stability and underscoring causal links between geographic assets and long-term patterns over boom cycles.

Culture and heritage

Architectural landmarks

Montauban originated as a bastide town founded in 1144 by Count Alphonse Jourdain, featuring a rigid grid layout of orthogonal streets designed for defensive enclosure and efficient market organization, with blocks aligned around a central square to optimize surveillance and trade flow. This chessboard pattern, characteristic of southwestern French bastides, prioritized functional urban planning over organic growth, enclosing the settlement within protective ramparts of which partial remnants survive despite later demolitions for ring roads.
The Place Nationale, reconstructed in the 17th century under royal architect Bernard Campmartin after the 1629 siege, forms a rectangular arcaded square emblematic of local pink brick construction, where double-tiered porticos with pilasters and ribbed vaults supported ground-level commerce while elevating upper residences, enhancing airflow and mitigating fire risks through separated wooden elements.
The Cathdrale Notre-Dame-de-l'Assomption, built from 1692 to 1739 atop the city's highest elevation, exemplifies neoclassical engineering with its pilaster-framed facade, triglyph-metope friezes, and vertical barrel vaults asserting structural dominance, designed sequentially by Franois d'Orbay, Jules Hardouin-Mansart, and Robert de Cotte to project Catholic authority post-Protestant control.
The glise Saint-Jacques, originating in the 13th century with a chur extension circa 1500, employs meridional Gothic techniques in its single-nave structure vaulted on ogives, narrow lancet windows for light control, and polygonal apse for acoustic reinforcement, later fortified with machicolations during the Wars of Religion to serve dual civilian and defensive roles until 1739.
The Pont Vieux, engineered between 1311 and 1335 under Philip IV's patronage, stretches 205 meters across the Tarn with seven pointed-arch masonry piers reinforced against scour, facilitating regional linkage as stipulated in the 1144 charter while its flat deck and spouts managed medieval traffic and floodwaters.

Museums and cultural institutions

The Muse Ingres Bourdelle serves as Montauban's premier cultural institution, housing extensive collections from two native artists: painter Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres and sculptor Antoine Bourdelle. Ingres, born in Montauban in 1780, bequeathed his personal collection in 1867, including approximately 4,500 drawings and 44 paintings, which form the core of the museum's holdings. The institution also preserves around 100 Bourdelle drawings and 70 sculptures, representing the second-largest such collection after Paris's Muse Bourdelle. These works, alongside 14th- to 16th-century artifacts, underscore the museum's role in preserving regional artistic and historical heritage, including artifacts linked to Montauban's Protestant history during the Wars of Religion, when the city functioned as a key Huguenot stronghold.
Following a 13 million renovation completed in 2019, the museum expanded its exhibition space and restored over 500 works, aiming to enhance public access and double annual attendance from pre-renovation levels of about 40,000 visitors to 80,000. Visitor figures from earlier years, such as 50,481 in 2007, indicate steady interest in its unique holdings. The museum's documentation center further supports scholarly research with catalogs and resources on its collections.
Complementing the museum, the annual Dess'Ingres festival, launched in 2022, celebrates drawing in homage to Ingres through workshops, exhibitions, and artist villages, fostering public engagement with visual arts. Other institutions, such as the Muse des Jouets with its collection of historical toys and the Musum Victor Brun focusing on natural history, contribute to Montauban's cultural landscape, though they draw smaller audiences compared to the Ingres Bourdelle. These entities collectively preserve local artifacts, including potential historical ties to the Wars of Religion era, emphasizing empirical documentation over narrative embellishment.

These events reflect enduring Catholic customs in a city with a Protestant past, though participation has declined with national trends showing reduced religious practice.
Efforts to revive the Occitan language, historically spoken in the region, include cultural associations promoting its use in local songs and signage, yet proficiency remains low at under 5% among residents, constrained by French dominance and generational transmission gaps.

Montauban is served by Montauban-Ville-Bourbon railway station, which lies on the BordeauxSte line and accommodates TGV high-speed services connecting to Bordeaux in the northwest and Toulouse in the southeast, with journey times of approximately 1 hour to each city under current infrastructure. The station handles intercity and regional TER Occitanie trains, facilitating connectivity within the Occitanie region, though the existing conventional rail limits top speeds to around 200 km/h, constraining overall efficiency compared to dedicated LGV lines; a new LGV BordeauxToulouse line, set for completion in 2032, is projected to add millions of passengers annually across the corridor by reducing travel times further.
The A62 motorway, part of the European route E72, provides primary road access, running northwest from Montauban toward Bordeaux over 2x2 lanes and southeast to Toulouse with widened 2x3 lanes to accommodate higher volumes, enabling efficient freight and passenger movement with smooth traffic flow due to its design and maintenance. This infrastructure causally supports regional logistics by linking Montauban to major ports and industrial hubs, though tolls and occasional congestion near Toulouse can impact cost-effectiveness for short-haul trips.
Public bus services are operated by the Transports du Grand Montauban (TGM) network, comprising 8 regular lines traversing the urban area and connecting key hubs like the railway station, with additional peri-urban routes and on-demand options promoting accessibility for local commuters. The Tarn River offers limited navigation via the Canal de Montech to Montauban, primarily for recreational boating rather than freight, as commercial traffic has declined due to shallower depths and lock constraints, rendering it inefficient for bulk goods compared to road or rail alternatives.
Air travel relies on proximity to Toulouse-Blagnac Airport, located 53 km southeast, with bus and train connections taking about 1 hour, providing access to international flights while the lack of a local airport avoids redundant infrastructure costs but increases dependence on regional hubs. Cycling infrastructure has expanded since the 2000s through greenways like the Voie Verte along the Canal des Deux Mers and new rail-trail conversions, such as the planned MontaubanOrgueil path, enhancing urban mobility and reducing short-trip car reliance via dedicated, low-maintenance paths integrated with rural heritage routes.

Urban development and utilities

The Grand Montauban agglomeration has prioritized wastewater infrastructure upgrades, exemplified by the 15 million methanization unit at the Verdi treatment plant, inaugurated on September 18, 2025, in partnership with the Saur group. This facility anaerobically digests sludge to produce biogas, enabling on-site renewable energy generation and reducing external energy dependency for treatment processes, with operations commencing in 2024. While promoted as advancing territorial energy transition, the net sustainability gains of biomethane from wastewaterpotentially offsetting fossil fuel use but involving methane handling and incomplete carbon recapturerequire rigorous lifecycle assessments beyond promotional claims, as empirical data on long-term emission reductions remains preliminary.
Water and sanitation utilities are delegated to Saur subsidiaries, with Saur France operating the Verdi plant and O d'alba handling collective sanitation services, supporting a network serving over 60,000 inhabitants amid demographic pressures. These arrangements include 40 million in broader investments since the early 2020s to modernize two major stations, enhancing resilience to droughts and floods while accommodating a 1.5% annual population growth rate. Cost-benefit evaluations indicate improved operational efficiency, though dependency on private operators raises questions about long-term public oversight and tariff stability without transparent performance metrics.
Flood defense infrastructure, critical given the Tarn River's history of centennial floods exceeding 5,000 m/s, relies on a 20-km dike system, but a January 2025 prefectural danger study exposed vulnerabilities including erosion risks and potential breaches affecting 10,000 residents, with no full protective upgrades implemented post-2020 events. Mandated responses include population alerts by April 2025 and crisis drills, yet the absence of quantified reinforcement costs or probabilistic risk models underscores gaps in proactive engineering over reactive planning.
Urban development integrates utility expansions with housing, as seen in the ZAC Bas Pays zone where over 100,000 m of earthworks provide flood-resistant terracing for new residential units, aligning with agglomeration growth targets of 500 annual housing starts. Smart city pilots, including connected sensors for waste and lighting since 2022 and the 2023 "Ville Internet" label, aim to optimize energy use, but territorial data reveal persistent per capita consumption around 5,000 kWh electricity annually, with limited verifiable reductions from digital tools amid the 2008 climate plan's GHG targets.

Notable people

Artists and intellectuals

Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres (17801867), born in Montauban to a family of artisans, emerged as a key Neoclassical painter emphasizing linear precision and idealized anatomy in portraits and historical scenes. His early training occurred locally under his father, a sculptor, painter, and musician, before he advanced to Paris in 1797 to study with Jacques-Louis David and secure the Prix de Rome in 1801.
Antoine Bourdelle (18611929), likewise born in Montauban, developed as a sculptor fusing archaic monumentality with expressive dynamism, producing early commissions such as the Monument aux Morts unveiled there in 1894 amid local controversy over its unconventional form. After apprenticing in the city and studying in Toulouse, he relocated to Paris in 1893 to assist Auguste Rodin, while maintaining ties through works like The Great Warrior of Montauban (1898).
Montauban's cultural milieu also supported Occitan literary efforts, with Bourdelle associating with writers Antonin Perbosc (18611944) and Prosper Estieu, who standardized the language's orthography and advanced its poetic traditions via publications and the Flibrige movement. Additionally, intellectuals like jazz historian Hugues Panassi (19121974), a native, contributed analytically to the genre's study, founding the Hot Club de France in 1932 and defending its African-American roots against commercialization in works such as Le Jazz chaud (1934).

Political and military figures

Jean-Pierre Doumerc (17671847), a native of Montauban, served as a general of division in the French army during the Napoleonic era. Enlisting in 1784, he participated in campaigns of the French Revolutionary Wars, advancing to command cavalry regiments by 1805. Doumerc led the 5th Cuirassier Division at the Battle of Waterloo on June 18, 1815, where his forces executed a critical charge against British squares before he sustained a severe thigh wound from grapeshot, contributing to the French retreat. Post-war, he inspected cavalry under the Restoration and Bourbon regimes until his retirement in 1835.
Arnaud Simeon (17851870), born in Montauban, attained the rank of brigadier-general in the French Army amid the Napoleonic Wars. His service included infantry and staff roles in southern France, though detailed records of specific engagements remain limited compared to higher-profile commanders. Simeon's career reflected the regional contributions to France's military efforts during the early 19th century expansion and defense.
Montauban's strategic position during the Wars of Religion positioned it as a Protestant stronghold, enduring a prolonged siege from July to August 1629 by royal forces under Cardinal Richelieu, culminating in capitulation on August 21 and the imposition of Catholicism. Local military leadership, including captains like those under Henri de La Tour d'Auvergne, coordinated defenses, but the outcome enforced royal control, reshaping the town's governance and demographics through forced conversions and emigration.
In World War II, Montauban hosted active Resistance networks, facilitating intelligence and sabotage against German occupation, with the local maquis aiding Allied advances. The city was liberated on August 19, 1944, by combined Resistance fighters and Free French forces, minimizing destruction relative to other regional centers; a dedicated museum preserves artifacts from these operations, underscoring civilian-military collaboration despite risks of reprisal.

Other prominent residents

mile Borel (18711956), a foundational French mathematician renowned for advancing measure theory, probability, and the Borel conjecture in set theory, resided in Montauban during his early education after entering the local Lyce at age 11 as a recognized child prodigy. His time in the city marked the beginning of contributions that influenced modern analysis and foundational work in topology, including the development of Borel sets essential to Lebesgue measure.
In sports, particularly rugby union, Montauban has nurtured talents through its historic club, US Montauban, which has competed in top French leagues and produced players achieving professional success. The city's facilities and tradition support a range of athletic pursuits, from amateur to elite levels, underscoring its role as a regional hub for physical recreation and competition.
