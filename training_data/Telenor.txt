 Telenor ASA is a Norwegian majority state-owned multinational telecommunications company headquartered in Fornebu, near Oslo, Norway. Founded in 1855 as Telegrafverket, a state-owned telegraph service, it evolved into a modern telecom operator and was renamed Telenor in 1995 following partial privatization while retaining majority government ownership of approximately 54%. With over 170 years of history, Telenor provides a wide range of services including mobile and fixed-line telephony, broadband internet, television broadcasting, data communications, and digital solutions across eight markets: Norway, Sweden, Denmark, Finland in the Nordics, and Bangladesh, Pakistan, Thailand, and Malaysia in Asia. As of 2024, the company serves 207 million mobile customers and employs around 11,000 people globally, generating annual revenue of approximately $7.5 billion.
Telenor's operations are divided into regional segments, with strong incumbency in the Nordics where it leads in mobile and fixed broadband markets, and significant presence in Asia through key investments like Grameenphone in Bangladesh, True Corporation in Thailand, CelcomDigi in Malaysia, and Telenor Pakistan. The company has been pivotal in advancing digital infrastructure, from early telegraph services to pioneering 5G deployments, and emphasizes sustainable connectivity and customer trust in its strategy. Under CEO Benedicte Schilbred Fasmer, who assumed the role in December 2024, Telenor focuses on organic growth, strategic partnerships such as with Vodafone, and expanding fiber and IoT solutions amid competitive pressures in mobile data and spectrum costs. In recent years, it reported solid Q3 2025 performance with service revenues of NOK 16.3 billion and adjusted EBITDA of NOK 9.5 billion, driven by Nordic expansions despite Asian headwinds.

Overview

Corporate Profile

Telenor ASA, originally founded in 1855 as Telegrafverket, serves as Norway's primary telecommunications provider and has grown into a multinational corporation headquartered in Fornebu, Norway. The company employs approximately 11,000 people as of 2024, reflecting its streamlined operations following strategic divestitures. In 2024, Telenor reported annual revenues of NOK 79.93 billion, with organic service revenue growth of 2.1% in Q1 2025 and continued 2-3% growth through Q3, driven by expansions in mobile and broadband services.
As a leader in the telecommunications sector, Telenor focuses on mobile communications, fixed broadband, television services, and related digital solutions, serving 207 million mobile customers across eight markets: Norway, Sweden, Denmark, Finland in the Nordics, and Bangladesh, Pakistan, Thailand, and Malaysia in Asia as of 2024. Its market capitalization stood at approximately NOK 205 billion as of November 2025, underscoring its position as a key player in global connectivity.
Telenor's mission has evolved from its origins as a state-owned monopoly managing Norway's telegraph and telephone networks to a modern purpose of "empowering societies" by "connecting you to what matters most," emphasizing innovative digital services and responsible business practices across its international footprint.

Ownership and Governance

Telenor ASA is majority owned by the Norwegian Ministry of Trade, Industry and Fisheries, which holds 53.97% of the company's shares as of June 2025. This state ownership reflects Telenor's origins as a public entity, with partial privatization initiated in 2000 through successive share offerings to the public and institutional investors. Following its initial public offering in 2001, Telenor has been listed on the Oslo Stock Exchange under the ticker symbol TEL, enabling broader shareholder participation while maintaining significant government influence.
The company's executive leadership is headed by President and CEO Benedicte Schilbred Fasmer, who assumed the role in December 2024, succeeding Sigve Brekke after his nearly decade-long tenure since 2015. The Board of Directors, comprising ten members including three employee representatives as of May 2025, is chaired by Jens Petter Olsen, re-elected for a two-year term at the Corporate Assembly meeting. Key board committees include the Audit Committee, responsible for financial oversight; the Compensation and HR Committee, handling executive remuneration and personnel policies; and the Sustainability Committee, focused on environmental, social, and governance (ESG) integration across operations.
Telenor's corporate governance framework adheres to the Norwegian Public Limited Liability Companies Act and aligns with the Norwegian Code of Practice for Corporate Governance, emphasizing transparency, accountability, and equal treatment of shareholders. The board oversees strategic direction, risk management, and ethical standards, with a dedicated focus on sustainability through annual reporting and committee oversight. In recent shareholder activities, the Annual General Meeting on May 21, 2025, approved a total dividend of NOK 9.60 per share for the 2024 financial year, payable in two tranches of NOK 5.00 and NOK 4.60.

History

Early Development (Telegraph and Telephone)

Telenor traces its origins to 1855, when the Norwegian government established the state-owned Telegrafverket as the national telegraph administration to manage and expand telegraph services across the country. This entity was created to facilitate rapid communication in a geographically challenging nation, beginning with the construction of Norway's first telegraph line connecting Christiania (now Oslo) and Drammen, which opened on January 1, 1855, and spanned 56 kilometers. By 1857, telegraph infrastructure had become a national priority, with lines extending to key coastal and inland areas, including challenging terrains like the Saltfjellet mountains in the 1860s, where telegraph poles were erected to link remote regions. As a state monopoly, Telegrafverket controlled all telegraph operations, ensuring standardized development and integration with postal services under the broader Norwegian Postal and Telegraph Administration framework.
The transition to telephone services marked a significant evolution for Telegrafverket, with the first official telephone exchange opening in Christiania in 1880, enabling local voice communication through manual switchboards operated by trained staff. This innovation quickly gained traction, as telephones were installed in businesses and public offices, with the service expanding to other major cities like Bergen and Trondheim by the mid-1880s. By 1900, telephone networks had achieved nationwide coverage, albeit unevenly due to Norway's rugged landscape, connecting urban centers and rural districts through a combination of overhead lines and early underground cables. Throughout this period, Telegrafverket maintained its monopoly status, overseeing all installations, tariffs, and maintenance as part of the integrated postal-telegraph system, which prioritized public utility over private enterprise.
Key technological advancements in the 1920s propelled the telephone system's efficiency, including the introduction of automatic switching to reduce reliance on human operators. Scandinavia's first automatic telephone exchange was installed in Skien in 1920, followed by the opening of the Frogner exchange in Oslo in 1921, which served 6,000 subscribers using rotary dial technology imported from Western Electric. These step-by-step automatic systems gradually replaced manual ones, with full automation of the national network completing by 1985, though the 1920s installations laid the groundwork for scalable growth.
World War II severely disrupted Norway's communication infrastructure during the German occupation from 1940 to 1945, with sabotage, bombing, and resource shortages damaging lines, exchanges, and equipment across the country. Post-war reconstruction efforts by Telegrafverket, supported by government funding and international aid, focused on rapid restoration, rebuilding damaged networks and expanding capacity to meet surging demand in the late 1940s and 1950s. By the early 1950s, the telephone system had not only recovered but begun modernization, incorporating improved cabling and rural extensions, solidifying Telegrafverket's role as the unchallenged provider until deregulation in the 1990s.

Deregulation and Privatization

The deregulation of Norway's telecommunications sector began in the early 1990s, influenced by the European Economic Area (EEA) Agreement, which aligned Norwegian policies with EU directives on market liberalization. In 1994, the state-owned Televerket was restructured as a public limited company named Norwegian Telecom, marking the initial step toward opening the market to competition. This reform was part of a phased approach to dismantle the long-standing monopoly, with further liberalization occurring through 1998.
By 1995, the company underwent a significant reorganization and rebranding to Telenor AS, separating it from broader postal services and positioning it as a dedicated telecommunications entity fully owned by the Norwegian government. The monopoly on fixed-line services officially ended on January 1, 1998, allowing private operators to enter the market and fostering increased competition in voice, data, and infrastructure services. This full deregulation transformed Telenor from a protected state utility into a competitive player, prompting investments in digital infrastructure to maintain market leadership.
Privatization efforts accelerated in the late 1990s to enhance Telenor's efficiency and global competitiveness. In December 2000, Telenor conducted a partial initial public offering (IPO) on the Oslo Stock Exchange, reducing state ownership from 100% to 54% and raising capital for expansion. This marked the rebranding to Telenor Group, emphasizing its evolving role as an international telecommunications provider. The IPO, the largest in Nordic history at the time, enabled Telenor to fund technological upgrades while retaining majority government control.
Parallel to these reforms, Telenor advanced its mobile services, launching the Nordic Mobile Telephone (NMT) network in 1981 as the world's first fully automatic cellular system, which supported roaming across Nordic countries. In 1993, Telenor introduced the digital Global System for Mobile Communications (GSM) network in Norway, upgrading from analog to digital technology and laying the groundwork for modern mobile broadband. These early mobile initiatives, combined with deregulation, positioned Telenor to capitalize on the booming demand for wireless services amid the shift to competitive markets.

International Expansion

Telenor's initial foray into international markets began in the 1990s within Scandinavia, starting with Denmark where it entered through the establishment of Sonofon in 1991 as a mobile operator, later acquiring a controlling stake in 2000 for approximately $1.67 billion to strengthen its position as the country's second-largest mobile provider. In Sweden, Telenor initiated mobile operations in the late 1990s and expanded significantly by acquiring Vodafone Sweden in 2005, rebranding it as Telenor Sverige the following year to consolidate its Nordic footprint. These early ventures capitalized on regional deregulation, allowing Telenor to build a foundation in neighboring markets with established telecom infrastructures. Telenor also pursued initial expansions into other European markets, including Russia in 1994 and countries like Greece, Ireland, Germany, and Austria in 1997, though many of these operations were later divested.
The 2000s marked a major push into Asia, driven by high-growth potential in emerging economies. Telenor entered Bangladesh in 1997 via a joint venture with Grameen Telecom to form Grameenphone, which became the country's largest mobile operator and a model for rural connectivity. This success spurred further expansion, including an initial investment in Malaysia's DiGi in 1999 and increasing its stake to 61% in 2001, entry into Thailand in 2000 through a partnership that evolved into dtac, and the launch of operations in Pakistan in 2005, where it rapidly grew to serve millions in underserved areas. In India, Telenor formed Uninor as a joint venture with Unitech in 2006, achieving full ownership in 2012 amid regulatory challenges, before exiting the market in 2017 through a merger with Bharti Airtel to focus on more viable operations.
Subsequent key acquisitions and mergers reinforced Telenor's global strategy, particularly in Asia and the Nordics. In 2019, Telenor acquired a 54% stake in Finland's DNA for 1.5 billion, marking its entry into the Finnish market and enhancing Nordic consolidation by integrating fixed and mobile services. In Asia, Telenor completed the merger of its Thai subsidiary dtac with True Corporation in March 2023, creating the country's largest telecom operator with over 50 million subscribers and significant synergies. Similarly, in 2022, Telenor and Axiata merged Digi and Celcom to form CelcomDigi, Malaysia's leading telco, with both parents holding equal 33.1% stakes to drive digital infrastructure growth.
This expansion reflects a strategic shift toward Asia as a core growth engine, alongside Nordic stability, with Asian operations contributing 29% of group revenues in 2024 while recent mergers position the region for accelerated market share and efficiency gains. By 2025, Telenor continues to prioritize Asian investments for long-term value creation, balancing them with consolidation efforts in the Nordics to optimize its international portfolio.

Operations

Nordic Operations

Telenor's Nordic operations encompass its fully owned subsidiaries in Norway, Sweden, Denmark, and Finland, forming the core of its mature European footprint with a focus on mobile, fixed broadband, and emerging 5G services. As of 2025, these markets generate stable revenues through high penetration rates and infrastructure investments, contributing significantly to the group's overall EBITDA growth of 12.5% in Q2 2025, driven by service revenue expansion of 3.7%. The region benefits from a competitive landscape regulated by national authorities emphasizing spectrum allocation and consumer protection, where Telenor contends with incumbents like Telia and Tele2 amid ongoing consolidations and fiber expansions.
In Norway, Telenor maintains 100% ownership of its operations, positioning it as the country's leading telecommunications provider with the largest share of mobile subscriptions since 2019. The company holds a dominant position in mobile services, supported by its best overall mobile network performance in the first half of 2025, including top overall mobile download speeds of 183.12 Mbps and median 5G download speeds of 255.4 Mbps. For broadband, Telenor leads the market, bolstered by its July 2025 acquisition of GlobalConnect's consumer fiber division, which serves 140,000 customers and elevates its fiber subscriber share from 22% to 29%, enhancing fixed-line competitiveness. 5G rollout has achieved near-nationwide coverage by 2025, aligning with earlier plans for full accessibility, while regulatory oversight from the Norwegian Communications Authority ensures fair competition in a duopolistic environment dominated by Telenor and Telia.
Telenor Sverige, the wholly owned Swedish subsidiary, operates as a key player in a highly competitive market shared with Telia, Tele2, and Tre, focusing on integrated mobile and fixed services. In 2025, it contributes to Nordic mobile subscriber growth, adding thousands of postpaid users amid flat top-line revenue but 7.5% EBITDA improvement from cost efficiencies. Mobile market dynamics feature intense rivalry, with Telenor tying for second in 5G coverage experience scores of 3.84 points, while broadband efforts emphasize fiber transformation. Through the Net4Mobility joint venture with Tele2, Telenor has activated 5G across 90% of Sweden's landmass and 99.9% of the population by September 2025, matching 4G extents and supported by licenses valid until year-end. The Swedish Post and Telecom Authority regulates spectrum auctions and wholesale access, fostering innovation in a market projected to reach USD 7.29 billion in MNO revenues for 2025.
In Denmark, Telenor Denmark remains an independent wholly owned entity following the abandonment of a 2015 merger attempt with Telia due to EU regulatory opposition, operating in a consolidated market now influenced by Telia's 2023 divestiture to Norlys. As of 2025, it ranks as a top mobile operator, securing second place in consistent quality with an 88% score, just behind Telia, and contributing to 3.2% Nordic mobile service revenue growth in Q3. The company benefits from Denmark's leading 5G availability of 83.9% in Q2 2025, driven by low-band spectrum deployments, in a regulatory framework overseen by the Danish Energy Agency that promotes nationwide coverage. Competition remains fierce among four main MNOs, with the market valued at USD 4.14 billion in 2025, emphasizing high-speed mobile and fixed broadband penetration.
Telenor's Finnish operations are conducted through DNA, acquired to full 100% ownership in 2019, making it the third-largest mobile operator with approximately 30% market share in a trio dominated by Elisa and Telia. In 2025, DNA drives Nordic growth with 9.7% EBITDA rise in Q2 and 1% revenue increase to EUR 276 million in Q3, fueled by mobile postpaid additions and a EUR 120 million fiber investment plan targeting all customer housing companies by 2028. It shares second place in global mobile download speeds at 17.9-18.5 Mbps, reflecting strong 5G integration in Finland's mature, digitally advanced market with 100% 4G coverage. The Finnish Transport and Communications Agency regulates competition, emphasizing 5G and future 6G developments in a USD 4.5 billion telecom sector.

Asian Operations

Telenor's Asian operations form a cornerstone of its global portfolio, encompassing high-growth markets in Bangladesh, Pakistan, Thailand, and Malaysia. These regions account for a significant share of the group's overall revenue, with Asia contributing approximately 25% of Telenor's total service revenues as of Q3 2025 through robust service growth and digital service expansions. In the third quarter of 2025, the Asia cluster reported service revenues of NOK 4,163 million, marking a 4.4% year-over-year increase, driven by data usage and enterprise solutions despite macroeconomic pressures.
In Bangladesh, Telenor holds a 55.8% stake in Grameenphone, the country's leading mobile operator with approximately 85.6 million subscribers and a market share of around 45% as of Q3 2025. Grameenphone has focused on 5G rollout, launching services to enhance connectivity and support digital inclusion, with rising 5G traffic contributing to a 1.4% revenue increase to BDT 40.1 billion in Q3 2025. However, operations face regulatory challenges, including high spectrum renewal costs projected for 2026more than double the Asia-Pacific medianand ongoing macroeconomic headwinds that pressured EBITDA margins.
Telenor maintains 100% ownership of its operations in Pakistan, serving over 42 million subscribers and holding the position as the third-largest mobile player with a market share of approximately 22% as of August 2025. The unit has seen strong performance in digital services, particularly mobile financial services like Easypaisa, which bolstered 15.1% service revenue growth to NOK 994 million in Q3 2025. Regulatory delays in the proposed sale to Pakistan Telecommunication Company Limited (PTCL), approved by the Competition Commission of Pakistan but potentially extending into 2026, have contributed to subscriber base contraction and heightened competitive pressures from price wars and voice-to-data shifts. 5G preparations are advancing, with a planned launch in June 2025 to capitalize on spectrum auctions and improve network capabilities.
Following the 2023 merger of its dtac subsidiary with True Corporation, Telenor holds a 30.3% stake in the combined entity, Thailand's largest mobile operator with about 52% market share and over 49 million subscribers. The merger has strengthened True's position, enabling a nationwide 5G network covering 93% of the population and partnerships with Huawei for advanced infrastructure deployment. In Q3 2025, True reported service revenues of THB 41.3 billion, with EBITDA up 8% year-over-year, reflecting synergies in cost efficiencies and 5G-driven data growth.
In Malaysia, Telenor co-owns CelcomDigi with a 33.1% stake alongside Axiata, making it the nation's top mobile operator by subscriber base and market share. CelcomDigi's postpaid subscribers reached 5.84 million in Q1 2025, with overall revenues growing 2.3% year-over-year in Q2 to support profitable expansion. 5G deployments face challenges from a dual-network structure and escalating costs, prompting a NOK 530 million adjustment to Telenor's share of results in Q3 2025 and calls for restructuring to ensure sustainability. Despite these hurdles, CelcomDigi's focus on enterprise 5G and cost efficiencies positions it for long-term growth in Malaysia's digital economy.

Core Services and Technologies

Telenor's core services revolve around mobile telecommunications, providing voice and data connectivity through extensive 4G and 5G networks across its operational markets. The company serves approximately 207 million mobile subscribers, delivering reliable voice services and high-speed data plans that support streaming, remote work, and mobile broadband usage. In key regions like Norway, Telenor has pioneered 5G fixed wireless access (FWA) to complement traditional mobile services, offering speeds up to 1 Gbps in urban areas while expanding coverage to rural zones.
Fixed broadband forms another pillar of Telenor's offerings, with a strong emphasis on fiber optic infrastructure to deliver ultra-high-speed internet. In Norway, Telenor provides fiber-to-the-premises (FTTP) services capable of 1 Gbps download speeds to households and businesses, supported by ongoing rollouts that aim to phase out legacy copper networks by 2025. To bolster this, Telenor acquired GlobalConnect's consumer fiber business in July 2025 for NOK 6 billion (approximately $595 million), increasing its fiber subscription market share from 22% to 29% and passing over 431,000 homes with fiber connectivity. These investments enable symmetric gigabit speeds and low-latency connections, essential for modern digital demands.
For enterprise customers, Telenor offers integrated solutions including cloud services and cybersecurity to support business operations and digital transformation. Through a partnership with Google Cloud announced in 2024, Telenor provides AI-driven data analytics platforms and scalable cloud infrastructure, enabling enterprises to process large datasets securely and efficiently. In cybersecurity, Telenor Cyberdefence, established in 2024, delivers cloud-based managed security services such as 24/7 Security Operations Center (SOC) monitoring, incident response, and advisory for Nordic businesses facing rising cyber threats. Consumer-facing digital tools, like the MyTelenor app, allow users to manage accounts, check balances, purchase data bundles, and access services via a unified mobile portal, enhancing self-service across markets.
Telenor commits substantial resources to network infrastructure, with capital expenditures supporting 5G densification and fiber expansion; in recent quarters, these investments have driven EBITDA growth while preparing for future technologies. The company actively participates in spectrum auctions to secure bandwidth for enhanced services, notably benefiting from Thailand's 2025 auction outcomes that aligned with its strategic goals for mobile capacity. These efforts ensure robust, future-proof networks tailored to regional needs, such as high-density urban 5G in Asia and fiber-dominant broadband in the Nordics.

Research and Innovation

Telenor Research

Telenor Research was established in 1967 as the research institute of the Norwegian Telegraph Administration (Televerket), initially aimed at advancing telecommunications technologies and attracting top talent to the organization. Over the decades, it has evolved from applied research in fixed telephony and early mobile networks to broader digital innovation, contributing to key developments such as the Nordic Mobile Telephone (NMT) system, GSM standards, SMS, and the MPEG-4 video compression standard used in platforms like YouTube. This evolution reflects a shift toward integrating business insights with scientific inquiry to address emerging challenges in connectivity and digital services.
Headquartered in Fornebu, Norway, Telenor Research maintains significant facilities in Trondheim, including the Telenor-NTNU AI-Lab established in 2017 in collaboration with the Norwegian University of Science and Technology (NTNU) and SINTEF. The lab serves as a hub for artificial intelligence research, focusing on applications in telecom networks and beyond. Current focus areas encompass advanced networks like 5G and 6G, artificial intelligence and data analytics (including speech-to-text and chatbots), cyber security, behavioral economics, and user experience design to enhance service innovation and customer trends.
Telenor Research fosters extensive collaborations with academic institutions, such as NTNU and the Norwegian School of Economics (NHH), as well as international partners like Harvard School of Public Health for projects on disease prediction using mobile data. These partnerships have led to numerous patents and standards contributions since inception, emphasizing high-impact innovations over exhaustive patent filings in recent years. The unit comprises a multidisciplinary team of around 41 researchersas of 2021predominantly PhD holders from diverse nationalities, driving Telenor's competitive edge through applied research that occasionally informs areas like IoT solutions.

IoT and Machine-to-Machine Solutions

Telenor IoT (the branded global operations of Telenor Connexion), the specialized IoT and machine-to-machine (M2M) connectivity unit of the Telenor Group, delivers managed global connectivity solutions for enterprise and industrial applications. Established to focus on non-consumer IoT deployments, it operates across more than 200 countries through partnerships with over 400 mobile networks, enabling scalable device connectivity for diverse sectors. In November 2025, Telenor consolidated its Nordic IoT operations under Telenor Connexion to create a unified global IoT powerhouse.
A core component of Telenor IoT's offerings is its IoT Connectivity Management Platform, which provides tools for remote device provisioning, performance monitoring, usage analytics, and SIM lifecycle management. This platform supports over-the-air updates and diagnostics, helping customers optimize large-scale IoT fleets without on-site interventions. Complementing this, Telenor IoT's global roaming capabilities utilize multi-IMSI SIM cards that automatically select optimal networks, ensuring uninterrupted connectivity for mobile assets like vehicles and machinery across international borders. By early 2025, these services had supported the deployment of more than 25 million IoT SIM cards worldwide, reflecting the unit's expanding role in enterprise IoT ecosystems.
Telenor IoT emphasizes compliance with cellular IoT standards such as NB-IoT for low-data-rate, battery-efficient applications in remote areas and LTE-M for moderate-speed, mobility-focused use cases like asset tracking. These technologies enable cost-effective, wide-coverage connectivity while supporting power-saving modes essential for M2M devices. On security, Telenor IoT adheres to ISO 27001 and GDPR standards, incorporating end-to-end encryption and secure provisioning; the unit has also contributed to the development of GSMA's IoT Security Guidelines, which outline best practices for risk assessment, secure boot, and data protection in IoT deployments.
In practical applications, Telenor IoT powers solutions across key industries. In the automotive sector, it partners with Volvo Cars to enable connected services, including the Volvo On Call app for remote vehicle control, emergency assistance, and over-the-air updates, as well as embedded SIMs in the Volvo Wallbox for reliable EV home charging connectivity across Europe. For smart cities, collaborations like that with Capelon integrate IoT for urban sustainability, connecting sensors in parking systems, waste management bins, and street lighting to optimize resource use and reduce emissions in European municipalities. In agriculture, Telenor IoT supports precision farming through connectivity for FarmDroid's autonomous seeding robots, which use GPS and real-time data to plant crops efficiently while minimizing soil disruption and input costs. These implementations highlight Telenor IoT's focus on reliable, low-latency M2M solutions tailored to operational efficiency. The unit continues to experience strong growth, driven by demand in these areas and innovations like eSIM adoption under the GSMA SGP.32 standard for future-proof device management.

Broadcasting and Media

Television and Satellite Services

Telenor has historically been a key player in Nordic pay-TV through its ownership of Canal Digital, a provider of satellite and broadband TV services that served over 1.2 million subscribers across the region. In July 2025, Telenor agreed to sell its 50% stake in Allentethe merged entity of Canal Digital and Viasat Consumerto Viaplay Group for SEK 1.1 billion, a deal completed on November 13, 2025, marking the end of its direct ownership in this pay-TV operation.
From the early 2000s until 2024, Telenor operated a significant satellite platform via its Telenor Satellite division, which managed the Thor spacecraft fleet positioned at 0.81 West for direct-to-home (DTH) broadcasting across Europe, the Middle East, and Africa. Key launches included Thor 5 in February 2008, which enhanced Ku-band capacity for TV distribution; Thor 6 in July 2009, expanding coverage for digital video broadcasting; and Thor 7 in April 2015, introducing hybrid Ku- and Ka-band transponders for both broadcasting and broadband services. In January 2024, Telenor divested its satellite business to the state-owned Space Norway for NOK 2.36 billion, retaining access to capacity through ongoing partnerships.
Today, Telenor continues to deliver television services in the Nordics, focusing on IPTV, cable TV via fiber networks, and integrated streaming options through platforms like T-We in Norway, which combines live TV, on-demand content, and partnerships with services such as Netflix. In September 2025, Telenor extended its agreement with Space Norway to supply IPTV and linear over-the-top (OTT) streaming for pay-TV markets in Norway, Sweden, and Finland, leveraging Thor satellite capacity for reliable content delivery. These services cover Norway, Sweden, and Denmark, where Telenor customers access bundled TV packages including local channels, international content, and HBO Max via multi-year distribution deals. Cable TV remains prominent in urban areas through Telenor's fixed-line infrastructure, while streaming integration allows seamless transitions to digital formats. Allente, now fully owned by Viaplay Group following the November 2025 completion, serves approximately 840,000 subscribers across the region as of November 2025.

Digital Broadcasting Initiatives

Telenor has advanced its digital broadcasting capabilities through strategic partnerships focused on streaming and on-demand content delivery, particularly in the Nordic region. In 2022, Telenor entered a long-term distribution agreement with Viaplay Group, enabling its approximately 1 million TV customers in Sweden and Norway to access Viaplay's streaming service alongside linear television channels. This partnership built on earlier collaborations, including the 2019 merger of Telenor's Canal Digital and Viaplay Group's Viasat Consumer operations into the Allente joint venture, which expanded hybrid TV offerings combining satellite, IPTV, and streaming. Although Telenor divested its 50% stake in Allente to Viaplay in July 2025 for SEK 1.1 billiona deal completed on November 13, 2025the distribution deal ensures continued integration of Viaplay's on-demand content into Telenor's platforms.
In parallel, Telenor has invested in over-the-top (OTT) platforms to enhance user experience across linear and nonlinear content. A key example is the T-We app, launched for Norwegian customers, which provides access to live TV, catch-up services, video-on-demand (VOD), and series on devices like mobile phones, tablets, and smart TVs. To support this, Telenor partnered with Broadpeak in August 2025 to deploy a unified content delivery network (CDN) across Norway, Sweden, and Finland, enabling seamless delivery of live TV, personal video recorder functions, and VOD with reduced latency. Additionally, a September 2025 agreement with Space Norway extended IPTV and linear OTT streaming services to pay-TV markets in the Nordics, leveraging satellite and IP infrastructure for broader reach. In Asia, where Telenor serves over 70 million customers, similar OTT initiatives include localized streaming partnerships, such as the 2021 collaboration with BOLT Digital in Pakistan to deliver premium live and on-demand content via the BOLT+ platform to Telenor Microfinance Bank's users.
Telenor has also pioneered 5G integration for broadcasting to support high-quality, mobile-first media consumption. In March 2022, the company launched a full TV solution over 5G in Norway, utilizing wireless broadband to deliver linear channels, VOD, and interactive features without fixed-line dependencies, marking a shift toward network-agnostic digital delivery. This initiative aligns with Telenor's broader 5G rollout, including nationwide activation with Tele2 in Sweden by September 2025, covering 90% of the landmass and enabling enhanced streaming capabilities. In Asia, Telenor's 5G deployments, such as in Bangladesh through Grameenphone, facilitate improved OTT performance for digital content, though specific broadcasting pilots remain in early stages as of late 2025.
To bolster content libraries, Telenor secured key partnerships for premium programming. In June 2025, it renewed a multi-year agreement with Warner Bros. Discovery, granting Nordic customers access to HBO Max's streaming catalogincluding titles like The White Lotusand a suite of linear TV channels across Norway, Sweden, Denmark, and Finland. This deal, covering Telenor's 1 million TV and OTT subscribers in the region, emphasizes bundled access to global hits and local favorites, driving adoption of digital platforms. Overall, these efforts have contributed to steady growth in Telenor's digital service revenues, with Nordic operations reporting 2.1% year-over-year increases in Q3 2025, reflecting rising engagement with streaming amid cord-cutting trends.

Subsidiaries and Divestitures

Current Subsidiaries

Telenor's current subsidiaries form the core of its international footprint, primarily in the Nordic region and Asia, contributing to over 200 million mobile subscribers and a substantial portion of the group's revenue. These entities are fully integrated into Telenor's governance framework, with board oversight from the parent company and alignment on sustainability goals such as science-based emissions targets and digital inclusion initiatives.
Key holdings include Grameenphone in Bangladesh, where Telenor holds a 55.8% stake, operating as the market leader with 84.3 million subscribers and generating approximately 19% of the group's total revenue in 2024 through mobile services focused on digital inclusion and online safety training for millions of users. In Malaysia, Telenor's 33.1% ownership in CelcomDigi positions it as a major telecom associate with 20.2 million subscribers, emphasizing 5G infrastructure and contributing to Telenor's Asian investment strategy managed by a dedicated team. The post-merger entity in Thailand, True Corporation (incorporating DTAC), reflects Telenor's 30.3% stake (28.6% direct and 1.7% indirect) in a market-leading operator serving 49.4 million subscribers; the 2023 merger completion added significant scale, realizing synergies and enhancing Telenor's presence in Southeast Asia with validated science-based targets.
In the Nordics, Telenor maintains 100% ownership of DNA in Finland, a second-largest mobile provider with 2.8 million mobile and 0.7 million broadband subscribers, supporting 5G upgrades and digital skills training. Telenor Sverige in Sweden, also 100% owned, serves 3.0 million mobile and 0.7 million broadband customers as the third-largest mobile operator, advancing network modernization and renewable energy sourcing via power purchase agreements. Similarly, Telenor Denmark, fully owned, operates as the second-largest mobile provider with 1.7 million mobile and 0.1 million broadband subscribers, focusing on IT transformations and 5G rollouts alongside solar energy initiatives. Telenor Norway, 100% owned, is the incumbent operator with 2.6 million mobile and 0.7 million broadband subscribers, driving core Nordic services and digital inclusion efforts. In Pakistan, Telenor Pakistan, 100% owned, serves 43.2 million subscribers as a leading mobile operator, with a focus on data services; pending divestiture to PTCL following regulatory approvals in 2025, expected completion by 2026.

Former Operations

Telenor divested its operations in Myanmar in 2022, selling the business to M1 Group, a Lebanon-based consortium led by Shwe Byain Phyu, for an enterprise value of approximately 5.2 billion Norwegian kroner (NOK). The decision was driven by escalating geopolitical risks following the military coup in February 2021, which led to widespread violence, human rights concerns, and threats to employee safety, rendering continued operations untenable. The sale resulted in a significant financial loss, with Telenor booking a write-down of 6.5 billion NOK in the first quarter of 2021 and an additional accumulated loss of 800 million NOK upon completion, reflecting the sharp devaluation of assets amid the crisis.
In India, Telenor exited the market in 2017 through the sale of its operations to Bharti Airtel, with the transaction effectively closing customer migrations by 2018. The withdrawal stemmed from prolonged regulatory challenges, including the 2012 2G spectrum scam that invalidated licenses and forced repeated legal battles, compounded by fierce competition from Reliance Jio's disruptive entry. Financially, the exit was costly, marked by cumulative impairments exceeding 10 billion NOK over the years, including a 3.9 billion NOK write-down in 2012 and further losses of 3.1 billion NOK in operating expenses by 2016, as the venture failed to achieve sustainable profitability despite initial rapid subscriber growth.
Telenor sold its stake in Russian operator VimpelCom (now part of VEON) in 2016, offloading approximately 33% of shares on the public market. The divestiture was prompted by ongoing bribery scandals involving the company's Uzbek unit, which led to a $795 million settlement with U.S. and Dutch authorities, alongside shareholder conflicts with Russian billionaire Mikhail Fridman over corporate governance. This move followed earlier dilutions of Telenor's voting rights and marked a strategic retreat from a problematic asset that had drawn regulatory scrutiny and reputational damage.
Regarding Ukraine, Telenor's indirect presence through VEON's subsidiary Kyivstar ended with the complete sale of its remaining VEON stake in 2019, comprising about 9% of shares. The full divestment aligned with Telenor's broader portfolio optimization efforts in the late 2010s, aiming to reduce exposure to emerging market risks after years of underperformance and governance issues at VEON. This exit liquidated Telenor's legacy involvement in Ukrainian telecom dating back to 1998 via acquisitions like Golden Telecom.
As part of its international restructuring in the 2010s, Telenor also divested legacy assets tied to VimpelCom, including incremental stake sales between 2015 and 2019 that progressively reduced its ownership from over 40% to zero. These transactions, totaling billions in proceeds but incurring impairments due to VEON's scandals, allowed Telenor to refocus on core Nordic and Asian markets while mitigating geopolitical and compliance risks in Russia and adjacent regions.

Controversies

Regulatory and Ethical Issues

Telenor has encountered significant regulatory scrutiny in Norway due to its dominant position in the telecommunications market, leading to antitrust investigations and fines in the 2000s and 2010s. In 2006, the Norwegian Competition Authority examined Telenor's practices amid concerns over market foreclosure in fixed-line services, though no fine was imposed at that time. By 2014, following a 2012 raid, authorities opened a formal antitrust probe into Telenor's alleged abuse of dominance in the fixed and mobile broadband markets as well as national roaming, where Telenor held substantial control over infrastructure. This investigation, along with related probes, culminated in fines, including a record NOK 788 million in 2018 for anticompetitive practices in the mobile market.
Spectrum allocation has presented ongoing challenges for Telenor, particularly in Asia during the 2010s. In India, Telenor's joint venture Uninor was severely impacted by the 2G spectrum scandal, where the Supreme Court canceled 122 licenses in 2012, including 22 held by Uninor, due to irregularities in the 2008 allocation process that favored certain operators at undervalued prices. This ruling forced Telenor to restart operations through new auctions, incurring substantial financial losses, including an impairment charge of NOK 4.1 billion (approximately $710 million). In Europe, Telenor navigated spectrum disputes indirectly through merger attempts, such as the failed 2015 bid to merge its Danish unit with TeliaSonera, which regulators blocked over fears of reduced competition in spectrum holdings and mobile services. In January 2025, Telia sued Telenor in Norway for damages arising from Telenor's anticompetitive practices in the mobile market during the 2000s and 2010s.
On ethical fronts, Telenor established robust data privacy measures in its Nordic operations to comply with evolving regulations, including the EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) effective from 2018. The company implemented internal controls for personal data handling in customer services, such as mobile and broadband, though early compliance efforts revealed gaps in areas like voicemail security, prompting audits in the late 2010s. Telenor's anti-corruption programs, formalized with a zero-tolerance policy since the early 2000s, were strengthened following the 2014 Uzbekistan probe involving its associate VimpelCom, which uncovered bribery in license acquisitions; this led to enhanced global training initiatives by 2016. Regarding sustainability, Telenor aligned its reporting with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) starting in the mid-2010s, integrating targets like reduced inequalities (SDG 10) into its corporate responsibility framework from 2016 onward, with annual reports emphasizing digital inclusion in emerging markets.
Labor relations in Telenor's Norwegian operations have involved periodic tensions with unions, particularly during collective bargaining. A notable strike in May 2006 disrupted services after negotiations between Telenor and the EL & IT Forbundet union broke down over wage increases and working conditions, lasting several days and affecting thousands of employees. Union involvement also played a key role in the 1999 merger talks with Sweden's Telia, where Norwegian labor representatives raised concerns about job security and relocation, contributing to the deal's collapse after two months of negotiations. These episodes highlight Telenor's engagement with social partners under Norway's collective bargaining system, emphasizing fair labor practices amid market liberalization.

Recent Challenges (2020s)

In the early 2020s, Telenor's operations in Myanmar faced severe scrutiny amid the country's political instability following the February 2021 military coup. Allegations emerged that Telenor Myanmar shared sensitive customer data, including names, addresses, and phone numbers of up to 18 million subscribers, with the junta, facilitating surveillance, arrests, and executions of activists and civilians. These claims intensified in 2025 when leaked internal documents, reported by Norwegian broadcaster NRK, revealed systematic data handovers to military authorities even after the coup, prompting civil society groups like Defend Myanmar Democracy and the Myanmar Internet Project to file lawsuits in Norway against Telenor for complicity in human rights abuses. In April 2024, Telenor initiated secret arbitration proceedings in Singapore against Myanmar under a bilateral investment treaty, seeking compensation for financial losses from its 2022 exit sale, amid ongoing criticism for engaging legally with the junta. In response to escalating risks, Telenor announced its exit in July 2021 and completed the sale of its Myanmar subsidiary to the military-linked Shwe Byain Phyu (a proxy for Lebanon's M1 Group) in March 2022, granting the junta broader access to user information and drawing criticism for an irresponsible divestment that prioritized financial interests over user safety.
The COVID-19 pandemic posed additional operational hurdles for Telenor from 2020 to 2021, straining networks across its Asian markets due to surging data usage from remote work, online education, and digital services. In Myanmar alone, internet traffic on Telenor's network rose by 27% between March and April 2020, contributing to broader regional disruptions that reduced quarterly revenues in Asia by impacting customer mobility and economic activity. The crisis accelerated Telenor's digital transformation, with the company forecasting a sustained shift toward remote technologies and increased employee expectations for flexible work arrangements post-pandemic.
Regulatory delays marked Telenor's 2023 merger of its Thai subsidiary dtac with Charoen Pokphand Group's True Corporation, creating Southeast Asia's largest mobile operator with over 51 million customers. Thai authorities, including the National Broadcasting and Telecommunications Commission, imposed extensive conditions on the deal after prolonged reviews and appeals, citing competition concerns, with final amalgamation completed in March 2023 following an 11-hour approval meeting in late 2022.
Cybersecurity emerged as a persistent challenge in 2025, particularly in Pakistan, where Telenor faced the fallout from sector-wide data incidents, including a reported leak of 3.2 million International Mobile Subscriber Identity (IMSI) and International Mobile Equipment Identity (IMEI) records advertised on the dark web. The Pakistan Telecommunication Authority (PTA) investigated these breaches, denying direct telecom involvement but confirming vulnerabilities in national data handling that exposed subscriber privacy. Concurrently, Telenor's Asian expansions, including 5G deployments in markets like Thailand and Pakistan, navigated escalating US-China geopolitical tensions over telecommunications infrastructure, with US sanctions on Chinese vendors like Huawei complicating supply chains and forcing reliance on alternative technologies amid broader tech decoupling efforts.
