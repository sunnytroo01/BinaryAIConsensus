 1787 was a pivotal year in American history, marked primarily by the Constitutional Convention in Philadelphia, where delegates drafted the United States Constitution to replace the ineffective Articles of Confederation. The convention, convened from May 25 to September 17, involved 55 delegates from 12 states (Rhode Island absent) who ultimately rejected mere amendments to the Articles and created a new framework establishing a stronger federal government with separated powers, checks and balances, and mechanisms for representation.
The resulting document, signed on September 17, addressed critical weaknesses such as the inability to regulate commerce, levy taxes, or maintain a standing army, while incorporating compromises like the Great Compromise on legislative representation and the Three-Fifths Compromise on slavery's role in apportionment. Complementing these efforts, Congress under the Articles passed the Northwest Ordinance on July 13, organizing the Northwest Territory, prohibiting slavery there, and outlining a process for new states' admission on equal footing with originals. These developments laid foundational structures for national expansion and governance, influencing the subsequent ratification debates and the formation of political factions.

United States Constitutional Convention

Background and Convening

The Articles of Confederation, ratified in 1781, established a weak central government that lacked authority to levy taxes, regulate interstate or foreign commerce, or compel states to fulfill financial obligations from the Revolutionary War, resulting in mounting national debt and economic disarray by the mid-1780s. These deficiencies hindered the Confederation Congress's ability to address trade barriers between states and foreign tariffs that stifled American exports, exacerbating postwar depression and prompting calls for reform among nationalists like James Madison and Alexander Hamilton.
Shays' Rebellion, an armed uprising by debt-burdened farmers in western Massachusetts from August 1786 to February 1787, underscored the federal government's impotence in suppressing domestic insurrections without state cooperation or a standing army. Led by Daniel Shays, the rebels closed courts to halt foreclosures and debt collections, culminating in an unsuccessful attack on the Springfield arsenal on January 25, 1787, which required privately funded militia to quell. The event alarmed elites nationwide, demonstrating the risks of anarchy under the Articles and galvanizing support for a stronger national framework capable of maintaining order.
In response to these crises, the Annapolis Convention convened on September 11, 1786, with delegates from five states to address commercial disputes but issued a broader call on September 14 for all states to send representatives to a general convention in Philadelphia the following May to revise the Articles. The Confederation Congress, after initial reluctance, endorsed this on February 21, 1787, authorizing a meeting "for the sole and express purpose of revising the Articles of Confederation."
The convention achieved quorum on May 25, 1787, in Philadelphia's State House, with 55 delegates appointed by 12 statesRhode Island declined participation due to opposition to centralized powerand immediately adopted rules for secrecy to foster candid debate, shuttering windows and swearing participants to silence. George Washington was unanimously elected president on May 29, leveraging his prestige from the Revolutionary War to unify proceedings amid the assembly's diverse interests.

Key Debates

The central debates at the 1787 Constitutional Convention revolved around the structure of representation in the national legislature, pitting the Virginia Plan against the New Jersey Plan. The Virginia Plan, introduced on May 29 by Virginia Governor Edmund Randolph and primarily drafted by James Madison, advocated for a strong national government with a bicameral legislature where representation in both houses would be proportional to state population or financial contributions, effectively favoring larger states. This framework proposed a national executive and judiciary with veto powers over state laws, aiming to supplant the weak Articles of Confederation with a consolidated authority capable of addressing interstate commerce failures and security threats under the prior system. In contrast, the New Jersey Plan, presented on June 15 by William Paterson of New Jersey, sought to preserve the confederation's equal state sovereignty by maintaining a unicameral Congress with one vote per state, while modestly enhancing federal powers over taxation and trade to remedy specific confederation deficiencies without undermining small states' influence. Delegates like Paterson argued that proportional representation would erode state equality and invite majority domination, whereas proponents such as Madison countered that equal suffrage perpetuated inefficiency and factional gridlock evident in the Confederation Congress's inability to enforce requisitions or regulate commerce.
Discussions on separation of powers emphasized preventing concentration of authority to avert tyranny, drawing implicitly from Montesquieu's analysis of balanced government forms as essential for liberty's preservation amid human ambition. Madison's detailed notes record extensive deliberations from late May through July on dividing legislative, executive, and judicial functions, with early resolutions establishing a bicameral legislature to check impulsive popular assemblies through a senate of longer terms and broader constituencies. On executive authority, delegates debated a single versus plural head, term length, and reelection eligibility; figures like Elbridge Gerry warned of monarchical risks in a strong executive, while Alexander Hamilton advocated a lifetime appointment akin to Britain's for stability, arguing that weak executives under confederations had failed to execute laws or command respect internationally. These exchanges underscored causal concerns over factionalism, with Madison positing that separated powers with mutual checkssuch as legislative impeachment of executiveswould mitigate self-interest's corrosive effects on governance, contrasting Lockean views of limited government with pragmatic needs for energetic administration.
Debates on national versus state sovereignty highlighted tensions between confederated autonomy and unified governance efficacy, with Federalist-oriented delegates pressing for a national veto over state laws to curb parochial policies that undermined collective security and economic vitality. Madison argued that state sovereignty had engendered rivalries, debt defaults, and trade barriers under the Articles, necessitating a paramount national framework to secure republican liberty through scale and uniformity, as fragmented authority invited external predation and internal disorder. Opponents, including smaller-state representatives, invoked fears of centralized overreach dissolving state identities, yet data from confederation-era failuressuch as Shays' Rebellion in 1786-1787 exposing state impotencebolstered calls for a vigorous national structure to enforce contracts, suppress insurrections, and foster commerce, prioritizing causal mechanisms for stability over abstract equalitarianism.

Compromises and Controversies

The Connecticut Compromise, proposed by delegates Oliver Ellsworth and Roger Sherman of Connecticut, resolved the impasse between large states favoring proportional representation in a unicameral legislature and small states insisting on equal suffrage to protect their sovereignty. It established a bicameral Congress, with the House of Representatives apportioned according to state population and the Senate granting each state two seats regardless of size, thereby balancing democratic majoritarianism with federalist equality among states. Adopted on July 16, 1787, after prolonged deadlock that nearly dissolved the convention, the measure passed by a narrow 5-4 vote among attending delegations, with Massachusetts divided and Pennsylvania absent. This pragmatic resolution averted collapse but entrenched tensions over federal structure, as small states like New Jersey viewed proportional schemes as existential threats to their influence, while large states like Virginia prioritized population-based power to reflect economic and demographic realities.
Slavery permeated several compromises, reflecting Southern delegates' defense of it as essential to their agrarian economies reliant on coerced labor for staples like rice and tobacco, contrasted with Northern delegates' moral qualms and fears of disproportionate Southern political power. The Three-Fifths Compromise, revived from Articles of Confederation precedents, counted enslaved persons as three-fifths of a free person for apportioning House seats and direct taxes, granting Southern states added representation without full enfranchisement of slaves, who lacked voting rights. Southern proponents, such as South Carolina's John Rutledge, argued for full counting as property integral to state wealth, rejecting Northern proposals to exclude slaves entirely or count them only for taxation; Northern concessions aimed to secure Southern ratification and union, prioritizing national cohesion over immediate equity. The Fugitive Slave Clause in Article IV, Section 2 mandated return of escaped laborers across state lines without trial by jury, addressing Southern concerns over property flight to free states like Pennsylvania, where such escapes undermined plantation systems; debates on August 29, 1787, linked this to broader slavery bargains, with delegates like Rutledge insisting on enforcement to prevent economic sabotage. Additionally, Article I, Section 9 deferred any congressional ban on the international slave trade until 1808, a 20-year window Southern states demanded to sustain labor imports, as Charles Pinckney warned prohibition would doom ratification in Carolina and Georgia; Northern delegates, including Gouverneur Morris, decried the trade as immoral piracy yet yielded to preserve the framework, enabling Congress to impose a 10-dollar head tax in the interim. These clauses empirically bolstered Southern leverageenhancing their electoral and legislative cloutwhile deferring abolitionist pressures, fostering short-term stability but sowing seeds of sectional discord that erupted in 1861.
Debates over the executive branch highlighted fears of monarchical consolidation versus weak leadership, culminating in the Electoral College as a hybrid mechanism. Direct popular election was rejected by Southern delegates wary of Northern voter majorities and by aristocrats distrusting mass judgment; congressional selection risked legislative dominance and corruption, as James Madison noted in his convention records. The compromise vested election in electors apportioned by congressional seatsfavoring smaller states via Senate equalityallowing indirect choice to filter demagoguery while checking Congress, with no initial term limits to permit experienced continuity against aristocratic perpetuity. Gouverneur Morris and others advocated indefinite eligibility to incentivize virtue and accountability through frequent elections, rejecting fixed terms as rigid and prone to cabal; this reflected originalist aims for a vigorous yet restrained executive, subordinate to enumerated powers and impeachment, amid pervasive anti-monarchical sentiment post-Revolution. Controversies persisted, with figures like Elbridge Gerry decrying the system as aristocratic, yet it passed as a bulwark against pure democracy's volatility, empirically enabling stable transitions without executive overreach until later amendments.

Outcome and Ratification

On September 17, 1787, thirty-nine delegates affixed their signatures to the United States Constitution in Philadelphia, concluding the Constitutional Convention after four months of deliberations. Of the fifty-five delegates who had attended at least part of the sessions, sixteen were absent or declined to sign, including prominent figures such as George Mason and Edmund Randolph of Virginia, and Elbridge Gerry of Massachusetts, who objected primarily to the absence of explicit protections for individual rights and insufficient checks on federal power. The signed document comprised a preamble and seven articles outlining the structure of a federal republic, including separation of powers among legislative, executive, and judicial branches, with provisions for state ratification by conventions rather than legislatures to bypass entrenched interests.
The convention immediately resolved to transmit the Constitution to the Congress of the Confederation for submission to the states, stipulating that ratification by conventions in nine of the thirteen states would establish the new frame of government. Congress, meeting in New York, received the document on September 20 and unanimously agreed on September 28, 1787, to forward it to the states with a call for ratifying conventions, initiating the process without endorsing its content. Early momentum built as Delaware ratified unanimously on December 7, 1787, followed by Pennsylvania on December 12, 1787, by a vote of 46 to 23 in its convention, marking the second state approval and demonstrating viability amid regional divisions.
Ratification efforts quickly revealed an emerging Federalist-Anti-Federalist schism, with Anti-Federalists like Mason voicing apprehensions over centralized authority and the lack of a bill of rights to safeguard liberties against potential federal overreach. Federalist proponents countered by pledging post-ratification amendments to address these deficiencies, a concession empirically aiding approvals in key states and averting the confederation's fiscal and diplomatic frailties. This dynamic underscored causal pressures for union, as unchecked state autonomy had previously contributed to interstate conflicts and economic instability under the Articles of Confederation.

Other Political and Diplomatic Events

JanuaryMarch

In Massachusetts, the armed uprising known as Shays' Rebellion, driven by indebted farmers protesting high taxes and debt foreclosures under the post-Revolutionary economic strain, reached its climax and suppression in January and February. On January 25, 1787, approximately 1,500 rebels under Daniel Shays advanced on the federal arsenal in Springfield to seize arms and supplies, but they were repelled by a force of about 1,200 state militia led by Major General Benjamin Lincoln, resulting in four rebel deaths and the dispersal of the attackers. Government forces, funded privately due to state fiscal constraints, pursued the remnants; by early February, they routed Shays' followers at Petersham, forcing Shays to flee to Vermont and effectively ending organized resistance. This episode exposed the Articles of Confederation's inadequacies, as the central government could neither raise troops nor provide direct aid, relying instead on ad hoc state militias and private financing, which fueled urgency for a stronger national framework to prevent domestic insurgencies.
In France, amid a deepening fiscal crisis exacerbated by war debtsincluding support for the American Revolutionand an inequitable tax system that spared privileged estates, Controller-General Charles Alexandre de Calonne summoned the Assembly of Notables on February 22, 1787, at Versailles. Comprising 144 carefully selected aristocrats, clergy, and magistrates, the assembly was tasked with endorsing Calonne's reform package, which included a universal land tax and suppression of internal customs barriers to broaden the revenue base beyond the burdened Third Estate. However, the notables rejected these measures, insisting on parliamentary registration and decrying attacks on exemptions as violations of traditional rights, leading to Calonne's dismissal in April and the assembly's dissolution in May without resolution. The failure underscored entrenched resistance from vested interests to structural fiscal overhaul, intensifying royal bankruptcy risks and eroding monarchical legitimacy as precursors to broader revolutionary pressures.
Elsewhere in Europe, simmering tensions in the Dutch Republic saw early 1787 manifestations of the ongoing Patriot movement, with civic militias and pamphlets challenging Stadtholder William V's authority and advocating constitutional limits on hereditary rule amid economic stagnation. These stirrings, building on unrest since 1780, prompted diplomatic maneuvering but did not yet escalate to open conflict until later Prussian intervention. In the Balkans, preliminary alignments toward Russo-Austrian cooperation against Ottoman dominance hinted at Serbian mobilization potential, though full hostilities awaited the summer declaration of the Russo-Turkish War.

AprilJune

In April 1787, the Dutch Patriot movement, advocating for republican reforms and greater popular influence against the stadtholder William V of the House of Orange, intensified its challenge to the established order in the Dutch Republic. Pro-republican militias, bolstered by civic guards reformed to exclude Orangist sympathizers, controlled key cities including Utrecht and Overijssel, where armed Patriot forces numbered around 6,000 by spring, demonstrating the fragility of centralized authority reliant on hereditary princely power amid widespread discontent over economic stagnation and perceived corruption.
On April 21, a massive demonstration convened on Amsterdam's Dam square, where protesters occupied the town hall and presented a petition bearing 16,000 signatures demanding the removal of pro-stadtholder officials from the city government. This action led to a purge of Orangist elements in Amsterdam's magistracy, marking a high point of Patriot ascendancy and exposing the empirical weaknesses of absolutist-leaning governance, as local elites yielded to organized popular pressure without immediate military backlash.
Throughout May and June, Patriot committees continued to consolidate control, exercising de facto authority in reformed municipalities and pressing for a national assembly to curtail the stadtholder's veto powers and aristocratic privileges, though internal divisions over radicalism began to emerge. These developments highlighted causal tensions between entrenched oligarchic structures and emerging demands for merit-based representation, prefiguring foreign intervention by Prussian forces in September to restore Orange dominance.

JulySeptember

In France, Controller-General Lomnie de Brienne continued efforts to enact fiscal reforms following the dismissal of Charles Alexandre de Calonne in May, proposing edicts to establish a universal land tax and eliminate fiscal exemptions amid ongoing financial crisis. The Parlement of Paris and other sovereign courts resisted, refusing to register the measures without the convening of the Estates General, escalating tensions between the monarchy and nobility through the summer months. These debates highlighted deepening divisions over absolutist authority and representative governance, with parlements asserting their role as guardians against arbitrary taxation.
On August 19, Russia formally declared war on the Ottoman Empire, igniting the Russo-Turkish War of 17871792 after Ottoman demands for the return of Crimea and naval incidents in the Black Sea. Catherine the Great's expansionist ambitions in the south, coupled with Ottoman military unpreparedness, prompted the conflict, which saw initial Russian offensives despite logistical challenges. The declaration followed the imprisonment of the Russian ambassador in Constantinople and marked a renewal of imperial rivalries over Black Sea access and territorial control.
In Britain, abolitionist Granville Sharp promoted the establishment of a free settlement in Sierra Leone as a refuge for impoverished free blacks and former slaves, with an expedition of approximately 400 passengers departing Plymouth in April aboard the Atlantic, Vernon, and Pallas. Sharp drafted a constitution emphasizing British legal principles and communal land ownership, aiming to foster self-sufficient agriculture and countervail the slave trade's disruptions. This initiative reflected growing humanitarian concerns among British reformers, though the voyagers faced disease and delays en route.
In Asia, Mysore ruler Tipu Sultan pursued diplomatic overtures to France and the Ottoman Empire to secure military aid against British East India Company forces during the ongoing Third Anglo-Mysore War, offering trade concessions and anti-colonial alignment. Concurrently, Vietnamese prince Nguyn nh's envoy Pierre Pigneau de Bhaine arrived in France by mid-1787, negotiating preliminary terms for French support against the Ty Sn rebels, laying groundwork for a formal alliance treaty later that year. These efforts underscored European powers' expanding influence in regional power struggles through opportunistic partnerships.

OctoberDecember

On November 28, 1787, French diplomat Pigneau de Bhaine, acting on behalf of Nguyn nh (later Emperor Gia Long of Vietnam), signed the Treaty of Versailles with representatives of King Louis XVI at the Palace of Versailles. The agreement committed France to provide military aid, including ships, troops, and munitions, to support Nguyn nh's campaigns against the Siamese kingdom and the Ty Sn rebels who controlled much of Vietnam, in exchange for territorial concessions such as the islands of Poulo Condore and a trading enclave at Tourane (modern Nng). This alliance reflected France's strategic interest in countering British influence in Southeast Asia and establishing footholds for future colonial expansion, though French domestic turmoil following the treaty limited immediate fulfillment of aid promises.
In the United States, the momentum toward federal ratification accelerated in December as states convened to consider the Constitution transmitted by the Confederation Congress after its September signing. Delaware became the first state to ratify on December 7, 1787, approving the document unanimously in its convention. Pennsylvania ratified two days later, on December 12, 1787, by a vote of 46 to 23 in a contentious convention marked by Federalist advocacy for a stronger national government and Anti-Federalist concerns over centralized power and the absence of a bill of rights. As the most populous state at the time, Pennsylvania's endorsement provided significant early validation for the Constitution's viability, influencing subsequent state debates despite opposition from figures like Thomas Mifflin who demanded amendments.
British parliamentary proceedings in late 1787 included ongoing scrutiny of East India Company governance amid corruption allegations against former Governor-General Warren Hastings, whose impeachment process had begun earlier in the year under Edmund Burke's motion. These sessions, extending into the autumn, focused on administrative reforms and accountability in British India, building on the 1784 Pitt's India Act but yielding no major legislative changes by year's end.

Date Unknown

In Britain, reformers established the Society for Effecting the Abolition of the Slave Trade, the first dedicated organization campaigning against the transatlantic slave trade. Composed initially of twelve members including Quakers and evangelicals such as Granville Sharp and Thomas Clarkson, the society systematically gathered eyewitness accounts from former slaves and seamen to document the trade's cruelties, while producing pamphlets and petitions to sway public and parliamentary opinion. These activities marked a pivotal shift toward organized opposition to slavery, exerting long-term influence on British colonial policies and inspiring nascent anti-slavery advocacy in North American territories reliant on slave labor.
Across the Ottoman Empire, administrative factions maneuvered amid escalating tensions with Russia, implementing provisional bureaucratic adjustments to mobilize resources for impending conflict, though these changes were incremental and overshadowed by the broader decline in central authority.
In the United States, individual states responded to the evident frailties of the Articles of Confederationsuch as interstate trade barriers and inadequate revenue mechanismsthrough legislative deliberations and executive communications urging stronger federal coordination, which amplified pressure for systemic overhaul without formalized timelines.

Exploration and Colonial Ventures

First Fleet to Australia

The First Fleet, comprising 11 ships, departed Portsmouth, England, on May 13, 1787, under the command of Captain Arthur Phillip, tasked with establishing a British penal colony at Botany Bay in New South Wales. The expedition carried approximately 736 convictspredominantly convicted of property crimes amid Britain's rising criminalityalong with 78 marines for guard duty, naval officers, surgeons, and civilian settlers, totaling around 1,400 individuals, plus livestock and provisions for a projected two-year self-sufficiency period. This venture addressed Britain's acute prison overcrowding, exacerbated by the American Revolution's closure of transportation routes to North American colonies after 1776, which had previously absorbed over 50,000 convicts; by the mid-1780s, domestic jails and prison hulks on the Thames were strained beyond capacity, prompting empirical necessity for an alternative outlet.
The fleet's logistical design emphasized durability over speed, with purpose-built transports like the Lady Penrhyn and chartered merchant vessels stocked for equatorial navigation, including anti-scurvy rations of sauerkraut and malt despite prevailing medical skepticism toward citrus preventives. Phillip's instructions from the British government mandated a convict labor-based settlement reliant on agriculture for sustenance, strategically positioned to secure territorial claims against potential French expansion in the Pacific and to exploit rumored resources, though primary records indicate no exaggerated optimism about immediate profitability. Delays in assemblyships gathered from late 1786reflected administrative hurdles, but the convoy sailed intact, stopping at Tenerife and Rio de Janeiro en route, where Phillip oversaw repairs and supplemented supplies amid tropical hazards.
Challenges during the 1787 outbound leg included disciplinary enforcement among convicts, segregated by sex to curb onboard liaisons, and navigational strains from uncharted southern latitudes, yet mortality remained low at under 2% by voyage's end, attributable to Phillip's proactive hygiene measures rather than any inherent benevolence. This imperial initiative, rooted in pragmatic penal reform over punitive excess, laid causal foundations for Australia's demographic transformation, channeling subsequent waves of free migration and convict transport that by 1868 totaled over 160,000 arrivals, reshaping global settlement patterns through Britain's excess labor export.

Other Colonial Developments

In 1787, British philanthropists spearheaded by Granville Sharp initiated plans for a settlement in Sierra Leone as a refuge for freed slaves and impoverished Black Londoners, motivated by abolitionist ideals and the practical aim of alleviating urban poverty while testing self-sustaining free labor communities. Approximately 400 settlers, including former slaves from North America and the Caribbean, arrived on 10 May aboard ships organized by the Committee for the Relief of the Black Poor, establishing the Province of Freedomlater known as Granville Townon the Sierra Leone Peninsula south of the river's mouth. Local Temne rulers initially granted land and welcomed the group, viewing the venture as a potential trading partnership, though the colony faced immediate challenges from disease and supply shortages.
Spain advanced colonial consolidations in the Americas through the Bourbon reforms, emphasizing administrative efficiency and economic revitalization to bolster imperial revenues and defense against rival powers. From 1787 to 1792, these measures included expanding the number of open trans-Atlantic trade ports in both Spain and its viceroyalties, easing imports of machinery for colonial industries, and establishing new consulados (merchant guilds) to regulate commerce and stimulate local production in regions like New Spain and Peru. Such reforms aimed to centralize control, reduce smuggling, and integrate peripheral territories more tightly into the metropolitan economy, reflecting Spain's expansionist drive to sustain its vast holdings amid fiscal strains.
The British East India Company pursued minor administrative and commercial actions in its Indian territories, maintaining territorial gains from prior wars while under Governor-General Lord Cornwallis, who began implementing judicial and revenue reforms to stabilize rule over Bengal and surrounding principalities. These efforts focused on curbing corruption among Company servants and securing trade routes, driven by profit motives and the need to defend monopolistic interests against local rulers and European competitors, without major new conquests that year.
French missionary Pierre-Joseph-Georges Pigneau de Bhaine negotiated overtures toward Indochina, securing the Treaty of Versailles on 28 November 1787 between France and Vietnamese prince Nguyn nh of Cochinchina. The agreement promised French military aidships, troops, and munitionsto help Nguyn nh reclaim his throne from the Tay Son rebels, in return for ceding the island of Poulo Condore, ports like Tourane and Faifo, and exclusive trading privileges for France. This alliance represented an expansionist bid to establish a foothold in Southeast Asia, compensating for lost influence in North America and opening avenues for missionary and mercantile penetration.

Scientific and Technological Advances

Astronomy

William Herschel, using his newly constructed 40-foot reflecting telescope at Observatory House in Slough, England, discovered the two largest moons of Uranuslater named Titania and Oberonon January 11, 1787. These observations marked the first identification of satellites orbiting Uranus, confirming its status as a planet with a retinue akin to Jupiter and Saturn, and providing direct empirical evidence of its dynamical system. Herschel's detection relied on resolving the faint objects against Uranus's disk, a feat enabled by the telescope's superior light-gathering power, which yielded positional data precise enough to distinguish them from background stars.
The moons' apparent magnitudes and orbital proximity to UranusTitania at approximately 436,000 km and Oberon at 583,000 km, with periods of about 8.7 and 13.5 days, respectivelyoffered initial data for estimating the planet's mass and distance from the Sun via Keplerian orbital mechanics applied to telescopic measurements. Though full orbital elements required later refinements due to observational challenges with 18th-century instruments, Herschel's findings contributed to calibrating the solar system's scale by verifying Uranus's position beyond Saturn through satellite dynamics, challenging earlier assumptions of a compact planetary arrangement. This work underscored the value of large-aperture telescopes for empirical celestial mapping, influencing subsequent distance determinations that placed Uranus at roughly 19.2 AU from the Sun.

Chemistry and Physics

In 1787, French physicist and inventor Jacques Charles empirically derived a key relationship for gases, observing that the volume of a fixed quantity of gas at constant pressure varies directly with its absolute temperature, a principle later formalized as Charles's law. This discovery stemmed from precise measurements during hydrogen balloon ascents, where Charles noted proportional expansions upon heating, extrapolating zero volume at approximately -273C, which laid groundwork for the absolute temperature scale without reliance on prior caloric theories.
Concurrent with these gas law investigations, a committee of the Acadmie Royale des Sciences, including Antoine Lavoisier, Louis-Bernard Guyton de Morveau, Claude-Louis Berthollet, and Antoine-Franois Fourcroy, published Mthode de nomenclature chimique, proposing a composition-based system for naming substances that discarded archaic terms tied to phlogiston doctrine. By classifying compounds systematicallye.g., denoting oxygen-containing acids as "oxygns" and salts by base-acid combinationsthis nomenclature reflected experimental evidence from combustion and decomposition reactions, promoting clarity and causal accuracy in describing elemental combinations over hypothetical inflammable principles.
Advances in heat quantification also progressed, as Lavoisier integrated calorimetric techniques with his caloric fluid hypothesis, treating heat as an indestructible substance conserved in reactions, evidenced by ice-melting experiments measuring latent heats in substances like water and metals. These efforts, building on earlier respiration studies, emphasized quantitative balances between heat input and chemical change, fostering empirical rigor in thermodynamics precursors.

Notable Figures

Arthur Middleton, a signer of the United States Declaration of Independence from South Carolina, died on January 1, 1787, at Goose Creek, South Carolina, aged 44, from a fever. Middleton's legacy endures through his contributions to the Continental Congress and his defense of Charleston during the Revolutionary War, where he was captured by British forces in 1780.
Charles Gravier, Comte de Vergennes, France's foreign minister from 1774 to 1787, died on February 13, 1787, at Versailles, aged 67. Vergennes played a pivotal role in securing French alliance and financial support for the American Revolution, negotiating the 1783 Treaty of Paris that ended the war and recognized American independence.
Carl Friedrich Abel, a German composer and virtuoso viola da gamba player, died on June 20, 1787, in London, aged 63. Abel's legacy includes over 40 symphonies, chamber works, and his collaboration with Johann Christian Bach in establishing London's Bach-Abel concert series, which popularized classical music concerts in England.
Alphonsus Liguori, an Italian Catholic bishop, theologian, and founder of the Redemptorists, died on August 1, 1787, at Nocera dei Pagani, aged 90. Canonized in 1839 and declared a Doctor of the Church in 1871 for his moral theology and devotional writings, Liguori's influence persists in Catholic moral teaching and the promotion of popular piety.
Afonso V, Manikongo (king) of the Kingdom of Kongo from 1785, died suddenly in 1787, possibly poisoned by his successor, precipitating a succession crisis. His brief reign amid ongoing Portuguese influence and internal strife marked the kingdom's late decline, contributing to prolonged turmoil until Henrique II's stabilization.
