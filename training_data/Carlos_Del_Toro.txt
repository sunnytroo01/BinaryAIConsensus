Carlos Del Toro

1x
 Carlos Del Toro (born 1961) is a Cuban-born American businessman, retired U.S. Navy officer, and government official who served as the 78th United States Secretary of the Navy from August 2021 to January 2025.
Immigrating from Havana, Cuba, to the United States as a child refugee in 1962, Del Toro grew up in New York City's Hell's Kitchen neighborhood before attending the U.S. Naval Academy, where he earned a Bachelor of Science in electrical engineering and was commissioned as a surface warfare officer in 1983.
Over a 26-year naval career, Del Toro rose to the rank of commander, holding key billets including commissioning commanding officer of the destroyer USS Bulkeley (DDG 84) and various shore assignments in engineering, operations, and legislative affairs. After retiring from active duty, he founded SBG Technology Solutions, Inc., in 2004, serving as its CEO and growing it into a firm providing technology services to government clients. Del Toro holds advanced degrees, including a Master of Arts in national security and strategic studies from the Naval War College and a Master of Science in space systems engineering from the Naval Postgraduate School.
As Secretary of the Navy, Del Toro oversaw a department with over 900,000 personnel and a budget exceeding $200 billion annually, emphasizing readiness amid great power competition, shipbuilding reforms, and supply chain resilience. His tenure included public criticisms of defense contractors for prioritizing shareholder returns over reinvestment in capacity and infrastructure, as well as a 2024 finding by the U.S. Office of Special Counsel that he violated the Hatch Act by endorsing President Joe Biden's reelection and disparaging former President Donald Trump during official travel.

Early life and education

Childhood and immigration

Carlos Del Toro was born in 1961 in Havana, Cuba. In 1962, at the age of one, his parents fled the island nation as refugees to the United States, amid Fidel Castro's consolidation of communist power following the 1959 revolution and the failed Bay of Pigs invasion.
Their departure formed part of the initial surge in the Cuban exodus, driven by the regime's political repressionincluding mass arrests, executions of opponents, and suppression of dissentcoupled with economic policies that nationalized private enterprises, farms, and homes, causing acute shortages and eroding living standards for many. This post-revolutionary outflow, the largest refugee movement from Cuba to the U.S. in history, saw the island's emigrant population swell from around 79,000 in 1960 to over 400,000 by the early 1970s, predominantly comprising educated professionals and middle-class families escaping the causal failures of centralized planning and authoritarian control. By 1980, cumulative Cuban refugees exceeded one million, underscoring the regime's systemic human rights abuses and economic mismanagement as primary drivers rather than generalized instability.
Upon arrival, Del Toro's family settled in Hell's Kitchen, a gritty Manhattan neighborhood marked by high poverty rates, gang activity, and immigrant hardships in the 1960s and 1970s. Growing up amid these urban challenges, including his father's multiple low-wage jobs to support the household, Del Toro navigated environments of scarcity and peril that demanded personal toughness and self-reliance from a young age. The direct experience of communist-induced displacement, followed by adaptation to American inner-city struggles, causally reinforced his formative understanding of individual agency over state dependency and the perils of totalitarian governance.

Formal education

Del Toro attended the United States Naval Academy, graduating in 1983 with a Bachelor of Science degree in electrical engineering. This foundational engineering education provided him with technical proficiency in systems design and analysis, core to naval engineering applications.
He later earned a Master of Science in space systems engineering from the Naval Postgraduate School, focusing on orbital mechanics, satellite systems, and space-based technologies integral to modern defense architectures. Del Toro also obtained a Master of Arts in national security and strategic studies from the Naval War College, emphasizing geopolitical strategy, military planning, and resource allocation in contested environments. Additionally, he completed a master's degree in legislative affairs from George Washington University, which covered policy formulation, congressional processes, and government operations.
Del Toro stands as the only Secretary of the Navy to have graduated from the Naval Academy, Naval Postgraduate School, and Naval War College, underscoring his comprehensive academic grounding in naval technical and strategic disciplines.

Military service

Enlistment and early career

Del Toro immigrated to the United States from Cuba in 1962 at the age of one with his parents, who were refugees fleeing the communist regime. Growing up in the Hell's Kitchen neighborhood of New York City, he was drawn to the Navy by an early affinity for the water and inspiration from the 1934 film Here Comes the Navy, which depicted service life aboard ships.
He entered the United States Naval Academy in 1979 and graduated in 1983 with a Bachelor of Science degree in electrical engineering. Del Toro cited his family's adoption by the U.S. as a primary motivation for pursuing a naval career, stating that "joining the U.S. Navy was my opportunity to show my gratitude for this country that took us in." This decision aligned with the broader Cold War context, where threats from Soviet expansion underscored the value of military service in defending American interests and freedoms.
Upon graduation, Del Toro was commissioned as a Surface Warfare Officer, marking the start of his active-duty naval service focused on surface fleet operations. His early assignments encompassed foundational roles at sea and ashore, emphasizing practical experience in shipboard engineering, operations, and leadership within the surface community. These positions provided hands-on exposure to naval engineering systems and operational readiness, laying the groundwork for his progression through the officer ranks.

Key commands and retirement

Del Toro advanced through surface warfare assignments, culminating in his command as the commissioning commanding officer of the Arleigh Burke-class guided-missile destroyer USS Bulkeley (DDG-84) on December 8, 2001. Under his leadership, Bulkeleyone of the Navy's early mixed-gender surface combatantsunderwent sea trials and initial operational capability, focusing on Aegis combat system integration and fleet readiness for ballistic missile defense roles. His prior sea and shore tours as a surface warfare officer included deployments supporting Operations Desert Shield and Desert Storm, where U.S. naval forces emphasized power projection amid post-Cold War force reductions.
Throughout his mid-career, Del Toro held critical billets that sustained surface fleet operational tempo during the 1990s drawdown, prioritizing combat effectiveness in an era of shrinking budgets and shifting threats from Soviet naval rivalry to regional contingencies. These roles underscored a focus on warfighting proficiency, including weapons systems oversight and unit-level training to maintain lethality against evolving adversaries.
Del Toro retired from active duty in 2004 at the rank of commander after 22 years of commissioned service, transitioning to the private sector.

Private sector career

Defense contracting leadership

Following his retirement from the U.S. Navy in 2004 at the rank of commander, Del Toro founded SBG Technology Solutions, Inc., a service-disabled veteran-owned small business headquartered in Alexandria, Virginia, with additional offices in San Diego, California. The firm specialized in information technology governance, systems engineering, program management, cybersecurity assessments, training, and enterprise modernization services tailored for federal government clients, with a focus on supporting Department of Defense programs including Navy shipbuilding and logistics sustainment.
As president and chief executive officer, Del Toro led SBG in securing contracts via competitive federal procurement processes, such as those under the General Services Administration's 8(a) STARS II Governmentwide Acquisition Contract, which facilitated IT and engineering support for agencies like the Department of Veterans Affairs and naval aviation commands. The company provided specialized capabilities, including hardware and software reviews for cybersecurity and Mobile User Objective System engineering support, amid fluctuating DoD budgets that emphasized cost efficiency and small business participation.
Under Del Toro's stewardship from 2004 to 2021, SBG expanded operations and earned accolades for performance, including ranking among the top 100 diversity-owned businesses in Virginia and recognition as one of the nation's fastest-growing small businesses, reflecting effective adaptation to the competitive landscape of defense-adjacent government contracting. This phase highlighted Del Toro's application of operational expertise from his naval career to build a viable enterprise delivering technical solutions to sustainment and modernization challenges in naval logistics and IT infrastructure.

Secretary of the Navy

Nomination and confirmation

President Joe Biden announced his intent to nominate Carlos Del Toro, a retired Navy commander and defense industry executive, as the 78th Secretary of the Navy on June 11, 2021, selecting him to lead the Department of the Navy amid ongoing challenges in fleet readiness and great power competition. Del Toro's nomination followed the expiration of Kenneth Braithwaite's term under the previous administration, during which the position had been held in acting capacity, and emphasized his 26 years of active-duty service as a surface warfare officer, including command of guided-missile destroyers, over candidates without direct naval operational experience.
Del Toro testified before the Senate Armed Services Committee on July 13, 2021, where he outlined priorities centered on countering China's naval expansion through accelerated shipbuilding to reach a 355-ship fleet, restoring industrial base capacity hampered by maintenance backlogs, and addressing recruitment shortfalls that had left the Navy facing personnel gaps of over 20,000 sailors. He committed to prioritizing warfighting readiness over non-essential initiatives, stressing first-principles needs like reliable deterrence in the Indo-Pacific rather than redistributive domestic policies, and faced questions on integrating legacy systems with emerging technologies amid budget constraints. The hearing reflected bipartisan recognition of naval underinvestment, with senators pressing for empirical fixes to empirically verifiable shortfalls in ship repairs and crew training.
The full Senate unanimously confirmed Del Toro by voice vote on August 7, 2021, signaling broad support for his practitioner background in a role requiring technical grasp of maritime logistics over political alignment. He was sworn in on August 9, 2021, during a private ceremony at the Marine Barracks in Washington, D.C., administered by Secretary of Defense Lloyd Austin, marking the first time a Hispanic American held the position and initiating his mandate to tackle deferred maintenance on over 50% of the fleet and revitalize shipyards strained by years of underfunding.

Tenure and responsibilities (20212025)

Carlos Del Toro served as the 78th Secretary of the Navy from August 2021 to January 2025, overseeing the Department of the Navy's operations, which encompassed the U.S. Navy and Marine Corps amid heightened peer competition from China and Russia. His responsibilities included managing over 900,000 personnelcomprising active-duty sailors (approximately 330,000), Marines (approximately 168,000), reservists, and civiliansand an annual budget exceeding $210 billion, with fiscal year 2025 requests reaching $257.6 billion. 
In the early phase of his tenure, Del Toro prioritized addressing supply chain disruptions lingering from the COVID-19 pandemic, which had delayed shipbuilding and maintenance efforts across multiple programs. These challenges compounded inherited fleet readiness gaps, including ship delivery delays of up to three years in key programs like Virginia-class submarines and Constellation-class frigates, stemming from prior administrations' underinvestment in industrial base capacity and labor shortages. He directed internal reviews to quantify these delays and stabilize production metrics, emphasizing verifiable progress in procurement and yard throughput over broader doctrinal changes.
Mid-tenure efforts shifted toward workforce retention amid post-Afghanistan withdrawal recruiting shortfalls, where the Navy missed goals by thousands despite strong overall retention rates among serving personnel. Del Toro's oversight included congressional testimonies highlighting access barriers to high school campuses and economic competition for talent, while sustaining operational deployments in contested regions to counter adversarial advances. 
In his final weeks, Del Toro named 19 vessels on January 13-17, 2025, including future aircraft carriers USS William J. Clinton (CVN-82) and USS Ronald Reagan (CVN-83), Virginia-class submarines, and destroyers, marking a procedural capstone to fleet expansion planning amid ongoing industrial constraints. This administrative action underscored continuity in naval posture, with empirical focus on metrics like hull completions rather than reallocating resources to non-core priorities.

Key initiatives and achievements

In September 2023, Del Toro called for a "new maritime statecraft" to integrate naval diplomacy, shipbuilding, and commercial shipping into a cohesive whole-of-government strategy, aiming to counter China's maritime dominance by fostering allied investments and revitalizing U.S. industrial capacity rather than relying on fragmented departmental approaches. This initiative emphasized leveraging executive authority to attract foreign shipyard investments from allies like Japan and South Korea, including tours of facilities in those nations to promote joint ventures in U.S. yards.
Del Toro prioritized accelerating shipbuilding to expand the fleet beyond its approximately 296 battle force ships as of 2024, criticizing domestic yards for slow production ratesoften delivering ships years late and below targetsattributable to supply chain bottlenecks, workforce shortages, and insufficient private investment rather than mere funding gaps. Despite these pushes, including calls for robotics and digitization in yards, the Navy failed to meet the long-standing 355-ship goal from 2016, with projections indicating continued shortfalls through 2025 due to persistent industrial base constraints.
On personnel, Del Toro emphasized sailor retention and lethality amid recruiting challenges, announcing workforce initiatives in November 2024 to address civil service mariner shortages through targeted incentives, while the Navy under his tenure exceeded active-duty enlisted recruiting goals for fiscal years 2024 (40,978 recruits) and 2025's early benchmarks, marking levels unseen since the early 2000s, though afloat billets remained understaffed due to prior shortfalls. Retention rates stayed historically high, supported by mental health forums established in March 2024 to integrate resilience programs across the Department of the Navy.

Criticisms and controversies

In January 2024, during an official trip to the United Kingdom, Secretary Del Toro participated in BBC interviews where he stated that the reelection of President Joe Biden was "vitally important" and criticized former President Donald Trump as a threat to democracy, prompting a complaint to the Office of Special Counsel (OSC). On September 5, 2024, the OSC ruled that these remarks violated the Hatch Act, which prohibits federal officials from using their authority to influence elections, as Del Toro was acting in his official capacity without clarifying personal views. Del Toro apologized on September 14, 2024, acknowledging the violation but facing no formal punishment, a decision critics argued undermined military nonpartisanship by blurring official duties with partisan advocacy.
Del Toro drew criticism for rhetoric targeting defense contractors' profit practices, particularly in a February 15, 2024, speech at the WEST conference where he accused firms of prioritizing stock buybacks over workforce investment and research, labeling it "goosing stock prices" amid delays in ship deliveries. He announced a "deep dive" into chronic underperformance, promising accountability measures like penalties for misconduct, while urging companies to reinvest record profits into the industrial base rather than shareholder returns. Supporters viewed this as necessary pressure to address empirical shortfalls in production timelines, but detractors contended it overlooked root causes such as excessive regulations and bureaucratic delays, potentially deterring investment without reforming government-imposed constraints.
During a May 16, 2024, Senate Armed Services Committee hearing, Republican senators, including Dan Sullivan and Kevin Cramer, accused Del Toro of misallocating resources by elevating climate change initiatives over shipbuilding, citing the Navy's fiscal year 2025 budget request that allocated funds for environmental goals amid stalled fleet expansion. Del Toro countered that 75% of his time focused on shipbuilding and denied deprioritizing lethality, but lawmakers pointed to data showing trade-offs, such as deferred procurement impacting readiness against China's naval buildup, with only 287 battle force ships achieved against a 355-ship goal.
As his tenure ended, Del Toro on January 14, 2025, rebuked Congress for "false accusations" of opacity in submarine program funding, claiming lawmakers' delays caused notification gaps rather than Navy withholding. This followed a December 9, 2024, bipartisan congressional report asserting the Navy failed to disclose cost overruns and funding shortfalls for Virginia-class submarines to the House and Senate armed services committees, eroding trust and complicating appropriations. Lawmakers countered with evidence of repeated lapses in required briefings under Title 10 mandates, attributing delays to executive branch shortcomings rather than legislative inaction.

Post-tenure activities

Advisory roles and recent statements

Following his departure as Secretary of the Navy on January 20, 2025, Carlos Del Toro assumed the role of president and CEO of Del Toro Global Associates, Inc., a strategic advisory firm focused on assisting commercial and government clients with risk management, supply chain optimization, and operational efficiencies drawn from his public and private sector experience. In May 2025, he joined the advisory board of C2 Technologies, Inc., a defense technology firm, to provide guidance on innovation and emerging technologies amid dynamic geopolitical challenges. Del Toro was appointed to the board of directors of Future in Tech (FiT), a technology investment entity, in July 2025, leveraging his naval leadership to advise on rapid capability development for defense applications. On October 21, 2025, he became a member of the Dean's Advisory Board at the Clinton School of Public Service, University of Arkansas, contributing insights on public policy and leadership from his tenure overseeing 900,000 personnel.
Del Toro has engaged in speaking events post-tenure, including as a featured speaker at the 2025 Concordia Annual Summit, where he discussed lessons from naval modernization efforts and the imperative for private-sector agility in government contracting to address shipbuilding delays and industrial base constraints experienced during his service. In his advisory capacity, he has emphasized applying private-sector practicessuch as streamlined procurement and performance-based incentivesto enhance public sector outcomes, critiquing inefficiencies like contractor stock buybacks that divert resources from capacity-building, a theme rooted in his prior push for accountability in defense supply chains.
In October 2025, Del Toro publicly critiqued proposed changes at the U.S. Naval Academy under the incoming administration, opposing the reduction or removal of civilian faculty and arguing that shifting emphasis from rigorous academics to "lethality" would undermine educational quality and long-term officer development. He contended that civilian instructors provide specialized expertise essential for producing technically proficient leaders, warning that militarizing the faculty could erode the academy's dual mission of intellectual and martial preparation. Proponents of the reforms, however, assert that the academy's unique reliance on civilian-dominated academicsunlike other service academieshas diluted the warrior ethos, prioritizing credentialism over empirical operational readiness and combat effectiveness, as evidenced by historical data on midshipmen performance in high-stakes environments favoring ethos-driven training. Del Toro's stance reflects a continuity from his 2024 reflections on statecraft, where he highlighted the Navy's need for balanced innovation amid fiscal constraints, but post-tenure, it underscores a pivot toward defending institutional academic structures against perceived overcorrections in lethality-focused reforms.

Personal life

Family and heritage

Carlos Del Toro was born in Havana, Cuba, on January 22, 1961. He immigrated to the United States with his parents as a refugee in 1962 at the age of one, shortly after Fidel Castro's communist regime consolidated power following the 1959 revolution, which prompted mass exoduses due to political repression and economic collapse under socialism. Raised in New York City's Hell's Kitchen neighborhood, Del Toro's family relied on his father's manual labor in jobs such as dishwashing to sustain themselves amid the challenges of immigrant assimilation.
Del Toro is married to Betty Del Toro, whom he has described as a lifelong partner and co-founder of their post-military business. The couple has four children, though further details on his immediate family remain private. His Cuban-American heritage, forged from his parents' flight from socialism's coercive failuresevidenced by Cuba's post-revolution poverty rates exceeding 50% and over a million exiles by 1970instilled a realist perspective on the causal links between collectivist policies and human suffering, contrasted with the individual agency enabled by U.S. freedoms that facilitated his family's upward mobility.
