The Remaining
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Remaining is a 2014 American supernatural horror film directed by Casey La Scala. The story follows a group of close friends gathered for a wedding whose celebration is abruptly interrupted by apocalyptic events depicted as the biblical Rapture and subsequent tribulations foretold in the Book of Revelation, compelling the survivors to evade demonic entities while grappling with themes of faith, regret, and salvation. Starring Alexa PenaVega as Skylar, Shaun Sipos as Tommy, Bryan Dechart as Dan, and Johnny Pacar as Aiden, the film blends elements of disaster thriller and faith-based narrative to explore end-times prophecy.
Released theatrically on September 5, 2014, The Remaining targeted audiences interested in Christian eschatology, positioning itself as a cinematic interpretation of scriptural warnings about the world's final days. It earned approximately $1.17 million in North American box office receipts and $2.3 million worldwide, reflecting its modest independent production scale aimed at niche markets rather than broad commercial appeal. Critical reception was generally unfavorable, with a 60% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on a limited sample of 10 reviews and a Metacritic score of 38 out of 100, often citing formulaic execution despite its ambitious fusion of horror and religious motifs. Audience responses varied, with some praising its unapologetic embrace of biblical literalism amid supernatural terror, while others critiqued its pacing and reliance on genre conventions. The film distinguishes itself in the horror genre through its explicit grounding in evangelical interpretations of prophecy, diverging from secular apocalyptic tales by emphasizing personal accountability to divine judgment.

Synopsis

Plot Summary

The film The Remaining depicts a group of friends gathered for the wedding of Skylar and Dan, which coincides with the onset of biblical apocalyptic events interpreted as the Rapture. As the ceremony concludes, sudden vanishings occur: Skylar's parents disappear in an elevator, leaving their bodies behind, while other guests, including children, are found lifeless with eyes open, sparking panic among the survivors.
The remaining friendsSkylar, Dan, videographer Tommy, Jack, and later othersattempt to reunite with Jack's girlfriend Allison, who had stormed off after an argument, amid escalating chaos including crashing airplanes, a massive earthquake, and enormous hailstones forcing them to seek shelter first in a library and then a church. Skylar, suspecting the Rapture based on scriptural prophecies, urges the group to examine their faith, joined by a minister who explains the disappearances as believers being taken to heaven, leaving clothes and bodies as evidence. Demonic entities, resembling locusts from Revelation 9, emerge to torment the unbelievers with venomous stings and abductions, inflicting gruesome wounds and heightening the horror.
As injuries mountSkylar succumbs to demonic venomand secrets are confessed in the church, the survivors grapple with regret over past dismissals of Christianity, facing a stark choice between defiant survival and submission to God. Attempts to reach safety at a relief camp fail amid further attacks, culminating in the sounding of the last trumpet and descent of fallen angels, underscoring the narrative's theme that only acceptance of faith offers escape from the ensuing doom. The group ultimately perishes, emphasizing the irreversible consequences of their prior unbelief.

Cast and Characters

Principal Roles

Johnny Pacar stars as Tommy, the groom whose wedding celebration serves as the story's inciting event amid escalating apocalyptic events.
Shaun Sipos portrays Jack, a close friend of the group who confronts the unfolding chaos following the rapture.
Bryan Dechart plays Dan, another member of the wedding party grappling with survival and spiritual doubt in the post-rapture world.
Alexa PenaVega depicts Skylar, the bride whose union with Tommy draws the friends together before global catastrophe strikes.
Italia Ricci appears as Allison, contributing to the ensemble of survivors navigating demonic threats and personal reckonings.
Liz E. Morgan rounds out the core group as Sam, facing supernatural horrors alongside the others in the film's depiction of tribulation.

Production

Development and Writing

Casey La Scala originated the concept for The Remaining in response to the stalling of another project, Amityville: The Awakening, due to rights complications, allowing him to advance this script more rapidly. Influenced by his Lutheran background, childhood teachings on the Rapture and Tribulation, and personal loss including his father's death, La Scala envisioned a narrative centered on end-times events depicted in the Book of Revelation, particularly the seven trumpets and global calamities.
The initial draft, penned by La Scala, adopted a more secular tone and was structured as found-footage from a single point-of-view perspective to evoke a first-person shooter experience. He began by developing character arcs before layering in biblical elements, aiming to prioritize emotional depth and redemption themes over overt preaching. The script evolved into a hybrid format blending traditional narrative with found-footage segments to accommodate broader storytelling, budget limitations, and enhanced character exploration.
La Scala co-wrote the final version with Chris Dowling, incorporating input from faith-based consultants to align depictionssuch as the Rapture mechanics and trumpet judgmentswith evangelical interpretations of scripture while retaining mainstream horror appeal. Challenges included revising non-biblical inventions, like falling birds, for greater authenticity and navigating the tension between graphic supernatural horror and a PG-13 rating, often implying scares off-screen. Influences extended to works like Stephen King's The Stand for its blend of human drama and apocalyptic scale. La Scala described the writing as his primary passion, emphasizing intimate knowledge of the script to facilitate directing decisions.

Filming and Technical Aspects

Principal photography for The Remaining took place primarily in Wilmington, North Carolina, utilizing local locations to depict the film's apocalyptic events following a wedding celebration. The choice of Wilmington provided a mix of urban and coastal settings suitable for scenes involving chaos and survival amid biblical cataclysms, with production wrapping under the oversight of Affirm Films.
Cinematographer Doug Emmett handled the visual capture, emphasizing practical on-location shooting to maintain a grounded realism despite the supernatural elements. The film incorporates a handheld camera technique in its initial sequences to convey urgency and immediacy during character introductions and the onset of disasters, evoking a documentary-like tension akin to found-footage styles.
Technical effects were constrained by the production's modest budget, with director Casey La Scala prioritizing implication over explicit visuals for infernal entities such as winged demons, achieved through sound design, shadows, and selective practical elements rather than extensive CGI. This approach extended to earthquake simulations and mass vanishings, relying on editing and atmospheric cues to heighten horror without overt spectacle, aligning with the film's focus on theological dread over high-production spectacle. Post-filming, behind-the-scenes documentation by EPK Films captured crew workflows, though specific equipment details like camera models remain unpublicized in available production records.

Post-Production

Post-production on The Remaining encompassed editing, visual effects integration, sound design, and scoring to realize the film's apocalyptic horror elements within an 88-minute runtime. Editor Paul Covington assembled the principal footage, incorporating a handheld camera style in the opening montage to evoke immediacy and a pseudo-found-footage aesthetic, which transitions into more conventional narrative shots amid the Rapture-inspired chaos. 
Visual effects by Nathan Abbot augmented practical effects for cataclysmic sequences, including earthquakes, structural collapses, and supernatural manifestations drawn from biblical eschatology, ensuring seamless blending with live-action plates despite the film's modest budget. Sound post-production featured foley artistry by Tara Blume to heighten tension through amplified impacts and atmospheric dread, alongside re-recording mixing by Tom Boykin to balance dialogue, effects, and an original score composed by Nathan Whitehead, which underscored themes of judgment and redemption with orchestral swells and dissonant cues. Color grading by Jonathan Reid and digital intermediate editing by Vahe Giragol finalized the visual tone, emphasizing desaturated palettes for post-Rapture desolation. Consulting editor Glenn Garland provided oversight to maintain pacing across the thriller's escalating disasters.

Themes and Biblical Elements

Apocalyptic Narrative and Rapture Depiction

The film depicts the Rapture as a sudden, instantaneous event occurring amid a wedding celebration among a group of young friends, where select individualssuch as the bride's parentsvanish without trace, leaving behind only their clothing and personal effects in a manner consistent with premillennial dispensationalist interpretations of 1 Thessalonians 4:16-17. Accompanied by ominous trumpet blasts echoing globally, the sequence conveys foreboding terror through found-footage aesthetics, capturing immediate pandemonium including earthquakes, structural collapses, and widespread accidents like plummeting aircraft. This portrayal emphasizes the event's selectivity, sparing only true believers while thrusting the protagonistsinitially bewildered and faith-questioninginto survival mode.
In the ensuing narrative, the survivors navigate a post-Rapture landscape marked by escalating apocalyptic signs drawn from the Book of Revelation, including demonic manifestations that hunt and possess the unraptured, symbolizing spiritual warfare during the Tribulation period. The film illustrates causal consequences of the event through scenes of societal breakdown, such as looted cities and biblical plagues inferred from scripture, prompting characters to consult the Bible directlyhighlighting the term "Rapture" as an interpretive concept rather than explicit terminologyand confront personal unbelief as the root of their predicament. This progression underscores a cautionary framework where the "remaining" endure judgment, with opportunities for late repentance amid intensifying horrors like supernatural entities enforcing divine wrath. 
Thematically, the depiction aligns with a pre-Tribulation Rapture theology, portraying the vanishing as a divine separation of the faithful prior to seven years of escalating calamities, though it amplifies horror elementssuch as graphic demon attacksfor dramatic effect rather than strict literalism. Critics have noted the film's reliance on visual shorthand for end-times chaos, including derelict infrastructure and ethereal adversaries, to evoke urgency in examining one's spiritual state, while avoiding deeper exegesis in favor of visceral survival thriller dynamics.

Theological Motivations and Scriptural Basis

The film The Remaining draws its theological framework from premillennial dispensationalism, portraying a pre-tribulation rapture followed by catastrophic judgments during the Great Tribulation, as envisioned by director Casey La Scala in alignment with certain evangelical interpretations of end-times prophecy. La Scala, raised in a Lutheran tradition emphasizing the Rapture and Tribulation, sought to convey the raw terror of these events to underscore the urgency of authentic faith over nominal spirituality or cultural Christianity, motivating viewers to self-examine their relationship with Christ amid apocalyptic chaos. This approach serves as a cautionary evangelical tool, highlighting that mere religiosity or delayed commitment leaves individuals vulnerable to divine wrath, with characters' post-rapture conversions emphasizing personal acceptance of Jesus as essential for salvation.
Scripturally, the rapture sequence is rooted in passages depicting believers being suddenly taken to spare them from coming judgments, echoing 1 Thessalonians 4:16-17's description of the Lord descending with a shout and the dead in Christ rising first, though the film innovates by showing vanishings during a wedding reception without explicit trumpet sounds to heighten immediacy. Subsequent tribulations align closely with Revelation 8-9, incorporating the seven trumpets and woes: hailstones of immense size pummeling the earth (Revelation 8:7), locust-like creatures tormenting the unsealed (Revelation 9:3-10), and the abyss opening to release demonic entities (Revelation 9:1-2), which the protagonists witness and interpret via direct biblical consultation. La Scala explicitly consulted these texts for accuracy, structuring the narrative to transition from natural disasters to supernatural assaults, mirroring Revelation's progression.
Additional scriptural underpinnings include Matthew 24:42's call to vigilance"Therefore keep watch, because you do not know on what day your Lord will come"invoked to explain the rapture's unpredictability and the peril of unpreparedness, as characters like Skylar grapple with prior complacency in faith. The film reinforces Romans 10:9's requirement of confessing Jesus as Lord for salvation, portraying post-rapture baptisms and declarations as potential paths to redemption, though theological discussions in promotional materials question whether such late professions suffice amid tribulation. This basis prioritizes a literal reading of prophetic texts to evoke horror not for sensationalism but to align with biblical warnings of judgment, distinguishing genuine believers through their pre-rapture commitment.

Release and Distribution

Theatrical Premiere

The film had its world premiere at the London FrightFest Film Festival on August 25, 2014. It then received a limited theatrical release in the United States on September 5, 2014, distributed by Sony Pictures through its faith-based division Affirm Films. The rollout targeted select theaters, reflecting the film's independent production budget and niche appeal to Christian audiences amid broader end-times genre interest. International expansion followed, with a release in the Philippines on September 24, 2014. No major red-carpet events or wide promotional premieres were reported beyond the festival screening, consistent with the film's modest marketing strategy focused on church networks and genre festivals.

Marketing and Home Media

The marketing campaign for The Remaining emphasized its blend of supernatural horror and biblical end-times themes, positioning the film as a thriller inspired by the Book of Revelation to appeal to both genre enthusiasts and Christian audiences. Affirm Films, a division of Sony Pictures, promoted the movie through digital trailers on platforms like YouTube and iTunes, which secured high visibility in search engine results alongside IMDb listings, reflecting a budget-conscious indie strategy focused on online discoverability rather than large-scale advertising. Promotional efforts included interviews with cast members, such as Alexa Vega, highlighting the film's depiction of the Rapture as a cautionary tale rather than a celebratory event, shared via Christian media outlets to underscore its theological undertones.
Home media distribution followed the September 2014 theatrical release, with Sony Pictures Home Entertainment issuing The Remaining on Blu-ray, DVD, and Digital HD formats on January 27, 2015. The Blu-ray edition featured 1080p high-definition video in a 1.85:1 aspect ratio with Dolby TrueHD 5.1 audio, alongside standard DVD and digital options for broader accessibility. Special features were limited but included director commentary and behind-the-scenes content, aligning with the film's modest production scale. Retail availability through major outlets like Amazon, Walmart, and Barnes & Noble supported ongoing sales to home viewers interested in faith-based horror. 

Reception

Critical Assessments

Mainstream critics offered mixed assessments of The Remaining, with aggregate scores reflecting general reservations about its execution and theological framing despite acknowledging its technical competence relative to similar faith-based productions. On Rotten Tomatoes, the film holds a 60% approval rating from 10 reviews, indicating a divided response. Metacritic assigns a weighted score of 38 out of 100 based on four reviews, categorizing it as "generally unfavorable." These metrics stem from a limited pool of critiques, as the film's niche appeal and modest $850,000 budget limited wide coverage from major outlets.
Critics frequently praised the film's found-footage style for creating immediacy and tension, particularly in its opening sequences depicting a wedding disrupted by apocalyptic events. Variety noted its "fitfully exciting" melodrama, crediting director Casey La Scala's handling of horror elements amid theological content. Empire magazine awarded 60 out of 100, likening it to Paranormal Activity transposed to a wedding setting, suggesting it delivers adequate scares without major innovations. Some reviewers, such as Nathan Rabin, described it as "stirringly adequate" and "totally acceptable" within the subgenre of explicitly Christian horror, implying it exceeds expectations for low-budget entries that often prioritize evangelism over craft.
However, detractors highlighted narrative inconsistencies, underdeveloped characters, and a perceived mismatch between horror tropes and biblical literalism. The Washington Post assigned a score of 12 out of 100, criticizing its reliance on generic scares over substantive depth. The Dissolve gave 50 out of 100, faulting the film's attempt to blend rapture theology with supernatural horror as uneven, resulting in a story that feels contrived rather than revelatory. Variety further questioned its theological underpinnings, arguing the depiction of post-rapture chaos veers into speculative territory unsupported by scripture, potentially alienating viewers seeking doctrinal fidelity. Christianity Today observed that, as a thriller, it "doesn't quite hit the mark," with pacing issues diluting the impact of its end-times premise.
Overall, assessments underscore a divide: while the film garners credit for competent production values and restraint compared to sensationalist peers, its fusion of eschatological themes with horror conventions drew skepticism from secular critics wary of proselytizing intent, and qualified endorsement from those tolerant of genre constraints.

Audience and Box Office Performance

The Remaining debuted in a limited theatrical release on September 5, 2014, generating $159,143 during its opening weekend from 67 theaters. The film concluded its domestic run with a total gross of $1,169,603, accounting for its entire worldwide box office as no international earnings were reported. This performance aligned with expectations for an independent faith-based production, which prioritized targeted distribution over broad commercial appeal.
General audience reception proved polarized, reflected in a 37% score on Rotten Tomatoes from over 2,500 verified user ratings, where viewers frequently cited uneven pacing, acting, and reliance on genre tropes as shortcomings. Similarly, IMDb aggregated a 4.3 out of 10 rating across 9,672 user votes, with critiques centering on predictable plotting and limited character development amid the apocalyptic premise. In contrast, segments of the intended Christian demographic expressed stronger approval, valuing the film's scriptural allusions and cautionary themes on judgment and redemption, as evidenced in affirmative assessments from outlets like The Christian Post and Movieguide. Home video sales supplemented theatrical revenue, estimating $863,438 from domestic DVD and Blu-ray units.

Faith Community Responses

Faith communities, particularly within evangelical Christianity, largely welcomed The Remaining as an innovative tool for evangelism, appreciating its dramatization of the Rapture's aftermath drawn from interpretations of Revelation and 1 Thessalonians 4:16-17. Reviews from outlets affiliated with conservative Christian organizations highlighted the film's emphasis on genuine relational faith over nominal religion, portraying survivors' regrets and demonic torments as a stark warning against spiritual complacency. The Christian Post described it as a "terrifying cautionary tale" that provides both believers and skeptics with a visceral perspective on end-times events, praising its high-quality effects and unapologetic biblical messaging.
Plugged In, a review service from Focus on the Family, commended the movie's theological corestressing salvation through Christ amid apocalyptic chaoswhile cautioning parents about its intense violence, including locust-like attacks and body counts evoking Revelation 9, which could overwhelm younger audiences despite the redemptive intent. Similarly, Christian Spotlight on the Movies rated it an "above average" effort in the faith-based horror genre, valuing its independent production's aim to depict tribulation horrors but noting potential viewer unease with deviations from fuller eschatological details, such as the absence of the Antichrist or the 144,000 witnesses.
Church discussion guides distributed by Affirm Films, Sony's faith division that handled release, encouraged group viewings to probe questions of authentic faith's necessity, aligning with premillennial dispensationalist views prevalent in many evangelical circles. Director Casey La Scala, in interviews with Christian media, defended the film's scriptural fidelity, intending the horror elements to evoke real fear of separation from God rather than mere entertainment, resonating with audiences seeking culturally engaging apologetics. While some grassroots responses critiqued the rapture's understated depiction or pastoral character's exclusion for lacking personal devotion, the overall reception framed it as a timely prod to self-examine faith amid secular media's end-times tropes.

Controversies and Critiques

Theological Accuracy Debates

The film's portrayal of the pre-tribulation rapture, where believing Christians are instantaneously taken to heaven while leaving their physical bodies behind at a wedding ceremony, has drawn scrutiny for deviating from predominant theological interpretations. While some historical commentators like Matthew Poole have suggested bodies may remain post-rapture, most biblical scholars argue this mechanic lacks clear scriptural support, emphasizing instead a transformation of living believers without explicit mention of discarded corpses.
Critics have highlighted omissions of key eschatological figures and events, such as the Antichrist's rise and the sealing of the 144,000 witnesses from Revelation 7, which the film condenses into a rapid sequence of judgments focused on a small group of survivors rather than the full seven-year tribulation outlined in Daniel 9:27 and Revelation. This compression prioritizes horror elements over comprehensive prophetic sequencing, potentially misleading viewers on the tribulation's structure.
The depiction of demonic locusts from Revelation 9 attacking both believers and unbelievers contradicts Revelation 9:4, which specifies harm only to those without God's seal, raising concerns about inaccurate rendering of divine protection during judgments. Additionally, the film's assumption of post-rapture conversions being nearly impossible or fatal aligns with certain pre-tribulational views but has been critiqued for overstating barriers to salvation amid tribulations.
Broader debates question the film's pre-tribulation rapture framework itself, with detractors like writer Clive Price arguing it instills fear of being "left behind" at the expense of emphasizing God's kindness leading to repentance (Romans 2:4), potentially causing spiritual distress rather than hope. Such critiques reflect ongoing divisions between pre-tribulational dispensationalism, which the film endorses, and alternative views like post-tribulationism or amillennialism that reject a secret rapture preceding widespread judgments.

Horror Genre Sensationalism Claims

Some reviewers contended that The Remaining inappropriately adapted apocalyptic biblical imagery into mainstream horror conventions, amplifying supernatural threats like demonic locust swarms and body-possessing entities from Revelation 9 to generate tension through graphic violence and jump scares, rather than emphasizing scriptural nuance. This approach, critics argued, risked exploiting audience fears of death and chaos to underscore the film's pre-tribulation rapture premise, where survivors confront escalating tribulations post-event.
Washington Post critic Mark Jenkins characterized the film as "a low-budget, low-impact attempt to rewrite the Book of Revelation as a horror flick," pointing to its core contradiction: while deaths serve as gateways to spiritual elevation for the faithful, the narrative builds suspense via mounting casualties and unseen terrors, potentially diluting eschatological gravity in favor of genre thrills. Crosswalk reviewer Shawn McEvoy echoed this, faulting the production for methodological inconsistencyteasing horror elements like shadowy monsters without fully committing, thus failing to effectively "scare" while alienating Christian viewers wary of sensational depictions.
Such critiques highlighted the film's visual effects budget constraints, which rendered some prophetic calamities (e.g., vanishing souls leaving empty clothing, plummeting aircraft) more derivative of disaster-horror hybrids than authentically reverent, prompting accusations of prioritizing box-office appeal over doctrinal fidelity. Plugged In, while acknowledging the intent to illustrate post-rapture accountability, cautioned against the "high body count" and "gory wounds," suggesting the intensityuncommon in faith-oriented cinemacould unsettle audiences seeking inspirational rather than visceral content. These observations, from outlets spanning secular and evangelical perspectives, underscored broader debates on whether infusing end-times theology with horror tropes fosters evangelism or merely commodifies scripture.
