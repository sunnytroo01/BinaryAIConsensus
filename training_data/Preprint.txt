 A preprint is a preliminary version of a scholarly manuscript that researchers make publicly available online, typically on dedicated preprint servers, before undergoing formal peer review and publication in a journal. These documents allow for rapid dissemination of research findings while remaining freely accessible to the global scientific community. Preprints originated in the physical sciences, where physicists circulated unpublished papers via mail as early as the late 1940s, but the digital era transformed the practice with the launch of the first online server, arXiv, in 1991 by Paul Ginsparg at Los Alamos National Laboratory. Initially focused on high-energy physics, arXiv expanded to include mathematics, computer science, and other fields, now hosting over 2.8 million articles as of November 2025 and serving as a cornerstone of open access sharing. The model proliferated across disciplines, with biology adopting bioRxiv in 2013 and health sciences following with medRxiv in 2019, reflecting a broader shift toward accelerating scientific communication.
Preprints play a crucial role in modern scholarly publishing by enabling researchers to establish priority for discoveries, solicit early feedback from peers, and share results without the delays of traditional journal timelines, which can span months or years. This speed proved invaluable during the COVID-19 pandemic, when servers like medRxiv and bioRxiv hosted over 10,000 articles in the first year alone, informing public health responses and global collaborations. Despite not being peer-reviewed, preprints undergo basic moderation for appropriateness and are often linked to subsequent peer-reviewed versions, fostering transparency and iterative improvement in research. Their growth has raised discussions on quality control and potential for misinformation, yet major funders and journals, including those from Nature and Science portfolios, increasingly endorse them as complementary to formal publication. Today, preprints represent a vital tool for open science, with usage surging across STEM fields and beyond, democratizing access to cutting-edge knowledge.

Definition and Basics

Definition

A preprint is an unpublished scholarly manuscript that authors share publicly to disseminate research findings rapidly before undergoing formal peer review or publication in a journal. It represents an early version of a research paper, typically uploaded to an online platform where it becomes freely accessible to the global research community. Unlike final published articles, preprints are not subject to editorial copy-editing, formatting standards, or peer validation at the time of release.
Core characteristics of preprints include their completeness as a draft document, encompassing the full text, figures, tables, and often supplementary materials such as data or methods details, presented in a single file like a PDF. They are commonly versioned, allowing authors to update and revise the manuscript over time while maintaining a timestamped record of changes. Preprints lack the polished presentation of journal articles, retaining the author's original wording and structure without professional editing.
The primary purpose of preprints is to accelerate the sharing of scientific knowledge, enabling immediate access to new discoveries and fostering early feedback from peers to refine the work. They also establish priority for ideas and findings, providing a citable record that can support career progression and collaboration. By bypassing traditional delays, preprints promote open science principles and broader dissemination in fields where timely information is critical.
Preprints commonly take the form of draft research papers across disciplines, such as experimental studies in the natural sciences, theoretical analyses in physics, or empirical investigations in the social sciences and humanities. For instance, they might include initial reports on clinical trials in medicine or qualitative analyses in sociology, all shared as complete manuscripts to invite community input.

Distinction from Other Publications

Preprints differ from peer-reviewed journal articles primarily in the absence of formal peer review, editorial oversight, and production processes such as copyediting and formatting. While peer-reviewed articles undergo rigorous evaluation by independent experts to validate methodology, results, and conclusions before publication, preprints represent the author's version of a manuscript deposited on a public server without such scrutiny, allowing for immediate dissemination but potentially containing errors or unrefined content. In many academic fields, preprints are not considered formal publications, though they are widely used to establish priority via timestamps and DOIs, and are citable, unlike the final validated journal versions.
In contrast to working papers, which are often informal drafts circulated within specific disciplines like economics for internal feedback and typically hosted on institutional repositories without persistent identifiers, preprints are more structured documents intended for broader public access. Preprints are usually complete manuscripts ready for submission, archived on dedicated servers with digital object identifiers (DOIs) for stable citation and discoverability, emphasizing open accessibility over limited distribution.
Preprints also stand apart from conference proceedings and other forms of gray literature, such as technical reports or theses, by serving as independent, full-length manuscripts not bound to a specific event or organizational output. Conference proceedings capture concise summaries or extended abstracts presented at meetings, often with some peer review but tied to the conference timeline, whereas preprints prioritize rapid, unrestricted sharing of comprehensive research texts outside any event context. Similarly, while preprints fall under gray literature as non-commercially published works, they specifically focus on expedited full-text availability for scholarly scrutiny, distinct from the varied formats and purposes of other gray materials like policy briefs.
Regarding legal and versioning aspects, preprints enable authors to retain copyright and post iterative updates to reflect revisions from feedback or new data, with servers maintaining version histories without requiring retraction of earlier iterations, unlike the fixed nature of final publications where changes demand formal errata or corrections. This flexibility supports ongoing refinement while preserving timestamped records for priority claims, provided authors adhere to licensing terms that typically allow non-commercial reuse with attribution.

Terminology and Neighbor Concepts

Preprint is part of a family of version terms that are often conflated:

Preprint

any version that has not yet undergone formal peer review (often called the authors original manuscript or submitted manuscript).

Postprint

the authors accepted manuscript after peer review and acceptance, but before publisher typesetting and formatting (often AAM, accepted author manuscript).

Version of record (VoR)

the final published, copyedited, typeset, formatted version issued by the publisher, contrasted with earlier versions such as preprints and postprints.

E-print

a broad umbrella that can include preprints and postprints (field-dependent usage).

These distinctions are not mere vocabulary: they structure citation, responsibility, and public trust.

Historical Development

Origins and Early Use

The practice of sharing preprints originated in the mid-20th century as a response to delays in traditional journal publishing, particularly in rapidly advancing fields like physics where timely dissemination was essential for collaboration and establishing intellectual priority. Following World War II, physicists faced an information crisis due to the explosion of research output, leading to the informal circulation of unpublished manuscripts via mail to peers across institutions, allowing results to reach the community months ahead of formal publication. This pre-digital approach was especially prevalent in high-energy physics (HEP), where journal backlogs could extend to 23 years, prompting researchers to prioritize speed over peer review in sharing preliminary findings.
In the late 1950s, CERN established the world's first centralized preprint repository in Geneva, collecting working papers and reports submitted by scientists from global institutions to facilitate rapid exchange within the HEP community. These documents were duplicated and mailed to subscribers, with the system evolving from simple filing cabinets to a more structured index by the early 1960s, reflecting the growing volume of submissions driven by international collaborations at accelerators like CERN's Proton Synchrotron. By the early 1960s, preprint proliferation in HEP had become unmanageable without coordination, as individual labs and researchers exchanged thousands of copies annually through postal networks and in-house distributions.
Key milestones in the 1960s included the founding of the SLAC Library in 1962, tasked with acquiring and cataloging HEP preprints under the guidance of CERN experts, and the launch of SLAC's Preprints in Particles and Fields (PPF) service in 1969, which distributed weekly lists and copies to over 300 institutions worldwide. At Los Alamos National Laboratory, a hub for post-war nuclear and particle physics research, preprint sharing gained traction in the 1960s through internal reports and mailed exchanges among scientists, setting the stage for formalized archives in the following decade. Usage remained confined to specialized disciplines, with physics leading due to its collaborative demands; in economics, the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) initiated its formal working paper series in 1973, enabling economists to circulate draft research via mail to claim priority and solicit feedback before journal submission. These early efforts underscored the motivation to accelerate knowledge transfer in fast-paced fields, where delays could hinder progress or allow others to preempt discoveries.

Evolution in the Digital Age

The digital era marked a pivotal shift for preprints, transitioning from physical distribution to electronic platforms that enabled global, instantaneous access. In 1991, physicist Paul Ginsparg launched arXiv at Los Alamos National Laboratory as an automated email-based repository for physics preprints, fundamentally revolutionizing the practice by allowing researchers to submit and retrieve manuscripts electronically without the logistical burdens of mailing paper copies. This innovation quickly expanded beyond initial physics submissions, incorporating mathematics and computer science by the mid-1990s, and demonstrated the potential of centralized digital archives to accelerate knowledge sharing across disciplines.
Building on arXiv's model, preprint adoption proliferated to other fields throughout the 1990s and 2000s, with dedicated servers emerging for biology via bioRxiv in 2013, operated by Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory to facilitate rapid dissemination in the life sciences. Social sciences followed suit with the launch of SocArXiv in 2016, providing an open archive hosted on the Open Science Framework to support interdisciplinary work in sociology, economics, and related areas. By the 2020s, this expansion reached the humanities, exemplified by increased use of platforms for fields like the history of science, where researchers leveraged general repositories such as OSF Preprints to share working papers and foster collaborative inquiry.
Key technological advancements further propelled this evolution, including the assignment of Digital Object Identifiers (DOIs) to preprints starting in 2016 through Crossref, which enhanced their citability and integration into scholarly databases. Open-access mandates from funders, such as those under Plan S introduced in 2018 by cOAlition S, increasingly encouraged preprint deposition to comply with immediate accessibility requirements. Additionally, integration with ORCID identifiers allowed preprint servers to automatically link submissions to authors' persistent profiles, improving attribution and discoverability across platforms like arXiv and bioRxiv.
Notable events underscored the growing reliance on digital preprints, particularly during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, when servers like medRxiv and bioRxiv experienced a surge in virology-related submissions to enable rapid global sharing of research on the virus. In the 2020s, the artificial intelligence and machine learning communities further embraced arXiv, with submissions in categories like cs.LG (machine learning) more than doubling from 2019 levels, reflecting the field's need for quick iteration and community feedback on rapidly evolving models.

Role in Academia

Integration into Publishing Workflow

Preprints are typically posted after the initial drafting of a manuscript but prior to formal submission to a peer-reviewed journal, allowing authors to establish priority and solicit early feedback without preempting the traditional publication process. This placement in the workflow enables iterative updates; for instance, authors may revise and repost versions incorporating peer review comments after journal acceptance, aligning the preprint more closely with the final published article. Such versioning maintains a public record of the research's evolution while preserving the preprint's role as an interim step.
Many academic publishers have adapted their policies to accommodate preprints, explicitly permitting their posting without risking claims of scooping or prior publication. For example, PLOS journals endorse preprint deposition and include scooping protection policies that safeguard authors' priority from the preprint's timestamp, even if similar work appears elsewhere in the interim. Similarly, Nature and its affiliates support preprint sharing as part of open science practices, viewing it as compatible with subsequent journal submission. This integration often aligns with the "green open access" pathway, where preprints serve as self-archived versions of manuscripts to fulfill open access mandates without conflicting with subscription-based journal models.
By facilitating community-driven review before formal peer review, preprints create a feedback loop that accelerates the refinement of research and can shorten overall publication timelines. Researchers receive informal critiques from peers via comments on platforms or direct correspondence, enabling revisions that strengthen submissions to journals and potentially reducing time-to-publication by weeks or months through early issue identification. This pre-submission scrutiny complements rather than replaces journal peer review, fostering a more efficient workflow.
Adoption of preprints varies significantly by discipline, with routine use in STEM fields such as physics, where over 95% of high-energy physics articles are deposited on servers like arXiv before journal publication, reflecting a long-standing norm. In medicine, preprinting is emerging, particularly accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic through platforms like medRxiv, though adoption remains lower compared to physics due to concerns over clinical accuracy. In the humanities, preprint practices are still nascent, with limited but growing uptake on specialized servers, driven by efforts to adapt open sharing models to interpretive scholarship.
Following initial sharing, authors can pursue longer-term strategies to further promote their preprints. A common approach is to submit the manuscript to peer-reviewed journals across various disciplines, as many editors actively monitor preprint servers to identify and recruit promising submissions. Additionally, creating supplementary visuals, such as diagrams or infographics illustrating key concepts, and sharing them as images can enhance dissemination and engagement. Authors may also crosspost summary versions on academic blogging platforms like Medium or Substack, linking to the full preprint PDF, to reach broader audiences and foster discussions.

Influence on Career Advancement

In the 2020s, universities have increasingly recognized preprints as valid scholarly outputs in evaluations for tenure and promotion, allowing them to be listed on curricula vitae alongside peer-reviewed publications. For instance, within the University of California system, UCSF faculty job advertisements encourage applicants to include preprint server publications with DOIs in their CVs, while UC Davis added a dedicated "preprints" category to its faculty achievements database to facilitate tracking for promotion reviews. Similarly, the UC Academic Senate has endorsed policies that remove restrictions on preprint use in academic workflows, supporting their integration into personnel evaluations.
Funding agencies have also adapted guidelines to incorporate preprints, particularly since the 2010s, to demonstrate research progress and establish priority. The National Institutes of Health (NIH) issued a 2017 notice permitting investigators to cite preprints and other interim research products in grant progress reports, biosketches, and renewal applications, treating them as evidence of NIH-supported outputs. The National Science Foundation (NSF) similarly allows preprints with DOIs to be included in biographical sketches as products of prior support, aiding in grant proposals and reports to highlight ongoing contributions. These policies enable researchers to claim credit for work without awaiting peer review, which can affirm priority in competitive funding landscapes.
Preprints offer substantial benefits to early-career researchers by accelerating visibility and citation accrual compared to traditional publishing timelines. Junior researchers can disseminate findings immediately, building a public record that garners feedback and collaborations faster than the typical 3-6 month peer-review delay in life sciences journals. Studies show that preprinting correlates with higher productivity metrics for early-career scholars, including increased citations for subsequent peer-reviewed versions, as it enhances discoverability through open access and social media attention.
Despite these advances, challenges persist in the uniform recognition of preprints across academia, with institutional policies varying widely and some disciplines still emphasizing journal impact factors over preprint metrics. In fields like humanities and social sciences, where peer-reviewed journals remain the gold standard, preprints may face skepticism due to concerns over quality assurance and potential citation dilution, limiting their weight in tenure dossiers. This uneven adoption can disadvantage researchers in conservative evaluation frameworks that prioritize established publication venues.

Preprint Platforms

Categories of Servers

Preprint servers can be categorized by their disciplinary scope, distinguishing between discipline-specific platforms, which focus on particular fields to facilitate targeted dissemination and community engagement, and general or multidisciplinary servers that accommodate submissions across diverse subjects. Discipline-specific servers, such as those dedicated to physics, mathematics, biology, or medicine, enable researchers to share work within specialized audiences, often with subject-area categorization for easier discovery. In contrast, general servers accept preprints from any discipline, promoting broader accessibility and interoperability, as seen in platforms like Zenodo that integrate with various research outputs beyond manuscripts. General-purpose repositories that accept preprints alongside datasets, software, and other research outputs have also been used to host experiments at the boundary of AI authorship and digital humanities. Projects such as Aisentica deposit philosophical preprints and machine-readable identity schemas for an AI-based Digital Author Persona, Angela Bogdanova, on platforms like Zenodo, assigning DOIs and linking them to an ORCID profile. In these cases, the server functions simultaneously as a preprint archive and as an infrastructure node where a non-human personas contributions are registered, versioned, and made discoverable in the same way as those of human authors. This dichotomy allows authors to select servers aligned with their field's norms while general options support interdisciplinary work.
Another key classification differentiates between pure archives and overlay journals. Pure preprint archives serve as repositories for unrefereed manuscripts, emphasizing rapid sharing without formal evaluation, where content is deposited, versioned, and made publicly available. Overlay journals, however, operate atop these archives by curating and peer-reviewing selected preprints, providing a layer of validation while leveraging the server's infrastructure for hosting and distribution. This model transforms archives into foundational resources for post-deposition review processes, blending preprint speed with journal-like rigor.
Servers also vary by governance structure, contrasting institutional repositories, typically hosted and managed by universities or research organizations to capture outputs from affiliated scholars, with community-driven platforms operated by non-profits or scholarly consortia independent of single institutions. Institutional servers prioritize local compliance and integration with university systems, often supporting preprints alongside theses and datasets. Community-driven servers, such as those run by academic societies or open science initiatives, foster global collaboration and discipline-wide standards through volunteer moderation and decentralized funding. This division influences sustainability, with community models relying on collective contributions to maintain neutrality and scalability.
Regarding access models, the vast majority of preprint servers operate on free and open principles, providing unrestricted public access to deposited content under permissive licenses like Creative Commons, without subscription barriers or paywalls. Gated access is uncommon but may occur in institutional contexts where submissions require affiliation verification, though final preprints remain openly downloadable. Most platforms support versioning, allowing authors to upload revised manuscripts with timestamps and identifiers to track evolution without overwriting originals, ensuring transparency in iterative research. Moderation policies typically involve light screening for plagiarism, relevance to scope, and basic formatting, conducted by volunteers or staff to uphold quality without imposing peer review, though some servers apply stricter checks for sensitive fields like health sciences. These features collectively prioritize openness while safeguarding scholarly integrity.

Prominent Examples and Features

One of the most prominent preprint servers is arXiv, launched in 1991 and primarily focused on physics, mathematics, computer science, and related quantitative fields. By November 2025, arXiv hosts over 2.8 million submissions, reflecting its central role in disseminating early research in these disciplines. Key features include an endorsement system, where new submitters require approval from established users to ensure relevance and quality, and daily email announcements that notify subscribers of new postings across categories.
In the life sciences, bioRxiv, established in 2013 by Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory, serves as a leading platform for biology preprints, with over 300,000 papers posted as of November 2025 and continued monthly growth of approximately 4,000 new submissions. Its companion server, medRxiv, launched in 2019 in collaboration with Yale University and BMJ, targets health sciences and clinical research, accumulating over 75,000 preprints as of November 2025. Both platforms integrate with PubMed, enabling preprints to be indexed and discoverable alongside peer-reviewed literature upon author designation. medRxiv employs a structured screening process to evaluate submissions for ethical compliance, clinical trial registration, and potential public health implications, ensuring rapid posting while mitigating risks associated with unreviewed health-related claims.
SSRN, originally founded in 1994 and acquired by Elsevier in 2016, specializes in social sciences, economics, law, and humanities, hosting more than 1.7 million preprints and research papers as of late 2025. Operating on a commercial model with free access for users, SSRN tracks download metrics to generate alternative impact indicators, allowing authors to monitor engagement and visibility beyond traditional citations. These download counts serve as a form of altmetrics, providing real-time data on readership across its network of over 65 disciplines.
Other prominent platforms include OSF Preprints for broad visibility across disciplines, PsyArXiv for psychology and cognitive science, SocArXiv for social sciences, and Zenodo for permanent archiving. These servers feature user-friendly upload processes that assign digital object identifiers (DOIs) and permanent links upon submission. For OSF Preprints, users select a service, enter a title and abstract, upload a PDF file, add metadata such as contributors, subjects, tags (keywords), and license, complete author assertions, and optionally attach supplements before submitting for moderation; a DOI and persistent URL are automatically provided for citation and discoverability. PsyArXiv, hosted on the OSF platform, offers a streamlined workflow where authors enter the title and abstract, upload a PDF, and provide keywords and other metadata, resulting in DOI assignment after moderation. SocArXiv follows a similar process on OSF, involving account creation, PDF upload, addition of abstract and keywords, and submission for moderation, yielding a DOI and permanent link upon approval. Zenodo enables uploads by creating a new deposit, adding files such as PDFs via drag-and-drop, filling metadata including title, creators, resource type (e.g., preprint), publication date, and optional keywords, reserving a DOI, and publishing to assign the permanent DOI for long-term access.
Preprint servers also extend to the humanities, where adoption has accelerated in the 2020s amid broader open scholarship initiatives. Humanities Commons (CORE), part of the larger Humanities Commons network launched in 2016, supports preprints in fields like literature, history, and philosophy, emphasizing community-driven curation. Common features across such disciplinary servers include data linking to repositories for supplementary materials and API access for programmatic integration with tools like ORCID or institutional repositories, facilitating metadata exchange and enhanced discoverability.

Benefits and Limitations

Key Advantages

Preprints enable rapid dissemination of research findings, significantly reducing the time from completion to public visibility from months or even years in traditional journal publishing to mere days or weeks. This acceleration is particularly vital in fast-evolving fields such as artificial intelligence, where arXiv preprints allow immediate sharing of breakthroughs to inform ongoing developments, and epidemiology, as evidenced during the COVID-19 pandemic when preprint servers like medRxiv and bioRxiv facilitated swift global response by posting thousands of studies ahead of formal peer review.
By making manuscripts publicly available early, preprints foster valuable peer feedback that enhances manuscript quality through iterative improvements and encourages interdisciplinary collaboration by exposing work to diverse audiences beyond immediate networks. Community-based review initiatives, such as those on platforms like bioRxiv, exemplify how preprints solicit constructive input from global experts, often leading to stronger final publications.
Preprints promote equity and broader access in academia by eliminating subscription fees and publication costs, thereby lowering barriers for researchers in under-resourced regions of the Global South who may lack institutional support for traditional journals. This openness has been shown to increase citations for associated peer-reviewed articles, with studies indicating an average boost of around 36% due to heightened visibility among diverse readerships, particularly benefiting underrepresented authors.
Finally, preprints establish clear timestamps upon submission, providing verifiable proof of authorship and priority that protects against scooping while aligning with open science principles by promoting transparency and reproducibility from the outset. Many journals now recognize these timestamps as valid for priority claims, further integrating preprints into ethical research practices.

Primary Challenges

One of the primary challenges of preprints is the absence of formal peer review prior to public dissemination, which serves as a critical quality gatekeeping mechanism in traditional publishing. Without this scrutiny, preprints can propagate errors, flawed methodologies, or even fraudulent data, as evidenced by several high-profile cases during the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic. For instance, a preprint posted on medRxiv in April 2020 claimed hydroxychloroquine increased mortality risk in COVID-19 patients based on a large dataset, but subsequent investigations revealed methodological flaws such as confounding factors in the observational design, leading to widespread media coverage and policy debates. Similarly, data from the Surgisphere Corporation, used in peer-reviewed articles published in 2020 in journals such as The Lancet and The New England Journal of Medicine, alleged increased risks associated with hydroxychloroquine but were later found to rely on unverifiable data, contributing to retractions. These examples highlight how the lack of initial oversight can amplify misinformation in fast-moving research areas, potentially eroding public trust in scientific outputs.
Public posting of preprints also raises risks of scooping and priority disputes, where researchers fear that sharing preliminary work could enable others to claim intellectual ownership by publishing similar ideas first in peer-reviewed venues. Although preprint servers like arXiv provide timestamps to establish priority dates, inadequate documentation or delays in posting can exacerbate these concerns, particularly in competitive fields such as physics or biology. A global survey of over 3,500 researchers in 2023 found that concerns including scooping fears and low reliability deterred many from posting preprints, with respondents from lower-income countries reporting heightened worries due to limited resources for rapid follow-up publications. These disputes can undermine collaboration and innovation, as authors may withhold work to protect their ideas, contrasting with the intended benefits of early feedback.
The proliferation of uncurated preprints contributes to visibility overload, overwhelming researchers with an influx of content that dilutes discoverability and complicates the interpretation of citation metrics. With platforms like bioRxiv hosting approximately 40,000 new submissions annually as of 2022 (rising to over 50,000 by 2025), the sheer volume can bury high-quality work amid lower-effort or preliminary drafts, making it harder for scholars to identify impactful research without additional filtering tools. This overload also leads to citation dilution, as preprints garner citations independently of their eventual peer-reviewed versions, skewing traditional impact measures like h-indexes and potentially undervaluing rigorous journal publications. For example, analyses show that preprint citations often precede journal versions but can fragment attribution, reducing the perceived influence of finalized papers.
Ethical concerns further complicate preprint adoption, including dual publication issues and equity gaps favoring English-dominant fields. While most major journals now permit preprints without viewing them as prior publication, lingering policies in some outlets treat them as duplicative, risking rejection or ethical violations if licensing conflicts arise between open-access preprint terms and journal copyrights. Additionally, preprint servers predominantly operate in English, creating barriers for non-native speakers and researchers in non-Western contexts, where language proficiency and access to translation resources limit participation. These gaps highlight broader systemic biases in scholarly communication, where linguistic dominance reinforces exclusion. Recent developments as of 2025 include emerging concerns over AI-generated content in preprints, prompting calls for better moderation tools.

Current Trends and Future Outlook

Recent Adoption Patterns

The global volume of preprints has seen substantial growth from 2019 to 2025, with major platforms like arXiv recording nearly double the annual submissions over this period. In 2019, arXiv received approximately 168,000 new submissions, while by 2024, monthly submissions reached records of over 24,000, projecting an annual total exceeding 288,000. By late 2025, monthly submissions exceeded 26,000, setting new records. This expansion reflects broader trends across preprint ecosystems, where deposits have increased at rates far outpacing traditional journal articles, with overall preprint growth estimated at around 10-12% annually in recent years prior to the pandemic. The COVID-19 pandemic dramatically accelerated this trajectory in biomedicine, where platforms like medRxiv experienced a 10-fold surge in submissions between January and May 2020 alone, driven by the urgent need for rapid dissemination of research on the virus.
Adoption patterns vary significantly by academic field as of 2025, with physics exhibiting the highest uptake, where preprints constitute over one-third of all publications in many subfields. Mathematics and computer science follow closely with strong integration, while life sciences show moderate adoption, reaching about 10.7% of outputs as preprints by 2023. In contrast, social sciences demonstrate lower but growing usage, with around 40% familiarity among researchers but limited routine practice, and humanities remain at the lowest levels, with adoption rates of 10-20% due to entrenched traditions of book-based publishing.
Demographic trends highlight increased preprint engagement among early-career researchers (ECRs), who post preprints at significantly higher rates (e.g., 1.5 times the frequency) than later-career colleagues, often viewing them as tools for faster feedback and visibility. Researchers from the Global South have also shown rising participation, with diverse experiences indicating broader accessibility through platforms tailored to regional needs, though challenges like resource limitations persist. Altmetrics data further reveal higher engagement for preprints from these groups, including elevated downloads and citations compared to non-preprint outputs.
Key influencing factors include open access mandates such as Plan S, launched in 2018, which has indirectly boosted preprint use by emphasizing immediate availability of research outputs. Funder requirements from organizations like the Gates Foundation, which now encourage or mandate preprint deposition, have further propelled adoption by integrating preprints into grant compliance frameworks.

Emerging Policies and Innovations

In the 2020s, major academic publishers have shifted their policies to accommodate preprints more explicitly, fostering integration into traditional workflows. Elsevier's updated sharing policy permits authors to post preprints on servers at any time prior to submission and encourages linking them to the final published version via DOI after acceptance, a change emphasized in their 2020 revisions to promote rapid dissemination without viewing preprints as prior publication. Similarly, Springer Nature's editorial policies, refined in the early 2020s, state that preprint posting does not constitute prior publication and actively encourage it during peer review, with requirements for authors to disclose preprint DOIs upon journal submission and update preprints post-publication. These adjustments reflect a broader acceptance, allowing preprints to serve as provisional records while maintaining journal integrity.
Institutions and funding bodies have developed guidelines to evaluate preprints for credibility and use in decision-making. The National Institutes of Health (NIH) recommends treating preprints as non-peer-reviewed in systematic reviews and funding assessments, urging clear disclaimers about their provisional status to mitigate risks of misinformation, as outlined in 2020 guidance amid the COVID-19 response. The Coalition for Open Access/Research Repositories (COAR) and ASAPbio's 2022 recommended practices for repositories include screening preprints for plagiarism, ethical compliance, and basic quality checks before public posting, while promoting metadata standards for discoverability and evaluation by evaluators.
Technological innovations are enhancing preprint reliability and management. Preprint servers like bioRxiv and arXiv increasingly employ AI-driven tools for moderation, such as iThenticate and GPTZero adaptations, to detect plagiarism and AI-generated content, addressing a surge in suspicious submissions reported in 2025. Specifically, on October 31, 2025, arXiv announced updated moderation practices for review articles and position papers in the computer science category, requiring evidence of successful peer review before acceptance. This measure aims to handle the increased volume of AI-generated submissions while preserving the platform's commitment to open access and rapid dissemination. Blockchain integration offers immutable versioning and timestamping; for instance, proposals combine version control systems with blockchain ledgers to create tamper-proof records of scientific outputs, ensuring provenance without centralized authority.
As concerns over AI-generated content grow, some initiatives focus not only on detecting undisclosed machine authorship but also on formalizing transparent models of credit. One approach, explored in AI authorship experiments such as Aisentica, is to register an AI system as a Digital Author Persona, assign it persistent identifiers like ORCID, and list it explicitly in the metadata of preprints archived with DOIs. This model uses existing preprint and identifier infrastructures to distinguish between hidden AI assistance and openly declared AI personas, providing a possible template for future policies that accommodate non-human contributors while keeping accountability with human supervisors.
Looking ahead, hybrid peer review models blend preprint speed with structured validation. The Publish-Review-Curate (PRC) framework, advanced in 2024, involves posting preprints followed by public or journal-led review and curation, as implemented by platforms like eLife's Reviewed Preprint service, which publishes the manuscript alongside open reviews and an editorial assessment. To promote equity, multilingual servers like SciELO Preprints support submissions in languages beyond English, developed through open-source initiatives to broaden global participation.
Global efforts, such as the UNESCO Recommendation on Open Science adopted in 2021 by 194 countries, advocate for open access to publicationsincluding preprintsto accelerate knowledge sharing and inclusivity, though implementation varies. Emerging 2025 regulations pose data privacy challenges, with preprint servers facing scrutiny over metadata leakage (e.g., EXIF in figures) and editorial correspondence under frameworks like the EU's GDPR, requiring anonymization to balance transparency and protection.
