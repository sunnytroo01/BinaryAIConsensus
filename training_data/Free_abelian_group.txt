 In abstract algebra, a free abelian group is an abelian group equipped with a basisa generating set such that every element can be uniquely expressed as a finite integer linear combination of the basis elements, with no nontrivial relations other than commutativity. These groups are torsion-free, meaning no non-identity element has finite order, and they are isomorphic to direct sums of copies of the cyclic group , where the number of copies equals the rank, defined as the cardinality of any basis. For instance, the integers form a free abelian group of rank 1 with basis {1}, while is free of rank 2 with basis {(1,0), (0,1)}.
Free abelian groups admit a universal property: for any set S, the free abelian group on S, denoted , consists of formal sums _{s S} n_s s with n_s and only finitely many nonzero, under componentwise addition, and any map from S to another abelian group A extends uniquely to a group homomorphism from to A. All bases of a given free abelian group have the same cardinality, ensuring the rank is well-defined, and every subgroup of a free abelian group is itself free abelian, though possibly of lower rank.
These structures are central to the fundamental theorem of finitely generated abelian groups, which decomposes such groups as direct sums of a free abelian group and a finite torsion group, facilitating their complete classification. Beyond group theory, free abelian groups underpin free -modules in module theory, serve as chain groups in algebraic topology, and appear in the study of divisors in algebraic geometry.

Constructions

Direct sums and products

Free abelian groups can be constructed as direct sums of copies of the cyclic group Z\mathbb{Z}Z. In contrast, the direct product iIZ\prod_{i \in I} \mathbb{Z}iIZ comprises all functions from III to Z\mathbb{Z}Z with componentwise addition, allowing elements with arbitrary (possibly infinite) support. When III is finite, the direct sum and direct product coincide and are thus free abelian of rank I|I|I. However, for infinite III, the direct product is generally not free. For instance, the countable direct product ZN\mathbb{Z}^\mathbb{N}ZN, known as the Baer-Specker group, lacks a basis over Z\mathbb{Z}Z, as established by Baer's theorem showing it is not free abelian. This non-freeness extends to uncountable products, where the cardinality of potential basis elements would fail to generate the full group without relations. Specker's theorem further characterizes subgroups of such products, confirming that while countable subgroups may be free, the overall structure is not.

Basis and rank

A basis for a free abelian group FFF is a subset BFB \subseteq FBF that is linearly independent over Z\mathbb{Z}Z (meaning if bSnbb=0\sum_{b \in S} n_b b = 0bSnbb=0 for finite SBS \subseteq BSB and nbZn_b \in \mathbb{Z}nbZ, then all nb=0n_b = 0nb=0) and generates FFF (every element of FFF is a unique Z\mathbb{Z}Z-linear combination of elements of BBB). Any linearly independent subset of FFF can be extended to a basis of FFF. This follows from Zorn's lemma applied to the poset of linearly independent subsets containing the given one, ordered by inclusion; every chain in this poset has an upper bound given by the union of the sets in the chain.
The rank of a free abelian group is the cardinality of any basis; this is well-defined, as any two bases have the same cardinality, and thus serves as a complete isomorphism invariant. A free abelian group of finite rank nnn is isomorphic to Zn\mathbb{Z}^nZn. For infinite rank, the rank equals the cardinality of the group itself; free abelian groups of countable rank are isomorphic to the direct sum of countably many copies of Z\mathbb{Z}Z (denoted Z()\mathbb{Z}^{(\omega)}Z()), while those of uncountable rank \kappa are isomorphic to the direct sum of \kappa copies of Z\mathbb{Z}Z (denoted Z()\mathbb{Z}^{(\kappa)}Z()), and constructing bases becomes non-recursive and difficult under certain set-theoretic assumptions like V=LV = LV=L.

Relation to free modules over other rings

In general, for a commutative ring RRR with identity and a set XXX, the free RRR-module on XXX is the direct sum xXR\bigoplus_{x \in X} RxXR, where each copy of RRR corresponds to an element of XXX, and XXX serves as a basis in the sense that every element of the module can be uniquely expressed as a finite RRR-linear combination of basis elements. This construction generalizes the notion of free abelian groups to arbitrary rings, but the properties differ significantly depending on RRR.
When R=ZR = \mathbb{Z}R=Z, the integers, free Z\mathbb{Z}Z-modules coincide precisely with free abelian groups, as every abelian group is naturally a Z\mathbb{Z}Z-module via the action ng=g++gn \cdot g = g + \cdots + gng=g++g (nnn times) for positive nnn, and similarly for negative nnn. Moreover, since Z\mathbb{Z}Z is a principal ideal domain (PID), every submodule of a free Z\mathbb{Z}Z-module is itself free; this follows from the structure theorem for modules over PIDs, which decomposes them into direct sums of cyclic modules, with free ones having no torsion components. Consequently, free abelian groups have no torsion elements, distinguishing them from potentially torsion-containing modules over Z\mathbb{Z}Z.
In contrast, over rings that are not PIDs, such as the polynomial ring Z\mathbb{Z}Z, free modules exhibit more complications: submodules of free Z\mathbb{Z}Z-modules need not be free. For instance, consider the free Z\mathbb{Z}Z-module Z\mathbb{Z}Z of rank 1; its submodule I=(2,x)I = (2, x)I=(2,x), the ideal generated by 2 and xxx, is not free because it is not principal (as Z\mathbb{Z}Z is a unique factorization domain but not a PID, and non-principal ideals cannot be free of rank 1). This highlights a key structural difference: while the PID property of Z\mathbb{Z}Z ensures simplicity in submodule structure for free modules, non-PID rings like Z\mathbb{Z}Z allow for torsion-free submodules that lack bases.
Furthermore, free abelian groups interact naturally with localization: if FFF is a free abelian group with basis of cardinality \kappa, then its localization FS=FZZSF_S = F \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} \mathbb{Z}_SFS=FZZS at a multiplicative subset SZ{0}S \subseteq \mathbb{Z} \setminus \{0\}SZ{0} is a free ZS\mathbb{Z}_SZS-module of the same rank \kappa, since localization is flat and preserves the linear independence of the basis. Here, ZS\mathbb{Z}_SZS is itself a PID (or a field if S=Z{0}S = \mathbb{Z} \setminus \{0\}S=Z{0}, yielding Q\mathbb{Q}Q), so the localized module retains the freeness characteristic of modules over such rings.

Subgroups and quotients

A fundamental theorem in the theory of abelian groups states that every subgroup of a free abelian group is itself free abelian. This result holds for free abelian groups of arbitrary rank, including infinite rank, and relies on the axiom of choice. The proof constructs a basis for the subgroup by well-ordering the elements of the original basis and selecting leading terms from subgroup elements to ensure linear independence and generation. An alternative approach uses basis extension techniques, extending a basis of the subgroup to a basis of the whole group.
The rank of such a subgroup $ H $ satisfies $ \rank(H) \leq \rank(G) $, where the rank is defined as the cardinality of a basis (or, for finite rank, the dimension of $ G \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} \mathbb{Q} $ as a $ \mathbb{Q} $-vector space). For free abelian groups of finite rank, this inequality is strict unless $ H = G $.
When $ G \cong \mathbb{Z}^n $ for finite $ n $, subgroups are finitely generated and can be classified using integer matrices. Specifically, any subgroup $ H $ is the image of a map $ \mathbb{Z}^m \to \mathbb{Z}^n $ given by an $ m \times n $ integer matrix of full rank (meaning the rows are linearly independent over $ \mathbb{Q} $, with $ m = \rank(H) \leq n $). The Smith normal form provides a canonical representation: there exist bases $ {x_1, \dots, x_n} $ for $ G $ and integers $ d_1 \mid d_2 \mid \dots \mid d_r $ with $ d_i \geq 1 $ and $ r \leq n $ such that $ H $ is freely generated by $ d_1 x_1, \dots, d_r x_r $.
Quotient groups of free abelian groups by free subgroups need not be free. For instance, the quotient $ \mathbb{Z} / n\mathbb{Z} $ for $ n > 1 $ arises as $ G / H $ where $ G = \mathbb{Z} $ and $ H = n\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z} $ is free of rank 1, but the quotient is cyclic of order $ n $ and thus torsion. In general, such quotients decompose as direct sums of cyclic groups via the Smith normal form applied to the relation matrix.

Torsion-freeness and divisibility

Free abelian groups are torsion-free, meaning that no non-identity element has finite order. Equivalently, for any nonzero integer $ n $, the endomorphism given by multiplication by $ n $ is injective on the group. This property holds because, in a free abelian group generated by a basis $ {e_i}{i \in I} $, any relation $ n \sum{i \in I} k_i e_i = 0 $ with finite support implies $ n k_i = 0 $ for each $ i $, forcing all $ k_i = 0 $ since $ \mathbb{Z} $ is an integral domain.
In contrast to their torsion-freeness, non-trivial free abelian groups are not divisible. An abelian group is divisible if, for every element $ g $ and nonzero integer $ n $, there exists $ h $ such that $ n h = g $. For a free abelian group of positive rank, a basis element $ e $ cannot satisfy this for $ n > 1 $, as it would require $ h = \frac{1}{n} e $, which lies outside the group. This holds regardless of whether the rank is finite or infinite, as the indivisibility of basis elements persists.
Free abelian groups are also slender, a property capturing their limited capacity to accommodate homomorphisms from infinite products of copies of $ \mathbb{Z} $. Specifically, any group homomorphism $ \phi: \mathbb{Z}^\omega \to G $, where $ G $ is free abelian and $ \omega $ denotes the countable infinite product, vanishes on all but finitely many coordinate copies of $ \mathbb{Z} $. This slenderness underscores their distinction from divisible groups like $ \mathbb{Q} $, which is torsion-free and divisible but not free, as it admits no $ \mathbb{Z} $-basis.
The freeness of an abelian group, when combined with torsion-freeness, guarantees the existence of a $ \mathbb{Z} $-basis, providing a structural characterization unique to free abelian groups among torsion-free abelian groups. Subgroups of free abelian groups inherit torsion-freeness.

Invariants and symmetry

Free abelian groups are classified up to isomorphism by their rank: two free abelian groups are isomorphic if and only if they have the same rank, making the rank a complete invariant. For a free abelian group of finite rank nnn, the inner automorphism group is trivial due to commutativity, so the outer automorphism group coincides with the full automorphism group.

In algebraic geometry, the Picard group Pic(X)\operatorname{Pic}(X)Pic(X) of a variety XXX classifies isomorphism classes of line bundles and is fundamentally tied to the free abelian group of divisors modulo linear equivalence. Ring theory and group rings

The group ring Z\mathbb{Z}Z of a free abelian group GGG with basis BBB is constructed as the free Z\mathbb{Z}Z-module on the set GGG, equipped with multiplication extended from the group operation via convolution of formal sums. This ring is commutative since GGG is abelian.
If GGG is free abelian of finite rank nnn with basis B={e1,,en}B = \{e_1, \dots, e_n\}B={e1,,en}, then Z\mathbb{Z}Z is isomorphic to the Laurent polynomial ring Z\mathbb{Z}Z, where each xix_ixi corresponds to eie_iei. For infinite rank, Z\mathbb{Z}Z consists of finite Z\mathbb{Z}Z-linear combinations of elements of GGG, forming a commutative ring with the monoid structure induced by the free abelian monoid generated by BBB and their inverses.
As a Z\mathbb{Z}Z-module, Z\mathbb{Z}Z is free with basis {ggG}\{g \mid g \in G\}{ggG}, reflecting its construction as formal sums with integer coefficients. Ideals in Z\mathbb{Z}Z for finite-rank GGG inherit properties from the Laurent polynomial ring, such as being principal in certain cases or studied via graded structures; modules over Z\mathbb{Z}Z correspond to Z\mathbb{Z}Z-modules equipped with a compatible GGG-action, often analyzed through their decomposition into eigenspaces for the generators.
In representation theory, free abelian groups appear as underlying structures for modules over group rings, where Z\mathbb{Z}Z-modules provide integral representations of GGG; characters of GGG form the dual group, which for finite-rank free abelian GGG is a compact abelian group, linking to unitary representations. A concrete example is G=ZG = \mathbb{Z}G=Z, where ZZ\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}ZZ, the ring of Laurent polynomials in one variable, which serves as a fundamental object for studying cyclic actions and extensions in commutative algebra.
