 The first law of thermodynamics is a fundamental physical principle that expresses the conservation of energy in thermodynamic systems, stating that the change in the internal energy of a closed system equals the net heat transfer into the system minus the net work done by the system. This law, often mathematically formulated as U=QW\Delta U = Q - WU=QW (where U\Delta UU is the change in internal energy, QQQ is the heat added to the system, and WWW is the work done by the system), prohibits the creation or destruction of energy but allows its conversion between forms such as heat and mechanical work. It applies universally to all processes in isolated systems, serving as the cornerstone for understanding energy balances in physical and chemical transformations.
The formulation of the first law emerged in the mid-19th century amid efforts to reconcile heat with mechanical energy. British physicist James Prescott Joule conducted pivotal experiments in the 1840s demonstrating the mechanical equivalent of heat, showing that a fixed amount of work always produces the same quantity of heat, thus establishing their interconvertibility. Building on Joule's empirical findings, German physicist Rudolf Clausius provided the first mathematically precise statement of the law in 1850, integrating it into a broader framework of thermodynamic principles. Clausius's work emphasized that energy conservation holds for cyclic processes, resolving earlier inconsistencies in the caloric theory of heat.
In practice, the first law underpins the analysis of energy efficiency in engines, refrigerators, and chemical reactions, enabling predictions of system behavior without violating conservation principles. For instance, it explains why perpetual motion machines of the first kinddevices that produce work without energy inputare impossible, as they would contradict the law's energy balance. The principle extends to open systems through modifications like the steady-flow energy equation, widely used in engineering for turbines and compressors. Overall, it forms the basis for the second and third laws of thermodynamics, providing a rigorous foundation for modern thermodynamics across physics, chemistry, and engineering disciplines.

Overview and Definition

Core Definition

The first law of thermodynamics states that for a closed thermodynamic system, the change in its internal energy is equal to the heat transferred to the system minus the work done by the system. This principle, expressed mathematically as U=QW\Delta U = Q - WU=QW, where U\Delta UU represents the change in internal energy, QQQ the net heat added to the system, and WWW the net work performed by the system, embodies the conservation of energy in thermal processes.
Internal energy UUU is the total energy contained within the system arising from the microscopic kinetic and potential energies of its constituent particles, such as molecular vibrations, rotations, and translations, independent of the system's macroscopic position or motion. Heat QQQ denotes the transfer of energy across the system boundary solely due to a temperature difference between the system and its surroundings, without involving mechanical forces. Work WWW, in this context, primarily refers to pressure-volume (PV) work, where energy is transferred as the system expands or contracts against an external pressure, calculated as the integral of pressure over volume change, representing organized energy transfer via force applied over a distance.
This law applies specifically to closed systems, which exchange energy but not matter with their surroundings, ensuring that any change in internal energy results purely from heat and work interactions. In contrast, open systems permit mass flow, altering the formulation to account for enthalpy changes, though the core conservation principle remains. The law originated from 19th-century experiments establishing energy equivalence in thermal and mechanical contexts.

Historical Context

The first law of thermodynamics emerged in the mid-19th century amid the Industrial Revolution, a period marked by rapid advancements in steam engine technology that demanded a deeper understanding of heat and mechanical work to improve efficiency and power generation. This key era, spanning the 1840s to 1860s, was driven by the need to reconcile conflicting views on heat's nature, ultimately leading to the formulation of energy conservation as a fundamental principle. The development was closely tied to industrial motivations, as engineers and scientists sought to explain energy transformations in engines without relying on outdated models.
Prior to this, early misconceptions dominated, particularly the caloric theory advanced by Antoine Lavoisier and Pierre-Simon Laplace in the late 18th century, which posited heat as an indestructible fluid-like substance called caloric that could flow between bodies but not be created or destroyed. This theory, formalized in their 1783 work Mmoire sur la chaleur, treated heat as a conserved entity analogous to matter, explaining thermal phenomena through fluid transfer rather than energy conversion. However, emerging observations from mechanical processes, such as friction generating heat, revealed inconsistencies, as caloric could not account for heat appearing without a corresponding fluid source.
The transition to energy conservation was propelled by pivotal influences from Julius Robert von Mayer, James Prescott Joule, and Hermann von Helmholtz, who in the 1840s challenged caloric theory by proposing that heat and work were interchangeable manifestations of a single conserved quantity. Mayer's 1842 insights, Joule's contemporaneous arguments, and Helmholtz's 1847 synthesis emphasized that total energy remains constant across transformations, resolving the mechanical versus caloric debate. These developments elevated thermodynamics to a distinct science, independent of mechanics, by framing energy conservation as the unifying law governing heat engines and broader physical processes.

Significance in Thermodynamics

The first law of thermodynamics establishes energy conservation as a core axiom for all thermodynamic processes, dictating that energy can neither be created nor destroyed but only transformed or transferred within a system. This principle forms the bedrock of thermodynamics by providing a consistent framework for quantifying changes in internal energy, heat, and work, thereby enabling precise predictions of system behavior under various conditions. Without this law, subsequent thermodynamic concepts, such as the second law and entropy, would lack a foundational basis for evaluating energy distributions and irreversibilities.
The law's implications extend beyond isolated thermodynamics, forging essential connections to other scientific domains. In chemistry, it underpins the definition of enthalpy, a state function critical for analyzing energy changes in chemical reactions and phase transitions, allowing chemists to predict reaction feasibility and heat flows in processes like combustion or synthesis. In engineering, it governs the design and optimization of devices such as heat engines and turbines, where balancing energy inputs and outputs determines operational efficiency and power output. These linkages highlight the law's role in bridging theoretical physics with practical applications in energy systems.
A key limitation of the first law is its silence on the directionality of spontaneous processes; it permits both reversible and irreversible transformations equally, leaving the tendency toward disorderquantified by entropy increaseto the second law. Despite this, the law maintains universal applicability, holding across scales from macroscopic engineering systems to microscopic quantum environments, where it ensures energy conservation amid fluctuations and coherence effects in quantum thermodynamic protocols.
This foundational status makes the first law indispensable for comprehending energy dynamics in complex systems, serving as a prerequisite for modeling heat engines, refrigeration cycles, and even biological processes like cellular metabolism, where energy conservation regulates nutrient conversion and waste heat dissipation. In Earth systems, it facilitates analyses of energy balances in atmospheric and oceanic circulations, informing climate models and resource management strategies.

Historical Development

Early Formulations

In 1842, Julius Robert von Mayer proposed that heat and work are interchangeable manifestations of a fundamental "force," asserting that they could be converted into one another without loss, forming the basis of a conserved quantity underlying natural phenomena. He derived this insight theoretically from observations of blood oxygenation in tropical climates and gas expansion work, estimating the mechanical equivalent of heat as equivalent to a 365-meter fall for raising water's temperature by 1C, without conducting direct experiments.
Building on such ideas, Rudolf Clausius provided a more precise thermodynamic formulation in 1850, stating that the heat content of a body can only increase or decrease without equivalent compensation through work performed on or by the body, thereby establishing the conservation principle in heat processes. This verbal expression, rooted in his analysis of cyclic transformations, emphasized that work and heat transformations maintain a fixed proportionality, as later articulated: "Work may transform itself into heat, and heat conversely into work, the quantity of the one bearing always a fixed proportion to that of the other."
These early statements emerged within the thermodynamic framework, centered on the operation of heat engines and reversible cycles like the Carnot cycle, where heat absorption and rejection drive work output without invoking an explicit internal energy function at the outset. The formulations directly addressed the impossibility of perpetual motion machines of the first kind, which would produce net work indefinitely without energy input, thereby ruling out uncompensated creation of heat or work in closed cycles.

Key Experiments and Contributors

One of the earliest empirical insights into the relationship between heat and mechanical work came from Julius Robert von Mayer, a German physician, during his 1840 voyage as a ship's doctor to Java in the Dutch East Indies. While treating feverish sailors through bloodletting, Mayer observed that their venous blood appeared unusually bright red compared to that of patients in cooler climates, attributing this to reduced oxidation in the warmer tropics where less internal heat production was needed. This led him to propose that vital heat could arise from mechanical forces, such as muscular work, rather than solely from chemical processes, foreshadowing the convertibility of work into heat. In 1842, Mayer published these ideas in Annalen der Chemie und Pharmacie, estimating the mechanical equivalent of heat as the falling of a 1 kg mass through 365 meters to raise the temperature of 1 kg of water by 1C, equivalent to approximately 3.58 J/cal, which was the first such numerical estimate and close to but slightly below the modern value of 4.186 J/cal, though his work lacked experimental precision and received little initial recognition.
Building on such conceptual foundations, James Prescott Joule conducted meticulous experiments in the 1840s to quantify the mechanical equivalent of heat, decisively demonstrating that heat is a form of energy convertible from mechanical work. Joule's apparatus consisted of a copper calorimeter filled with water, equipped with a paddle wheel driven by falling weights connected via strings over pulleys; as the weights descended, they turned the wheel, stirring the water and causing a measurable temperature rise despite no external heat source. Through repeated trials between 1843 and 1850, accounting for friction and other losses, Joule determined that the mechanical equivalent of heat the work required to produce one calorie of heatwas approximately 4.184 joules per calorie (J/cal), a value remarkably close to the modern standard of 4.184 J/cal for the international calorie. His findings, detailed in presentations to the British Association for the Advancement of Science and later in his 1850 paper "On the Mechanical Equivalent of Heat," provided direct experimental evidence against the caloric theory and supported energy conservation.
Hermann von Helmholtz, a German physiologist and physicist, synthesized these empirical advances into a more formal theoretical framework in his seminal 1847 paper "ber die Erhaltung der Kraft" (On the Conservation of Force), presented to the Physical Society of Berlin. Drawing on Joule's experiments and Mayer's insights, Helmholtz argued for the indestructibility and uncreatable nature of force (later termed energy), applying the principle across mechanical, thermal, electrical, and chemical domains to show that work could transform into heat without loss. This work marked a pivotal mathematical and philosophical articulation of energy conservation, influencing the development of thermodynamics by unifying disparate phenomena under a single law.
Earlier influences included Sadi Carnot's 1824 analysis of heat engines, which, though rooted in the caloric theory, highlighted the limited convertibility of heat to work in cyclic processes and inspired later researchers like Joule and Helmholtz to explore quantitative equivalences. Complementing these efforts, William Thomson (later Lord Kelvin) contributed through his 1850s analyses of thermodynamic cycles, reconciling Carnot's efficiency limits with Joule's energy equivalence and helping establish the first law's application to heat engines.

Evolution to Modern Statement

In the early 20th century, the first law underwent consolidation through theoretical refinements that emphasized the internal energy $ U $ as a state function, independent of the path taken between states. Max Planck played a pivotal role in this development, formalizing the law in his 1897 Treatise on Thermodynamics as $ \Delta U = Q + W $, where $ W $ represents work done on the system, thereby underscoring $ U $'s path independence and its role in energy conservation for thermodynamic processes. This approach built on earlier empirical foundations, providing a rigorous mathematical framework that facilitated broader applications in physics. Planck's formulation helped resolve ambiguities in earlier statements, establishing internal energy as a fundamental thermodynamic potential.
The mechanical equivalent approach to the first law gained dominance in the mid-20th century as it integrated seamlessly with emerging theories of relativity and quantum mechanics, affirming the law's universal applicability across scales. In special relativity, early efforts by Planck and Einstein in 1907 extended the law to moving systems, transforming thermodynamic quantities under Lorentz transformations to maintain energy conservation in inertial frames. Quantum mechanics further validated this by treating internal energy in terms of quantized states, where the law manifests as the conservation of total energy in isolated quantum systems, without contradicting classical limits. These integrations confirmed the first law's robustness, shifting focus from caloric theories to a purely mechanical conservation principle.
Key milestones in the 1900s included the standardization of the first law's expression in textbooks as $ \Delta U = Q - W $, where $ W $ is work done by the system, becoming prevalent in physics curricula by the 1920s and 1930s. This form addressed sign convention discrepancies, with older conventions following Clausius (work by the system positive) contrasting Planck's (work on the system positive); the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) resolved this in its 1988 Quantities, Units and Symbols in Physical Chemistry, recommending $ \Delta U = q + w $ with $ w $ as work on the system to promote consistency in chemical thermodynamics. By the late 20th century, this IUPAC convention was widely adopted in interdisciplinary texts, minimizing confusion in applications spanning chemistry and physics.
Modern updates to the first law include relativistic formulations essential for high-energy physics, where internal energy encompasses rest mass energy via $ E = mc^2 $, ensuring conservation in particle accelerators and cosmological contexts. These extensions, refined since the 1960s, maintain the law's core structure while accounting for relativistic effects on energy-momentum tensors.

Conceptual Statements

Original Thermodynamic Approach

The original thermodynamic approach to the first law of thermodynamics arose from analyses of heat engines and cyclic processes, where conservation principles were applied to heat and work exchanges without invoking a distinct internal energy function. In his seminal 1850 work, Rudolf Clausius formulated the law specifically for closed cycles, asserting that the total heat absorbed by a system equals the total mechanical work it performs over a complete cycle, expressed as Q=W\oint \delta Q = \oint \delta WQ=W. This statement, derived from empirical observations of engines like the steam engine, underscored that energy transformations between heat and work must balance in periodic operations, preventing uncompensated creation of motive power. Clausius emphasized that no process could yield net work solely from heat without equivalent expenditure elsewhere, laying the groundwork for energy conservation in macroscopic terms.
Central to this perspective was the rejection of perpetual motion machines of the first kinddevices purportedly capable of producing continuous work without any energy input, such as drawing heat from a single uniform-temperature source. Clausius and contemporaries like William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) derived the law from the empirical impossibility of such machines, positing that heat cannot spontaneously convert into work indefinitely without external compensation. For instance, in cyclic operations, any apparent gain in work would require an equal heat input, ensuring the total "energy" remains unaltered. This formulation highlighted that heat could not be "created from nothing," aligning with broader conservation ideas while focusing on observable quantities like temperature differences and pressure-volume changes in engines.
Although influential, the original approach had limitations, as it initially lacked a comprehensive microscopic interpretation of energy, relying instead on phenomenological descriptions of heat as a fluid-like entity or motive force. It prioritized macroscopic observablesheat transfers and mechanical outputsover a unified energy state variable, which Clausius later introduced in 1865 as the internal energy UUU. This heat-centric view contrasted with emerging mechanical equivalents but established thermodynamics as a distinct field grounded in cycle-based conservation.

Mechanical Equivalent Approach

The mechanical equivalent approach to the first law of thermodynamics emerged in the mid-19th century as a paradigm shift, conceptualizing heat not as a fluid (caloric) but as a form of mechanical energy convertible through work. This perspective integrated thermodynamics with the established principle of energy conservation in mechanics, positing that heat arises from the kinetic motion of microscopic particles. James Prescott Joule's experiments provided the empirical foundation, demonstrating that a fixed quantity of mechanical work always produces an equivalent amount of heat, regardless of the method. In his paddle-wheel apparatus, falling weights turned paddles in water, generating frictional heat proportional to the work done, thus establishing the mechanical equivalent of heat, denoted as $ J $, which quantifies the conversion factor between mechanical units (like foot-pounds) and thermal units (like calories). Joule's principle specifically asserts that heat is a mode of motionnamely, the aggregate kinetic energy of molecular vibrations and collisionsrather than a separate substance, laying the groundwork for viewing thermal phenomena as mechanical processes at the molecular scale.
Hermann von Helmholtz further formalized this approach in his 1847 treatise ber die Erhaltung der Kraft (On the Conservation of Force), extending the conservation of mechanical energy to include thermal energy within isolated systems. He argued that the total energy, encompassing both macroscopic mechanical work and the invisible kinetic energies of heat, remains constant, with transformations between forms governed by equivalence rather than creation or destruction. Helmholtz's framework derived the first law directly from the broader mechanical conservation principle, applying it to thermodynamics through Joule's experimental validations, which confirmed the interconvertibility of heat and work without loss. This derivation emphasized that in any process, the change in a system's total energy balances the inputs from mechanical work and thermal transfers, unifying disparate fields under a single conservation law.
Central to this approach is the concept of internal energy $ U $, which encapsulates all non-macroscopic forms of energy within a system, including molecular kinetic energies (heat), potential energies from intermolecular forces, and chemical energies. Unlike macroscopic mechanical energy, $ U $ is path-independent and state-dependent, serving as the conserved quantity in isolated systems where external work and heat alter its value predictably via the mechanical equivalent $ J $. This formulation resolved earlier inconsistencies in heat theories by treating thermal effects as redistributions of mechanical energy at the particulate level, influencing subsequent thermodynamic developments.

Revised Conceptual Formulation

The modern conceptual formulation of the first law of thermodynamics unifies the early thermodynamic insights with the principles of energy conservation from mechanics, stating that for any process occurring in a closed systemwhere matter does not exchange with the surroundingsthe increase in the system's internal energy equals the net heat energy added to the system minus the net work energy performed by the system on its surroundings. This perspective treats heat and work as interchangeable forms of energy transfer, ensuring that the total energy remains conserved while allowing for transformations between these modes.
This revised approach resolves ambiguities in the original thermodynamic formulations, which viewed heat as a separate entity, by defining internal energy as a state function that depends solely on the system's current thermodynamic statesuch as temperature and volumerather than the specific path of the process. The integration stems from the mechanical equivalent of heat, experimentally established by James Prescott Joule, which demonstrated that heat is not a distinct substance but a measurable form of mechanical work, thereby enabling internal energy to be quantified consistently across processes.
In contemporary physics, this unified formulation assumes prior understanding of historical developments and foundational approaches, providing a complete framework that extends to microscopic scales; for instance, it has been confirmed in quantum field theory contexts, where energy conservation holds without violations in standard particle physics experiments.

Evidence and Verification

Adiabatic Processes

An adiabatic process is a thermodynamic process in which no heat is transferred to or from the system, so $ Q = 0 $. According to the first law of thermodynamics for a closed system, the change in internal energy is then solely due to work, expressed as $ \Delta U = -W $, where $ W $ is the work done by the system. This relation highlights that any mechanical work performed during the process directly alters the internal energy without thermal exchange, providing a key test case for verifying the conservation of energy in isolated systems.
A seminal verification came from James Prescott Joule's free expansion experiments in 1845, where a gas was allowed to expand into a vacuum within an insulated container, ensuring no heat transfer or external work. For an ideal gas, Joule observed no temperature change upon expansion, implying $ \Delta U = 0 $ since no work was done ($ W = 0 $) and $ Q = 0 $, consistent with the first law as the internal energy depends only on temperature for ideal gases. In contrast, his measurements for real gases like air showed a slight cooling during rarefaction, attributed to intermolecular forces, but the effect diminished at lower densities, supporting the law's applicability. These results confirmed that energy conservation holds even in irreversible adiabatic expansions without heat involvement.
Joule further demonstrated the conversion of work to internal energy through adiabatic compression in insulated cylinders, where rapid compression of air led to measurable temperature increases proportional to the work input. In these setups, the absence of heat loss allowed the mechanical work to fully account for the rise in internal energy, as $ \Delta U = -W $ with $ W $ negative for compression. His 1850 paddle-wheel experiments reinforced this by using falling weights to stir water in an insulated calorimeter, producing a temperature rise equivalent to the mechanical work expended, with no unaccounted energy loss. These early 19th-century tests by Joule provided empirical evidence that work directly transforms into internal energy in adiabatic conditions, establishing the mechanical equivalence central to the first law.
The implications of these adiabatic verifications extend to the concept of adiabatic invariants in gases, where certain quantities remain constant during slow, reversible adiabatic changes, forming the basis for relations like the pressure-volume behavior in ideal gases. Joule's work laid the groundwork for understanding how such invariants preserve energy conservation without heat, influencing later developments in gas dynamics and engine efficiency.

Work and Heat Measurements

Heat measurement in thermodynamic processes is typically conducted using calorimetry, a technique that quantifies the heat transfer associated with reactions or physical changes. In constant-volume processes, such as those in a bomb calorimeter, the heat released or absorbed directly corresponds to the change in internal energy, U, since no work is performed (q_V = U). This device, developed in the late 19th century, involves igniting a sample in an oxygen-filled steel vessel submerged in water, where the temperature rise of the surrounding water is measured to calculate the heat via the calorimeter's heat capacity.
Work measurement, particularly in expansion or compression processes, often relies on pressure-volume (PV) diagrams or mechanical devices like dynamometers. PV diagrams, historically captured using indicator diagrams in engines since the 18th century, allow the work done by a system to be calculated as the area under the curve on a plot of pressure versus volume. Dynamometers, used in engine testing, measure torque and rotational speed to determine power output, which integrates to work over time in thermodynamic cycles.
Empirical support for the first law arises from the consistent determination of U through independent measurements of heat and work leading to the same final state, regardless of the path taken. For instance, in non-adiabatic processes, adding measured Q and W yields the same U as direct calorimetric assessment at the endpoint. This path independence has been verified in countless laboratory settings, providing robust evidence for energy conservation in thermodynamic systems.
A representative example is the isobaric process, where work is given by $ W = P \Delta V $ (for expansion, work done by the system) and heat by $ Q = \Delta H $, with U = Q - W = H - P V holding true. In chemical reactions, such as combustion in an isobaric calorimeter (e.g., a coffee-cup setup), the measured enthalpy change H matches the difference between heat and expansion work, confirming the first law. For combustion of fuels like glucose, experiments yield U -2800 kJ/mol from bomb calorimetry and consistent values from isobaric paths, underscoring the law's reliability.
Since the 1850s, following foundational experiments by Joule and others establishing the mechanical equivalent of heat, thousands of such measurements across diverse systemsfrom gases to reactionshave shown no violations of the first law, solidifying its foundational status in physics and chemistry.

General Reversible and Irreversible Cases

The first law of thermodynamics applies universally to both reversible and irreversible processes in closed systems, stating that the change in internal energy U\Delta UU equals the heat added to the system minus the work done by the system, U=QW\Delta U = Q - WU=QW. For reversible processes, which are quasistatic and proceed through a series of equilibrium states, the path is well-defined and calculable, allowing the heat and work to be expressed as line integrals along that path: U=QrevWrev\Delta U = \int \delta Q_\text{rev} - \int \delta W_\text{rev}U=QrevWrev. This formulation assumes infinitesimal changes where the system remains in equilibrium, enabling precise computation of thermodynamic quantities, as demonstrated in ideal gas expansions under controlled conditions.
In contrast, irreversible processes occur spontaneously and involve non-equilibrium states, such as friction or rapid expansions, where the heat QQQ and work WWW differ from their reversible counterparts for the same initial and final states. Despite these differences, the first law still holds, with U\Delta UU remaining path-independent because internal energy UUU is a state function. For instance, in processes involving friction, mechanical work is converted into heat, increasing QQQ relative to the reversible case, but the net U\Delta UU matches that of the reversible path between the same endpoints.
Empirical verification confirms the law's applicability across these cases, as U\Delta UU depends only on the initial and final states, not the process type. A key example is the free expansion of a gas into a vacuum, an irreversible adiabatic process in a closed system with no heat or work (Q=0Q = 0Q=0, W=0W = 0W=0), where U=0\Delta U = 0U=0 holds for an ideal gas between initial and final states at the same temperature, even though the process is non-quasistatic. This confirms energy conservation and the state function nature of U in irreversible conditions.
No counterexamples to the first law have been observed in classical thermodynamics for closed systems, underscoring its universality as a conservation principle applicable to all process types. This robustness is evident in diverse experimental contexts, from piston expansions to chemical reactions, where measured U\Delta UU consistently aligns with QWQ - WQW regardless of reversibility.

The symbols $ \delta Q $ and $ \delta W $ denote inexact differentials because heat and work are path-dependent, whereas $ dU $ is an exact differential.

State Functions and Exact Differentials

In thermodynamics, the internal energy $ U $ is classified as a state function, meaning its value depends solely on the current state of the system, characterized by variables such as temperature $ T $, volume $ V $, and composition, rather than the history of processes leading to that state. This property ensures that the change in internal energy $ \Delta U $ between two states is path-independent, a direct consequence of the first law, which posits $ \Delta U = Q - W $ where $ Q $ and $ W $ are heat and work, respectively. In infinitesimal form, $ dU $ is an exact differential, satisfying the condition that its line integral around any closed path vanishes: $ \oint dU = 0 $. This exactness arises because $ U $ can be expressed as a function of independent state variables, such as entropy $ S $ and volume $ V $, via $ dU = T , dS - p , dV $, where $ T = \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial S} \right)_V $ and $ p = -\left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial V} \right)_S $.
The exact nature of $ dU $ implies that the second mixed partial derivatives are equal, a mathematical property known as the curvature condition or Clairaut's theorem:
2UTV=2UVT.
\frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial T \partial V} = \frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial V \partial T}.
TV2U=VT2U.
Although $ U $ is fundamentally $ U(S, V) $, this equality holds when expressing $ U $ in terms of conjugate variables like $ T $ and $ V $ through appropriate transformations. This equality of mixed partials leads to Maxwell relations, which connect thermodynamic derivatives; for instance, from the exactness of $ dU $, one derives $ \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial V} \right)_S = -\left( \frac{\partial p}{\partial S} \right)_V $. These relations stem from the integrability conditions of exact differentials and provide constraints on the thermodynamic behavior of state functions.
In contrast, heat $ Q $ and work $ W $ are not state functions because they are path-dependent, meaning their values depend on the specific sequence of states traversed during a process. For example, the work done in expanding a gas can vary significantly between an isothermal path and an adiabatic path connecting the same initial and final states, as $ W = \int p , dV $ integrates along the trajectory. Similarly, heat transfer $ Q $ lacks path independence, as demonstrated by the fact that $ \oint \delta Q \neq 0 $ for a cyclic process, unlike the closed integral of $ dU $. This path dependence is denoted by using $ \delta Q $ and $ \delta W $ for their infinitesimals, emphasizing they are inexact differentials.
The status of $ U $ as a state function enables the construction of other thermodynamic potentials through Legendre transforms, which shift the natural variables by replacing extensive variables with their intensive conjugates. For instance, the enthalpy $ H $ is obtained as $ H = U + pV $, with $ dH = T , dS + V , dp $, facilitating analysis at constant pressure; the Helmholtz free energy $ F = U - TS $, with $ dF = -S , dT - p , dV $; and the Gibbs free energy $ G = U - TS + pV $, with $ dG = -S , dT + V , dp $. These transforms preserve the exact differential property and the path independence of the potentials, allowing thermodynamic relations to be expressed in experimentally convenient variable sets.

Extensions to Complex Systems

Spatially Inhomogeneous Systems

In spatially inhomogeneous systems, the first law of thermodynamics is extended beyond uniform state assumptions to account for internal gradients in properties such as temperature, density, or composition across the system's volume. This formulation is essential in continuum mechanics, where the system is treated as a continuous medium with local variations. Open Systems: Matter and Energy Transfer

In open systems, also known as control volumes, matter can cross the system boundary in addition to energy exchanges via heat and work, necessitating an extension of the first law to account for the energy transported by mass flows. Unlike closed systems where the change in internal energy depends solely on heat and work, open systems require terms for incoming and outgoing streams to conserve energy.

In chemical reactors, the first law applies similarly to steady-state operations where reactants enter and products exit, with the enthalpy flow terms incorporating reaction energies indirectly through state properties. Conversely, for isothermal operation, external cooling must remove heat equivalent to the reaction enthalpy plus any sensible heat differences between streams.

Non-Equilibrium and Multi-Contact Scenarios

In non-equilibrium scenarios for open systems, the first law of thermodynamics maintains its form as a conservation principle, stating that the change in internal energy U equals the heat added Q minus the work done W plus the net energy transported by mass flows across the boundaries. This holds even when processes are irreversible, such as in rapid expansions or compressions where local thermodynamic equilibrium is not achieved, because the law applies to the overall energy balance rather than assuming reversible paths. For instance, in shock waves, the abrupt transition across the discontinuity conserves total energy, with kinetic energy converting to internal energy irreversibly, as verified in compressible flow analyses.
For multi-contact open systems involving multiple inlets and outlets, the differential form of the first law accounts for the full energy content of the flowing mass, including internal, kinetic, and potential contributions:

In computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations of non-equilibrium flows, such as supersonic inlets with multiple ports, this equation integrates into the energy conservation form to model irreversible losses accurately.
When combining the first law with the second law in the equilibrium limit for open systems, the internal energy differential becomes dU = T dS - P dV + dn, where T is temperature, S entropy, P pressure, V volume, the chemical potential, and n the number of moles; this relation arises from the conservation of energy and the definition of entropy production in systems allowing matter exchange. This form highlights how chemical potential drives mass transfer in equilibrium approximations, even as non-equilibrium effects like dissipation are accounted for separately in the overall balance.

In modern CFD applications for reactor design, non-equilibrium multi-contact models apply the first law to simulate turbulent mixing and shock-like reaction fronts, optimizing energy efficiency in industrial-scale operations.
