 Srinivasa Ramanujan (18871920) was a self-taught Indian mathematician whose groundbreaking contributions to number theory, infinite series, continued fractions, and elliptic functions revolutionized modern mathematics, despite his lack of formal academic training. Born on December 22, 1887, in Erode, in the Madras Presidency (British India)which is in present-day Tamil Naduto a poor Brahmin family, Ramanujan displayed prodigious talent from an early age, independently deriving complex theorems by studying a single advanced textbook. His intuitive approach led to over 3,900 results, many unpublished during his lifetime, which continue to inspire research today.
Ramanujan's mathematical journey began in earnest after moving to Kumbakonam at age one, where he attended local schools but failed to complete a degree due to his singular focus on mathematics over other subjects. In January 1913, at age 25, he sent a letter to British mathematician G. H. Hardy at Trinity College, Cambridge, outlining novel theorems that Hardy described as "scarcely possible to believe," prompting Ramanujan's invitation to England in 1914. There, under Hardy's mentorship, he formalized his ideas, publishing 37 papers on topics including the partition function p(n)for which he provided an asymptotic formulaand identities like the RogersRamanujan theorems.
Despite achieving remarkable honors, including election as a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1918the second Indian to do soand as the first Indian Fellow of Trinity College, Cambridge, Ramanujan's health deteriorated due to malnutrition and tuberculosis, exacerbated by the harsh English climate. He returned to India in 1919 and died on April 26, 1920, at age 32, leaving behind three notebooks and a sheaf of loose pages known as the "Lost Notebook"discovered in 1976 by mathematician George Andrews and containing approximately 600 formulas, including groundbreaking work on mock theta functionsthat mathematicians like G. N. Watson later explored for decades. Ramanujan's legacy endures through ongoing applications of his formulas in fields like black hole physics and string theory, cementing his status as one of history's greatest intuitive geniuses.

Early Life

Family and Childhood in Kumbakonam

Srinivasa Ramanujan was born on December 22, 1887, in Erode, Tamil Nadu, into a poor Tamil Brahmin family. His parents were K. Srinivasa Iyengar, who worked as an accounting clerk in a cloth merchant's shop earning a modest salary of about 20 rupees per month, and Komalatammal, a homemaker who supplemented the family income by singing devotional hymns at the local temple. Ramanujan had two older siblings who died in infancy, and two younger brothers who survived into adulthood. The family resided in a small traditional home on Sarangapani Sannidhi Street in Kumbakonam after relocating there when Ramanujan was one year old, as his father had secured employment in the town, approximately 280 kilometers from Madras.
The Ramanujan family adhered to orthodox Brahmin traditions as devout Hindus, practicing strict vegetarianism and participating in daily rituals centered around temple worship. Komalatammal's involvement in temple singing exposed young Ramanujan to sacred music and texts from an early age, fostering a deep religious environment in the household. This pious setting influenced the family's lifestyle, emphasizing spiritual devotion alongside their modest socioeconomic circumstances in the temple town of Kumbakonam.
Ramanujan showed early signs of exceptional intelligence. His childhood was marked by health challenges, including a severe case of smallpox contracted in December 1889 at nearly two years old, from which he fortunately recovered amid an outbreak that claimed thousands of lives in the region. These early experiences in a nurturing yet constrained family environment laid the foundation for his later pursuits, with formal education beginning around age five in Kumbakonam.

Initial Exposure to Mathematics

Ramanujan's first formal contact with mathematics came at age 5, when he enrolled in primary school in Kumbakonam and began learning arithmetic basics. This early introduction sparked a natural affinity, as he quickly grasped numerical concepts beyond his peers. By age 10, just before entering secondary education, he had already demonstrated exceptional ability in arithmetic, achieving the highest scores in his district across subjects including English, Tamil, geography, and mathematics during primary school examinations.
Ramanujan attended Kangayan Primary School, where, just before turning 10 in November 1897, he passed his primary examinations in English, Tamil, geography, and arithmetic with the best scores in the district, earning merit certificates for academic excellence. He then transferred to Town Higher Secondary School in January 1898, a more rigorous institution that introduced him to structured mathematical instruction. There, under the guidance of teachers, he delved into trigonometry and geometry by age 11, rapidly advancing through the curriculum and outpacing classmates in these areas. His proficiency led to scholarships recognizing his mathematical prowess, including awards for topping classes throughout his school years.
Even in these formative school years, Ramanujan's innate curiosity prompted initial independent explorations. These efforts, supported briefly by his family's encouragement of education, laid the groundwork for his prodigious development before age 16. Upon graduating from Town Higher Secondary School in 1904, he received the K. Ranganatha Rao prize for outstanding achievement in mathematics, affirming his early dominance in the subject.

Career Beginnings in India

Educational Challenges and Self-Study

In 1904, at the age of 16, Srinivasa Ramanujan enrolled at Government Arts College in Kumbakonam with a scholarship earned through his strong performance in mathematics during secondary school. However, his intense focus on mathematical pursuits led him to neglect other subjects, resulting in poor performance and the loss of his scholarship by the end of the year. This setback forced him to leave the college in 1905, amid growing financial difficulties for his family.
Determined to continue his studies independently, Ramanujan relocated to Madras in 1906 and enrolled at Pachaiyappa's College to prepare for the First Arts examination. Once again, his singular devotion to mathematics caused him to fail in non-mathematical subjects, leading to his departure after just three months due to failing health and inability to meet academic requirements. By age 20, these repeated failures left him without formal qualifications, deepening his isolation and reliance on self-directed learning.
Ramanujan's self-study was profoundly shaped by George Shoobridge Carr's A Synopsis of Elementary Results in Pure and Applied Mathematics (1886), which he acquired around 1903 and used as his primary resource. The book's concise presentation of thousands of theorems with minimal proofs inspired Ramanujan to develop his own unique notation and intuitive approaches, emphasizing symbolic manipulation over rigorous derivations. By 1904, he had begun filling personal notebooks with original results on topics such as infinite series, continued fractions, and elliptic functions, amassing thousands of entries through solitary exploration.
These years of obsessive self-study were marred by severe financial hardships, as the loss of scholarships left Ramanujan and his family in poverty, often resulting in irregular meals and nutritional deficiencies. His unrelenting concentration on mathematics exacerbated health issues, including frequent illnesses that further disrupted any attempts at structured education and contributed to physical exhaustion during this period.

Job Struggles and Local Patronage

Following his departure from college in 1905 due to a focus on mathematics over other subjects, Ramanujan faced severe financial hardship in his early twenties, relying on sporadic tutoring while pursuing independent research in Madras. On 14 July 1909, at age 21, he entered an arranged marriage to S. Janaki Ammal, a ten-year-old girl selected by his mother; the ceremony proceeded without his father's presence, and the couple lived separately until she reached puberty, as was customary. This union intensified the need for stable employment, prompting Ramanujan to seek clerical positions amid ongoing poverty.
In early 1910, Ramanujan approached V. Ramaswamy Aiyer, the founder of the Indian Mathematical Society (established in 1907), seeking patronage and a clerkship in his office as Deputy Collector in Tirukkoilur. Impressed by Ramanujan's notebooks filled with original theorems, Aiyer recognized his talent but declined a revenue department role, instead referring him to P. V. Seshu Iyer, a mathematics professor at the Society. Through this connection, Ramanujan presented his work at a Society meeting later that year, marking his initial local recognition and leading to the publication of his first paper on Bernoulli numbers in the Journal of the Indian Mathematical Society in 1911. Aiyer's endorsement helped bridge Ramanujan's isolation, fostering gradual acceptance within Madras's mathematical circles.
By December 1910, Ramanujan secured further support from R. Ramachandra Rao, a prominent mathematician and Collector of Nellore who co-founded the Indian Mathematical Society. Rao, struck by Ramanujan's "shining eyes" and frayed notebook during their meeting, provided a monthly stipend of 25 rupees for a year to sustain his research, along with recommendations for housing and employment. This aid enabled Ramanujan to take a temporary clerical position in the Madras Accountant General's Office in 1911, though it lasted only a few weeks before termination, likely due to his preoccupation with mathematics. Rao then arranged a brief accounting role under S. Narayan Iyer, the Society's treasurer and Chief Accountant at the Madras Port Trust. In February 1912, with a recommendation from E. W. Middlemast, professor of mathematics at Presidency College, Ramanujan obtained a permanent clerkship (Class III, Grade IV) in the Port Trust's accounts section starting 1 March, on a salary of 25 rupees per month; the sympathetic management allowed him flexible hours for study. Under Narayan Iyer's guidance, Ramanujan began submitting problems and theorems to the Journal of the Indian Mathematical Society, including early works on elliptic functions, solidifying his local patronage by 1913.

Outreach to English Mathematicians

Despite encouragement from local patrons to publish his work in India, Ramanujan independently reached out to prominent British mathematicians in 1912 and 1913, beginning with a letter to M. J. M. Hill, a professor at University College London. Hill's response was polite but dismissive, advising Ramanujan to seek recognition closer to home rather than pursuing international publication. Subsequent letters to H. F. Baker and E. W. Hobson, both fellows of the Royal Society at Cambridge, elicited no reply, leaving Ramanujan undeterred in his quest for validation from the global mathematical community.
On January 16, 1913, Ramanujan sent a pivotal letter to G. H. Hardy at Trinity College, Cambridge, enclosing a sheaf of theorems and formulas spanning nine pages, including over 50 unproved statements on topics from infinite series to elliptic integrals. Hardy, initially skeptical and suspecting either a hoax or the work of an eccentric amateur, consulted his collaborator J. E. Littlewood; their joint assessment shifted from doubt to profound admiration, with Hardy later describing Ramanujan's talent as incomparable, rating it a perfect 100 on a scale where only a handful of historical figures like Euler approached 80. This recognition marked the turning point in Ramanujan's outreach, as Hardy deemed the results "scarcely possible to believe" yet evidently original and deep.
The ensuing correspondence between Ramanujan, Hardy, and Littlewood in 1913 focused on verifying and refining these claims, with Hardy and Littlewood confirming several through rigorous proofs. A standout example was Ramanujan's asymptotic approximation for the partition function $ p(n) $, which estimates the number of ways to write $ n $ as a sum of positive integers; they established its validity using complex analysis, laying groundwork for the exact formula later published jointly. This exchange not only validated Ramanujan's intuition but also highlighted gaps in his formal training, prompting Hardy to advocate for closer collaboration.
By mid-1913, Hardy extended a formal invitation for Ramanujan to join him at Cambridge, emphasizing the need for in-person guidance to harness his potential. Ramanujan faced significant opposition from his family and community, rooted in Brahmin cultural taboos against overseas travel, which was seen as polluting and a violation of caste purity; his mother, a devout follower of Goddess Namagiri, initially refused consent. After persistent persuasion from local supporters and a visionary dream in which Namagiri granted permission, Ramanujan accepted in early 1914.
Arranging the journey involved securing a British visa and travel funding, facilitated by Hardy's appeals to the India Office and endorsements from Cambridge academics. The University of Madras provided a two-year research scholarship of 75 rupees per month to support his studies abroad, while patrons including E. W. Hobson, now convinced of Ramanujan's merit, contributed letters of recommendation to ease bureaucratic hurdles. These efforts enabled Ramanujan's departure from Madras on March 17, 1914, setting the stage for his transformative period in England.

Period in England

Arrival and Partnership with G.H. Hardy

Ramanujan departed from Madras on March 17, 1914, aboard the S.S. Nevasa, following encouragement from G.H. Hardy based on their pre-arrival correspondence. The voyage was largely calm, though he experienced seasickness for three days, and he arrived in London on April 14, 1914, where he was met by E.H. Neville, a fellow of Trinity College, Cambridge, whom Hardy had arranged to assist him.
Upon arrival, Ramanujan stayed four days in London before traveling to Cambridge, where he spent two weeks as a guest at Neville's home. Hardy had arranged modest lodging for him, initially at Neville's residence and then, from April 30, 1914, in rooms at Trinity College. As an orthodox Brahmin vegetarian from southern India, Ramanujan faced significant cultural shocks in England, including the unfamiliar cold weather and the prevalence of non-vegetarian food options, which complicated his daily adaptation.
Hardy promptly introduced Ramanujan to the Cambridge mathematical community, facilitating his integration at Trinity College. Their partnership began almost immediately, with the two mathematicians engaging in joint verification of theorems from Ramanujan's notebooks, often working intensively at Trinity. Hardy recognized Ramanujan's extraordinary intuitive grasp of advanced topics but noted gaps in his formal training, such as unfamiliarity with foundational texts like Cauchy's Cours d'analyse.
Under Hardy's mentorship, alongside J.E. Littlewood, Ramanujan received guidance on constructing rigorous proofs, a stark contrast to his self-taught, intuition-driven methods developed in India. While Ramanujan initially resisted some formal approachesquestioning established results despite their verificationhe gradually accepted the collaborative framework, allowing Hardy to refine and substantiate his ideas within European mathematical standards. This partnership marked the beginning of their productive collaboration, which soon led to joint publications, including their first in 1917 on asymptotic formulae for the partition function.

Academic Work and Fellowships at Cambridge

Upon arriving at Cambridge in 1914, Srinivasa Ramanujan quickly established a productive partnership with G.H. Hardy, who recognized the exceptional originality of his mathematical insights. In March 1916, Ramanujan was awarded a Bachelor of Arts degree by research from the University of Cambridge without the need for formal examinations, in recognition of his substantial contributions, including seven published papers on topics such as highly composite numbers. This degree, later reclassified as a PhD equivalent, marked a significant academic milestone and allowed him to focus intensively on research.
During his time at Cambridge from 1914 to 1919, Ramanujan produced a remarkable body of work, publishing a total of 21 papers, many of which were presented to the London Mathematical Society. These included explorations of hypergeometric series, elliptic functions, and asymptotic formulas in combinatorial analysis, often building on his intuitive approaches to infinite series and partitions. His collaboration with Hardy resulted in five joint publications, including influential works on the partition function and modular forms, which demonstrated rigorous proofs for some of Ramanujan's earlier conjectures. These efforts not only advanced number theory but also highlighted Ramanujan's ability to generate novel results despite his limited formal training.
Ramanujan's achievements culminated in prestigious fellowships that affirmed his standing in the mathematical community. On 2 May 1918, he was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society, becoming one of the youngest individuals ever to receive this honor at the age of 30. Later that year, on 10 October 1918, he was elected the first Indian Fellow of Trinity College, Cambridge, securing financial stability and institutional support for his ongoing research.
The ongoing World War I profoundly affected Ramanujan's Cambridge years, imposing wartime challenges that complicated his daily life and work. Food shortages, particularly of vegetables and other items suitable for his strict vegetarian Brahmin diet, made it difficult to maintain adequate nutrition, as special imports became scarce. Additionally, the war effort transformed Cambridge into a more militarized environment, with many colleagues absent on duty and general restrictions limiting academic and social activities. Despite these obstacles, Ramanujan sustained his prolific output until his departure in 1919.

Final Years and Death

Health Issues and Return to India

Ramanujan's health, never robust, began to decline noticeably in the spring of 1917 during his time in England, likely exacerbated by the cold, damp climate and challenges in maintaining a suitable vegetarian diet amid World War I rationing. By May 1917, he was admitted to a nursing home in Cambridge, where initial examinations pointed to a gastric ulcer, though this diagnosis was soon abandoned due to lack of supporting evidence. Symptoms included persistent weakness, recurrent fevers reaching up to 102F, and emaciation, but notably without significant coughing or other crude signs of pulmonary involvement.
Over the following two years, Ramanujan cycled through at least five sanatoria and nursing homes in an effort to manage his condition, reflecting the diagnostic uncertainty among his physicians. He was transferred to Matlock House Sanatorium in Derbyshire by December 1917, where he remained until June 1918 under the care of specialists like Harold Batty Shaw, who provisionally diagnosed tuberculosis based on fever patterns and general debility, though the absence of lung lesions cast doubt on this. Later moves included Fitzroy House in London and Colinette House in Putney by late 1918, where treatments focused on rest, monitored diets incorporating Indian staples like rice and curds, and consultations that speculated on obscure blood poisoning or an "Oriental germ." G.H. Hardy, in correspondence, observed that Ramanujan's vegetarianism and the wartime food shortages contributed to possible nutritional deficiencies, such as vitamin B12 shortage, while emphasizing the toll of isolation from his homeland. Retrospective analyses have proposed hepatic amoebiasis as a more fitting explanation, stemming from earlier episodes of dysentery in India around 1906 and 1909, aligning with the intermittent fevers and abdominal pains without clear respiratory symptoms.
By November 1918, signs of recovery emergedRamanujan gained weight, his temperature stabilized, and he resumed some light mathematical workprompting plans for his return to India, which had been postponed due to the war. With Hardy's support and arrangements facilitated by Cambridge authorities, he departed England on the S.S. Nagoya on 27 February 1919, arriving in Bombay on 13 March 1919 after a voyage that, while physically taxing, allowed him to reconnect with Indian cuisine and customs. He then traveled to Kumbakonam to rejoin his family, carrying with him a reputation as a leading mathematician but facing renewed health challenges upon arrival, including fevers and severe stomach pains that soon led to a confirmed tuberculosis diagnosis by local physicians.

Last Months and Passing

Ramanujan departed from Liverpool on February 27, 1919, aboard the S.S. Nagoya, in a severely weakened condition due to prolonged illness during his time in England. He arrived in Bombay on March 13, 1919, emaciated and requiring assistance to disembark, his health further deteriorated by the voyage.
Upon reaching Madras, G.H. Hardy had arranged for Ramanujan to receive care at a nursing home affiliated with the Madras Medical College, where he was attended by physicians from September 1919 until his death. Despite his frailty, Ramanujan continued mathematical work, filling entries in what became known as his "lost notebook," including explorations of mock theta functions during this final year. His arranged marriage to S. Janakiammal (Janaki), contracted in 1909 when she was ten, was consummated in 1919 after his return, with Janaki joining him in Madras; she provided devoted care, supported by patrons and family members who ensured financial and daily assistance amid his declining health.
Ramanujan's condition worsened progressively, leading to his relocation to his parents' home in Kumbakonam. He died there on April 26, 1920, at the age of 32. Although historically cited as dying from tuberculosis and malnutrition, the official cause was recorded as tuberculosis, a diagnosis based on symptoms like fever and weight loss observed by attending physicians. However, a retrospective analysis of his medical records and symptoms by D.A.B. Young in 1994 concluded that Ramanujan actually succumbed to hepatic amoebiasisa parasitic liver infection common in India at the time. This condition was likely a complication of severe bouts of dysentery he contracted in India in 1906 and 1909. The parasite remained dormant for years before his health collapsed in England in 1917, leading to a fatal misdiagnosis because doctors at the time were unfamiliar with tropical diseases. This error was a tragic turning point, as hepatic amoebiasis was a treatable and curable condition even by 1920 standards using emetine injections, whereas the tuberculosis he was treated for was not effectively curable at the time.
Ramanujan's body was cremated in accordance with Brahmin customs, though the ceremony was marred by social ostracism: many relatives refused to attend due to his overseas travel, which they viewed as a violation of caste purity, and the officiating priest reportedly fled without completing the rites. The exact site where his ashes were immersed in the Kaveri River has been lost to history.

Personal Life

Family Dynamics and Daily Habits

Ramanujan's marriage to S. Janaki Ammal was arranged by his mother in 1909 when he was 22 and she was ten years old, following traditional Brahmin customs that deferred consummation until the bride reached puberty around 1912. The couple remained childless throughout their brief time together, as Ramanujan departed for England in 1914 and did not reunite with Janaki until 1919, limiting their shared life to just a few months before his death in 1920. After Ramanujan's passing, Janaki faced significant poverty, working as a seamstress in Madras while adopting and raising a boy as her own, amid unsubstantiated claims that she had remarried.
Ramanujan shared a close bond with his mother, Komalatammal, who lived with him and Janaki in a small apartment in Triplicane after their marriage and supplemented the family's income by singing devotional songs at a local temple. However, she initially opposed his 1914 trip to England, citing orthodox Brahmin prohibitions against overseas travel that were believed to pollute one's caste status, leading to family tensions until she relented following a dream interpreted as divine approval from Goddess Namagiri.
Ramanujan adhered to a strict vegetarian diet rooted in his Brahmin upbringing, often preparing simple meals like rice with salt, lemon juice, or buttermilk himself, even in England where suitable food was scarce and wartime shortages exacerbated his challenges. His work habits were intense and irregular, frequently extending into late nights or early mornings after daytime clerical duties, with reports of sessions lasting until dawn that disrupted normal sleep patterns. Socially reserved and shy from youth, he maintained minimal interactions beyond mathematical discussions, preferring isolation for his calculations and rarely engaging in casual conversations or family topics. To conserve scarce paper, he performed most computations on a slate during his school and early professional years, erasing results with his elbowwhich roughened the skinand only transcribing final formulas into notebooks. In England, he expressed discomfort with formal Western attire, feeling uneasy in European suits, shoes, and stockings while reluctantly abandoning his traditional dhoti, turban, and hair tuft, which caused him visible distress as adaptations to colonial expectations. These routines, combined with nutritional limitations from his diet, likely contributed to his declining health during his Cambridge years.

Spiritual Beliefs and Philosophical Views

Srinivasa Ramanujan was a devout Hindu from an orthodox Iyengar Brahmin family, deeply immersed in religious traditions that shaped his worldview throughout his life. His faith centered on the goddess Namagiri Thayar, the family deity associated with the Namagiripiram temple in Namakkal, whom he credited as the primary source of his mathematical insights. Ramanujan believed that Namagiri appeared to him in dreams and visions, revealing complex formulas that he would transcribe upon waking, viewing these revelations as direct divine communication rather than products of logical deduction.
For instance, Ramanujan described a dream in which he saw a red screen formed by flowing blood, upon which a hand wrote a number of elliptic integrals that he committed to paper upon waking. He generally attributed his discoveries in areas such as elliptic integrals, infinite series (including approximations for ), q-series, and mock theta functions to divine inspiration through dreams and visions from Namagiri, although specific equations are rarely linked to individual dreams in historical sources.
Ramanujan's philosophical perspective on mathematics emphasized its spiritual essence, seeing it as an expression of divine intuition and beauty intertwined with the cosmos. He famously stated, "An equation for me has no meaning unless it expresses a thought of God," reflecting his conviction that mathematical truths were manifestations of the sacred. This outlook stood in stark contrast to the rigorous, proof-based atheism of his collaborator G. H. Hardy, underscoring Ramanujan's rejection of atheistic rationalism in favor of a faith-driven approach to discovery. As an orthodox Brahmin, he adhered strictly to Hindu purity rituals and vegetarianism, which influenced his daily practices and initially deterred him from overseas travel due to religious prohibitions against crossing the sea.
Even amid severe health struggles in his final years, Ramanujan's spiritual convictions provided sustenance, as expressed in letters to his wife Janaki where he conveyed optimism and gratitude rooted in his faith, continuing his work until shortly before his death. A pivotal instance of divine intervention occurred through his mother's dream, in which Namagiri granted permission for his journey to England, resolving familial religious objections and affirming his belief in goddess-guided destiny.

Modular Forms, Theta Functions, and Mock Theta Functions

Ramanujan extensively utilized theta functions in his notebooks to derive identities related to sums of squares, including a proof of Jacobi's four-square theorem. His general theta function, defined as $ f(a, b) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} a^{n(n+1)/2} b^{n(n-1)/2} $ for $ |ab| < 1 $, served as a foundational tool for these results. By specializing parameters, such as setting $ a = q $ and $ b = -q^3 $, Ramanujan obtained series expansions that aligned with the Jacobi triple product identity, $ \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} (-1)^n q^{n(n+1)/2} x^n = (q; q)\infty (-x q; q)\infty (-q/x; q)\infty $, which he recorded in entry 19 of his second notebook. This identity enabled him to establish the number of representations of an integer $ n $ as a sum of four squares, $ r_4(n) = 8 \sum{d|n, 4 \nmid d} d $, by expanding the square of the theta function $ \theta_3(0, q) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} q^{n^2} $ and extracting coefficients.
Ramanujan's work on modular forms culminated in his definition of the Delta function, $ \Delta(\tau) = q \prod_{n=1}^{\infty} (1 - q^n)^{24} $ where $ q = e^{2\pi i \tau} $, introduced in his 1916 paper on arithmetical functions. This function generates the Ramanujan tau coefficients $ \tau(n) $ via its q-expansion, $ \Delta(\tau) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \tau(n) q^n $, and is the unique cusp form of weight 12 for the full modular group SL(2, ). As the modular discriminant, $ \Delta(\tau) = (2\pi)^{12} \eta(\tau)^{24} $ where $ \eta $ is the Dedekind eta function, it plays a central role in the theory of modular forms, distinguishing it from Eisenstein series by its cuspidal nature and vanishing constant term.
In his final letter to G.H. Hardy dated January 12, 1920, Ramanujan introduced mock theta functions as q-series that mimic the asymptotic behavior of theta functions near roots of unity but lack full modularity. He provided 17 examples without a formal definition, describing them qualitatively as having "Eulerian" forms with rapid growth at rational points on the unit circle, unlike ordinary theta functions. Among these, five basic third-order mock theta functions were highlighted: $ f(q) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{q^{n(n+1)/2}}{(q; q){n+1}} $, $ \phi(q) = \sum{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-q)^{n^2}}{(q^2; q^2)n} $, $ \psi(q) = \sum{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-q)^{n^2}}{(q; q^2)_{2n-1}} $, and their companions $ \chi(q) $ and others, classified by their transformation properties under $ q \to e^{2\pi i /3} $. These functions appeared in Ramanujan's lost notebook as well, underscoring their unfinished nature at his death.
Ramanujan's mock theta functions exhibit deep connections to partition theory, where their coefficients generate restricted partitions, such as into distinct parts or with bounded differences, extending classical identities like the Rogers-Ramanujan theorems. For instance, the third-order mock theta $ f(q) $ relates to the generating function for partitions into distinct parts with even rank. Later interpretations revealed that mock theta functions are the holomorphic components of harmonic weak Maass forms of weight 1/2, whose non-holomorphic completions transform modularly under SL(2, ); the "shadow" is a unary theta function of weight 3/2. This framework, developed by Zwegers, unifies mock thetas with Maass forms and explains their partial modularity. As of 2025, research continues with new antiquantum q-series identities and congruences for Ramanujan's third-order mock theta functions, extending their applications in partition theory and modular forms.
Following Ramanujan's death, J.V. Uspensky contributed to the rediscovery and analysis of mock theta functions in the 1930s and 1940s, verifying several identities through asymptotic expansions and q-series manipulations in papers such as his 1941 work on Ramanujan's functions. Modern proofs of the original conjectures, including asymptotic behaviors and transformation formulas, were established using the harmonic Maass form perspective; for example, Bruinier and Funke provided integral representations linking mock thetas to periods of cusp forms, confirming Ramanujan's claims for all 17 functions. These developments have since connected mock thetas to diverse areas, including quantum modular forms and black hole partition functions.

Key Results and Notebooks

The Ramanujan Conjecture and Partitions

One of Srinivasa Ramanujan's significant contributions in number theory involved the Ramanujan tau function, (n), which arises as the coefficients in the q-expansion of the discriminant modular form (z) = (z)^{24}, where (z) is the Dedekind eta function. Contents and Rediscovery from Notebooks

Ramanujan maintained three primary notebooks during the period from 1903 to 1914, in which he recorded over 3,500 unproven mathematical results spanning diverse topics in analysis, number theory, and special functions. These notebooks, written in a dense, compact script, served as his personal repository of discoveries, often jotted down during periods of intense isolation and without formal proofs, reflecting his intuitive approach to mathematics. The first notebook, comprising 134 pages divided into 16 chapters, primarily covers elementary results in arithmetic, algebra, and basic sums and products. The second notebook, an expanded revision of the first at 252 pages and 21 chapters, delves into infinite series, continued fractions, integrals, and q-series, building on the foundational material with greater complexity. The third notebook, spanning 233 pages across 17 chapters, focuses on advanced topics such as elliptic functions, modular equations, and theta functions, showcasing Ramanujan's deepening engagement with transformation theory and elliptic integrals.
In 1914, upon arriving in Cambridge, England, Ramanujan presented his notebooks to G. H. Hardy, who recognized their extraordinary value and collaborated with him to publish select results during his stay. Following Ramanujan's return to India in 1919 and his death in 1920, his widow Janaki Ammal entrusted the notebooks to the University of Madras, where they were preserved in the library, though initially overlooked by the mathematical community. In addition to these three, a "lost notebook"a collection of about 140 loose sheets containing roughly 600 entries, mostly on mock theta functions and related identitiesemerged from Ramanujan's final months in India (19191920). This manuscript was likely sent to Hardy in one of Ramanujan's last letters but became separated from his main works; it remained unknown to most until its rediscovery in 1976 by George E. Andrews, who found it among the papers of G. N. Watson in the Wren Library at Trinity College, Cambridge.
The rediscovery sparked renewed interest in Ramanujan's unpublished ideas, particularly his mock theta functions, which puzzled mathematicians for decades due to their unconventional behavior. In 2002, Sander Zwegers' doctoral thesis provided a breakthrough by interpreting these functions as components of real-analytic modular forms, completing the harmonic Maass forms framework and linking them to contemporary number theory. Comprehensive scholarly efforts to verify and elucidate the notebooks culminated in Bruce C. Berndt's five-volume series, Ramanujan's Notebooks (published between 1985 and 1998), which systematically proved or referenced proofs for approximately 90% of the entries across all notebooks, confirming their accuracy and illuminating their interconnections with modern mathematics. These volumes not only rescued Ramanujan's archival legacy from obscurity but also facilitated ongoing research into his results, such as partition identities briefly noted in the notebooks.

Famous Examples like the Hardy-Ramanujan Number

One of the most celebrated anecdotes involving Srinivasa Ramanujan centers on the number 1729, known as the Hardy-Ramanujan number or the smallest taxicab number. In 1919, while visiting the ailing Ramanujan in a Putney hospital, G.H. Hardy arrived by taxi numbered 1729 and remarked that it seemed dull. Ramanujan immediately responded that 1729 was interesting as the smallest positive integer expressible as the sum of two positive cubes in two different ways: 103+93=123+13=172910^3 + 9^3 = 12^3 + 1^3 = 1729103+93=123+13=1729. This story, recounted by Hardy, highlights Ramanujan's intuitive grasp of number theory, as he had previously explored sums of cubes in his work on taxicab numbers.
Ramanujan also derived elegant closed forms for certain infinite nested radicals, originating from entries in his notebooks. A famous example is the infinite nested radical 1+21+31+41+=3\sqrt{1 + 2\sqrt{1 + 3\sqrt{1 + 4\sqrt{1 + \cdots}}}} = 31+21+31+41+=3. He also considered the similar form 12+222+332+442+=3\sqrt{1^2 + 2\sqrt{2^2 + 3\sqrt{3^2 + 4\sqrt{4^2 + \cdots}}}} = 312+222+332+442+=3, recorded as Entry 4 in Chapter XIV of his first notebook and Chapter XII of his second. These results demonstrate his ability to simplify complex infinite expressions into finite forms without rigorous limits, relying on pattern recognition.
In his 1914 paper on modular equations, Ramanujan developed approximations for \pi using continued fractions derived from class invariants of elliptic modular functions, achieving remarkable accuracy. One such approximation, based on the continued fraction expansion tied to quadratic forms, yields 3.14159265\pi \approx 3.141592653.14159265, correct to eight decimal places, far surpassing contemporary manual computations. These methods, rooted in transformations of elliptic integrals, provided efficient ways to compute \pi without infinite series, emphasizing conceptual elegance over iteration.
Ramanujan contributed alternative series expressions for Euler's constant 0.57721\gamma \approx 0.577210.57721, the limit =limn(k=1n1klnn)\gamma = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left( \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k} - \ln n \right)=limn(k=1nk1lnn). In his 1917 paper, published in the Messenger of Mathematics, he derived novel summation expressions for \gamma, extending classical definitions by incorporating integrals and products, influencing later analytic number theory.
Hardy, reflecting on Ramanujan's prodigious talent, once rated mathematicians on a scale of pure ability from 0 to 100, assigning himself 25, J.E. Littlewood 30, David Hilbert 80, and Ramanujan the maximum of 100. This assessment, recalled by Paul Erds from conversations with Hardy, underscores the profound impact Ramanujan had on his mentor, positioning him as unparalleled in raw mathematical intuition.

Recognition and Legacy

Contemporary Appraisals by Mathematicians

G. H. Hardy, in his official report of June 16, 1916, to the University of Madras, described Ramanujan as "the most remarkable mathematician I have ever known," emphasizing his extraordinary intuitive gift for mathematics, which Hardy compared to that of Euler in its depth and originality. Hardy highlighted Ramanujan's profound and invincible originality, noting that he produced results of great significance in number theory and analysis despite minimal formal training, often arriving at truths through intuition rather than rigorous proof. This assessment underscored Ramanujan's unique ability to treat positive integers as "personal friends," as illustrated by his insightful anecdotes during their collaborations. In his 1921 obituary published in the Proceedings of the London Mathematical Society, Hardy rated mathematicians on the basis of pure talent on a scale from 0 to 100, assigning himself a score of 25, his collaborator J. E. Littlewood 30, David Hilbert 80, and Ramanujan 100, reflecting awe at the sheer productivity and insight of Ramanujan's work.
J. E. Littlewood, Hardy's close collaborator at Cambridge, viewed Ramanujan's output as comparable to that of several ordinary mathematicians combined, marveling at how his self-taught methods yielded thousands of novel results in a short time. This perspective emerged from their joint examination of Ramanujan's initial submissions, which astonished both Hardy and Littlewood.
E. H. Neville, who played a key role in facilitating Ramanujan's arrival in England, praised his groundbreaking contributions to elliptic functions, describing them as exceptionally innovative and worthy of immediate recognition by the Cambridge mathematical community. Similarly, G. N. Watson dedicated years to verifying over 600 identities from Ramanujan's notebooks, confirming their validity through rigorous proofs and extending them into formal theorems, particularly in areas like mock theta functions and continued fractions. Watson's efforts, detailed in his papers during the 1920s and 1930s, solidified the authenticity of Ramanujan's intuitive discoveries.
Ramanujan's election to the Royal Society in 1918 faced some opposition rooted in racial biases, with debates questioning the suitability of an Indian mathematician without traditional European training, though Hardy's advocacy ensured his fellowship. Despite such challenges, contemporaries recognized his unparalleled talent. In his letters to Hardy, Ramanujan exhibited characteristic humility, writing phrases like "I am sending you some of my results... but you are a greater mathematician than I," and acknowledging Hardy's superior rigor in providing proofs for his conjectures.

Posthumous Honors and Institutions

Following Ramanujan's death in 1920, numerous honors and institutions were established in his memory to recognize his profound contributions to mathematics.
The Ramanujan Institute for Advanced Study in Mathematics was founded in 1950 at the University of Madras by philanthropist Dr. Rm. Alagappa Chettiar as a lasting tribute to Ramanujan, serving as a center for advanced mathematical research and education.
In 2005, Shanmugha Arts, Science, Technology & Research Academy (SASTRA) University instituted the SASTRA Ramanujan Prize, an annual award of US$10,000 and a citation given to mathematicians aged 32 or younger for exceptional work in fields influenced by Ramanujan's ideas, such as number theory and infinite series.
That same year, the International Centre for Theoretical Physics (ICTP), in collaboration with the International Mathematical Union, established the Ramanujan Prize for young mathematicians from developing countries, providing an annual cash award of $10,000 to researchers under 45 for outstanding contributions to the mathematical sciences conducted primarily in developing nations.
The Government of India declared 2012 the National Mathematical Year to mark the 125th anniversary of Ramanujan's birth, with December 22 designated as National Mathematics Day, celebrated annually thereafter to promote mathematical awareness and honor his legacy. This initiative was complemented by global recognition, as mathematical societies worldwide, including the International Mathematical Union, organized events and publications throughout 2012 as the "Year of Ramanujan."
Other dedications include the naming of asteroid (4130) Ramanujan, discovered on February 17, 1988, by astronomer R. Rajamohan at the Vainu Bappu Observatory in India, in recognition of Ramanujan's genius. Additionally, India Post issued a commemorative postage stamp featuring Ramanujan in 2011 to celebrate his enduring influence on mathematics.

Ongoing Impact and Recent Developments

Ramanujan's mock theta functions, initially introduced in his 1920 letter to G. H. Hardy, continue to inspire rigorous proofs of related identities in modern number theory. In 2020, advancements in q-series techniques facilitated the verification of several entries from Ramanujan's lost notebook, building on foundational work by George E. Andrews and Frank Garvan to establish connections between mock theta functions and partition identities. These proofs employ modular forms and combinatorial interpretations to confirm Ramanujan's conjectured relations, demonstrating the enduring analytical power of his original formulations.
Ramanujan's contributions to modular forms have found profound applications in theoretical physics, particularly in understanding black hole entropy within string theory. Recent analyses in the 2020s highlight how mock modular forms, extending Ramanujan's theta functions, encode the microscopic counting of black hole states, providing exact matches to macroscopic entropy formulas derived from general relativity. Freeman Dyson's earlier observations on the growth of partition functions have been revitalized through these connections, linking Ramanujan's asymptotic behaviors to quantum gravity models where modular invariance ensures consistency across physical regimes.
Partition identities originating from Ramanujan's work are increasingly applied in quantum physics, as evidenced by dedicated research in 2024. A special issue in MDPI's Mathematics journal explored these identities' roles in quantum statistical mechanics and conformal field theories, where Rogers-Ramanujan-type identities model particle spectra and phase transitions. For instance, generalizations of Ramanujan's partition congruences appear in the enumeration of quantum states, offering tools to predict behaviors in low-dimensional quantum systems.
The Ramanujan Machine, launched in 2021 as an AI-driven initiative, automates the discovery of mathematical conjectures reminiscent of Ramanujan's intuitive leaps, particularly in continued fractions for fundamental constants. This project employs algorithms to generate infinite families of identities, such as polynomial continued fractions equating ratios of zeta values, which have been verified and extended by mathematicians. By mimicking Ramanujan's pattern recognition without human bias, it has produced dozens of novel conjectures, fostering collaborative proofs and highlighting the potential of machine learning in pure mathematics.
Verification of entries in Ramanujan's notebooks remains an active pursuit, with significant progress in 2022 on elliptic integrals. Researchers deduced new identities for incomplete elliptic integrals of the first kind from pages 5153 of the lost notebook, generalizing Ramanujan's expressions through hypergeometric series and theta function transformations. These developments not only confirm the original claims but also reveal broader applications in special functions, underscoring the notebooks' untapped depth even a century later.
In 2025, the ongoing impact of Ramanujan's work was further recognized through prestigious awards. The ICTP Ramanujan Prize was awarded to Claudio Muoz Cern of the University of Chile for his contributions to dispersive partial differential equations, while the SASTRA Ramanujan Prize went to Alexander Smith of Northwestern University for his work in number theory. Additionally, the American Mathematical Society organized a special session on recent developments in areas influenced by Ramanujan's mathematics at its fall meeting.

Cultural Influence

Depictions in Media and Literature

Robert Kanigel's 1991 biography The Man Who Knew Infinity: A Life of the Genius Ramanujan chronicles Srinivasa Ramanujan's early life in India, his self-taught mathematical prowess, and his transformative collaboration with G.H. Hardy at Cambridge University. The book draws on archival materials, letters, and interviews to highlight Ramanujan's intuitive approach to mathematics and the cultural challenges he encountered in Britain.
This biography served as the basis for the 2015 biographical drama film The Man Who Knew Infinity, directed by Matt Brown. Starring Dev Patel as Ramanujan and Jeremy Irons as Hardy, the film portrays Ramanujan's 1913 letter to Hardy, his relocation to England amid World War I, and the intellectual partnership that produced groundbreaking results in number theory. It emphasizes themes of racial prejudice, academic skepticism, and Ramanujan's unorthodox methods, receiving praise for its performances while drawing some criticism for simplifying mathematical elements.
In fiction, David Leavitt's 2007 novel The Indian Clerk reimagines the Hardy-Ramanujan relationship as a complex interplay of admiration, cultural clash, and personal turmoil. Narrated primarily from Hardy's viewpoint, the book fictionalizes events like Ramanujan's arrival in Cambridge and their joint work, incorporating real mathematical concepts to underscore the protagonists' contrasting worldviews. It explores Hardy's internal conflicts, including his atheism and homosexuality, against Ramanujan's devout spirituality.
Ramanujan's story has also inspired stage productions, such as Ira Hauptman's play Partition, which premiered in 2003 at the Aurora Theatre and was staged by Bangalore Little Theatre in 2004. The play dramatizes Ramanujan's journey from a Madras clerk to a Cambridge scholar, focusing on his evolving bond with Hardy and the "partition" between their mathematical and personal lives. It blends historical facts with imaginative elements, including Ramanujan's rumored pursuit of Fermat's Last Theorem, to convey the intensity of his genius.
Documentaries have further brought Ramanujan's legacy to audiences, including the 2013 documentary The Genius of Srinivasa Ramanujan, which features interviews with number theorists and discusses his work and its modern relevance.

Numerous institutions bear his name, reflecting his enduring influence. Streets and roads, such as those in Chennai and Erode, are also named after him, symbolizing local pride in his origins.
In 2025, global tributes continued through prestigious awards named in his honor.
