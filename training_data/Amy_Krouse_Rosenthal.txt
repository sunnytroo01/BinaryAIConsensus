 Amy Krouse Rosenthal (April 29, 1965 March 13, 2017) was an American author, filmmaker, and public speaker renowned for her prolific output of over 30 award-winning children's books, memoirs, short films, and viral videos that emphasized themes of kindness, creativity, and everyday wonder.
Born Amy Renee Krouse in Chicago to parents Paul Krouse and Ann Wolk, both publishers, she grew up in the city and later graduated from Tufts University before studying at the Sorbonne in Paris. After working in advertising, Rosenthal transitioned to writing, debuting with her memoir Encyclopedia of an Ordinary Life in 2005, which Amazon named one of the top 10 memoirs of the decade for its innovative, alphabetical structure chronicling personal anecdotes.
Her children's books, often illustrated by collaborators like Tom Lichtenheld, became bestsellers and earned accolades including Christopher Awards and selections as New York Times Notable Books; standout titles include Little Pea (2005), Spoon (2009), Duck! Rabbit! (2009), Yes Day! (2009), Exclamation Mark (2013), and I Wish You More (2015), celebrated for their playful humor and life lessons. She also created guided journals like Encyclopedia of Me and short films such as The Beckoning of Lovely (2008), blending storytelling with interactive media. As a TED speaker and NPR contributor, Rosenthal's talks and projects, hosted on her website whoisamy.com, which was named one of the best official author websites, promoted positivity and community engagement.
In her personal life, Rosenthal was married to Jason Rosenthal for 26 years and had three children, residing in Chicago where she infused her work with family-inspired optimism. Diagnosed with ovarian cancer in September 2015, she publicly shared her experience through her 2016 memoir Textbook Amy Krouse Rosenthal and a poignant Modern Love essay in the New York Times, "You May Want to Marry My Husband," published just 10 days before her death, which urged readers to consider her husband as a potential partner and garnered nearly 4.5 million views for its raw vulnerability and love. She passed away at home on March 13, 2017, at age 51, leaving a legacy of joyful creativity that continues to inspire through adaptations like the 2021 Netflix film Yes Day and the Amy Krouse Rosenthal Foundation, which supports ovarian cancer research and children's literacy initiatives.

Early life and education

Childhood in Chicago

Amy Krouse Rosenthal was born on April 29, 1965, in Chicago, Illinois, to a Jewish family. Her parents, Paul Krouse and Ann Wolk Krouse, were both publishers who provided a nurturing environment for their children. She had three siblings: sisters Beth Kaufmann and Katie Froelich, and brother Joe Krouse.
Rosenthal was raised in the middle-class suburb of Northbrook, Illinois, where her family later moved to Lake Forest during her high school years. As the only Jewish child in her Lake Forest High School class, she navigated a preppy, affluent community that contrasted with her family's values. Growing up near Ragdale, an artists' and writers' colony in Lake Forest, exposed her to creative pursuits from an early age.
From her early years, Rosenthal displayed a penchant for creativity, delighting in playing with words, inventing games, and collecting intriguing phrases that would later inform her writing style. These inclinations were evident in her childhood activities, such as crafting simple creations for family milestones, reflecting a joyful exploration of language and ideas. Chicago's vibrant cultural landscape, including its coffeehouse scenes and artistic communities, further nurtured her imaginative outlook during family visits and outings.

Higher education

Rosenthal attended Lake Forest High School in Lake Forest, Illinois, graduating in 1983. During her time there, she participated in extracurricular activities including cheerleading, gymnastics, tennis, and the school's talent show, which reflected her early creative inclinations rooted in childhood interests in storytelling and performance.
She majored in French at Tufts University in Medford, Massachusetts, spending her junior year studying abroad at the Sorbonne in Paris, and graduated in 1987. Her university years built on her foundational creativity, preparing her for professional pursuits in communication and media.
Following graduation, Rosenthal returned to Chicago and began her professional career in advertising and marketing as a freelance copywriter at a small agency on the city's River East side before working at the larger firm Foote, Cone & Belding (now FCB), where she spent nine years. These roles involved crafting concise, engaging ad copy, which sharpened her distinctive witty and succinct writing style that would later define her authored works.

Writing career

Adult books

Amy Krouse Rosenthal's adult books are characterized by their innovative structures and introspective humor, often drawing from the minutiae of everyday experiences to explore themes of serendipity, connection, and ordinary wonder. Her debut memoir, Encyclopedia of an Ordinary Life (2005), published by Riverhead Books, an imprint of Penguin, presents a personal narrative in the form of an A-to-Z encyclopedia, with short, cross-referenced entries on topics ranging from "ambition" to "zest," capturing the essence of mundane moments without traditional chronological storytelling. This format allows for a nonlinear, playful examination of life, blending wit and vulnerability, and the book was praised for its refreshing take on memoir-writing by critics including NPR's Nancy Pearl, who highlighted its clever adaptation of encyclopedic style to personal reflection.
Prior to this, Rosenthal's The Book of Eleven: An Itemized Collection of Brain Lint (1998), published by Andrews McMeel Publishing, offered an early glimpse into her humorous voice through lists of eleven items on quirky subjects such as olives, answering machines, and death, eschewing conventional narrative for zany, observational vignettes that celebrate serendipitous insights from daily life. The work's brevity and Seinfeld-esque wit on everyday absurdities received positive notice for its clever, unpretentious humor, positioning it as a precursor to her later explorations of the ordinary.
In Textbook Amy Krouse Rosenthal (2016), also from Penguin, Rosenthal expanded her experimental approach by structuring the book like a school textbook, divided into units mimicking subjects such as Art, Math, and History, filled with nonlinear reflections, observational essays, and interactive prompts that invite reader participation. The creation process involved crowdsourcing elements like "Readers' Self-Portraits," where contributors submitted personal images and stories, fostering a communal sense of connection and serendipity, while innovative features such as text-message promptsvia a dedicated phone numberallowed real-time engagement, blending print with digital multimedia to create an "explosively human" experience. This New York Times bestselling title was lauded in the Chicago Tribune for emphasizing themes of human connection and unadorned beauty through its wistful, interactive format.
Across these works, Rosenthal's style consistently employs humor and concision to elevate the mundane, often integrating multimedia or interactive componentslike cross-references in Encyclopedia or text prompts in Textbookto mirror life's playful, serendipitous nature, a lightness that echoes subtly in her children's books without overlapping their whimsical narratives.

Children's books

Amy Krouse Rosenthal authored more than 30 illustrated children's books between 2005 and 2017, establishing her as a prolific voice in picture book literature through whimsical narratives and innovative visual storytelling. Her children's works drew on the playful, concise language of her adult writing, adapting it to engaging, age-appropriate tales that blended humor, heart, and subtle life lessons.
Among her major titles, Little Pea (2005), illustrated by Jen Corace and published by Chronicle Books, follows a young pea grappling with the "spoonful of vegetables" dilemma required at dinner, humorously exploring picky eating habits through a vegetable family's perspective. Duck! Rabbit! (2009), co-illustrated by Tom Lichtenheld and also from Chronicle Books, presents an optical illusion debate between friends who alternately see a duck or rabbit in a simple silhouette, encouraging readers to question perception and perspective. The book earned recognition as an ALA Notable Children's Book in 2010 and appeared on multiple year-end best-of lists, including Publishers Weekly's Best Children's Books of 2009. Later successes included I Wish You More (2015), illustrated by Tom Lichtenheld for Chronicle Books, a #1 New York Times bestseller that has sold over 1.5 million copies and conveys boundless positivity through escalating wishes like "I wish you more hugs than you can hold." Uni the Unicorn (2014), illustrated by Brigette Barrager and published by Random House Books for Young Readers, became a New York Times bestseller, depicting a unicorn's magical belief in human girls amid a fantastical realm, spawning a popular series with themes of imagination and friendship.
Rosenthal's children's books frequently centered on optical illusions, as in Duck! Rabbit!, where visual ambiguity sparks debate and delight, and emotions paired with positivity, evident in titles like I Wish You More that affirm joy and resilience through rhythmic, uplifting phrases. Everyday challenges, such as the relatable frustration of mandatory vegetables in Little Pea, were rendered with gentle humor to foster empathy and discussion. Her output included ongoing series like the "Okay" books, starting with The OK Book (2007), illustrated by Tom Lichtenheld for HarperCollins, which celebrates mediocrity as a starting point for growth through clever wordplay and rotations of the letters "OK."
Several of Rosenthal's children's titles achieved bestseller status on the New York Times lists, including I Wish You More at #1 and Uni the Unicorn among the top picture books, underscoring their broad appeal and commercial impact. Her works also garnered awards and honors in the children's category.

Journals and miscellaneous writings

In 2006, Amy Krouse Rosenthal initiated her line of interactive keepsake journals with The Belly Book: A Nine-Month Journal for You and Your Growing Belly, published by Potter Style, a division of Crown Publishing Group. This pregnancy-focused journal featured trimester-based prompts for recording personal reflections, photos, and milestones, emphasizing self-celebration and mindfulness during a transformative life stage.
The line expanded significantly in 2008 with Your Birthday Book: A Keepsake Journal, encouraging annual entries from birth to age 18 to capture evolving family memories and growth through guided questions on achievements, dreams, and gratitudes. Over the subsequent years, Rosenthal developed approximately 11 titles in total, including My Baby Book: A Keepsake Journal for Baby's First Year (2010), The Grandparent Book: A Keepsake Journal (2010), and Encyclopedia of Me: My Life from A to Z (2014), all published primarily through Potter Style. These works utilized creative prompts to foster gratitude, self-expression, and interpersonal connections, such as itemized lists of favorite things or reflective exercises on daily joys, positioning them as entrepreneurial tools for personal documentation rather than traditional narratives. Partnerships with Potter Style facilitated wide distribution via major retailers, enhancing Rosenthal's brand as an innovator in reflective self-help formats.
Beyond journals, Rosenthal's miscellaneous writings included essays and columns in esteemed outlets like O, The Oprah Magazinesuch as a version of her "Midterm Essay" exploring life's unexpected lessonsand The New York Times. Her most notable piece, the 2017 Modern Love essay "You May Want to Marry My Husband," candidly profiled her husband Jason as she faced terminal ovarian cancer, blending humor, love, and vulnerability to inspire readers worldwide; it amassed millions of views shortly after publication and sparked widespread media coverage. These shorter works often echoed her journal themes by prompting reflection on everyday wonders and resilience.

Multimedia and public projects

Films and videos

Amy Krouse Rosenthal began exploring filmmaking around 2008, marking a shift from her established writing career to visual storytelling as a means to capture serendipitous moments and everyday creativity. Inspired by acquiring a simple toy camera, she transitioned into creating short films that emphasized improvisation, personal narration, and minimal production setups, often self-filming with basic equipment to maintain an authentic, unpolished aesthetic. This evolution allowed her to blend her whimsical narrative style with moving images, distributing early works primarily through online platforms like YouTube and her personal website, whosamy.com, to reach a global audience interactively.
Her debut film, 17 Things I Made (2008), exemplifies this approach as a 2-minute-45-second montage showcasing 17 ordinary items and experiences she created, such as a homemade sign or a family moment, culminating in an invitation for viewers to contribute an "18th thing." Self-produced and featuring Rosenthal alongside her husband Jason and sons Justin and Miles in archival-style footage, the video highlights themes of serendipity and communal creativity, filmed casually to evoke spontaneity without professional editing or sets. Uploaded to YouTube, it quickly gained traction, amassing views and inspiring follow-up projects by encouraging audience participation in real-time. This work laid the foundation for her multimedia ethos, emphasizing low-tech production to prioritize emotional resonance over technical polish.
Rosenthal expanded her video oeuvre with shorts like The Beckoning of Lovely series, beginning in 2008 with episodes timed to dates such as 8/08/08, where she documented global acts of connection through simple recordings of people gathering at 8:08 p.m. These collaborative efforts involved family and online contributors, blending live-action footage with her voiceover narration to explore themes of unity and surprise. In 2010, she partnered with the Toronto-based animation studio Thought Bubble for Kindness Thought Bubble, a meditative short film on empathy's impact, where she provided the script and narration while the firm handled animation, resulting in a winner at the Peace on Earth Film Festival for its evocative visuals and concise 3-minute runtime. Later, in 2017, amid her illness, she produced a book trailer for That's Me Loving You, adapting her children's book into a gentle, self-narrated video that reassures viewers of enduring love through soft imagery and rhyme, shared via Facebook and YouTube to promote the publication. These pieces, often under five minutes, were hosted on whosamy.com's dedicated films sectionfeaturing over 40 early web shortsand YouTube, underscoring her preference for accessible, digital distribution over traditional cinema.

Interactive events and installations

Amy Krouse Rosenthal's interactive events and installations emphasized communal participation, serendipity, and fostering unexpected connections in urban settings, often transforming public spaces in Chicago into arenas for collective joy and reflection. Her flagship project, The Beckoning of Lovely, launched on August 8, 2008, at 8:08 p.m., drew hundreds of participants to Millennium Park's Cloud Gate sculpture, known as "The Bean," where attendees engaged in synchronized activities such as waving arms to "beckon" positive experiences and sharing wishes via text messages and in-person interactions. This inaugural event set the tone for a series of free gatherings on palindromic datesSeptember 9, 2009; October 10, 2010; and November 11, 2011each attracting progressively larger crowds, with the final event drawing thousands who participated in choreographed movements, laughter exercises, and wish-making to promote themes of surprise and human connection. The project received widespread media coverage for its whimsical yet profound approach to community building, culminating in edited video compilations that captured the events' essence.
In 2010, Rosenthal created The Money Tree, an impromptu street installation in Chicago where she and collaborators clipped 100 one-dollar bills to a tree in a public park, inviting passersby to notice and take the money while filming reactions to explore themes of generosity, trust, and overlooked opportunities in everyday life. The event highlighted urban integration by turning a mundane cityscape into a site of interactive surprise, with participants' responsesranging from hesitation to delightunderscoring Rosenthal's interest in prompting spontaneous kindness and observation; the resulting short film went viral, amassing millions of views and sparking discussions on social behavior. Attendance was informal but drew dozens of direct interactors, amplified by online sharing that extended its reach.
Rosenthal extended her participatory ethos in 2011 with Life is a Marathon, a pop-up event where a group of strangers gathered in downtown Chicago to cheer enthusiastically for commuters exiting offices after a long workday, mimicking the energy of marathon spectators to deliver unexpected encouragement and connection. This installation blurred the lines between performers and audience, with participants holding signs and shouting affirmations to hundreds of surprised individuals, reinforcing themes of communal support and the transformative power of small, synchronized acts in public spaces. The event's lighthearted yet impactful design garnered media attention for its ability to foster momentary unity among urban dwellers, with footage capturing the genuine smiles and interactions that emerged. In 2014, Rosenthal revisited The Beckoning of Lovely through eight hour-long summer strolls across Chicago, where participants wore yellow spots, distributed temporary tattoos, collected Flickr-submitted wishes, and even collaborated on a custom perfume, further embedding themes of creativity and surprise into the city's fabric with crowds in the hundreds per walk.

Other professional contributions

Radio and media appearances

From the mid-2000s to 2010, Amy Krouse Rosenthal hosted "The Writers' Block Party," a monthly literary and music variety segment on Chicago's WBEZ, NPR's local affiliate, where she conducted quirky interviews with emerging and established writers, performers, musicians, and humorists in a fast-paced, 8-minute format designed to fit diverse talents into a single broadcast. The show, part of the Eight Forty-Eight news magazine program, showcased regional talent through live readings, performances, and discussions, often re-launched with special themes like public art events at Millennium Park.
Rosenthal's contributions extended to national radio outlets, including NPR's All Things Considered, where she appeared in 2005 to discuss her memoir Encyclopedia of an Ordinary Life, highlighting its structure of alphabetical, bite-sized reflections on everyday experiences. Her radio style was distinctly conversational, blending humor and insight to explore the absurdities of daily life through anecdotal lists and interactive elements, mirroring the whimsical tone of her written work.
In print media, Rosenthal contributed essays to The New York Times, notably the 2017 Modern Love column "You May Want to Marry My Husband," a poignant personal ad for her husband written amid her terminal illness, which drew widespread attention for its blend of wit and vulnerability.

Speaking engagements and TED talks

Amy Krouse Rosenthal was a frequent speaker at TEDx events, where she shared insights on creativity, life, and human connections through her distinctive blend of storytelling and interactive concepts. In 2010, at TEDxWaterloo, she delivered "7 Notes on Life," a talk exploring personal reflections on existence structured around musical notes, which has garnered over 178,000 views on the TED platform. Her 2011 TEDxSanDiego presentation, "The Crevices of Life," emphasized the significance of overlooked everyday moments in fostering deeper fulfillment, drawing from her experiences as a filmmaker and author. In 2012, she spoke at TEDxSMU on themes of inspiration and creation, further showcasing her ability to weave personal anecdotes with universal lessons.
Beyond TEDx, Rosenthal engaged audiences at various conferences and festivals between 2010 and 2016. At the 2011 Cusp Conference in Chicago, she presented on "the design of everything," highlighting how ordinary acts can spark innovative thinking and connections. In 2012, she appeared at the Library of Congress National Book Festival on the Family Storytelling Stage, discussing her children's books and encouraging interactive participation from attendees. She also contributed to the main TED conference in 2012 as an experiential designer, creating immersive experiences, and shared a personal story during the TEDYou session that received a standing ovation for its emphasis on wandering and serendipity.
Rosenthal's speaking engagements had a profound impact on her audiences, often inspiring them to recreate or adapt her ideas in their own lives. For instance, her early project "17 Things I Made," a 2008 YouTube video that evolved into live gatherings like the 2008 "Beckoning of Lovely" event, prompted fans worldwide to organize similar collaborative happenings, fostering a sense of communal creativity. Her talks similarly motivated listeners to embrace small, intentional acts of kindness and innovation, as evidenced by widespread fan recreations of her conceptual exercises shared online and in community events.

Personal life

Marriage and family

Amy Krouse Rosenthal married Jason B. Rosenthal on their wedding day in 1991 after meeting on a blind date arranged by a family friend of Jason's father-in-law. The couple shared a creative partnership, often brainstorming long-term goals together, including ideas that influenced Amy's writing on everyday joys and relationships.
They had three children: sons Justin (born around 1993) and Miles (born around 1995), and daughter Paris (born around 1998). Family routines, such as shared meals and playful activities, frequently inspired Rosenthal's work, including books like Yes Day! that captured the whimsy of parenting and household dynamics.
The family resided on a tree-lined street on Chicago's North Side, where Rosenthal raised her children amid a supportive urban environment. As part of Chicago's Jewish community, the Rosenthals participated in local cultural activities, reflecting their heritage in family traditions.

Illness and death

In September 2015, Amy Krouse Rosenthal was diagnosed with advanced ovarian cancer after experiencing severe abdominal pain that she and her doctors initially suspected was appendicitis, leading her to seek emergency care. The pain, which began on her right side, prompted immediate medical attention, revealing the cancer's presence. Following the diagnosis, she underwent treatment, including surgery and chemotherapy, which initially appeared successful, but the cancer recurred in July 2016, marking a significant setback in her battle.
As her condition worsened, Rosenthal publicly shared her story through her 2016 memoir Textbook Amy Krouse Rosenthal and a poignant essay titled "You May Want to Marry My Husband," published in The New York Times' Modern Love column on March 3, 2017. Written from her hospital bed on Valentine's Day, the piece served as a loving "dating profile" for her husband Jason, encouraging him to seek new love after her death and reflecting on their 26 years together with humor and grace. The essay resonated globally, attracting nearly 4.5 million readers and sparking widespread emotional responses, including messages of support from strangers moved by her vulnerability and optimism.
Rosenthal died on March 13, 2017, at her home in Chicago at the age of 51, just 10 days after the essay's publication. Her family held a private funeral, and Jason Rosenthal expressed profound grief, describing himself as "shocked at the beauty" of her words yet "emotionally ripped apart" by the loss. Throughout her illness, her husband and three children provided unwavering support, helping her maintain moments of joy amid the challenges.

Legacy

Awards and recognition

Amy Krouse Rosenthal received numerous accolades throughout her career, reflecting her innovative contributions to children's literature, multimedia projects, and public speaking from her debut in 2005 until her death in 2017. Her works were frequently praised for their whimsical yet profound approach to storytelling, earning recognition from major publications and educational initiatives. These honors spanned categories including writing, film, and speaking engagements, highlighting her versatility as a creator.
In the realm of writing, Rosenthal's accolades began with her 2005 memoir Encyclopedia of an Ordinary Life, which Amazon named one of the top 10 memoirs of the decade for its creative, alphabetized structure. Her children's books garnered significant industry recognition starting in the mid-2000s; for instance, Exclamation Mark (2013), co-authored with Tom Lichtenheld, was selected as an ALSC Notable Children's Book by the American Library Association, celebrating its clever exploration of punctuation and self-expression. The 2014 book Uni the Unicorn achieved New York Times bestseller status, praised for its imaginative take on belief and friendship. In 2015, I Wish You More also became a New York Times bestseller, noted for its uplifting messages of aspiration and abundance, while Rosenthal was selected as the featured author for the Global Read Aloud program's picture book author study, reaching millions of students worldwide through synchronized reading events.
Rosenthal's short films earned honors for their inventive, participatory style. Her 2010 animated project Thought Bubble: Kindness, created in collaboration with others, won Best Animation at the 2011 Peace On Earth Film Festival, underscoring her ability to convey emotional concepts through accessible visuals.
For speaking and public engagement, Rosenthal was recognized as a dynamic presenter, delivering TEDx talks that amplified her philosophical and creative insights. Her 2010 TEDxWaterloo presentation, "7 Notes on Life," and 2012 TEDxSMU talk explored themes of serendipity and making connections, establishing her as an influential voice in inspirational discourse. Additionally, her contributions to NPR and WBEZ as a radio host and commentator from the late 2000s onward highlighted her multimedia presence, though formal awards in this area were less documented.

Posthumous impact

Following Amy Krouse Rosenthal's death in 2017, her creative works saw significant posthumous extensions through major film adaptations on Netflix, amplifying their reach to global audiences. The 2021 family comedy Yes Day, directed by Miguel Arteta, was directly based on her 2009 children's book of the same name and starred Jennifer Garner as a mother who agrees to say "yes" to her children's requests for one adventurous day, alongside Edgar Ramrez and Jenna Ortega. The film garnered substantial success, viewed in 53 million households during its first 28 days of release. In 2023, Netflix premiered Family Switch, directed by McG and featuring Garner, Ed Helms, and Emma Myers in a body-swap narrative about family empathy, inspired by Rosenthal's 2010 book Bedtime for Mommy. It debuted as the top-watched streaming movie on the platform for the week of December 8, 2023.
Rosenthal's books, journals, and short films have maintained enduring popularity, with her uplifting themes of kindness and wonder continuing to resonate in educational and community settings. Posthumous publications in the Uni the Unicorn series, such as Uni the Unicorn: Luck of the Unicorn (2024) and Uni's First Recital (2024), have extended her imaginative storytelling. The Amy Krouse Rosenthal Foundation, dedicated to her legacy, funds childhood literacy programs that incorporate her materials to foster creativity and positive mindsets among young readers. As of 2025, the foundation's Yellow Umbrella Campaign supports ovarian cancer research, literacy initiatives, and events like a virtual gathering on wonder and generosity in October 2025.
Her family's efforts have played a key role in preserving and sharing her influence. In 2018, her husband Jason Rosenthal published the memoir My Wife Said You May Want to Marry Me, reflecting on grief, healing, and new beginnings in response to Amy's viral New York Times essay encouraging him to find love after her death. Tributes in Chicago, her longtime home, include a 2019 public art installation in Lincoln Park's Grandmother's Garden, commissioned by Jason Rosenthal and created by artist Susan Giles, which features Amy's signature yellow umbrella symbolizing connection and joy. The foundation also organizes occasional events recreating elements of her interactive projects, such as gatherings inspired by her 2008 "8/08/08" flash mob at Millennium Park.
On a broader scale, Rosenthal's oeuvre has fueled cultural conversations around embracing serendipity and positivity since 2017, particularly through the foundation's support for ovarian cancer research and literacy initiatives that echo her philosophy of finding magic in ordinary moments. Jason Rosenthal's 2018 TED Talk, "The journey through loss and grief," further extends this resonance by exploring resilience and aliveness in the face of loss.
