 In mathematics, the fourth power of a number nnn is the result of multiplying nnn by itself four times, expressed as n4=nnnnn^4 = n \times n \times n \times nn4=nnnn. This operation is a specific case of exponentiation, where the exponent 4 indicates repetition of multiplication.
Also known as a quartic power or biquadrate, the fourth power plays a fundamental role in algebra and number theory. In algebraic contexts, it defines quartic polynomials and functions, which are expressions of the form ax4+bx3+cx2+dx+e=0ax^4 + bx^3 + cx^2 + dx + e = 0ax4+bx3+cx2+dx+e=0, where the highest-degree term is raised to the fourth power; these equations, solvable via methods like Ferrari's formula, arise in geometry, physics, and engineering for modeling curves and systems with four roots.
In number theory, fourth powers are central to classical problems, such as Fermat's proof that no positive integers aaa, bbb, and ccc satisfy a4+b4=c4a^4 + b^4 = c^4a4+b4=c4, a special case of Fermat's Last Theorem established using infinite descent. Additionally, Waring's problem demonstrates that every natural number can be expressed as the sum of at most 19 fourth powers of nonnegative integers, with only finitely many requiring the full 19 terms. These properties highlight the fourth power's importance in additive representations and Diophantine equations.

Fundamentals

Definition

In mathematics, the fourth power of a number nnn is defined as the product obtained by multiplying nnn by itself four times, expressed as n4=nnnnn^4 = n \times n \times n \times nn4=nnnn. This operation is a specific case of exponentiation, where the exponent 4 indicates repeated multiplication of the base nnn. While the concept applies to any real number nnn, it is typically introduced using positive integers to illustrate the iterative nature of the process.
The fourth power is equivalently the square of n2n^2n2, written as (n2)2(n^2)^2(n2)2, or the product of nnn and its cube n3n^3n3. For instance, the fourth power of 2 is 24=(22)2=42=162^4 = (2^2)^2 = 4^2 = 1624=(22)2=42=16, and similarly, 34=333=327=813^4 = 3 \times 3^3 = 3 \times 27 = 8134=333=327=81. These equivalences highlight the hierarchical structure of powers, allowing computation through intermediate steps.
In contrast to lower powers, such as the square (n2=nnn^2 = n \times nn2=nn) or the cube (n3=nnnn^3 = n \times n \times nn3=nnn), the fourth power requires one additional multiplication, representing a further iteration in the exponentiation process. This progression underscores how each successive integer exponent builds upon the previous by extending the repetition of the base.

Notation and Terminology

The fourth power of a number $ n $ is standardly denoted as $ n^4 $ or $ n^{\mathrm{4}} $, where the superscript indicates exponentiation through repeated multiplication: $ n^4 = n \times n \times n \times n $. This notation follows the general convention for powers in arithmetic and algebra, with the base $ n $ raised to the exponent 4.
Historically, alternative terms have been used to describe fourth powers, reflecting their conceptual roots as the square of a square. The term "biquadrate" (or "biquadratic" in some contexts) directly translates to this idea and was commonly employed in older mathematical texts to denote the fourth power.
In the 16th century, the Welsh mathematician Robert Recorde coined the term "zenzizenzic" for the fourth power in his 1557 treatise The Whetstone of Witte, adapting the Italian phrase censo di censo (square of a square) into a more concise English form. This terminology was part of a broader system Recorde developed for powers, incorporating "zenzic" for squaring and extending to higher exponents like "sursolid" for fifth powers and beyond, which influenced early modern nomenclature before the widespread adoption of exponential notation in the 17th and 18th centuries. Over time, such verbose terms fell out of use in favor of the succinct $ n^4 $ and "fourth power," standardizing the expression in contemporary mathematics.

Representations as Sums

Waring's Problem

Waring's problem addresses the representation of every natural number as a sum of at most g(k)g(k)g(k) kkk-th powers of nonnegative integers, where g(k)g(k)g(k) is the smallest such number that works for all natural numbers. For fourth powers (k=4k=4k=4), Edward Waring conjectured in 1770 that g(4)=19g(4) = 19g(4)=19, meaning every natural number can be expressed as the sum of at most 19 fourth powers. This conjecture formed part of Waring's broader explorations into sums of powers, inspired by earlier results like Lagrange's four-square theorem.
The finiteness of g(k)g(k)g(k) for any fixed kkk, including k=4k=4k=4, was rigorously established by David Hilbert in 1909 through the Hilbert-Waring theorem, which employed analytic methods involving multiple integrals to prove the existence of such representations. The exact value g(4)=19g(4) = 19g(4)=19 was confirmed much later, in 1986, by Ramachandran Balasubramanian, Jean-Marc Deshouillers, and Franois Dress, who demonstrated that 19 terms suffice for all natural numbers and that fewer do not, as certain small numbers require precisely 19. Only seven natural numbers79, 159, 239, 319, 399, 479, and 559demand all 19 fourth powers, with all larger numbers representable using 18 or fewer. For instance, the small number 23 requires eight fourth powers: 23=124+71423 = 1 \cdot 2^4 + 7 \cdot 1^423=124+714.
A related quantity, G(4)G(4)G(4), denotes the smallest sss such that every sufficiently large natural number is a sum of at most sss fourth powers. Harold Davenport proved in 1939 that G(4)=16G(4) = 16G(4)=16, showing that 16 terms suffice for all n>13792n > 13792n>13792, while some numbers arbitrarily large require at least 16 due to modular constraints (e.g., residues modulo 16). Indeed, 13792 is the largest number needing 17 fourth powers, highlighting the transition from the general bound g(4)g(4)g(4) to the asymptotic behavior captured by G(4)G(4)G(4).

Euler's Sum of Powers Conjecture

Euler's sum of powers conjecture, proposed by Leonhard Euler in 1769, posited that for any integer k2k \geq 2k2, at least kkk positive kkkth powers are required to sum to another kkkth power, i.e., a1k++amk=bka_1^k + \dots + a_m^k = b^ka1k++amk=bk with positive integers ai,ba_i, bai,b implies mkm \geq kmk. For k=4k=4k=4, this implied that at least four fourth powers are needed to express another fourth power.
The conjecture for fourth powers was disproved in 1988 by Noam Elkies, who constructed a counterexample using three terms:

This solution was derived from properties of elliptic curves, providing a parametric method to generate an infinite family of such counterexamples. Elkies' approach involved searching for rational points on the curve v2=u3+Au+Bv^2 = u^3 + A u + Bv2=u3+Au+B associated with the equation, yielding integer solutions after scaling.
In the same year, Roger Frye identified the smallest known counterexample (minimizing the largest base) through an exhaustive computer search on a Connection Machine supercomputer, requiring about 100 hours of computation:

These results imply that the minimal number of positive fourth powers needed to sum to a fourth power is three, contradicting Euler's bound of four. The conjecture's failure for k=4k=4k=4 parallels its earlier disproof for k=5k=5k=5 using four terms, suggesting that for higher exponents, fewer than kkk terms may often suffice, though the exact minimal number remains an open question for k>5k > 5k>5.

Diophantine Equations

Fermat's Last Theorem for Exponent 4

Fermat's Last Theorem posits that there are no positive integers aaa, bbb, and ccc satisfying an+bn=cna^n + b^n = c^nan+bn=cn for any integer n>2n > 2n>2. The specific case for exponent 4 concerns the Diophantine equation x4+y4=z4x^4 + y^4 = z^4x4+y4=z4, which has no solutions in positive integers x,y,zx, y, zx,y,z.
In the 1630s, Pierre de Fermat claimed to have proved this case, stating in a marginal note in his copy of Diophantus's Arithmetica (published posthumously in 1670) that he possessed a demonstration too large to fit in the margin. Fermat's approach relied on his method of infinite descent, a technique he developed to show the nonexistence of solutions by assuming a minimal counterexample and deriving a strictly smaller one, leading to an infinite regress impossible in the positive integers.

Other Fourth-Power Equations

The equation x4+y4=z2x^4 + y^4 = z^2x4+y4=z2 has no nontrivial positive integer solutions, a result proved by Pierre de Fermat using the method of infinite descent. This equation is closely related to Fermat's Last Theorem for exponent 4 but features a right-hand side that is a square rather than a fourth power. In modern number theory, similar equations of the form x4+y4=cz2x^4 + y^4 = c z^2x4+y4=cz2 for nonzero integer ccc are studied via elliptic curves; for instance, the curve u2=v3+cvu^2 = v^3 + c vu2=v3+cv arises after a change of variables, and its rank determines the number of solutions, with positive rank yielding infinitely many rational points for certain ccc.
A notable class of equations involving sums of fourth powers is x4+y4+z4=w4x^4 + y^4 + z^4 = w^4x4+y4+z4=w4, which admits nontrivial positive integer solutions, disproving Euler's conjecture that at least four fourth powers are required to sum to another fourth power. General Quartic Solvability

The general quartic equation takes the form $ x^4 + a x^3 + b x^2 + c x + d = 0 $, where the coefficients $ a, b, c, d $ are typically real or complex numbers. Unlike polynomials of degree five or higher, this equation is always solvable by radicals, meaning its roots can be expressed using a finite combination of the four basic arithmetic operations and nth roots. This solvability was established in the 16th century and stands in contrast to the AbelRuffini theorem, which proves that no such general radical solution exists for quintic or higher-degree polynomials with arbitrary coefficients.
The primary method for solving the general quartic, known as Ferrari's method, begins by depressing the equation to eliminate the cubic term. Through the substitution $ x = y - \frac{a}{4} $, the equation transforms into the depressed form $ y^4 + p y^2 + q y + r = 0 $, where $ p, q, r $ are determined from the original coefficients. An auxiliary variable is then introducedtypically by writing the equation as $ (y^2 + m)^2 = (2 m - p) y^2 - q y + (m^2 - r) $ for some $ m $which allows the right-hand side to be expressed as a perfect square. This leads to a resolvent cubic equation in $ m $, whose real root enables the quartic to be factored into quadratics solvable by the quadratic formula. The roots of the original equation are thus obtained as radical expressions involving nested square roots and the cube roots from the resolvent.
Lodovico Ferrari developed this approach around 1540 as a student of Gerolamo Cardano, building on Cardano's earlier work on cubics. Ferrari's solution was first published in Cardano's Ars Magna in 1545, marking the first complete algebraic resolution of the general quartic. Later, Leonhard Euler provided an alternative method in the 18th century, also relying on a resolvent cubic but utilizing all three of its roots to express the quartic's solutions, offering a more symmetric formulation. Both methods confirm the radical solvability of quartics, with the full expressions involving up to cube roots and square roots, though they are complex and rarely used in practice beyond symbolic computation. For the special case of biquadratic equations where $ q = 0 $, the process simplifies further without a full cubic resolvent.
