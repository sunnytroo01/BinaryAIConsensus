Model M keyboard
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Model M keyboard is a series of mechanical computer keyboards originally designed and produced by IBM starting in 1985, featuring a patented buckling spring key switch mechanism that delivers sharp tactile feedback and a characteristic "clicky" sound, with each switch rated for a minimum of 25 million keystrokes for exceptional longevity. First introduced as the "Enhanced Keyboard" with the IBM 3161 terminal, it became the standard input device for IBM's Personal System/2 (PS/2) computers in 1987, establishing the modern PC keyboard layout with 101 keys, including dedicated function keys, a numeric keypad, and duplicated control and alt keys for ergonomic efficiency.
The keyboard's construction emphasizes durability and quality, utilizing a heavy steel backplate for stability, injection-molded plastic housing made from materials like Noryl or polycarbonate, and dye-sublimated PBT keycaps resistant to wear, resulting in a total weight of around 2.3 kilograms (5 pounds). Its buckling spring design, which originated from IBM's earlier typewriter technologies and was patented in 1983, employs a spring-loaded hammer that buckles to register keypresses on an underlying membrane circuit, providing consistent actuation force of approximately 65 grams while minimizing fatigue during extended typing sessions. Early models connected via a detachable AT or PS/2 DIN plug with a coiled cable up to 3.7 meters long, though later variants support USB for modern compatibility.
Production of the Model M spanned multiple facilities, beginning at IBM's facilities in the United States and United Kingdom, before the division was spun off to Lexmark International in 1991 and subsequently acquired by Unicomp in 1996, where manufacturing continues today in Lexington, Kentucky using original tooling for faithful reproductions like the "New Model M." Over its history, the line expanded to include variations such as the 84-key Space Saving Keyboard (SSK), 122-key terminal models, point-of-sale (POS) units with integrated trackballs, and quieter rubber-dome "Quiet Touch" editions introduced in 1993, though the original buckling spring version remains the most celebrated for its uncompromising build and typing dynamics. Despite discontinuation by IBM in 1999, the Model M's influence endures in the mechanical keyboard enthusiast community, where vintage units are prized for their reliabilitymany still functional after decades of useand modern equivalents preserve its legacy for professional and hobbyist applications.

History

Development and Origins

The IBM Model M keyboard emerged in the mid-1980s as a successor to the earlier Model F, with development led by IBM's teams in Lexington, Kentucky, focusing on cost reduction while preserving the tactile quality of the buckling spring mechanism. The original buckling spring design, patented in 1978 by IBM engineer Richard Hunter Harris as the "Buckling Spring Torsional Snap Actuator," had debuted in the Model F keyboards of the early 1980s, providing a distinctive snap-action feedback through a coiled spring that buckled under pressure to actuate a switch. To make production more economical for broader adoption, IBM engineer Edwin T. Coleman III developed a membrane-based assembly in late 1983, patented in 1985, which replaced the Model F's capacitive sensing with a simpler, lower-cost membrane while retaining Harris's core spring mechanism for improved durability and typing feel.
Early prototypes were rigorously tested in 1984 and 1985 to refine the design. The "Captain" prototype, an initial version of the Enhanced Keyboard layout, underwent human factors evaluations across IBM facilities in Lexington, White Plains, Kingston, Boca Raton, and San Jose, allowing iterative adjustments to key travel, actuation force, and overall ergonomics for enhanced typing precision and reduced fatigue. Similarly, the "Battleship" prototypesearly 122-key converged keyboard variantswere developed for terminal applications and tested for robustness, incorporating feedback to strengthen the plastic housing and spring mounts against wear, ensuring longevity in demanding office environments. These tests emphasized balancing the sharp tactile "click" with quieter operation, solidifying the mechanism's reputation for reliable performance.
The Model M was formally integrated as the standard peripheral with IBM's Personal System/2 (PS/2) computer line, announced in April 1987, where it served as the default enhanced 101-key keyboard (part number 1391401) to complement the new systems' advanced interfaces and ergonomics. This launch marked the culmination of the pre-production efforts, positioning the Model M as a durable, high-quality input device central to IBM's evolving personal computing ecosystem.

Production Timeline

Production of the Model M keyboard began in 1985 at IBM's manufacturing facility located at 740 West New Circle Road in Lexington, Kentucky, with additional output at facilities in Greenock, Scotland, and Guadalajara, Mexico. This plant, originally established for typewriter production, transitioned to keyboard manufacturing and became the primary site for assembling the Model M, which was initially developed to provide a durable input device for IBM's expanding line of personal computers. The buckling spring mechanism, central to the design, facilitated efficient assembly processes suitable for large-scale output.
By 1987, production had reached significant scale, aligning with the introduction of the IBM Personal System/2 (PS/2) computer series, where the Model M served as the standard keyboard. Peak output at the Lexington facility exceeded 1 million units annually during this period, with reports indicating millions produced each year to meet demand from corporate and consumer markets. This surge reflected IBM's dominance in the PC sector and the keyboard's reputation for reliability, contributing to widespread adoption in office environments.
In the early 1990s, production began to decline amid broader shifts in the personal computing industry toward lower-cost membrane keyboards and IBM's internal restructuring, including the divestiture of its printer and keyboard divisions to Lexmark in 1991. Despite these changes, manufacturing continued under various arrangements, with IBM maintaining some output at its Greenock, Scotland facility until the end of direct production in 1999. Over the lifespan of the original IBM and Lexmark eras, an estimated 10 million or more Model M units were produced across variants, underscoring the keyboard's commercial impact.

Manufacturer Transitions

In 1990, IBM licensed its buckling spring patents, including the membrane sheet assembly technology (US Patent 4,528,431), to Lexmark International as part of a broader divestiture of non-core assets. This agreement, formalized ahead of Lexmark's spin-off from IBM in 1991, granted Lexmark exclusive rights to produce Model M keyboards using the original IBM designs, leading to manufacturing from 1991 to 1996 at Lexmark's facilities in Lexington, Kentucky. During this period, Lexmark produced variants such as the 122-key version for mainframes, maintaining the core mechanical specifications while adapting for contemporary IBM systems.
Lexmark's exit from keyboard production in 1996 stemmed from the expiration of its five-year agreement with IBM and shifting corporate priorities toward printers, prompting the sale of its keyboard-related assets, tooling, and remaining patents. These were acquired by Unicomp, a new company founded in 1996 by Dean Mullins, a mechanical engineer who had worked on Model M production at Lexmark since 1987. The transfer included critical intellectual property such as the buckling spring actuator patents (e.g., US Patent 4,118,611 from 1978 and subsequent refinements), enabling Unicomp to sustain manufacturing without IBM's direct involvement.
Legally, the patent assignments to Unicomp were executed through formal transfers filed with the United States Patent and Trademark Office, preserving the technical integrity of the buckling spring mechanism while prohibiting the use of IBM trademarks to avoid infringement claims. Technically, this allowed Unicomp to replicate the original IBM membrane sheet assembly and spring-over-capacitor design with minimal alterations, ensuring compatibility with legacy systems through retained tooling from Lexmark's production lines.

Design and Mechanics

Buckling Spring Mechanism

The buckling spring mechanism employed in the Model M keyboard is a patented keyswitch design originating from IBM, featuring a coiled compression spring positioned between the keycap and a pivoting actuator or hammer, which interacts with a underlying rubber membrane sheet to register keystrokes. This three-part systemspring, hammer, and membraneoperates without traditional electrical contacts within the switch itself, relying instead on mechanical buckling to trigger electrical registration.
During actuation, downward pressure on the keycap compresses the spring axially until it reaches its critical buckling point, causing a catastrophic lateral collapse that generates a sharp tactile "snap" and audible click as the spring's torque pivots the hammer to contact the membrane, completing the circuit at a precise depth of about 2.5 mm. This buckling occurs at an actuation force of approximately 65-70 grams, providing immediate and consistent feedback without the need for additional dampening or lubrication, which contributes to the mechanism's reliable performance over extended use.
The design's advantages include exceptional durability, with the spring and hammer components rated for a minimum of 25 million actuation cycles depending on the variant, far exceeding typical rubber dome or early mechanical switches that often fail after 5-20 million cycles due to wear on contacts or domes. This longevity stems from the absence of sliding metal contacts or lubricants that degrade over time, ensuring a uniform feel and reducing failure rates in high-volume typing environments. In comparison to pure membrane keyboards, the buckling spring delivers superior tactile precision and auditory confirmation, enhancing typing accuracy and user satisfaction without compromising on the membrane's cost-effective electrical sensing.

Construction and Materials

The Model M keyboard features a robust construction centered around a curved steel backplate that provides structural rigidity and contributes significantly to its durability. This steel plate, often weighing over 1 pound (0.45 kg) on its own, supports the buckling spring mechanisms and is paired with a steel mounting plate to secure the internal components, resulting in an overall keyboard weight of approximately 2.02.5 kg for original models depending on the specific model and configuration. The external case is typically made of engineering plastics such as Noryl or polycarbonate, which encase the metal elements while maintaining a solid, tank-like feel that resists flexing during use.
Keycaps on the Model M are constructed from polybutylene terephthalate (PBT) plastic with dye-sublimated or pad-printed legends, chosen for its superior resistance to shine, fading, and wear compared to ABS alternatives. The legends ensure long-term legibility even after decades of heavy typing. Internally, the keyboard employs thin membrane sheets that register key presses when actuated by the buckling springs, connected via flat ribbon cables to the controller board for reliable signal transmission without mechanical contacts prone to failure.
The assembly process relies on a riveted construction using approximately seven plastic rivets to fasten the steel plate, PCB, and membrane assembly to the plastic case, a method that enhances tamper resistance by requiring destructive drilling for disassembly. This design, while making repairs challenging, underscores the keyboard's emphasis on longevity and security, with no provisions for user-serviceable parts beyond keycap removal. The buckling spring serves as the primary actuated component housed within this rigid framework.

Layout and Ergonomics

The Model M keyboard established the standard 101-key ANSI layout, evolving from the earlier IBM PC/XT's 83-key design and the PC/AT's 84-key configuration by incorporating a dedicated six-key navigation cluster (including arrow keys, Insert, and Delete) alongside a full row of 12 function keys (F1F12) arranged in two banks above the alphanumeric section, as well as an integrated numeric keypad for enhanced data entry efficiency. This layout became the foundational template for most subsequent PC keyboards, prioritizing touch typists with its logical clustering of editing and cursor controls separate from the main typing area. A variant, the 122-key Model M, was developed specifically for IBM 3270 and 5250 terminal systems, featuring an expanded function key array (24 keys in a top row and additional keys on the left) integrated with a numeric pad to support mainframe data processing workflows.
Ergonomic design in the standard Model M emphasizes usability through a uniform 19 mm key pitch, which facilitates comfortable touch typing by aligning with natural finger placement and reducing reach strain during extended sessions. The keyboard includes flip-out adjustable tilt legs that allow for viewing angle customization, typically ranging from 5 to 15 degrees depending on leg extension, promoting neutral wrist positioning to minimize fatigue. These features, combined with the buckling spring mechanism's tactile feedback, support intuitive key registration without excessive force.
User experience is enhanced by the buckling spring's design, which features approximately 2.5 mm of pre-travel before actuation, enabling reduced finger travel as the spring's snap-back halts descent before bottoming out, thereby lowering overall hand motion compared to contemporaries like rubber dome keyboards that often require fuller key depression. This contributes to efficient typing, with proficient users achieving speeds up to 120 words per minute (WPM) in performance tests, attributed to the mechanism's precise actuation and minimal overtravel.

Variants and Models

Standard Variants

The standard variants of the Model M keyboard encompass the core models produced by IBM, primarily featuring the buckling spring over membrane contact mechanism for tactile feedback and durability across all designs. These variants were developed for general computing and professional environments, emphasizing reliability in office and terminal applications.
The 101-key desktop model, designated by part number 1391401, was introduced in 1986 as the IBM Enhanced Keyboard for personal computers, including the IBM PS/2 series. It utilized a modular 6-pin SDL to 6-pin mini-DIN PS/2 connector, supporting the IBM Mode 2 protocol with compatibility for scancode sets 1, 2, and 3, making it suitable for standard PC setups in home and office environments. This layout included dedicated function, navigation, and numeric sections, establishing the foundational ANSI keyboard arrangement still prevalent today.
The 122-key terminal model, exemplified by part numbers such as 1389160 and 1395660, entered production starting in September 1985 for use with mainframe systems like the IBM 3270 and 5250 display stations. Intended for data entry and terminal operations in enterprise settings, it incorporated additional function and command keys for specialized workflows, along with locking step keys (e.g., for Shift and Alt) to support prolonged input sessions and integrated indicator lights for Caps Lock, Num Lock, and Scroll Lock status. Production continued through the early 1990s, with variants adapting to evolving terminal hardware while maintaining the robust all-plastic chassis.
The 84-key compact model, known as the Space Saving Keyboard with part numbers like 1391472 and 1391518, was announced in September 1986 to address space-constrained workstations, such as those paired with the IBM 3162 ASCII Display Station. This tenkeyless design omitted the dedicated numeric keypad to reduce footprint to approximately 40.6 cm wide, yet preserved full functionality through a Num Lock-activated overlay on the main alphanumeric keys (e.g., mapping numbers to the home row and right-side keys). It retained the PS/2 connectivity and buckling spring switches of its full-size counterparts, ensuring consistent typing experience in compact office or portable setups.

International and Specialized Models

IBM produced Model M keyboards with adaptations for international markets starting in 1987, featuring region-specific key layouts to support local languages and symbols. The United Kingdom variant included a dedicated key for the pound sterling () symbol, while the German layout incorporated keys for the sharp s () and umlaut characters (, , ), and the French model used the AZERTY arrangement instead of QWERTY. These international versions were manufactured at IBM facilities such as the Greenock plant in Scotland and carried unique assembly part numbers tailored to European, Middle Eastern, and African (EMEA) regions, exemplified by 1396790 for EMEA-compatible models.
Specialized Model M variants addressed specific use cases beyond standard desktop computing. The Quiet Touch edition, released in 1993, employed rubber dome switches rather than buckling springs to significantly reduce typing noise while maintaining a similar tactile feel, with the IBM Basic Keyboard (part number 71G4644) serving as a primary example produced by Lexmark. In the 1990s, IBM and its licensees developed enhanced models integrating the TrackPoint pointing stick, such as the Model M13 (part number 13H6705), which combined the buckling spring mechanism with an embedded isometric joystick for compact, all-in-one input devices suitable for portable systems.
OEM adaptations of the Model M were created for third-party brands, including versions for Wyse terminals and AT&T systems, often featuring customized branding and connectivity. Compact 83-key mini models, akin to the Space Saving Keyboard (SSK, part number 1370475), were designed for space-limited applications like laptop docking stations or embedded terminals, produced from 1986 onward.

Part Number Features

The IBM Model M keyboards utilize a part number system, often prefixed with "SK" for stock keeping units in IBM's inventory, to denote specific configurations including layout, interface type, and sensing mechanism. For instance, the SK8110 part number corresponds to the 122-key terminal model, while the SK prefix generally indicates buckling spring over membrane sensing variations. These part numbers also encode connectivity details, distinguishing between the older AT interface using a 5-pin DIN connector and the later PS/2 interface with a 6-pin mini-DIN connector.
A representative comparison of features can be seen in the SK6092 and 1397092 models. The SK6092, a 101-key variant produced in 1986, features a 5-pin DIN connector for AT compatibility, operates at 5V, employs a standard debounce timing of 20ms to filter key bounce, and includes three LEDs for Num Lock, Caps Lock, and Scroll Lock indicators. In contrast, the 1397092, introduced in 1991 for PS/2 systems, uses the mini-DIN connector, maintains the same 5V operation and 20ms debounce for consistent responsiveness, but integrates PS/2 protocol for bidirectional communication with the host, while retaining the identical LED configuration. Both models rely on membrane sensing beneath the buckling spring mechanism, ensuring reliable key registration without the higher precision of capacitive alternatives found in predecessor designs like the Model F.
Compatibility with modern USB-equipped PCs requires active adapters, such as PS/2-to-USB converters, as the Model M lacks native USB support; AT models may need an additional AT-to-PS/2 adapter before USB conversion. These keyboards exhibit limited N-key rollover, supporting only 2-3 simultaneous key presses due to their matrix scanning design, which prioritizes office use over gaming demands. Later reproductions by Unicomp address this by enhancing rollover to up to 10 keys, but original IBM units adhere to the standard limitation for reliability in professional environments.

Reception and Legacy

Initial Adoption and Reviews

Following the launch of IBM's Personal System/2 (PS/2) line in April 1987, the Model M keyboard saw widespread adoption as the standard input device bundled with these systems, which were specifically targeted at corporate and business environments to enhance productivity through improved graphics, networking, and reliability features. The PS/2 series, including models like the 30, 50, 60, and 80, quickly gained traction among enterprises, with IBM reporting sales of approximately 3 million units within the first two years, solidifying the Model M's presence in professional settings where durability was paramount.
Professional reviews highlighted the Model M's superior reliability and tactile feedback compared to cheaper membrane keyboards from competitors like Cherry, positioning it as a benchmark for business-grade peripherals. In a contemporary assessment, PC Magazine praised its innovative 101-key layout and ergonomic design, dubbing it "the keyboard of the future" for its potential to set industry standards in typing consistency and longevity. The buckling spring mechanism contributed to this acclaim, offering a crisp, responsive feel that outperformed lighter alternatives in high-volume office use, though its audible click was occasionally noted as disruptive in shared workspaces.
Despite its strengths, the Model M faced criticisms for its premium pricingaround $295 for early variants like the 1986 IBM 3191 Enhanced Keyboardand substantial weight of approximately 2.5 kg, which deterred adoption among home users seeking more affordable, portable options. These factors limited its appeal beyond enterprise deployments, where the keyboard's robust all-metal construction and long-term dependability justified the investment, ultimately influencing subsequent standards for keyboard durability in the PC industry.

Cultural Impact and Collectibility

The IBM Model M keyboard experienced a resurgence in popularity within retro computing enthusiast communities starting in the early 2000s, driven by online forums, YouTube unboxing and typing demonstration videos, and a growing appreciation for its mechanical typing experience amid the rise of customizable keyboards. Enthusiasts often hail it as the "gold standard" for mechanical keyboards due to its tactile feedback and durability, with Minecraft creator Markus "Notch" Persson describing it as "basically the best keyboard ever made." This revival has led to strong demand in secondary markets, where mint-condition units frequently resell for over $200, reflecting its status as a sought-after vintage item.
The Model M's influence extends to contemporary keyboard designs, particularly through Unicomp's New Model M, which directly replicates the original's buckling spring mechanisma coil spring that provides distinctive tactile and auditory feedbackwhile incorporating modern USB connectivity and a compact layout inspired by the 1980s IBM original. Within typing communities, the keyboard's loud, crisp "click" sound has inspired humorous discussions and memes celebrating its assertive auditory profile, often contrasted with quieter modern alternatives.
Collectibility is heightened by early variants, such as white-label IBM models produced between 1987 and 1992 with part numbers like 1391401, which command premium prices due to their historical significance and condition. Preservation efforts, including the Model M Archive Project that catalogs serial numbers and production dates and as of 2025 includes thousands of entries, alongside initiatives by specialists like ClickyKeyboardswhich has restored and resold over 4,000 to 5,000 units while maintaining a private museumunderscore the community's commitment to safeguarding these keyboards. Its cultural footprint also appears in media, notably in the 2006 British sitcom The IT Crowd, where it symbolizes quintessential IT hardware.

Modern Production and Availability

Unicomp, having acquired the manufacturing rights and tooling from Lexmark in 1996, continues to produce buckling spring keyboards faithful to the original IBM Model M design in Lexington, Kentucky. Current offerings as of November 2025 include the New Model M, a 104-key model available in black or white with USB connectivity, priced at $189 USD. The Ultra Classic provides a 104-key layout in USB format for $199 USD, maintaining the same tactile feedback and durability while accommodating modern interfaces. The Classic 101-key model remains available but is recommended to be replaced by the New Model M. These keyboards are hand-assembled to meet ongoing demand from enthusiasts and professionals seeking robust input devices.
A robust repair ecosystem supports the longevity of both vintage and new Model M keyboards, addressing common issues like the failure of electrolytic capacitors, which often degrade after 20 years or more, causing intermittent key registration or power problems. Detailed community guides outline capacitor replacement procedures, recommending soldering tools and compatible modern components such as 2200F, 16V electrolytic capacitors to restore functionality without altering the original design. Aftermarket parts suppliers offer replacements for keycaps, springs, and stabilizers, enabling users to maintain or customize their keyboards cost-effectively.
New Unicomp Model M keyboards are available directly through the company's website, with shipping worldwide and options for custom layouts. Vintage IBM-era models can be acquired via online marketplaces like eBay, where functional units typically sell for $80 to $300 USD, influenced by condition, rarity, and included accessories such as original cables. To enhance compatibility with 2025 computing setups, third-party adaptersincluding PS/2-to-USB converters and Bluetooth HID modulesallow legacy Model M keyboards to pair wirelessly with devices, preserving their use in contemporary environments.
