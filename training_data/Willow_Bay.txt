 Willow Bay (born Kristine Carlin Bay; December 28, 1963) is an American broadcast journalist, author, academic administrator, and former fashion model renowned for her pioneering work in television news and her leadership in media education. Over a career spanning more than three decades, she has anchored major programs at networks including ABC, CNN, NBC, MSNBC, and Bloomberg Television, while also contributing to digital journalism and authoring a guide on parenting amid global challenges. Currently, she serves as the dean of the USC Annenberg School for Communication and Journalism, the first woman in that role, where she oversees programs in journalism, communication, and public relations. In 2025, she oversaw a study on the proliferation of athlete-owned media. Additionally, Bay is a business leader, holding a controlling stake in the National Women's Soccer League's Angel City FC alongside her husband, Disney CEO Bob Iger. In September 2024, Bay and Iger completed their acquisition of a majority stake in Angel City FC, valuing the team at $250 million (as of the deal announcement).
Born in New York City to middle-class parents Deidre L. Bay and Eugene A. Bay, Willow Bay grew up aspiring to a career in journalism despite an early detour into modeling. She attended Phillips Academy in Andover, Massachusetts, before earning a Bachelor of Arts degree cum laude in literature from the University of Pennsylvania in 1985. Bay later obtained a Master of Business Administration from New York University's Stern School of Business, which equipped her with business acumen that complemented her media pursuits.
Bay's professional journey began in the fashion industry during her college years, when she signed with Ford Models and became the exclusive model and spokesperson for Este Lauder cosmetics in her sophomore year at Penn. This modeling work, which included high-profile campaigns and covers, funded her education and provided on-camera experience, but she transitioned to journalism in the late 1980s, driven by her lifelong interest in reporting. Her early broadcast roles included co-hosting a cable travel show with Matt Lauer and serving as a fashion correspondent on ABC's Live with Regis and Kathie Lee.
In her journalism career, Bay reported for NBC News and covered entertainment and lifestyle topics before anchoring ABC's Good Morning America weekend edition from 1993 to 1998. She joined CNN in 1998 as co-anchor of Moneyline News Hour (later Lou Dobbs Moneyline), a prime-time business news program, where she interviewed world leaders, CEOs, and figures like the Dalai Lama; she continued contributing from Los Angeles after 2000. Bay later worked at MSNBC and Bloomberg Television as a correspondent, focusing on business and economic stories, and served as a senior editor at HuffPost from 2012 to 2014, bridging traditional and digital media. In 2003, she published Talking to Your Kids in Tough Times: How to Answer Your Child's Questions About the World We Live In, a book offering guidance for parents navigating post-9/11 anxieties and global events.
Transitioning to academia, Bay was appointed director of the USC Annenberg School of Journalism in 2014, where she emphasized innovation in reporting and public relations education. In 2017, she advanced to dean of the full USC Annenberg School for Communication and Journalism, holding the Walter H. Annenberg Chair in Communication and Journalism, and has since led initiatives on media literacy, free speech, and diversity in the field. Bay received the Los Angeles Press Club's Joseph M. Quinn Award in 2015 for her contributions to journalism and communication.
On a personal note, Bay married Robert A. Iger, then president of ABC, on October 7, 1995, in Bridgehampton, New York, in a ceremony officiated by Cantor William Walton and the Rev. Timothy Winters; the couple has two sons, Robert Maxwell Iger and William Iger. She also serves on the board of the International Women's Media Foundation, supporting women journalists worldwide.

Early life and education

Early life

Willow Bay was born Kristine Carlin Bay on December 28, 1963, in New York City. She is the daughter of Eugene A. Bay, a prominent advertising and publishing executive who served as publisher of Field & Stream magazine and later worked as a marketing consultant, and Deidre L. Bay, executive director of the Colonial Dames of America in New York.
Bay attended Dominican Academy, an all-girls Catholic school in New York, before graduating from Phillips Academy in Andover, Massachusetts, in 1981.
Raised in New York City amid its bustling media environment, Bay developed an early fascination with broadcasting and print media, shaped in part by her father's career in publishing. During her high school years, at the age of 15, she pursued her growing interest in journalism by applying for an editorial internship at Seventeen magazine. Although rejected for the role, the interview prompted magazine staff to suggest modeling as an alternative path, providing her first opportunity in that field.

Education

She pursued her undergraduate education at the University of Pennsylvania, graduating cum laude in 1985 with a Bachelor of Arts in English literature. Throughout her time at Penn from 1981 to 1985, Bay continued her modeling career, which she had started as a teenager, balancing professional commitments with rigorous academic demands. Her studies in English literature cultivated an appreciation for narrative forms and communication, laying a foundation for her eventual engagement with media and storytelling.
Following her undergraduate degree, Bay earned a Master of Business Administration from New York University's Stern School of Business in 1991. This graduate education equipped her with business acumen that facilitated her transition from modeling to broadcast journalism, as she leveraged her MBA to pursue opportunities in business reporting and align her passion for narrative-driven content with professional media roles.

Professional career

Modeling and pre-journalism

Bay's entry into modeling occurred during her sophomore year of high school in New York, when, at age 15, she interviewed for an editorial internship at Seventeen magazine; rather than securing the writing position, she was scouted for modeling and soon appeared on the magazine's cover. This unexpected pivot marked the beginning of a professional career that spanned her high school years, college, and early post-graduation period, primarily based in New York with some international assignments.
Throughout the 1980s, Bay became a prominent figure in the fashion industry, most notably as the face of Este Lauder cosmetics for a six-year contract starting around 1983, during which she featured in numerous print advertisements and campaigns that defined the brand's elegant, all-American aesthetic. She also graced covers of Seventeen multiple times, including the October 1981 issue, and appeared in editorials and covers for other major fashion publications, contributing to her status as a sought-after model in both the U.S. and abroad, including work in Paris. Her assignments often involved high-profile photography sessions, such as those with Victor Skrebneski for Este Lauder, showcasing her in sophisticated beauty and fragrance promotions.
Although the modeling work offered substantial financial independence, Bay grew dissatisfied with its limitations and used the earnings to fund her education, ultimately transitioning away from the industry in her mid-20s to pursue business studies and her longstanding interest in journalism. This shift allowed her to leverage the discipline and poise gained from modeling into more substantive professional pursuits.

Broadcast journalism

Bay's earliest broadcast roles in the early 1990s included co-hosting the Travel Channel's Etc., Etc. with Matt Lauer in 1991 and serving as a fashion correspondent on ABC's Live with Regis and Kathie Lee. She began her broadcast journalism career as co-host of NBC's "NBA Inside Stuff," a weekly magazine show featuring athlete interviews and behind-the-scenes NBA coverage, alongside Ahmad Rashad from 1991 to 1998. This role, leveraging her prior on-camera experience as a model, marked her entry into sports reporting and helped build her television presence. At NBC, she later served as a correspondent for the "Today" show, contributing segments on various news topics.
In the mid-1990s, Bay joined ABC News as a general assignment correspondent, covering major events including elections and natural disasters for programs like "World News Weekend." She co-anchored "Good Morning America Sunday" from 1993 to 1998, delivering morning news updates and interviews. Her work at ABC emphasized live reporting and on-location stories, establishing her as a versatile network journalist.
Bay transitioned to CNN in 1999, becoming the first woman to co-anchor the flagship financial news program "Moneyline" with Stuart Varney, a role she held until 2001 focusing on market analysis and business developments. She also anchored other CNN programs like "Business Unusual" and "Pinnacle." Subsequently, she freelanced as an anchor and reporter for MSNBC and NBC News in 2005 and 2006, and served as a special correspondent for Bloomberg Television, hosting the primetime series "Women to Watch" that profiled female leaders in business and beyond. Her reporting across these networks centered on business, finance, and sports.
Throughout her broadcast career, Bay covered significant stories such as financial market fluctuations during her "Moneyline" tenure and conducted high-profile interviews with figures including world leaders, CEOs, and the Dalai Lama. She also handled live event reporting, including celebrity and athlete profiles from her sports journalism beginnings.

Digital and editorial roles

In the late 2000s, Willow Bay shifted from traditional broadcast journalism to digital media platforms, leveraging her television experience to adapt to online storytelling formats. She joined The Huffington Post in 2007 as a senior editor, where she played a key role in expanding the site's reach as a pioneering digital news outlet.
From 2007 to 2014, Bay served as senior editor and senior strategic advisor at The Huffington Post, overseeing editorial content and growth initiatives that included multimedia elements and opinion-driven pieces to engage a broader online audience. During this period, she contributed her own articles to the platform on topics such as philanthropy and societal issues.
Bay's digital work extended to Bloomberg, where she acted as a special correspondent starting in 2010, covering major events like the Milken Institute Global Conference and contributing to the network's evolving multimedia coverage in a period of increasing digital integration for news organizations. This role bridged her broadcast background with emerging online strategies, emphasizing concise, impactful reporting suited to both television and digital distribution.
Throughout the 2000s and 2010s, Bay's editorial efforts focused on innovative content creation, including long-form articles that explored societal issues. Her contributions helped transition traditional journalistic practices to interactive digital environments, fostering audience engagement through diverse formats like video embeds and user-generated opinion sections at The Huffington Post.

Academic leadership

USC Annenberg directorship

In March 2014, Willow Bay was named director of the USC Annenberg School of Journalism at the University of Southern California, assuming the position in July of that year. Her appointment was praised for bringing her extensive background in broadcast journalism and digital media to academic leadership at a time when the industry faced rapid transformation.
Bay's directorship focused on modernizing the school to address the decline of traditional media outlets and the rise of digital platforms. She launched the state-of-the-art Annenberg Media Center in 2015 within the newly constructed Wallis Annenberg Hall, creating a central facility for hands-on training in multimedia production, including video, audio, and interactive storytelling. The center, which was named in 2015 in honor of Julie Chen Moonves and Leslie Moonves through a CBS gift but had the Moonves portion of the name suspended in 2018 following allegations of sexual misconduct against him, equipped students with professional-grade tools to foster innovation in journalism. Additionally, Bay introduced curriculum updates, including a new Bachelor of Arts in Journalism program that integrated digital skills such as data visualization and social media reporting, welcoming its inaugural cohort in 2015. These initiatives emphasized practical, multimedia training to prepare graduates for diverse career paths beyond print and broadcast.
Under Bay's guidance, the school experienced growth in both faculty and student enrollment, strengthening its capacity to deliver contemporary journalism education. She recruited notable faculty experts in digital and investigative reporting, enhancing the program's depth and industry relevance. Enrollment in journalism programs rose, attracting more students interested in adaptive media skills amid shifting job markets. Bay's prior experience as a Huffington Post executive editor and broadcast anchor directly informed her approach, enabling her to infuse real-world perspectives into administrative decisions and curriculum design, thus facilitating a smooth transition from professional practice to educational reform.

Deanship and initiatives

In 2017, Willow Bay was appointed dean of the USC Annenberg School for Communication and Journalism, becoming the first woman to hold the position in the school's 45-year history. In this role, she succeeded her prior position as director of the USC Annenberg School of Journalism, expanding her oversight to the entire institution.
Bay leads an organization with approximately 195 faculty members and 2,300 undergraduate and graduate students across communication and journalism disciplines. Under her deanship, the school has maintained its integrated structure, fostering interdisciplinary collaboration between communication studies and journalism programs to address converging media landscapes.
Key initiatives during Bay's tenure include the launch of the Annenberg Inclusion Initiative in 2017, which conducts research on diversity and inclusion in entertainment and media, influencing industry practices through data-driven reports. The school also advanced the Annenberg Diversity Initiative with a 2019 five-year strategic plan aimed at enhancing representation and equity in curriculum, faculty hiring, and student recruitment. In response to technological shifts, Bay oversaw the development of programs addressing AI ethics in media, including the 2024 USC Annenberg Relevance Report on AI's impact in public relations, which emphasizes mitigating algorithmic biases and ethical accountability. Global partnerships have expanded through initiatives like the World Bank Summer Institute, integrating international perspectives into communication training.
From 2024 to 2025, Bay's leadership garnered recognition, including the inaugural Communications Leader of the Year award from the Saban Community Clinic's Communicator Awards in 2024. She also received the Naismith Memorial Basketball Hall of Fame's Curt Gowdy Transformative Media Award in 2024 for her contributions to sports media innovation. Amid evolving industry challenges, the school under Bay produced studies on social media harms, critiquing the emphasis on individual responsibility over corporate accountability, and advocated for protecting youth freedom of expression in discussions around potential regulations.
In November 2025, the school announced plans to merge its three master's journalism programs into a unified program starting in the 2027-28 academic year to streamline education amid evolving media landscapes.
Bay's deanship has emphasized inclusive storytelling as a core principle, with programs like the Annenberg Inclusion Initiative promoting diverse narratives in media production and analysis. Her vision prioritizes preparing students for a dynamic media environment through adaptive curricula that integrate emerging technologies, ethical frameworks, and global viewpoints, ensuring graduates are equipped to navigate rapid industry transformations.

Personal life

Marriage and family

Willow Bay met Bob Iger in the mid-1990s while working as a television correspondent, and the couple began dating on June 13, 1994. They married on October 7, 1995, in an interfaith ceremony officiated by a cantor and a Roman Catholic priest at a private home in Bridgehampton, New York.
Bay and Iger have two sons together: Robert "Max" Iger and William Iger. Iger also has two daughters from his previous marriage, Kate and Amanda.
The couple has balanced their high-profile careersBay as a journalist and academic leader, and Iger as a media executivewhile prioritizing family privacy and making selective joint public appearances at events like the Met Gala. They reside in a Brentwood mansion in Los Angeles, purchased in 2006.

Philanthropy and advocacy

Willow Bay has been actively involved in philanthropy and advocacy focused on advancing women in media and journalism. She serves as chair of the International Women's Media Foundation's (IWMF) Los Angeles Advisory Committee, where she has helped organize annual fundraisers and events to support women and nonbinary journalists globally. Previously, as co-chair of the IWMF board, Bay contributed to initiatives like the Wallis Annenberg Justice for Women Journalists Fund, which provides emergency support and training for female reporters facing adversity.
Bay's initiatives include leading fundraising efforts for scholarships supporting women in journalism. Through her role at USC Annenberg, she has championed programs like the Iger-Bay Scholarship, established with her husband to provide financial aid to underrepresented students pursuing journalism degrees, with a focus on enhancing diversity in newsrooms. She has also hosted exclusive events at her home for the IWMF's Courage in Journalism Awards, raising funds for scholarships and reporting grants aimed at women journalists in 2024 and beyond. Bay has delivered speeches on gender equity in broadcasting, including discussions at IWMF events in 2023 and 2024, emphasizing the need for inclusive newsroom practices and equal opportunities for female reporters.
Her advocacy efforts have earned notable recognition. In 2015, Bay received the Joseph M. Quinn Award for Lifetime Achievement from the Los Angeles Press Club, honoring her contributions to journalism and support for women in the field. Earlier, in 2013, she was awarded the Womens Leadership Award by the Hollywood Radio & Television Society for her work promoting female executives in media.
Bay's broader impact includes mentorship programs that extend her advocacy beyond USC Annenberg. She has spearheaded diversity initiatives, such as partnerships with organizations like ViacomCBS to fund scholarships for graduates of historically Black colleges and universities, aiming to increase representation of women and minorities in newsrooms through 2025. These efforts, including guest lectures and advisory roles, have mentored emerging journalists on navigating gender barriers in broadcasting and digital media.

Sports ventures

Angel City FC ownership

In July 2024, Willow Bay and her husband, Bob Iger, announced an agreement to acquire a controlling stake in Angel City FC, a professional women's soccer team in the National Women's Soccer League (NWSL) based in Los Angeles. The transaction, valued at an enterprise level of $250 million, marked the highest valuation for any women's sports franchise globally at the time and was unanimously approved by the NWSL Board of Governors. The deal closed on September 5, 2024, solidifying their position as the team's primary owners.
As part of the acquisition, Bay assumed the role of controlling owner, serving as the club's governor on the NWSL Board and taking primary responsibility for overseeing operations and strategic direction. The investment included an additional $50 million infusion to bolster the club's growth, with specific allocations toward upgrading player facilities and enhancing competitive resources, including salary support to strengthen the roster. This capital commitment positioned Angel City FC as a leader in elevating standards across women's professional sports, enabling investments in infrastructure that had previously lagged behind other NWSL teams.
Following the closure, Bay's early leadership focused on stabilizing and professionalizing the front office while reinforcing the club's deep ties to the Los Angeles community. Key hires included Carmen Bona as the inaugural President of Business Operations and the promotion of co-founder Julie Uhrman to CEO in February 2025, tasked with driving revenue, operational efficiency, and overall leadership, along with other executive additions to refine soccer and commercial strategies. These moves complemented ongoing initiatives in community engagement, such as expanded youth programs and local partnerships, aligning with Bay's vision for the team as a cultural and social anchor in the region.
In November 2025, the ownership group expanded with new investors, including NBA star Chris Paul, contributing to an updated team valuation of $280 million as of that month.

Impact on women's sports

Willow Bay's involvement with Angel City FC has significantly advanced the visibility and economic viability of women's soccer, leveraging her position as controlling owner to champion the National Women's Soccer League (NWSL) as a model for gender equity in professional sports. Through this role, Bay has emphasized sustainable growth for the league, stating that the investment represents "only the beginning" for women's sports by prioritizing athlete welfare and community impact over short-term gains. Her advocacy aligns with broader efforts to empower female athletes, drawing on her media expertise to enhance storytelling and representation in women's athletics.
In her strategic vision for the NWSL, Bay has focused on long-term expansion and athlete empowerment, including co-leading the "Owning the Narrative" report released in 2025, which examines the rise of athlete-owned media ventures and their role in amplifying voices and driving economic value in women's sports. She has publicly advocated for the league's growth by highlighting the need for increased investment in infrastructure, talent development, and global outreach to maintain its status as the premier women's soccer league worldwide. Bay's statements underscore a commitment to holistic empowerment, where athletes are positioned as leaders in their narratives and business ecosystems, fostering a shift toward billion-dollar valuations in women's sports media.
Bay's public engagements have further amplified her influence, including a keynote at the 2024 espnW Summit, where she declared that women's sports "are not little versions of men's sports" and called for equitable valuation and investment to match their cultural significance. In 2025, she co-presented the Sports Philanthropist of the Year award at the ESPYS Sports Humanitarian Awards to Michele Kang, recognizing contributions to women's sports equity and reinforcing Bay's role in celebrating off-field leadership. These appearances have positioned her as a key voice in discussions on athlete advocacy, linking her media background to improved visibility for women's leagues through innovative broadcasting and content strategies.
The broader effects of Bay's efforts are evident in the elevated profile of women's sports, where her ownership has contributed to record valuations and inspired similar investments across the NWSL and beyond. By integrating her journalism experience, Bay has promoted enhanced media coverage, including athlete-led productions that boost engagement and revenue, thereby challenging historical undervaluation of women's athletics.
As of 2025, Bay has outlined ambitious updates for NWSL expansion, including plans for new franchises and improved media partnerships to expand global reach and domestic viewership. These initiatives aim to sustain momentum from recent growth, ensuring sustained investment in women's soccer while addressing equity gaps in coverage and resources.
