 The Federal Reserve Note is the principal form of paper currency circulating in the United States, constituting legal tender for all debts, public charges, taxes, and dues as established by federal statute. Authorized under Section 16 of the Federal Reserve Act of 1913, these notes are issued by the twelve regional Federal Reserve Banks upon approval by the Board of Governors and serve as direct obligations of the United States government. Each note bears a distinctive seal and serial number identifying the issuing Reserve Bank, along with anti-counterfeiting features such as watermarks, security threads, and color-shifting ink managed through periodic redesigns.
Federal Reserve Notes must be fully collateralized by eligible assets held by the issuing Reserve Banks, including gold certificates, Treasury securities, and other approved holdings, to ensure their soundness amid the system's role in monetary policy implementation. Initially redeemable in gold or lawful money, the notes transitioned to fiat status following the suspension of dollar-gold convertibility in 1971, enabling flexible expansion of the money supply but sparking enduring debates over inflationary pressures and the central bank's influence on economic cycles. Printed by the Bureau of Engraving and Printing, FRNs dominate U.S. currency in circulation, with denominations from $1 to $100 supporting everyday transactions while embodying the shift from commodity-backed to government-backed money.

Definition and Characteristics

Legal Definition and Tender Status

Federal Reserve Notes are defined under Section 16 of the Federal Reserve Act, codified at 12 U.S.C. 411, as promissory instruments issued at the discretion of the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System primarily to facilitate advances to Federal Reserve Banks and other authorized purposes. These notes bear the inscription "This note is legal tender for all debts, public and private," reflecting their status as direct obligations of the United States government, redeemable in lawful money on demand at the Treasury or any Federal Reserve Bank, though post-1933 amendments suspended gold redeemability, establishing them as fiat currency.
Pursuant to 31 U.S.C. 5103, Federal Reserve Notes, alongside United States coins and circulating notes of Federal Reserve Banks and national banks, constitute legal tender for all debts, public charges, taxes, and dues within the United States. This designation ensures their acceptance in settlement of obligations, with federal courts affirming their equivalence to "lawful money" under precedents such as Milam v. United States (524 F.2d 629, 9th Cir. 1975). However, while mandatory for discharging existing debts, no federal law compels private businesses, individuals, or organizations to accept them for new transactions if alternative payment terms are specified in advance. All issued designs remain valid legal tender indefinitely, irrespective of age or series.

Physical Design and Security Features

Federal Reserve Notes are printed in a uniform small size measuring 6.14 inches by 2.61 inches, adopted with the Series 1928 beginning in July 1929 to reduce manufacturing costs by approximately 30 percent compared to the prior large-size format of 7.375 inches by 3.125 inches. This standardization facilitated more efficient production and alignment with international note dimensions.
The notes utilize a distinctive paper blend of 75 percent cotton and 25 percent linen, which enhances durability for an average lifespan of 4.5 to 15.5 years depending on denomination and enhances tactile identification. Embedded within the paper are short and long red and blue synthetic security fibers, distributed randomly to deter counterfeiting. Printing employs intaglio techniques for raised ink that provides a textured feel, particularly noticeable on portraits and borders.
Design elements on the obverse include a central portrait of a U.S. historical figuresuch as George Washington on the $1 notepositioned within an oval frame, with the black U.S. Treasury seal to the left bearing "The Department of the Treasury" and the denomination, and the green Federal Reserve Bank seal to the right displaying a letter (AL) and numeral (112) denoting the issuing district. Paired serial numbers appear in black on the upper right and green on the lower left, alongside the series year indicating design approval. The reverse features denomination numerals, the issuing bank's identifiers, and symbolic engravings, such as the reverse of the Great Seal on the $1 note.
Key security features common across denominations include a watermark portrait matching the printed figure, visible when held to light; an embedded plastic security thread running vertically, inscribed with denomination-specific text (e.g., "USA FIVE" on $5 notes) and fluorescent under ultraviolet light; and color-shifting ink applied to the lower-right denomination numeral, which shifts from copper to green when tilted. Microprintingtiny text in borders and scrolls readable only under magnificationresists reproduction due to ink bleeding in copiers. Higher-denomination notes incorporate advanced elements, such as the $100's blue 3D security ribbon woven into the paper, displaying bells and "100s" that change and move when tilted. These features, periodically updated since the 1990s, aim to stay ahead of counterfeiting technologies while maintaining public usability.

Denominations and Nicknames

The Federal Reserve currently issues notes in denominations of $1, $2, $5, $10, $20, $50, and $100. Higher denominations of $500, $1,000, $5,000, and $10,000 were issued until December 27, 1945, after which production ceased due to reduced demand following World War II, though these notes remain legal tender and can be redeemed at face value. The $2 denomination, introduced in 1862 as a Legal Tender Note and later as a Federal Reserve Note in 1917, remains in limited production primarily for commemorative or replacement purposes.
Colloquial nicknames for U.S. dollar denominations, often rooted in historical slang from the 19th and early 20th centuries, include "buck" or "single" for the $1 note (from frontier-era deer pelts traded as currency units); "fin" or "fiver" for the $5 note (from "five" abbreviated); "sawbuck" for the $10 note (evoking the X-shaped ends of a sawhorse resembling the Roman numeral X for ten); "double sawbuck" or "twenty" for the $20 note; and "C-note" for the $100 note (from the Roman numeral C for century). These terms persist in informal American English despite varying regional usage. Federal Reserve Notes in general are commonly called "greenbacks", a term originating from the green ink used on Legal Tender Notes during the Civil War and extended to modern paper currency. Discontinued higher-denomination notes lacked widespread unique nicknames but were sometimes referred to by their face value or presidential portraits, such as "Grand" for $1,000 (featuring Grover Cleveland).

Historical Development

Establishment Under the Federal Reserve Act

The Federal Reserve Act, enacted on December 23, 1913, and signed by President Woodrow Wilson, created the Federal Reserve System as the central banking authority of the United States and authorized the issuance of Federal Reserve Notes to serve as an elastic currency responsive to economic demands. Section 16 of the Act specified that these notes, issued by individual Federal Reserve Banks, would be obligations of the United States government, receivable by all national and member banks, Federal Reserve Banks, and for all federal taxes, customs duties, and public dues. They were redeemable in lawful money on demand at the United States Treasury in Washington, D.C., or at the issuing Federal Reserve Bank.
Under the Act's provisions, each of the twelve Federal Reserve Banks could apply to its local Federal Reserve Agent for notes, depositing collateral equal to the amount requested, consisting of at least 40 percent in gold certificates or lawful money and the remainder in eligible commercial paper or United States government obligations. This gold reserve requirement, combined with discounting of short-term commercial paper, aimed to link note issuance directly to real economic activity rather than fixed asset holdings, providing greater flexibility than prior national bank notes. The Federal Reserve Board reviewed applications, approved note allotments, and maintained oversight of collateral, with the notes printed by the Bureau of Engraving and Printing but distributed through the Agents to the Banks.
Federal Reserve Notes supplanted earlier forms of paper currency, such as National Bank Notes and gold certificates, by centralizing issuance under the Federal Reserve System while designating the notes as legal tender for all public and private debts. The first series of these notes, known as Series 1914 large-size notes, began production in 1914 following the organization of the Reserve Banks, with initial circulation commencing later that year as the system became operational on November 16, 1914. Denominations ranged from $5 to $100 initially, featuring portraits of historical figures and seals identifying the issuing district, marking a shift toward a unified national currency framework.

Shift from Gold-Backed to Fiat Currency

Under the Federal Reserve Act signed into law on December 23, 1913, Federal Reserve Notes were introduced as the primary circulating currency, issued by Federal Reserve Banks against collateral including at least 40 percent gold reserves and eligible commercial paper. These notes were redeemable in gold on demand at the United States Treasury in Washington, D.C., or in gold or lawful money at Federal Reserve agents, reflecting the era's adherence to the gold standard which constrained monetary expansion to available metal stocks. This redeemability underpinned public confidence, as the notes explicitly promised payment in specie, limiting the Federal Reserve's ability to issue currency beyond gold-backed limits.
The shift began amid the Great Depression, when deflationary pressures and banking panics prompted President Franklin D. Roosevelt to issue Executive Order 6102 on April 5, 1933, criminalizing the hoarding of gold coin, bullion, and certificates by private citizens and requiring their surrender to the Treasury at the official price of $20.67 per ounce. This effectively suspended domestic redeemability of Federal Reserve Notes for gold, as individuals could no longer exchange notes for physical metal, transitioning the currency toward reliance on government decree. The order aimed to prevent gold outflows that exacerbated bank runs, but it centralized control over reserves and enabled monetary expansion decoupled from domestic gold holdings.
The Gold Reserve Act, enacted on January 30, 1934, formalized this domestic detachment by transferring all monetary gold from Federal Reserve Banks and private hands to the U.S. Treasury, nationalizing approximately 700,000 metric tons of gold. The Act devalued the dollar by raising the gold price to $35 per ounce, increasing the Treasury's gold certificate account by over 60 percent and authorizing Federal Reserve Notes to be issued against government securities rather than direct gold backing. While FRNs remained indirectly linked to gold through international commitments under the 1944 Bretton Woods Agreementwhere foreign central banks could convert dollars at the fixed ratethis marked the effective end of gold redeemability for U.S. citizens, rendering the notes fiat domestically as their value derived from legal tender laws and the government's promise rather than metal reserves.
The final step occurred on August 15, 1971, when President Richard Nixon announced the suspension of dollar convertibility into gold for foreign governments, known as the "Nixon Shock," amid rising U.S. inflation, trade deficits, and a drain on Fort Knox reserves as European nations redeemed dollars en masse. U.S. gold stocks had fallen to about 8,100 metric tons by then, insufficient to cover short-term foreign dollar claims exceeding $40 billion. This severed the last gold tether, fully establishing Federal Reserve Notes as unbacked fiat currency, valued solely by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government and enforced through legal tender status. The move facilitated flexible monetary policy but introduced volatility, as currency issuance became unconstrained by commodity limits, contributing to subsequent dollar depreciation and the end of fixed exchange rates.

Major Redesigns and Policy Influences

The most significant early redesign of Federal Reserve Notes occurred in 1928 with the introduction of small-size notes, reducing dimensions from 7.4218 by 3.125 inches to 6.14 by 2.61 inches, a change aimed at lowering manufacturing costs through reduced paper usage and alignment with international standards following economic strains post-World War I. This shift standardized the format across U.S. currency types, facilitating easier handling and vending machine compatibility, while maintaining core elements like portraits and vignettes but with updated engravings for efficiency.
In the late 20th century, policy-driven redesigns focused on counterfeiting deterrence amid rising threats from advanced reprographic technologies such as color photocopiers and scanners. The Series 1996 notes, announced by the Treasury Department in 1990 and first issued with the $100 denomination in 1996, incorporated innovations including larger off-center portraits, fine-line printing, watermarks matching the portrait, and embedded security threads visible under ultraviolet light, representing the first comprehensive overhaul since 1928 to preempt counterfeiting advances. Subsequent denominations followed: $50 in 1997, $20 in 1998, $10 and $5 in 2000, reflecting a phased rollout policy to minimize disruption while enhancing public familiarity with features.
The Series 2004 redesign extended these efforts by adding subtle background colorspale peach for the $20, blue for the $100, and othersto aid quick denomination identification and further complicate digital reproduction, alongside color-shifting ink on numerals that changes from copper to green when tilted. These modifications stemmed from interagency collaboration, including the Secret Service's Advanced Counterfeit Deterrence committee, underscoring U.S. monetary policy's emphasis on preserving currency credibility as a fiat system's cornerstone, where trust hinges on perceived unforgeability rather than intrinsic value. The $5 note received its update in 2008, completing the cycle for circulating denominations and demonstrating ongoing adaptation to evolving threats without altering the predominantly green palette established since 1861.

Production and Distribution

Manufacturing by the Bureau of Engraving and Printing

The Bureau of Engraving and Printing (BEP), operating under the U.S. Department of the Treasury, exclusively manufactures Federal Reserve Notes at its two production facilities: one in Washington, D.C., established in 1862, and another in Fort Worth, Texas, which began currency production in 1991 to expand capacity amid rising demand. These sites handle the full printing process, producing billions of notes annuallyequivalent to over $2 trillion in value in circulation as of recent estimatesto fulfill orders from the Federal Reserve Board based on projected net demand and reserve needs.
Currency production utilizes specialized materials, including a unique paper blend of 75% cotton and 25% linen, sourced from a single U.S. mill in Massachusetts, which provides durability and a distinctive texture while incorporating embedded red and blue synthetic fibers for basic anti-counterfeiting. Inks are formulated with metallic and pigment components for color-shifting effects and raised intaglio printing, ensuring tactile security features. Engraved steel plates, maintained and updated by BEP's engravers, transfer intricate designs via high-pressure intaglio presses, which imprint recessed ink into the paper for a raised feel upon drying.
The printing sequence starts with the reverse side: large sheets (approximately 22.8 by 10.5 inches) accommodating 32 to 50 notes per sheet, depending on denomination, receive green ink applications in multiple runs for elements like guilloche patterns and numerals. Sheets then cure in secure vaults for 72 hours to prevent distortion from moisture. The obverse follows with black ink for portraits, vignettes, Treasury and Federal Reserve seals, and serial numbersprinted in a format of one letter (series year indicator), eight digits, and one letter (issuing district)followed by overprinting of denomination in green. Security elements, including three-dimensional security ribbons woven during papermaking for $100 notes or microprinted threads for others, are embedded prior to printing, with features like watermarks formed by paper thickness variations during sheet formation.
Post-printing, automated systems inspect sheets via high-speed cameras and sensors for defects such as misprints, color deviations, or pinholes; flawed sheets are segregated and destroyed via shredding or pulping to maintain integrity, with a production efficiency targeting minimal waste under federal oversight. Validated sheets are trimmed, separated into individual notes, counted, and banded into secure packages or "bricks" of 4,000 notes each, then transported under armed escort to Federal Reserve vaults for distribution. Annual variable costs for printing, including paper, ink, and labor, totaled approximately $361.9 million in fiscal year 2024, reflecting economies of scale in high-volume intaglio operations. Recent enhancements, such as larger-sheet printing introduced around 2018, have boosted throughput by enabling more notes per impression while preserving anti-counterfeiting standards.

Issuance Through Federal Reserve Banks

Federal Reserve Notes are issued exclusively through the twelve regional Federal Reserve Banks, each acting as the issuing authority for notes imprinted with its specific seal and alphanumeric designation (A through L, corresponding to Boston through San Francisco). The Board of Governors authorizes the issuance under Section 16 of the Federal Reserve Act, with notes supplied from the Bureau of Engraving and Printing to the Federal Reserve Agent at each Reserve Bank. These agents exchange the notes for collateral pledged by the Reserve Bank, ensuring backing typically equivalent to the notes' face value, though collateral eligibility has broadened over time to include all bank assets since October 2003.
Upon issuance, the notes become direct liabilities of the specific Reserve Bank, recorded as "Federal Reserve notes outstanding" on its balance sheet and published weekly in the Board's H.4.1 statistical release. Although legally obligations of the United States and receivable for all public dues, taxes, and redeemable in lawful money, the operational liability rests with the issuing bank, which bears a first lien on its assets. The Reserve Banks are assessed for production costs by the Board based on the prior year's circulation volume, with payments remitted monthly.
The Reserve Banks distribute these notes into circulation by fulfilling orders from approximately 8,400 depository institutions via FedCash Services, prioritizing fit recirculable notes supplemented by new prints as needed. Institutions submit orders electronically through the FedLine Web platform, debiting their reserve accounts at the district Reserve Bank, with shipments handled by armored carriers for efficiency and security. This process ensures currency availability to meet public demand without direct issuance to individuals, as depository institutions serve as the conduit to end users. Unfit notes returned to Reserve Banks are verified, destroyed if necessary, and offset against new issuances to maintain supply balance.

Circulation and Replacement Processes

Federal Reserve notes enter circulation through Federal Reserve Banks, which distribute them to depository institutions upon request in exchange for credits to the institutions' reserve accounts at the Fed. Depository institutions subsequently release the notes to the public via cash withdrawals, payments, and automated teller machines, facilitating their use in everyday transactions across the economy.
As notes endure handling and environmental exposure, they degrade, prompting a replacement cycle managed by the Federal Reserve System. Depository institutions deposit used notes back to Reserve Banks, which process and sort them using high-speed equipment to assess fitness for reissuance based on criteria such as soil level, tears, and overall integrity. Fit notes are verified, bundled, and returned to circulation, while unfit notesdefined as those failing to meet minimum quality standardsare removed from the supply.
Unfit notes undergo cancellation and destruction at designated Reserve Bank facilities through shredding into confetti-like particles or incineration, with rigorous accounting to prevent counterfeiting or loss, as mandated by Treasury regulations. The average lifespan of notes, influenced by circulation frequency and denomination-specific usage patterns, ranges from 5.7 years for $10 notes to 11.1 years for $20 notes, with lower denominations like $1 notes lasting approximately 7.2 years due to higher handling volume.
Replacement printing addresses both unfit note destruction and net demand growth, with the Federal Reserve Board annually ordering production from the Bureau of Engraving and Printing. For calendar year 2026, the order targets 3.8 to 5.1 billion notes valued at $108.9 to $139.6 billion to meet estimated net demand from domestic and international customers, as Federal Reserve Notes are widely held and used internationally as a reserve currency, reflecting a primary need to substitute destroyed currency amid stabilizing post-pandemic trends. As of December 2024, approximately 55.4 billion notes remained in circulation, underscoring the scale of ongoing processing to sustain public access.

Evolution of Series and Designs

Large-Size Notes (Pre-1928)

Large-size Federal Reserve Notes, measuring 7.375 by 3.125 inches, were issued from November 1914 until their replacement by smaller notes in 1928. These notes represented the first currency produced under the Federal Reserve Act of 1913, designed to provide an elastic currency supply responsive to economic needs. Printed by the Bureau of Engraving and Printing, they featured intricate engravings, including portraits of notable Americans on the obverse and allegorical or historical vignettes on the reverse.
The initial Series 1914 encompassed denominations from $5 to $100, with the $5 note bearing a portrait of Abraham Lincoln, the $10 featuring Alexander Hamilton, the $20 Grover Cleveland, the $50 Ulysses S. Grant, and the $100 Benjamin Franklin. Higher denominations of $500, $1,000, $5,000, and $10,000 were introduced in Series 1918, incorporating portraits such as William McKinley on the $500. These notes displayed a blue Federal Reserve Bank seal indicating the issuing district and were redeemable in gold on demand until 1933.
Distinguishing features included larger format for detailed security elements like fine-line intaglio printing and guilloche patterns to deter counterfeiting, though production costs were higher due to the size. The transition to small-size notes in Series 1928, reducing dimensions to 6.14 by 2.61 inches, aimed to lower paper and ink usage, streamline manufacturing, and facilitate easier handling and storage in banking operations. Large-size notes continued in limited circulation post-1928 but were gradually phased out as small-size versions proliferated.

Small-Size Notes (19281995)

Small-size Federal Reserve Notes were introduced beginning with Series 1928, marking a shift from the previous large-size format measuring 7.375 by 3.125 inches to a more compact 6.14 by 2.61 inches. This redesign aimed to reduce production costs, particularly on specialized banknote paper, and align U.S. currency more closely with international standards for portability and handling efficiency. Initial production focused on denominations from $5 to $100, with higher denominations up to $10,000 also issued, though the latter saw limited circulation.
The Series 1928 notes retained core portrait designs from their large-size predecessors, such as Alexander Hamilton on the $5, Andrew Jackson on the $10 and $20, Ulysses S. Grant on the $50, and Benjamin Franklin on the $100, but adapted them to the smaller format with centered vignettes and simplified borders. The reverse sides featured ornate guilloch patterns in green ink, emphasizing denomination numerals. Early issues included the phrase "Redeemable in gold on demand at the United States Treasury, or in gold or lawful money at any Federal Reserve Bank," reflecting adherence to the gold standard until its suspension. Printing began in 1928, with public release occurring on July 10, 1929.
Subsequent series, such as 1928A through 1928D, incorporated updates to Treasury and Federal Reserve officials' signatures and minor plate revisions, but maintained the black-and-green color scheme and intaglio printing method. The Series 1934 redesign removed the gold redemption clause following the Gold Reserve Act of 1934, which nationalized gold holdings and devalued the dollar, shifting the obligation to "payable to the bearer on demand." Higher denominations like the $500 (William McKinley), $1,000 (Grover Cleveland), $5,000 (James Madison), and $10,000 (Salmon P. Chase) were printed through 1945 but ceased circulation in the 1960s due to low demand and security concerns, with remaining stocks destroyed by 1969.
Lower denominations expanded the series' scope: $1 notes featuring George Washington debuted in Series 1963 alongside the phase-out of silver certificates, and $2 notes with Thomas Jefferson followed in 1976. Design consistency persisted through series like 1950, 1963, 1969, 1976, and 1985, with periodic signature changes and subtle enhancements for durability, such as improved paper composition. By the 1990 series, initial security features like a plastic strip were added to the $100 note to combat counterfeiting. These small-size notes, produced until Series 1995, formed the backbone of U.S. circulating currency, emphasizing functionality over aesthetic overhaul until the large-portrait redesigns of 1996.

Large-Portrait and Color-Enhanced Notes (1996Present)

The Series 1996 $100 Federal Reserve Note introduced the large-portrait design, featuring an enlarged and off-center portrait of Benjamin Franklin to enhance detail visibility and deter counterfeiting amid advances in color photocopying technology. This marked the first major U.S. currency redesign in 66 years, with issuance to banks beginning March 25, 1996. Key security enhancements included a watermark replicating the portrait, visible when held to light; a plastic security thread embedded in the paper, bearing "USA 100" and shifting from black to copper; microprinting around the portrait collar reading "THE UNITED STATES OF AMERICA"; and fine-line engraving patterns difficult for scanners to reproduce. These features built on small-size note standards while prioritizing authentication under normal handling conditions.
The large-portrait format expanded to other denominations in subsequent series, applying similar off-center, detailed portraits and security elements to $50 (Series 1997), $20 (Series 1998), $10 (Series 2000), and $5 (Series 2000) notes, while $1 and $2 retained smaller vignette-style portraits. For instance, the $50 note incorporated a large portrait of Ulysses S. Grant with matching watermark and security thread displaying "USA 50." This phased rollout aimed to standardize anti-counterfeiting measures across circulating denominations without immediate full replacement of existing notes, all of which remained legal tender.
Beginning with the Series 2004 $20 note, issued October 9, 2003, Federal Reserve Notes incorporated subtle background colorspeach, aqua, and greenfor faster visual denomination identification by users and cash handlers, complementing existing security features like color-shifting ink on the numeral. The $50 Series 2004 followed in late 2004, adding blue and red backgrounds with flag and quill motifs woven into the design. These color enhancements extended to $10 (Series 2006), $5 (Series 2008), and others, using non-dominant hues to avoid interfering with monochrome authentication tools while improving public familiarity.
The $100 note received its most advanced color-enhanced redesign in Series 2013, circulated from October 8, 2013, featuring a woven blue 3-D security ribbon with bells and "100" images that appear to move when tilted; a copper-to-green color-shifting bell within a blue inkwell; enhanced intaglio printing for tactile differentiation; and expanded color elements including orange and yellow in the numeral 100. These innovations, developed through collaboration between the Bureau of Engraving and Printing, Secret Service, and Federal Reserve, targeted sophisticated digital counterfeiting without altering the note's core dimensions or portrait style.
As of 2025, no new series redesigns have been issued beyond these updates, with production focused on replacing worn notes across denominations via annual print orders ranging from 4.1 to 5.9 billion units valued at $83.2 to $113.0 billion, determined by circulation demand and fitness returns. All prior large-portrait and color-enhanced notes continue in parallel circulation, supported by the Federal Reserve's policy of indefinite legal tender status for U.S. currency regardless of series date. Ongoing evaluations by the Advanced Counterfeit Deterrent committee monitor printing technologies for potential future iterations, prioritizing embedded features over aesthetic overhauls.

Recent and Proposed Changes

In May 2025, the U.S. Currency Program began producing nonsequential straps of $20 Federal Reserve notes as part of operational efficiencies enabled by new printing equipment at the Bureau of Engraving and Printing. These straps feature serial numbers that may include gaps, do not end in "01" or "00," and exclude star notes, though all notes remain legal tender at face value regardless of issuance sequence. This change applies specifically to $20 denominations initially and reflects adaptations to modern production processes without altering note validity or security features.
The Federal Reserve Board's 2025 currency print order totaled between 4.1 billion and 5.9 billion notes, valued at $83.2 billion to $113.0 billion, prioritizing completion of series updates with updated signatures of the Treasury Secretary and Treasurer alongside production improvements. For 2026, the order range decreased to 3.8 billion to 5.1 billion notes, attributed to reduced demand projections and investments in equipment for testing the next banknote family. These adjustments align with declining cash usage trends, as evidenced by lower overall print volumes compared to prior years, while maintaining reserves against wear and circulation needs.
Proposed redesigns focus on enhanced security and counterfeiting deterrence under the Advanced Counterfeit Deterrence (ACD) committee's oversight, with primary emphasis on authentication features rather than aesthetic changes. Production of a new enhanced $10 Federal Reserve note series is slated to begin in 2026, incorporating advanced security elements as part of a phased rollout for lower denominations. Broader plans for a "next family" of notes involve equipment upgrades and testing, potentially introducing periodic series updates every two years starting with the $10, though specifics on features remain under development by the Treasury and Federal Reserve.
Legislative proposals include the Harriet Tubman Tribute Act of 2025, introduced in March 2025, which directs the Treasury Secretary to feature abolitionist Harriet Tubman on all $20 notes, reviving a 2016 initiative delayed amid prior administrations' priorities. As of October 2025, the bill awaits congressional action, with Treasury timelines previously indicating implementation no earlier than 2028 if approved, balancing historical representation against production costs and security priorities. No confirmed changes to higher denominations or discontinuation of existing series have been announced, preserving the current small-size note framework established since 1928.

Economic Role and Implications

Function in the U.S. Monetary System

Federal Reserve Notes function as the primary circulating paper currency within the U.S. monetary system, comprising over 99 percent of all U.S. currency in circulation as of recent estimates. Federal Reserve Notes are the standard U.S. banknotes and are widely held and used internationally as a reserve currency. Issued by the twelve Federal Reserve Banks under authority granted by the Federal Reserve Act of 1913, these notes serve as a medium of exchange for goods, services, and financial obligations, facilitating transactions from retail purchases to interbank settlements. Their denomination ranges from $1 to $100, with higher denominations like $500 notes discontinued from production in 1945 but remaining legal tender if held.
As legal tender, Federal Reserve Notes are mandated by federal statute to be accepted for payment of all public and private debts, taxes, and dues, except where specific laws dictate otherwise, such as certain import duties historically. This status derives from Section 16 of the Federal Reserve Act and subsequent legal affirmations, positioning them as redeemable at face value in lawful money at the U.S. Treasury or any Federal Reserve Bank, though in practice, they themselves constitute lawful money following the abandonment of gold redeemability in 1971. In the broader monetary framework, they form the currency component of money stock measures like M1, alongside demand deposits, enabling the public to hold cash outside depository institutions for liquidity and transaction purposes.
Unlike commodity-backed currencies of the past, Federal Reserve Notes operate as fiat money, deriving value from the full faith and credit of the U.S. government and widespread acceptance rather than intrinsic worth or fixed redemption in specie. The Federal Reserve manages their supply through orders to the Bureau of Engraving and Printing, distribution to depository institutions, and replacement of unfit notes, aiming to maintain adequate availability without excess that could disrupt price stability. This process integrates FRNs into the monetary base, where they represent liabilities of the Federal Reserve Banks, backed by collateral such as U.S. Treasury securities held in reserve, supporting the system's role in promoting maximum employment and stable prices.

Impact on Purchasing Power and Inflation

The issuance of Federal Reserve Notes, as unbacked fiat currency, has contributed to a sustained erosion of the U.S. dollar's purchasing power through monetary expansion that outpaces economic output growth. Since the Federal Reserve's establishment in 1913, the dollar has lost approximately 96-97% of its purchasing power, as measured by the Consumer Price Index (CPI), requiring about $31 in 2024 to purchase what $1 bought in 1913. This decline reflects cumulative inflation averaging around 3% annually over the period, driven in part by the Fed's ability to increase the money supply via note issuance and related operations without commodity constraints.
Under the fiat system formalized after the 1971 suspension of dollar-gold convertibility, Federal Reserve Notes' value derives from legal tender laws and public acceptance rather than intrinsic backing, enabling discretionary money creation that empirically correlates with higher inflation volatility compared to commodity standards. Historical analyses indicate that fiat regimes exhibit greater long-term inflation than gold-linked systems, with U.S. money supply (M2) expanding over 20-fold since 1971 while real GDP grew about 10-fold, exerting upward pressure on prices. The quantity theory of money posits that such expansions, absent proportional velocity or output adjustments, diminish each note's real value, as evidenced by CPI spikes during periods of aggressive Fed balance sheet growth, such as the 1970s (peaking at 13.5% annual inflation in 1980) and post-2020 quantitative easing.
The Fed's explicit 2% inflation target since 2012 institutionalizes mild purchasing power loss, compounding to halve the dollar's value roughly every 35 years, which proponents argue anchors expectations but critics contend systematically transfers wealth from savers to borrowers and the government via seigniorage. Empirical data from Bureau of Labor Statistics CPI series confirm this trajectory: from 1913 (CPI 9.9) to September 2025 (CPI 321), yielding a multiplier effect that underscores the causal link between fiat note proliferation and diminished store-of-value function. While external factors like supply shocks contribute, the Fed's control over base money via Federal Reserve Notes issuance remains the primary lever, as pre-1913 deflationary episodes under partial gold standards showed lower average price level changes.

Ties to Federal Reserve Monetary Policy

Federal Reserve Notes constitute the bulk of currency in circulation, forming a key component of the U.S. monetary base alongside bank reserves held at Federal Reserve Banks. The monetary base, often denoted as M0 or the adjusted monetary base, totaled approximately $5.8 trillion as of September 2025, with currency in circulationpredominantly Federal Reserve Notesaccounting for about $2.4 trillion of that figure. The Federal Reserve influences the overall size of this base through tools such as open market operations, where purchases of securities inject reserves into the banking system, potentially enabling increased demand for physical currency if economic activity expands. However, the issuance of notes themselves does not directly expand the base; when depository institutions order currency from a Reserve Bank, the transaction debits their reserve balance and credits their vault cash, leaving the total base unchanged but altering its composition.
The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) implements monetary policy primarily by targeting the federal funds rate, which affects short-term interest rates and, indirectly, the opportunity cost of holding non-interest-bearing Federal Reserve Notes versus deposits. Lower policy rates, as seen during accommodative periods like 20082015 or 20202022, reduce the incentive to shift funds into interest-bearing assets, potentially sustaining or elevating demand for physical currency. Conversely, tighter policy with higher rates, such as the 525 basis point increases from March 2022 to July 2023, can encourage economization of cash holdings, though empirical data shows currency in circulation continued rising to over $2.3 trillion by late 2023 amid such hikes, driven more by structural factors like international demand than domestic policy shifts alone.
In quantitative easing episodes, the Fed's balance sheet expansionsreaching $8.9 trillion by mid-2022primarily boost reserve balances, but sustained liquidity can indirectly support currency growth if public and bank demand responds to heightened economic confidence or hoarding behaviors. Currency orders from banks are collateralized by eligible assets and based on projected needs, with the Fed reimbursing the Bureau of Engraving and Printing for production costs quarterly; this process ensures supply meets demand without the notes serving as an active policy lever, unlike reserve adjustments. Historical data indicates currency in circulation grew from $826 billion in December 2000 to $2.07 trillion by December 2020, correlating with base expansions but also reflecting steady demand inelasticity to short-term policy changes. Thus, while Federal Reserve Notes embody the tangible extension of the Fed's liabilities, their ties to policy operate through broader liquidity channels rather than direct issuance controls.

Legal Framework and Limitations

Statutory Authorizations and Restrictions

Federal Reserve Notes were statutorily authorized by the Federal Reserve Act, enacted on December 23, 1913, with Section 16 providing the framework for their issuance, codified primarily in 12 U.S.C. 411. Under this provision, notes are issued at the discretion of the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System to Federal Reserve Banks for the purpose of making advances, upon application by the banks to the local Federal Reserve agent. The notes constitute obligations of the United States but represent direct liabilities of the issuing Federal Reserve Bank, redeemable in lawful money on demand at the Treasury or any Federal Reserve Bank.
Their status as legal tender for all debts, public and private, including taxes and dues, is established by 31 U.S.C. 5103, which explicitly includes Federal Reserve Notes alongside United States coins and circulating notes of Federal Reserve and national banks. However, this legal tender designation does not compel private businesses, persons, or organizations to accept them for goods or services, as no federal statute mandates such acceptance.
Issuance is restricted by requirements for collateralization under 12 U.S.C. 412, mandating that each Federal Reserve Bank pledge eligible assetssuch as gold certificates (historically), Treasury notes, or other specified securitieswith the local Federal Reserve agent against outstanding notes. No collateral is required for notes held in the vaults of or otherwise by Federal Reserve Banks themselves. Originally backed in part by gold reserves, the requirement for gold redemption was suspended domestically in 1933 and internationally in 1971; by the Act of March 18, 1968 (Public Law 90-269), the statutory gold reserve backing for Federal Reserve Notes was fully eliminated, rendering them fiat currency without commodity redeemability.
Additional restrictions prohibit Federal Reserve Banks from paying out notes issued through another Reserve Bank, subjecting violators to a 10% tax on the face value of such notes, as per provisions in the Federal Reserve Act. Private issuance or counterfeiting of Federal Reserve Notes is criminalized under 18 U.S.C. 334, punishable by fine or imprisonment up to five years. States and territories may impose taxes on Federal Reserve Notes, subject to federal limitations under 31 U.S.C. 5154, but cannot discriminate against them relative to other currency forms. Denominations, designs, and production volumes remain under the joint authority of the Secretary of the Treasury and the Federal Reserve Board, with no statutory cap on total issuance beyond collateral and policy constraints.

Distinction from Other U.S. Currency Forms

Federal Reserve Notes differ from United States Notes primarily in their issuer and legal obligation: FRNs are issued by the twelve Federal Reserve Banks under the authority of the Federal Reserve Act of 1913, serving as liabilities of the issuing Reserve Bank secured by collateral such as U.S. Treasury securities deposited with the U.S. Treasury, whereas United States Notes (also known as Legal Tender Notes) were issued directly by the U.S. Department of the Treasury pursuant to the Legal Tender Act of 1862 and represented direct, unrestricted obligations of the federal government itself. This distinction meant that outstanding USNs were initially classified as part of the public debt until a 1982 statutory change exempted them, while FRNs are not counted as sovereign debt but as assets backing the Federal Reserve's balance sheet. Both forms bear the inscription "legal tender for all debts, public and private," conferring equivalent status under 31 U.S.C. 5103, but USN issuance was capped by law at $346 million after 1945, limiting their circulation compared to the unlimited authorization for FRNs.
In contrast to redeemable certificates like Silver Certificates and Gold Certificates, which circulated until the mid-20th century, FRNs have no commodity backing and are irredeemable in specie following Executive Order 6102 in 1933 (ending gold redemption) and the Coinage Act of 1965 (phasing out silver). Silver Certificates, authorized under the Pittman Act of 1918 and last issued in 1954 for $1 bills, promised payment in silver bullion or coins upon demand until redemption ceased on June 24, 1968, after which they became non-redeemable fiat alongside FRNs. Gold Certificates, restricted to bank use after 1933 and fully withdrawn by 1934, explicitly stated redeemability in gold coin, a feature absent from FRNs, which declare only general redeemability in "lawful money" but in practice function as fiat currency without fixed asset linkage.
FRNs also diverge from earlier National Bank Notes and Federal Reserve Bank Notes in issuance mechanism and risk allocation: National Bank Notes, permitted under the National Banking Acts of 18631864, were obligations of individually chartered national banks backed by U.S. government bonds held as collateral, with over 10,000 varieties issued until their phase-out by the Banking Act of 1935 amid the Great Depression. Federal Reserve Bank Notes, a transitional emergency currency authorized in 1915 and notably issued during the 1933 banking crisis (e.g., Series of 1929 and 1934), were direct liabilities of the issuing Reserve Bank without the broader system's collateral pooling that underpins FRNs, making them rarer and confined to specific crises rather than routine circulation. Unlike these decentralized or bank-specific forms, FRNs centralize issuance through the Federal Reserve System, comprising 99% of circulating U.S. paper currency as of recent estimates, with legacy types like USNs comprising less than 1% of the $2.3 trillion in notes outstanding in 2023.
U.S. coins, minted by the Treasury under the Coinage Act of 1792 and subsequent laws, represent base money distinct from FRNs as promissory notes; coins are tangible specie or clad compositions (e.g., cupronickel since 1965) with intrinsic metallic value or seigniorage, while FRNs derive value solely from government decree and public acceptance, not physical composition. This separation underscores FRNs' role as supplementary circulating medium rather than primary money, though both integrate into the monetary base via Federal Reserve distribution to depository institutions. No new non-FRN paper currency has entered production since the suspension of USNs in 1971, solidifying FRNs' dominance in the fiat system.

Judicial Challenges and Design Disputes

The constitutionality of Federal Reserve Notes (FRNs) as legal tender has been upheld by the U.S. Supreme Court through precedents establishing Congress's authority to issue fiat currency under Article I, Section 8 of the Constitution, which grants power to coin money and regulate its value. In the Legal Tender Cases, including Knox v. Lee (1871) and Juilliard v. Greenman (1884), the Court affirmed that paper notes without specie backing could be made legal tender for public and private debts, rejecting arguments that such issuance violated the Coinage Clause or constituted prohibited bills of credit. These rulings, while predating the Federal Reserve Act of 1913, directly underpin the validity of FRNs, which Congress authorized as obligations of the United States redeemable in lawful money.
Direct judicial challenges to FRNs post-1913 have uniformly failed in federal courts, with rulings confirming their status as lawful money equivalent to coined dollars. For instance, in Milam v. United States (1975), the Ninth Circuit held that FRNs constitute "lawful money" under 12 U.S.C. 411, dismissing claims that they lack constitutional backing due to the absence of gold or silver redemption. Similarly, tax protester and sovereign citizen arguments asserting FRNs are not "real" dollarsoften citing their promissory phrasing or lack of metallic standardhave been rejected as frivolous, with courts imposing sanctions for such filings. No Supreme Court decision has invalidated FRNs' legal tender quality, though scholarly analyses continue to debate the Federal Reserve's broader structural alignment with separation of powers, without altering currency validity.
One notable lower-court outlier occurred in 1969 during First National Bank of Montgomery v. Daly in Minnesota, where Justice of the Peace Martin Mahoney ruled that FRNs were unconstitutional for lacking intrinsic value and violating due process in mortgage foreclosures; however, this decision lacked precedential force, was not upheld on appeal, and reflected fringe interpretations dismissed by federal judiciary. Broader challenges to the Federal Reserve's emergency powers or note issuance under Section 16 of the Federal Reserve Act have also been rebuffed, with courts deferring to congressional intent absent clear statutory violation.
Design-related disputes have been minimal and largely resolved in favor of Treasury and Federal Reserve authority over note features for security and uniformity. Courts have upheld the mandatory inscription "Federal Reserve Note" and evolving anti-counterfeiting elements, such as watermarks and security threads introduced in series redesigns (e.g., 1996), as within the Bureau of Engraving and Printing's discretion under 31 U.S.C. 5114. Challenges to symbolic elements, like the motto "In God We Trust" added to all currency by 1957 under Public Law 84-140, were rejected in Aronow v. United States (1970), where the Ninth Circuit deemed it ceremonial rather than religious establishment. Older-design FRNs remain legal tender indefinitely, preempting claims of obsolescence, with no successful suits forcing redesigns beyond administrative updates for fraud prevention.

Criticisms and Alternative Perspectives

Defenses: Stability and Modernization Benefits

The issuance of Federal Reserve Notes enables the central bank to supply an elastic currency that adjusts to seasonal and cyclical demands, thereby mitigating liquidity shortages that previously fueled banking panics under the inelastic national banking system. Prior to 1913, the U.S. experienced frequent financial disruptions, including the Panic of 1907, due to rigid currency supplies tied to government bonds; the Federal Reserve Act addressed this by authorizing notes backed by commercial paper and gold reserves, allowing expansion during stress periods. This mechanism has empirically reduced the frequency and severity of systemic banking failures, with data showing no nationwide panics after 1933 compared to over a dozen in the preceding decades. Proponents, including Federal Reserve officials, credit this flexibility with stabilizing the financial system by serving as a lender of last resort, as demonstrated during the 2008 crisis when note circulation surged to provide emergency liquidity without relying on fixed asset constraints.
Modernization of Federal Reserve Notes has enhanced their durability, security, and usability, adapting to technological threats and economic evolution. Series redesigns, such as the 1996 introduction of color-shifting ink and watermarks, and the 2013 $100 note with a blue 3D security ribbon, have incorporated advanced anti-counterfeiting measures developed through collaboration between the Bureau of Engraving and Printing and the Secret Service. These updates have contributed to a sustained decline in counterfeit detections, with Secret Service reports indicating fewer than 10 incidents per million genuine notes annually in recent years, down from higher rates in earlier decades. Furthermore, the shift to smaller note sizes in 1929 and subsequent polymer-like substrates in experimental trials have improved portability and reduced production costs, supporting efficient circulation in a high-velocity economy while maintaining public trust in the currency's integrity. Such innovations underscore the notes' role in facilitating seamless transactions amid rising digital alternatives, without compromising their status as legal tender.

Critiques: Inflationary Effects and Centralization Risks

Critics contend that Federal Reserve Notes, as fiat currency, facilitate chronic inflation by enabling the central bank to expand the money supply without constraints tied to commodity reserves. Since the Federal Reserve's creation in 1913, the U.S. dollar has lost over 96% of its purchasing power, with $1 in 1913 equivalent to approximately $30 in 2023 dollars based on Consumer Price Index data. This long-term debasement contrasts with pre-1913 periods under gold-standard constraints, where annual inflation averaged near zero, and is linked by economists such as those in the Austrian tradition to the Cantillon effect, whereby new money benefits early recipients (e.g., banks and government) at the expense of later holders through rising prices.
Empirical correlations reinforce these concerns, as surges in broad money measures like M2 have preceded inflationary episodes. For instance, M2 expanded by over 25% year-over-year in 2020-2021 amid quantitative easing and fiscal stimulus, contributing to consumer price inflation peaking at 9.1% in June 2022the highest since 1981before moderating with tighter policy. Historical patterns, including the 1970s stagflation following the 1971 gold standard suspension, show similar dynamics, where money supply growth outpaced economic output, eroding real wages and savings while benefiting debtors, including the federal government.
The centralization of monetary authority in the Federal Reserve introduces risks of amplified policy failures and reduced accountability, as decisions by an unelected board affect the entire economy without direct market discipline. With assets exceeding $7 trillion as of 2023up from under $1 trillion pre-2008this structure fosters moral hazard, as seen in repeated bailouts that socialize losses while privatizing gains for financial institutions. Critics, including former Congressman Ron Paul, argue that such concentration distorts price signals, encourages excessive leverage, and invites political pressure to monetize deficits, potentially undermining independence amid rising U.S. debt levels surpassing 120% of GDP. This setup contrasts with decentralized alternatives like free banking, where competing issuers historically maintained stability through redeemability requirements, avoiding the systemic risks of monopoly control.

Sound Money Advocacy and Historical Alternatives

Sound money advocates promote currencies redeemable in or backed by precious metals like gold and silver, positing that such systems inherently constrain money supply growth to the limited supply of those commodities, thereby preserving purchasing power and curbing inflationary pressures from discretionary issuance. This perspective, rooted in arguments from Austrian School economists including Ludwig von Mises and Friedrich Hayek, contends that fiat currencies such as the Federal Reserve Note enable central banks to expand the money supply without corresponding economic output, leading to debasement and wealth transfer from savers to debtors via inflation. Empirical analyses support this by showing higher correlations between money growth and inflation under fiat regimes compared to commodity standards, where price levels exhibit greater long-term stability due to fixed convertibility rules.
Critics of the Federal Reserve Note highlight its role in the dollar's erosion since 1913, when the (/page/Federal Reserve) was established; by 2021, the dollar had lost about 96% of its purchasing power, requiring roughly $26 in 2020 to match $1 from 1913, a decline advocates attribute to fiat flexibility allowing persistent monetary expansion untied to specie reserves. Proponents argue this contrasts with sound money's protective function against civil liberties erosion, as unlimited fiat printing historically facilitates deficit-financed government spending without immediate taxation or borrowing constraints. Organizations like the Sound Money Defense League advance this view through state-level initiatives, such as exempting gold and silver transactions from capital gains taxes to encourage their use as media of exchange and challenge federal fiat dominance.
Historically, the United States employed alternatives to pure fiat through the gold standard, effective de facto from the 1830s and formally from 1900 until its domestic suspension in 1933 via Executive Order 6102, which prohibited private gold ownership and halted redeemability. Under this regime, gold certificatesauthorized in 1863 and first issued in 1865served as paper claims redeemable for specific gold coin amounts held in Treasury vaults, ensuring direct convertibility and limiting issuance to gold stocks. Similarly, silver certificates, mandated by the Bland-Allison Act of 1878 and circulated until 1964, were exchangeable for silver dollars or bullion, embodying bimetallic principles that tied currency value to metal reserves rather than government decree. These instruments coexisted with United States Notes (greenbacks), which, though fiat during the Civil War from 1862, were later constrained and phased out by 1971, offering a partial historical counterpoint to the expansive, unbacked Federal Reserve Note series introduced in 1914. Advocates cite these eras' relative price stabilityevidenced by lower long-run inflation gains under gold standardsas empirical validation for reviving commodity-linked systems over centralized fiat control.
