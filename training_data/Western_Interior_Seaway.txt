Western Interior Seaway
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Western Interior Seaway was a vast epicontinental sea that divided North America into eastern (Appalachia) and western (Laramidia) landmasses during the Late Cretaceous epoch, from approximately 98.5 to 66 million years ago. This shallow inland body of water, at its peak spanning about 3,000 miles (4,800 km) from the Gulf of Mexico to the Arctic Ocean and reaching widths of up to 1,000 miles (1,600 km), covered much of the central United States, including regions from Texas to Montana, and extended into parts of Canada and Mexico. Its presence resulted from a combination of global high sea levels, tectonic subsidence in the continental interior due to the subduction of the Farallon Plate beneath North America, and flexural downwarping of the foreland basin adjacent to the rising Sevier Orogeny mountains in the west.
The seaway's fluctuating shorelines, driven by eustatic sea-level changes and episodic transgressions and regressions, created diverse environments ranging from deep marine basins to coastal plains and deltas, depositing thick sequences of shales, sandstones, limestones, and coal beds preserved today in formations such as the Mancos Shale, Niobrara Formation, and Pierre Shale. These sediments, often exceeding 3,000 meters in thickness in areas like Wyoming, record a dynamic paleogeography with eastward-migrating shorelines and extensive mire belts that supported peat accumulation, contributing to significant coal resources in the modern Rocky Mountain region. Paleontologically, the seaway was a hotspot for marine biodiversity, hosting diverse ecosystems of mosasaurs, plesiosaurs, ammonites, rudist bivalves, and foraminifera, while its margins influenced terrestrial dinosaur faunas on the isolated landmasses.
By the Maastrichtian stage (around 7266 million years ago), tectonic uplift from the Laramide Orogeny and falling sea levels led to the seaway's gradual regression and eventual disappearance, with its final retreat coinciding with the end-Cretaceous mass extinction event approximately 66 million years ago. The geological record of the Western Interior Seaway provides critical insights into Cretaceous climate, ocean circulation, and the tectonic evolution of the North American interior, with its strata serving as key references for biostratigraphy and paleoenvironmental reconstruction across the continent.

Geological Formation

Tectonic and Sedimentary Origins

The formation of the Western Interior Seaway was primarily driven by tectonic subsidence in the Western Interior Basin, resulting from flexural loading associated with the Sevier orogeny and early phases of the Laramide orogeny. This subsidence began around 100 Ma during the mid-Cretaceous, as thrust loading from the Sevier fold-and-thrust belt in the proto-Rocky Mountains depressed the continental crust, creating a foreland basin that extended across central North America. The flexural response of the lithosphere to this loading produced a broad, asymmetric depression, with maximum subsidence rates of up to 50-100 m per million years in areas like southwestern Wyoming and northern Utah, superimposed on broader epeirogenic effects. Initial Laramide-style deformation, emerging around 80 Ma, further contributed to this subsidence through basement-involved deformation, enhancing the basin's accommodation space.
Initial marine transgression into the subsiding basin occurred from two directions, with waters advancing from the Arctic Ocean to the north and the Gulf of Mexico to the south, eventually merging by the mid-Cretaceous around 95 Ma to establish the continuous seaway. This northward incursion formed an early precursor phase, often referred to as the early Greenhorn Sea, while southerly waters flooded across the Comanche Shelf, facilitated by the basin's flexural downwarp. The convergence of these water masses created a narrow, elongated seaway that widened over time, dividing the North American continent.
Sedimentary infilling of the seaway commenced with coarse clastic deposits derived from the eroding Sevier highlands to the west, including sandstones and shales transported by fluvial and deltaic systems into the proximal foredeep. As water depths increased in the central basin, these transitioned to finer-grained mudstones and pelagic carbonates, such as chalks and limestones, which accumulated in quieter, deeper waters away from terrigenous input. This shift reflected the basin's evolving depositional environments, with clastics dominating the western margins and carbonates characterizing the axial and eastern regions.
These regional processes were amplified by a global mid-Cretaceous sea-level rise of approximately 100-200 m, attributed to enhanced seafloor spreading and thermal expansion linked to superplume activity in the mantle. The Cretaceous superplume event increased oceanic ridge volumes and hydrothermal alteration, reducing seawater density and elevating eustatic levels, which promoted widespread inundation of continental margins including the Western Interior Basin.

Paleogeographic Extent and Dimensions

The Western Interior Seaway achieved its maximum paleogeographic extent during the mid-Cretaceous period, extending from the Yukon Territory and Alaska in the north to the Gulf of Mexico in the south, thereby bisecting the North American continent and isolating the eastern landmass of Appalachia from the western landmass of Laramidia. These maximum dimensions occurred during the Turonian stage (~93.9-89.9 Ma), varying with sea-level fluctuations. This division created two distinct continental blocks, with the seaway occupying the region of the present-day Great Plains and Midwest.
At its peak, the seaway spanned approximately 4,800 km (3,000 miles) in length, reached a maximum width of about 1,000 km, and attained depths up to 300 m in its central basins, though typical depths were much shallower across much of its area. These dimensions reflect the seaway's role as a vast epicontinental feature, influenced by tectonic subsidence along the western margin that accommodated its broad footprint.
The seaway exhibited variable oceanic connectivity, with narrow straits along its western margin providing limited exchange with the Pacific Ocean and broader eastern linkages facilitating stronger connections to the proto-Atlantic Ocean through the Gulf of Mexico. Bathymetric profiles revealed extensive shallow epicontinental shelves, typically under 100 m deep along the margins, gradually deepening toward axial troughs in the central portions where depths exceeded 200 m. This configuration supported diverse depositional environments, from nearshore shallows to deeper basinal settings.

Temporal Development

Early Cretaceous Phases

The Early Cretaceous phases of the Western Interior Seaway began with the initial marine incursions that established a narrow, episodic connection across the North American interior, setting the stage for later expansions. The first major incursion from the north, known as the Mowry Sea, formed around 100-95 Ma during the late Albian to early Cenomanian stages, marking a transgressive episode that flooded the central basin with shallow marine waters. This event deposited the Mowry Shale, a widespread unit of organic-rich marine mudrocks, primarily in the northern and central regions from Montana to Wyoming and extending southward.
The Mowry Sea remained limited in extent during this period, with a tenuous southern connection to the Gulf of Mexico that allowed only intermittent water exchange, preventing full integration of northern and southern arms. Water depths were generally shallow, under 200 meters, reflecting the epeiric nature of the seaway and supporting low-energy depositional environments. Sedimentation was dominated by clastic materials, including shales, sandstones, and minor conglomerates, along with volcanic ash layers derived from western arc volcanism, which contributed to the fine-grained, siliceous character of the Mowry Shale.
By approximately 95 Ma, these early incursions had begun to divide North America into two emerging landmasses: proto-Laramidia in the west and Appalachia in the east, separated by the nascent seaway. Coastal plain environments prevailed along the margins, characterized by fluvial and deltaic systems that supplied terrigenous sediments to the basin, with non-marine deposits like the Dakota Formation interfingering with marine shales. This partitioning influenced sediment routing and terrestrial ecosystems, as rising sea levels encroached on low-relief continental interiors.
A significant Cenomanian sea-level rise around 95 Ma, driven by eustatic changes associated with the Greenhorn cyclothem, facilitated the initial merger of the northern Mowry Sea and the southern Gulf arm, enhancing connectivity and broadening the seaway's influence across the continent. This event marked a transition from isolated embayments to a more unified shallow seaway, with continued clastic input from eroding highlands. The merger set the foundation for subsequent expansions, though the system remained dynamic and responsive to global sea-level fluctuations.

Late Cretaceous Transgressions and Regressions

The Late Cretaceous phase of the Western Interior Seaway, spanning approximately 90 to 70 million years ago, was characterized by multiple transgressive-regressive cycles that alternated between marine flooding and retreat, driven primarily by eustatic sea-level fluctuations superimposed on regional tectonics. Recent chronostratigraphic studies (as of 2025) have refined the timing of these cycles using radiometric dating, confirming the Campanian durations while highlighting local variations in transgression extents. These oscillations resulted in dynamic shoreline migrations, with peak transgressions expanding the seaway to its maximum paleogeographic extent of roughly 4,800 km in length and 1,620 km in width. Over this 20-million-year interval, the interplay of global sea-level changes and localized subsidence in the foreland basin facilitated repeated inundations, depositing distinctive marine strata across the North American interior.
The Niobrara Cycle, from about 90 to 80 Ma during the late Turonian to Santonian stages, represented a period of peak transgression, particularly in the Coniacian-Santonian interval (88.5-83.5 Ma), when high sea levels led to widespread deposition of chalky limestones and shales. This phase is exemplified by the Niobrara Formation, including its Fort Hays Limestone Member, which formed in offshore environments at water depths of 15-50 m initially, transitioning to deeper settings of 150-300 m or more during the highstand. Coccolith-rich carbonate muds dominated these deposits, reflecting high productivity and stable marine conditions across the central seaway, with platform carbonates accumulating in the east. The cycle concluded with a regressive phase marked by variable carbonate content in the Smoky Hill Member, signaling gradual shallowing.
Subsequent transgressions during the Campanian stage (79-72 Ma) included the Claggett and Bearpaw events, which renewed marine flooding and extended inland up to approximately 1,000 km from the coasts. The Claggett Transgression initiated at the onset of Campanian II, depositing the Claggett Shale as marine shales in relatively deep water, intervening between near-shore Eagle Formation and continental Judith River Formation deposits. Following a brief regression, the Bearpaw Transgression caused a major sea-level rise, leading to extensive shale deposition in the Bearpaw Formation (up to ~300 m thick in northern regions) and Pierre Shale, with the transgression influencing southern areas including fluvial deposits up to 920 m thick in the Kaiparowits Plateau, Utah. These events reflect highstand conditions with deepened waters, contrasting earlier cycles by emphasizing rapid incursions into continental interiors.
Intervening and culminating regression phases, especially from 72 Ma onward into the Maastrichtian, were strongly influenced by the emerging Laramide Orogeny, which initiated uplift in the western cordillera and promoted eastward shoreline retreat. This tectonic activity drove deltaic progradation, as seen in formations like the Pictured Cliffs Sandstone, Lance Formation, and Pine Ridge Sandstone, where fluvial and coastal plain sediments advanced into shallowing marine areas. In these restricted, lowstand settings, evaporite formation occurred locally in hypersaline lagoons and sabkhas along the regressive margins, contributing to gypsiferous and halite-bearing strata in peripheral basins. Overall, these regressions reduced seaway connectivity, shifting deposition from open marine to marginal environments and setting the stage for further basin evolution.

Terminal Decline

The Maastrichtian stage (ca. 7266 Ma) marked the final phase of the Western Interior Seaway, characterized by the last significant marine incursions represented by the Bearpaw and Lewis Seas before an irreversible retreat. The Bearpaw Sea, centered in what is now Montana, Alberta, and Saskatchewan, formed during an early Maastrichtian transgression, depositing marine shales of the Bearpaw Formation with thicknesses up to 102 m in regions like the western Cypress Hills. Similarly, the Lewis Sea occupied parts of Wyoming and Colorado, evidenced by the Lewis Shale, which reached thicknesses of 762 m in the Hanna Basin and reflected shallow marine conditions along the seaway's western margin. These seas briefly re-established connectivity from the Arctic to the Gulf of Mexico, but their extent was limited compared to earlier Cretaceous highs, with shorelines shifting eastward and southeastward by the late Maastrichtian.
The accelerated retreat of these seas, culminating in the seaway's complete disappearance by 66 Ma, resulted from intensified Laramide orogeny and a global eustatic sea-level fall. Laramide-style thick-skinned thrusting and uplifts along the western margin, particularly in Wyoming and Montana, reduced basin accommodation and partitioned the foreland, driving the Bearpaw regression (R9) and confining marine influence to coastal zones. Concurrently, a pronounced drop in global sea level around 66 Ma further promoted shoreline progradation, transitioning the seaway from open marine to restricted, evaporative conditions with O seawater values of ~2. This rapid regression fully terrestrialized the interior by the Cretaceous-Paleogene boundary, eliminating the epicontinental marine pathway.
The terminal decline coincided with the Cretaceous-Paleogene mass extinction at 66 Ma, influenced by the Chicxulub asteroid impact and Deccan Traps volcanism, which contributed to regional anoxic conditions and biotic collapse within the seaway. The Chicxulub impact, evidenced by an iridium anomaly at the boundary, disrupted marine circulation and triggered short-term oceanic perturbations, while Deccan eruptions released sulfur and CO, exacerbating hypoxia in epicontinental settings like the Western Interior. These events amplified environmental stress during the seaway's final regression, leading to widespread anoxia and the extinction of marine biota.
Following the seaway's closure, the Western Interior emerged as a mature foreland basin dominated by fluvial and coastal plain deposits, signaling a shift to terrestrial sedimentation. Formations such as the Hell Creek (up to 247 m thick in Montana) and Lance (305774 m in Wyoming and Montana) record nonmarine fluvial systems with scattered coal mires, confined to subsiding intermontane basins and retreating coastal areas. This terrestrialization established the foundational architecture of the modern Great Plains, with sediment thicknesses exceeding 3,000 m in depocenters like the Hanna Basin.

Paleoenvironment

Climate and Oceanographic Conditions

The Western Interior Seaway maintained tropical warm waters strongly influenced by connections to low-latitude oceanic realms, such as the Gulf of Mexico, which facilitated the influx of heat and moisture. A 2024 study utilizing clumped isotope thermometry on ammonite shells reconstructed surface water temperatures ranging from 22C to 44C during peak greenhouse intervals, reflecting the intense solar insolation and limited latitudinal temperature gradients characteristic of the mid-Cretaceous. Deeper waters, in contrast, exhibited thermal stratification, with temperatures of 25C to 38C in the Campanian and 9C to 28C in the Maastrichtian, driven by density differences between warm, low-salinity surface layers and denser bottom waters sourced from polar regions.
Oceanographic conditions were marked by pronounced stratification, with surface currents predominantly flowing northward from the Tethys-linked Gulf of Mexico toward the Arctic Ocean, transporting nutrient-rich, warm waters that enhanced productivity along the seaway's axis. This circulation pattern, combined with the seaway's epicontinental shallowness and restricted deep-water exchange, led to persistent anoxic zones in bottom waters, exacerbated by high organic matter flux from surface blooms that depleted oxygen below the thermocline. Such stratification promoted the preservation of organic-rich sediments and limited benthic diversity in deeper basins.
Episodes of widespread anoxia punctuated the seaway's history, most notably during the Cenomanian-Turonian boundary event (Oceanic Anoxic Event 2, or OAE2), when expanded oxygen minimum zones extended across much of the water column due to intensified global warming, nutrient runoff, and sluggish ventilation. This regional anoxia mirrored broader oceanic anoxic events (OAEs), driven by volcanic outgassing and carbon cycle perturbations that amplified hypoxia in semi-enclosed basins like the seaway.
The overlying paleoclimate was humid subtropical, featuring seasonal monsoons fueled by the seaway's evaporative surface and surrounding orogenic highlands, which intensified rainfall and fluvial input during the mid-Cretaceous greenhouse world. Elevated atmospheric CO levels and poleward heat transport under these conditions suppressed ice formation and sustained high humidity, fostering lush coastal vegetation and episodic freshwater incursions that further stratified the water column.

Sedimentology and Depositional Basins

The sedimentology of the Western Interior Seaway is characterized by a predominance of fine-grained clastic deposits, such as shales and mudstones, in the deeper central basins, reflecting low-energy, pelagic sedimentation in a subsiding foreland setting. These mud-dominated successions, including units like the Mowry Shale and Pierre Shale, accumulated as pelagic detrital muds and organic-rich layers, often exceeding hundreds of meters in thickness in axial regions. In contrast, the marginal shelves hosted more carbonate-rich sediments, such as chalks and limestones, formed through biogenic precipitation and minor clastic input in shallower, higher-energy environments; examples include the Greenhorn Limestone and Niobrara Formation, where calcareous muds and chalk beds represent platform-to-basin transitions.
Depositional environments shifted cyclically with sea-level fluctuations, featuring deltaic systems during regressive phases when terrigenous influx increased, as seen in prodeltaic mudstones of the Ferron Sandstone Member and Dunvegan Formation, characterized by hyperpycnal flows from small, sediment-laden rivers draining adjacent orogens. Transgressive intervals promoted hemipelagic deposition across the broader seaway, with fine-grained suspensions settling in low-oxygen waters, leading to widespread black shales that signal episodic anoxia and enhanced organic preservation. These black shales, such as those in the Mancos and Belle Fourche members, formed under restricted circulation, with diffuse stratification and minimal bioturbation indicating fluid mud dynamics on prodeltaic shelves.
The seaway's basins evolved as an asymmetric foreland system driven by flexural subsidence from Sevier orogenic loading, creating eastward-deepening troughs that accommodated thick sediment piles, reaching up to ~3 km in central depocenters like the Hanna Basin during the Maastrichtian. This subsidence pattern resulted in wedge-shaped clastic accumulations, with maximum thicknesses of over 2,000 m in western Utah-Wyoming regions during the Coniacian-Santonian, tapering eastward as the basin shallowed toward the stable craton. Sediment provenance was dominated by erosion of the rising Sevier highlands to the west, supplying coarse clastics via orogen-transverse fluvial systems, while volcanic arcs contributed ash-rich layers, including bentonites from Cenomanian-Turonian eruptions, which interbedded with mudstones and enhanced basin fill.

Biota

Marine Fauna and Invertebrates

The Western Interior Seaway hosted a rich assemblage of marine reptiles during the Late Cretaceous, dominated by plesiosaurs and mosasaurs that occupied distinct ecological niches based on water depth and prey availability. Plesiosaurs, such as the short-necked polycotylids including Polycotylus, were adapted for agile swimming in deeper offshore waters, preying on fish and smaller marine reptiles with their robust flippers and conical teeth. In contrast, mosasaurs like Tylosaurus, reaching lengths of up to 15 meters, dominated shallower coastal and nearshore environments as apex predators, using their powerful tails for propulsion and serrated teeth to ambush fish, seabirds, and even other reptiles. This niche partitioning minimized direct competition, with plesiosaurs favoring pelagic zones and mosasaurs exploiting benthic and epipelagic habitats, as evidenced by isotopic and morphological analyses of fossils from seaway deposits.
Fish communities in the seaway were diverse, encompassing both ray-finned actinopterygians and cartilaginous chondrichthyans that formed the base of the food web. Large predatory teleosts like Xiphactinus, growing to approximately 5 meters, were voracious piscivores, often preserved with gut contents revealing swallowed prey such as smaller fish and even young mosasaurs. Sharks, including the durophagous Ptychodus species that attained lengths of up to 10 meters, specialized in crushing shelled invertebrates like ammonites and bivalves with their pavement-like teeth, inhabiting mid-shelf depths where such prey was abundant. These assemblages, spanning the Cenomanian to Maastrichtian, reflect a dynamic ecosystem influenced by periodic anoxic events that temporarily reduced oxygen levels and altered distributions.
Invertebrates were integral to the seaway's biota, with cephalopods and bivalves serving as key index fossils for biostratigraphy and paleoecology. Ammonites, particularly the straight-shelled genus Baculites, were widespread and evolutionarily rapid, allowing precise correlation of strata across the basin; species like Baculites eliasi appear near the Campanian-Maastrichtian boundary. Bivalves such as inoceramids formed dense shell beds in dysaerobic bottom waters, acting as biofacies indicators and primary producers in the benthic food chain, with genera like Inoceramus reaching sizes over 1 meter in diameter. In shallow, tropical margins, rudist bivalves constructed reef-like buildups, replacing traditional coral-dominated structures and supporting diverse epifaunal communities in epeiric settings.
Avian and pterosaur taxa adapted to the seaway's margins contributed to aerial and diving predation. Flightless diving birds like Hesperornis, a hesperornithiform reaching 1.5 meters, used foot-propelled swimming to hunt fish in nearshore lagoons, with fossils indicating a fully marine lifestyle. Pterosaurs such as Pteranodon, with wingspans up to 7 meters, soared over open waters, diving for squid and fish along coastal cliffs, their remains often associated with chalk deposits from the Niobrara Formation. These groups highlight the seaway's role as a cradle for avian evolution in marine environments.

Flora and Terrestrial Ecosystems

The coastal regions flanking the Western Interior Seaway supported diverse vegetation adapted to deltaic swamps and lowlands, characterized by abundant ferns, cycads, and emerging early angiosperms during the mid- to Late Cretaceous. Ferns, including species like Cladophlebis and Lonchopteris, dominated understory habitats in these wetland environments, while cycads and bennettitales formed scattered stands in more open, periodically flooded areas. Early angiosperms, such as magnoliids and platanoids, began to appear in the Albian stage, gradually increasing in diversity and abundance by the Cenomanian-Turonian, often as small trees or shrubs in swamp margins.
In contrast, the highlands of Laramidia, the western landmass bordering the seaway, were covered by extensive conifer-dominated forests, including taxa such as Araucariaceae and Taxodiaceae (e.g., redwood-like forms), which thrived in upland settings with better drainage and provided a canopy over mixed angiosperm undergrowth. Plant megafossils and wood remains from formations like the Dakota and Mesaverde groups reveal these conifers as key components of the terrestrial landscape, contributing to soil stabilization and biomass accumulation in tectonically active regions.
Pollen and fossil records from seaway-adjacent sediments, such as those in the Woodbine and Eagle formations, indicate humid, warm-temperate conditions that fostered lush coastal vegetation, with mean annual temperatures around 2025C and high precipitation supporting mangrove-like fringes of ferns and early angiosperms along brackish estuaries. Palynological assemblages dominated by Aquilapollenites and trilete fern spores reflect a mesic climate without frost, promoting dense swamp forests that extended into paralic depositional environments. These records show a shift toward greater angiosperm pollen diversity by the Campanian, signaling enhanced floral productivity under stable, moisture-rich regimes.
Terrestrial ecosystems along the seaway's margins were heavily influenced by dinosaur-dominated faunas, particularly on Laramidia's western shores, where herds of hadrosaurs like Edmontosaurus grazed in fern- and angiosperm-rich lowlands, and apex predators such as tyrannosaurids (Tyrannosaurus and relatives) patrolled conifer woodlands. These ecosystems featured high herbivore biomass, with ornithischian dinosaurs shaping vegetation structure through browsing and trampling, while limited east-west migration across the seaway's barrier promoted endemism, isolating Laramidian assemblages from eastern Appalachia. The seaway's persistent presence restricted terrestrial dispersal, fostering distinct biogeographic provinces.00823-6)
Interactions between terrestrial ecosystems and the seaway included nutrient runoff from deltaic swamps and highlands, where angiosperm expansion increased organic matter export, enhancing marine primary productivity through terrigenous inputs of phosphorus and nitrogen. This fluvial delivery, documented in black shale deposits like the Niobrara Formation, supported algal blooms and benthic communities, linking land vegetation dynamics to seaway eutrophication. Conversely, the seaway acted as a formidable barrier to terrestrial organism dispersal, limiting gene flow and contributing to evolutionary divergence in coastal biotas.

Geological Legacy

Associated Stratigraphic Formations

The stratigraphic record of the Western Interior Seaway is preserved in a series of Upper Cretaceous rock units that document its episodic transgressions and regressions across the North American continent. These formations, spanning the Cenomanian to Maastrichtian stages, consist primarily of marine shales, chalks, limestones, and sandstones, reflecting varying depositional environments from deep pelagic basins to nearshore settings.
During the early transgression in the Cenomanian-Turonian (approximately 98.588.5 Ma), the Greenhorn Formation represents a key unit of marine shales transitioning to chalky limestones, deposited in the central seaway across Colorado, Kansas, and Wyoming. This formation, up to 71 m thick in Colorado, includes members such as the Bridge Creek Limestone and Hartland Shale, marking the initial flooding of the basin with pelagic sediments. The Niobrara Formation, from the Turonian-Campanian (93.572 Ma), signifies the seaway's peak extent, comprising organic-rich calcareous shales and chalks in the eastern and central regions, with thicknesses reaching 384 m in Wyoming; its Fort Hays Limestone and Smoky Hill Shale members highlight highstand conditions during maximum flooding. In the late phase, the Campanian-Maastrichtian Pierre Shale (83.565.5 Ma) forms widespread mudstones up to 900 m thick in Wyoming and Montana, recording fluctuating regressions with detrital inputs from eroding highlands.
Regional variations in these deposits reflect proximity to sediment sources and basin margins. In the western clastic wedges, the Mancos Shale (Turonian-Campanian, 93.572 Ma) dominates in Utah and New Mexico, consisting of thick marine shales and sandstones up to 920 m, interbedded with coals from coastal plains during regressions. Eastern equivalents include the Selma Group (Campanian-Maastrichtian, 83.565.5 Ma) in central Texas, featuring calcareous shales and chalks up to 76 m thick, indicative of more stable carbonate-dominated shelves.
Biostratigraphic correlation across these basins relies on inoceramid bivalves and ammonites, which define precise zonations. In the Greenhorn Formation, the inoceramid Mytiloides mytiloides and ammonite Neocardioceras juddi zones facilitate interregional ties, while the Niobrara's Scaphites hippocrepis II zone aligns chalk deposits. The Pierre Shale is zoned by Baculites species, such as B. grandis and B. clinolobatus, alongside inoceramid assemblages, enabling precise dating and correlation from Wyoming to Alberta.
The seaway's terminal decline is marked by the Maastrichtian Fox Hills Sandstone (7265.5 Ma), a regressive unit of nearshore sandstones and shales up to 84 m thick in Wyoming and South Dakota, signaling the rapid retreat of marine waters and transition to terrestrial deposition.

Economic Resources and Modern Research

The Niobrara Formation, a key stratigraphic unit associated with the Western Interior Seaway, serves as a major hydrocarbon play, particularly for oil and natural gas in the Denver-Julesburg Basin of Colorado, Wyoming, and Nebraska. Advances in hydraulic fracturing and horizontal drilling since the early 2010s have significantly boosted production, with the formation yielding substantial reserves through these techniques. A 2025 U.S. Geological Survey assessment estimates undiscovered technically recoverable resources in the Southwestern Wyoming Province portion of the Niobrara Formation at approximately 703 million barrels of oil and 5.8 trillion cubic feet of natural gas, underscoring its ongoing economic importance.
Coal beds interbedded in formations such as the Mancos Shale, deposited during regressions on coastal plains, contribute to significant coal resources in the modern Rocky Mountain region.
Beyond hydrocarbons, bentonite clays derived from volcanic ash layers within Seaway deposits represent another valuable resource, widely used in drilling fluids, sealants, and industrial applications. These clays formed through the alteration of mid- to Late Cretaceous ash falls into the marine basin, with prominent beds identified in formations like the Pierre Shale across the Western Interior. Geochemical analyses confirm their provenance from Cordilleran arc volcanism, providing markers for stratigraphic correlation while supporting commercial mining operations in states such as Wyoming and South Dakota.
Recent research has leveraged advanced techniques to deepen understanding of the Seaway's legacy. Clumped isotope paleothermometry applied to fossil carbonates in 2024 has refined estimates of Late Cretaceous seawater temperatures, revealing gradients from 20C to 40C across the basin and highlighting hydrological influences on paleoclimate. Additionally, genome-resolved metagenomic analyses of Late Cretaceous dinosaur fossils, such as those from the Centrosaurus-bearing Dinosaur Park Formation, have uncovered ancient microbial communities, offering insights into post-mortem biodiversity and taphonomic processes within Seaway-adjacent environments.
The Seaway's deposits also illuminate its connections to global events, including mid-Cretaceous Oceanic Anoxic Events (OAEs), where restricted circulation and phosphorus recycling enhanced organic carbon burial in black shales, as evidenced by integrated geochemical records from OAE-2. Toward the end-Cretaceous, progressive regression and environmental shifts in the basin contributed to pre-impact ecosystem stress, with terrestrial sections showing heightened instability in vegetation and sedimentation patterns leading into the K-Pg extinction.
