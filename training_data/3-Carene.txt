3-Carene
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 3-Carene, chemically known as -carene or 3,7,7-trimethylbicyclohept-3-ene, is a bicyclic monoterpene hydrocarbon with the molecular formula CH and a molecular weight of 136.23 g/mol. It appears as a colorless, clear liquid that is insoluble in water but miscible with fats and oils, exhibiting a sweet, pungent odor reminiscent of turpentine and fir needles. This compound features a distinctive cyclopropane ring fused to a cyclohexene structure, contributing to its stability up to approximately 250C, though it resinifies upon exposure to oxygen. Physically, it has a boiling point of 170 C, a melting point below 80 C, a specific gravity of 0.865, and low water solubility of 4.581 mg/L at 25C.
Naturally occurring as a phytoncide, 3-carene is a major constituent of pine tree essential oils (genus Pinus, family Pinaceae), where it ranks as the second most abundant monoterpene after -pinene and can comprise up to 42% of turpentine oil depending on the source. It is also present in the essential oils of other conifers like cedar and juniper (Juniperus communis), as well as in diverse plants and foods including citrus fruits, rosemary, basil, dill, ginger, nutmeg, parsley, pepper, strawberry, tea, tomato, and wine.
3-Carene serves as a key raw material in perfumes, cosmetics, flavors, and terpene resins due to its woody, earthy scent. Its global production for fragrance use is estimated at 10100 metric tons annually, with concentrations up to 0.014% in hydroalcoholic products. Biologically, it demonstrates anti-inflammatory, antifungal, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, including activity against bacteria like Pseudomonas fluorescens and Brochothrix thermosphacta by disrupting cell membranes and inducing oxidative stress. Additionally, it exhibits anxiolytic and sleep-enhancing effects by positively modulating GABA_A-benzodiazepine receptors, potentially aiding in stress reduction and treatment of sleep disorders. In atmospheric chemistry, its oxidation contributes to secondary organic aerosol formation, accounting for about 6% of global monoterpene emissions.

Nomenclature and structure

Names and identifiers

3-Carene is systematically named 3,7,7-trimethylbicyclohept-3-ene according to IUPAC nomenclature. Common names include -carene, car-3-ene, and (+)-3-carene for the naturally occurring enantiomer. The molecular formula is $ C_{10}H_{16} $.
The name "carene" derives from its initial isolation from the carene fraction of turpentine oils, a natural resin from pine trees.

Molecular structure

3-Carene is a bicyclic monoterpene characterized by a fused ring system consisting of a three-membered cyclopropane ring and a six-membered cyclohexene ring. This structure corresponds to the bicyclohept-3-ene core, where the cyclopropane ring is fused to the cyclohexene at bridgehead carbons 1 and 6.
Key structural features include a carbon-carbon double bond between positions C3 and C4 in the six-membered ring, a methyl group attached to C3, and geminal dimethyl groups at C7, the carbon at the apex of the cyclopropane ring. The standard skeletal formula depicts the bicyclic framework with the cyclopropane bridge spanning C1 and C6, and numbering proceeds from one bridgehead through the longest bridge (four carbons) to the other bridgehead, then along the one-carbon bridge, and finally the zero-carbon direct bond.
Regarding stereochemistry, 3-carene exists as enantiomers due to the chiral centers at C1 and C6; the naturally occurring form is the (+)-enantiomer with (1S,6R) configuration. Racemic mixtures, containing equal proportions of both enantiomers, are also known and can be prepared synthetically.
3-Carene represents an unsaturated derivative of the carane skeleton, which is the fully saturated bicycloheptane system bearing the same methyl substituents.

Physical and chemical properties

Physical properties

3-Carene is a colorless to pale yellow liquid at room temperature. It possesses a characteristic odor described as sweet and turpentine-like, with prominent pine and citrus notes.
The density of 3-carene is 0.857 g/cm at 25 C. Its boiling point is 170 C at standard atmospheric pressure (760 mmHg). The melting point is below -80 C, indicating it remains liquid and non-crystalline under typical ambient conditions.
3-Carene exhibits low solubility in water, approximately 0.0005 g/L at 20 C, but is readily soluble in organic solvents such as ethanol, diethyl ether, and hydrocarbons. The refractive index is 1.474 at 20 C. For the naturally occurring (+)-enantiomer, the optical rotation is approximately 16 (neat).

Chemical properties

3-Carene exhibits good thermal stability under normal conditions, remaining stable up to approximately 250C, though it tends to resinify upon prolonged exposure to air and oxygen. It shows reactivity toward strong oxidizing agents, potentially reacting vigorously, and with reducing agents, releasing hydrogen gas exothermically. Additionally, 3-carene is susceptible to oxidation and undergoes acid-catalyzed isomerization.
As a flammable liquid, 3-carene has a flash point of 47C and is combustible, forming vapors that can ignite. It lacks ionizable hydrogens, rendering it non-acidic with no measurable pKa. Being a non-polar hydrocarbon, 3-carene is insoluble in water but miscible with organic solvents, consistent with its low dielectric constant typical of such compounds.

Occurrence and biosynthesis

Natural occurrence

3-Carene is a bicyclic monoterpene predominantly found in the essential oils of coniferous trees, including Pinus species such as Scots pine (P. sylvestris), cedar (Cedrus spp.), and cypress (Cupressus spp.). It serves as a major component of turpentine oil derived from these trees, with concentrations reaching up to 43.4% in P. sylvestris essential oil from certain regions. The compound was first isolated from turpentine oil in the early 20th century, highlighting its long-recognized presence in pine-derived resins.
In addition to conifers, 3-carene occurs in the flowers of Cannabis sativa, where it is present in higher concentrations in select strains such as Super Lemon Haze and AK-47. It is also present in lower concentrations in the essential oils of rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis), typically around 0.2-1.4%, and in the peels of certain citrus species.
Naturally, 3-carene exists predominantly as the (+)-enantiomer. As a volatile organic compound (VOC), it contributes to plant ecology by enhancing emissions in response to herbivore attack, aiding in defense against insects, and acting as a kairomone to attract predators or parasitoids of pests.

Biosynthesis

The biosynthesis of 3-carene, a bicyclic monoterpene, occurs primarily in the cytosol of conifer cells through the mevalonate (MVA) pathway, which generates the universal isoprenoid precursors isopentenyl pyrophosphate (IPP) and dimethylallyl pyrophosphate (DMAPP) from acetyl-CoA via mevalonic acid intermediates. These precursors condense head-to-tail, catalyzed by geranyl pyrophosphate synthase, to form the linear C10 substrate geranyl pyrophosphate (GPP). Although crosstalk with the plastidial methylerythritol phosphate (MEP) pathway can contribute IPP/DMAPP in some plants, the MVA pathway predominates for monoterpene production in gymnosperms like conifers, supporting resin duct-localized synthesis.
The committed step in 3-carene formation is catalyzed by the enzyme (+)-3-carene synthase, a monoterpene cyclase that converts GPP into the bicyclic (+)-3-carene structure with high specificity. This enzyme, belonging to the terpene synthase family, initiates the reaction through metal ion-dependent ionization of GPP, generating an allylic carbocation that undergoes electrophilic cyclization: the intermediate -terpinyl cation rearranges via a 1,3-hydride shift to form a pinyl cation, followed by cyclopropane ring closure between C1 and C7, and final deprotonation at C3 to yield the carane skeleton. The stereochemistry is tightly controlled, retaining configuration from (E,E)-GPP and producing the (+)-enantiomer (dextrorotatory) predominantly in conifers.
At the genetic level, (+)-3-carene synthase is encoded by species-specific terpene synthase genes exhibiting evolutionary plasticity, allowing functional divergence through point mutations that alter product profiles. For instance, in Pinus densiflora (Japanese red pine), the Pd3-cars gene encodes a synthase with 95% identity to orthologs in other pines, enabling high 3-carene output. Similar genes, such as PsTPS-3car in Picea sitchensis (Sitka spruce), form small multigene families (2-4 copies) that vary in copy number and expression, contributing to intraspecific terpene diversity.
Biosynthesis of 3-carene is regulated by developmental and environmental cues, particularly in conifers where it accumulates in oleoresin as a defense compound. Enzyme activity and gene expression peak during early needle development, declining with maturation, and are upregulated under biotic stresses like herbivory or pathogen attack via jasmonate signaling. In Pinus densiflora, infestation by pine wood nematodes induces Pd3-cars transcription and elevates 3-carene emissions, enhancing resistance. Methyl jasmonate treatment similarly boosts synthase activity in Picea abies (Norway spruce), linking terpene production to wound-induced defenses.

Synthesis and reactions

Laboratory synthesis

3-Carene is typically obtained through isolation from natural sources such as turpentine oil, where its content varies depending on the pine species, reaching up to 42% in some cases. Early laboratory efforts focused on purifying 3-carene from essential oils of conifers like Pinus sylvestris via fractional distillation and chromatography, achieving high purity but limited scalability due to variable natural abundance.
Acid-catalyzed isomerization of more abundant terpenes like -pinene can produce 3-carene as a minor product in mixtures with camphene and limonene, though selectivities are typically low (often <5%). The reaction proceeds via carbocation intermediates, allowing skeletal rearrangement to form the characteristic bicycloheptene skeleton.
Modern laboratory syntheses have explored total routes to access defined stereoisomers, independent of natural variability. One approach utilizes Diels-Alder cycloaddition of -stannyl enones with dienes like isoprene or cyclopentadiene, followed by tin-mediated cyclopropanation to construct the fused cyclopropane ring.
Enantioselective syntheses prioritize the biologically relevant (+)-3-carene. These routes typically afford 50-80% overall yields on multigram scales, making 3-carene viable as a chiral building block for complex targets like (+)-ingenol, where it serves as the starting material in a 14-step sequence with 7.2% overall yield.
A primary challenge in laboratory synthesis remains precise stereocontrol of the strained cyclopropane ring, particularly ensuring syn-fusion and gem-dimethyl substitution without epimerization, often requiring cryogenic conditions or chelating ligands to achieve high diastereoselectivity (>20:1 dr).

Chemical reactions

3-Carene, a bicyclic monoterpene with an exocyclic double bond and a fused cyclopropane ring, undergoes acid-catalyzed isomerization primarily to -carene (2-carene) via migration of the double bond into the six-membered ring. This transformation is facilitated by solid acid catalysts such as hydrogen-exchanged mordenite, achieving high selectivity (>90%) under mild conditions, or basic oxides like MgO and CaO, where the main product results from double bond migration within the cyclohexene moiety. Other positional isomers, such as 4-carene, can form under stronger acidic conditions, but 2-carene predominates due to thermodynamic stability.
Epoxidation of 3-carene targets the C3-C4 exocyclic double bond, yielding 3,4-epoxycarane (carene oxide) as the trans isomer with respect to the orientation of the epoxide and cyclopropane rings. This reaction proceeds stereoselectively with peracids like peracetic acid, often generated in situ from hydrogen peroxide and acetic acid, under weakly basic aqueous conditions to minimize side reactions. Chemo-enzymatic variants using lipases to form peroxy acids in situ enhance efficiency and enantioselectivity for the (+)-3-carene enantiomer.
Electrophilic addition reactions to the C3-C4 double bond of 3-carene follow Markovnikov's rule, with hydrogenation providing a key example by saturating the double bond to afford carane. Catalytic hydrogenation over Rh/AlO at ambient temperature and moderate hydrogen pressure (e.g., 20-50 bar) selectively yields cis-carane with high conversion (>95%), preserving the cyclopropane ring. Hydrohalogenation with HX (X = Cl, Br) adds the halogen to the less substituted carbon, though strained systems like 3-carene may lead to concomitant cyclopropane involvement; for instance, HCl addition under mild conditions primarily functionalizes the alkene without ring disruption.
The strained cyclopropane ring in 3-carene is susceptible to base- or Lewis acid-mediated ring-opening, generating acyclic terpenoid derivatives through cleavage of peripheral bonds. Lewis acids like perfluoroalkanoic acids promote nucleophilic attack, opening the ring to form -lactones or rearranged acids while retaining stereochemistry at key centers. Under basic conditions or with milder acids such as phosphoric acid in condensations (e.g., with formaldehyde), the ring opens after initial protonation, yielding linear products like 2-methyl-6-isopropenyl-2-hexenal derivatives.
3-Carene serves as a chiral starting material in total syntheses of pharmaceuticals, leveraging its bicyclic framework and stereocenters. A notable 14-step route from (+)-3-carene constructs (+)-ingenol, the core of ingenol esters used in anti-cancer and HIV latency-reversing agents, via key steps including ring expansion and selective functionalizations to achieve 1.2% overall yield. Similarly, derivatives from 3-carene provide chiral auxiliaries for asymmetric synthesis of efavirenz, a potent HIV-1 reverse transcriptase inhibitor, enabling high enantiopurity in key cyclopropanation steps.

Uses and biological activity

Industrial and commercial uses

3-Carene serves as a key component in the fragrance and flavor industries due to its distinctive citrusy, pine-like, and woody odor profile. It is employed as a raw material in perfumes, cosmetics, and soaps, where it imparts herbal, resinous, and terpenic notes, often comprising up to 1% in fragrance concentrates. In flavor applications, it enhances citrus, pine, and tropical profiles in products such as baked goods (at 10-50 ppm), beverages (15-50 ppm), and chewing gum (60-100 ppm), contributing to fresh, peppery, and juniper-like tastes. Additionally, it acts as a precursor for synthetic flavor compounds derived from turpentine.
In essential oils and aromatherapy, 3-carene is extracted from sources like pine, cedar, basil, and citrus oils, providing a sweet, diffusive scent that evokes forest-like qualities. It is incorporated into therapeutic oils and wellness products for its aromatic properties, often as part of blends mimicking natural pine or cedar essences. Its presence in juniper and rosemary oils further supports its use in aromatherapy formulations aimed at sensory enhancement.
3-Carene contributes to polymer and resin production as a renewable feedstock, particularly in bio-based materials. Through processes like catalytic condensation with formaldehyde, it forms terpenoid resins suitable for adhesives and coatings. It can also undergo oxidative transformations, such as hydroboration-oxidation and Baeyer-Villiger oxidation, to yield lactones that polymerize into polyesters with glass transition temperatures ranging from -15C to 50C and melting points up to 170C. Reductive ozonolysis produces 3-carene diol, which copolymerizes with compounds like dimethyl terephthalate to create semi-crystalline polyesters for industrial applications. These modifications leverage its reactivity to produce sustainable alternatives to petroleum-derived resins.
In agriculture, 3-carene shows potential as a precursor for biopesticides, exhibiting strong insecticidal activity against pests like the maize weevil (Sitophilus zeamais). As a fumigant, it achieves LC values of 0.610 ppm after 24 hours, with 100% mortality at 3 ppm, and reduces progeny production by up to 95% while minimizing grain damage by 95% at higher concentrations. Its repellent effects (60-68% at 3-4 ppm) and inhibition of immature stages further support its role in essential oil-based formulations for pest control.
Global production of 3-carene primarily derives from turpentine, with worldwide turpentine output estimated at approximately 280,000 metric tons annually. 3-Carene constitutes up to 42% of turpentine composition depending on the source, such as sulfate turpentine from the pulp industry, yielding potential annual volumes of tens of thousands of tons for isolation and derivatives. In the fragrance sector, its commercial use aligns with the broader terpene resin market, driven by demand for natural scent ingredients in perfumes and flavors.

Biological and pharmacological effects

3-Carene, a bicyclic monoterpene, interacts with biological systems primarily through its roles as a phytoncide and modulator of inflammatory pathways. In human-relevant models, it exhibits anxiolytic effects by enhancing sleep duration and reducing latency in pentobarbital-induced sleep tests, mediated by potentiation of GABA_A-benzodiazepine receptor responses, which may contribute to stress reduction. Additionally, 3-carene demonstrates anti-inflammatory activity via inhibition of cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), suppressing inflammatory infiltrates and COX-2 overexpression triggered by nociceptive stimuli in preclinical assays.
The compound shows potential in promoting bone healing by stimulating osteoblast differentiation. At low concentrations, 3-carene significantly increases alkaline phosphatase activity and expression in mouse MC3T3-E1 osteoblastic cells, an early indicator of bone formation and repair processes. These effects align with its broader anti-injury properties, including antinociceptive actions observed in essential oil formulations that reduce pain transmission.
Toxicity assessments indicate low acute risk from 3-carene. Oral administration yields an LD50 of approximately 4.8 g/kg in rats, classifying it as minimally toxic. However, high concentrations act as irritants to skin and eyes, causing moderate inflammation upon direct contact. As a volatile organic compound (VOC) emitted from wood and essential oils, 3-carene can accumulate in indoor environments, potentially impacting air quality in poorly ventilated spaces.
3-Carene possesses antimicrobial properties, particularly in essential oil contexts. It exhibits antifungal effects against Candida species, inhibiting growth in strains tested with terpene-rich oils like those from Juniperus. Antibacterial activity targets food-borne pathogens and spoilage bacteria, such as Listeria monocytogenes and Staphylococcus aureus, by disrupting cell membranes and metabolic processes in blends containing 3-carene.
Beyond direct pharmacology, 3-carene functions as a phytoncide in pine-derived emissions, contributing to stress alleviation by promoting relaxation and immune modulation in exposure studies. In cannabis varieties where it occurs alongside cannabinoids, 3-carene may enhance the entourage effect, amplifying pain relief through synergistic anti-inflammatory and analgesic interactions.
Current research on 3-carene remains preclinical, emphasizing in vitro and animal models for anti-injury mechanisms and osteoblast promotion, with no pharmaceutical drugs approved for clinical use as of 2025.
