Basic Concepts

Definition and Motivation

A series expansion represents a function f(z)f(z)f(z) in a domain as an infinite sum of simpler terms, typically of the form f(z)=n=0an(zc)nf(z) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n (z - c)^nf(z)=n=0an(zc)n, where the coefficients ana_nan are determined by the properties of fff at a point ccc, allowing the function to be reconstructed from these terms. This approach generalizes the idea of finite polynomial approximations to infinite sequences, providing a way to express complex or transcendental functions through algebraic operations.
At its foundation, a series expansion relies on the concept of infinite series summation, where the value of the series is defined as the limit of the partial sums sN=n=0Nan(zc)ns_N = \sum_{n=0}^{N} a_n (z - c)^nsN=n=0Nan(zc)n as NNN approaches infinity, assuming this limit exists. This summation process enables the function to be approximated by truncating the series at a finite number of terms, with the error decreasing as more terms are included, provided the series behaves appropriately in the region of interest.
The primary motivation for series expansions lies in their ability to approximate intricate functions using polynomials, which are easier to manipulate computationally and analytically. They facilitate term-by-term operations such as differentiation and integration, preserving the function's properties while simplifying calculations, and uncover both local behavior near the expansion point and global insights across the domain. For instance, the Taylor series serves as a prototypical example, expanding functions around a specific point to reveal their smoothness and analytic structure.
Historically, series expansions emerged as tools for solving differential equations and evaluating integrals involving transcendental functions, with Isaac Newton employing infinite series in the late 17th century to handle such problems in his work on calculus. Leonhard Euler later advanced their systematic use around 1730, building on foundations laid by Newton, Leibniz, and others to develop rigorous methods for representing functions and addressing convergence in physical and astronomical applications.

Convergence Criteria

In the context of power series expansions, the radius of convergence $ R $ determines the domain where the series $ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n (z - c)^n $ converges to an analytic function. This radius is given by the Cauchy-Hadamard formula:
R=1limsupnan1/n, R = \frac{1}{\limsup_{n \to \infty} |a_n|^{1/n}}, R=limsupnan1/n1,
with the convention that $ R = 0 $ if the lim sup is infinite and $ R = \infty $ if the lim sup is zero. The series converges absolutely within the open disk $ |z - c| < R $ in the complex plane, forming the disk of convergence, and diverges for $ |z - c| > R $. Inside this disk, the sum is holomorphic, allowing term-by-term differentiation and integration.
To determine the radius of convergence practically, several tests are employed. The root test examines $ L = \limsup_{n \to \infty} |a_n|^{1/n} $, where the series converges absolutely if $ L < 1 $ (corresponding to $ |z - c| < 1/L = R $) and diverges if $ L > 1 $. Similarly, the ratio test uses $ L = \lim_{n \to \infty} |a_{n+1}/a_n| $ (if the limit exists), yielding convergence for $ |z - c| < 1/L $ and divergence for $ |z - c| > 1/L $; the case $ L = 1 $ is inconclusive. These tests are particularly effective for power series involving factorials or exponentials, and the comparison test can be applied by bounding the series against a known convergent power series with a smaller radius.
Power series exhibit absolute convergence inside the disk of convergence, meaning $ \sum |a_n (z - c)^n| $ converges, which implies ordinary convergence and justifies rearrangements and operations. On the boundary $ |z - c| = R $, conditional convergence may occur at certain points, where the series converges but not absolutely, though such cases are less common in complex settings and require separate verification using tests like Abel's theorem for radial limits. The behavior at the boundary is highly variable: the series may converge at some points and diverge at others, often due to singularities of the represented function lying on or near the circle of convergence, which prevent extension beyond the disk. These singularities, such as poles or branch points, typically form a nonempty closed subset of the boundary, limiting the radius as the closest such point to the center.

Power Series Expansions

Taylor Series

The Taylor series provides a way to represent a function as an infinite sum of terms calculated from the function's derivatives at a single point, allowing approximation of the function near that point. This expansion is particularly useful for functions that are infinitely differentiable, enabling the construction of polynomials that increasingly match the original function as more terms are included. The series is named after Brook Taylor, who formalized the concept in 1715, though earlier work by James Gregory contributed to its development.
The general form of the Taylor series for a function $ f(z) $ expanded around a point $ c $ is given by

where $ f^{(n)}(c) $ denotes the $ n $-th derivative of $ f $ evaluated at $ c $, and the factorial $ n! $ normalizes the terms. When $ c = 0 $, the expansion simplifies to the Maclaurin series, a special case that centers the series at the origin and is commonly used for functions defined around zero. The coefficients in the series are determined directly from the higher-order derivatives of the function at the expansion point $ c $, with each coefficient $ \frac{f^{(n)}(c)}{n!} $ capturing the contribution of the $ n $-th order behavior.
To quantify the approximation error after truncating the series at the $ n $-th term, the Lagrange form of the remainder is employed:

The Taylor series equals the original function precisely when $ f $ is analytic within its radius of convergence, meaning the series converges to $ f(z) $ for all $ z $ in the largest open disk centered at $ c $ where $ f $ has no singularities. In this domain, the function is holomorphic, and the infinite series representation holds exactly, underscoring the deep connection between differentiability and analytic continuation in complex analysis.

Dirichlet Series

A Dirichlet series is a series of the form $ f(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{a_n}{n^s} $, where $ s $ is a complex variable and the coefficients $ a_n $ are complex numbers. This representation generalizes power series by replacing powers of a fixed base with reciprocals of integers raised to the power $ s $, making it particularly suited for encoding arithmetic properties of sequences in the complex plane.
Prominent examples include the Riemann zeta function $ \zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^s} $, which converges absolutely for $ \Re(s) > 1 $, and the Dirichlet eta function $ \eta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n^s} $, which converges (conditionally) for $ \Re(s) > 0 $. The eta function relates to the zeta function via $ \eta(s) = (1 - 2^{1-s}) \zeta(s) $, allowing it to serve as an analytic continuation tool for $ \zeta(s) $ into the strip $ 0 < \Re(s) \leq 1 $.
Convergence of a general Dirichlet series occurs in a half-plane $ \Re(s) > \sigma_c $, where $ \sigma_c $ is the abscissa of convergence, while absolute convergence holds for $ \Re(s) > \sigma_a $ with $ \sigma_a \leq \sigma_c + 1 $. Within the region of convergence, the series defines an analytic function, and partial summation techniques relate the abscissa to the growth of the partial sums $ A(x) = \sum_{n \leq x} a_n $.
The Riemann zeta function admits an analytic continuation to a meromorphic function on the entire complex plane, with a simple pole at $ s = 1 $, via the relation $ \zeta(s) = \frac{\eta(s)}{1 - 2^{1-s}} $ for $ \Re(s) > 0 $ and further extensions using integral representations. It satisfies the functional equation $ \zeta(1-s) = 2 (2\pi)^{-s} \Gamma(s) \cos\left(\frac{\pi s}{2}\right) \zeta(s) $, which equates values across the critical line $ \Re(s) = \frac{1}{2} $ and reveals trivial zeros at negative even integers. This equation, derived from properties of the Gamma function and Poisson summation, underscores the deep symmetry in the zeta function's analytic structure.

Applications

In Analysis and Computation

Series expansions play a crucial role in mathematical analysis by providing tools for approximating functions and solving equations with high precision. In numerical approximation, Pad approximants offer a significant improvement over traditional Taylor series truncations by representing functions as ratios of polynomials, which can capture singularities and reduce truncation errors more effectively for certain classes of functions. For instance, the Pad approximant of order to a function f(z) is defined as P_m(z)/Q_n(z), where deg(P_m) m and deg(Q_n) n, and it matches the Taylor series coefficients up to order m+n. This approach is particularly useful in asymptotic analysis where polynomial approximations diverge, as demonstrated in the original work by Pad, which extended earlier ideas from Frobenius.
In the solution of differential equations, power series methods enable the construction of exact or approximate solutions for ordinary differential equations (ODEs) around regular singular points. The Frobenius method, a cornerstone technique, assumes a solution of the form y(x) = x^r a_k x^k, where r is determined by the indicial equation, allowing for series solutions even when standard Taylor expansions fail due to singularities. This method is widely applied to linear ODEs with variable coefficients, such as Bessel's equation, and provides analytic insights into the behavior of solutions near critical points. Developed by Georg Frobenius in the late 19th century, it remains a fundamental tool in qualitative analysis of ODEs.
Term-by-term operations on series facilitate integration and optimization in analysis, where the integral of a power series a_n (x - c)^n converges to (a_n / (n+1)) (x - c)^{n+1} within the radius of convergence, preserving uniformity under suitable conditions. This property underpins numerical quadrature techniques and variational methods, enabling the optimization of functionals represented by series, such as in the calculus of variations. For example, integrating Fourier or Taylor series term-by-term simplifies the computation of antiderivatives for periodic or analytic functions, a result formalized in classical real analysis texts.
Computational tools have integrated series expansions into practical workflows, with software like Mathematica providing robust implementations for symbolic and numerical manipulation. The Series function in Mathematica computes Taylor, Laurent, or Puiseux series expansions up to specified orders, supporting operations like differentiation, integration, and composition, which are essential for automated analysis and visualization. This capability, documented in Wolfram's official resources, extends to high-precision arithmetic and has been pivotal in computational mathematics since the system's inception.

Both series converge everywhere, reflecting their status as entire functions with infinite radius of convergence. These expansions highlight the oscillatory nature through alternating signs and factorial denominators, which ensure fast convergence; partial sums provide excellent local approximations near $ x = 0 $, improving with more terms.
For the natural logarithm, the Mercator series gives the expansion about $ x = 0 $ for $ \ln(1 + x) $:
ln(1+x)=n=1(1)n+1xnn,x<1
\ln(1 + x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-1)^{n+1} \frac{x^n}{n}, \quad |x| < 1
ln(1+x)=n=1(1)n+1nxn,x<1
This series has a radius of convergence of 1, converging conditionally at the endpoint $ x = -1 $ but diverging at $ x = 1 $. Partial sums approximate $ \ln(1 + x) $ effectively within the interval of convergence, with error decreasing as more terms are included, though slower near the boundaries compared to the exponential or trigonometric cases. These examples demonstrate the utility of Taylor series in deriving approximations for elementary functions.

Special Functions

Special functions, such as those arising in solutions to differential equations and integral transforms, are frequently expressed through infinite series or products that encode their global analytic structure. These representations not only define the functions in regions of convergence but also facilitate the derivation of functional identities that link them to other mathematical objects, enabling applications in quantum mechanics, signal processing, and number theory. Unlike polynomial approximations for elementary functions, these expansions for special functions often involve generalized factorials or rising products, reflecting their intricate pole and zero structures.
The Bessel function of the first kind, $ J_\nu(z) $, admits a power series expansion centered at the origin:
J(z)=(z2)k=0(1)kk!(+k+1)(z2)2k.
J_\nu(z) = \left( \frac{z}{2} \right)^\nu \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^k}{k! \, \Gamma(\nu + k + 1)} \left( \frac{z}{2} \right)^{2k}.
J(z)=(2z)k=0k!(+k+1)(1)k(2z)2k.
This series converges for all finite complex $ z $, making $ J_\nu(z) $ an entire function when $ \nu $ is a non-negative integer and analytic elsewhere except for a branch point at $ z = 0 $ for non-integer $ \nu $. The expansion arises from solving Bessel's differential equation and underpins identities such as recurrence relations that connect $ J_\nu(z) $ to derivatives and neighboring orders, essential for orthogonal expansions in cylindrical coordinates.
The Gauss hypergeometric function $ {}_2F_1(a, b; c; z) $ is fundamentally defined by its hypergeometric series:
2F1(a,b;c;z)=n=0(a)n(b)n(c)nn!zn,
{}_2F_1(a, b; c; z) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(a)_n (b)_n}{(c)_n \, n!} z^n,
2F1(a,b;c;z)=n=0(c)nn!(a)n(b)nzn,
where the Pochhammer symbol $ (a)_n = \frac{\Gamma(a + n)}{\Gamma(a)} $ for positive integer $ n $, with $ (a)_0 = 1 $. The series converges absolutely for $ |z| < 1 $; on the unit circle $ |z| = 1 $, convergence holds absolutely if $ \Re(c - a - b) > 0 $, conditionally under $ -1 < \Re(c - a - b) \leq 0 $ and $ z \neq 1 $, and diverges otherwise. Analytic continuation extends the function beyond this disk, and it satisfies transformation identities, including Euler's relation $ {}_2F_1(a, b; c; z) = (1 - z)^{-a} {}_2F_1(a, c - b; c; z/(z - 1)) $, which maps the argument and reveals connections to other hypergeometric forms.
The reciprocal of the Gamma function $ \Gamma(z) $ possesses the Weierstrass canonical product representation:
1(z)=zezn=1(1+zn)ez/n,
\frac{1}{\Gamma(z)} = z e^{\gamma z} \prod_{n=1}^\infty \left(1 + \frac{z}{n}\right) e^{-z/n},
(z)1=zezn=1(1+nz)ez/n,
where $ \gamma \approx 0.5772156649 $ is the Euler-Mascheroni constant; this infinite product converges uniformly on compact subsets of the complex plane excluding the non-positive integers, where $ \Gamma(z) $ has simple poles. This form explicitly constructs $ 1/\Gamma(z) $ as an entire function with zeros at $ z = 0, -1, -2, \dots $, and it yields identities like the reflection formula $ \Gamma(z) \Gamma(1 - z) = \pi / \sin(\pi z) $, which relates values across the unit interval. The Riemann zeta function, defined by its Dirichlet series, relates to the Gamma function through a functional equation that interchanges arguments via Gamma and trigonometric factors, highlighting series extensions in analytic number theory.
