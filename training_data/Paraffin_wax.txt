Paraffin wax
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Paraffin wax is a soft, white or colorless solid derived from petroleum, consisting primarily of a mixture of straight-chain (normal) alkanes with the general formula CnH2n+2C_nH_{2n+2}CnH2n+2, where nnn typically ranges from 20 to 40 carbon atoms. It is characterized by its low melting point, typically between 47C and 65C, heat capacity, and insolubility in water, making it a versatile material in various industrial applications.
Produced as a byproduct of the petroleum refining process, paraffin wax is obtained through dewaxing operations on lubricating oil fractions, involving solvent extraction or chilling and filtering to separate the solid hydrocarbons from the liquid components. It can also be synthesized via the Fischer-Tropsch process from syngas, though petroleum-derived sources dominate commercial production. Chemically inert, paraffin wax exhibits a density of approximately 0.880.92 g/cm and remains stable up to temperatures around 250C, with a volumetric expansion of approximately 1020% upon melting.
The material's key applications include the manufacture of candles, where its clean-burning properties are valued; coatings for paper, cardboard, and food packaging to provide moisture resistance; and cosmetics, such as in lip balms and hand creams for its emollient effects. Additionally, it serves in electrical insulation, crayons, floor polishes, and emerging uses like phase-change materials for thermal energy storage in solar systems.

Composition and Properties

Chemical Composition

Paraffin wax is primarily composed of a mixture of straight-chain normal alkanes and branched isoalkanes, consisting mainly of hydrocarbons with carbon chain lengths ranging from C20 to C40 and following the general molecular formula CnH2n+2 for the alkane components. These hydrocarbons are saturated, non-polar molecules derived from petroleum fractions, with the straight-chain variants dominating the structure to impart crystallinity.
In contrast to paraffin wax, microcrystalline wax features a higher proportion of branched isoalkanes and naphthenic (cyclic) hydrocarbons, typically with chain lengths of C30 or greater, resulting in a more amorphous and flexible microstructure.
Paraffin wax is classified into refinement grades based on purity and residual oil content, which affects its hydrocarbon concentration. Fully refined paraffin wax contains over 99% hydrocarbons with less than 0.5% oil content, achieved through extensive purification to minimize non-hydrocarbon impurities. Semi-refined paraffin wax includes up to 5% oil, offering a balance of cost and performance for less demanding applications, while scale wax, an intermediate crude form, retains higher impurities and oil levels often exceeding 5%.
The hydrocarbon chain length distribution and compositional ratios in paraffin wax are typically analyzed using gas chromatography, which separates and quantifies individual alkane components for quality control and specification. Standard paraffin wax compositions generally feature 60-90% normal alkanes, with the balance comprising isoalkanes and minor branched structures, varying by source crude and processing.
Unrefined forms of paraffin wax, such as slack or scale wax, often contain impurities including aromatic hydrocarbons and naphthenes, which contribute to color, odor, and instability. These impurities are removed during refinement through methods like hydrotreatment or clay adsorption, yielding the high-purity hydrocarbon profile characteristic of fully refined grades.

Physical Properties

Paraffin wax appears as a soft, white to colorless solid at room temperature, exhibiting a waxy texture and becoming translucent when melted.
Its melting point typically ranges from 46C to 68C, with variations depending on the grade; low-melt varieties (4656C) are commonly used in cosmetics, while high-melt grades (6068C) suit applications like coatings. The melting range is influenced by the lengths of the hydrocarbon chains in its composition.
The density of solid paraffin wax is approximately 900 kg/m at room temperature, decreasing to 750800 kg/m in the molten state due to thermal expansion.
Paraffin wax is insoluble in water but soluble in non-polar solvents such as benzene, chloroform, ether, and hot oils.
It demonstrates low volatility with a boiling point exceeding 370C, is odorless and tasteless, and non-toxic in its pure, fully refined form. Paraffin wax exhibits high thermal stability up to approximately 200C and a volumetric coefficient of thermal expansion of about 0.0008 K^{-1}.
Electrically, it serves as an excellent insulator with a dielectric constant of 2.02.5 and resistivity ranging from 10 to 10 ohm-meters. Paraffin wax is utilized in neutron moderation due to its low absorption cross-section.

History and Development

Discovery and Early History

Paraffin wax was first isolated in 1830 by the German chemist Karl von Reichenbach during his investigations into the dry distillation of coal tar and other bituminous substances. Reichenbach named the substance "paraffin," derived from the Latin terms parum affinis, meaning "little affinity," to reflect its chemical inertness and resistance to reactions with other substances. This discovery emerged from his broader early 19th-century experiments on paraffin oils and solid hydrocarbons obtained from the destructive distillation of organic materials like coal tar, which yielded a range of waxy residues previously overlooked in chemical analysis.
Unlike earlier waxes such as spermaceti, derived from whale oil, or beeswax, which were costly to produce and emitted smoky flames when burned, paraffin wax demonstrated greater purity and cleaner combustion properties from the outset. These attributes stemmed from Reichenbach's observations of its non-reactive nature, setting it apart as a more stable alternative for potential practical uses.
In its initial phase, paraffin wax found limited applications primarily in laboratory settings for chemical experiments and as an early lubricant for machinery, prior to the mid-19th-century transition toward petroleum-derived sources. A pivotal development occurred in 1850 when Scottish chemist James Young patented a method for extracting and purifying paraffin wax from shale oil in Scotland, marking an early step toward scalable production from mineral sources. This innovation briefly referenced the potential for improved candlemaking over traditional tallow, offering a less odorous and more efficient burning material.

Commercial Production and Advancements

The commercial production of paraffin wax underwent a significant shift in the 1850s toward petroleum refining in both the United States and Scotland, enabled by the discovery of abundant crude oil resources. In Scotland, chemist James Young patented a process in 1850 for distilling paraffin wax from oil shale, establishing the world's first commercial oil refinery near Bathgate and facilitating industrial-scale output. In the United States, Edwin Drake's successful oil well in Titusville, Pennsylvania, in 1859 spurred the development of petroleum-based refining, with early refineries extracting wax as a byproduct of kerosene production. This transition built briefly on Karl von Reichenbach's foundational distillation techniques from the 1830s, allowing for more scalable extraction from natural petroleum seeps and shales. Price's Patent Candle Company in England capitalized on these advancements, achieving large-scale production by 1860 through imports of crude petroleum from Burma and innovative steam distillation methods, which dramatically lowered candle manufacturing costs and boosted market adoption.
The early 20th century marked a production boom in the United States, where paraffin wax emerged as a valuable byproduct of expanding kerosene refining operations amid rising demand for lighting and industrial applications. This growth was driven by the integration of wax separation into standard petroleum processing, transforming what was once a niche material into a cornerstone of the refining industry. Key technological advancements followed, including the adoption of hydrotreating processes in the 1920s, which employed hydrogen under pressure to remove impurities and yield higher-purity paraffin grades suitable for diverse uses beyond candles.
Post-World War II innovations further revolutionized production efficiency, particularly through automation in dewaxing processes that separated wax crystals from lubricating oils using solvent extraction and chilling filtration systems. These automated methods, implemented widely in the 1950s, reduced labor intensity and improved yield consistency compared to manual techniques. As of 2022, global paraffin wax production stood at around 2.15 million tons annually, dominated by China (over 1.2 million tons per year), followed by the United States and India as key contributors.
Recent developments emphasize sustainability, with post-2020 research advancing bio-based alternatives derived from renewable feedstocks like plant oils and Fischer-Tropsch synthesis from biomass, offering lower carbon footprints while mimicking traditional paraffin properties. Additionally, integration with plastic recycling has gained traction, as pyrolysis of waste polyolefinssuch as high-density polyethyleneproduces upcycled paraffin-like waxes in yields up to 93%, providing a circular economy pathway to repurpose non-biodegradable plastics. These innovations address environmental pressures while maintaining the material's versatility in packaging and coatings.

Manufacturing Processes

Raw Material Extraction

Paraffin wax is primarily sourced from petroleum crude oil. Although initially derived from coal tar in the 19th century, production has shifted predominantly to petroleum sources since the late 1800s due to the scalability of oil refining.
The extraction process commences with fractional distillation of crude oil in refineries, where the feedstock is heated under vacuum to separate components by boiling point. This isolates the middle distillate fractions, boiling between 200400C, which serve as precursors for lubricating oils and contain paraffin hydrocarbons. During subsequent lubricating oil production, these distillates undergo dewaxing to remove wax crystals that would impair cold-weather performance.
Dewaxing involves chilling the distillate to crystallize the waxes and diluting it with solvents such as methyl ethyl ketone to enhance separation, followed by filtration or centrifugation to isolate the solid phase. The resulting slack wax, a semi-solid byproduct with 1030% oil content, represents the crude paraffin precursor. This slack wax requires further purification to yield refined paraffin wax.
Yields of paraffin wax from crude oil typically range from 13% per barrel, varying by crude type; paraffinic crudes, rich in straight-chain hydrocarbons, produce higher quantities than naphthenic crudes, which have lower wax content. 
Alternative synthetic routes, such as the Fischer-Tropsch process converting natural gas or biomass into hydrocarbons, provide sources of paraffin-like waxes and constitute a growing share of the market, approximately 10-15% as of 2023.

Refining and Purification

The refining and purification of paraffin wax begins with slack wax, a byproduct obtained from the distillation of lubricating oil fractions in crude oil processing. This raw material, containing 530% oil and impurities, undergoes de-oiling to separate the wax crystals from residual oils. Solvent extraction is a common method, where the slack wax is mixed with solvents such as methyl ethyl ketone (MEK) or propane, which preferentially dissolve the oils. The mixture is then chilled to induce crystallization of the higher-melting paraffin hydrocarbons, followed by separation through filtration or centrifugation to recover the wax cake. Alternatively, the sweating process involves gradually heating slabs of slack wax above 50F (10C), allowing lower-melting oils and waxes to melt and drain out, thereby reducing oil content to levels suitable for further processing.
Following de-oiling, hydrotreating enhances the wax's purity and stability through catalytic hydrogenation. In this step, the semi-refined wax is reacted with hydrogen gas in the presence of catalysts like nickel-tungsten on alumina (Ni-W/AlO) at temperatures of 250350C and pressures around 3.5 MPa. This process saturates unsaturated compounds, removes aromatics, sulfur, nitrogen, and polar impurities, resulting in improved color, odor, and oxidation resistance.
For applications requiring higher purity, such as food-grade paraffin, additional bleaching and deodorizing treatments are applied. These involve adsorption with activated clay to remove color bodies and trace contaminants or oxidation with hydrogen peroxide to achieve deodorization and further impurity reduction. Fully refined waxes produced this way meet U.S. FDA regulations under 21 CFR 172.890 for indirect food contact, with minimal impurities to ensure safety; they typically have low oil content (below 0.5% as per industry standards).
The final purified fractions are often blended to tailor properties like melt point, which ranges from 4668C depending on the hydrocarbon chain lengths. Mixing different de-oiled fractions allows for custom specifications, while incorporating small amounts of microcrystalline wax improves flexibility and adhesion without significantly altering the melt profile.
Quality control throughout refining ensures consistency and compliance, with standard tests including ASTM D87 for melting point via cooling curve method and ASTM D938 for congealing point to verify thermal behavior. Since the 1950s, automation in solvent recovery and filtration has improved efficiency in these processes.

Applications

Industrial and Commercial Uses

Paraffin wax serves as a versatile material in various industrial applications due to its hydrophobic properties, low reactivity, and suitable melting point range of approximately 4668C, which facilitates processing in coatings and compounds.
In packaging, paraffin wax is widely applied as a moisture barrier coating on paperboard, particularly for liquid-containing containers such as milk cartons, where it prevents leakage and extends shelf life by sealing porous surfaces. Early formulations involved dipping cartons in molten paraffin wax to achieve full waterproofing, a practice that has evolved into more efficient extrusion or emulsion-based applications for modern food packaging. Additionally, paraffin wax emulsions are incorporated into corrugated boxes to enhance water resistance and structural integrity, reducing moisture absorption during shipping and storage of dry goods.
In the rubber and plastics sectors, paraffin wax functions as an extender in tire production, where it is blended into rubber compounds to improve flexibility and processing ease without interfering with vulcanization. It also acts as a release agent in molding operations, facilitating the easy detachment of molded parts from dies and minimizing surface defects. This inert hydrocarbon base ensures compatibility with synthetic rubbers and plastics, maintaining material integrity during high-temperature extrusion or injection processes.
For electrical applications, paraffin wax provides insulation in cable manufacturing, leveraging its high dielectric strength of 200300 kV/inch to prevent electrical conduction and protect against environmental degradation. It is also utilized in potting compounds for electronic components, where it encapsulates circuits to offer moisture resistance and mechanical stability while allowing for rework due to its low melting point.
Other industrial uses include the formulation of patterns for investment casting, where paraffin wax-based blends create precise, low-shrinkage molds that are easily removed by melting after shell formation. In textiles, it is employed as a sizing agent in warp sizing to enhance yarn smoothness, reduce breakage, and improve weaving efficiency by providing lubricity and antistatic effects. Paraffin wax further contributes to polish formulations, such as floor waxes, where it is emulsified with other ingredients to deliver gloss, durability, and ease of application on hard surfaces.
Industrial and commercial uses represent a significant portion of global paraffin wax consumption, driven by demand in packaging, rubber, and electrical sectors. Post-2020, there has been notable growth in its incorporation into composite materials, particularly phase change composites for thermal management, supported by advancements in nanoparticle-enhanced formulations that improve energy storage efficiency.

Consumer and Emerging Applications

Paraffin wax is widely used in candle production, where it accounts for approximately 42% of the global market share in applications as of 2024, valued for its ability to burn cleanly and evenly without excessive soot production. This property makes it a preferred base for both unscented and scented varieties, with the latter often incorporating 8-12% fragrance oils to enhance scent throw during combustion. Its low cost, derived from petroleum refining, further supports its dominance in consumer candle markets for home decor and aromatherapy. However, the use of paraffin wax and synthetic fragrances in candles has raised concerns regarding potential health effects, such as headaches, and contributions to indoor air pollution, as documented in scientific studies. For a detailed discussion, see the Safety, Health, and Environmental Impact section.
In cosmetics, paraffin wax serves as an emollient and occlusive agent, helping to lock in moisture and provide a smooth texture in products like lip balms and lotions. It is a key component in formulations such as petroleum jelly-based balms, where it contributes to the protective barrier on skin, typically comprising a significant portion of the hydrocarbon blend. Paraffin wax treatments are also common in pedicures, where warm wax is applied to feet to soften skin, relieve dryness, and improve circulation during spa sessions.
As a food additive, paraffin wax functions as a glazing or coating agent, designated E905 in the European Union, to preserve freshness and appearance on items like cheese rinds and fruits such as apples and citrus. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approves its use as a direct food additive, including as a release agent and surface-finishing agent in confections, to ensure safety. This application leverages its inert, non-toxic nature when food-grade, preventing moisture loss without altering flavor.
Other consumer products incorporating paraffin wax include crayons, where it forms the primary waxy binder mixed with pigments to create smooth, vibrant drawing tools suitable for children. It is also applied for waterproofing leather boots and shoes by melting and rubbing it into the material to form a repellent barrier against water and stains. In medical contexts, paraffin-impregnated gauze dressings are used for wound care, providing a non-adherent layer that soothes minor burns, abrasions, and ulcers while allowing exudate drainage.
Emerging applications of paraffin wax highlight its versatility in innovative technologies, particularly as a phase-change material (PCM) for thermal energy storage. In solar heating systems, it absorbs and releases heat at controlled temperatures (typically 45-55C), enabling efficient storage for residential and industrial use to balance energy fluctuations. NASA has explored paraffin-based PCMs for spacecraft applications to maintain payload temperatures during missions by storing thermal energy in wax with melting points between -10C and 10C.
In cycling, paraffin wax has gained traction post-2020 as a chain lubricant, applied via hot immersion to create a low-friction coating that reduces drivetrain wear. Studies show paraffin-treated chains exhibit a wear rate about 40% of that seen with oil-based lubricants, extending chain life under dry conditions while minimizing dirt accumulation. This method, popularized by enthusiasts and brands, improves efficiency by lowering mechanical drag compared to traditional wet lubes.

Safety, Health, and Environmental Impact

Occupational and Health Safety

Occupational exposure to paraffin wax primarily occurs through inhalation of fumes generated during heating or melting processes and through direct skin contact with the solid or molten material. When paraffin wax is heated above approximately 200C, it can release fumes containing small amounts of acrolein, a highly irritating compound formed from the thermal decomposition of hydrocarbons. Skin contact with molten wax can cause thermal burns, while prolonged exposure to the solid form may lead to mild irritation or contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals.
Regulatory agencies have established exposure limits to mitigate risks from paraffin wax fumes. The National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) recommends a recommended exposure limit (REL) of 2 mg/m as an 8-hour time-weighted average (TWA) for fumes. The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) has no specific permissible exposure limit (PEL) for paraffin wax fumes, though general standards for nuisance dust apply, limiting total dust to 15 mg/m and respirable dust to 5 mg/m over an 8-hour TWA.
Health effects from exposure vary by route and duration. Acute inhalation of fumes can cause irritation to the eyes, nose, throat, and respiratory tract, potentially leading to discomfort, nausea, or coughing; eye contact may result in redness and tearing, while skin exposure to hot wax causes burns. Chronic exposure to fumes has been classified by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) as Group 3 (not classifiable as to its carcinogenicity to humans) for highly refined paraffin waxes and related mineral oil mists, indicating inadequate evidence of carcinogenicity but potential risks from prolonged respiratory irritation. The solid wax itself is generally non-toxic and non-carcinogenic when handled properly.
In consumer settings, burning paraffin wax in candles can expose individuals to additional health risks through combustion emissions. These include volatile organic compounds (VOCs) such as toluene and benzene, which are potential carcinogens, as well as particulate matter contributing to indoor air pollution. Synthetic fragrances, commonly added to candles and often derived from petroleum, release further VOCs that may cause headaches, vertigo, respiratory irritation, mucosal issues, and transient decreases in lung and cognitive function, particularly in sensitive populations or poorly ventilated spaces.
Safe handling practices are essential to minimize hazards. Adequate ventilation or local exhaust systems should be used when heating wax to control fume concentrations, and personal protective equipment (PPE) including heat-insulating gloves, safety goggles, and NIOSH-approved respirators with dust/mist/fume filters is recommended for molten operations. Paraffin wax presents a fire hazard due to its combustible nature, with a flash point of approximately 199C and autoignition temperature around 245C; avoid open flames and use dry chemical, foam, or carbon dioxide for firefighting, as water may spread molten wax.

Environmental Considerations

Paraffin wax production, derived primarily from petroleum refining, contributes significantly to greenhouse gas emissions due to its reliance on fossil fuels. The process involves energy-intensive steps such as distillation and solvent deoiling, with estimates indicating a carbon footprint of approximately 3.75 kg CO equivalent per kg of paraffin wax produced globally. Refining accounts for a substantial portion of these emissions, often exceeding 80% in solvent-based processes, while water consumption in these operations can reach notable levels for cooling and purification, exacerbating resource strain in water-scarce regions.
Once released into the environment, paraffin wax exhibits high persistence as it is non-biodegradable under typical conditions, leading to long-term accumulation in marine and terrestrial ecosystems. Studies have detected paraffin wax particles in North Sea sediments and water columns, where they form aggregates that trap microplastics and contribute to broader pollution. Degraded paraffin products can fragment into micro-wax particles, mimicking microplastics and persisting in sediments for extended periods, potentially disrupting benthic habitats.
In addition to production and disposal impacts, the use of paraffin wax in consumer products like candles contributes to indoor environmental pollution through the release of VOCs, particulate matter, and other emissions during combustion, which can degrade household air quality and pose risks to indoor ecosystems.
Disposal of paraffin wax poses additional challenges, with incineration releasing volatile organic compounds (VOCs) such as benzene and toluene, which contribute to air pollution and photochemical smog formation. Recycling rates for wax-containing products vary by region and type, typically around 20-30% in major markets such as the UK and USA, hindered by difficulties in separating wax from substrates like paper or glass in items such as coated packaging or spent candles. Landfill disposal leverages its inert nature to prevent leaching, but this perpetuates non-degradable waste accumulation.
Regulatory frameworks address these impacts through restrictions on impurities in paraffin wax. Under the EU REACH regulation, paraffin waxes must comply with purity specifications limiting polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) to below 0.005% in certain applications, due to their carcinogenic potential, with registration required for volumes over 100 tons annually. These measures aim to mitigate environmental release of toxic contaminants during production and use.
Sustainability initiatives are advancing alternatives and recovery methods to reduce paraffin wax's ecological footprint. Bio-based paraffin substitutes derived from plant oils, such as soy, offer a renewable option with carbon footprint reductions of up to 50% compared to petroleum-derived wax, driven by post-2020 innovations in hydrogenation processes. Circular economy efforts include industry pilots for candle wax recovery, such as those launched since 2022, which reclaim and reprocess used wax to achieve higher recycling yields and minimize virgin material use.
