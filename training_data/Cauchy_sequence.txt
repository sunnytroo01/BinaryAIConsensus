 A Cauchy sequence is a sequence {xn}\{x_n\}{xn} in a metric space (such as the real numbers with the standard metric) for which the terms become arbitrarily close to one another as the sequence progresses; formally, for every >0\epsilon > 0>0, there exists a positive integer NNN such that xmxn<|x_m - x_n| < \epsilonxmxn< whenever m,n>Nm, n > Nm,n>N.
This concept, introduced by Augustin-Louis Cauchy in the early 19th century, addresses limitations in defining convergence without presupposing the existence of a limit point, particularly in incomplete spaces like the rational numbers, where sequences may "bunch up" but fail to converge to a rational limit. In the real numbers, a sequence {xn}\{x_n\}{xn} converges if and only if it is a Cauchy sequence. This property, known as the Cauchy convergence criterion, is equivalent to the completeness axiom for R\mathbb{R}R. This equivalence underscores the foundational role of Cauchy sequences in real analysis, enabling the rigorous construction of the real numbers from the rationals by adjoining limits of all Cauchy sequences of rationals, thus filling "gaps" in the rationals.
Key properties include that every convergent sequence is Cauchy (via the triangle inequality) and every Cauchy sequence is bounded, though the converse holds only in complete spaces. Cauchy sequences generalize to normed vector spaces and are essential in functional analysis for defining Banach spaces, where completeness ensures solvability of equations like fixed-point problems. Examples include decimal expansions of real numbers, which form Cauchy sequences converging to the represented value, while the harmonic series partial sums do not qualify as Cauchy due to persistent differences between terms.

Complete metric spaces

A metric space (X,d)(X, d)(X,d) is said to be complete if every Cauchy sequence in XXX converges to a point in XXX. This property ensures that the space contains all its limit points with respect to the metric, providing a robust framework for limits and continuity in analysis. Completeness is a fundamental concept that distinguishes certain metric spaces from others, allowing for the consistent application of sequential convergence without "gaps" in the space.
The notion of completeness in metric spaces was formalized by Maurice Frchet in his 1906 doctoral dissertation, where he introduced abstract metric spaces and explored their properties, including convergence of sequences. A key example of a complete metric space is the real line R\mathbb{R}R equipped with the standard metric d(x,y)=xyd(x, y) = |x - y|d(x,y)=xy. The completeness of R\mathbb{R}R follows from its least upper bound property: every nonempty subset of R\mathbb{R}R that is bounded above has a least upper bound in R\mathbb{R}R. To see this equivalence, consider a Cauchy sequence {xn}\{x_n\}{xn} in R\mathbb{R}R; it is bounded, so the set S={xn:nN}S = \{x_n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}S={xn:nN} has a supremum supSR\sup S \in \mathbb{R}supSR, and one can show that xnsupSx_n \to \sup SxnsupS. Alternatively, completeness can be proved using the nested interval theorem: for a Cauchy sequence, construct nested closed intervals whose lengths tend to zero, and their intersection is a single point serving as the limit./01%3A_Tools_for_Analysis/1.05%3A_The_Completeness_Axiom_for_the_Real_Numbers)
Closed subsets of complete metric spaces are themselves complete; for instance, the interval with the subspace metric from R\mathbb{R}R is complete, as any Cauchy sequence in converges to a point within it. Similarly, Euclidean space Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn with the Euclidean metric d(x,y)=i=1n(xiyi)2d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - y_i)^2}d(x,y)=i=1n(xiyi)2 is complete, since it is a finite product of complete spaces R\mathbb{R}R, and completeness preserves under such products. Another important example is the space CCC of continuous functions on a compact interval equipped with the supremum norm f=supxf(x)\|f\|_\infty = \sup_{x \in } |f(x)|f=supxf(x); this forms a complete metric space, known as a Banach space, where uniform limits of continuous functions remain continuous.
Complete metric spaces exhibit significant topological properties, such as being Baire spaces: the intersection of countably many dense open sets is dense, a result known as the Baire category theorem. This theorem underscores the "large" nature of complete metric spaces, preventing them from being meager (a countable union of nowhere dense sets). The importance of completeness lies in its role in enabling the construction of limits essential for real and functional analysis; without it, many theorems on convergence, fixed points, and operator theory would fail, as seen in the development of Hilbert and Banach spaces.

Incomplete metric spaces

A metric space is said to be incomplete if it is not complete, meaning there exists at least one Cauchy sequence in the space that does not converge to any point within the space.
A classic example of an incomplete metric space is the set of rational numbers Q\mathbb{Q}Q equipped with the standard metric d(x,y)=xyd(x, y) = |x - y|d(x,y)=xy. The space of all polynomials on , denoted PPP, equipped with the supremum norm p=supxp(x)\|p\|_\infty = \sup_{x \in } |p(x)|p=supxp(x), provides yet another example of incompleteness. These examples highlight "gaps" in incomplete metric spaces, where Cauchy sequences fail to converge internally, underscoring the need for completions to achieve a structure where all such sequences have limits.

Generalizations

In uniform spaces

In a uniform space (X,U)(X, \mathcal{U})(X,U), where U\mathcal{U}U is a uniformity consisting of entourages (symmetric subsets of XXX \times XXX satisfying certain axioms), a sequence {xn}nN\{x_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}{xn}nN in XXX is Cauchy if for every entourage UUU \in \mathcal{U}UU, there exists NNN \in \mathbb{N}NN such that (xm,xn)U(x_m, x_n) \in U(xm,xn)U whenever m,n>Nm, n > Nm,n>N. When the uniform space is metrizable, meaning U\mathcal{U}U is induced by a metric ddd, the Cauchy condition reduces precisely to the standard metric definition: for every >0\epsilon > 0>0, there exists NNN such that d(xm,xn)<d(x_m, x_n) < \epsilond(xm,xn)< for all m,n>Nm, n > Nm,n>N. A uniform space is complete if every Cauchy net (a generalization of sequences indexed by directed sets) converges in XXX. Unlike metric spaces, where sequences suffice due to first countability, nets are necessary in general uniform spaces to ensure completeness captures convergence properly. The framework of uniform spaces, introduced by Andr Weil in 1937 to axiomatize notions like uniform continuity and compactness without metrics, also links to totally bounded sets: a uniform space is compact if and only if it is complete and totally bounded (every entourage admits a finite cover).
This generalization proves advantageous for non-metrizable cases, such as the indiscrete (trivial) uniformity on a set XXX with more than one point, where the sole entourage is XXX \times XXX; here, every sequence is Cauchy, yet the space is not Hausdorff and completeness holds trivially via convergence of all nets to any point.

For locally convex topological vector spaces, a key property is sequential completeness: every Cauchy sequence converges in the space. This holds if and only if the space is sequentially complete, distinguishing it from mere topological completeness, which requires convergence of all Cauchy nets or filters.

In contrast, many important topological vector spaces are complete. The Schwartz space S(R)\mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R})S(R) of rapidly decreasing smooth functions, defined by the countable family of seminorms p,(f)=supxRxDf(x)p_{\alpha,\beta}(f) = \sup_{x \in \mathbb{R}} |x^\alpha D^\beta f(x)|p,(f)=supxRxDf(x), is a complete Frchet space. However, spaces of distributions, such as the tempered distributions S(R)\mathcal{S}'(\mathbb{R})S(R) or general distributions D(R)\mathcal{D}'(\mathbb{R})D(R), arise as duals or completions of these test function spaces to handle non-convergent Cauchy elements in applications like partial differential equations.
Barrelled topological vector spaces, where every closed convex balanced absorbing set (barrel) is a neighborhood of the origin, play a crucial role in extending properties of Cauchy nets. In such spaces, the uniform boundedness principle holds: a pointwise bounded family of continuous linear functionals is equicontinuous, which ensures that Cauchy nets in the space or its dual behave uniformly, preventing unbounded growth in tails and facilitating convergence results beyond sequences.

In categories

In category theory, the Cauchy completion of a small category C\mathcal{C}C is constructed as the full subcategory of the presheaf category consisting of those presheaves that are retracts of representable functors, equivalently, the closure of C\mathcal{C}C under absolute colimits; this yields a dense embedding CC^\mathcal{C} \hookrightarrow \hat{\mathcal{C}}CC^ where C^\hat{\mathcal{C}}C^ is Cauchy complete, meaning every idempotent morphism splits. In enriched settings, such as categories enriched over a monoidal category V\mathcal{V}V (e.g., metric-enriched categories over Met\mathbf{Met}Met), the Cauchy completion functor similarly embeds objects into a completion via colimits of Cauchy filters or absolute weighted colimits, preserving the enrichment.
Examples include the Cauchy completion of posets, which for an Artinian poset (a poset where every descending chain stabilizes) is itself already complete, embedding densely into a larger lattice; similarly, the simplex category \Delta of finite ordinals and order-preserving maps, underlying simplicial sets, is Cauchy complete as its idempotents split naturally. Lawvere metric spaces generalize classical metrics as categories enriched over (,,+)(, \geq, +)(,,+), where hom-objects serve as distances; their Cauchy completion recovers the usual metric completion by symmetrizing and quotienting Cauchy sequences in this enriched sense.
Freyd's abstract completion extends this to categories with zero morphisms, constructing an exact completion via a reflective subcategory where kernels and cokernels are added universally, applicable to preadditive categories seeking abelian-like structure. In modern homotopy type theory, Cauchy sequences formalize synthetic analysis by defining real numbers as quotients of Cauchy sequences of rationals, enabling univalent completions that align with higher-dimensional geometry without classical axioms like choice. A key theorem states that every small category admits a Cauchy completion unique up to equivalence, as the universal idempotent-complete category receiving a dense functor from the original.
