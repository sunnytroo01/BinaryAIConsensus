Thaana
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Thaana is the official script of the Dhivehi language, spoken by approximately 540,000 people primarily in the Maldives and Minicoy Island in Lakshadweep, India, and is characterized as a vocalized abjad or consonant alphabet written from right to left. It consists of 24 basic consonant letters, with vowels indicated by diacritics (known as fili) placed above or below the consonants, and includes a vowel-killer symbol (sukun) to indicate the absence of a vowel or to geminate consonants. The letter alifu functions as a null consonant or to represent vowels independently, while long vowels are typically formed by doubling the short vowel diacritics. Unlike many South Asian scripts, Thaana does not derive from the Brahmi family; instead, its first nine letters are based on Arabic numerals (19), and the remaining consonants draw from earlier Maldivian forms, reflecting a unique blend of local and Islamic influences.
Developed in the late 17th to 18th century by an unknown inventor, Thaana first appeared in Maldivian government documents around 17031705, gradually replacing the older left-to-right Dives Akuru script, which had been used since at least the 12th century for Buddhist and early Islamic texts. Its creation is believed to have been tailored to the phonological structure of contemporary Dhivehi, facilitating bilingual writing alongside Arabic for religious and magical purposes, such as incantations (fandita), and its arbitrary letter order may have been designed for secrecy. By the 18th century, Thaana had become the dominant script, incorporating "dotted" variants of letters like ghainu and zaa to accommodate Perso-Arabic loanwords, and it withstood a brief attempt at Latinization in 1976 before being reinstated in 1978, contributing to the Maldives' high literacy rate of 98.6% as of 2022. Modern usage employs Western Arabic numerals (left-to-right) within the script, and Thaana remains essential for official, literary, and digital communication in Dhivehi, underscoring its role in preserving Maldivian cultural identity.

History

Origins and Early Development

The Thaana script emerged in the Maldives during the late 17th century as a replacement for the earlier Dhives Akuru script, which had been used for writing the Dhivehi language since at least the 12th century. This development occurred in the context of local Maldivian innovations following the Islamization of the islands in 1153 CE, when Arabic script influences began to shape writing practices while preserving the phonetic structure of Dhivehi, an Indo-Aryan language with roots in ancient Prakrit and close ties to Sinhalese. The earliest known records of Thaana date to 1705 CE in government documents, marking the script's initial form.
The consonant shapes in early Thaana were uniquely derived from numerals: the first nine letters (representing h, sh, n, r, b, l, k, alifu, and v) were adapted from Eastern Arabic numerals 1 through 9, while the subsequent nine (m, dh, th, d, kh, z, gh, t, y) drew from Dhives Akuru-derived numerals 10 through 18. Vowel indications were incorporated using Arabic-style diacritics (fili) placed above, below, or to the sides of consonants, reflecting the script's abjad structure. This numeral-based design is thought to have been intentional, possibly to maintain secrecy from outsiders amid the Maldives' strategic position in Indian Ocean trade routes. The script adopted a right-to-left writing direction, borrowed from Arabic.
Originally, Thaana included 25 consonants, among them the now-obsolete aviyani (representing the retroflex nasal //), along with a null consonant alifu used as a vowel carrier. Early manuscripts from the 17th and 18th centuries, such as those dating to 1705 CE, demonstrate this full set in use for administrative and religious texts. The script was initially known as Gabulhi Thaana ("incomplete Thaana") or simply Tna, a term possibly linked to its abbreviated or evolving nature during this formative period. Despite heavy Arabic influences from Islamic scholarship and trade, Thaana retained Indic phonetic elements suited to Dhivehi's syllable structure, distinguishing it from pure adaptations of the Arabic alphabet.

Standardization and Reforms

In 1953, the Maldivian government officially abolished the letter aviyani (), which represented the retroflex nasal sound //, from official documents, thereby reducing the core set of Thaana consonants to 24 and simplifying the alphabet by merging it with the function of the letter nunu ().
To accommodate sounds from Arabic loanwords in religious and literary texts, the Maldivian government introduced additional letters known as thikijehi thaana in 1957; these consist of diacritic modifications, such as dots added to existing consonants, to represent phonemes like /q/ (qaafu, ), /x/ (khaa, ), and // (ghainu, ), expanding the script's utility for Islamic adaptations without altering its fundamental structure.
During the mid-1970s, under President Ibrahim Nasir, the Maldivian government pursued modernization efforts by attempting to replace Thaana with a Latin-based romanization system called Mal Latin or Dhivehi Latin, introduced in 19761977 for official documents, education, and telex communications to facilitate technological integration and bilingualism; this led to a period of dual-script usage in schools but encountered significant public resistance due to cultural attachment to Thaana.
The Thaana script was reinstated as the official writing system in 1978 by President Maumoon Abdul Gayoom shortly after he assumed office, restoring its primacy in government, education, and daily use while allowing limited continued application of the Latin system in informal contexts like digital messaging; this reform also involved adopting the spelling "Thaana" (reflecting the phonetic // sound in English transcription) during the romanization period to align with international conventions.

Characteristics

Consonants

Thaana features 24 core consonant letters, each corresponding to a distinct phoneme in the Dhivehi language and written as independent symbols aligned on the middle line of text. These consonants form the skeletal structure of words, with vowels indicated by optional diacritics attached above or below them. The inventory includes both native Dhivehi sounds and adaptations for sounds influenced by Arabic and Indic languages.

Vowels

The Thaana script employs a system of ten vowel diacritics, known as fili, to represent five short vowels (/a/, /i/, /u/, /e/, /o/) and their five long counterparts (/a/, /i/, /u/, /e/, /o/). These diacritics are always attached to a base consonant or the vowel carrier letter alifu (, U+0787), functioning in an abugida-like manner where they modify the pronunciation of the preceding consonant without an inherent vowel sound in the base letters themselves.
Short vowels are indicated by single marks, while long vowels use modified or doubled forms of these marks, except for the long a (/a/), which has a distinct vertical stroke below the base. A consonant without any fili is typically pronounced with an implicit short /a/ (// in some analyses), unless modified by the sukun diacritic (, U+07B0), which denotes a consonant without a following vowel. The fili are positioned above, below, or occasionally to the side of the base, contributing to Thaana's characteristic multi-line layout: consonants align on a middle baseline, with upper-line marks (e.g., for /i/) and lower-line marks (e.g., for /u/) extending the vertical space of glyphs and creating a visually dense appearance in text.

Orthography

Vowel Representation Rules

In Thaana orthography, vowels are primarily represented using diacritics known as fili, which are combining marks placed above or below consonants to indicate the following vowel sound. These diacritics are obligatory in fully vocalized texts, such as religious scriptures and formal documents, where every vowel is explicitly marked to ensure precise pronunciation. However, in everyday writing and informal contexts, vowel diacritics are frequently omitted, with readers relying on contextual cues and linguistic knowledge to infer the intended sounds; a bare consonant without a diacritic typically implies the short // vowel, similar to the inherent vowel in many abugida systems.

These are used consistently even when vowel diacritics are omitted, maintaining readability in both vocalized and defective texts.

Additional Letters and Loanwords

Thaana's core alphabet of 24 consonants has been extended through modifications to represent non-native phonemes, primarily those in Arabic loanwords introduced via Islam. These extensions, referred to as thiki jehi Thaana or "dotted Thaana," involve adding diacritics such as dots or lines to existing letters, allowing transcription of sounds absent in standard Dhivehi phonology.
Specific examples include qaaf (, U+07A4), formed by placing two dots below kaafu (, U+0786) to denote /q/ as in Arabic ; khaa (, U+079A), created by adding one dot above kaafu for /x/ corresponding to Arabic ; and ghain (, U+07A3), derived from alifu (, U+0787) with a horizontal line for // matching Arabic . Other modifications cover sounds like // (aal, from daal with dots) and // (aa, from haa with dots), enabling precise rendering of Arabic terms in Dhivehi texts. These are encoded in the Unicode Thaana block (U+0780U+07BF) as distinct characters rather than combining marks.
Such letters are predominantly employed in religious and Islamic contexts, such as Quranic transliterations or scholarly works, where fidelity to Arabic pronunciation is prioritized over Dhivehi nativization. In secular writing, however, usage varies, with many loanwords adopting simplified spellings using core letters, leading to dialectal and stylistic differences across texts. Not all extensions are universally standardized, resulting in inconsistent application even within formal publications.
Similar dotted modifications accommodate Perso-Arabic sounds from Persian and Urdu loanwords, which entered Dhivehi through historical trade and cultural exchanges. For instance, terms borrowed via Urdu may use khaa or ghain to preserve original fricatives. Adaptations for Sanskrit-derived vocabulary remain rare and non-standardized, though occasional proposals for further extensions have appeared in linguistic discussions.

Unicode and Computing

Encoding Standards

Thaana is encoded in the Unicode Standard within the dedicated block from U+0780 to U+07BF, which spans 64 code points in the Basic Multilingual Plane. This block was introduced in Unicode version 3.0, released in September 1999, to support the script used for writing the Dhivehi language. Of these code points, 50 are assigned, covering the core repertoire of the script, while 14 remain unassigned or reserved.
The assigned code points include 24 basic consonant letters (U+0780U+0797), 10 combining vowel signs for short and long vowels (U+07A6U+07AF), the sukun diacritic (U+07B0), the alifu null consonant at U+0787, additional letters for loanwords at U+0798U+07A5, and a dialectal variant at U+07B1 (THAANA LETTER NAA). For instance, the consonant Haa (representing /h/) is at U+0780 (), the long aa vowel sign (representing /a/) is at U+07A7 (), and the sukun (indicating no vowel) is at U+07B0 ().
Thaana characters are classified with the right-to-left bidirectional class (R) in the Unicode Bidirectional Algorithm, enabling proper rendering of text from right to left. This supports logical storage ordering, where mixed-language text combining Thaana with left-to-right scripts like Latin is visually reordered by applications following the algorithm, ensuring correct display without altering the underlying code point sequence.
The Thaana block is synchronized with ISO/IEC 10646, the international standard for the Universal Character Set, as Unicode maintains identical character assignments in this regard for full interoperability across global systems.

Input Methods and Support

Input methods for the Thaana script primarily rely on phonetic keyboard layouts mapped to standard QWERTY keyboards, allowing users to type Romanized approximations that are transliterated into Thaana characters. Popular implementations include the Segha phonetic layout, where sequences like "haa" produce the consonant h (), and the Hassan Hameed variant, which adjusts mappings for vowels and diacritics such as alifu and sukun. These layouts often use dead keys or modifier combinations to insert vowel diacritics (fili) above or below consonants, with typewriter-style arrangements providing direct key-to-character mapping for efficiency in professional typing.
Operating systems and mobile platforms offer built-in or easily installable support for Thaana input, particularly when the Maldives locale is enabled. On Android and iOS, Google and Apple provide native keyboards for Dhivehi, integrating phonetic input methods that convert Latin keystrokes to Thaana Unicode via the device's input method editor (IME). Windows includes the Dhivehi keyboard layout with phonetic transliteration, while macOS requires third-party extensions or custom installations for full functionality. Fonts such as Noto Sans Thaana, an unmodulated sans-serif design with multiple weights and 90 glyphs supporting the Thaana block, ensure proper rendering across these platforms.
Rendering Thaana text presents challenges due to its right-to-left directionality and the vertical positioning of diacritics, which can cause misalignment or overlap in bidirectional contexts mixing Latin and Thaana. Older software may exhibit display issues with complex shaping, such as improper stacking of vowels on consonants, but modern solutions leverage OpenType features like glyph substitution and positioning to handle these dynamically. The Unicode Bidirectional Algorithm serves as the foundation, ensuring correct ordering in mixed-script documents.
Development tools for Thaana include open-source GitHub projects that facilitate testing and font creation. For instance, Thaana Faker generates random Dhivehi text in Thaana script for development and quality assurance purposes, supporting JavaScript and TypeScript environments since its post-2020 release. Font design efforts, such as the Noto Thaana repository, provide automated builds and resources for creating OpenType-compliant fonts that linearize the script's multi-line structure into horizontal rendering. These tools, including Microsoft's guidelines for Thaana OpenType implementation, aid developers in addressing shaping complexities.

Usage and Significance

Role in Dhivehi Language

Thaana serves as the primary script for writing Dhivehi, also known as Maldivian, an Indo-Aryan language spoken by approximately 370,000 people primarily in the Maldives and Minicoy Island in India. As the official script, it has been integral to education, media, and government communications since its reinstatement in 1978 by President Maumoon Abdul Gayoom, following a brief period of Latin script promotion in the mid-1970s. This reinstatement ensured Thaana's dominance in formal literacy, with current literacy rates exceeding 95% among Maldivians.
The script's phonemic accuracy aligns closely with Dhivehi's phonological inventory, which includes 29 consonants and 10 vowels, allowing for a precise representation of the language's sounds through its 24 basic consonant letters and vowel diacritics. Thaana functions as an abugida-alphabet hybrid, where vowels are typically marked with diacritics (fili) above, below, or beside consonants, but short vowels can be optionally omitted in everyday writing to promote conciseness while maintaining readability for native speakers. This feature supports efficient expression in both spoken and written forms, adapting to Dhivehi's morphophonology without significant ambiguity.
In contemporary contexts, Thaana is employed in official government documents, educational materials, and public signage across the Maldives, reinforcing its role in national administration and identity. Newspapers such as Haveeru, the longest-running daily in the Maldives since 1979, which resumed operations in 2024 following legal proceedings, publish primarily in Thaana to reach local audiences with news and commentary. Digital media, including websites and social platforms, increasingly incorporate Thaana for content creation and dissemination, while in the tourism sector, it appears alongside English on signs, menus, and promotional materials in guesthouses and local islands to accommodate visitors.
Dialectal variations of Dhivehi, such as Mahl spoken on Minicoy Island, utilize the Thaana script to accommodate its archaic features and Malayalam loanwords, though the standard Thaana orthography predominates in the Maldives. Amid a 2025 policy shift in Minicoy schools favoring Hindi, Malayalam, and English, local efforts continue to preserve Mahl instruction. This adaptation ensures continuity in writing practices across regions while preserving linguistic distinctions.

Cultural and Historical Importance

Thaana serves as a profound symbol of Maldivian national identity, encapsulating the cultural synthesis of Islamic and Indic elements that emerged following the Maldives' conversion to Islam in 1153 CE under Sultan Muhammad al-Adil. This script, developed in the late 17th century amid the islands' strategic position as an Indian Ocean crossroads, reflects the integration of Arab Muslim influences with pre-existing South Asian traditions, distinguishing Maldivian heritage from neighboring regions. Its adoption marked a shift from earlier scripts like Dhives Akuru, used during the Buddhist era, to a system that facilitated the expression of Islamic scholarship and local narratives, thereby reinforcing communal bonds in a maritime society.
The script's influences underscore its unique hybrid nature: it incorporates Arabic-style diacritics for vowels and a right-to-left directionality, while deriving many consonant shapes from inverted Arabic numerals and elements of the indigenous Dhives Akuru script, particularly in numeral forms. This blend sets Thaana apart from pure alphabets like Arabic, which lacks inherent vowel notation and features cursive connectivity, and from abugidas such as Devanagari, which employs left-to-right horizontal arrangements without vertical stacking of modifiers. Initially employed by Muslim scholars for transcribing Quranic excerpts into Dhivehi and crafting magical incantationssecretive writings that preserved esoteric knowledgeThaana evolved to document folklore and oral epics, safeguarding intangible cultural heritage against colonial disruptions like Portuguese incursions in the 16th century.
In the 20th century, Thaana faced challenges to its prominence, including a brief 1976 governmental push toward left-to-right Latin script for modernization, which was reversed in 1978 under President Maumoon Abdul Gayoom as a cultural policy affirming its indispensable role in national expression. Preservation efforts continue to emphasize its vitality, with initiatives like the 2020 Thaana Mallow app enhancing digital accessibility for visually impaired users while preserving the script's core form, ensuring its relevance in a globalized world without diluting its historical essence. These endeavors highlight Thaana's enduring function in maintaining Maldivian oral traditions through written media, from folk tales to religious adaptations.
