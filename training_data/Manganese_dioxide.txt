Manganese dioxide

 Manganese dioxide is an inorganic chemical compound with the formula MnO, appearing as a black to brown powder or crystalline solid, and it occurs naturally as the mineral pyrolusite, the principal ore of manganese. It has a molecular weight of 86.94 g/mol, a density of 5.0 g/cm, and decomposes at 535 C without melting, while being insoluble in water. As a strong oxidizing agent, manganese dioxide exists in various polymorphs, including -MnO (hollandite structure with 22 tunnels), -MnO (rutile structure with 11 tunnels), -MnO (used in batteries), and -MnO (layered birnessite), each with distinct crystal frameworks formed by edge- and corner-sharing MnO octahedra.
Historically, pyrolusite has been utilized since ancient times; for instance, Egyptians and Romans employed it to decolorize glass tainted by iron impurities, and Neanderthals selectively gathered manganese oxides like pyrolusite for fire-making and possibly pigment production around 50,000 years ago. In modern applications, manganese dioxide is a critical component in dry-cell and alkaline batteries, serving as the cathode material in zinc-carbon and zinc-manganese dioxide cells due to its ability to facilitate proton intercalation and provide high capacity. It is also widely used as an oxidant in organic synthesis, in the production of manganese metal and alloys like ferromanganese for steelmaking, as a pigment and decolorizer in glass and ceramics, and in water treatment for removing contaminants.
Regarding safety, manganese dioxide is classified as harmful if inhaled or ingested, potentially causing respiratory irritation, and chronic exposure may lead to manganism, a neurological disorder resembling Parkinson's disease affecting the central nervous system. Despite these hazards, its environmental role is notable, as natural manganese oxides contribute to geochemical processes like oxidation in soils and sediments. Ongoing research explores nanostructured forms of MnO for advanced energy storage, catalysis, and biomedical applications, leveraging its polymorphic versatility.

Properties

Crystal structure and polymorphs

Manganese dioxide (MnO) exhibits a rich polymorphism, with all polymorphs constructed from Mn(IV) ions in octahedral coordination, forming MnO octahedra that share edges and corners to create diverse frameworks. These structures determine the material's porosity, defect sites, and ion accessibility, which in turn influence its reactivity, such as in catalytic processes or ion intercalation. The nonstoichiometric nature of many polymorphs arises from oxygen vacancies and partial substitution by lower-valent cations, leading to mixed Mn(III)/Mn(IV) states and structural flexibility.
The primary polymorph, -MnO (pyrolusite), adopts a rutile-type structure (tetragonal, space group P4/mnm) consisting of corner-sharing MnO octahedra arranged in a three-dimensional network with narrow 11 tunnels (approximately 2.3 2.3 ) along the c-axis. This dense framework is the most thermodynamically stable form under ambient conditions, with minimal defects in its ideal stoichiometric composition (MnO).
-MnO (hollandite or cryptomelane) features a body-centered tetragonal tunnel structure (space group I4/m) formed by double chains of edge-sharing MnO octahedra linked by corner-sharing, creating larger 22 tunnels (approximately 4.6 4.6 ) that can accommodate stabilizing cations such as K or Ba. Its nonstoichiometric composition is represented as (0 < x < 0.25), where cation doping in the tunnels compensates for charge balance and oxygen vacancies, enhancing structural stability and reactivity by facilitating guest ion diffusion.
-MnO consists of an intergrowth of ramsdellite (12 tunnels) and nsutite-like phases (11 and 21 tunnels), resulting in an orthorhombic structure (space group Pnma) with disordered edge- and corner-sharing MnO octahedra. This defective arrangement introduces significant nonstoichiometry through oxygen vacancies and Mn(III) incorporation, promoting higher reactivity compared to more ordered polymorphs due to increased surface defect sites and variable tunnel sizes that aid proton or cation insertion.
-MnO (birnessite) displays a layered structure (hexagonal or monoclinic, space group C2/m) composed of two-dimensional sheets of edge-sharing MnO octahedra stacked with interlayer spacing of about 7 , often hydrated and intercalated with cations like Na or Ca. Its inherent nonstoichiometry stems from vacancies in the octahedral sheets and interlayer cations, which expand the lattice and create reactive sites at edges and defects, favoring surface-mediated processes over bulk diffusion.
-MnO (akhtenskite) is a metastable hexagonal polymorph with a compact structure involving intergrowths of 11 and 12 tunnels from edge-sharing MnO octahedra, exhibiting nonstoichiometry via oxygen vacancies that distort the framework. This configuration leads to enhanced reactivity through exposed defect sites, though its instability limits long-term applications compared to tunnel or layered variants.

Physical and thermal properties

Manganese dioxide, with the chemical formula MnO, has a molar mass of 86.937 g/mol and appears as a black-to-brown powder or solid. It exhibits a density of 5.0 g/cm and is insoluble in water, making it suitable for applications requiring stability in aqueous environments.
Thermally, manganese dioxide decomposes at 535 C into manganese(III) oxide (MnO) and oxygen gas according to the reaction 2MnO MnO + O, rather than melting at atmospheric pressure. The standard molar heat capacity at 298 K is 54.1 J/molK, reflecting its relatively low capacity to store thermal energy compared to many metals. Thermal conductivity values for the solid form range from 0.2 to 0.5 W/mK, indicating poor heat transfer efficiency, which influences its use in insulation or catalytic processes.
Properties vary among its polymorphs due to differences in crystal packing, affecting density and other bulk characteristics. For instance, the -polymorph (pyrolusite) has a higher density of approximately 5.03 g/cm, while the -polymorph (hollandite) is less dense at around 4.2 g/cm, impacting mechanical handling and sintering behavior. Chemical properties

Manganese dioxide (MnO) displays amphoteric properties, dissolving in strong acids to yield Mn ions and in strong bases to produce manganate species. For instance, it reacts with concentrated hydrochloric acid to form soluble manganese(II) chloride alongside chlorine gas evolution. In fused potassium hydroxide with an oxidizing agent such as air or oxygen, it converts to potassium manganate(VI), KMnO.
Under ambient conditions, MnO remains chemically inert in air and water, showing no significant decomposition or reactivity. Its solubility in neutral water is negligible, on the order of 10 g/L, though this varies with pH: solubility rises markedly in acidic media due to protonation and dissolution, and in highly alkaline environments via formation of soluble manganates. Thermal decomposition occurs above approximately 500 C, where MnO reduces to manganese(III) oxide, MnO, releasing oxygen.
The redox behavior of MnO is characterized by the Mn(IV)/Mn(III) couple, with a standard potential of +0.95 V versus the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) in acidic conditions, positioning it as a potent oxidant in environmental and electrochemical processes.
Identification of MnO polymorphs relies on spectroscopic techniques, particularly Raman spectroscopy for vibrational modes and X-ray diffraction (XRD) for crystal structure. Characteristic Raman peaks arise from Mn-O stretching vibrations, while XRD patterns reflect lattice spacings unique to each polymorph. Occurrence and production

Natural occurrence

Manganese dioxide is most abundantly found in nature as the mineral pyrolusite (-MnO), a black, metallic-looking oxide that serves as the principal ore for manganese extraction. Global reserves of manganese ore, predominantly pyrolusite, are estimated at approximately 1.7 billion tonnes of contained manganese, with major deposits concentrated in South Africa, Australia, China, Gabon, and Brazil. These reserves form through various geological processes, including sedimentary deposition in ancient marine environments, where manganese precipitates from oxygenated waters, and supergene enrichment via weathering of primary manganese-bearing rocks in tropical climates. Hydrothermal activity also contributes, as hot, mineral-rich fluids deposit manganese oxides in veins and replacements within host rocks.
Other notable manganese dioxide minerals include psilomelane, a hydrated barium manganese oxide with a tunnel structure (often denoted as -MnO variant), and manganite (-MnOOH), which occurs as prismatic crystals or fibrous masses in low-temperature hydrothermal or sedimentary settings. Biogenic forms of manganese dioxide arise from microbial oxidation of Mn(II) by bacteria in soils, freshwater systems, and marine environments, producing poorly crystalline oxides that contribute to natural manganese cycling. In oceanic settings, these biogenic processes play a role in the formation of polymetallic nodules on the seafloor, particularly in the Pacific Clarion-Clipperton Zone, where concentric layers of manganese and iron oxides accrete around nuclei over millions of years at rates of millimeters per million years.
Manganese-rich ocean floor nodules, covering up to 30% of the abyssal plains, can contain up to 30% MnO alongside iron oxides and trace metals like nickel and cobalt, making them a significant potential resource despite extraction challenges. Annual global production of natural manganese dioxide ore reached approximately 56 million tonnes (gross weight) in 2022 and remained around 5660 million tonnes as of 2024, primarily from high-grade deposits in the aforementioned regions, supporting industrial demands while highlighting the mineral's widespread geological abundance.

Synthetic production methods

Synthetic production of manganese dioxide (MnO) primarily involves chemical, electrolytic, and advanced methods tailored to achieve specific polymorphs and purity levels for applications such as batteries and pigments. Chemical methods are widely used for laboratory-scale and lower-purity products, while electrolytic processes dominate industrial production of high-purity -MnO.
One common chemical approach is the thermal decomposition of manganese(II) nitrate, where Mn(NO) decomposes to form MnO according to the reaction Mn(NO) MnO + 2NO + O, typically at temperatures around 400 C, yielding -MnO suitable for catalytic uses. Another chemical method involves the oxidation of Mn salts, such as MnSO, using potassium permanganate (KMnO) in aqueous solution, which proceeds via comproportionation to produce MnO precipitates; this reaction is balanced as 2MnO + 3Mn + 2HO 5MnO + 4H and is effective for generating birnessite-like -MnO. Hydrothermal synthesis offers control over polymorphs like -, -, and -MnO by reacting precursors such as KMnO under high temperature (120220 C) and pressure in water, with ion ratios (e.g., K/H) directing the tunnel or layered structures formed.
The electrolytic method (EMD) is the primary industrial route for battery-grade MnO, involving anodic oxidation of MnSO in sulfuric acid electrolyte (typically 0.5 M MnSO and 0.25 M HSO) at temperatures of 9095 C, with cell voltages of 1.61.8 V and current densities of 12 A/dm. The process flow includes leaching manganese ore or waste with HSO and a reductant like dextrin to dissolve Mn, followed by purification via pH adjustment to remove impurities, and electrolysis where Mn is oxidized to -MnO deposited on the anode (e.g., titanium or lead), achieving >99% purity with <0.1 wt.% impurities. Energy consumption for EMD production ranges from 1.42.0 kWh/kg MnO, influenced by current efficiency and cell design.
Chemical manganese dioxide (CMD), produced via precipitation or decomposition methods, offers lower purity (typically 8595% MnO) at reduced cost, making it suitable for pigments and ferrites, whereas EMD's higher purity and fibrous -phase structure justify its elevated production costs for electronics and batteries. Global EMD production accounts for the majority of high-purity synthetic MnO output as of 2023, with CMD comprising the remainder for non-critical applications.
Recent advances emphasize sustainability, such as bioleaching of manganese from ores or spent batteries using bacteria like Acidithiobacillus ferrooxidans, which oxidize Mn to Mn under acidic conditions, enabling recovery rates up to 90% and subsequent precipitation of MnO; post-2020 developments have optimized mixed microbial consortia for efficient, low-energy extraction from secondary resources.

Chemical reactions

Reduction reactions

Manganese dioxide (MnO) undergoes reduction reactions that lower the oxidation state of manganese from +4 to +3 or +2, typically via a stepwise mechanism involving Mn(III) intermediates. The process begins with a one-electron reduction to form manganese(III) oxyhydroxide (MnOOH), followed by further reduction to Mn, with the pathway dictated by the reductant's strength and environmental conditions such as pH. Stronger reductants and lower pH promote complete reduction to Mn by facilitating proton-coupled electron transfer, while milder conditions may stabilize Mn(III) species. This stepwise nature arises from the thermodynamic instability of Mn(III) in aqueous media, where it can disproportionate unless complexed or structurally incorporated.

Oxidation reactions

Manganese dioxide serves as a versatile oxidant in various chemical processes, primarily through a surface-mediated electron transfer mechanism where substrates adsorb onto the MnO surface, facilitating electron abstraction and often enabling catalytic regeneration of the oxidant as reduced manganese species are reoxidized. This heterogeneous process is particularly effective for selective oxidations, with the standard reduction potential for the MnO/Mn couple (MnO(s) + 4H + 2e Mn + 2HO) at 1.23 V in acidic media, underscoring its thermodynamic favorability for oxidizing a range of substrates. The reaction typically proceeds without overoxidation due to the mild nature of MnO, and its activity can be enhanced by the presence of oxygen or other regenerants. Applications

Historical and traditional uses

Manganese dioxide, primarily in its natural form as pyrolusite, has been utilized by humans since prehistoric times. Archaeological evidence from Neanderthal sites, such as Pech-de-l'Az I in France dating to approximately 50,000 years ago, indicates that early hominins collected and processed manganese dioxide nodules to facilitate fire-starting. The mineral, when powdered and mixed with tinder, lowers the ignition temperature of organic materials by about 100C, enabling easier spark ignition and suggesting intentional chemical knowledge for on-demand fire production.
In addition to practical uses, manganese dioxide served as a black pigment in prehistoric art. At Lascaux Cave in France, dated to around 17,000 BCE, artists employed manganese oxides alongside iron oxides and charcoal to create durable black hues in wall paintings depicting animals and symbols, demonstrating early exploitation of the mineral's staining properties.
During ancient and medieval periods, manganese dioxide found applications in craftsmanship. In Roman glassmaking from the 1st century BCE onward, it was added as a decolorizer to neutralize green tints caused by iron impurities in silica sand, allowing production of clearer vessels through oxidation of ferrous iron to ferric forms. By the medieval era, particularly from the 14th century in Europe, pyrolusite was ground into powders for use as a black pigment in ceramics, as documented in alchemical texts like the Montpellier Liber diversarum arcium, where it imparted dark tones to glazes and decorative slips.
In the 18th and 19th centuries, manganese dioxide played a key role in emerging chemical industries. Starting in the 1780s, it was reacted with sulfuric acid and salt to generate chlorine gas for bleaching textiles, marking an early industrial application that relied on the mineral's oxidizing properties.
Culturally, manganese dioxide held significance in traditional practices and resource extraction. In ancient Chinese medicine, pyrolusite was widely used in various formulations, reflecting its integration into early pharmacopeias alongside other earth-derived remedies. Its mining history, tied to pyrolusite deposits, underscores a long-standing human-mineral relationship, from Paleolithic collection to medieval quarrying, symbolizing resource ingenuity in pigment and process technologies.

Batteries and energy storage

Manganese dioxide serves as a key cathode material in various electrochemical energy storage devices due to its high theoretical capacity, low cost, and environmental compatibility. In primary batteries, it is predominantly used in alkaline zinc-manganese dioxide (Zn/MnO) cells and zinc-carbon batteries, where it undergoes reduction to deliver stable voltage and energy output. These batteries account for a significant portion of the global disposable battery market, with manganese dioxide consumption for such applications estimated at approximately 500,000 tonnes annually.
The alkaline Zn/MnO battery operates with a zinc anode in potassium hydroxide electrolyte and a manganese dioxide cathode, providing a nominal voltage of 1.5 V and higher capacity compared to acidic variants. The primary reduction mechanism at the cathode involves the one-electron transfer: MnO + HO + e MnOOH + OH, which proceeds through proton-coupled electron transfer, enabling efficient discharge. This chemistry is exemplified in the Leclanch cell configuration, originally developed for zinc-carbon systems but adapted for alkaline electrolytes, where manganese dioxide mixed with carbon enhances conductivity and moisture retention. Zinc-carbon cells, using ammonium chloride or zinc chloride electrolytes, similarly rely on MnO but exhibit lower capacity due to the acidic environment limiting MnO utilization.
Electrolytic manganese dioxide (EMD), produced via electrodeposition, outperforms chemical manganese dioxide (CMD) in these batteries, offering higher discharge capacity (up to 300 mAh/g) and better rate performance owing to its -phase structure with tunnel morphology that facilitates ion diffusion. In contrast, CMD, synthesized through chemical precipitation, provides marginally lower capacity (around 250 mAh/g) but is more cost-effective for low-drain applications like zinc-carbon cells. Both maintain the 1.5 V plateau, but EMD's superior crystallinity reduces polarization, extending service life in high-drain devices.
In advanced primary batteries, lithium-manganese dioxide (Li/MnO) cells utilize MnO as the cathode with a lithium anode, delivering a higher nominal voltage of 3 V and energy density up to 300 Wh/kg, suitable for long-shelf-life applications like cameras and medical devices. Emerging rechargeable zinc-ion batteries (ZIBs) incorporate nanostructured MnO cathodes to address limitations in cycling stability, with recent 2024 reviews highlighting high-rate performance exceeding 200 mAh/g at 1C and over 80% capacity retention after 1000 cycles through mild acidic electrolytes. As of 2025, innovations in electrolyte design using organic molecules have enabled practical aqueous Zn/MnO batteries with enhanced stability, capacities up to 450 mAh/g, and scalable high-voltage operation. These ZIBs leverage similar MnO reduction mechanisms but enable reversibility via Zn intercalation or conversion reactions.
Despite these advantages, MnO cathodes face challenges such as manganese dissolution during cycling, which leads to capacity fade via Mn leaching and Jahn-Teller distortion. Nanostructuring, such as forming nanosheets or doping with heteroatoms, mitigates these issues by enhancing structural integrity and electrolyte contact, improving cyclability in ZIBs to over 500 cycles. In the disposable battery market, MnO-based cells hold over 90% share for alkaline and zinc-carbon types, driven by their dominance in consumer electronics.

Organic synthesis and catalysis

Manganese dioxide (MnO) serves as a mild, selective oxidant in organic synthesis, particularly for the conversion of allylic and benzylic alcohols to the corresponding aldehydes and ketones under ambient conditions, avoiding over-oxidation to carboxylic acids. This reaction proceeds via a heterogeneous suspension of active MnO in neutral media, such as dichloromethane or ether, at room temperature, with typical yields exceeding 80% for substrates like geraniol or benzyl alcohol. The selectivity arises from the reagent's ability to target activated alcohols through a radical-mediated mechanism, preserving sensitive functional groups like double bonds. In catalytic applications, MnO facilitates the oxidation of carbon monoxide (CO) to carbon dioxide (CO) in automotive exhaust systems, often as a component in three-way catalysts due to its high oxygen storage capacity and low-temperature activity. It also promotes the epoxidation of alkenes using peroxides like tert-butyl hydroperoxide, with supported MnO variants achieving up to 95% selectivity for epoxide formation from substrates such as styrene. Additionally, active MnO catalyzes the dehydrogenation of alcohols to carbonyl compounds, exhibiting high selectivity in competitive reactions involving primary and secondary alcohols.
Active manganese dioxide (AMD), a highly reactive form of MnO, is prepared by the oxidation of manganese(II) sulfate with potassium permanganate in aqueous solution at elevated temperatures, followed by filtration and drying, yielding a finely divided black powder with enhanced surface area for laboratory use. In organic synthesis, AMD is employed for selective oxidations similar to those described above, but its superior reactivity allows for shorter reaction times and broader substrate tolerance. On an industrial scale, MnO-based catalysts have been explored for the aerobic oxidation of cyclohexanol to adipic acid, offering a greener alternative to nitric acid processes with yields up to 70% in electrochemical setups.
Recent developments in nanostructured MnO, such as nanowires and hollow spheres synthesized via green methods like plant extract reduction, have advanced its role in sustainable catalysis, enabling solvent-free reactions for amine coupling and heterocycle formation with recyclability over five cycles and minimal metal leaching. These materials exhibit improved selectivity in bio-derived glycerol transformations to N-substituted formamides, achieving 90% yields under mild conditions, thus promoting environmentally benign organic processes.

Environmental remediation and other uses

Manganese dioxide serves as an effective filtration media in water treatment processes, particularly in the greensand process, where it coats glauconite sand to oxidize and remove dissolved iron (Fe) and manganese (Mn) ions from groundwater, preventing staining and improving water quality. The coating is regenerated using potassium permanganate, enabling continuous operation in municipal and private systems.
Beyond iron and manganese removal, manganese dioxide adsorbs arsenic species from contaminated water, with modified forms like -MnO demonstrating high affinity for As(III) and As(V) through surface complexation and oxidation mechanisms, achieving removal efficiencies up to 99% under optimized conditions. Similarly, it adsorbs heavy metals such as lead, cadmium, and chromium via ion exchange and precipitation, with manganese dioxide-loaded biochar composites showing enhanced capacities due to increased surface area.
In catalytic applications for pollutant degradation, manganese dioxide facilitates the breakdown of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) like formaldehyde through advanced oxidation processes, leveraging its redox properties to generate reactive oxygen species. For dye pollutants, -MnO structures enable Fenton-like reactions with hydrogen peroxide, achieving over 90% decolorization of azo dyes like malachite green by promoting hydroxyl radical formation.
Manganese dioxide-rich polymetallic nodules on ocean floors are a primary source for extracting valuable metals including nickel, copper, and cobalt through hydrometallurgical processes involving acid leaching and reductive roasting, supporting global supply chains for these critical materials.
As a pigment, manganese dioxide imparts a stable black color to ceramic glazes and bodies, used historically and in modern formulations to achieve durable dark hues resistant to high firing temperatures. In corrosion prevention, it functions as an inhibitive pigment in protective coatings, where nanoparticles enhance barrier properties and reduce underfilm corrosion rates on steel substrates by forming passive layers. For supercapacitors, manganese dioxide electrodes exhibit pseudocapacitance via reversible Mn/Mn redox transitions, delivering specific capacitances exceeding 200 F/g in aqueous electrolytes.
Emerging research highlights biogenic manganese dioxide, produced by manganese-oxidizing bacteria, for bioremediation, where it sequesters heavy metals and degrades organic contaminants like antibiotics in soil and water through enhanced adsorption and oxidation. In the 2020s, composites such as reduced graphene oxide-manganese dioxide have shown promise for CO capture from flue gases at elevated temperatures, with adsorption capacities up to 5 mmol/g via physisorption and chemical reaction to form carbonates. Additionally, manganese dioxide-integrated coatings, like MXene-MnO hybrids on textiles, provide antimicrobial activity by generating reactive oxygen species that disrupt bacterial cell walls, offering durable protection against pathogens.

Safety and environmental impact

Health and toxicity

Inhalation of manganese dioxide dust poses significant health risks, primarily through respiratory irritation and systemic neurotoxicity. Exposure to particulate manganese dioxide can trigger an inflammatory response in the lungs, leading to pneumonitis and increased susceptibility to bronchitis. Chronic inhalation results in manganese accumulation in the brain, particularly in the basal ganglia, causing manganisma Parkinson-like syndrome characterized by symptoms such as dystonia, bradykinesia, gait disturbances, and psychiatric effects. The neurotoxic mechanism involves redox cycling between Mn and Mn ions, which generates reactive oxygen species like hydrogen peroxide during interactions with dopamine, leading to oxidative stress and neuronal damage.
Ingestion and dermal exposure to manganese dioxide exhibit low acute toxicity. The oral LD50 in rats exceeds 3478 mg/kg, indicating it is not highly toxic in single exposures, while dermal LD50 values are greater than 2000 mg/kg. However, chronic exposure through contaminated water or food can contribute to neurological effects, including cognitive impairments and motor dysfunction, due to cumulative manganese buildup.
Under the Globally Harmonized System (GHS), manganese dioxide is classified as harmful if swallowed (H302) and harmful if inhaled (H332), reflecting its potential for acute respiratory and gastrointestinal irritation. The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) sets a permissible exposure limit (PEL) of 5 mg/m as a ceiling value for manganese compounds, including manganese dioxide, to prevent adverse health effects.
Occupational case studies highlight risks in manganese mining and battery production, where workers inhaling dust over years have developed manganism, as documented in reports from dry-cell battery factories and ore processing sites. Mitigation strategies include the use of personal protective equipment (PPE) such as respirators and the implementation of local exhaust ventilation systems to reduce airborne concentrations below exposure limits.

Ecological and regulatory concerns

Manganese dioxide (MnO), while relatively insoluble in neutral water, can release bioavailable manganese ions under reducing conditions, such as in anaerobic sediments or acidic environments, contributing to environmental contamination from mining, battery production, and industrial discharges. These releases pose risks to aquatic ecosystems, where elevated manganese levels disrupt microbial communities and biogeochemical cycles, including nitrogen and sulfur transformations in sediments. In soils, chronic exposure from mining tailings can lead to manganese accumulation, affecting plant growth through chlorosis and reduced nutrient uptake, with critical tissue levels ranging from 100 to 5000 mg/kg depending on species and soil pH.
Ecological toxicity is particularly pronounced in aquatic organisms, where manganese acts as a systemic toxicant at concentrations exceeding essential requirements. Acute toxicity tests show 96-hour LC values for fish ranging from 2.4 mg/L (coho salmon in soft water) to over 3000 mg/L in harder waters, with invertebrates like Daphnia magna exhibiting 48-hour LC as low as 0.8 mg/L. Algae are highly sensitive, with EC values around 1.51.9 mg/L for species such as marine diatoms and freshwater Scenedesmus, where manganese inhibits chlorophyll synthesis and induces iron deficiency. Chronic exposure causes immunosuppression, reproductive impairment, and reduced growth in invertebrates and fish, with effects exacerbated in soft, low-pH waters (hardness <50 mg/L CaCO) due to increased bioavailability. Bioaccumulation occurs in aquatic biota, with factors up to 1000 in invertebrates, potentially magnifying trophic transfer in food webs. MnO nanoparticles, emerging from industrial applications, have shown additional mitochondrial toxicity in aquatic species, though broader ecosystem studies are ongoing.
Regulatory frameworks address these concerns through discharge limits and water quality standards to protect ecosystems. In Canada, the Canadian Council of Ministers of the Environment (CCME) sets freshwater guidelines for aquatic life protection: chronic values of 430 g/L at 50 mg/L hardness and pH 7.5, scaling with hardness (25670 mg/L) and pH (5.88.4), while acute benchmarks reach 3600 g/L under similar conditions. British Columbia's guidelines propose chronic limits of 0.61.9 mg/L and acute limits of 0.63.9 mg/L, adjusted for hardness, to safeguard sensitive species like Daphnia and salmonids. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) establishes a secondary maximum contaminant level of 0.05 mg/L for manganese in drinking water to prevent aesthetic issues, alongside a lifetime health advisory of 0.3 mg/L, indirectly supporting ecological protection by limiting releases. Under the European Union's REACH regulation, MnO is registered for environmental risk assessment, with safety data sheets mandating avoidance of environmental release and classification as hazardous to aquatic life (H411: toxic to aquatic life with long-lasting effects). Canada's 2025 risk management scope proposes pollution prevention plans and potential regulations for manganese compounds to mitigate releases from industrial sources, emphasizing sediment and wastewater controls. The International Programme on Chemical Safety (IPCS) recommends guideline values of 0.2 mg/L for soft freshwater and 0.3 mg/L for marine environments to protect 95% of species.
