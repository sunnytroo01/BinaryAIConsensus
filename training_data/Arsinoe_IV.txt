 Arsinoe IV (Greek: ; c. 6863 BC 41 BC) was a Ptolemaic princess of Egypt, the youngest daughter of Ptolemy XII Auletes and half-sister to Cleopatra VII, Ptolemy XIII, and Ptolemy XIV. During the Alexandrian War of 4847 BC, she escaped Ptolemaic royal custody, took command of the Egyptian army after executing the general Achillas and appointing Ganymedes, and was proclaimed queen by Alexandrian forces opposing Julius Caesar's support for Cleopatra, briefly co-ruling with Ptolemy XIII before her defeat and capture. Pardoned by Caesar, she was displayed in his Roman triumph of 46 BC but spared execution and granted sanctuary at the Temple of Artemis in Ephesus, only to be murdered there in 41 BC on Cleopatra's urging during her alliance with Mark Antony. Her rivalry with Cleopatra exemplified the intra-dynastic strife that characterized the declining Ptolemaic regime amid Roman intervention.

Family Background

Parentage and Siblings

Arsinoe IV was the daughter of Ptolemy XII Auletes, who ruled Ptolemaic Egypt from 8058 BCE and again from 5551 BCE amid chronic instability marked by two exiles and mounting debts to Rome for his restorations. This precarious dependence on Roman patrons exacerbated internal family rivalries, as Ptolemy XII's children competed within a dynasty that emulated pharaonic traditions of endogamy to concentrate power and divine legitimacy among siblings and half-siblings.
Her mother is unknown, though Ptolemaic records and naming patterns suggest she differed from Cleopatra V Tryphaena, the likely mother of Ptolemy XII's elder offspring; the younger children, including Arsinoe, appear to stem from a separate union, possibly with a concubine, consistent with the dynasty's flexible matrimonial strategies to secure heirs. Arsinoe was thus probably a half-sister to Cleopatra VII and full sister to the brothers Ptolemy XIII and Ptolemy XIV, with Berenice IV as an elder half-sister who had earlier challenged their father's authority by usurping the throne during his 5855 BCE exile.

Estimated Birth Year

Scholarly estimates place the birth of Arsinoe IV between 68 and 63 BC, derived primarily from inferences about her maturity during the Alexandrian War of 4847 BC, when she demonstrated leadership in rallying supporters and commanding forces. Ancient historians such as Plutarch, in his Life of Caesar, and Cassius Dio, in Roman History, portray her as a youthful princess actively involved in court intrigue and military decisions, suggesting she was likely 15 to 20 years old at the time, though neither provides an explicit age or birth date. 
This range aligns with Ptolemaic family chronology, positioning Arsinoe as younger than Cleopatra VII (born 69 BC) but older than her brothers Ptolemy XIII and XIV, whose accessions in the early 50s BC imply Arsinoe's birth in the mid-60s BC as the most common scholarly consensus. Some reconstructions, emphasizing her "barely adolescent" status in contemporary analyses, favor a later date around 6361 BC to reconcile her reported ambition and tactical acumen with typical ages for such roles in Hellenistic courts.
The absence of direct epigraphic or documentary evidence from Ptolemaic records contributes to ongoing debate, as royal birth dates were often omitted or approximated in Greco-Roman historiography focused on political events rather than personal timelines. Claims of precise years, such as exactly 63 BC, lack primary support and stem from secondary extrapolations rather than verifiable data.

Ascension to Power

Proclamation as Co-Ruler

Following Ptolemy XII Auletes' death in early 51 BC, Cleopatra VII initially shared rule with her younger brother Ptolemy XIII Theos Philopator under the terms of their father's will, which mandated sibling co-regency to preserve Ptolemaic dynastic legitimacy. By 49 BC, however, Ptolemy XIII's regentschief eunuch Pothinus and general Achillashad marginalized Cleopatra, removing her name from official documents and forcing her exile after she assembled an independent army, reflecting internal factional tensions favoring a more insular Egyptian policy over her external alliances. Arsinoe IV, the youngest full sister of Cleopatra and Ptolemy XIII, remained in Alexandria under the control of Ptolemy XIII's faction during this period.
The arrival of Julius Caesar in Alexandria in July 48 BC, ostensibly to arbitrate the succession and secure Egypt's debts to Rome, escalated the crisis when he demanded Cleopatra's restoration, sparking the Alexandrian War. In response, Ptolemy XIII's advisors, seeking to counter Cleopatra's Roman support and rally domestic opposition, proclaimed Arsinoe IV as co-ruler and queen alongside Ptolemy XIII around mid-48 BC. This elevation, orchestrated by figures like the eunuch Ganymedes (Arsinoe's tutor), positioned her as a symbolic leader of the anti-Caesar, nationalist elements within the Ptolemaic regime, leveraging her royal blood to legitimize their resistance without altering Ptolemy XIII's nominal kingship.
The proclamation adhered to longstanding Ptolemaic conventions of sibling co-rule and divine kingship, which emphasized familial unity and Egyptian pharaonic traditions to secure priestly and popular endorsement, though direct temple oracles for Arsinoe are unattested. Unlike Cleopatra's claim, which relied on Roman military backing, Arsinoe's brief queenship represented the court faction's bid for indigenous legitimacy amid the war, as noted in Roman accounts that portray it as a desperate maneuver by Ptolemy's partisans. No extensive coinage bearing Arsinoe's name alongside Ptolemy XIII survives, likely due to the brevity and chaos of her elevation, but the act underscored the dynasty's reliance on internal propaganda over foreign intervention.

Role in the Alexandrian War

Escape from Alexandria and Alliances

In late 48 BC, following Julius Caesar's arrival in Alexandria and his support for Cleopatra VII, Arsinoe IV escaped from confinement in the royal palace with the aid of her tutor Ganymedes, a eunuch influential in Ptolemaic court circles. She joined the Ptolemaic army under commander Achillas, encamped outside the city, thereby positioning herself at the head of forces loyal to her brother Ptolemy XIII and opposed to the Roman-Cleopatra alliance.
Arsinoe quickly asserted authority by having herself proclaimed queen, forming a coalition with Egyptian military leaders and Ptolemaic loyalists who rejected Cleopatra's Roman-backed claim to the throne. This alliance enabled her forces to exert brief influence over parts of the Nile Delta, leveraging the army's position to contest Caesar's reinforcements attempting to cross via Pelusium.
The Bellum Alexandrinum, a contemporary account linked to Caesar's circle, details Arsinoe's independent command: amid tensions between Ganymedes and Achillas, she ordered Achillas's execution and installed Ganymedes as supreme commander, consolidating her leadership over the anti-Cleopatra Ptolemaic faction. Caesar's Commentarii de Bello Civili similarly notes her active role in directing operations against Roman positions, underscoring her strategic agency despite the inherent bias in these Roman-authored sources favoring Cleopatra's side.

Capture and Defeat

In early 47 BC, during the culminating phases of the Alexandrian War, the Ptolemaic forces loyal to Arsinoe IV and Ptolemy XIII mounted a desperate defense against Julius Caesar's legions, initially gaining ground by besieging his troops in Alexandria and disrupting their water supply through control of the canals. Ganymedes, Arsinoe's appointed commander after she orchestrated the execution of Achillas, leveraged the numerical superiority of Egyptian leviescomprising tens of thousands of local infantry and irregularsto pressure Caesar's smaller, more professional force of about 10,000 legionaries. However, this strategy faltered due to the inherent limitations of undisciplined native troops, who proved vulnerable to Roman tactical maneuvers, including rapid fortifications, well-digging to bypass water controls, and naval adaptations using smaller biremes to outflank larger Egyptian quinqueremes.
Caesar's arrival of reinforcements under Mithridates of Pergamum shifted the momentum, enabling a pursuit of Ptolemy XIII's main army toward the Nile Delta. In the Battle of the Nile, Roman and allied forces assaulted and overran the Egyptian camp despite its natural defenses of marshes and the river, routing the defenders in close-quarters fighting. Ptolemaic casualties were severe, with a "vast multitude" slain in the camp and during the rout, though exact figures remain unrecorded; Ptolemy XIII drowned amid the chaos when his overloaded flagship capsized during flight. Caesar's losses totaled approximately 400 legionaries in the broader engagements, underscoring the asymmetry in combat effectiveness between drilled Roman cohorts and the Ptolemaic reliance on massed but uncoordinated levies.
With organized resistance collapsing, Arsinoe IVestimated to have been 15 to 18 years old and actively proclaimed as a rival pharaoh to rally native supportsurrendered to Caesar, who took her into custody to neutralize her as a potential focal point for further unrest. This transfer to Roman control ended her brief tenure as a claimant, exposing the Ptolemaic strategy's core weakness: dependence on ethnically Egyptian forces lacking the cohesion and engineering prowess of Caesar's veterans, which proved decisive in both land assaults and riverine operations.

Captivity and Roman Triumph

Imprisonment Under Caesar

Following her capture by Roman forces in January 47 BCE during the final stages of the Alexandrian War, Arsinoe IV was detained under guard in Alexandria as a political hostage to neutralize her claim to the Ptolemaic throne. This occurred after Caesar's reinforcements, including the arrival of legions from Asia, enabled the breaking of the Egyptian siege and the decisive naval victory at the Nile Delta, where Ptolemy XIII perished and Arsinoe's allied commander Ganymedes surrendered her position. Unlike the eunuch Pothinus, whom Caesar ordered executed for his role in Pompey's murder, Arsinoe was not put to death, reflecting Caesar's calculated restraint toward Ptolemaic royals who posed ongoing risks to stability.
Arsinoe's youthlikely in her mid-teens at the time, given her estimated birth around 6863 BCEmay have influenced this decision, as executing a young princess risked inflaming Egyptian factions further amid Caesar's efforts to consolidate Cleopatra VII's rule alongside Ptolemy XIV. Transported to Rome later in 47 BCE, she remained imprisoned there under Roman oversight, serving as a symbol of Caesar's Egyptian victories without immediate punitive measures. No ancient accounts record personal interactions between Arsinoe and Caesar's inner circle or any attempts by her to forge alliances, underscoring her status as a contained threat rather than a rehabilitated figure.
Caesar's approach prioritized pragmatic control over vengeance, aiming to legitimize Cleopatra's regime by sidelining rivals like Arsinoe without the backlash of regicide, which had already destabilized the dynasty through the deaths of Ptolemy XII's other children. This hostage arrangement aligned with broader Roman strategy in client kingdoms, where live captives could deter rebellions or be leveraged diplomatically, though Arsinoe's utility diminished as Cleopatra's position solidified.

Participation in Caesar's Triumph (46 BC)

Arsinoe IV was paraded alive and in chains during the Egyptian portion of Julius Caesar's quadruple triumph in Rome on September 20-21, 46 BC, serving as a living emblem of Rome's subjugation of Ptolemaic Egypt following the Alexandrian War. Ancient accounts, including those of Appian and Plutarch, depict her procession amid lavish displays of Egyptian spoils, such as Nile-themed floats with model crocodiles and pyramids, underscoring the exoticism of the defeated dynasty rather than portraying her as a formidable adversary. This segment of the triumph highlighted Caesar's victory over Ptolemaic forces, with Arsinoe positioned among captives to symbolize the collapse of resistance centered on her brief queenship.
At the time, Arsinoe was estimated to be between 17 and 20 years old, based on scholarly reconstructions of her birth around 66-63 BC derived from Ptolemaic genealogical records and chronological alignments with her siblings' known ages. Unlike the Gallic leader Vercingetorix, who was ritually strangled in prison shortly after the same triumphal sequence, Arsinoe was not executed, reflecting Caesar's selective clemency toward royal exiles of Hellenistic lineage, whom Romans often treated as potential diplomatic assets rather than immediate threats requiring sacrificial closure.
The Roman crowd's response emphasized her youth and Ptolemaic regality over enmity, viewing her as an alluring, foreign princess whose humiliation evoked pity rather than fear, with reports of public sympathy influencing Caesar's decision to forgo her death and instead grant her sanctuary post-triumph. This reaction aligned with broader patterns in Roman triumphs, where female captives from eastern monarchies often garnered empathetic attention, distinguishing Arsinoe from more vilified barbarian leaders.

Exile in Ephesus

Conditions of Exile

Following Julius Caesar's quadruple triumph in Rome on 26 September 46 BC, Arsinoe IV was spared executionunlike many other captives such as Vercingetorixand instead banished to Ephesus in the Roman province of Asia Minor. This decision reflected Caesar's policy of selective clemency toward Ptolemaic royals, possibly to maintain favor with Cleopatra VII and Ptolemy XIV, her co-rulers in Egypt. Ephesus, a key Hellenistic city under Roman administration, placed her under indirect imperial oversight, though specific terms of surveillance or stipend remain unrecorded in surviving accounts.
Arsinoe took refuge in the Temple of Artemis, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, which granted asylum to fugitives and exiles as a sacred obligation. Cassius Dio notes that she was "maintained" there until 41 BC, suggesting the temple provided basic sustenance and protection, consistent with its role in sheltering political refugees amid the era's temple economies supported by donations and state grants. Her youthlikely in her late teens to early twenties, given a birth circa 6863 BCand gender further constrained her circumstances, confining her to priestly or supplicatory roles within the sanctuary's ritual isolation, where movement beyond the temenos (sacred precinct) would have violated asylum norms and invited Roman intervention.
No ancient sources document marriages, alliances, or offspring for Arsinoe during this roughly five-year period, distinguishing her static exile from Cleopatra's active diplomatic maneuvers in the eastern Mediterranean. This evidentiary gap aligns with the temple's emphasis on ritual purity over secular ties, effectively neutralizing her as a Ptolemaic claimant amid Rome's stabilizing influence over client kingdoms.

Potential Political Activity

While primary ancient sources provide no direct attestation of political initiatives undertaken by Arsinoe IV during her exile at the Temple of Artemis in Ephesus, her eventual execution there in 41 BC reflects a perception among Ptolemaic and Roman elites that she retained latent potential to challenge Cleopatra VII's authority. Dio Cassius records that Mark Antony ordered her death at Cleopatra's instigation, motivated by apprehension that Arsinoe, as a surviving member of the Ptolemaic royal line, "might aim at the kingdom." Plutarch similarly attributes the act to Cleopatra's fear of her sister's rival claims, noting Arsinoe's prior prominence in the Alexandrian War where she had been acclaimed queen by Egyptian forces opposing Julius Caesar's intervention.
This perceived threat likely drew from Arsinoe's earlier mobilization of nationalist sentiment against Roman-backed rule, as detailed in Dio Cassius's account of her 48 BC proclamation, which rallied troops and civilians under the banner of Ptolemaic legitimacy amid widespread resentment toward foreign dominance. Residual sympathies among anti-Roman Egyptians, who had viewed her as a symbol of indigenous resistance during the war, may have persisted, fostering speculation of covert influence even in sanctuary. However, no epigraphic, numismatic, or textual evidence documents ongoing agitation, alliances with Eastern Hellenistic courts such as those in Pontus or Bithynia, or coordination with Ptolemaic exiles.
Confined to priestly duties within the temenos, Arsinoe's operational scope was constrained by Roman oversight and the sacred asylum's protocols, which prioritized ritual over secular power. The consolidation of Roman control over Egypt following Caesar's victory eroded any viable networks of support, rendering active political revival improbable; ancient historians emphasize Cleopatra's diplomatic entrenchment with Rome as the decisive factor in neutralizing dynastic rivals, rather than documented plots from Ephesus. Thus, while her royal bloodline evoked caution, empirical assessment privileges the absence of corroborated activity, attributing her elimination more to precautionary realpolitik than substantiated intrigue.

Execution (41 BC)

Antony's Involvement and Cleopatra's Influence

In 41 BC, Mark Antony ordered the execution of Arsinoe IV at the urging of Cleopatra VII, who regarded her sister as a lingering threat to her sole control over the Ptolemaic throne and the position of her son Caesarion as heir. This act occurred amid Antony's deepening alliance with Cleopatra, forged during their meeting at Tarsus earlier that year, as he consolidated Roman influence in the East. Cassius Dio attributes the decision primarily to Antony's deference to Cleopatra, noting that despite perceptions of it stemming from his passion for her, the Roman public response was one of approval rather than reproach, viewing it as a pragmatic elimination of a foreign dynastic risk.
Cleopatra's insistence reflected entrenched Ptolemaic patterns of intra-family violence, where rulers routinely neutralized siblings to avert coups, as evidenced by prior instances such as Ptolemy II's orchestration of his brother's death and Arsinoe II's exile of rivals. Arsinoe's prior display in Caesar's 46 BC triumph had elevated her visibility as a potential claimant, amplifying Cleopatra's concerns, particularly as Antony's military preparations demanded unwavering Egyptian loyalty and resource supply without internal Ptolemaic discord.
Antony's acquiescence prioritized strategic imperatives over sanctuary protocols or personal sentiment; by endorsing Cleopatra's request, he ensured Egypt's alignment with triumviral objectives, including logistical support for the Parthian campaign launched in 36 BC, treating Ptolemaic succession as a Roman-stabilized asset rather than an internal affair. This deference underscored the alliance's causal dynamic, where Antony leveraged Cleopatra's local authority to secure broader imperial aims, unburdened by exceptional moral scruple toward Hellenistic norms of kin elimination.

Circumstances at the Temple of Artemis

In 41 BC, Mark Antony ordered the execution of Arsinoe IV while she resided under the protection of asylum at the Temple of Artemis in Ephesus. Plutarch recounts that Antony dispatched soldiers who forcibly removed her from the sanctuary and killed her on the temple steps, an act that directly violated the sacred right of refuge granted by the goddess. This location, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, underscored the sacrilege, as the temple served as a major cult center for Artemis, protector of suppliants.
Arsinoe, estimated to have been around 22 years old based on her birth circa 63 BC, met her end in this manner despite her prior pardon by Julius Caesar and subsequent exile to Ephesus following his triumph. Ancient accounts, including Plutarch's, emphasize the brutality of dragging a royal Ptolemaic figure from holy ground, yet they report no recorded protests from Ephesian priests, local populace, or Roman officials at the time, suggesting the political exigencies of Antony's eastern campaigns overshadowed potential outrage.
The execution deviated from prevailing Roman customs of honoring temple asylum, even in conquests, as evidenced by precedents like the sparing of suppliants in earlier campaigns; Antony's compliance here prioritized Cleopatra's insistence on eliminating a dynastic threat over such traditions. Later interpretations view this as a concession to secure Ptolemaic loyalty amid Antony's Parthian preparations, with the desecration's symbolismequating a sister's blood with profane ambitiondebated in modern historiography but unpunished in antiquity.

Tomb and Archaeological Identification

Discovery of the Octagon Tomb

The Octagon, an octagonal monument in the center of Ephesus, was initially uncovered during Austrian-led excavations starting in 1904, with early interpretations viewing it as a trophy monument rather than a tomb. Further investigations in 1929, directed by archaeologist Josef Keil, revealed a sarcophagus in the structure's burial chamber containing a well-preserved skeleton of a young female, estimated at the time to be aged 1518 years at death. 
In the early 1990s, Austrian archaeologist Hilke Thr of the Austrian Academy of Sciences conducted a re-excavation of the Octagon, recovering additional postcranial bones from the grave chamber and anteroom, which were associated with the cranium found decades earlier. Thr proposed that the remains belonged to Arsinoe IV, arguing that the monument's distinctive octagonal form and elaborate mausoleum-like architecture echoed Ptolemaic funerary styles from Egypt, while its prominent position overlooking the Temple of Artemissite of Arsinoe's execution in 41 BCsuggested a deliberate commemorative placement for the Ptolemaic princess. The initial osteological assessment of the skeleton's youth further aligned with Arsinoe's approximate age during her capture by Julius Caesar in 48 BC, positioning the Octagon as a potential exile-era burial honoring her status.

Forensic Analyses and Debates

In the early 2000s, osteological analyses of the skeletal remains excavated from the Octagon tomb in 1926 revealed a female individual aged approximately 20 to 25 years at death, with robust bone structure and dental wear consistent with a diet including gritty foods. These findings, led by Austrian forensic anthropologists Karl Groschmidt and Fabian Kanz, supported preliminary claims by archaeologist Hilke Thr that the tomb housed Arsino IV, given the structure's construction around 40 BC near the Temple of Artemis, aligning with ancient accounts of her execution site. However, the estimated age conflicted with historical chronologies placing Arsino's birth circa 68 BC and death in 41 BC, implying she was about 27 years oldprompting critics to highlight the imprecision of skeletal aging methods for young adults, which can vary by up to five years based on epiphyseal fusion and pubic symphysis changes.
Facial reconstruction efforts, including a 2009 forensic anthropology project by Caroline Wilkinson, interpreted the cranium's morphologysuch as moderate prognathism and nasal aperture widthas suggestive of partial sub-Saharan African ancestry, which some linked to potential non-Macedonian maternal heritage in the Ptolemaic line. Proponents argued this bolstered the Egyptian royal connection, but detractors contested the reconstruction's subjectivity, noting that such traits occur variably in Mediterranean populations and that environmental factors like nutrition influence cranial form, potentially introducing interpretive bias without genetic corroboration. Attempts to extract ancient DNA for mitochondrial haplotype analysis failed, attributed to postmortem degradation and prior conservation treatments with preservatives that contaminated samples, leaving molecular confirmation elusive.
Scholarly disputes emphasized empirical shortcomings: no inscriptions, Ptolemaic cartouches, or diagnostic grave goods identified the occupant, with the tomb's octagonal design paralleled in local Asia Minor heroa rather than uniquely evoking Alexandrian lighthouses as Thr proposed. Critics, including classicists reviewing the architectural chronology via coin finds, argued the structure's reuse or alternative attribution to Roman-era elites ignored broader epigraphic evidence of Ephesian burial practices, rendering the Arsino hypothesis circumstantial rather than probative. These debates underscored a reliance on associative locationproximity to the Templeover direct forensic or artefactual linkage, with age and ancestry interpretations remaining contested due to methodological limits in pre-genomic archaeology.

Recent Developments (2025 DNA Study)

In January 2025, an interdisciplinary team published a genetic and morphological analysis of the cranium excavated from the Octagon tomb in Ephesus, definitively excluding its identification as Arsino IV. The study, appearing in Scientific Reports, employed ancient DNA extraction from petrous bone samples, alongside computed tomography (CT) scans and osteological examination, to assess sex, age, and pathological features.
Genetic sequencing confirmed the individual was male, with an XY karyotype lacking evidence of sex chromosome aneuploidy, directly contradicting prior assumptions of a female based on incomplete 20th-century assessments. Morphological indicators, including cranial suture closure and dental development, dated the remains to an adolescent aged 1114 years at death, accompanied by developmental anomalies such as mandibular prognathism and facial asymmetry suggestive of endocrine disruptions, though not specifically Klinefelter syndrome. Radiocarbon dating placed the burial between 36 BCE and 205 CE, overlapping Arsino's presumed execution date of 41 BCE but rendering the sex mismatch conclusive grounds for rejection.
This analysis overturns the long-standing hypothesis linking the Octagon to Arsino, implying later reuse of the structurepossibly as a cenotaph or secondary interment siteor misattribution during initial excavations. The findings underscore methodological pitfalls in early 20th-century archaeology, where architectural parallels to Ptolemaic styles prompted speculative royal attributions without genetic corroboration, and highlight the necessity for molecular verification in contested ancient identifications. Consequently, Arsino's burial location remains unknown, with no empirically supported candidates emerging from current evidence.

Historiography and Legacy

Primary Ancient Sources

The principal primary sources on Arsinoe IV derive from Roman and Greek authors, primarily documenting her role in the Alexandrian War (4847 BC), her capture by Julius Caesar, her display in his triumph (46 BC), and her execution (41 BC). These texts, while valuable for chronological and event-based details, reflect the perspectives of Roman elites, often framing Ptolemaic figures like Arsinoe as chaotic adversaries to Roman order, with limited insight into Egyptian internal dynamics.
Julius Caesar's Commentarii de Bello Civili (Book 3) provides the most contemporaneous account of Arsinoe's activities during the Alexandrian War, describing her escape from Caesar's custody in Alexandria around October 48 BC, her assumption of command over Ptolemaic forces alongside Ganymedes, and her role in rallying opposition against Roman intervention, including the execution of the general Achillas. As a firsthand participant writing defensively to justify his Egyptian campaign to the Roman Senate, Caesar's narrative prioritizes military exigencies and portrays Arsinoe as an opportunistic leader exacerbating factional strife, though its empirical details on troop movements and sieges align with archaeological evidence of the conflict's intensity.
Plutarch's Life of Caesar (chapters 4849) corroborates Arsinoe's capture by Caesar's forces in 47 BC after the defeat of Ptolemaic resistance, her transport to Rome, and her participationshackled and in a gilded cageas a symbol of Egyptian subjugation during Caesar's quadruple triumph in 46 BC. In his Life of Antony (chapter 26), Plutarch recounts her later asylum in the Temple of Artemis at Ephesus and her execution there on Mark Antony's orders in 41 BC, at Cleopatra VII's insistence, noting the sacrilege of violating the sanctuary. Composed circa 100 AD from a synthesis of earlier Roman memoirs and oral traditions, Plutarch's accounts emphasize biographical moralismhighlighting Arsinoe's ambition and tragic endbut retain reliability for public events like triumphs, cross-verified by numismatic evidence of her brief queenship and inscriptions.
Cassius Dio's Roman History (Books 42 and 48) echoes these events, detailing in Book 42 Arsinoe's flight to lead anti-Caesarian forces and her eventual surrender, while Book 48 (24.3) specifies Antony's directive to kill her despite her sacred refuge, framing it as a concession to Cleopatra amid the Second Triumvirate's power struggles. Appian's Civil Wars (Book 5, chapter 9) similarly notes her supplication in a temple of Artemis (at Miletus or Ephesus) and Antony's assassins ending her life, portraying the act as part of Cleopatra's consolidation of Ptolemaic rule post-Philippi (42 BC). Both authors, writing in the 2nd3rd centuries AD, rely on lost intermediaries like Asinius Pollio but introduce annalistic biases favoring Roman agency, yet their convergence on the execution's timing and motivedated to early 41 BClends causal weight, supported by Antony's subsequent donations to Ephesian cults as potential atonement.
No surviving native Egyptian sources, such as Demotic papyri or temple inscriptions from Arsinoe's putative supporters, document her queenship or death, creating a evidentiary gap that privileges Roman-centric narratives and obscures potential legitimacy derived from Ptolemaic traditions of sibling rivalry and divine kingship. This lacuna underscores the sources' limitations in capturing causal factors like domestic Egyptian factionalism, reliant instead on indirect inferences from coinage bearing Arsinoe's name and titles circa 48 BC.

Interpretations of Her Role in Ptolemaic Politics

Scholars interpret Arsinoe IV's political role as that of a legitimate dynastic claimant whose actions adhered to Ptolemaic norms of contested sibling successions, where heirs vied aggressively for co-rulership without fixed primogeniture. As a daughter of Ptolemy XII Auletes, she positioned herself as an alternative to Cleopatra VII during the Alexandrian War of 48 BCE, escaping palace confinement to assume command of Ptolemaic forces after aligning with the faction led by Ptolemy XIII and general Achillas. Her acclamation as queen by army elements demonstrated initiative in exploiting the power vacuum, yet her reliance on advisors like Ganymedeswho assassinated Achillas to consolidate her positionrevealed underlying factional instability and her own military inexperience against Caesar's superior legions.
The rivalry with Cleopatra exemplified standard Ptolemaic court dynamics, characterized by philoi-driven factions rather than exceptional moral lapses, with Arsinoe backing the anti-Roman camp while Cleopatra secured Caesar's support. Historians note that Arsinoe's leadership provoked resentment among conservatives uncomfortable with a sole female commander absent a male consort, contributing to her faction's cohesion issues post-Ptolemy XIII's death in the Battle of the Nile in January 47 BCE. This contrast underscores Cleopatra's pragmatic edge in foreign alliances, though Arsinoe's bid mobilized native Egyptian resistance, highlighting her as an autonomous actor in dynastic intrigue beyond mere foil to her sister's narrative.
Arsinoe's legacy in Ptolemaic historiography illustrates the era's causal pivot toward Roman dominance, where internal ambitions faltered without external backing, rendering her queenship a fleeting episode in the dynasty's decline. Her defeat facilitated Cleopatra's restoration but exposed the limits of isolated Ptolemaic agency, emphasizing evidentiary demands for verifying such figures' impacts amid sparse ancient accounts like those of Caesar and Cassius Dio.
