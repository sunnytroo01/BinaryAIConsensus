Tisagenlecleucel

 Tisagenlecleucel, sold under the brand name Kymriah and formerly known by the developmental code CTL019, is a CD19-directed genetically modified autologous T-cell immunotherapy approved for the treatment of relapsed or refractory B-cell precursor acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) and certain lymphomas. Developed by Novartis Pharmaceuticals Corporation in collaboration with the University of Pennsylvania, it represents the first chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy to receive regulatory approval.
Tisagenlecleucel works by extracting a patient's own T cells, genetically engineering them ex vivo to express a CAR that targets the CD19 antigen on malignant B cells, and then infusing the modified cells back into the patient to elicit an immune response against cancer cells. The CAR construct incorporates signaling domains from 4-1BB and CD3 to activate T-cell proliferation, cytokine release, and cytotoxicity upon binding to CD19-expressing cells. This personalized approach has demonstrated high response rates in clinical trials, such as an 83% complete remission rate in pediatric and young adult patients with B-cell ALL in the ELIANA study.
The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) granted tisagenlecleucel its initial approval on August 30, 2017, for patients up to 25 years of age with B-cell precursor ALL that is refractory or in second or later relapse. Subsequent approvals expanded its use to adult patients with relapsed or refractory large B-cell lymphoma after two or more lines of systemic therapy in May 2018, and to adult patients with relapsed or refractory follicular lymphoma after two or more lines of systemic therapy under accelerated approval in May 2022. It is not indicated for primary central nervous system lymphoma. The therapy has also received approvals from the European Medicines Agency and other regulatory bodies worldwide.
Despite its efficacy, tisagenlecleucel carries significant risks, including cytokine release syndrome (CRS), neurological toxicities, and the potential for secondary T-cell malignancies, as highlighted in boxed warnings. Long-term data from pivotal trials, such as five-year follow-up showing 55% overall survival and sustained remissions in 44% of initial responders among pediatric ALL patients, underscore its transformative potential in oncology while emphasizing the need for specialized administration in certified centers.

Overview and Mechanism

Description

Tisagenlecleucel is a CD19-directed genetically modified autologous T cell immunotherapy, classified as a form of adoptive cell transfer in cancer treatment. It represents the first gene therapy approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA), marking a milestone in personalized immunotherapy approaches.
The composition of tisagenlecleucel involves the patient's own T cells, collected via leukapheresis and genetically modified ex vivo. These autologous T cells are transduced with a lentiviral vector that encodes a chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) specifically targeting the CD19 antigen, a protein expressed on B cells. The CAR incorporates a murine anti-CD19 single-chain variable fragment (scFv) for antigen recognition, a human CD8 hinge and transmembrane domain, a 4-1BB (CD137) costimulatory domain to enhance T cell persistence and activation, and a CD3 signaling domain for intracellular signal transduction.
As a CAR-T cell therapy, tisagenlecleucel exemplifies the use of engineered immune cells derived from the patient to provide a tailored therapeutic response against malignancy. The final product is a suspension of cryopreserved CAR-positive viable T cells, administered as a single infusion after manufacturing and quality control.

Mechanism of Action

Tisagenlecleucel is an autologous chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy that targets CD19 on B cells. The therapeutic process begins with the collection of a patient's peripheral blood mononuclear cells, primarily T cells, via leukapheresis. These T cells are isolated, activated, and genetically modified ex vivo using a lentiviral vector encoding the CAR transgene. The modified T cells are then expanded in culture to achieve the required dose and reinfused into the patient following lymphodepleting chemotherapy.
The CAR construct in tisagenlecleucel comprises an extracellular antigen-binding domain derived from a murine anti-CD19 single-chain variable fragment (scFv), a CD8 hinge and transmembrane domain for membrane anchoring, and intracellular signaling domains consisting of the CD3 chain for primary activation and the 4-1BB (CD137) costimulatory domain. Upon binding to CD19 on target cells, the CAR facilitates antigen-specific recognition without the need for major histocompatibility complex presentation. This interaction induces clustering of the CAR, leading to phosphorylation of immunoreceptor tyrosine-based activation motifs (ITAMs) in the CD3 domain by kinases such as Lck, which activates downstream pathways including MAPK/ERK for proliferation and NFAT for cytokine production. The 4-1BB domain provides additional costimulation, promoting anti-apoptotic signals (e.g., via Bcl-2 and Bcl-xL), enhanced cytokine secretion (such as IL-2 and IFN-), and sustained T-cell cytotoxicity through NF-B and PI3K/AKT signaling.
The activated tisagenlecleucel cells proliferate and exert cytotoxic effects on CD19-expressing cells, including malignant B cells, resulting in targeted tumor lysis and clearance of normal B cells. This selective depletion manifests as B-cell aplasia, a hallmark pharmacodynamic effect observable in peripheral blood and bone marrow, which correlates with clinical response and requires ongoing immunoglobulin replacement therapy. Compared to other CAR T therapies that incorporate the CD28 costimulatory domaincharacterized by rapid but transient expansion and higher exhaustionthe 4-1BB domain in tisagenlecleucel fosters central memory T-cell differentiation, longer persistence in vivo, and reduced tonic signaling, thereby supporting more durable antitumor activity.

Clinical Applications

Indications

Tisagenlecleucel is approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of patients up to 25 years of age with B-cell precursor acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) that is refractory or in second or later relapse. It is also indicated for adult patients with relapsed or refractory large B-cell lymphoma, including diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) not otherwise specified, high-grade B-cell lymphoma, primary mediastinal large B-cell lymphoma, and DLBCL arising from follicular lymphoma, after two or more lines of systemic therapy. The therapy is not indicated for primary central nervous system lymphoma. In May 2022, the FDA expanded approval to include adult patients with relapsed or refractory follicular lymphoma (grade 1, 2, or 3A) after two or more lines of systemic therapy, under accelerated approval based on overall response rate and durability of response.
The European Medicines Agency (EMA) has authorized tisagenlecleucel for similar indications, including pediatric and young adult patients up to 25 years of age with B-cell ALL that is refractory, in relapse after relapse, or in relapse after allogeneic stem cell transplantation. For adults, it is approved for relapsed or refractory DLBCL after at least two lines of systemic therapy and, since June 2022, for relapsed or refractory follicular lymphoma after two or more lines of systemic therapy.
Eligibility for tisagenlecleucel requires confirmation of CD19-positive malignancy, as the therapy targets the CD19 antigen on B cells. Patients must demonstrate adequate organ function, including hepatic, renal, and cardiac assessments, to tolerate lymphodepleting chemotherapy and infusion. Active infections must be resolved prior to treatment, and central nervous system involvement is generally limited to controlled or asymptomatic cases, with exclusion of active or primary CNS disease in lymphoma indications.
Tisagenlecleucel is under investigation in clinical trials for other CD19-positive B-cell malignancies, such as chronic lymphocytic leukemia and mantle cell lymphoma, though these uses remain off-label and unapproved.

Administration

Patient preparation for tisagenlecleucel infusion involves lymphodepleting chemotherapy to reduce endogenous lymphocytes and create an environment conducive to CAR-T cell expansion. For pediatric and young adult patients with B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (B-ALL) up to 25 years of age, the regimen consists of fludarabine 30 mg/m intravenously daily for 4 days and cyclophosphamide 500 mg/m intravenously daily for 2 days, typically administered starting 7 to 11 days prior to infusion to allow completion 2 to 14 days before the tisagenlecleucel administration. For adult patients with relapsed or refractory diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) or follicular lymphoma (FL), the standard lymphodepleting regimen is fludarabine 25 mg/m intravenously daily for 3 days and cyclophosphamide 250 mg/m intravenously daily for 3 days, or alternatively bendamustine 90 mg/m intravenously daily for 2 days; infusion occurs 2 to 11 days after completion for DLBCL or 2 to 6 days for FL. Lymphodepletion may be omitted if the patient's white blood cell count is below 1 10/L within one week prior to infusion.
Dosing of tisagenlecleucel is a single intravenous infusion tailored to the indication and patient characteristics, containing a specific range of chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-positive viable T cells. In pediatric and young adult B-ALL patients up to 25 years, the dose is 0.2 to 5.0 10 CAR-positive viable T cells per kg of body weight for patients weighing 50 kg or less, or 0.1 to 2.5 10 CAR-positive viable T cells for those over 50 kg. For adults with relapsed or refractory DLBCL or FL after two or more lines of systemic therapy, the dose is 0.6 to 6.0 10 CAR-positive viable T cells, independent of body weight or surface area. The product is supplied in one to three patient-specific cryopreserved infusion bags, with a total volume of 10 to 50 mL per bag, and must be confirmed for identity and viability prior to use.
The infusion process requires administration in a certified treatment center equipped with intensive care unit capabilities to manage potential complications. Patients receive premedication with acetaminophen and an H-antihistamine, such as diphenhydramine, approximately 30 to 60 minutes prior to infusion to mitigate infusion-related reactions, while prophylactic systemic corticosteroids are avoided. The thawed infusion bags are administered intravenously at a rate of 10 to 20 mL per minute, adjusted lower for smaller children or volumes, without using a leukocyte-depleting filter; the entire contents of each bag are infused, followed by rinsing with 10 to 30 mL of 0.9% sodium chloride. Infusion must begin within 30 minutes of thawing the product at 37C, and tocilizumab should be readily available on-site for potential cytokine release syndrome management.
Post-infusion care emphasizes close monitoring for early signs of cytokine release syndrome and neurological toxicities. Patients are monitored daily for at least one week following infusion and should remain within proximity of their healthcare facilityideally at a REMS-certified centerfor at least two additional weeks to facilitate rapid intervention if needed. Restrictions include avoiding driving or operating machinery for at least two weeks post-infusion due to risks of neurological events, as well as limiting exposure to crowds and refraining from live-virus vaccines during this period to reduce infection risks.

Safety and Adverse Effects

Common Adverse Effects

Tisagenlecleucel treatment frequently leads to hematologic toxicities, including anemia, neutropenia, and thrombocytopenia, due to the therapy's impact on bone marrow function and immune reconstitution. In patients with relapsed or refractory B-cell precursor acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL), these cytopenias occur in nearly all cases, with 100% of pediatric and young adult recipients experiencing neutropenia, anemia, and thrombocytopenia; grade 3 or higher events at day 28 post-infusion affect 40% for neutropenia and 27% for thrombocytopenia, typically resolving within weeks to months. Similar patterns of severe hematologic toxicities are observed in adults with diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL), where grade 3 or 4 neutropenia occurs in 82%, anemia in 59%, and thrombocytopenia in 56% of patients (with prolonged grade 3 or higher cytopenias at day 28 in 25%, 25%, and 39%, respectively).
Infections represent another prevalent adverse effect, arising from B-cell aplasia and resultant hypogammaglobulinemia that compromises humoral immunity. Infections of any grade occurred in 65% of pediatric and young adult ALL patients and 58% of adult DLBCL patients post-infusion, including pathogen-unspecified infections in 57% and 48%, respectively; viral infections affect 37% in the ALL cohort. Prophylactic measures, such as antimicrobial therapy, are often employed to mitigate this risk during the period of immunosuppression.
Neurological effects are commonly reported, manifesting as headache, mild encephalopathy, and fatigue in 20% to 40% of treated individuals. Headache occurs in 35% of pediatric and young adult ALL patients and 21% to 25% in adults with DLBCL or follicular lymphoma (FL), while encephalopathy affects 30% in the ALL group and 16% in DLBCL patients. These symptoms are generally transient and self-limiting, contributing to overall post-infusion monitoring needs.
Additional common adverse effects include fever, nausea, and hypotension, frequently as part of milder cytokine release syndrome that arises in most patients but remains manageable with supportive care. Fever is noted in 42% of pediatric and young adult ALL patients and 35% of adult DLBCL patients, nausea in 27% to 29% across indications, and hypotension in 25% to 29%. These events underscore the importance of close observation in the initial weeks following infusion.

Serious Risks and Management

One of the most serious risks associated with tisagenlecleucel therapy is cytokine release syndrome (CRS), a systemic inflammatory response triggered by rapid activation and proliferation of CAR T cells. CRS occurs in approximately 70-90% of patients treated for acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL), with incidence varying by disease type and grading system. It is graded using the American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy (ASTCT) consensus criteria, which assess fever, hypotension, and hypoxia, among other features. Grade 3 or 4 CRS, occurring in 10-20% of cases, can lead to life-threatening organ dysfunction and requires intensive care unit (ICU) admission. Management involves prompt supportive care, including fluid resuscitation and vasopressors for hypotension; for grade 2 or higher, the interleukin-6 receptor antagonist tocilizumab is administered intravenously (8 mg/kg for adults and children weighing more than 30 kg, 12 mg/kg for those under 30 kg), often repeated if needed. Corticosteroids, such as dexamethasone (10 mg/m IV every 6 hours), are added for grade 3 or 4 CRS or if tocilizumab is insufficient, with rapid resolution typically observed within hours to days. Prophylactic measures, such as preemptive tocilizumab in high-risk patients, have been explored to mitigate severity, though routine use is not standard.
Another critical complication is immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS), characterized by cerebral edema, confusion, aphasia, seizures, and impaired motor function, with an incidence of 20-40% across indications. In pivotal trials like ELIANA (pediatric ALL), any-grade ICANS occurred in 40% of patients, with grade 3 or higher in 13%; in JULIET (diffuse large B-cell lymphoma), it was 20% overall, with 9% severe. Symptoms often emerge within 7-10 days post-infusion, sometimes concurrent with or following CRS resolution. Diagnosis relies on ASTCT grading, incorporating the Immune Effector Cell-Associated Encephalopathy (ICE) score and clinical assessments like neuroimaging for elevated intracranial pressure. Initial management emphasizes supportive measures, including seizure prophylaxis with levetiracetam and monitoring in a specialized unit. For grade 2 or higher, high-dose corticosteroids (e.g., dexamethasone 10 mg/m IV every 6 hours) are the mainstay, tapered based on response. Refractory cases may require interleukin-1 blockade with anakinra (100-200 mg subcutaneously every 6-12 hours, adjusted for renal function), which has shown safety and efficacy in reducing steroid dependence without increasing infection risk.
Tisagenlecleucel carries a boxed warning for secondary malignancies, expanded by the FDA in April 2024 to include T-cell malignancies and other cancers following treatment. This update followed reports of 22 T-cell malignancies (including CAR-positive lymphomas) across approved CAR T therapies, with serious outcomes like hospitalization and death. The incidence is low, estimated at 0.1-1% based on post-marketing surveillance, representing a small fraction (about 0.15%) of adverse event reports among over 12,000 cases. Risk factors may include prior chemotherapy exposure, but causality with the CAR T construct remains under investigation. As of June 2025, the FDA removed REMS requirements for CAR T therapies, and 2025 analyses have found no evidence of a causal association with secondary T-cell malignancies, though the boxed warning persists and patients require lifelong monitoring, including serial blood counts, imaging, and flow cytometry to detect early signs of T-cell proliferation or malignancy. No specific preventive strategies exist beyond informed consent and vigilant follow-up.
Hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis/macrophage activation syndrome (HLH/MAS) is a rare but severe hyperinflammatory syndrome overlapping with CRS and ICANS, occurring in less than 5-6% of tisagenlecleucel-treated patients. In the ELIANA trial, it affected 6% of pediatric ALL cases, while JULIET reported 2% in lymphoma patients, often presenting with persistent fever, cytopenias, hyperferritinemia (>10,000 ng/mL), and organ failure within days of infusion. Diagnosis follows HLH-2004 criteria adapted for immune effector cell contexts, excluding infection mimics via bone marrow biopsy showing hemophagocytosis. Management mirrors severe CRS with tocilizumab and corticosteroids as first-line, escalating to etoposide or anakinra for refractory disease; however, no standardized CAR T-specific guidelines exist, and mortality can exceed 50% without rapid intervention.

Efficacy and Clinical Evidence

Pivotal Trials

The pivotal trials for tisagenlecleucel, a CD19-directed chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy, provided the primary evidence supporting its regulatory approvals for relapsed or refractory B-cell malignancies. These phase 2, single-arm studies evaluated efficacy in pediatric and adult populations with acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) and non-Hodgkin lymphoma, focusing on overall response rates (ORR) as key endpoints while addressing challenges like manufacturing success rates.
The ELIANA trial (NCT02435849) was a phase 2, single-cohort, multicenter, global study conducted across 25 centers, enrolling 92 patients with relapsed or refractory B-cell ALL, of whom 75 received a tisagenlecleucel infusion after lymphodepleting chemotherapy. Participants were pediatric and young adult patients (median age 11 years; range 3-23 years) who were at least 3 years old at screening, had at least 5% lymphoblasts in bone marrow, and expressed CD19 on malignant cells; exclusion criteria included prior anti-CD19 therapy or active central nervous system involvement. The primary endpoint was ORR (complete remission or CR with incomplete hematologic recovery) within 3 months of infusion, which was achieved in 81% of infused patients (95% CI, 71-89), with 60% attaining CR and all responders being minimal residual disease (MRD)-negative at a sensitivity of less than 0.01%. Event-free survival was 73% at 6 months and 50% at 12 months, with overall survival of 90% at 6 months and 76% at 12 months; a 3-year update reported a median event-free survival of 24 months. Limitations included manufacturing failures in 7 of 92 enrolled patients (8%), primarily due to poor cell growth or technical issues, leading to treatment ineligibility.
The JULIET trial (NCT02445248) was a phase 2, single-group, open-label, multicenter, international study enrolling 165 adults with relapsed or refractory diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) after at least two prior lines of therapy (including rituximab and anthracycline) and ineligibility for autologous stem cell transplantation, with 111 patients ultimately receiving tisagenlecleucel infusion. Inclusion required patients to be at least 18 years old and have histologically confirmed DLBCL not otherwise specified or primary mediastinal subtypes; exclusions encompassed prior CD19-directed therapy, active central nervous system disease, or prior allogeneic transplant. The primary endpoint was best ORR, which reached 52% (95% CI, 41-62) among 93 efficacy-evaluable infused patients, including 40% CR and 12% partial response. The median duration of response was not reached in the primary analysis (median follow-up 14 months), with relapse-free survival estimated at 65% at 12 months among responders; longer-term data at 40.3 months median follow-up confirmed durable responses in complete responders. Key limitations were a 33% rate of discontinuation before infusion due to disease progression, adverse events, or manufacturing issues, alongside a median time from enrollment to infusion of 54 days.00375-2/fulltext)
The ELARA trial (NCT03568461) was a phase 2, single-arm, multinational study enrolling 98 adults with relapsed or refractory follicular lymphoma after at least two prior systemic therapies or relapse after autologous stem cell transplant, with 97 patients receiving tisagenlecleucel. Participants had histologically confirmed grade 1-3a follicular lymphoma; specific inclusion and exclusion details emphasized prior treatment failure without active autoimmune disease or prior CAR T-cell therapy. The primary endpoint was CR rate, which was 68.1% (95% CI, 57.7-77.3), with an ORR of 86.2% (95% CI, 77.5-92.4) at a median follow-up of 29 months in the 4-year update. Median progression-free survival, duration of response, and overall survival were not reached, with estimated 24-month rates of 57.4%, 66.4%, and 87.7%, respectively, demonstrating durable responses particularly in high-risk subgroups. No manufacturing failure rates were highlighted as a limitation in primary reports, though the single-arm design precluded direct comparisons.

Real-World Outcomes

Real-world data from the Center for International Blood and Marrow Transplant Research (CIBMTR) registry indicate that tisagenlecleucel achieves an overall response rate (ORR) of 86% (complete remission or complete remission with incomplete hematologic recovery) in pediatric and young adult patients with relapsed/refractory B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (B-ALL), with a median follow-up of 32.1 months. In this cohort of 578 infused patients, 24-month relapse-free survival was 50.3%, and overall survival was 63.8%. Relapse rates are higher among older patients (aged 18 years), with 40.9% experiencing CD19-positive relapse compared to younger patients.
For relapsed/refractory diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL), an Australian multicenter registry analysis of 63 patients reported an ORR of 79.4% (60.3% complete response), with a median progression-free survival of 14.6 months (95% CI: 3.026.4) as of December 2024. In a larger US CIBMTR registry cohort of patients with aggressive B-cell lymphomas (n=968 efficacy-evaluable), tisagenlecleucel yielded a 59.5% ORR and 44.5% complete response rate, with median progression-free survival of 4.1 months and overall survival of 16.4 months (24-month rates: 28.4% and 43.6%, respectively). Safety profiles have improved in real-world settings due to increased clinical experience, with grade 3 cytokine release syndrome occurring in only 6% of cases and grade 3 immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome in 7.4%.
Long-term follow-up from the phase 2 ELARA trial's 4-year update in high-risk relapsed/refractory follicular lymphoma (efficacy-evaluable population) demonstrated durable responses, with 48-month progression-free survival of 50.2% overall and 66.1% among complete responders; median overall survival was not reached, with a 48-month rate of 79.3%. In B-ALL, real-world infection density post-infusion was 0.542 per 100 person-days at risk by day 100, associated with a low infection-related mortality of 0.2% but highlighting ongoing vulnerability in this population.
Outcomes with tisagenlecleucel are influenced by factors such as the number of prior lines of therapy (3 lines increasing infection risk; HR 1.86), pretreatment disease burden (5% blasts linked to inferior event-free and overall survival), and center experience, which correlates with reduced severe adverse events compared to pivotal trials. Real-world efficacy mirrors trial benchmarks, but manufacturing success rates have risen to approximately 96%, minimizing treatment delays.

Development and Regulation

Research History

Tisagenlecleucel originated from research in the laboratory of Carl H. June at the University of Pennsylvania, where his team pioneered the development of CD19-targeted chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy in the early 2010s. This work built upon foundational preclinical studies of CD19 CAR constructs in mouse models during the 2000s, which demonstrated potent antitumor activity against B-cell malignancies without cross-resistance to standard therapies. These early experiments established the feasibility of engineering patient-derived T cells to express CARs that specifically recognize and eliminate CD19-positive cancer cells, laying the groundwork for translation to human trials.
The first-in-human application occurred in 2010, when June's team treated three adult patients with relapsed or refractory chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) using autologous CD19 CAR T cells, resulting in complete remissions in two patients and partial remission in the third, with durable responses observed over subsequent years. These initial successes between 2010 and 2012 highlighted the therapy's potential but also revealed challenges, such as managing cytokine release syndrome, prompting refinements in dosing and supportive care. Building on this momentum, phase 1 trials expanded to pediatric and young adult patients with B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) starting in 2012 at the Children's Hospital of Philadelphia in collaboration with the University of Pennsylvania, where early data showed high rates of molecular remission and confirmed the therapy's feasibility in this population.
In August 2012, Novartis entered into an exclusive global licensing agreement with the University of Pennsylvania to advance the clinical development and commercialization of the CD19 CAR T-cell technology, including tisagenlecleucel (then known as CTL019). This partnership facilitated larger-scale trials and manufacturing improvements while leveraging Penn's expertise in cellular immunotherapy. The encouraging early clinical remissions also led to the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) granting orphan drug designation to tisagenlecleucel for the treatment of B-cell precursor ALL in patients up to 25 years of age in January 2014, providing incentives for further research into this rare condition.

Regulatory Approvals

Tisagenlecleucel, marketed as Kymriah, received breakthrough therapy designation from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in April 2017 for the treatment of relapsed or refractory diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL). The FDA granted accelerated approval for pediatric and young adult patients up to 25 years old with B-cell precursor acute lymphoblastic leukemia (B-ALL) that is refractory or in second or later relapse on August 30, 2017. On May 1, 2018, the FDA expanded approval to include adult patients with relapsed or refractory large B-cell lymphoma after two or more lines of systemic therapy. Accelerated approval was further extended on May 27, 2022, to adult patients with relapsed or refractory follicular lymphoma after two or more lines of systemic therapy, based on overall response rate and duration of response.
The European Medicines Agency (EMA) issued a marketing authorisation for tisagenlecleucel on August 23, 2018, for pediatric and young adult patients up to 25 years with relapsed or refractory B-ALL, as well as for adult patients with relapsed or refractory DLBCL after two or more lines of systemic therapy. This was expanded to adult patients with relapsed or refractory follicular lymphoma after two or more lines of systemic therapy under conditional marketing authorisation on May 4, 2022.
Approvals in other regions followed shortly after. Health Canada authorized tisagenlecleucel on September 5, 2018, for both B-ALL in patients up to 25 years and DLBCL in adults. The Therapeutic Goods Administration in Australia granted approval on December 19, 2018, for the B-ALL indication, with subsequent expansions. Japan's Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare approved it on March 26, 2019, as the first CAR-T therapy in Asia for both indications. The EMA's Paediatric Committee fulfilled the pediatric investigation plan for tisagenlecleucel on March 26, 2020, confirming compliance with requirements for pediatric development.
Post-approval measures include the FDA's Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy (REMS) program, initially implemented in 2017 to manage risks such as cytokine release syndrome and neurological toxicities, which was removed on June 26, 2025, following safety data reviews. In April 2024, the FDA updated the prescribing information with a boxed warning for secondary T-cell malignancies observed after treatment with CD19-directed CAR-T therapies, including tisagenlecleucel. As of the FDA's July 2025 update, no new indications have been approved.

Production and Pharmacology

Manufacturing Process

Tisagenlecleucel is an autologous chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy, requiring personalized manufacturing for each patient to ensure compatibility and efficacy. The process begins with leukapheresis, typically performed on Day 1 at the treatment site, where peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) are collected from the patient via a continuous-flow apheresis procedure lasting 3-6 hours. This step yields the starting material rich in T cells, which are critical for the therapy.
Following collection, the PBMCs are shipped to a centralized manufacturing facility, where T cells are isolated using magnetic bead-based selection to achieve high purity, typically exceeding 90%. These isolated T cells are then activated ex vivo using anti-CD3/CD28 beads or similar stimuli to promote proliferation and prepare them for genetic modification. This activation phase occurs within 24-48 hours post-leukapheresis.
On Days 3-4, the activated T cells undergo lentiviral transduction, where a replication-incompetent lentiviral vector encoding the anti-CD19 CAR transgene is introduced, resulting in CAR expression on approximately 20-40% of transduced cells. The transduced cells are then expanded in a bioreactor system under controlled conditions of temperature, pH, and nutrient supply, scaling up from millions to billions of cells over 10-14 days, reaching target doses of 0.2-5 10^6 CAR-positive viable T cells/kg (for pediatric/young adult B-cell ALL patients 50 kg), 0.1-2.5 10^8 (for B-cell ALL patients >50 kg), or 0.6-6.0 10^8 (for large B-cell lymphoma and follicular lymphoma).
Quality control assessments are conducted throughout and culminate before release, verifying cell viability (>80%), CAR expression levels, sterility, and potency via assays such as flow cytometry and vector copy number analysis (limited to <5 copies per cell to minimize insertional mutagenesis risks). Upon passing these checks, the final product is formulated in cryopreservative media, frozen in infusion bags, and shipped back to the clinical site under controlled conditions. The entire vein-to-vein timeline targets 22 days from apheresis to infusion, an improvement from the initial 3-5 weeks achieved through process optimizations like enhanced transduction efficiency.
Manufacturing occurs at Novartis-owned facilities, including those in Morris Plains, New Jersey (United States), Stein (Switzerland), Les Ulis (France), and Kobe, Japan, with capacity expansions implemented after 2017 to address growing demand and reduce wait times. These sites adhere to current good manufacturing practices (cGMP) and employ closed-system technologies to minimize contamination risks.
Despite advancements, challenges persist, including a manufacturing failure rate of approximately 1-4% as of recent commercial data due to issues like low cell yields or transduction inefficiencies, which have been mitigated through improvements in vector stability, automated bioreactor controls, and refined selection protocols to enhance reliability and scalability.

Pharmacokinetics

Tisagenlecleucel, a chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy targeting CD19, demonstrates distinct pharmacokinetic characteristics post-infusion due to its nature as autologous, genetically modified T cells that proliferate in vivo. Following intravenous administration, tisagenlecleucel undergoes rapid expansion, typically peaking 7-14 days after infusion, with maximum transgene levels (Cmax) measured by quantitative PCR (qPCR) at approximately 34,700 copies/g DNA in responders with pediatric B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (B-ALL). In these patients, expansion is characterized by a short doubling time of about 0.78 days and a fold expansion of up to 3,900, after which levels follow a bi-exponential decline. Persistence is a key metric of efficacy, with detectable transgene copies persisting beyond 1 year in roughly 50% of B-ALL responders and up to 2 years in blood and bone marrow; in adults with relapsed/refractory diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL), peak expansion is lower (Cmax 5,210 copies/g DNA in responders), but persistence can extend to 18 months.
Distribution of tisagenlecleucel occurs primarily in the blood and bone marrow, with rapid trafficking to tumor sites driven by CD19 expression on malignant B cells. In B-ALL patients, bone marrow partitioning starts at 44% of blood levels by day 28 post-infusion and increases to 67-69% by months 3-6, reflecting targeted migration and local proliferation. Widespread dissemination to lymphoid tissues is observed within hours, supporting systemic antitumor activity.
As living cells, tisagenlecleucel does not undergo formal metabolism or elimination like small-molecule drugs; instead, CAR-T cells persist through proliferation until host immune rejection, T-cell exhaustion, or loss of antigen stimulation leads to decline. The bi-exponential decay includes an initial phase (half-life 4.3 days) due to effector cell contraction and a terminal phase (half-life 16.8-220 days in responders) indicative of memory cell longevity, rendering traditional half-life metrics less applicable.
Pharmacokinetics vary by patient population, with higher expansion and shorter terminal half-life in pediatric/young adult B-ALL patients compared to adults with DLBCL, potentially due to differences in disease biology and immune microenvironment. Greater peak expansion correlates with improved overall response rates (ORR), as responders exhibit 74% higher Cmax and 104% higher area under the curve (AUC0-28d) than nonresponders in B-ALL. No significant effects on kinetics are seen from factors like pretreatment tumor burden, prior therapies, or age within pediatric cohorts, and interventions for cytokine release syndrome (e.g., tocilizumab) have no significant impact on expansion kinetics.

Society and Culture

Brand Names and Availability

Tisagenlecleucel is marketed under the brand name Kymriah by Novartis Pharmaceuticals Corporation. As a complex biologic therapy involving genetically modified autologous T cells, no generic or biosimilar versions are available.
Kymriah is available exclusively through a restricted distribution program, administered only at authorized treatment centers equipped to manage the infusion process and potential adverse reactions, such as cytokine release syndrome. In the United States, it is accessible at over 100 such centers, many of which are accredited by the Foundation for the Accreditation of Cellular Therapy (FACT). Globally, as of September 2025, Kymriah has been approved in 25 countries and is provided at approximately 240 certified treatment centers. In the European Union, availability has been supported by centralized authorization from the European Medicines Agency since 2018, enabling distribution across member states. In June 2025, the FDA removed the Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy (REMS) requirements for Kymriah, facilitating broader access while still limiting administration to certified centers. For regions without regulatory approval, Novartis offers access through managed access programs, including compassionate use initiatives, to eligible patients on a case-by-case basis.
The product is supplied in single-use, patient-specific infusion bags made of ethylene vinyl acetate (EVA) with polyvinyl chloride (PVC) tubing, typically containing 10 to 50 mL of cell suspension across one to three bags per dose, with a target of 1.2 10 to 6 10 CAR-positive viable T cells. Kymriah must be stored and transported frozen at -120C, such as in the vapor phase of liquid nitrogen within a temperature-monitored system, with a shelf life of 9 months from manufacture. After thawing at 37C, which takes approximately 3 to 5 minutes per bag, the product must be infused within 30 minutes while maintained at room temperature (20C to 25C) to preserve cell viability; it cannot be refrozen or stored post-thaw.

Cost and Access

Tisagenlecleucel, marketed as Kymriah, carries a high list price in the United States, set at $475,000 per treatment for pediatric and young adult patients with relapsed or refractory B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (B-ALL). For non-Hodgkin lymphoma indications, the price is lower at $373,000 per treatment. Novartis has implemented value-based agreements with payers, including the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS), tying reimbursement to patient outcomes such as sustained remission.
Reimbursement for tisagenlecleucel in the US became available through Medicare and Medicaid effective August 7, 2019, covering autologous CAR T-cell therapy for cancer when administered at Medicare-approved facilities. Private insurers provide variable coverage, often through case-by-case approvals or outcomes-based contracts, though initial rollout faced delays due to negotiation challenges. In the European Union, the list price is approximately 320,000 per treatment, subject to health technology assessment (HTA) evaluations by national bodies that influence reimbursement decisions.
Access to tisagenlecleucel is hindered by its elevated cost, which can result in treatment delays during prior authorization processes, and manufacturing timelines typically ranging from 3 to 4 weeks from apheresis to infusion, though this can vary. In low- and middle-income countries, availability remains severely restricted, primarily limited to clinical trial participation due to infrastructure and funding constraints in regions such as Southeast Asia, Africa, and Latin America. To address these barriers, Novartis has expanded initiatives like the KYMRIAH CARES patient support program in 2024 and 2025, offering financial assistance and access coordination for eligible uninsured or underinsured patients in the US.
Economic analyses indicate that tisagenlecleucel is cost-effective for pediatric B-ALL, with incremental cost-effectiveness ratios (ICERs) ranging from $50,000 to $100,000 per quality-adjusted life-year (QALY) gained compared to standard therapies, supporting its pricing through substantial long-term survival benefits. However, debates persist regarding its value for adult lymphoma indications, where higher ICERs and less pronounced QALY gains raise questions about affordability relative to outcomes.
