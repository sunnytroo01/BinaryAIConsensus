Patent Cooperation Treaty

 The Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) is an international treaty administered by the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) that enables inventors and companies to file a single international patent application, designating multiple countries for protection of an invention, thereby streamlining the global patent filing process and reducing initial costs. This unified procedure has the legal effect of a regular national filing in each of the PCT's Contracting States, allowing applicants to postpone decisions on entering specific national phases while benefiting from an international search and optional preliminary examination.
Adopted on June 19, 1970, in Washington, D.C., the PCT entered into force on January 24, 1978, initially with 18 Contracting States party to the Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property. It was amended in 1979 and further modified in 1984 and 2001 to adapt to evolving needs in international patent cooperation. As of November 2025, the PCT counts 158 Contracting States, representing about 90% of the world's population and 95% of global economic activity.
The PCT's core mechanism divides the process into an international phase (up to 30 or 31 months from the priority date) and subsequent national or regional phases. In the international phase, the application is filed at a receiving officetypically a national patent office, WIPO's International Bureau, or a regional office like the European Patent Officeand subjected to a mandatory international search by one of the designated International Searching Authorities, producing a report on prior art and a written opinion on patentability. Applicants may also request an optional international preliminary examination under Chapter II for a more detailed non-binding assessment. The application and search report are published 18 months after the priority date, enhancing global access to technical information. Following this, applicants enter the national phase in chosen jurisdictions to pursue examination and potential grant, where local laws apply; the PCT itself does not confer enforceable patent rights. This system has processed millions of applications since its inception, with over 3 million published by 2017, underscoring its role as a cornerstone of international intellectual property protection.

Overview

Purpose and Scope

The Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) is an international treaty administered by the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) that enables inventors and applicants to file a single international patent application, which has the same effect as national applications filed separately in each of the contracting states designated by the applicant. This unified filing procedure simplifies the initial steps for seeking patent protection across multiple jurisdictions, allowing applicants to delay decisions on specific countries until later in the process.
The core purpose of the PCT is to streamline international patent filings by providing a harmonized international phase that includes an international search report, an optional preliminary examination, and a means to defer national or regional phase entries, while also promoting the exchange of technical information among patent offices worldwide. It does not itself grant patents or conduct substantive examination of patentability; instead, it establishes a priority date for the invention and facilitates the transition to national or regional phases where designated offices perform the necessary reviews and issue patents according to their domestic laws. Developed in the 1960s to address the challenges of fragmented national patent systems, the PCT focuses on procedural efficiency rather than substantive rights.
The scope of the PCT extends to 158 contracting states as of November 2025, encompassing most major economies and representing 94.1% of global gross domestic product as of 2022. It applies to inventions across all technological fields that are eligible for patent protection under the national laws of the contracting states, though certain subject matter may be excluded in specific jurisdictions, such as plant varieties, animal breeds, or inventions related to nuclear processes in some states. The international phase of a PCT application typically lasts up to 30 or 31 months from the priority date, providing applicants with additional time to evaluate market potential and prepare for national filings without losing priority.

Key Benefits

The Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) offers significant cost savings for applicants by allowing a single international application to serve as the basis for protection in multiple contracting states, thereby avoiding the need for immediate separate national or regional filings with their associated fees and formalities. This unified approach includes one international filing fee and initially one set of translations, deferring substantial expenses such as national office fees (estimated at around 33% of total costs), translation costs (18-36%), and agent fees (25-50%) until later in the process.
A key advantage is the deferral of time-sensitive decisions, providing applicants up to 30 months from the priority date (or 31 months in certain cases) to enter the national or regional phase, during which they can assess market potential, refine the invention, secure funding, or test commercial viability without incurring full national prosecution expenses. This extended period, administered by the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO), allows for strategic planning and reduces the risk of premature commitments to unprofitable jurisdictions.
The PCT enhances global visibility through early publication of the international application, typically at 18 months from the priority date, accompanied by the international search report and made accessible via WIPO's PATENTSCOPE database. This publicity notifies competitors of the invention, deters potential infringement, and opens avenues for licensing agreements or partnerships by signaling the applicant's intent to seek broad protection.
Applicants gain access to expert prior art searches via the international search report (ISR) and written opinion prepared by qualified International Searching Authorities (ISAs), such as national patent offices, which provide an early, objective assessment of patentability and potential claims amendments before committing to costly national phases. This professional evaluation, delivered within 16 to 18 months of filing, helps identify weaknesses and informs decisions on whether to proceed, potentially avoiding wasted resources on non-viable inventions.
The system provides flexibility through the optional Chapter II international preliminary examination, which allows applicants to obtain a non-binding opinion on patentability to strengthen their application prior to national entry, and includes fee reductions for certain applicants, such as up to 90% discounts on international filing fees for nationals or residents of least-developed countries and other developing nations. These measures particularly support small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), individual inventors, and universities by lowering barriers to international patenting.
Strategically, the PCT establishes a uniform priority date across all designated contracting states, preserving the 12-month Paris Convention priority from the initial filing and enabling consistent enforcement against intervening third-party filings in multiple jurisdictions. This harmonized timeline simplifies portfolio management and bolsters defensive strategies globally.

Historical Development

Origins and Negotiation

In the 1960s, the rapid growth in international trade and technological invention highlighted the inefficiencies of filing separate patent applications in multiple countries, prompting calls for a streamlined international system. This need arose despite the foundational framework of the Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property (1883), which established priority rights under Article 4 but did not address the administrative burdens of multi-jurisdictional filings, such as duplicated efforts, high costs, and backlogs in patent offices. The proposal for reform gained momentum through the International Bureau of the United International Bureaux for the Protection of Intellectual Property (BIRPI), the predecessor to the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO), which advocated for a centralized mechanism to facilitate global patent protection.
The initiative for the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) originated from a resolution by the Executive Committee of the Paris Union in September 1966, proposed by the United States Government to mitigate issues like unjustified monopolies in countries without substantive patent examination. BIRPI played a pivotal role as the key initiator, developing a detailed plan for a treaty that would allow a single international application designating multiple states, accompanied by an international search report on prior art prepared by designated authorities. This plan was first presented to a Committee of Governmental Experts from 25 Paris Union member states in October 1967, where it underwent initial review and revisions to incorporate feedback on features like optional patentability certification and international publication within 18 to 24 months. Subsequent sessions in 1968, including working groups and another expert committee, refined the draft to emphasize cooperation without encroaching on national examination processes.
Preparations culminated in the Diplomatic Conference on the Patent Cooperation Treaty, held in Washington, D.C., from May 25 to June 19, 1970, at the invitation of the United States Government and organized by BIRPI. The conference involved representatives from 51 states, focusing on achieving a balance between international unity in filing and publication and the preservation of national sovereignty in granting patents. Core debates centered on ensuring the treaty would not delay national patent grants or impose a centralized examination that could undermine domestic procedures, leading to the rejection of proposals for a full international patent granting authority due to concerns over sovereignty. Instead, the final text emphasized an international phase for search, opinion, and publication, deferring substantive decisions to national or regional offices, thereby building directly on the Paris Convention's priority mechanism while addressing filing inefficiencies. The treaty was signed on June 19, 1970, and entered into force on January 24, 1978.

Adoption and Entry into Force

The Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) was concluded on June 19, 1970, in Washington, D.C., following a diplomatic conference convened by the United International Bureaux for the Protection of Intellectual Property (BIRPI), and remained open for signature by any state that was a party to the Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property until December 31, 1970. The treaty's adoption marked the culmination of international negotiations initiated in the 1960s to streamline multinational patent filings, with the final text establishing a unified procedure for international patent applications.
For the treaty to enter into force, Article 63 required the deposit of instruments of ratification or accession by at least eight states, including no fewer than four states that were parties to the Paris Convention and had received more than 40,000 patent applications in 1968 (indicating significant patent activity). This threshold was met when the eighth such instrument was deposited, triggering entry into force three months later on January 24, 1978, initially binding 13 contracting states, including key patent offices such as those of the United States and the United Kingdom. By the operational start date of June 1, 1978, the number of contracting states had grown to 18, enabling the receipt of the first international applications under the PCT.
Administration of the PCT was assigned to the International Bureau of the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO), which had assumed responsibilities from BIRPI upon WIPO's establishment on April 26, 1970, under the WIPO Convention. The PCT Assembly, comprising representatives from contracting states, promptly established the Regulations under the Treaty to govern procedural details, ensuring smooth implementation from the outset. Early milestones included the filing of the first international application on June 1, 1978, by U.S. resident Mrinmay Samanta for a method of producing high-purity glass, and a period of rapid expansion that saw the number of contracting states reach 100 with South Africa's accession effective March 16, 1999.

Amendments and Updates

The Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) has undergone several amendments and modifications since entering into force in 1978 to address evolving procedural needs and technological advancements. The treaty was first amended on September 28, 1979, primarily to refine administrative provisions, followed by modifications on February 3, 1984, which clarified international search procedures, including standardized time limits for search reports under Rule 42.1 and extensions for Article 19 amendments under Rules 46.1 and 46.3. These 1984 changes also simplified priority document handling and error rectification processes, such as allowing corrections for obvious errors in priority claims under Rule 4.10(b).
Further modifications on October 3, 2001, introduced options for electronic filing, offering a fee reduction of up to 200 Swiss francs to encourage digital submissions and streamline priority document transmission by receiving offices under Rule 4.10(c)(ii). In 2003, revisions to the Regulations enhanced the international preliminary examination process by introducing the international preliminary report on patentability under Rule 44bis and extending examination time limits under Rule 69.2, while also adjusting late furnishing fees to 25% of the international filing fee under Rule 12.3(e).
Subsequent updates in 2016 focused on procedural efficiencies, including simplified evidence requirements for priority restoration under Rule 26bis.3 and the introduction of "top-up" searches under Rule 66.1ter to incorporate post-search publications, alongside provisions treating epidemics as valid excuses for delays under Rule 82quater.1. The Assembly of the International Patent Cooperation Union, which approves such changes, emphasized digitalization between 2020 and 2024 through enhancements to electronic filing rules under Rule 89bis and pandemic-related time limit extensions notified by receiving offices to accommodate COVID-19 disruptions.
Recent developments include fee structure adjustments effective January 1, 2025, such as updated transmittal fees for certain receiving offices and continued reductions for digital filingsranging from 100 to 300 Swiss francs under the international filing feewhile maintaining 90% reductions for applicants from least developed countries. Amendments adopted by the PCT Assembly in July 2024 and entering into force on July 1, 2025, further support digital processes by modifying Rules 26 (language of applications), 33 (means of communication for search copies), 64 (preliminary examination procedures), 89bis (manner of filing), and 92 (correspondence).
These evolutions ensure the PCT's adaptability to technological progress, for instance, through ongoing updates to WIPO Standard ST.26, which standardizes electronic sequence listings for biotechnology inventions, allowing XML formats and multi-language support under Rules 5.2 and 13ter to handle complex nucleotide and amino acid disclosures efficiently.

Membership

Contracting States

The Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) currently has 158 contracting states, enabling applicants to seek patent protection in these jurisdictions through a single international application. These states encompass all 27 member states of the European Union (via designation of the European Patent Office), as well as major economies including the United States (joined 24 January 1978), China (1 January 1994), Japan (1 October 1978), and India (7 December 1998). Recent accessions include Uruguay, which deposited its instrument of accession on 7 October 2024, with the treaty entering into force for it on 7 January 2025.
A state becomes a contracting state to the PCT by depositing an instrument of ratification or accession with the Director General of the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) in Geneva. The treaty enters into force for the new state three months after the date of deposit, unless a different date is specified. Some contracting states participate through regional patent organizations, such as the African Regional Intellectual Property Organization (ARIPO), where member states like Kenya (26 June 1997) and Nigeria (4 October 2006) have acceded individually but benefit from regional designation options under the PCT.
The PCT applies to the entire territory of each contracting state upon entry into force, unless the state makes specific reservations excluding certain areas, such as overseas territories or dependencies. For instance, the Netherlands initially limited application to its European territory but extended it to Curaao and Sint Maarten following constitutional changes, with the PCT continuing to apply there without interruption. Similarly, France applies the PCT to metropolitan France and most overseas departments, with notifications for any territorial extensions published by WIPO.

International Searching and Examining Authorities

International Searching Authorities (ISAs) and International Preliminary Examining Authorities (IPEAs) are specialized patent offices appointed by the PCT Assembly to carry out the international search and, optionally, the preliminary examination of international applications. These authorities must be national patent offices of PCT Contracting States or intergovernmental organizations that possess the requisite infrastructure, expertise in patent searching and examination, and capacity to handle international work under standardized PCT procedures. The appointments are governed by Article 16 of the PCT for ISAs and Article 32 for IPEAs, ensuring high-quality outputs that assist applicants in assessing patentability before national phases.
As of 2025, 24 ISAs have been designated, including prominent national offices such as the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO), European Patent Office (EPO, acting as a regional authority), Japan Patent Office (JPO), China National Intellectual Property Administration (CNIPA), and Korean Intellectual Property Office (KIPO), alongside others like the patent offices of Australia, Brazil, Canada, India, Israel, and the Republic of Korea. Regional ISAs include the Eurasian Patent Office (EAPO) and Nordic Patent Institute (NPI). Each ISA specializes in certain languages (e.g., English, French, German, Japanese, or Spanish) and technical domains, allowing tailored searches based on the application's content. IPEAs, numbering 24 in 2025, are a subset of ISAs that additionally meet criteria for substantive examination, such as the EPO (available to most global applicants), USPTO, JPO, and CNIPA; not all ISAs qualify as IPEAs due to varying examination capabilities.
Applicants select an ISA when filing with a receiving Office, limited to those competent for that Office as specified in Annex C of the PCT Applicant's Guide; factors influencing choice include language alignment, technical expertise, search fees (ranging from approximately 1,000 to 3,000 USD, with reductions for small entities or nationals), and processing times. For example, non-European applicants may opt for the EPO due to its comprehensive coverage and English-language handling. Once the international search is completed, the same or a different competent IPEA can be chosen for preliminary examination under Chapter II.
These authorities operate under bilateral agreements with the International Bureau of WIPO, which outline operational standards, fee refunds, and quality controls to maintain uniformity. Notable examples include the EPO's expanded role as ISA for worldwide applicants since 1998, processing over 40% of PCT searches annually, and similar pacts with the USPTO and JPO to support efficient handling of applications from diverse origins.

International Phase Procedure

Filing the Application

The filing of an international application under the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) begins with submission to a competent Receiving Office (RO), which may be the national patent office or regional intellectual property organization of the applicant's country of residence or nationality, or the International Bureau (IB) of the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) acting as RO. Nationals or residents of any of the 158 PCT Contracting States may file directly with the IB if no national RO is available or for convenience. Diplomatic channels can also be used in exceptional cases, such as for applicants without direct access to an RO.
The application must meet specific formal requirements to establish an international filing date, including a standard request form (PCT/RO/101), a description of the invention sufficient to enable skilled persons to carry it out, one or more claims defining the matter for which protection is sought, an abstract summarizing the technical disclosure, and any necessary drawings. The application is filed in a single language accepted by the chosen RO, with common options including English, French, Arabic, Chinese, German, Japanese, Korean, Portuguese, Russian, and Spanish; no translation is required at filing unless specified by the RO. By submitting the PCT application, the applicant automatically designates all PCT Contracting States and any relevant regional arrangements for potential protection, without needing to list them individually.
A key feature of PCT filing is the ability to claim priority from one or more earlier applications filed in or for a PCT Contracting State or a Paris Convention member state, provided the PCT application is submitted within 12 months of the earliest priority date. This priority claim, governed by Article 8 of the PCT and Article 4 of the Paris Convention, preserves the earlier filing date for novelty purposes across designated states, allowing applicants to refine their invention details in the interim without losing rights. The claim is indicated in the request form, specifying the date, number, and filing office of the prior application.
Fees payable at filing include the international filing fee, set at 1,330 Swiss francs (approximately 1,530 USD as of November 2025), covering the basic processing by the IB; the international search fee, which varies by the elected International Searching Authority (ISA) and typically ranges from 1,000 to 2,500 Swiss francs (for example, 1,845 euros at the European Patent Office or 2,400 USD at the United States Patent and Trademark Office); and the transmittal fee, determined by the RO and often between 100 and 500 Swiss francs. These fees can be paid in equivalent currencies accepted by the RO or IB.
Electronic filing has become the preferred and, in many ROs, mandatory method since around 2020, facilitated through WIPO's ePCT system or national e-filing platforms, which ensures faster processing and compliance with digital standards. As of 2025, electronic submissions qualify for reductions in the international filing fee of 100, 200, or 300 Swiss francs depending on the format (e.g., full XML upload yields the maximum discount), with further incentives like waived paper-handling surcharges promoting digital adoption. Paper filings remain possible where permitted but incur higher costs and longer review times.
Special provisions offer significant fee relief to promote accessibility: applicants who are nationals of or residents in certain least developed or low-income countries (as listed in PCT Applicant's Guide Annex C) qualify for a 90% reduction in the international filing fee, applicable to individuals, micro-entities, small entities, universities, and non-profit research institutions meeting eligibility criteria. This reduction, aimed at supporting innovation in developing economies, requires a declaration at filing and does not apply to the search fee unless specified by the ISA.
Upon successful filing and payment, the RO forwards the application to the IB for recording and to the ISA for the subsequent international search, initiating the core examination of patentability.

International Search and Written Opinion

The international search and written opinion constitute a core mandatory component of the international phase under the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT), providing applicants with an early evaluation of their invention's potential patentability based on prior art. Following the filing of the international application, the designated International Searching Authority (ISA) performs a comprehensive prior art search to identify relevant documents that may impact the novelty and inventive step of the claimed invention. This process helps applicants assess the viability of pursuing national or regional phases and informs decisions on whether to amend claims or withdraw the application to avoid costs. The search is conducted in accordance with PCT Article 15 and Rules 42-44, utilizing international and national patent databases, non-patent literature, and other sources deemed relevant by the ISA.
The timeline for completing the international search and issuing the related documents is strictly regulated to ensure timely feedback. The ISA must establish and transmit the International Search Report (ISR) and the Written Opinion to the International Bureau (IB) no later than 16 months from the priority date or 9 months from the date of receipt of the international application by the receiving Office, whichever expires later; in practice, ISAs aim to complete this within these limits to facilitate publication. If the application claims priority from an earlier filing, the priority date serves as the reference point for calculating this period. Delays beyond the deadline may occur in exceptional cases, such as complex inventions, but quality oversight by the IB ensures adherence. For instance, the European Patent Office, acting as an ISA, targets completion within 9 months from filing to meet the 16-month overall deadline.
The search process involves the ISA examiner reviewing the international application, including claims, description, drawings, and abstract, to formulate search queries and retrieve pertinent prior art. The ISA identifies documents in categories such as those anticipating the claims (category X or Y) or making the invention obvious (category P), drawing from sources like the WIPO PATENTSCOPE database, national patent offices, and scientific literature. The resulting ISR, prepared on Form PCT/ISA/210, lists up to 250 relevant citations with brief explanations of their relevance to specific claims, including patent family information and non-documentary disclosures if applicable. It also includes a classification of the subject matter according to the International Patent Classification (IPC) or Cooperative Patent Classification (CPC). The IB performs quality control, reviewing a sample of ISRs for completeness and accuracy before transmission to the applicant and publication.
Accompanying the ISR, the Written Opinion of the ISA provides a preliminary, non-binding assessment of patentability under PCT Article 33 criteria, focusing on novelty, inventive step (non-obviousness), and industrial applicability. It evaluates whether the claims, as originally filed, appear to be novel (not anticipated by prior art under Article 33(2)), involve an inventive step (not obvious to a person skilled in the art under Article 33(3)), and are capable of industrial application (useful and manufacturable under Article 33(4)). The opinion is issued on Form PCT/ISA/237, stating reasons for any negative findings with references to cited documents, and it does not consider formal requirements like sufficiency of disclosure unless they directly affect patentability. This opinion serves as a basis for the subsequent optional international preliminary examination but is not appealable at this stage.
Applicants receive the ISR and Written Opinion via the IB, typically allowing them to gauge risks before entering national phases; however, no amendments to the application are permitted during this stage under Chapter I of the PCT. Instead, applicants may submit voluntary comments on the Written Opinion within 3 months of its receipt or 22 months from the priority date (whichever is earlier), which the ISA may consider for informational purposes in later proceedings but does not bind. If the initial search reveals gaps in coverage, such as language barriers, applicants can request a supplementary international search (SIS) from another designated Supplementary International Searching Authority (SISA) under PCT Rule 45bis, incurring an additional fee; the SIS report must be issued within 28 months from the priority date and supplements the original ISR without replacing it. This optional service enhances thoroughness, particularly for inventions with global prior art in non-English languages.
In recent developments, international patent offices including the EPO and USPTO have piloted AI-assisted tools for aspects of patent searching and examination in 2025, supporting efficiency in handling technical literature while maintaining human oversight.

Publication

The international publication of a Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) application represents a key milestone in the international phase, ensuring the timely disclosure of the invention to the public. Under Article 21 of the PCT, the International Bureau of the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) effects this publication promptly after the expiration of 18 months from the application's priority date (or the international filing date if no priority is claimed), unless the applicant has previously withdrawn the application. This timing balances the applicant's need for a period of confidentiality with the broader interest in making technical information available early, thereby establishing the publication date as the effective prior art date for the invention worldwide.
The content of the publication, as specified in PCT Rule 48.2, includes the complete international applicationcomprising the description, claims, any drawings, and the abstractalong with the international search report (ISR) and the written opinion of the International Searching Authority, provided these have been established by the time of publication. If amendments to the claims under Article 19 have been filed, a relevant statement indicating this is also included. Publications appear in WIPO's Official Gazette and are produced in one of ten designated languages of publication: Arabic, Chinese, English, French, German, Japanese, Korean, Portuguese, Russian, or Spanish. The choice of language follows the language in which the application was filed if it is one of these, or requires translation into one of them if filed in another language, with the applicant able to specify a preferred language of publication.
This publication serves essential purposes, including providing public notice of the filed invention to enable other innovators to assess novelty and avoid duplication, while also supporting defensive publication to prevent others from patenting similar ideas. Applicants may opt to withdraw the international application before publication under Rule 90 to preserve secrecy, but doing so forfeits the priority date and any associated benefits for refilings. All published PCT applications are made freely available online via WIPO's PATENTSCOPE database, which hosts over 5.3 million such documents as of 2025, facilitating global access and searchability.

International Preliminary Examination

The International Preliminary Examination is an optional procedure under Chapter II of the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) that provides applicants with a more in-depth assessment of their international application's patentability beyond the initial international search and written opinion. This examination is conducted by an International Preliminary Examining Authority (IPEA) and aims to offer a preliminary, non-binding opinion to guide applicants before entering national or regional phases. It builds on the written opinion issued during the international search phase, allowing applicants to address potential deficiencies early in the process.
To initiate the procedure, an applicant must file a demand for international preliminary examination separately from the initial PCT application, using the prescribed form (PCT/IPEA/401). The demand must be submitted to the competent IPEA within 22 months from the priority date or three months from the transmittal of the international search report and written opinion, whichever expires later. Applicants select an IPEA from those designated under the PCT, typically including the International Searching Authority (ISA) that handled the Chapter I phase or other qualified authorities such as the European Patent Office (EPO) or United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO). The demand includes payment of the necessary fees and, if required, a translation of the application into a language accepted by the chosen IPEA.
Once the demand is filed, the IPEA conducts a detailed examination, inviting the applicant to amend the claims, description, or drawings and to submit written observations on issues raised, including those from the written opinion. This interactive process may involve multiple rounds of communication and, in some cases, an interview with the examiner to clarify aspects of the invention. The examination concludes with the issuance of the International Preliminary Examination Report (IPER), which forms part of the International Preliminary Report on Patentability (IPRP Chapter II) and is transmitted to the applicant and the International Bureau within 28 months from the priority date.
The scope of the examination is limited to providing a preliminary opinion on whether the claimed invention appears to be novel, involve an inventive step (non-obviousness), and be industrially applicable, based on the amended claims and relevant prior art. It also addresses unity of invention and other formal requirements under PCT rules. The IPER is non-binding on national or regional patent offices but carries significant persuasive weight, as it represents a professional evaluation by an experienced authority.
Fees for the international preliminary examination include a preliminary examination fee set by the IPEAranging from approximately 1,000 to 2,000 USD depending on the authority, such as 1,915 EUR for the EPOand a handling fee of CHF 200 (approximately 230 USD as of November 2025) payable to the International Bureau. Reductions are available, including up to 90% for applicants from certain least-developed or developing countries meeting eligibility criteria under PCT Rule 57. Additional search fees may apply if the IPEA identifies inventions beyond the originally searched scope.
The procedure offers key advantages by enabling applicants to refine their application in response to examiner feedback, potentially increasing the likelihood of success in subsequent national phases while deferring full examination costs. It remains confidential between the applicant, IPEA, and International Bureau until the start of the national phase, providing strategic flexibility. As of 2025, the PCT-Patent Prosecution Highway (PCT-PPH) program facilitates expedited national examinations for green technologies by leveraging positive findings from the IPER, allowing faster processing in participating offices.

National and Regional Phases

Entry Requirements

Upon completion of the international phase, typically at the 30-month or 31-month deadline from the priority date, the applicant decides which designated Contracting States to enter for national or regional phase processing, as per PCT Articles 22 and 39. This transition requires fulfilling specific acts to maintain the application's validity in each selected jurisdiction, with failure to comply potentially leading to abandonment.
The core requirements for national phase entry include payment of the relevant national fees, submission of a translation of the international application into the official language of the office if the original language is not accepted, and provision of a copy of the international applicationthough the latter is often automatically furnished by the International Bureau to the designated office. Additionally, applicants must furnish the name and address of the inventor(s) if not already indicated in the international application, along with any required oath or declaration attesting to inventorship, and a power of attorney if represented by an agent, particularly for non-resident applicants. These elements ensure the application meets the formalities for substantive examination in the national or regional system, drawing on outputs from the international phase such as the international search report and written opinion.
All Contracting States are automatically designated upon filing the international application unless explicitly withdrawn, obligating the applicant to comply with entry formalities for protection in those territories under PCT Article 22(3) or 39(1). However, requirements vary significantly by office; for instance, the United States Patent and Trademark Office mandates an inventor's oath or declaration, which may be submitted with an application data sheet and can be postponed until examination. In contrast, the European Patent Office requires the claims to be filed in one of its official languages (English, French, or German), with translations of the description, claims, and drawings if the international application is not in those languages.
Entry costs typically encompass national filing and examination fees, translation expenses, and attorney fees, estimated at 5,000 to 20,000 USD per state depending on complexity and jurisdiction. As of 2025, over 80% of Contracting States mandate or strongly encourage digital submissions for national phase entry, facilitated by systems like WIPO's ePCT platform and the Digital Access Service (DAS) for secure document exchange, reducing paper-based processing.

Time Limits and Procedures

The standard time limit for entering the national phase of a PCT application is 30 months from the priority date, as established under Article 22(1) of the PCT. This deadline applies to most designated States, though certain Offices, such as the European Patent Office (EPO), extend it to 31 months from the priority date. A few other Offices may apply longer periods under their national laws, but deviations from the 30-month norm are limited and specified in the PCT Applicant's Guide for each jurisdiction.
Extensions beyond these time limits are rare and governed by Article 48 of the PCT, which permits designated Offices to excuse delays only if they result from unintentional errors or circumstances beyond the applicant's control, in accordance with Rule 82bis and applicable national law. Such relief requires a petition to the Office, typically within two months of the removal of the cause of delay or 12 months from the expiration of the time limit, whichever is earlier, and is not available as a routine extension mechanism.
The procedure for entering the national phase begins with the applicant fulfilling the specific requirements of each designated Office within the applicable time limit, including payment of national fees, submission of any required translations of the international application, and appointment of a local representative where mandated. The International Bureau transmits a copy of the international application to the designated Offices either upon the applicant's request or automatically if the Office requires it, after which the national or regional Office assumes responsibility for processing. No separate notification to the International Bureau of intent to enter the national phase is generally required, as designations are already recorded during the international phase; instead, the applicant deals directly with each Office to complete entry formalities.
Provisions for late entry vary by State but may include surcharges for limited extensions or restoration of rights in cases of unintentional delay. For example, in Japan, while the primary entry deadline is 30 months, reinstatement of rights is possible if a delay in submitting translations or appointing an agent is deemed unintentional, subject to a petition filed within two months of the impediment's removal or 12 months from the deadline and payment of a reinstatement fee of 212,100 Japanese yen. Similar mechanisms exist in other jurisdictions, such as the United States, where petitions to revive an abandoned application due to unintentional delay can be filed with fees scaled by time elapsed. However, late entry beyond these provisions typically results in permanent abandonment in that State.
Once entry is completed, prosecution proceeds independently before each national or regional Office, which conducts a full substantive examination under its own patent laws, including assessments of novelty, inventive step, and compliance with formal requirements. The applicant must respond to any office actions, such as requests for amendments or further information, directly with the Office, without centralized coordination through the International Bureau.
Designated Offices generally do not send reminders regarding the approaching national phase deadline, placing the onus on the applicant to monitor and comply. Failure to enter the national phase in a particular State leads to the international application being considered withdrawn with respect to that State under Article 22(3) or 39(3), resulting in abandonment and loss of rights therein, unless restoration is granted under applicable provisions.

Regional Phase Specifics

The regional phase under the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) allows applicants to enter a single procedure before a regional patent office, securing protection across multiple member states through one examination process, rather than separate national filings. Key regional organizations integrated with the PCT include the European Patent Office (EPO) under the European Patent Convention, the African Regional Intellectual Property Organization (ARIPO) under the Harare Protocol, and the African Intellectual Property Organization (OAPI) under the Bangui Agreement. These systems streamline protection in their respective territoriesEPO for 39 European states, ARIPO for 22 African states, OAPI for 17 African statesby centralizing filing, search, and examination. For GCC member states (Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates), no regional phase entry is available via the GCC Patent Office, which ceased accepting new unitary applications in 2021; applicants must instead enter the national phase separately in each state.
For the EPO, entry into the European regional phase occurs within 31 months from the priority date, requiring submission of the international application, any Article 19 amendments, a translation into English, French, or German if the application is not already in one of these languages, and payment of fees including the filing fee (135 EUR online or 285 EUR otherwise), designation fee (685 EUR), and examination fee (1,915 EUR, reducible if the EPO acted as International Searching Authority). The EPO conducts substantive examination, potentially including a supplementary European search if needed, leading to a European patent that must be validated in designated states via translation and fee payments where required. Since June 2023, granted European patents can opt for unitary effect, providing uniform protection across at least 18 participating EU member states without further national validations, simplifying post-grant enforcement and maintenance.
ARIPO's regional phase, covering 22 member states including Botswana, Ghana, Kenya, and newly acceded Mauritius effective August 2025, accepts English-language filings with entry at 31 months from the priority date. Applicants must pay a filing fee (232 USD online or 290 USD by paper) and designation fees (100 USD per state), followed by a substantive examination request within three years (1,000 USD fee), which ARIPO or designated national offices may conduct. Successful examination results in a regional patent automatically effective in designated states, with annual renewal fees escalating from 50 USD in the second year to 580 USD by the 20th year, offering cost efficiencies for multi-country coverage in Africa.
In the OAPI system, encompassing 17 French-speaking African states such as Cameroon, Senegal, and Gabon, entry into the regional phase is at 30 months from the priority date, with the international application filed in English or French and translated into French if necessary. Requirements include a power of attorney if the applicant is non-resident, filing fees (225,000 XAF for patents), publication fees (365,000 XAF), and priority claim fees (63,000 XAF per claim); an agent is mandatory for non-residents. Upon OAPI's approval following examination, the patent is automatically granted and enforceable in all 17 member states without additional national procedures, though annual maintenance fees apply from the second year (220,000 XAF for patents in years 2-5).
These regional phases provide significant advantages, including a unified examination avoiding redundant searches across jurisdictions and shared administrative fees; for instance, EPO entry typically costs about 10,000 EUR including translations and validations for broad coverage, far less than separate national applications in 20+ states. By 2025, enhancements like the EPO's unitary patent integration further simplify protection for over 18 EU states, promoting efficiency for inventors targeting diverse markets.

Patentability Criteria

Novelty and Inventive Step

Under the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT), novelty is a fundamental criterion for patentability, requiring that the claimed invention must not be anticipated by any prior art made available to the public anywhere in the world before the international filing date. Prior art encompasses any disclosure through written or oral description, use, or in any other way, as determined under the applicable national law of the International Searching Authority (ISA) for the purposes of the international preliminary examination per PCT Article 33(2). This establishes a global standard of absolute novelty, ensuring that the invention represents something new relative to existing knowledge at the time of filing.
Inventive step, also known as non-obviousness, requires that the claimed invention must not be obvious to a person skilled in the art in light of the prior art as of the international filing date, again applying the law of the ISA under PCT Article 33(3). While the PCT framework harmonizes the basic concept across Contracting States, the detailed interpretation and application of inventive step occur according to national or regional laws during the respective phases. For instance, in the United States, non-obviousness is evaluated using the Graham factors, which consider the scope and content of prior art, differences between the claimed invention and prior art, the level of ordinary skill, and secondary considerations such as commercial success.
In the international phase, novelty and inventive step are preliminarily assessed by the ISA through the Written Opinion accompanying the International Search Report (ISR), which cites relevant prior art and opines on whether the claims meet these criteria. If the applicant requests international preliminary examination, the International Preliminary Examining Authority (IPEA) conducts a more detailed review and issues the International Preliminary Examination Report (IPER), providing a non-binding opinion on patentability that aids decision-making in national phases. These assessments rely primarily on the prior art citations in the ISR, but applicants may submit arguments or amendments to distinguish their invention from the cited references.
Although the international phase applies absolute novelty without exceptions, many PCT Contracting States offer grace periods in their national laws to protect inventors from their own pre-filing disclosures, typically ranging from 6 to 12 months before the filing or priority date. These grace periods apply only during the national or regional phases and do not affect the international assessment, allowing disclosures by the inventor, applicant, or derived from them to be disregarded for novelty purposes if the application is filed within the specified timeframe.

Unity of Invention and Other Conditions

The requirement of unity of invention under the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) mandates that an international application relate to one invention only or to a group of inventions so linked as to form a single general inventive concept. This technical relationship among the inventions must involve one or more of the same or corresponding special technical features, meaning the inventions share a common purpose or solve the same technical problem. For instance, independent claims directed to different categories of inventions, such as a product and a method of manufacturing it, may satisfy unity if they are linked by a shared inventive concept. The International Searching Authority (ISA) assesses unity at the time of performing the international search, and if multiple groups lack unity, it invites the applicant to pay additional search fees for the extra inventions.
Industrial applicability, also known as utility, is a core patentability criterion in the PCT, requiring that the claimed invention be capable of industrial application, meaning it can be made or used in some kind of industry, including agriculture. Under PCT Article 33(4), this is evaluated according to its nature, and inventions lacking such applicabilitysuch as abstract ideas, mathematical methods as such, or certain methods of treatment of the human bodymay be excluded, though exclusions vary by national law. For example, in some Contracting States, methods of surgical or therapeutic treatment are not considered industrially applicable and thus unpatentable.
Sufficiency of disclosure ensures that the description of the invention in the international application discloses it in a manner sufficiently clear and complete for a person skilled in the art to carry it out without undue experimentation. This requirement, outlined in PCT Article 5, applies to the description, claims, and drawings, which must fully support the claims. In the United States, as a national phase consideration, the "best mode" requirement further mandates disclosure of the inventor's preferred embodiment known at the filing date, ensuring no concealment of the optimal way to practice the invention.
Other conditions under the PCT exclude certain subject matter from being regarded as inventions, such as discoveries, scientific theories, and mathematical methods as such, which cannot form the basis of patent claims unless they involve a technical application. National variations apply, particularly for software-related inventions; while the PCT itself does not exclude computer programs per se, patentability depends on whether they exhibit a technical effect or solve a technical problem in the designated states. These exclusions are assessed during the international search and preliminary examination, but final determination occurs in the national or regional phase.
If a lack of unity of invention is identified by the ISA or International Preliminary Examining Authority (IPEA), the applicant may pay additional fees under protest, allowing review of the decision; failure to pay results in restriction to the first claimed invention, with others considered withdrawn for international purposes. In the national phase, lack of unity may necessitate filing divisional applications to pursue separate inventions, subject to each office's rules on timing and fees. This assessment occurs primarily during the international search (PCT Article 17) and preliminary examination (PCT Article 34), ensuring efficient processing without prejudging national patentability.

Administration

Role of WIPO

The World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO), established by the WIPO Convention signed in 1967 and entering into force in 1970, assumed administrative responsibilities for the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) upon its entry into force on January 24, 1978. As a specialized agency of the United Nations since 1974, WIPO coordinates the international patent filing system under the PCT, enabling applicants to seek protection in multiple countries through a unified procedure.
WIPO's governance of the PCT is overseen by the PCT Assembly, comprising representatives from the contracting states, which convenes annually to approve regulations, budgets, and key appointments such as the Director General. This body ensures the treaty's effective implementation and adaptation to evolving needs, fostering international cooperation among patent offices.
In its policy role, WIPO promotes the harmonization of patent procedures across jurisdictions by issuing guidelines, such as the PCT Applicant's Guide, and facilitating treaty interpretations through its International Bureau, which serves as the operational arm. WIPO also provides essential support services, including the development of IT systems like ePCT for electronic filing and management, and PATENTSCOPE for global patent searching and analysis. Additionally, it offers training programs for Receiving Offices (ROs) and International Searching Authorities (ISAs) to enhance procedural efficiency and compliance.
Recent WIPO initiatives under the PCT emphasize the integration of artificial intelligence in patent searching, exemplified by enhancements to PATENTSCOPE's AI Index for identifying AI-related inventions, and alignment with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) through analysis of SDG-relevant PCT filings to support green technologies. These efforts, highlighted in the 2025 PCT Yearly Review, aim to leverage IP for innovation addressing global challenges like climate change.

International Bureau Functions

The International Bureau of the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) serves as the central administrative organ for the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT), executing key operational tasks to ensure the smooth processing and management of international patent applications. As part of its mandate, the Bureau performs specialized functions throughout the international phase, acting in multiple capacities to support applicants, receiving offices, international searching authorities (ISAs), and designated offices.
One primary function is serving as the Receiving Office (RO/IB) for applicants who are nationals or residents of PCT Contracting States but lack access to a national or regional receiving office, or in cases where direct filing is preferred. In this role, the International Bureau receives international applications, conducts preliminary formalities checks under PCT Rule 20, processes payments of fees such as the international filing fee, and issues official receipts to confirm submission. This enables streamlined entry into the PCT system for non-national applicants, ensuring compliance with treaty requirements before transmitting copies to relevant authorities.
The Bureau is also responsible for the publication and notification of international applications, which promotes transparency and global access to technical information. Under Article 21 of the PCT, it publishes each application approximately 18 months after the priority date (or earlier upon request), including the description, claims, drawings, and abstract, along with the international search report and written opinion once available. Additionally, the International Bureau disseminates copies of the published application and related documents to ISAs, international preliminary examining authorities (IPEAs), and designated or elected national offices, facilitating subsequent examination and notifications to applicants.
In terms of record-keeping, the International Bureau maintains the official international register of all PCT applications, serving as the authoritative repository for the record copy received from receiving offices per Article 12 and Rule 14. It handles administrative actions such as recording withdrawals of applications or designations (under Article 22(1) or Rule 90), processing changes in applicant details or ownership (Rule 92), and reviewing requests for amendment of claims under Article 19 to ensure procedural compliance before forwarding to the ISA for substantive review. This centralized registry ensures the integrity and traceability of application records throughout the international phase.
The International Bureau provides essential assistance to applicants and offices by responding to inquiries on PCT procedures, facilitating communications between parties involved in the process, and coordinating notifications such as entry into the international preliminary examination phase under Chapter II. It also manages fee reductions outlined in the Schedule of Fees, particularly for applicants from least developed countries or certain developing economies, applying reductions up to 90% on international filing and search fees to promote equitable access.
To modernize operations, the International Bureau oversees digital tools that enhance efficiency in PCT filings and document handling. The ePCT platform, administered by the Bureau, allows secure electronic filing, tracking, and management of international applications, supporting paperless submissions in multiple languages. Complementing this, the Digital Access Service (DAS) enables the electronic exchange and retrieval of priority documents from participating intellectual property offices, reducing paperwork and delays; applicants can request certified copies directly through the system upon providing access codes. These tools, integrated into the Bureau's infrastructure, streamline global patent cooperation while maintaining data security.

Usage Statistics

Annual Filings and Trends

The Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) has experienced steady expansion in international application filings since its operational start in 1978, when only 40 applications were submitted. By 2024, filings peaked at 273,900, with an average annual growth rate of 5% since 2000, as documented in WIPO's annual reviews. This growth reflects the treaty's increasing utility for global patent protection, with continued expansion amid the COVID-19 pandemic and a dip in 2023 before recovery in 2024.
Key trends include a marked shift to digital filing methods, with 95% of 2024 applications submitted electronically, enhancing efficiency and accessibility. Traditional manufacturing sectors have seen a relative decline amid broader economic shifts.
Additional metrics highlight the system's dynamics: Driving factors include rapid economic growth in Asia, which now accounts for 56.3% of global filings, and ongoing international harmonization efforts that encourage broader PCT adoption. The expansion to 158 contracting states has further supported this upward trajectory.

Top Applicants and Geographic Origins

In 2024, Huawei Technologies from China led as the top filer of international applications under the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT), submitting 6,600 applications, primarily in telecommunications and digital technologies. This marked the eighth consecutive year Huawei held the top position, underscoring the company's focus on global innovation protection. Samsung Electronics from the Republic of Korea ranked second with 4,640 applications, followed by Qualcomm Incorporated from the United States with 3,848, highlighting the dominance of technology corporations in high-volume PCT usage. LG Electronics from Korea also featured prominently among the top filers, with filings concentrated in consumer electronics and displays. Educational institutions have shown rising participation, with the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) in the United States emerging as a notable contributor among universities, reflecting growing academic involvement in international patenting. The share of women among inventors in published PCT applications reached 18% in 2024, up 0.2 percentage points from the previous year.
Geographically, Asia dominated PCT filings in 2024, accounting for 56.3% of the total 273,900 applications, driven largely by China, which originated approximately 70,160 applications or about 25.6% of the global total. Japan contributed around 17.7%, with strong showings in electronics and manufacturing, while the Republic of Korea added to Asia's lead through corporate-led filings in semiconductors and mobile technologies. Europe represented about 20% of origins, led by Germany and France, where automotive and pharmaceutical sectors fueled activity. The United States accounted for roughly 19.7%, with 54,087 applications focused on software and biotechnology. In contrast, regions such as Africa and Latin America together comprised less than 5%, indicating limited participation from these areas despite PCT membership.
