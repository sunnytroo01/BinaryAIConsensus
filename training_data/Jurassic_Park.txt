 Jurassic Park, later rebranded as Jurassic World, is an American science fiction adventure media franchise created by author Michael Crichton and produced primarily by Universal Pictures, revolving around the development and disastrous operation of a theme park featuring dinosaurs cloned from prehistoric DNA on the fictional Isla Nublar island off Costa Rica.
The franchise originated with Crichton's techno-thriller novel Jurassic Park, published in November 1990 by Alfred A. Knopf, which follows a team of genetic scientists who clone dinosaurs using ancient DNA extracted from mosquitoes preserved in amber, only for the park's security systems to fail, unleashing the creatures on visitors including a paleontologist, mathematician, and lawyer. The novel explores themes of bioethics, chaos theory, and the perils of unchecked scientific ambition, becoming a bestseller with over 416 pages and a cover price of $32.50.
In 1993, Universal Pictures adapted the novel into a blockbuster film directed by Steven Spielberg, starring Sam Neill, Laura Dern, Jeff Goldblum, and Richard Attenborough, which depicts experts touring the park in June 1993 before a tropical storm and sabotage over the weekend of June 1112 lead to deadly dinosaur attacks, including by Tyrannosaurus rex and Velociraptors. The film, co-written by Crichton and David Koepp, revolutionized visual effects with groundbreaking CGI dinosaurs created by Industrial Light & Magic, grossing $1,058,454,230 worldwide and earning three Academy Awards for sound, visual effects, and editing.
The franchise expanded with five sequels: The Lost World: Jurassic Park (1997), directed by Spielberg and grossing $618,638,999 worldwide, focusing on a second island expedition and corporate exploitation; Jurassic Park III (2001), directed by Joe Johnston and earning $365,900,000, involving a search for a missing boy on Isla Sorna; Jurassic World (2015), directed by Colin Trevorrow and achieving $1,671,063,641 worldwide as the franchise's highest-grosser, reopening the park with hybrid dinosaurs; Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom (2018), directed by J.A. Bayona and grossing $1,308,323,302, dealing with a volcanic eruption and dinosaur auctions; and Jurassic World: Dominion (2022), directed by Trevorrow and earning $1,004,004,592, uniting human and dinosaur worlds amid genetic threats. The most recent installment, Jurassic World Rebirth (2025), directed by Gareth Edwards, follows an extraction mission to the original facility and has grossed $868,613,830 worldwide. Cumulatively, the six main films have generated $6,895,204,381 in worldwide box office revenue as of November 2025, making it one of the highest-grossing franchises in cinematic history.
Beyond films, the franchise includes Crichton's sequel novel The Lost World (1995), comic books, video games, and extensive merchandising, but its most notable extensions are immersive theme park attractions at Universal destinations. Universal Orlando Resort features Jurassic World VelociCoaster, a high-speed roller coaster, Jurassic Park River Adventure, a water ride with animatronic dinosaurs, and the interactive Jurassic Park Discovery Center. Similar experiences exist at Universal Studios Hollywood with Jurassic World The Ride, a boat-based attraction showcasing a Mosasaurus feeding, and at Universal Studios Japan with Jurassic Park: The Ride. These attractions, operational since 1996 in various forms, have drawn millions by recreating the franchise's perilous dinosaur encounters.

Background

Premise and dinosaurs

The Jurassic Park franchise revolves around the scientific resurrection of dinosaurs via genetic engineering, as introduced in Michael Crichton's 1990 novel. The core premise involves recovering fragmented dinosaur DNA from the preserved blood of ancient mosquitoes trapped in amber, with incomplete sequences supplemented by DNA from contemporary species such as frogs to enable viable cloning and gestation in artificial eggs. This biotechnology, attributed to the fictional InGen corporation, aims to populate a theme park with living prehistoric creatures, underscoring themes of hubris in manipulating nature.
Central to the series are several iconic dinosaur species, each portrayed with behaviors and traits that blend paleo-inspired realism with dramatic embellishments. The Tyrannosaurus rex is depicted as the franchise's premier apex predator, a bipedal theropod reaching lengths of about 40 feet (12 meters) and heights of 20 feet (6 meters) at the hips, featuring a robust skull with 5060 serrated teeth and a bite force estimated at up to 12,800 pounds (57,000 newtons). In the lore, it exhibits territorial aggression, keen olfactory and auditory senses, and a reliance on motion detection for hunting, though real paleontology suggests it possessed advanced binocular vision and possibly some feathering absent in these scaly portrayals.
The Velociraptor, scaled up from the turkey-sized real species to human height for narrative impact, is shown as a cunning, pack-hunting carnivore about 6 feet (1.8 meters) tall and 13 feet (4 meters) long, with sickle-shaped claws and high intelligence enabling coordinated attacks and problem-solving. Franchise depictions emphasize their social hierarchy and vocal communication, diverging from actual Velociraptor mongoliensis fossils indicating smaller, feathered scavengers or opportunistic hunters from Late Cretaceous Asia. Triceratops, a quadrupedal herbivore armored with three facial horns and a large frill, measures up to 30 feet (9 meters) long and weighs around 612 tons, portrayed as defensive and herd-oriented, charging threats with its horns in a manner consistent with fossil evidence of intra-species combat but exaggerated for confrontation scenes. The Brachiosaurus, a towering sauropod exceeding 85 feet (26 meters) in length with an elongated neck for browsing high vegetation, is rendered as a docile giant, peacefully coexisting in enclosures and symbolizing the wonder of revival, though its vertical neck posture aligns more with modern sauropod interpretations than earlier horizontal models.
Dinosaur portrayals evolved across the franchise from relatively grounded 1993 film designs, informed by 1980s1990s paleontology like Jack Horner's work on behavior, to fantastical hybrids in later entries. The Indominus rex, introduced in Jurassic World (2015), represents this shift as an engineered super-predator, fusing Tyrannosaurus base genetics with Velociraptor for agility and intelligence, plus traits from cuttlefish for infrared camouflage, tree frogs for thermal regulation, and theropods like Carnotaurus and Giganotosaurus for osteoderm armor and sizereaching 50 feet (15 meters) long. Later, the Indoraptor in Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom (2018) combined Indominus traits with enhanced Velociraptor cunning for weaponized use. In Jurassic World Rebirth (2025), new mutations include the Distortus rex, a mutated Tyrannosaurus, and Mutadons, hybrid packs emphasizing environmental adaptation challenges. These hybrids highlight advanced genetic splicing for spectacle, portraying dinosaurs not just as revived relics but as malleable antagonists or engineered threats that challenge human control.
Throughout the series, dinosaurs serve as both awe-inspiring protagonistsevoking humanity's fascination with prehistoryand formidable antagonists, their behaviors driving conflicts that explore ethical boundaries in bioengineering, with hybrids amplifying risks of unintended mutations and ecological disruption.

InGen

International Genetic Technologies, Inc. (InGen) was a biotechnology firm founded in 1975 by entrepreneur John Hammond, who served as its first CEO and president, with initial operations based in San Diego, California. The company's headquarters were later established in a 200,000-square-foot facility at 100 Farallon Road in Palo Alto, California, facilitating research and administrative functions. Key executives included Hammond, the visionary leader behind the firm's ambitious projects, and Dr. Henry Wu, a geneticist recruited early on who rose to become InGen's chief geneticist, overseeing the development of advanced cloning techniques. InGen's corporate structure encompassed specialized divisions, such as InGen Construction for building facilities and InGen Security for operational protection, supporting its role as a leader in genetic engineering during the biotechnology boom of the late 20th century.
InGen pioneered genetic technologies centered on extracting dinosaur DNA from prehistoric mosquitoes preserved in amber, filling gaps with DNA from modern species like frogs to create viable embryos. This innovation underpinned Hammond's plan to develop Jurassic Park, a revolutionary theme park on Isla Nublar featuring live dinosaurs as attractions, intended to blend education and entertainment while generating substantial revenue. A critical component of these plans was Site B on Isla Sorna, acquired by InGen as a remote "factory floor" for cloning, incubating, and raising dinosaurs before transport to the main park, allowing large-scale production away from public scrutiny.
Following the catastrophic 1993 incident at Jurassic Park, which led to Hammond's death in 1997, InGen faced financial collapse and legal scrutiny, prompting a shift in leadership to Hammond's nephew, Peter Ludlow, who assumed the CEO role and pursued aggressive recovery strategies. Ludlow's tenure marked a fragmented phase, including expeditions to Isla Sorna to capture dinosaurs for a proposed San Diego-based park, but ended in further disasters that exacerbated the company's instability. In 1998, InGen was acquired by Masrani Global Corporation under Simon Masrani, who restructured it as a subsidiary to revive dinosaur-based ventures, culminating in the 2005 opening of Jurassic World on Isla Nublar using InGen's proprietary technologies. This evolution transformed InGen from a near-bankrupt innovator into a key asset within a multinational conglomerate, though ongoing incidents continued to challenge its operations through the franchise's later installments. Following the dissolution of Masrani Global after the 2015 incident and Biosyn's downfall in 2022, InGen's archived resources were acquired by Parker-Genix Pharmaceutical Engineering, which uses them for dinosaur-derived medical research in Jurassic World Rebirth (2025).

Biosyn

Biosyn is a fictional bioengineering corporation in the Jurassic Park franchise, positioned as a ruthless rival to InGen through its pursuit of genetic technologies for profit.
The company first appears in Michael Crichton's 1995 novel The Lost World, where it orchestrates corporate sabotage by attempting to steal InGen's dinosaur embryos from Isla Sorna, Site B. Biosyn's executive Lewis Dodgson recruits Dennis Nedry in the prior novel to facilitate the theft, aiming to reverse-engineer the dinosaur cloning process for competitive advantage. In The Lost World, Biosyn deploys a team, including paleontologist George Baselton, to capture live dinosaurs from the island for secretive research, highlighting its willingness to exploit InGen's failures without regard for ecological consequences.
Biosyn's role expands significantly in the 2022 film Jurassic World Dominion, where it establishes a sprawling sanctuary in the Dolomites mountain range of Italy to house captured dinosaurs from around the world. Under Dodgson's leadership, the facility serves as a facade for advanced genetic engineering, including the creation of giant locusts designed to devastate global agriculture and secure Biosyn's dominance in the food supply market. The company lures dinosaurs using thermal flares and maintains containment through an aerial deterrent system, but its operations prioritize corporate gain over safety.
Biosyn's unethical practices permeate its history in the franchise, encompassing industrial espionagesuch as the embryo heist plotand black-market activities like illegal dinosaur poaching and trafficking to fund its programs. Dodgson, portrayed as a callous opportunist, exemplifies the firm's disregard for regulations, using coercion and deception to acquire InGen's genetic assets post-bankruptcy. These actions contrast sharply with InGen's initial vision of a public theme park, instead channeling biotechnology toward monopolistic control.
Following the Biosyn Valley incident depicted in Jurassic World Dominion, the Italian government, in coordination with international bodies, seizes control of the sanctuary to transform it into a legitimate dinosaur preserve. This takeover integrates the facility into broader global efforts to manage and protect dinosaur populations, effectively dismantling Biosyn's operations and holding the company accountable for its violations.

Isla Nublar

Isla Nublar is a fictional volcanic island located in the North Pacific Ocean, approximately 120 miles (193 km) west of Costa Rica's mainland. In Michael Crichton's 1990 novel Jurassic Park, the island is described as a seamounta volcanic upthrust from the ocean floormeasuring about 8 miles (13 km) long and 3 miles (4.8 km) wide at its broadest point, encompassing roughly 22 square miles (57 km). This made it the largest private animal preserve in North America at the time, leased by International Genetic Incorporated (InGen) in the late 1980s to serve as the site for their groundbreaking dinosaur theme park. The island's rugged terrain includes dense jungles, rivers, mountains, and a central volcano, Mount Sibo, providing a natural barrier and diverse habitats for the cloned prehistoric species.
The Jurassic Park layout on Isla Nublar was designed as a self-contained resort with advanced infrastructure to showcase the dinosaurs safely. Central to the park was the Visitor Center, a large complex housing laboratories for genetic research, a restaurant, and an extraction theater for educational presentations. Guests toured via a monorail system that wound through the island, passing electrified enclosures such as the Tyrannosaurus rex paddocka 7-mile-long (11 km) perimeter fence enclosing a valley habitatand the Velociraptor pen, a forested containment area with observation towers. Additional facilities included utility sheds for maintenance, a control room for security monitoring, and jungle roads for staff vehicles, all integrated into the volcanic landscape to blend technology with the prehistoric theme. In the film adaptations, particularly Jurassic World (2015), the park expanded with attractions like the Gyrosphere valley and Main Street, a commercial hub, while retaining core elements like the monorail and updated enclosures.
Following the catastrophic security failure in 1993, as depicted in the original novel and film, Isla Nublar was abandoned, with surviving dinosaurs left to roam freely amid the ruins. The island remained dormant until InGen's successor efforts revived it as Jurassic World in 2005, only for another incident in 2015 to lead to mass evacuation and further decay. By 2018, in Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom, Mount Sibo's eruption threatened total annihilation, prompting a rescue operation that evacuated select dinosaurs while others perished in the pyroclastic flows and floods. The event released surviving specimens into the global ecosystem, setting the stage for widespread dinosaur integration into human habitats by the events of Jurassic World Dominion (2022), where Isla Nublar is referenced as the origin point for the escaped populations but no longer serves as an active sanctuary. Efforts to establish protected reserves for the dinosaurs shifted to continental locations, marking the island's transition from theme park to a symbol of ecological disruption.

Isla Sorna

Isla Sorna, also referred to as Site B, is a remote volcanic island situated off the western coast of Costa Rica, approximately 207 miles from the mainland, and serves as the sister island to Isla Nublar in the Las Cinco Muertes archipelago. It functioned as InGen's primary production facility for de-extinct dinosaurs, where cloning, incubation, and initial rearing occurred in specialized laboratories, nurseries, and enclosures before the animals were transported to other sites. The island's industrial infrastructure supported large-scale breeding operations, including automated systems for hatching eggs and monitoring juvenile growth.
Following the 1993 incident at Jurassic Park on Isla Nublar, Isla Sorna was abandoned after Hurricane Clarissa struck, severely damaging the facilities and power systems, which led to the release of dinosaurs into the wild. Without human intervention, the island's 22-square-mile terraincharacterized by dense jungles, rivers, and valleysevolved into a self-sustaining ecosystem where dinosaur populations formed herds, exhibited complex social behaviors, and engaged in natural predation dynamics, such as packs of carnivores hunting herbivores. This untamed environment became the subject of scientific expeditions studying prehistoric ecology in isolation.
In the Jurassic World era, surviving dinosaurs from Isla Sorna were captured and relocated to restock the theme park on Isla Nublar, providing foundational specimens for attractions like the Tyrannosaurus paddock and aquatic exhibits. By the time of the 2018 events in Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom, the island's dinosaur populations had been largely depleted through poaching and relocations, with remaining individuals evacuated as part of broader conservation efforts amid global threats to the species.

Novels

Jurassic Park (1990)

Jurassic Park is a science fiction novel written by Michael Crichton, first published by Alfred A. Knopf on November 20, 1990. The book quickly became a commercial success, debuting at number one on The New York Times bestseller list and selling over 1.5 million copies in its initial printing. Crichton, a trained physician known for blending scientific concepts with thriller elements, drew from his interest in biotechnology and paleontology to craft the story, which originated as a screenplay idea in the early 1980s before evolving into a full novel.
The plot centers on InGen, a biotechnology company led by eccentric entrepreneur John Hammond, which has successfully cloned dinosaurs using DNA extracted from ancient mosquitoes preserved in amber. Hammond constructs a theme park on the fictional Isla Nublar, off the coast of Costa Rica, featuring living dinosaurs as attractions. To garner expert endorsement before the park's opening, he invites paleontologist Dr. Alan Grant, his graduate student and paleobotanist Dr. Ellie Sattler, chaos theorist Dr. Ian Malcolm, and lawyer Donald Gennaro, along with Hammond's grandchildren, Tim and Lex Murphy. The narrative unfolds as a tropical storm hits the island, coinciding with a sabotage by InGen's disgruntled computer programmer Dennis Nedry, who disables security systems to steal dinosaur embryos for a rival firm. This leads to the escape of the Tyrannosaurus rex, which attacks the tour group, followed by chaos from packs of intelligent Velociraptors breaching enclosures and hunting survivors. Amid the escalating disaster, the characters confront ethical dilemmas about genetic engineering's risks, the limits of human control over nature, and the moral implications of resurrecting extinct species for profit, culminating in a desperate fight for survival and the park's ultimate collapse.
Key characters drive the novel's tension and philosophical debates. Dr. Alan Grant, a pragmatic dinosaur expert skeptical of the park's feasibility, evolves from reluctant visitor to protector of the children during the crisis. Dr. Ellie Sattler, intelligent and resourceful, applies her botanical knowledge to uncover flaws in the dinosaurs' engineered ecosystem, such as their unexpected ability to breed despite being designed as all-female. John Hammond embodies corporate ambition, viewing the park as a grand legacy but ignoring warnings about its dangers until it's too late. Dr. Ian Malcolm, the charismatic mathematician, serves as the voice of caution, repeatedly invoking chaos theory to argue that the park's complex systems are inherently uncontrollable. Dennis Nedry, the opportunistic programmer, provides comic relief early on but his greed precipitates the catastrophe.
The novel explores profound themes, particularly the hubris of scientists and corporations in tampering with nature without fully comprehending the consequences. Crichton critiques the unchecked pursuit of genetic innovation, portraying Hammond's vision as an arrogant bid to "play God" that disregards ecological balance and ethical boundaries. Central to this is chaos theory, articulated through Malcolm's lectures, which posits that certain deterministic systemsdespite following precise rulesexhibit unpredictable behavior due to extreme sensitivity to initial conditions, known as the butterfly effect. In the story, this manifests as small errors, like Nedry's shutdown or a minor storm, amplifying into total system failure, illustrating how life's complexity defies human prediction and control. Malcolm further explains fractal geometry as the mathematical framework underlying chaos, describing fractals as irregular, self-similar patterns with non-integer dimensions that model natural phenomena like coastlines or clouds, contrasting with traditional Euclidean geometry's smooth shapes. These concepts, drawn from real mathematical principles developed in the 1960s and 1970s, underscore the novel's warning that attempts to engineer biological systems overlook inherent unpredictability, encapsulated in Malcolm's refrain that "life finds a way." Each chapter preface features evolving fractal illustrations, reinforcing the theme visually.
The novel served as the foundation for the 1993 film adaptation directed by Steven Spielberg, with Crichton co-writing the screenplay.

The Lost World (1995)

The Lost World is the 1995 science fiction thriller novel by Michael Crichton, serving as the sequel to his 1990 bestseller Jurassic Park. Published on September 20, 1995, by Alfred A. Knopf, the book had an initial print run of two million copies and achieved sales of approximately 1.7 million units, topping the New York Times bestseller list for eight weeks. The title draws inspiration from Arthur Conan Doyle's 1912 adventure novel of the same name, which itself was influenced by early 20th-century expeditions to remote plateaus in South America, such as those exploring Venezuela's tepuis.
Set six years after the catastrophic failure of the Jurassic Park theme park on Isla Nublarwhich claimed the lives of several survivors, including key figures from the original incidentthe novel shifts focus to Isla Sorna, a neighboring island known as Site B. Following the death of InGen founder John Hammond, the company collapses into bankruptcy amid lawsuits and financial ruin, abandoning its dinosaur breeding facilities on Sorna where the creatures now thrive in a wild, unmanaged ecosystem. Hammond, before his death, dispatches paleontologist Richard Levine to document the dinosaurs' natural behaviors on Sorna, leading to an impromptu research expedition joined by chaos theorist Ian Malcolm, field biologist Sarah Harding, engineer Jack Thorne, and mechanic Eddie Carr, with two childrenKelly Curtis and Arby Bentonstowing away. Meanwhile, InGen's new CEO, Peter Ludlow (Hammond's nephew), mounts a rival corporate expedition to capture live dinosaurs for a new theme park in San Diego, hiring big-game hunter Roland Tembo to lead the effort. Complicating matters, Biosyna competing biotech firmsends industrial espionage expert Lewis Dodgson to steal dinosaur embryos, highlighting intercorporate rivalries. Central to the plot are the dynamics of a Tyrannosaurus rex family: a mated pair protects their injured infant, aggressively pursuing human intruders and destroying vehicles in retaliation, underscoring the dinosaurs' territorial instincts.
New characters expand the narrative beyond the original novel's survivors. Sarah Harding, a pioneering paleobiologist specializing in predator-prey relationships, serves as Malcolm's romantic interest and provides expert insights into dinosaur behaviors during the expedition. Peter Ludlow embodies corporate ambition, ruthlessly prioritizing profit over ethics as he seeks to salvage InGen by exploiting Sorna's resources. Roland Tembo, a seasoned African safari hunter, leads Ludlow's team with a code of professionalism but ultimately questions the morality of commodifying prehistoric life, capturing a T. rex as his personal trophy.
The novel delves into themes of corporate greed, portraying InGen and Biosyn as emblematic of unchecked biotechnology exploitation that disregards ecological consequences. It emphasizes ecosystem balance, illustrating how Sorna's dinosaurs have formed complex, self-sustaining food webs and social structures in the absence of human control, contrasting with the artificial containment of Isla Nublar. Crichton incorporates detailed paleontology debates, challenging preconceptions through evidence of dinosaur intelligencesuch as velociraptors demonstrating problem-solving and communicationand pack hunting strategies, where groups of raptors coordinate ambushes with strategic cunning, suggesting theropods operated in sophisticated social units rather than as solitary predators. These elements reinforce the book's cautionary exploration of chaos theory and the hubris of resurrecting extinct species without understanding their full behavioral repertoires.

Jurassic Park Adventures (20012002)

The Jurassic Park Adventures is a trilogy of original juvenile novels set in the Jurassic Park universe, authored by Scott Ciencin and published by Random House Books for Young Readers from 2001 to 2002. The series expands on the franchise's dinosaur-filled world through high-interest, low-reading-level stories designed to engage younger audiences with thrilling survival tales on Isla Sorna. Ciencin, a noted fantasy author specializing in scientifically accurate dinosaur fiction for children, crafted the books to blend adventure with educational elements.
The first book, Survivor (2001), follows 13-year-old Eric Kirby as he survives eight weeks alone on Isla Sorna after a parasailing accident strands him there, encountering various dinosaurs including Tyrannosaurus rex, Velociraptor, and Pteranodon; it serves as a prequel to the 2001 film Jurassic Park III by detailing Eric's backstory. Prey (2001) shifts to a group of teenagers who parachute onto the island to film a documentary, only to face deadly dinosaur threats like Compsognathus and Spinosaurus, emphasizing themes of peril and quick thinking. The concluding volume, Flyers (2002), reunites characters from the prior books, including Dr. Alan Grant, as they mount a rescue operation involving aerial pursuits and encounters with Pteranodon flocks. Each installment incorporates characters and dinosaur species from the Jurassic Park films while introducing original plots focused on youthful protagonists.
Aimed at readers ages 8 to 12, the series uses simplified language and fast-paced narratives to make the franchise accessible to elementary and middle-school children, often tying into the release of Jurassic Park III. Unique features include appended dinosaur fun facts, recommended websites for further learning, and six full-color punch-out dinosaur trading cards per book, enhancing the educational and collectible appeal. Reception has been generally positive among young readers for its excitement and dinosaur accuracy, though average Goodreads ratings hover around 3.3 out of 5 based on limited reviews, reflecting its niche as a promotional tie-in rather than a literary standout.

The Evolution of Claire (2018)

The Evolution of Claire is a young adult novel written by American author Tess Sharpe and published by Random House Books for Young Readers, an imprint of Penguin Random House, on June 26, 2018. The book serves as a prequel to the Jurassic World film trilogy, exploring the origins of Claire Dearing, a character portrayed by Bryce Dallas Howard in the movies as the park's ambitious operations manager. Set in 2004, the story follows Claire as a college sophomore interning at the under-construction Jurassic World theme park on Isla Nublar, less than a year before its grand opening.
In the narrative, Claire transitions from a part-time job at a local aquarium to an elite internship in Dr. Henry Wu's Genetics Division at InGen, where she assists with dinosaur research and care under the supervision of park visionary Simon Masrani. Her duties include monitoring enclosures and treating a wounded triceratops, but she soon encounters unsettling aspects of the operation, such as unusual dinosaur behaviors and restricted lab activities. Teaming up with fellow intern Justin, Claire investigates a mysterious incident that reveals deeper corporate secrets, thrusting her into perilous dinosaur encounters and forcing confrontations with park security. These events mark a pivotal shift in her perspective, highlighting the human cost of scientific ambition.
The novel delves into themes of corporate exploitation, portraying InGen's profit-driven approach to genetic engineering and animal welfare as ethically fraught, which awakens Claire to the moral complexities of commodifying living creatures. It also emphasizes personal growth, depicting Claire's evolution from an ambitious, rule-following young woman seeking excitement beyond her small-town roots to someone grappling with empathy and responsibility toward the dinosaurs. The story ties directly to the film canon by illustrating the formative experiences that shape Claire's pragmatic demeanor in Jurassic World, bridging her internship-era idealism with later cynicism.
Upon release, The Evolution of Claire achieved bestseller status on the New York Times young adult list and received praise for its character development and seamless integration with the Jurassic World universe, particularly its connections to Fallen Kingdom. Critics and readers commended Sharpe's first-person narrative for humanizing Claire and adding depth to the franchise's lore without relying on overt action sequences.

Maisie Lockwood Adventures (2022)

The Maisie Lockwood Adventures is a series of middle-grade novels set in the Jurassic World universe, following the adventures of Maisie Lockwooda young girl who is a human clonealongside her adoptive parents, Owen Grady and Claire Dearing, in a world where dinosaurs have integrated into human society following their release from captivity. Published by Random House in 2022 and aimed at readers ages 812, the series emphasizes themes of identity, human-dinosaur coexistence, friendship, and environmental protection through action-oriented stories that highlight Maisie's resourcefulness and moral courage.
Written by Tess Sharpe and illustrated by Chloe Dominique, the series consists of two books that depict everyday challenges and high-stakes rescues amid a dinosaur-populated landscape.
The first installment, Off the Grid, released on June 14, 2022, follows Maisie and her family as they travel across the United States in a van, evading threats while encountering poachers targeting vulnerable dinosaurs. Maisie, driven by her unique understanding of cloned life, teams up with a new friend named Violet to thwart the poachers and safeguard a group of dinosaurs, exploring her sense of belonging in a fractured world. The narrative underscores coexistence by portraying dinosaurs as both threats and allies, with Maisie learning to balance secrecy about her origins with acts of heroism.
In the second book, The Yosemite Six, published on September 27, 2022, the family tracks the velociraptor Blue to Yosemite National Park after discovering her abandoned tracker, leading to an extended stay where Maisie attends school for the first time and forms bonds with classmates, including a dinosaur enthusiast. The plot escalates as Maisie and her friends embark on a perilous quest to protect six young dinosaurs from a predatory threat in the park, blending schoolyard dynamics with wilderness survival and reinforcing themes of trust and collective responsibility in a shared ecosystem.
Throughout the series, Maisie's cloned heritagerevealed in Jurassic World Dominion as stemming from her genetic mother Charlotte Lockwoodserves as a subtle undercurrent, prompting reflections on what defines family and humanity amid evolving human-dinosaur relations.

Films

Jurassic Park (1993)

Jurassic Park is a 1993 American science fiction adventure film directed by Steven Spielberg and based on the 1990 novel of the same name by Michael Crichton. The story follows a group of experts invited to inspect a remote island theme park populated by cloned dinosaurs, where a tropical storm and industrial sabotage cause the park's security systems to fail, unleashing the prehistoric creatures and turning the visit into a desperate fight for survival. The narrative blends themes of scientific hubris and wonder with intense survival horror elements, as paleontologist Alan Grant, paleobotanist Ellie Sattler, and mathematician Ian Malcolm navigate attacks from velociraptors and a Tyrannosaurus rex while attempting to escape the island with Hammond's grandchildren.
The film itself does not display exact calendar dates on-screen; however, according to extended franchise canon timelines derived from tie-in sources and supplementary materials (such as the Jurassic Park Encyclopedia and timelines of the original trilogy), the events are set in June 1993 on Isla Nublar, with the main "Jurassic Park Incident" unfolding over the weekend of June 1112, 1993. Key events include: Early June (first week): A gatekeeper (Jophery Brown) is killed by a Velociraptor during a transfer, halting major construction. June 7: John Hammond invites Dr. Alan Grant and Dr. Ellie Sattler to the park. June 8: Dennis Nedry meets Lewis Dodgson to plan embryo theft. June 11 (Friday): The endorsement team (Grant, Sattler, Ian Malcolm, Donald Gennaro) arrives; lunch with Hammond; Lex and Tim Murphy arrive; tropical storm hits; Nedry sabotages systems; power outage; T. rex attacks (Gennaro and Nedry killed); group scatters. June 12 (Saturday): Ellie restores power; Robert Muldoon killed by raptors; further raptor attacks in visitor center; T. rex intervenes; survivors (Grant, Ellie, Malcolm, Lex, Tim) escape by helicopter.
Production began after Universal Pictures acquired the rights to Crichton's novel in 1989, with Spielberg attached as director in 1990. The film had a budget of $63 million, which was substantial for the era, and principal photography occurred from August to November 1992 in locations including Kauai, Hawaii, for the island exteriors, and various studios in California for interiors and controlled sequences. Spielberg collaborated with special effects pioneer Stan Winston Studio for the animatronic dinosaurs, which provided realistic close-up interactions, while Industrial Light & Magic (ILM) handled the groundbreaking computer-generated imagery (CGI) for full-body dinosaur shots and crowd simulations, marking a significant advancement in visual effects technology. Post-production extended into May 1993, with Spielberg overseeing edits remotely while preparing for Schindler's List.
The film premiered on June 11, 1993, and became a massive commercial success, grossing $1,104 million worldwide against its $63 million budget, making it the highest-grossing film of all time until 1997. It opened to $47 million in North America, setting records for the largest opening weekend at the time. Critically, Jurassic Park received widespread acclaim for its groundbreaking visual effects and suspenseful pacing, earning a 91% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 204 reviews, with an average score of 8/10. Spielberg employed restraint and a "less is more" approach in direction, delaying full dinosaur reveals and relying on subtle cues such as sounds, shadows, and environmental signs to build tension. A prime example is the T. rex attack scene, where the dinosaur's approach is shown through the trembling water in a cup on the dashboard, rippling with each heavy footstepa practical effect created by attaching a guitar string under the dashboard to vibrate the cupheightening fear through anticipation rather than immediate visual confirmation. Reviewers praised the seamless integration of practical and digital effects that brought the dinosaurs to life convincingly, with Roger Ebert noting the film's ability to evoke both awe and terror. At the 66th Academy Awards, it won three Oscars: Best Visual Effects, Best Sound Editing, and Best Sound, out of three nominations, highlighting its technical achievements.

The Lost World: Jurassic Park (1997)

The Lost World: Jurassic Park is the 1997 sequel to the 1993 film Jurassic Park, directed by Steven Spielberg and serving as the second installment in the franchise. Set four years after the events of the original, the story shifts focus to Isla Sorna, a neighboring island known as Site B, where InGen bred dinosaurs before their release on Isla Nublar. The narrative emphasizes corporate exploitation and survival amid escalating dangers, diverging from the first film's sense of wonder toward a darker action-adventure tone.
The plot centers on mathematician Ian Malcolm (Jeff Goldblum), who is recruited by John Hammond (Richard Attenborough) to lead an expedition to Isla Sorna to document the dinosaurs living in their natural habitat, as Hammond seeks to expose InGen's unethical plans. Malcolm's team, including paleontologist Sarah Harding (Julianne Moore), photographer Nick Van Owen (Vince Vaughn), and Malcolm's daughter Kelly (Vanessa Lee Chester), arrives on the island and encounters thriving ecosystems of dinosaurs, from herds of Stegosaurus to packs of Compsognathus. Jurassic Park III (2001)

Jurassic Park III is the third installment in the Jurassic Park film series, directed by Joe Johnston and released in 2001. The story centers on paleontologist Dr. Alan Grant, who returns from the previous films, as he is deceived by wealthy couple Paul and Amanda Kirby into joining an expedition to Isla Sorna, the island known as Site B. Their true motive is to rescue their son Eric and his girlfriend, who went missing after a parasailing accident near the island. The group, including Grant's assistant Billy Brennan and mercenary Udesky, flies over Sorna in a small plane, but it is attacked and crashes after encountering a massive Spinosaurus, forcing the survivors to navigate the dangerous terrain on foot.
As the group searches for Eric, they face relentless threats from the island's dinosaurs, including a harrowing escape from a Spinosaurus that kills several members and establishes itself as the dominant predator by defeating a Tyrannosaurus rex in a brutal confrontation. They stumble upon an abandoned aviary overrun by Pteranodons, where the flying reptiles capture Billy and Amanda, leading to a tense rescue sequence amid the foggy, enclosed structure filled with screeching pterosaurs. Eventually, the survivors reunite with the resourceful 12-year-old Eric, who has been evading dangers alone for days using scavenged supplies, and together they devise a plan to reach a barge on the island's coast while fending off pursuing raptors that demonstrate heightened intelligence by mimicking human sounds to lure prey. The film culminates in a chaotic river raft escape and a final Pteranodon assault at the water's edge, emphasizing themes of family reconciliation amid prehistoric peril.
Production on Jurassic Park III began under the direction of Joe Johnston, who replaced potential directors like Tim Burton and Peter Jackson, bringing his experience from films like Jumanji to helm the project with a focus on adventure and spectacle. The screenplay, credited to Peter Buchman, Alexander Payne, and Jim Taylor, underwent significant rewrites late in development, compressing the narrative into a tight 92-minute runtime to prioritize action over deeper character exploration. Visual effects were handled by Industrial Light & Magic (ILM), which created groundbreaking digital sequences for the Spinosaurus, including its dramatic plane crash attack and rex fight, using advanced motion capture and CGI to depict the creature's unique sail and aquatic traits; Stan Winston Studio contributed new animatronics for close-up interactions, addressing challenges in rendering the dinosaur's unprecedented scale and ferocity compared to prior T. rex designs. Filming took place primarily in Hawaii, with key locations including the lush Jurassic Kahili Ranch on Kauai for jungle scenes, Dillingham Airfield on Oahu for aerial sequences, and the north coast of Molokai for rugged terrain, while California studios handled interiors and additional effects work. The production faced logistical hurdles from Hawaii's remote sites and weather, but maintained a budget of $93 million, reflecting efficiencies from reusing series assets while innovating for new dinosaur behaviors.
The film premiered on July 18, 2001, distributed by Universal Pictures, and achieved commercial success by grossing $368.8 million worldwide against its $93 million budget, opening at $50.8 million domestically and benefiting from the franchise's established fanbase during a summer season dominated by action blockbusters.
Reception for Jurassic Park III was mixed, with critics praising its high-octane action sequences and thrilling dinosaur encounters, particularly the Spinosaurus's visceral presence, but often critiquing the thin plot, underdeveloped characters, and abbreviated runtime that sacrificed the wonder of the original films for relentless pacing. On Rotten Tomatoes, it holds a 49% approval rating based on 222 reviews, with the consensus noting it as "shorter, sharper, and louder" yet lacking depth. Metacritic scores it at 42 out of 100 from 30 critics, indicating average reviews, while audience scores are more favorable at 6.1 out of 10 on the site and 6/10 on IMDb from over 363,000 users, reflecting appreciation for its family-friendly thrills despite narrative shortcuts.

Jurassic World (2015)

''Jurassic World'' is a 2015 science fiction action film directed by Colin Trevorrow, serving as the fourth installment in the ''Jurassic Park'' franchise and the first in the ''Jurassic World'' trilogy. Set 22 years after the original ''Jurassic Park'', the story unfolds on Isla Nublar, where a fully operational dinosaur theme park called Jurassic World attracts millions of visitors annually. However, declining attendance prompts the park's management to unveil a new attraction: the Indominus rex, a genetically engineered hybrid dinosaur combining traits from Tyrannosaurus rex, Velociraptor, and other species, including cuttlefish for camouflage capabilities. When the Indominus rex escapes its enclosure due to its cunning intelligence, it embarks on a deadly rampage, forcing operations manager Claire Dearing (Bryce Dallas Howard) and velociraptor handler Owen Grady (Chris Pratt) to collaborate in containing the threat and evacuating park visitors, including Claire's visiting nephews, Zach and Gray. The chaos culminates in a high-stakes battle involving the park's resident Tyrannosaurus rex and Owen's trained raptors, ultimately subduing the hybrid and highlighting the perils of unchecked genetic innovation.
Production began in 2013 under Universal Pictures, with Trevorrow making his directorial debut on a major blockbuster after co-writing the screenplay with Derek Connolly, drawing inspiration from Michael Crichton's original novel while expanding on the theme park concept. The film's $215 million budget supported extensive practical and digital effects, including the creation of the Indominus rex through a combination of animatronics by Legacy Effects and CGI by Industrial Light & Magic. Principal photography occurred from April to November 2014, primarily at Kualoa Ranch on Oahu, Hawaii, for exterior jungle and park scenes, and at the abandoned Six Flags New Orleans site in Louisiana for the main street set, evoking the operational park envisioned in the franchise's lore. Steven Spielberg served as executive producer, ensuring ties to the original trilogy through subtle nods to characters like Dr. Henry Wu.
The film premiered on June 12, 2015, in the United States, distributed by Universal Pictures, and achieved unprecedented commercial success, grossing $1.67 billion worldwide and becoming the highest-grossing film of 2015 as well as the third-highest-grossing film of all time at that point. Its opening weekend alone earned $208.8 million domestically, surpassing previous franchise records and benefiting from strong international appeal, particularly in China where it earned over $228 million.
Critically, ''Jurassic World'' received mixed to positive reviews, praised as a thrilling blockbuster revival of the franchise with impressive visual effects and nostalgic elements, though some critiqued its formulaic plot and social commentary. It holds a 72% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 357 reviews, with an average score of 6.4/10, and a Metacritic score of 59/100 from 49 critics, indicating "mixed or average" reception. The film was shortlisted for the Academy Award for Best Visual Effects but did not receive a nomination, ultimately losing to Ex Machina; however, it garnered wins for visual effects at the Saturn Awards and Hollywood Film Awards, underscoring its technical achievements in dinosaur animation and destruction sequences.

Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom (2018)

Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom is a 2018 American science fiction action film directed by J.A. Bayona, serving as a direct sequel to Jurassic World (2015) and the fifth installment in the Jurassic Park franchise. The film shifts the narrative from the contained chaos of the island theme park to broader ethical dilemmas surrounding dinosaur extinction and human exploitation, introducing new themes of genetic manipulation and black-market auctions. With a production budget of $170 million, it emphasizes practical effects alongside visual effects to depict volcanic destruction and dinosaur interactions.
The plot unfolds three years after the destruction of the Jurassic World park on Isla Nublar. When the island's dormant Mount Sibo volcano shows signs of imminent eruption, former park operations manager Claire Dearing (Bryce Dallas Howard) leads a rescue mission to save the remaining dinosaurs from extinction. She recruits velociraptor trainer Owen Grady (Chris Pratt), paleoveterinarian Zia Rodriguez (Daniella Pineda), and systems analyst Franklin Webb (Justice Smith) for the operation, backed by the Dinosaur Protection Group. The team discovers that the expedition is funded by Benjamin Lockwood (James Cromwell), John Hammond's former partner, but uncovers a conspiracy involving Lockwood's estate manager Eli Mills (Rafe Spall), who plans to auction captured dinosaurs, including the hybrid Indoraptor, to the highest bidders on the mainland. As the volcano erupts, the group races to evacuate key dinosaurs like Blue the raptor amid chaos with brachiosaurs, ceratopsians, and carnivores stampeding toward the docks. The story culminates in pursuit sequences at the Lockwood estate in northern England, where the Indoraptora weaponized hybrid more intelligent and aggressive than the Indominus rexis unleashed, forcing ethical confrontations about releasing dinosaurs into the world.
Production began in February 2017 under director J.A. Bayona, a Spanish filmmaker known for horror-tinged dramas like The Orphanage (2007), marking his Hollywood debut. Filming occurred primarily in Hawaii, standing in for Isla Nublar, with key sequences shot at Kualoa Ranch on Oahu to recreate volcanic terrain and jungle environments. Additional location work took place in the United Kingdom, including Pinewood Studios for interiors and Cragside House in Northumberland for the Lockwood estate exteriors. To achieve realistic volcanic destruction, the production team employed practical effects, such as constructing a 40-foot roller coaster track for the gyrosphere cliff drop scene and using flammable kitty litter dispensed from nozzles to simulate flowing lava streams in the bunker sequence. These elements were blended with CGI from Industrial Light & Magic for dinosaur animations and environmental enhancements, emphasizing Bayona's vision of a darker, more suspenseful tone.
The film premiered internationally on June 6, 2018, in the United Kingdom, and was widely released in the United States on June 22, 2018, by Universal Pictures. It achieved significant commercial success, grossing $1.310 billion worldwide against its $170 million budget, making it the third-highest-grossing film of 2018 and the sixth in the franchise. Opening weekend earnings reached $148 million domestically, driven by strong international markets like China and the UK.
Critical reception was mixed, with praise for the visuals and action sequences but criticism of the plot's logical inconsistencies and underdeveloped characters. On Rotten Tomatoes, it holds a 47% approval rating based on 430 reviews, with an average score of 5.4/10, while Metacritic assigns a 51/100 from 59 critics, indicating "mixed or average" reviews. Critics acclaimed the cinematography by scar Faura and practical effects, particularly the volcanic opening and Indoraptor designs, for heightening tension, though some noted the story's reliance on franchise tropes overshadowed ethical themes.

Jurassic World Dominion (2022)

Jurassic World Dominion is a 2022 American science fiction action film directed by Colin Trevorrow from a screenplay he co-wrote with Emily Carmichael. It serves as the sequel to Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom (2018) and the sixth installment in the Jurassic Park franchise, marking the conclusion of the Jurassic World trilogy while reuniting the original trilogy's cast with the newer ensemble. The film explores the global integration of dinosaurs into human society four years after the events of Fallen Kingdom, where dinosaurs were released onto the mainland.
The plot centers on two interwoven storylines amid a world where dinosaurs coexist uneasily with humans, disrupting ecosystems and daily lifesuch as pterosaurs nesting on urban structures and herbivores blocking highways. Owen Grady (Chris Pratt) and Claire Dearing (Bryce Dallas Howard), now raising the cloned teenager Maisie Lockwood (Isabella Sermon) and protecting the orphaned velociraptor Beta (the asexually reproduced daughter of Blue), pursue Maisie after her kidnapping by Biosyn operatives in Malta's dinosaur black market. Meanwhile, paleontologists Ellie Sattler (Laura Dern) and Alan Grant (Sam Neill), with mathematician Ian Malcolm (Jeff Goldblum), investigate Biosyn Geneticsa rival company led by CEO Lewis Dodgson (Campbell Scott)for engineering giant, genetically modified locusts that devastate crops and threaten global food security. The narratives converge at Biosyn's remote valley headquarters in the Dolomites, where the heroes uncover Maisie's cloning origins tied to the locust plague, leading to a confrontation that dismantles the insects through reversed genetic engineering and culminates in a T. rex versus Giganotosaurus showdown, affirming dinosaur-human coexistence.
Production began with Trevorrow's return as director, building on his work from the 2015 Jurassic World, with principal photography spanning February to November 2020 despite COVID-19 delays. The film had an estimated production budget of $165 million, emphasizing extensive CGI for the locust swarms and dinosaur sequences, alongside practical effects for select creatures. Filming occurred primarily in Canada (British Columbia locations like Merritt for rural homestead scenes) and the United Kingdom (Pinewood Studios and outdoor sites in England for Biosyn valley and urban integrations), with additional shoots in Malta for the black market sequences.
Jurassic World Dominion was released theatrically by Universal Pictures on June 10, 2022, in various formats including IMAX and 3D. It grossed over $1 billion worldwide, with $376.8 million from North America and $625.1 million internationally, making it the third-highest-grossing film of 2022 despite pandemic recovery challenges.
Critical reception was mixed to negative, with reviewers divided on the film's ambitious global scope and franchise-culminating nostalgia. Metacritic scores it at 38 out of 100 from 60 critics, averaging around 3 out of 5 stars, praising the legacy cast reunion and dinosaur visuals while criticizing the overstuffed plot, underdeveloped locust threat, and reliance on fan service over fresh ideas.

Battle at Big Rock is a 2019 American live-action short film in the Jurassic World franchise, serving as the first canonical short to depict dinosaurs living freely on the North American mainland. Directed by Colin Trevorrow and written by Trevorrow and Emily Carmichael, the 10-minute film explores the immediate aftermath of events that allowed dinosaurs to escape into the wild, focusing on human-dinosaur interactions in a realistic, survival-horror context. Produced by Frank Marshall and Patrick Crowley, with visual effects supervised by David Vickery at Industrial Light & Magic, it premiered on September 15, 2019, simultaneously on FX and YouTube, garnering millions of views shortly after release.
Set in 2018, one year after the incidents in Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom, the story centers on a blended family of fourparents Dennis and Mariana, their young daughter Kadasha, and infant son Mateoembarking on a camping trip in the fictional Big Rock National Park in Northern California. While gathered around their campfire, the family encounters a mother Nasutoceratops and her calf, but the peaceful moment shatters when an adult Allosaurus attacks the young dinosaur. Caught in the ensuing chaos as the Nasutoceratops mother fights back, the family must fight for survival amid the battle, highlighting the dangers of coexisting with escaped prehistoric creatures. The film also briefly features other dinosaurs such as Compsognathus, Stegosaurus, Parasaurolophus, Pteranodon, and a Mosasaurus in news footage, establishing the broader ecological impact. This narrative marks the first major onscreen confrontation between humans and dinosaurs on the mainland, portraying a grounded, intimate incident rather than large-scale spectacle.
In production, Trevorrow aimed to expand the franchise's universe by showing everyday people facing dinosaur threats, drawing inspiration from real-world wildlife encounters to emphasize tension over action. Filmed in a concise format to fit promotional windows, the short utilized practical effects alongside CGI for authentic creature designs, introducing Allosaurus and Nasutoceratops as new species to the series. Its release strategy targeted both television and online platforms to reach a wide audience, aligning with Universal Pictures' marketing for upcoming Jurassic World installments.
As canon material, Battle at Big Rock bridges the timeline post-Fallen Kingdom, illustrating the spread of dinosaurs across the continental United States and setting the stage for global coexistence themes in later entries like Jurassic World Dominion. It underscores the franchise's shift toward exploring societal adaptation to dinosaurs in the wild, influencing subsequent stories about mainland ecosystems.
The short received positive reception for its suspenseful pacing, realistic family dynamics, and effective world-building within its brief runtime. On Rotten Tomatoes, it holds a 75% approval rating from critics based on five reviews, while audience scores highlight its thrilling execution. Fan response was enthusiastic, with a Jurassic Park Podcast Twitter poll showing 80% of over 6,800 voters rating it as "loved," often averaging around 4 out of 5 stars for realism and emotional stakes; IMDb users gave it a 6.9/10 from over 4,000 ratings.

Cancelled projects

In the early 1990s, shortly after the release of the original Jurassic Park film, Universal Cartoon Studios developed a pitch for an animated television series titled Escape from Jurassic Park. The series was envisioned as a direct continuation of the 1993 movie, set on Isla Nublar, where BioSyn executives, led by Lewis Dodgson, steal dinosaur eggs to establish a rival "DinoWorld" theme park in the Amazon rainforest. This leads to an outbreak of dinosaurs on the South American mainland, prompting John Hammond to assemble a team including Alan Grant, Ellie Sattler, and Ian Malcolm to contain the threat while preparing to reopen the original park to the public. The proposed first season outline included 13 episodes introducing new prehistoric creatures like Lycaenops and Ornithosuchus, culminating in a cliffhanger with the park's grand opening despite ethical concerns from the protagonists. The project advanced to concept art by William Stout and a full season script but was ultimately rejected in 1994, primarily due to Steven Spielberg's concerns over excessive merchandising saturation and potential narrative conflicts with the planned The Lost World: Jurassic Park sequel film.
Another unproduced animated series, Jurassic Park: Chaos Effect, emerged in 1998 amid the franchise's expansion following The Lost World: Jurassic Park. Drawing from Michael Crichton's concepts of genetic experimentation, the show would have depicted scientists at Site B creating hybrid dinosaurs through mismatched DNA splicing, resulting in creatures like the Velocirapteryx and Ultimasaurus. A team equipped with advanced technology, including characters inspired by Ian Malcolm and Eddie Carr, would combat these hybrids amid corporate exploitation of the chaos. The series was closely tied to a Kenner toy line of the same name, which introduced the hybrid designs and was intended to promote the program. Despite reaching discussions with Steven Spielberg and producing style guides and prototypes, the project was cancelled when the toy line was abruptly discontinued due to underwhelming sales and rights complications with Hasbro's acquisition of Kenner.
Following the release of Jurassic Park III in 2001, several television concepts surfaced in the mid-2000s and early 2010s but were ultimately scrapped as Universal prioritized feature film reboots over expanding into episodic formats. Rumors of a live-action series exploring untamed dinosaur populations on Isla Sorna circulated around 20052008, potentially bridging the gap between the original trilogy and new cinematic ventures, but these ideas were abandoned amid creative shifts toward revitalizing the franchise with Jurassic World in 2015. The focus on high-budget films, coupled with reboots emphasizing spectacle over serialized storytelling, led to the dismissal of these post-Jurassic Park III proposals, allowing resources to concentrate on theatrical releases rather than television development.

Jurassic World (Motion Comics) (2019)

Jurassic World (Motion Comics) is a series of four animated motion comic episodes released in late 2019, depicting original stories set in the Jurassic World universe after the release of dinosaurs on the North American mainland in Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom. The episodes explore everyday human-dinosaur encounters, providing supplementary lore to the franchise without featuring primary film characters. Produced by Universal Pictures and Amblin Entertainment, the series was uploaded to the official Jurassic World YouTube channel as free promotional content.
Each episode runs between 3 and 5 minutes and employs a motion comic format, blending static illustrated panels with subtle animations, voice-over narration, sound effects, and music to create a hybrid of comic book storytelling and video production. The first episode, "A Rising Tide" (November 29, 2019), follows a reporter investigating a Mosasaurus interrupting a Hawaiian surf contest. "Dinosaur Crossing" (December 6, 2019) portrays a Pteranodon causing traffic mayhem on a California highway. "The Roar That Rules It All" (December 13, 2019) shows a Ceratosaurus challenging lions at a zoo, while "Dinosaur Territory: Proceed With Caution" (December 20, 2019) depicts Compsognathus packs invading a national park, emphasizing caution in dinosaur habitats. The series received positive feedback from fans for enriching the Jurassic World timeline and offering accessible, bite-sized adventures that build anticipation for future installments. Employee Orientation Series: Presented by InGen (2023)

The Employee Orientation Series: Presented by InGen is a collection of three short web videos released in 2023 by Universal Pictures on the official Jurassic World YouTube channel to mark the 30th anniversary of the Jurassic Park franchise. Each episode adopts a mockumentary format, parodying corporate training materials through faux InGen instructional tapes that outline dinosaur handling procedures and safety protocols at the original Jurassic Park facility on Isla Nublar. The series humorously underscores the perils of the park's operations via overly optimistic narration and demonstrations, foreshadowing the catastrophic failures depicted in the films.
The first episode, titled "Secret Genetics Lab + Hatchery" and released on April 25, 2023, welcomes new staff to the facility where dinosaur embryos are developed and hatched, narrated by the animated character Mr. DNA and emphasizing extraction of DNA from ancient sources like amber-preserved mosquitoes. Running approximately one minute, it highlights the lab's role in genetic engineering while glossing over ethical and containment risks.
The second installment, "Tour Vehicle Safety 101," premiered on May 2, 2023, and provides guidelines for operating the park's Ford Explorer tour vehicles, including loading protocols and emergency responses to dinosaur encounters. Filmed using a fan-replicated vehicle prop, the video satirizes routine safety briefings by presenting them in a sterile, corporate tone amid the inherent dangers of the environment.
Concluding the series, "How to Care for Triceratops," released on May 9, 2023, focuses on veterinary duties at the Triceratops paddock, detailing feeding, monitoring, and medical care for juveniles under the oversight of Dr. Harding's team. The episode employs on-site footage to mimic hands-on training, ironically portraying dinosaur care as manageable despite the species' known aggression and the park's history of breaches.
Positioned within the franchise's pre-Jurassic World Dominion timeline, the series expands InGen's operational lore by illustrating everyday employee routines that contribute to the park's illusory stability. It has accumulated over 120,000 views across its playlist, praised by fans for revitalizing the original film's atmosphere through witty corporate satire.

Cast and crew

Film cast

The Jurassic Park film series features an ensemble of principal actors portraying key characters across its seven installments, with several performers reprising roles to maintain continuity in the franchise's narrative arcs involving paleontologists, park founders, and dinosaur handlers. In the original trilogy, directed by Steven Spielberg for the first two films and Joe Johnston for the third, Sam Neill stars as Dr. Alan Grant, a paleontologist central to the survival stories on Isla Nublar and Isla Sorna; he first appeared in Jurassic Park (1993), returned in Jurassic Park III (2001), and reprised the role in Jurassic World Dominion (2022). Laura Dern plays Dr. Ellie Sattler, Grant's colleague and romantic interest, debuting in the 1993 film with a brief cameo in Jurassic Park III and a major return in Dominion. Jeff Goldblum embodies the charismatic mathematician Dr. Ian Malcolm, known for his chaos theory warnings, leading The Lost World: Jurassic Park (1997) after his introduction in the original and reuniting with the core trio in Dominion. Richard Attenborough portrays John Hammond, the ambitious InGen founder whose vision drives the initial catastrophe, appearing in the first two films.
The Jurassic World trilogy shifts focus to a new generation of characters managing the revived park, with Chris Pratt as Owen Grady, a former Navy operative and velociraptor trainer who becomes a reluctant hero across Jurassic World (2015), Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom (2018), and Dominion. Bryce Dallas Howard stars opposite him as Claire Dearing, evolving from park operations manager to dinosaur rights advocate in the same three films. BD Wong recurs as Dr. Henry Wu, the geneticist behind the dinosaurs' creation, starting with a small role in Jurassic Park (1993) and reprising the role in the Jurassic World trilogy (20152022) where his arc involves corporate intrigue and ethical dilemmas. Justice Smith plays Franklin Webb, a tech-savvy operative introduced in Fallen Kingdom as part of Claire's rescue team and returning in Dominion to aid the global dinosaur crisis.
Recurring roles bridge the trilogies, emphasizing legacy connections; for instance, Goldblum's Malcolm provides advisory cameos in Jurassic World and Fallen Kingdom before his full return in Dominion, while Neill, Dern, and Wong unite with the newer cast in the 2022 finale to confront bioengineered threats. Notable cameos include Attenborough's Hammond in the first two films and brief appearances by original child actors Joseph Mazzello and Ariana Richards in The Lost World, underscoring the series' theme of generational impact.
The seventh film, Jurassic World Rebirth (2025), introduces a fresh ensemble five years after Dominion, led by Scarlett Johansson as Zora Bennett, a covert operative leading a high-stakes extraction mission for dinosaur DNA. Mahershala Ali portrays Duncan Kincaid, a team member with specialized skills in the operation; Jonathan Bailey plays Dr. Henry Loomis, a scientist aiding the quest; and Rupert Friend stars as Martin Krebs, a corporate antagonist complicating the mission. No original trilogy actors reprise roles in this entry, marking a narrative pivot to new human-dinosaur conflicts.

Key production crew

The Jurassic Park franchise has been shaped by a core group of directors who brought distinct visions to its films. Steven Spielberg directed the original Jurassic Park (1993) and its sequel The Lost World: Jurassic Park (1997), establishing the series' blend of spectacle and tension. Joe Johnston directed Jurassic Park III (2001). Colin Trevorrow helmed Jurassic World (2015) and Jurassic World Dominion (2022), revitalizing the saga with modern stakes and ensemble dynamics. J.A. Bayona directed Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom (2018), emphasizing emotional depth and environmental themes in the dinosaur-human conflict. Gareth Edwards took the reins for Jurassic World Rebirth (2025), the seventh installment, focusing on innovative visual storytelling drawn from his prior sci-fi work.
Screenwriting contributions have maintained narrative continuity across the films. David Koepp penned the screenplays for the first two entries, Jurassic Park and The Lost World: Jurassic Park, adapting Michael Crichton's novels with taut pacing and character-driven horror. He returned to write Jurassic World Rebirth, bridging the franchise's origins with contemporary plot elements. Derek Connolly co-wrote Jurassic World, Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom, and Jurassic World Dominion, collaborating with Trevorrow to expand on genetic engineering motifs and global threats.
Visual effects teams pioneered techniques that defined the series' realism. Stan Winston Studio created groundbreaking animatronics for the dinosaurs in the first three films, including full-scale puppets and hydraulic mechanisms that integrated seamlessly with live-action footage. Industrial Light & Magic (ILM) advanced CGI from the debut film onward, evolving the technology to depict herd behaviors, fluid movements, and complex environments across the franchise. Legacy Effects, succeeding Stan Winston Studio, contributed to hybrid dinosaur designs in later entries like Jurassic World, handling practical elements such as sculpting and animatronic references for creatures like the Indominus rex.
In television, Scott Kreamer served as showrunner and executive producer for Jurassic World: Camp Cretaceous (20202022) and its sequel series Jurassic World: Chaos Theory (2024present), overseeing animated storytelling that complements the live-action films with young protagonists and expanded lore.

Reception

Box office performance

The Jurassic Park franchise has grossed over $6.9 billion worldwide at the box office as of November 2025, establishing it as one of the highest-earning media franchises in film history. This cumulative total reflects the enduring commercial appeal of the series, driven by its blend of groundbreaking visual effects and adventure storytelling across seven feature films. The franchise's financial success has been bolstered by strong international performance, particularly in emerging markets, and ancillary revenue streams that extend beyond theatrical earnings.
The series peaked commercially with Jurassic World (2015), which earned $1.67 billion worldwide, setting multiple box office records including the highest global opening weekend at the time. Subsequent entries in the Jurassic World trilogy maintained billion-dollar status, though earnings showed a gradual decline: Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom (2018) at $1.31 billion, Jurassic World Dominion (2022) at $1.00 billion, and Jurassic World Rebirth (2025) at $869 million. Earlier films, while groundbreaking, generated lower unadjusted totals due to smaller global markets and budgets: Jurassic Park (1993) at $1.06 billion, The Lost World: Jurassic Park (1997) at $619 million, and Jurassic Park III (2001) at $366 million. Critical and public response

The original Jurassic Park (1993) received widespread acclaim, earning a 91% Tomatometer score on Rotten Tomatoes based on 204 reviews, with critics praising its groundbreaking visual effects and sustained tension. The film's critics consensus highlights it as "a spectacle of special effects and life-like animatronics, with some of Spielberg's best sequences of sustained awe and sheer terror since Jaws." Subsequent entries in the franchise have shown more varied reception, with an average Tomatometer score across the seven films ranging from 50% to 70%, reflecting diminishing critical enthusiasm over time. For instance, Jurassic World Rebirth (2025) holds a 50% score from 401 reviews, noted for its "rip-roaring set pieces" but criticized for relying on "fossilized clichs."
Common praises across the series center on the innovative visual effects and high-stakes tension, particularly in the early films, where animatronics and CGI created immersive dinosaur encounters that advanced cinematic technology. Roger Ebert's review of the 1993 film described the dinosaurs as "a triumph of special effects artistry," emphasizing their early and frequent appearances that deliver on the promise of awe-inspiring spectacle. However, post-2015 installments have drawn criticisms for narrative repetition, with plots often recycling the formula of escaped dinosaurs chasing human protagonists, leading to accusations of staleness and lack of innovation. Additionally, plot holessuch as inconsistencies in dinosaur behavior, containment systems, and scientific feasibilityhave been highlighted as persistent flaws, especially in later entries like Jurassic World Dominion and Rebirth.
The franchise has sparked extensive public discourse, particularly among fans debating the scientific accuracy of dinosaur depictions and the feasibility of genetic resurrection, often contrasting the films' dramatized paleontology with real-world discoveries. Ethical themes, such as the perils of de-extinction and human hubris in manipulating nature, have fueled broader conversations, with the series serving as a cautionary tale in academic and philosophical discussions on biotechnology. For example, debates around de-extinction ethics draw parallels to the films' warnings about ecological disruption and moral responsibility in reviving extinct species. On social media, these themes manifest in trending discussions and viral content, including fan analyses of genetic engineering risks and calls for responsible science amid advances in CRISPR technology.
Culturally, Jurassic Park ignited widespread "dino-mania," revitalizing public interest in paleontology and inspiring a surge in dinosaur-themed media, toys, and educational programs that popularized prehistoric life for generations. The franchise's legacy includes iconic memes, such as the "Clever girl" line from the velociraptor hunt scene, which originated in the 1993 film and has become a staple in online humor referencing surprise ambushes or underestimation. This phrase, delivered by character Robert Muldoon, has permeated pop culture through GIFs, parodies, and references in video games and TV shows, symbolizing the series' enduring influence on suspense tropes and internet meme culture. Overall, the films' blend of thrill and caution has shaped ongoing cultural conversations about science, ethics, and spectacle.

Accolades

The original Jurassic Park (1993) received three Academy Award nominations at the 66th ceremony and won all of them in technical categories: Best Visual Effects (Dennis Muren, Stan Winston, Phil Tippett, Michael Lantieri), Best Sound (Gary Summers, Gary Rydstrom, Shawn Murphy, Ron Judkins), and Best Sound Effects Editing (Gary Rydstrom). These achievements highlighted the film's groundbreaking use of computer-generated imagery and practical effects in depicting dinosaurs. At the 20th Saturn Awards, the film secured three wins: Best Science Fiction Film, Best Director for Steven Spielberg, and Best Writing for Michael Crichton and David Koepp.
The Jurassic World trilogy (20152022) earned recognition at the MTV Movie & TV Awards, with Jurassic World (2015) winning Best Action Performance for Chris Pratt in 2016. Subsequent entries like Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom (2018) and Jurassic World Dominion (2022) received nominations in categories such as Best Movie and Best Hero, underscoring the franchise's continued popularity in fan-voted honors. The animated series Jurassic World: Camp Cretaceous (20202022) garnered multiple Daytime Emmy Awards, including Outstanding Promotional Announcement In a Daytime Program for its 2020 launch campaign in 2021, and Individual Achievement in Animation for director Zesung Kang in 2021.
Across the franchise, entries have amassed over 20 wins, predominantly in technical fields like visual effects, sound design, and animation, reflecting innovations in CGI and storytelling. For Jurassic World Rebirth (2025), nominations for the 2026 Academy Awards in effects categories remain pending as of November 2025, following its July release.

Music

Film scores

The musical score for the original Jurassic Park (1993), composed by John Williams, features sweeping orchestral themes that evoke wonder and peril, including the majestic brass fanfare of "Welcome to Jurassic Park" and the adventurous motifs in "Journey to the Island," which underscore the film's exploration of Isla Nublar. Williams's score, blending lyrical strings with tense percussion to represent dinosaurs and human hubris, was recorded in California, though it notably missed an Academy Award nomination despite critical acclaim for its emotional depth.
Williams returned for The Lost World: Jurassic Park (1997), expanding his motifs with darker, more urgent cues like the predatory "The Hunt" and hybrid action sequences that integrate the original theme's four-note danger signal, heightening the sequel's chaotic tone. The score's hybrid stylemerging symphonic grandeur with electronic elements for dinosaur roarsreinforced the franchise's sonic identity, performed by a studio orchestra under Williams's direction.
For Jurassic Park III (2001), the score was composed by Don Davis, who honored Williams's legacy by incorporating variations on the original themes while introducing new action-driven material to accompany the film's rescue mission and encounters with a Spinosaurus. Davis's work emphasized intense percussion and orchestral swells for dinosaur chases, recorded with the Hollywood Studio Symphony to maintain the series' epic sound.
Subsequent live-action films shifted composers while honoring Williams's legacy. Michael Giacchino scored the Jurassic World trilogy (Jurassic World in 2015, Fallen Kingdom in 2018, and Dominion in 2022), weaving in Williams's themes alongside original material, such as the triumphant "Park Re-Creation" and pulse-pounding chase cues that fuse nostalgia with modern intensity. Giacchino's approach emphasized thematic continuity, using motifs like the island journey to bridge eras, recorded with full orchestras to maintain the series' epic scale.
For Jurassic World: Rebirth (2025), directed by Gareth Edwards, Alexandre Desplat composed the score, introducing new equatorial motifs inspired by the film's tropical settings and environmental themes while incorporating Williams's iconic elements for cohesion. Desplat's work, recorded at Abbey Road Studios with the London Symphony Orchestra, features tender piano passages and bombastic percussion in tracks like "Dino Spectacle," blending reverence for the originals with fresh hybrid action cues.

Andrews' music employs whimsical instrumentation, including playful woodwinds and percussion, to underscore the series' family-friendly escapades involving young park rangers thwarting sabotage.

These short clips use simple, repetitive musical stings to punctuate instructional segments on genetics labs, triceratops care, and tour vehicle operation, amplifying the ironic tone of the fictional employee handbook style.

Merchandise and other media

Toys

The Jurassic Park franchise has inspired extensive toy lines since its inception, beginning with Kenner Products' 1993 release tied to the original film. These initial offerings included detailed action figures of dinosaurs like the Tyrannosaurus rex and Velociraptors, as well as playsets replicating park enclosures and vehicles such as the Jurassic Park Jeep. Kenner, acquired by Hasbro in 1991, produced over 100 unique items in the first wave, featuring poseable figures with accessories like tranquilizer guns and collectible cards, which captured the film's sense of adventure and danger.
Hasbro continued the line through the late 1990s and early 2000s, expanding with toys for The Lost World: Jurassic Park (1997) and Jurassic Park III (2001), including larger-scale dinosaurs and interactive playsets like the T. rex paddock. In 2009, Hasbro revived interest with a basic figure assortment and dinosaur packs, emphasizing affordability for younger audiences. For the 2015 Jurassic World film, Hasbro launched an initial toy line featuring basic action figures and vehicles, though it generated approximately $100 million in retail sales that year amid the movie's blockbuster success. However, following underwhelming performance, Universal shifted the master toy license to Mattel in 2016, effective from 2017.
Under Mattel, the Jurassic World toy lines have evolved significantly, with action figures and playsets tied to each film installment, such as the Indominus rex and Indoraptor figures for Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom (2018). The Legacy Collection, introduced in 2021, reimagines classic Kenner designs in modern 5-inch scale, including a highly detailed T. rex figure with articulated jaws and realistic sculpting, spanning from 1993 originals to contemporary releases. Playsets like the Jurassic World Adventure Park emphasize modular enclosures for dinosaur containment scenarios, allowing children to recreate film sequences with multiple figures. Overall franchise merchandise, including toys, exceeded $1 billion in global sales by 1994 for the original film alone, with subsequent lines contributing to billions in cumulative revenue across the series.
Innovations in Mattel's lines include electronic features like roar-sound-emitting figures, such as the Roarin' Super Colossal Tyrannosaurus rex, which activates audio and motion via tail triggers. Post-Jurassic World Dominion (2022), app integration became prominent through the Jurassic World Play and Facts apps, where scanning codes on figures unlocks 3D augmented reality models, facts, and interactive challenges, enhancing collectibility for over 60 dinosaur species. These digital tie-ins, launched with Fallen Kingdom toys, bridge physical play with mobile experiences.
Collectible extensions include Hot Wheels' Jurassic World die-cast vehicles, featuring dinosaur-themed cars with character drivers like Owen Grady in a raptor-painted Ford Bronco, released in multi-packs since 2018. These 1:64-scale models, often bundled with mini dinosaur figures, appeal to vehicle enthusiasts while tying into the franchise's vehicular elements. Mattel renewed its licensing partnership with Universal in 2024, ensuring continued production through upcoming releases like Jurassic World Rebirth.

Board games

Several official board and tabletop games have been released based on the Jurassic Park franchise, emphasizing themes of survival and escape from dinosaur threats on Isla Nublar. These games typically involve players assuming roles as park survivors navigating perilous environments, often incorporating cooperative or asymmetrical gameplay to mirror the tension of the films. Key titles include Jurassic Park Danger! (2018), The Lost World board game (1997), and Escape from Jurassic Park (card game, 1993), each published to capitalize on the franchise's popularity following major film releases.
Jurassic Park Danger!, published by Ravensburger, is a semi-cooperative strategy game for 2-5 players set during the chaos of the original film. One player controls the dinosaurs, deploying them to hunt the other players, who portray 10 unique characters with individual objectives such as reaching the helicopter or laboratory while managing personal decks representing health and actions. The game features a modular board of island tiles that players assemble, promoting replayability through variable layouts, and relies on card play for movement, encounters, and survival tactics. Released in 2018 amid renewed interest from the Jurassic World series, it saw strong sales aligning with the franchise's ongoing theatrical success.
The Lost World board game, released in 1997 by Milton Bradley, captures the events of the second film through an asymmetrical team-based experience for 2-8 players. Human players roll dice to move across a 3D board representing Isla Sorna, aiming to collect supplies and reach an evacuation helicopter while avoiding dinosaur attacks; the dinosaur team uses similar dice mechanics to pursue and eliminate survivors. Encounters with dinosaurs like the T. rex and velociraptors introduce risk through combat resolution via dice rolls, emphasizing quick decisions and luck-based survival. Published concurrently with the film's premiere, it achieved peak sales during that period, benefiting from widespread merchandise tie-ins.
Escape from Jurassic Park, a card game from 1993 published by Parker Brothers, offers a faster-paced shedding mechanic for 2-4 players aged 8 and up. Players draw and play cards depicting dinosaurs and escape actions, with the goal of discarding all dinosaur cards first to "escape" the island; special cards allow stealing from opponents or forcing discards to simulate chaotic pursuits. Rounds last about 10 minutes, with the first to win three rounds declared the ultimate survivor, focusing on hand management rather than board navigation. Timed to the original film's blockbuster run, its sales surged alongside the movie's cultural impact.
Across these titles, core mechanics revolve around survival through dice-rolling for random dinosaur encounters in board-based games and strategic card play for evasion, with players embodying roles like scientists or hunters to heighten immersion. Publishers such as Ravensburger, Milton Bradley, and Parker Brothers (later acquired by Hasbro) have driven the franchise's tabletop adaptations, with sales consistently peaking during major film launches like 1993, 1997, and the Jurassic World era, reflecting the synergy between cinematic releases and merchandise. Some later entries, including those tied to Jurassic World Dominion, feature expansions with add-ons like new dinosaur miniatures and scenario modules to extend campaigns.

Comics

Topps Comics published the initial Jurassic Park comic book adaptation in 1993, consisting of a four-issue limited series that closely followed the plot of the first film while incorporating elements from Michael Crichton's novel, such as expanded scenes involving the velociraptors' intelligence and escape. The series was written by Walter Simonson for the first issue, with subsequent issues by Don McGregor and Steven Grant, and featured artwork by Gil Kane with inks by George Prez, emphasizing dynamic action sequences amid the island chaos. This adaptation was polybagged with trading cards and released to capitalize on the film's success, serving as an entry point for comic fans into the franchise's dinosaur-themed narratives.
Building on the adaptation, Topps expanded the universe with additional series from 1993 to 1995, including the two-issue Jurassic Park: Raptor miniseries, which explored raptor revenge plots post-film, and the four-issue Raptors Attack in 1994, depicting ongoing human-dinosaur conflicts with new characters facing escaped predators. The flagship follow-up, Return to Jurassic Park, ran for nine issues starting in April 1995, delving deeper into novel-inspired lore like genetic engineering mishaps and survivor tales, written by Steve Englehart and illustrated by Joe Staton for the early arcs, transitioning to Tom and Mary Bierbaum with art by various creators including Klaus Janson. These stories often featured vengeful raptors targeting remnants of the InGen team, blending horror and adventure while tying back to the original island's legacy. An annual one-shot in 1995 compiled short stories, such as "Death Lizards," focusing on isolated dinosaur encounters.
In the 2010s, IDW Publishing revived the franchise with original miniseries under license, beginning with Jurassic Park: Redemption in 2010, a five-issue arc written by Bob Schreck and drawn by Nate Van Dyke, centering on Dr. Henry Wu's secret hybrid dinosaur experiments and a team's mission to contain escaped creatures on Isla Sorna. This was followed by Jurassic Park: The Devils in the Desert (2011), a four-issue story by Miran Kim and Scott Ciencin exploring mercenaries hunting dinosaurs in the American Southwest, and Jurassic Park: Dangerous Games (2012), another four-issue miniseries by Joshua Williamson and Alberto Ponticelli, involving poachers and a T. rex rampage in a remote jungle. IDW also reprinted Topps' material as the Classic Jurassic Park collections (20102013), compiling the 1993 adaptation and Return to Jurassic Park arcs into five trade paperbacks for modern accessibility. Covers for these series often featured artwork by Tom Yeates, evoking classic pulp adventure styles.
Dark Horse Comics contributed through its international arm, publishing a UK-exclusive Jurassic Park anthology series from 1993 onward, which reprinted Topps stories alongside original one-shots and short tales, such as dinosaur survival scenarios and prehistoric what-ifs, running for over 100 weekly issues until 1996. These included standalone adventures like raptor hunts in urban settings and annual specials with new content, expanding the lore with European artists contributing to the mix of horror and sci-fi elements.

Video games

The Jurassic Park franchise has inspired numerous official video games since 1993, spanning platforms from consoles and arcades to mobile devices, often blending action, simulation, and management elements tied to the films' themes of dinosaur revival and chaotic parks. These titles, developed by various studios in collaboration with Universal, have evolved from straightforward adaptations to complex park-building simulations, reflecting the series' enduring popularity.
One of the earliest adaptations is the 1993 Sega Genesis title Jurassic Park, a side-scrolling action-platformer developed by BlueSky Software and published by Sega. Players control Dr. Alan Grant, navigating Isla Nublar while using weapons to fend off dinosaurs like velociraptors and Tyrannosaurus rex, across levels inspired by the original film, including the jungle and aviary. The game also features a bonus mode allowing play as a velociraptor, emphasizing survival and exploration in a 2D perspective.
In 2003, Blue Tongue Entertainment released Jurassic Park: Operation Genesis for PlayStation 2, Xbox, and PC, published by Vivendi Universal Games. This hybrid simulation-action game tasks players with constructing and managing a dinosaur theme park on procedurally generated islands, hatching over 25 dinosaur species while preventing escapes through defensive missions and ranger patrols. It combines park-building mechanics with on-foot combat sequences, earning praise for its ambitious scope despite technical limitations.
The franchise's mobile offerings include Jurassic Park Builder, a 2012 free-to-play construction and management simulation developed by Ludia for iOS and Android. Players build and expand a dinosaur park by collecting DNA, breeding species from the films, and constructing enclosures and facilities, with social features for trading resources. The game incorporated events tied to film releases but was discontinued in 2020.
Fighting games have also appeared, such as Warpath: Jurassic Park (1999), developed by DreamWorks Interactive for PlayStation. This 3D arena fighter pits dinosaurs from the franchise against each other in one-on-one battles, featuring species like Triceratops and Spinosaurus with unique movesets, and a create-a-dinosaur mode for custom combatants. It served as a precursor to more narrative-driven titles.
The Jurassic World Evolution series, developed by Frontier Developments and published by the studio in partnership with Universal, represents the franchise's most successful simulation entries. The inaugural Jurassic World Evolution (2018) for PC, PlayStation 4, and Xbox One lets players manage a bioengineered dinosaur park, balancing research, genetics, and guest satisfaction across campaign and sandbox modes. It sold over two million copies within its first year across digital and physical channels. The sequel, Jurassic World Evolution 2 (2021), expanded to include chaotic weather, marine reptiles, and a story mode spanning the Jurassic World trilogy, achieving nearly one million units sold shortly after launch. Jurassic World Evolution 3 (2025) for PC, PlayStation 5, and Xbox Series X/S further refines park management with enhanced AI behaviors, new biomes, and a campaign set post-Jurassic World Dominion, surpassing 500,000 units in early sales. The series has collectively sold nearly 10 million units, underscoring its commercial impact.
Recent expansions for Jurassic World Evolution 2 tie directly to Jurassic World Dominion (2022), including the Dominion Biosyn Expansion (2022), which adds a narrative campaign focused on Biosyn's valley, new facilities, and dinosaurs like Therizinosaurus, and the Dominion Malta Expansion (2022), introducing black-market smuggling mechanics and species such as Quetzalcoatlus. These packs enhance replayability with film-specific challenges.
Looking ahead, Saber Interactive announced Jurassic Park: Survival in 2023, a first-person survival horror game set between the events of the original Jurassic Park and The Lost World: Jurassic Park, slated for release in 2026 on PC, PlayStation 5, and Xbox Series X/S, with a potential tie-in to the 2025 film Jurassic World Rebirth.

Attractions

Theme park rides

The Jurassic Park River Adventure is a water-based thrill ride located in the Jurassic Park section of Universal's Islands of Adventure in Orlando, Florida. Opened on May 28, 1999, the attraction simulates a riverboat tour through a dinosaur habitat that goes awry, culminating in an 85-foot plunge past a massive Tyrannosaurus rex animatronic. Riders board large boats for a scenic journey past herbivores like Parasaurolophus and Triceratops before a raptor breakout forces an emergency evacuation through dark tunnels, ending with the dramatic drop.
Similar versions of the ride exist internationally. At Universal Studios Japan in Osaka, Jurassic Park: The Ride opened on March 31, 2001, featuring a comparable boat experience with animatronic dinosaurs and the signature T. rex finale, though it underwent a refurbishment closure from September 2023 to early 2025. In Resorts World Sentosa's Universal Studios Singapore, the Jurassic Park Rapids Adventure variant debuted in 2010 as a raft-style river rapids ride, emphasizing turbulent waters and dinosaur encounters without the steep drop, and remains operational.
The Jurassic World VelociCoaster, introduced in 2021 at Universal's Islands of Adventure, represents a high-thrill evolution with raptor-centric theming drawn from the Jurassic World films. This launched roller coaster propels riders to 70 mph in 2.4 seconds via linear synchronous motors, navigating a 4,700-foot track with four inversions, including a 155-foot top-hat element and raptor enclosure simulations where passengers "race" alongside Blue and her pack. The queue incorporates video elements featuring Owen Grady and Claire Dearing, tying into the franchise's narrative. Minimum height is 51 inches.
Post-opening updates to these rides have integrated elements from later films, including Jurassic World Dominion (2022). The VelociCoaster features Easter eggs such as references to the film's enhanced raptor behaviors and locust threats, enhancing immersion without major structural changes. These additions, combined with the coaster's debut, significantly boosted attendance in the Jurassic Park area, with average wait times often exceeding 120 minutes during peak seasons and contributing to Islands of Adventure's record visitor numbers in 2021 and 2022. The River Adventure received minor audio-visual refreshes to align with Jurassic World branding, though no full retheme has occurred.
Several Jurassic-themed rides have faced closures over time. At Universal Studios Hollywood, the original Jurassic Park: The Ride operated from 1996 until its closure on September 3, 2019, after which it was rethemed and reopened as Jurassic World The Ride in 2021 with updated Mosasaurus and Indominus rex elements. In Universal Studios Florida, the unrelated Jaws attraction permanently closed on January 2, 2012, due to high maintenance costs, with its Amity Island lagoon space later repurposed for The Wizarding World of Harry Potter Diagon Alley in 2014; this shift indirectly allowed expansion of Universal's dinosaur-themed portfolio elsewhere. Looking ahead, the Islands of Adventure's Jurassic Park River Adventure is scheduled for a major refurbishment from January 5, 2026, to November 19, 2026, potentially incorporating further franchise updates, though details remain undisclosed.

Exhibitions

The Jurassic World Exhibition is a touring attraction that debuted in Melbourne, Australia, in 2015, featuring life-size animatronic dinosaurs created by Creature Technology Company to recreate scenes from the Jurassic World films. The exhibit immerses visitors in interactive environments, including a research lab and hatching area, blending cinematic elements with educational displays on paleontology and dinosaur biology. It has visited numerous museums and venues worldwide, such as the Field Museum in Chicago, the Natural History Museum in London, and sites in Singapore, Bangkok, and Madrid, drawing over eight million visitors across more than 60 locations since its launch.
The Jurassic Park Discovery Center, a permanent interactive exhibit within the Jurassic Park area of Universal's Islands of Adventure in Orlando, Florida, opened in 1999 and offers hands-on experiences simulating the franchise's scientific themes. Visitors can participate in fossil digs to uncover replica bones, operate a DNA sequencing lab to "extract" dinosaur genetic material, and engage in trivia games like You Bet Jurassic, all while viewing a towering T. rex skeleton and a nursery with animatronic baby Velociraptors. These elements promote learning about prehistoric life through play, with educational content drawn from real paleontological concepts adapted from the films.
In 2025, following the release of Jurassic World Rebirth, new themed exhibits emerged at museums, incorporating aquatic dinosaur models to highlight the film's underwater sequences and prehistoric marine life. For instance, the Natural History Museum in London hosted a promotional pop-up with displays of aquatic species like Spinosaurus, depicted with webbed feet and adaptations for swimming, alongside interactive elements on dinosaur evolution in aquatic environments, open through September 2025. Similarly, the Sam Noble Oklahoma Museum of Natural History featured an Aquilops display tied to the movie, emphasizing small herbivorous dinosaurs in riverine habitats, while the Cranbrook Institute of Science showcased Spinosaurus models exploring its semi-aquatic lifestyle. These exhibits, part of broader promotional efforts, have attracted significant crowds, contributing to the franchise's cumulative millions in attendance, and often partner with institutions for STEAM education programs that connect film narratives to real-world science.

Other experiences

The Jurassic World Live Tour is an arena-based live spectacle produced by Feld Entertainment in collaboration with NBCUniversal, Amblin Entertainment, and director Colin Trevorrow, which debuted in September 2019 and has continued touring North America and internationally into 2025. The production features over 30 life-size dinosaurs, including mechanically operated models of Triceratops, Stegosaurus, and Tyrannosaurus rex that perform charging and battling stunts, alongside puppet-operated creatures such as Velociraptors, a Troodon named Jeannie, and a baby Stegosaur. These elements recreate key scenes from the Jurassic World films, with performers known as "dino-teers" operating the puppets for interactive audience moments, emphasizing high-energy acrobatics and narrative-driven escapes from dinosaur threats.
Virtual reality experiences extend the Jurassic Park universe through immersive simulations set on Isla Nublar. Jurassic World Aftermath, developed by Coatsink Software and published by Oculus Studios, is a stealth survival game released in two partsPart 1 on December 17, 2020, and Part 2 on September 30, 2021exclusively for the Oculus Quest platform. Players navigate abandoned research facilities, completing objectives while evading intelligent Velociraptors and other dinosaurs through hiding mechanics, distractions like radio calls, and tense cat-and-mouse encounters that evoke the peril of the original films. The game incorporates franchise lore, including voice acting by Jeff Goldblum as Dr. Ian Malcolm, to simulate park exploration and survival scenarios.
Guided tours provide behind-the-scenes access to dinosaur-themed encounters and historic filming locations. At Universal Orlando Resort's Islands of Adventure, the Raptor Encounter allows guests to meet Blue, the iconic Velociraptor from the Jurassic World series, in a controlled habitat setting where handlers share facts about raptor behavior and intelligence. Visitors approach the enclosure for photographs and observations of Blue's interactions, often including introductions to juvenile raptors, highlighting the blend of education and immersion in a live animal exhibit simulation. In Hawaii, Kualoa Ranch offers the 2.5-hour Jurassic Adventure Tour, a rugged off-road excursion in open-air vehicles through Kualoa, Hakipu'u, and Ka'a'awa Valleys, visiting key filming sites from Jurassic Park, Jurassic World, and Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom. Highlights include the Indominus rex paddock with its 60-foot walls, dinosaur cages, bunkers, and the gallimimus running field, providing cinematic context amid the lush rainforest terrain used in the productions.
Fan events, such as panels at major conventions, foster community engagement with the franchise. At San Diego Comic-Con 2025, held July 2427, Universal and partners hosted discussions including "Jurassic Park: Liability for Dinosaur Attacks Never Goes Extinct" on July 24, exploring legal themes from the films with experts, and Mattel's "Celebrating the Eras: A Crash Course in Jurassic World Toy Design" on July 25, featuring reveals of new merchandise tied to Jurassic World Rebirth. These sessions, alongside activations like the Her Universe Fashion Show with Jurassic-inspired apparel, offer insights into the series' ongoing cultural impact and future projects.
