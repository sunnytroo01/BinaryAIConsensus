 Hershey is a census-designated place in Derry Township, Dauphin County, Pennsylvania, United States. The community, with a population of 14,334 as of the 2020 census, developed as a planned industrial town around the chocolate manufacturing operations established by Milton S. Hershey.
The Hershey Company, headquartered in the town since its founding in 1905, remains the dominant economic force, producing iconic products such as Hershey's Kisses and employing thousands in confectionery and related industries. Hershey's establishment as a model company town included provisions for worker housing, schools, and churches funded by Hershey's philanthropy, reflecting his vision of industrial paternalism. The town's identity is further shaped by tourism attractions like Hersheypark, an amusement park opened in 1907 to boost employee morale, which now draws millions of visitors annually and contributes significantly to the local economy.

History

Early Settlement and Pre-Industrial Context

The area now known as Hershey, Pennsylvania, within Derry Township, Dauphin County, was originally part of the territory inhabited by the Susquehannock people, an Iroquoian-speaking tribe that occupied the lower Susquehanna River watershed from around 1550 AD. The Susquehannock maintained villages and engaged in agriculture, hunting, and trade, but their population declined sharply due to intertribal warfare with the Iroquois Confederacy, European-introduced diseases, and colonial conflicts by the late 17th century. By the early 18th century, the region had been largely depopulated of indigenous peoples, with the Iroquois claiming control over the Susquehanna Valley lands through conquest and treaties, including the 1701 treaty with William Penn.
European settlement in Dauphin County began with exploratory trading posts, such as John Harris's establishment on the Susquehanna River around 1720, facilitating interactions between colonists and remaining native groups or Iroquois intermediaries. The first permanent settlers were primarily Scotch-Irish Presbyterians, who arrived in the 1720s and established frontier communities focused on subsistence farming amid ongoing threats from native raids during the Walking Purchase disputes and French and Indian War. Derry Township itself was formally organized on August 1, 1729, as one of the original townships of Lancaster County (from which Dauphin County was later carved in 1785) for tax assessment purposes, reflecting the growing settler population in the fertile Swatara Creek valley.
Central to early community life was the formation of Derry Presbyterian Church, where Scots-Irish immigrants began gathering for worship near a spring as early as 1724, with the congregation officially recognized in 1729 and its first pastor, Reverend William Bertram, installed in 1732 by the Donegal Presbytery. The church's grove was patented to the congregation by the sons of William Penn in 1741, underscoring its role as a pioneering religious and social hub. Pre-industrial Derry Township remained predominantly agrarian, with settlers cultivating crops like corn and wheat on small family farms, supplemented by livestock rearing, and later limited quarrying of brownstone in the 19th century; the absence of major transportation infrastructure or manufacturing kept the economy localized and self-sufficient until the late 1800s. This rural character, characterized by dispersed homesteads and reliance on natural resources, provided the foundational context for subsequent industrial development.

Founding and Milton S. Hershey's Vision

Milton S. Hershey initiated the founding of what would become Hershey, Pennsylvania, in 1903 by acquiring over 1,200 acres of farmland in Derry Township, Dauphin County, after beginning land purchases in 1902 through a local real estate broker. This rural site, near Hershey's birthplace, was selected for its access to plentiful fresh milk from surrounding dairy farmscritical for mass-producing milk chocolate using processes he had adopted from Swiss manufacturersand for the undeveloped land's suitability for large-scale industrial and residential development. Construction on the chocolate factory commenced that year, with the facility completing operations in 1905 as the world's largest chocolate plant at the time, employing innovative steam-powered machinery to produce bars and other confections.
Hershey's vision extended far beyond the factory to encompass a self-contained model industrial town designed to foster worker loyalty and productivity through paternalistic provision of amenities. He directed the construction of affordable worker housing adjacent to the plant, emphasizing durable brick homes with modern conveniences like indoor plumbing, which were rare in early 20th-century rural Pennsylvania. Community infrastructure followed, including schools, churches of multiple denominations to accommodate diverse employees, wide tree-lined avenues, and public spaces for recreation, all funded by Hershey's personal fortune from prior caramel and chocolate ventures. This approach drew from Hershey's Mennonite-influenced upbringing and observations of European industrial towns, prioritizing environmental beauty and social stability to minimize labor unrest and turnover, as evidenced by his stated aim to create surroundings that enhanced workers' quality of life and output.
Central to Hershey's broader philanthropic vision was the integration of education and opportunity, exemplified by the 1909 founding of the Hershey Industrial School (later Milton Hershey School) on 500 acres within the township for orphaned boys, whom he trained in trades including factory work. Hershey articulated this ethos through actions rather than extensive public quotes, but records indicate his belief that industrial success required investing in human capital, stating in company contexts that beautiful, functional environments yielded greater enjoyment and efficiency for residents. By 1915, the town featured over 200 homes, a bank, and utility systems, transforming the former farmland into a planned community of about 2,000 residents tied economically to the chocolate enterprise. This model persisted as a company-controlled enclave until Hershey's death in 1945, when his estate directed assets toward perpetual trusts emphasizing community welfare over profit maximization.

Establishment as a Model Industrial Town

In 1903, following the sale of his Lancaster Caramel Company in 1900 and the subsequent growth of his chocolate production, Milton Hershey selected a rural site in Derry Township, Dauphin County, Pennsylvania, for a new expansive chocolate factory designed to leverage local dairy resources and available labor. Construction on the factory began that year, with the six-acre facility becoming operational in 1904, marking a pivotal shift from smaller-scale operations in Lancaster. Hershey simultaneously envisioned and initiated the development of a surrounding model industrial town to support his workforce, organizing the Hershey Improvement Company to manage infrastructure such as roads, building lots, and utilities.
By 1905, foundational town elements were in place, including affordable worker housing along streets like Cocoa Avenue, electric and water systems, telephone service, and businesses to serve residents. Public facilities emphasized community welfare, with the opening of McKinley School for grades 1 through 12 and the formation of the Volunteer Fire Company that year. Transportation infrastructure featured trolley and rail lines to connect the town, factory, and external areas, facilitating efficient movement of workers and raw materials. The Cocoa House, completed in 1910, functioned as an early community hub with a library and YMCA services. These developments reflected Hershey's deliberate strategy to foster a stable, self-sufficient environment for employees, avoiding the vices associated with urban industrial centers.
Hershey's model town approach embodied progressive-era industrial paternalism, prioritizing wholesome living conditions to enhance worker productivity and loyalty without the exploitative tenancy common in some company towns. Wide, tree-lined streets and integrated amenities aimed to provide comprehensive supporthousing, education, recreation, and utilitiesdirectly tied to the factory's needs, with the town formally named Hershey around 1906. This holistic planning, overseen personally by Hershey, distinguished the community as a benchmark for employer-provided urban development, enabling rapid population growth to over 1,500 residents by the late 1900s.

Expansion, World Wars, and Post-War Growth

In the years following the establishment of the Hershey Chocolate factory in 1905, the town expanded rapidly as Milton Hershey invested in infrastructure to support a growing workforce. By 1910, the community included over 1,000 residents, with new housing developments, a trolley line connecting to Harrisburg, and the opening of Hershey Park in 1907 to provide recreation for employees. The company's output surged, producing millions of chocolate bars annually by 1915, driven by innovations in milk chocolate processing and proximity to dairy farms, which enabled economies of scale. This period saw the construction of essential amenities like the Hershey Inn (1912) and community buildings, transforming Derry Church into a planned industrial town with low-cost housing rented to workers.
During World War I, Hershey Chocolate Corporation pivoted to military supply, producing chocolate bars for U.S. troops to boost morale and provide quick energy, with output increasing amid a 1917 sugar shortage that challenged civilian production but favored government contracts. The company's resilience stemmed from Hershey's vertical integration, including ownership of sugar plantations in Cuba established in 1916, which mitigated raw material disruptions. By war's end, Hershey's population had reached approximately 1,500, reflecting sustained employment despite national economic strains.
The interwar decades featured continued expansion, including the 1927 opening of the Hershey Theater and Sports Arena, funded by Hershey's profits amid national prosperity. The Great Depression prompted a major building campaign starting in 1930, employing 600 workers to construct landmarks like the Hotel Hershey (1933) and Community Building, which alleviated local unemployment while enhancing the town's appeal as a self-contained community.
World War II marked a peak in Hershey's industrial role, as the company produced over 3 billion non-melting D-ration barsdesigned to withstand 120F temperatures and deliver 600 caloriesfulfilling U.S. Army specifications for emergency sustenance. Additional Tropical Chocolate Bars, heat-resistant and formulated with less sugar to combat rationing, were manufactured in one- and two-ounce sizes for overseas troops. Military contracts comprised up to 70% of output, sustaining the factory during civilian shortages and earning the company the Army-Navy "E" Award for excellence in 1943. The Hotel Hershey temporarily housed 15 Vichy French diplomats and families as wartime detainees from November 1942 to 1944, under U.S. custody following diplomatic tensions.
Post-war growth accelerated with national economic expansion, as Hershey's leveraged pent-up consumer demand for chocolate and amusement, expanding Hersheypark with new rides by the late 1940s. Milton Hershey's death in 1945 transitioned control to a trust, but the company's sales rebounded to pre-war levels by 1947, fueling town development including suburban housing and infrastructure upgrades amid rising automobile use. Population swelled to over 2,000 by 1950, supported by stable factory jobs and emerging tourism, though the shift to car-dependent sprawl began eroding the original pedestrian-oriented model town.

Modern Era and Economic Evolution Since 1960

In the 1960s, the Hershey Chocolate Company pursued diversification beyond confectionery, acquiring H.B. Reese Candy Company in 1963, which produced Reese's Peanut Butter Cups, and two pasta manufacturers to hedge against fluctuating cocoa prices and expand revenue streams. This strategic shift sustained manufacturing employment in Hershey amid broader U.S. industrial challenges, with the company renaming itself Hershey Foods Corporation in 1968 to reflect its broadened portfolio. Local economic stability benefited from these moves, as the firm's operations continued to anchor the town's workforce, though reliance on chocolate sales persisted.
A pivotal development occurred in 1963 when the Milton Hershey School Trust funded the establishment of the Pennsylvania State University College of Medicine and Milton S. Hershey Medical Center through a $50 million gift, with construction beginning in 1966 and the medical center opening in 1970 as central Pennsylvania's first tertiary care facility. This academic medical center, integrated with Penn State, rapidly grew into a major employer, with over 18,000 staff by the 2020s, and generated more than $98 million in external research funding in fiscal year 2019 alone, fostering biotechnology and healthcare sectors that diversified the local economy from its chocolate-centric roots. The center's expansion, including specialized institutes for cancer and heart care, positioned Hershey as a regional hub for medical innovation and services, attracting professionals and stimulating ancillary businesses like housing and retail.
Hersheypark underwent a comprehensive redevelopment starting in 1971 as part of a five-year plan, transitioning from a modest 1920s-era picnic grove with basic rides to a modern theme park spanning over 110 acres by the 2000s, incorporating thrill rides, a water park, and seasonal events that drew millions of visitors annually. This evolution capitalized on the town's chocolate heritage to build a tourism economy, with expansions like the addition of roller coasters in the 1980s and 1990s generating substantial revenue through admissions, lodging, and Hershey Entertainment & Resorts Company operations, which employed thousands seasonally and year-round. Tourism's growth addressed earlier infrastructure strains from visitor influxes in the 1950s and 1960s, while complementing manufacturing by reducing economic vulnerability to commodity price swings.
By the late 20th century, Hershey's economy had evolved into a balanced mix of food processing, healthcare, education via the Milton Hershey School, and leisure, with the population of the Hershey CDP rising from approximately 7,000 in 1960 to 14,849 by 2020, reflecting suburban expansion and job opportunities. The Hershey Company, despite global acquisitions and international growth, maintained its headquarters and primary U.S. production in the area, employing around 3,000 locally as of recent reports, but the medical center and park collectively outpaced it as employment drivers. This diversification mitigated risks from agricultural dependencies, such as milk and cocoa supply disruptions, while sustaining per capita income above state averages through high-skill sectors.

Geography

Location and Physical Features

Hershey is an unincorporated census-designated place located entirely within Derry Township in Dauphin County, south-central Pennsylvania. It lies approximately 12 miles east of Harrisburg, the state capital, in the central part of the commonwealth. The community's central coordinates are approximately 401709N 763805W, positioning it in the southeastern portion of Dauphin County.
The terrain of Hershey features gently rolling hills characteristic of the Hershey Valley, a subsection of the broader Great Valley physiographic province in the Appalachian region. Average elevation in the area is about 443 feet (135 meters) above sea level, with variations due to the underlying Ordovician Beekmantown limestone formations that contribute to local karst features and groundwater dynamics. The valley's geology supports fertile soils suitable for agriculture, interspersed with urban development, though specific soil types include deep, well-drained varieties formed over limestone and adjacent shales.
Hydrologically, Hershey is proximate to Swatara Creek, a tributary of the Susquehanna River, which flows through the region and influences local drainage patterns and flood risks. The area's topography facilitates surface water flow toward the creek, with no major rivers directly bisecting the community but contributing to the broader Susquehanna River Basin watershed. These physical attributes, including the limestone bedrock prone to dissolution and cavity formation, have shaped both natural landscapes and human infrastructure considerations in the valley.

Urban Layout and Land Use

Hershey's urban layout originated from Milton Hershey's 1903 plan for a model industrial town within Derry Township, featuring a grid pattern centered on Chocolate Avenue and Cocoa Avenue as the primary crossroads. These wide avenues accommodate commercial and pedestrian activity, with iconic Hershey's Kiss-shaped street lamps lining Chocolate Avenue to evoke the town's chocolate heritage. The core community spans less than two square miles along Spring Creek, designed to integrate worker housing near the chocolate factory while preserving surrounding rural landscapes initially dominated by farms and woodlands.
Land use in Hershey emphasizes a mix of residential, commercial, industrial, and recreational zones under Derry Township's zoning ordinance, which includes 19 districts such as Rural (R), Low-Density Residential (LDR), Medium-Density Residential (MDR), High-Density Residential (HDR), and commercial categories to support varied development. Residential areas predominate in suburban-style neighborhoods radiating from the downtown core, while commercial uses concentrate along Chocolate Avenue for retail and tourism-oriented businesses. Industrial land is allocated primarily to Hershey Company facilities, including the original factory site, with design standards requiring minimization of land disturbance and preservation of natural features like wetlands and slopes. Recreational spaces, such as the 121-acre Hersheypark, occupy significant portions, contributing to the town's identity as a destination blending industry and leisure.
Derry Township's updated comprehensive plan promotes smart growth principles, advocating compact, walkable mixed-use development in the downtown area to counteract post-1945 sprawl that shifted activity away from Chocolate Avenue toward highways. This approach seeks to restore vitality to the central business district through form-based zoning elements that prioritize building form over strict use separation, while limiting low-density suburban expansion to maintain Hershey's planned legacy of integrated land uses. Institutional uses, including the Penn State Health Milton S. Hershey Medical Center and Milton Hershey School properties, further diversify land allocation, framing the urban core with educational and healthcare facilities.

Climate

Seasonal Patterns and Extremes

Hershey experiences a humid continental climate characterized by four distinct seasons, with cold, snowy winters influenced by continental polar air masses and hot, humid summers driven by warm, moist flows from the Gulf of Mexico. Winters from December to February feature average high temperatures ranging from 37F to 42F and lows from 23F to 27F, accompanied by frequent overcast skies and an average seasonal snowfall of about 25 inches, primarily from nor'easters and lake-effect enhancements off the Great Lakes. Spring (March to May) transitions with rising temperatures averaging highs of 52F to 71F and increasing precipitation from 3 to 4 inches monthly, often in the form of showers as frontal systems shift northward. Summers (June to August) bring the warmest conditions, with average highs of 82F to 85F and lows around 62F to 66F, marked by high humidity and convective thunderstorms contributing to peak annual precipitation of about 4 inches per month. Autumn (September to November) cools progressively, with highs dropping from 77F to 58F and foliage color changes peaking in October amid drier conditions averaging 3 inches of rain monthly.
Extreme weather events underscore the region's vulnerability to temperature swings and heavy precipitation. The record high temperature near Hershey, recorded at Harrisburg International Airport, reached 107F on July 3, 1966, during a prolonged heat wave exacerbated by high pressure subsidence and clear skies. The record low was -15F on February 9, 1899, reflecting Arctic outbreaks funneled by the Appalachian topography. Snowfall extremes include a single-storm record of 20.5 inches on January 23, 2016, from a potent winter storm, while the snowiest month on record was February 2010 with 35.1 inches. Precipitation extremes often manifest as flash flooding along the Swatara Creek, with the highest crest of 20.57 feet on September 8, 2011, triggered by remnants of Tropical Storm Lee dumping over 10 inches of rain in 48 hours, causing widespread inundation. Heat indices in summer can exceed 100F during humidity spikes, while winter wind chills drop below 0F amid nor'easters delivering gale-force winds and mixed precipitation.

Impacts on Local Economy and Lifestyle

Hershey's humid continental climate, characterized by warm summers averaging 82F (28C) highs and cold, snowy winters with averages around 21F (-6C) lows, shapes the local economy through its influence on tourism, the dominant sector. Summer warmth drives peak visitation to Hersheypark, supporting Dauphin County's $2.6 billion annual tourism revenue, as outdoor rides and water attractions thrive in mild heat but face operational limits during extremes exceeding 90F (32C), prompting temporary closures for safety and prompting visitors to seek shaded or aquatic options.
Winter precipitation, averaging 25-30 inches (64-76 cm) of snow annually, disrupts transportation and commerce, contributing to Pennsylvania's 20 billion-dollar winter storm events since 1980, which have caused widespread closures and economic losses estimated at $300-700 million per major shutdown day from lost productivity and cleanup. In Hershey, such events like the 2016 Winter Storm Jonas halted regional travel, delaying factory shipments and reducing short-term retail activity, though the Hershey Company's controlled indoor production mitigates direct weather vulnerabilities.
For residents, the four distinct seasons foster a lifestyle attuned to outdoor recreation in spring and fallsuch as hiking amid moderate 60-70F (15-21C) temperaturesbut demand adaptations like snow removal and heating during harsh winters, elevating household energy costs amid sub-freezing spells. Summer humidity exacerbates discomfort for outdoor work and events, encouraging indoor alternatives like the Hershey Gardens conservatory, while overall patterns promote year-round community activities tied to chocolate-themed holidays, balancing climatic challenges with the town's resilient, family-oriented ethos.

Socioeconomic Characteristics

As of the 2019-2023 American Community Survey estimates, the median household income in Hershey was $78,750, exceeding the Pennsylvania state median of $73,170. Per capita income stood at $51,671, reflecting contributions from high-wage sectors like manufacturing and professional services tied to local anchors such as the Hershey Company. The poverty rate was 9.0%, lower than the national average of 11.5% but indicative of disparities among seasonal tourism workers and lower-skilled employees in hospitality.
Educational attainment among residents aged 25 and older shows 89.5% holding a high school diploma or higher, slightly below the state figure of 91.9%. Notably, 49.1% possess a bachelor's degree or higher, more than double the state rate of 34.5% and surpassing the national average of 35.0%, driven by proximity to institutions like Penn State Hershey Medical Center and the Milton Hershey School's emphasis on vocational training. This elevated postsecondary education correlates with professional occupations in healthcare, education, and management, comprising a significant share of the workforce.
Housing reflects moderate affordability challenges, with the median value of owner-occupied units at $339,300 and median gross rent at $1,241, both above state medians of $263,400 and $1,037, respectively. Labor force participation exceeds the national rate at 63.3%, with unemployment typically under 4%, supported by stable employment in confectionery production and tourism but vulnerable to automation and seasonal fluctuations. Overall, Hershey's socioeconomic profile indicates relative prosperity anchored by corporate philanthropy and industry, tempered by income polarization between executives and entry-level positions.

Economy

Dominance of the Hershey Company

The Hershey Company traces its origins to 1894, when founder Milton S. Hershey acquired 1,200 acres of farmland in Derry Township, Dauphin County, to build a chocolate factory, capitalizing on the region's abundant milk supply from nearby dairy farms and limestone-filtered spring water ideal for processing. This strategic location choice not only enabled efficient production but also catalyzed the transformation of the rural area into a planned industrial community, with Hershey personally funding infrastructure like streets, utilities, and worker housing to sustain operations and workforce stability. By 1907, the factory employed over 1,000 workers, establishing the company's foundational economic leverage as the primary job creator and revenue generator in the nascent town, which was officially renamed Hershey in 1906 to reflect its benefactor's influence.
The company's dominance persisted through economic turbulence, exemplified by its response to the Great Depression, when Hershey averted layoffs by redirecting resources to public works projects, employing thousands in constructing enduring landmarks such as the Hershey Community Building and Highmeadow golf course, thereby stabilizing local incomes and preventing widespread unemployment in an era when Pennsylvania's industrial heartland faltered. This paternalistic approach, rooted in Hershey's vision of industrial self-sufficiency, intertwined corporate success with community welfare, funding amenities like parks, a zoo, and transportation systems that reduced worker commuting costs and enhanced retention.
In contemporary terms, The Hershey Company maintains its preeminent position as the third-largest employer in Dauphin County, behind only state government and the Milton S. Hershey Medical Center, with headquarters, research facilities, and core manufacturingincluding the iconic Hershey's Chocolate Factoryanchored in Hershey proper. Globally employing 20,030 full-time staff as of December 31, 2024, the firm's local footprint sustains thousands of direct jobs in production, logistics, and administration, while indirect effects amplify through supply chain dependencies on regional agriculture and services. Recent commitments, including a $1 billion investment in expanded Reese's processing capacity announced in April 2025, signal continued reinforcement of this economic centrality, bolstering tax revenues and skill development amid diversification pressures. The Hershey Trust Company's control of majority voting shares further entrenches this influence, directing a portion of corporate governance toward perpetuating Hershey's role as the town's economic cornerstone.

Tourism and Hospitality Sector

Hershey's tourism sector is predominantly driven by its chocolate heritage and family-oriented attractions, with Hersheypark serving as the primary draw, attracting 3.32 million visitors in 2023 and ranking as the 17th most-attended amusement park in North America according to the Themed Entertainment Association (TEA)/AECOM report. Hershey's Chocolate World, an adjacent experience center offering factory tours, chocolate-making demonstrations, and retail, complements the park by providing year-round access to the town's confectionery legacy, independent of seasonal park operations. These attractions collectively position Hershey as a key destination within Pennsylvania's Hershey-Harrisburg region, capitalizing on themed entertainment to generate visitor spending on admissions, merchandise, and confectionery products.
The hospitality sector underpins this tourism economy through a network of accommodations and dining options, many operated by Hershey Entertainment & Resorts Company, which manages properties such as The Hotel Hershey (a historic 1930s resort with 232 rooms) and the Hershey Lodge (665 rooms), alongside campgrounds and smaller lodges. These facilities reported contributing to an estimated $938.4 million in annual revenue for Hershey Entertainment & Resorts as of 2025 projections, supporting seasonal peaks during summer and Halloween events. Local restaurants, often featuring chocolate-infused menus, further enhance visitor retention, with downtown establishments along Chocolate Avenue offering casual dining tied to the town's branding.
Tourism's broader economic footprint in Dauphin County, where Hershey is located, yields an estimated $2.6 billion in annual impact, funding jobs in lodging, food services, and transportation while alleviating reliance on manufacturing. This sector's resilience is evident in post-pandemic recovery, with Hersheypark attendance nearing pre-2019 levels of 3.384 million, though it remains sensitive to weather and fuel costs affecting regional drive-in visitation.

Employment, Labor Relations, and Challenges

The Hershey Company's manufacturing operations serve as the primary source of employment in Hershey, employing approximately 3,000 workers at its local facilities as of the early 2000s, with recent expansions adding over 100 positions through a new digitally integrated plant announced in April 2025. Beyond chocolate production, secondary sectors include tourism-related services and healthcare, though these account for a smaller share of jobs compared to manufacturing, which dominates Dauphin County's employment landscape per quarterly census data.
Labor relations at Hershey facilities are shaped by representation from the Bakery, Confectionery, Tobacco Workers and Grain Millers International Union (BCTGM) Local 464, which has negotiated collective bargaining agreements since organizing in the 1930s. Historical tensions peaked during a 1937 sit-down strike by Italian immigrant workers demanding recognition, which escalated into violence after company resistance and state police intervention, ultimately leading to a smaller, company-preferred union prevailing over the CIO-affiliated group. A more recent major dispute occurred in 2002, when 2,800 Local 464 members struck for 42 days against proposed outsourcing and a potential company sale, resolving with concessions including job protections after national headlines highlighted worker grievances over executive pay amid cost-cutting.
Key challenges include economic overdependence on the Hershey Company, exposing the local workforce to risks from corporate decisions such as mergers or automation, as evidenced by 2002 strike fears and ongoing critiques of potential job relocation in takeover scenarios. Employee feedback highlights persistent issues with work-life balance and demanding production schedules, though the company has introduced flexible hours and incentives to mitigate turnover. Diversification efforts remain limited, with manufacturing's local quotient elevated in Dauphin County data, amplifying vulnerability to sector-specific downturns like projected earnings pressures in 2025.

Government and Infrastructure

Local Governance Structure

Hershey lacks status as an incorporated municipality and receives all local governmental services from Derry Township in Dauphin County. Derry Township operates as a second-class township under Pennsylvania law, governed by a three-member Board of Supervisors elected at-large to staggered six-year terms. The board holds legislative authority, including enacting ordinances, approving budgets, and appointing key officials such as the township manager, who serves as the chief administrative officer responsible for daily operations, policy implementation, and departmental oversight.
As of early 2025, the Board of Supervisors consists of Chairman Ronald E. Napikoski Jr., Vice Chairman Donald R. Warntz II, and Supervisor John T. Sipe, with meetings held publicly to address township affairs. The township manager position, filled by Zachary Jackson since January 2025 at an annual salary of $150,000, coordinates services like public works, zoning enforcement, and community development across the township's approximately 28 square miles, which encompass Hershey's commercial core and residential areas. This manager-supervised structure supports a population of about 24,717 as of the 2020 census, emphasizing fiscal responsibility and infrastructure maintenance amid Hershey's tourism-driven economy.
Derry Township's governance includes specialized bodies such as the Derry Township Municipal Authority for water and sewer services, and the Derry Township Police Department for law enforcement, both operating under board oversight. Higher-level coordination occurs through Dauphin County, led by three commissioners handling regional matters like courts and emergency services, but township-level decisions predominate for Hershey's immediate needs. This decentralized model reflects Pennsylvania's tradition of township autonomy, dating to Derry's establishment in 1729 and formal governance from 1759.

Transportation Networks

Hershey's transportation network is predominantly automobile-oriented, reflecting its status as a suburban community with heavy reliance on personal vehicles for access to attractions, employment, and regional connections. The primary highway providing external access is Interstate 81 (I-81), which links Hershey northward to I-78 and southward to I-83 near Harrisburg, with entry via exits such as 85 (PA 743) or 77 (PA 39). U.S. Route 322 (US 322) runs east-west through the area, intersecting local roads and connecting to Harrisburg approximately 12 miles west, while Pennsylvania Route 39 (PA 39) serves as a key east-west corridor through central Hershey, facilitating intra-community travel and links to nearby townships. These routes support high seasonal traffic volumes, particularly during tourism peaks, but Hershey lacks direct interstate frontage, requiring short detours from major limited-access highways like I-283.
Public transit options are limited and geared toward commuters and visitors connecting to Harrisburg. Capital Area Transit (CAT), operated by rabbittransit, provides Route 322 bus service on weekdays, running from downtown Hershey through Penn State Hershey Medical Center, Hummelstown, and the state capitol complex to downtown Harrisburg, with fares structured around a base rate plus zone additions. No fixed-route service operates evenings, weekends, or holidays within Hershey itself, though on-demand paratransit and ridesharing services like Uber are available for local mobility, alongside bicycle rentals in tourist areas.
Rail access is indirect, as Hershey has no passenger station; the nearest Amtrak stops are at Harrisburg (HAR), 11-22 minutes by car, served by Keystone Service and Pennsylvanian trains connecting to Philadelphia, New York, and Pittsburgh. From Harrisburg, shuttles or taxis bridge the gap to Hershey destinations. Air travel centers on Harrisburg International Airport (MDT), located 11 miles southwest and reachable in about 17 minutes via PA 441 or US 322, offering nonstop flights to roughly 70 daily destinations via airlines including American, Delta, and United. This proximity supports tourism but underscores the area's dependence on ground transport from the airport, with no dedicated rail link.

Education

Public Education System

The Derry Township School District operates as the public education system for Hershey and surrounding areas in Derry Township, Dauphin County, Pennsylvania, serving approximately 3,165 students across kindergarten through twelfth grade. The district encompasses five schools, including Hershey Elementary School (K-5), Hershey Middle School (6-8), and Hershey High School (9-12), with additional facilities supporting specialized programs. Enrollment demographics indicate a minority student population of 30% and an economically disadvantaged rate of 14.9%, with a student-teacher ratio of 14:1. 
Academic performance exceeds state averages, with 74% of elementary students proficient or above in reading and 68% in math based on Pennsylvania System of School Assessment (PSSA) results. At Hershey High School, 61.3% of students achieved proficiency in Algebra I, 77.4% in Biology, and 89.7% in Literature on Keystone Exams. The district ranks 17th among 494 Pennsylvania public school districts overall, with Hershey High School placing 44th out of 686 high schools statewide and Hershey Middle School 52nd out of 891 middle schools. Advanced Placement participation at the high school stands at 58%, reflecting strong preparation for postsecondary education. Average SAT math scores for the 2024 graduating class were 599.
The district's annual budget for the 2025-26 school year totals $36.4 million, with $12.4 million derived from state and federal sources, supporting operations amid Pennsylvania's fluctuating education funding landscape. Recent state budget impasses delayed payments, prompting temporary financial strains, though resolutions in October 2025 restored allocations including increases for basic and special education. The district maintains standards-based report cards and comprehensive planning through 2027, emphasizing academic progress and community alignment. 

Milton Hershey School and Philanthropic Institutions

The Milton Hershey School, established in 1909 by chocolate manufacturer Milton S. Hershey and his wife Catherine S. Hershey, initially served as a residential vocational institution for impoverished and orphaned boys, beginning operations with four students in 1910. The founders endowed the school with substantial resources derived from the Hershey Chocolate Company, transferring control of the company to the Hershey Trust Company in 1918 via a deed of trust explicitly designating proceeds to support the institution's operations for the benefit of "orphan children." Today, the school enrolls approximately 2,000 pre-kindergarten through 12th-grade students from low-income families, providing tuition-free education, on-campus housing in single-sex group homes, meals, clothing, medical care, and extracurricular activities, with admission criteria emphasizing family financial need, ages 415 at entry, academic potential, and absence of severe behavioral issues that could disrupt the community environment.
Funded primarily through the Hershey Trust Companya Pennsylvania-chartered entity founded by Milton Hershey in 1905 that holds a controlling interest in The Hershey Companythe school's endowment exceeds $17 billion in assets as of recent audits, positioning it as the wealthiest K-12 institution in the United States and enabling per-student expenditures historically surpassing $100,000 annually for comprehensive support services. The trust's governance mandates perpetual funding for the school while restricting principal expenditures to preserve long-term sustainability, a structure rooted in Hershey's intent to create enduring self-sufficiency for disadvantaged youth rather than short-term distributions.
Beyond the school, Hershey's philanthropic legacy extends to the M.S. Hershey Foundation, a nonprofit established to promote cultural and educational enrichment in the Hershey community, operating facilities such as The Hershey Story museum, Hershey Gardens botanical display, and Hershey Theatre for performing arts. These institutions, sustained by foundation grants and aligned with Hershey's vision of community welfare, complement the school's focus by fostering public access to heritage preservation and recreational amenities without direct ties to commercial operations. The Hershey Trust Company also oversees broader charitable initiatives tied to the founder's directives, ensuring alignment with his emphasis on practical aid over expansive social engineering.

Culture and Recreation

Major Attractions and Points of Interest

Hershey's primary attractions revolve around its chocolate heritage and recreational offerings established by Milton S. Hershey. Hersheypark, the town's flagship amusement park, opened on May 30, 1906, initially as leisure grounds for chocolate factory employees and expanded into Pennsylvania's largest amusement park with 14 roller coasters and over 70 rides as of 2025. The park features seasonal events, including Halloween-themed haunted houses and winter holiday lights, drawing millions of visitors annually.
Hershey's Chocolate World serves as an interactive visitor center simulating chocolate production, with free admission to the core Hershey's Chocolate Tour ride that traces cocoa beans to candy bars, ending in a complimentary sample. Additional paid experiences include 4D chocolate films, baking classes, and a retail store stocking Hershey's products; the site operates daily from 9 AM to 11 PM during peak seasons.
ZooAmerica North American Wildlife Park spans 11 acres and houses over 200 animals from 75 species native to North America, originating from Milton Hershey's 1910 private collection that began with prairie dogs in 1905. Exhibits are divided into biomes like Rocky Mountains and Cypress Swamp, with admission included in Hersheypark tickets for year-round access.
Hershey Gardens, a 23-acre botanical site opened in 1937, showcases themed gardens, a rose collection exceeding 3,000 varieties, and a Butterfly Atrium with exotic species; it emphasizes horticultural displays and a Children's Garden for interactive learning. The gardens overlook the town and feature seasonal blooms, attracting visitors for serene walks amid signature trees and fountains.
Downtown Hershey features Chocolate Avenue lined with Hershey's Kiss-shaped street lamps, symbolizing the town's confectionery identity, alongside the historic Hotel Hershey built in 1933 as a luxury resort with spa facilities. These elements collectively reinforce Hershey's status as a family-oriented destination tied to its industrial founding.

Sports and Community Events

Hershey serves as the home of the Hershey Bears, a professional ice hockey team in the American Hockey League (AHL) and primary affiliate of the Washington Capitals of the National Hockey League (NHL). The Bears, established in 1936, hold the distinction of being the longest continuously operating professional hockey franchise in North America and the AHL's winningest team with 13 Calder Cup championships as of 2023. They play home games at the GIANT Center, a 306,000-square-foot arena opened in 2001 with a hockey seating capacity of 10,500. The team's regular season spans October to April, drawing average crowds exceeding 7,000 per game and contributing significantly to local economic activity through ticket sales and concessions.
The GIANT Center extends beyond Bears games to host diverse sporting events, including basketball tournaments, wrestling competitions, and professional ice shows such as those featuring Disney on Ice. Hersheypark Stadium, an outdoor venue with a capacity of 30,000, supports community-level athletics by accommodating high school football games for Hershey High Schoolstate champions in 1970, 1984, and multiple recent titlesand track meets, as well as regional marching band contests that attract thousands annually. These facilities underscore Hershey's role in fostering youth and amateur sports development.
Community events in Hershey emphasize participatory recreation and civic involvement, often integrated with sports infrastructure. The Hershey Harrisburg Sports & Events Authority organizes tournaments for soccer, lacrosse, and volleyball, hosting over 50 events yearly that engage local youth leagues and visiting teams from across Pennsylvania. Fan-oriented gatherings, such as pre-game tailgates and community skate sessions at the GIANT Center's public rinks, promote social cohesion, with participation peaking during the Bears' playoff runsevidenced by sold-out crowds during their 2023 Calder Cup victory. These activities reflect Hershey's emphasis on accessible, family-centered athletics amid its company-town heritage.

Annual Festivals and Traditions

Hershey hosts several annual festivals that emphasize its chocolate heritage and community spirit, often organized in collaboration with local associations and Hershey Entertainment & Resorts Company. The most prominent is Choctoberfest, a fall event celebrating the town's confectionery roots, held annually on the second Saturday in October in ChocolateTown Square Park at the intersection of Chocolate and Cocoa Avenues. The 10th iteration occurred on October 11, 2025, from 11:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m., featuring local vendors, live music, craft beer tastings, and chocolate-themed activities such as sampling and demonstrations, drawing families and drawing on Hershey's identity as "The Sweetest Place on Earth."
The holiday season brings recurring traditions centered on illumination and festive attractions. Hershey Sweet Lights, a drive-through display of over one million lights along a three-mile route through Hershey Gardens and surrounding areas, operates annually from mid-November through early January, with vehicles admitted for a flat fee per car. Complementing this is Hersheypark Christmas Candylane, transforming the 121-acre amusement park into a winter wonderland with more than two million twinkling lights, holiday-themed rides, Santa Claus visits, and live entertainment, running select evenings from late November to New Year's Day. These events, which attract over 500,000 visitors combined in peak years, underscore Hershey's emphasis on family-oriented, seasonally themed public celebrations tied to its corporate landmarks.
Hershey New Year's Eve, a free, family-friendly street festival in downtown Hershey, marks the year's end with music, food vendors, character appearances, and a midnight countdown featuring a giant foil-wrapped Hershey's Kiss drop and bubble wrap "pops" for auditory excitement. Established as an annual tradition since 2005, the 20th edition on December 31, 2024, included daytime activities starting at 11:00 a.m. and evening festivities culminating at midnight, fostering community gathering in the town center. Additional traditions include annual observances of Milton S. Hershey's September 13 birthday through local exhibits and talks at The Hershey Story museum, reinforcing the founder's philanthropic legacy without formal festivals.

Controversies and Criticisms

Paternalistic Company Town Model

Milton Hershey founded the town of Hershey in 1903 as a planned industrial community centered on his chocolate factory, envisioning it as a "model town" insulated from urban vices and equipped with company-provided amenities to promote worker welfare and productivity. These included subsidized housing, a public library established in 1906, a YMCA in 1910, parks, and utilities managed by Hershey Improvement Company, which he controlled directly. The approach exemplified paternalism, wherein the employer assumed quasi-parental authority over employees' living conditions, leisure, and moral environment, often without resident input; Hershey personally approved major decisions, such as banning alcohol sales and dictating architectural styles to align with his vision of wholesome community life. This model drew from 19th-century industrial precedents but emphasized long-term stability over short-term profits, as Hershey reinvested factory earnings into town infrastructure rather than dividends.
Critics have characterized the system as overly autocratic, suppressing individual autonomy in favor of corporate dictates; for instance, during World War I sugar shortages in 1918, Hershey abruptly cut wages by 11% and eliminated bonuses, sparking unrest that exposed the fragility of enforced benevolence when economic pressures arose. Tensions escalated in the 1937 strike, when 500 unionizing workers walked out over stalled contract talks, prompting Hershey to deploy 400 state militia troops and swear in 600 deputies, resulting in clashes and 77 arrests that quelled the action without concessions. Such responses underscored how paternalism prioritized factory continuity over labor rights, contrasting the town's marketed image of harmony; contemporary accounts noted Hershey's reluctance to negotiate, viewing unions as disruptive to his self-imposed guardianship.
The paternalistic framework persisted post-Hershey's death in 1945 through the Milton Hershey School Trust, which retained controlling interest in Hershey Foods (now The Hershey Company) and town assets via Hershey Estates, exerting influence over local development and governance. This legacy fueled controversies, notably the 2002 attempt by Trust trustees to sell its 77% stake in the company for $4.5 billion to diversify assets for the school's benefit, a move blocked by Pennsylvania's attorney general and governor amid public outcry over potential job losses and erosion of the town's chocolate-centric identity. State intervention, including threats of legal action and legislative probes, preserved the status quo but drew accusations of parochial overreach, as the decision favored nostalgic community controlrooted in Hershey's original paternalismover fiduciary maximization for the Trust's impoverished beneficiaries, ultimately forgoing billions in potential endowment growth. Subsequent governance reforms in 2016 mandated greater independence for school operations from commercial interests, yet the intertwined structure continues to invite scrutiny for perpetuating insular decision-making.

Ethical Issues in Supply Chain and Reputation

The Hershey Company, headquartered in Hershey, Pennsylvania, has faced persistent criticism over child labor and forced labor in its cocoa supply chain, primarily sourced from West Africa, where approximately 70% of global cocoa originates from Ivory Coast and Ghana. Independent reports estimate that over 1.5 million children work in hazardous conditions on cocoa farms in these regions, including exposure to pesticides and machetes, often without pay or education. In 2001, Hershey joined other major chocolatiers in signing the Harkin-Engel Protocol, committing to eliminate the worst forms of child labor by 2005, a deadline later extended multiple times without full compliance, leading to accusations of broken pledges. Critics, including advocacy groups like Green America, launched the "Raise the Bar, Hershey!" campaign in 2010, highlighting ongoing trafficking and exploitation, which Hershey partially addressed by increasing traceable sourcing to 58% of its cocoa by 2020 but falling short of eradication goals.
Legal actions have amplified these concerns, damaging the company's reputation. In February 2021, a U.S. federal court allowed a landmark lawsuit against Hershey, Mars, and Nestl to proceed, alleging child slavery and trafficking in the supply chain dating back to purchases from farms using forced child labor as early as 2002. A January 2024 class-action suit accused Hershey and the Rainforest Alliance of deceptive marketing by certifying products as free from child labor and exploitative practices despite evidence of persistent violations in certified supply chains. Hershey maintains a Human Rights Policy prohibiting child and forced labor, investing in remediation programs like farmer cooperatives and traceability tech, yet third-party audits reveal gaps, with only partial supplier compliance. 
These supply chain ethical lapses have contributed to reputational harm for the Hershey brand, intertwined with the town's identity as a chocolate capital. Activist campaigns and media exposs, such as a 2020 NBC investigation questioning Hershey's sustainability commitments, prompted consumer boycotts and calls for transparency, eroding trust despite the company's $11 billion annual revenue largely from cocoa products. While Hershey reports progress in human rights due diligence, including a 2024 Statement Against Slavery and Human Trafficking covering its manufacturing supply chain, skeptics argue self-reported metrics understate systemic issues rooted in low farmer incomesaveraging $0.78 dailyand weak enforcement in sourcing regions. This has fueled broader scrutiny of corporate accountability, with Hershey's stock and market share occasionally pressured by ESG investor concerns over unremedied risks.

Local Development and Growth Disputes

In recent years, Derry Township officials and The Hershey Company have opposed a proposed warehouse and residential development on former quarry sites in adjacent South Hanover Township, citing risks of increased heavy truck traffic on Hersheypark Drive, a key access route to tourist attractions. The plan, which includes industrial buildings and homes at the Mumma and Pennsy Quarry properties in Hummelstown, drew formal objections from Derry in June 2025, emphasizing potential disruptions to local traffic patterns and the area's tourism-dependent economy.
The Hershey West End project, a 245-acre mixed-use development on land now controlled by the Milton Hershey School Trust, has faced repeated delays and revisions amid concerns over its scale, traffic impacts, and potential to compete with downtown revitalization efforts. Initial proposals for hundreds of housing units, including rentals, prompted worries in 2017 that it could overshadow core downtown growth, leading to scaled-back plans by May 2025 that eliminated apartments, reduced total units by nearly 50%, and incorporated more green space. A fifth extension for approvals was sought that month, reflecting ongoing negotiations between developers, township supervisors, and the Hershey Trust to balance housing needs with preservation of the community's character.
Commercial expansions have also sparked zoning battles, as seen in the 2023 rejection of a Sheetz convenience store and mixed-use project at the southwest corner of a local intersection, where Derry Township supervisors voted 5-0 against it for violations including inadequate parking, fueling station codes, and stormwater management. The developer appealed the decision, arguing shared parking provisions under the zoning ordinance should apply, but the board upheld opposition on multiple technical grounds. Similarly, Wawa abandoned plans for a Hershey-area store in May 2025 after failing to secure approvals, with community feedback highlighting traffic congestion from prior downtown builds as a recurring grievance.
These disputes underscore broader tensions in Hershey between accommodating population and economic growthdriven by tourism and proximity to Harrisburgand safeguarding the town's identity as a controlled, family-oriented destination rooted in Milton Hershey's vision. Earlier rezoning for 32 homes on a former golf course in 2015 passed by a narrow 3-2 vote, illustrating persistent divides over residential expansion even on Hershey-owned land. Community petitions, such as one against a Chipotle restaurant on West Chocolate Avenue, further reflect resident pushback against chain developments perceived to dilute the area's unique branding.

Legacy and Cultural Impact

In Popular Culture and Media

A major motion picture titled Hershey, focusing on the life of chocolate magnate Milton S. Hershey and his wife Catherine "Kitty" Hershey, entered production in May 2025, with principal photography concluding after seven weeks across 17 Pennsylvania locations including Hershey itself. Directed by Mark Waters and starring Finn Wittrock as Milton Hershey and Alexandra Daddario as Catherine, the film chronicles Hershey's journey from confectionery apprentice to building a global brand, emphasizing themes of perseverance, love, and philanthropy.
In television, Hershey has appeared in episodes highlighting its chocolate-themed attractions. The American Dad! episode "May the Best Stan Win" (Season 5, Episode 12, aired March 14, 2010) features Hersheypark as a central setting in a time-travel plot involving protagonist Stan Smith competing against his future self at the amusement park. The Good Doctor referenced Hershey multiple times in its January 15, 2018, episode "Islands: Part Two" (Season 1, Episode 12), including mentions of Penn State Hershey Medical Center and character Lea Dilallo's hometown ties to the area, portraying it as a relatable Pennsylvania community.
Historically, the Hershey Chocolate Corporation produced promotional films in the 1930s, such as those distributed to theaters, depicting the company's model town, milk chocolate manufacturing process, and support for Milton Hershey School as a beneficiary of its success. These self-produced media emphasized Hershey's utopian industrial community and industrial innovations, serving as early branded content rather than independent cultural portrayals.
In literature, Hershey features in non-fiction biographies like Michael D'Antonio's Hershey: Milton S. Hershey's Extraordinary Life of Wealth, Empire, and Utopian Dreams (2006), which details the town's founding as a planned community tied to chocolate production and philanthropy. Fictional works include children's mystery novels such as Carole Marsh's The Mystery in Chocolate Town: Hershey, Pennsylvania (part of the Real Kids Places series), where young protagonists visit during the candy company's centennial anniversary celebrations.

Notable Residents and Contributors

Milton S. Hershey (18571945), the founder of the Hershey Chocolate Company, played a pivotal role in establishing the community now known as Hershey, Pennsylvania, after acquiring farmland in Derry Township in 1894 and developing the town starting in 1903; he resided there from 1908 onward in High Point Mansion until his death. His contributions extended to philanthropy, including the 1909 founding of the Milton Hershey School for orphaned children, funded by his estate, which continues to operate as a tuition-free boarding school emphasizing vocational training.
Several individuals born or raised in Hershey have achieved prominence in sports and missionary work. Christian Pulisic, born September 18, 1998, in Hershey, is a professional soccer player who began his career locally before joining European clubs like Borussia Dortmund and Chelsea, and has captained the United States men's national team, scoring key goals in World Cup qualifiers. Valarie Allman, raised primarily in Hershey after her birth in Delaware on February 23, 1995, is a track and field athlete specializing in discus throw, winning Olympic gold medals in 2020 and 2024, becoming the first American woman to secure consecutive titles in the event. Nate Saint (19231956), born August 30, 1923, in Hershey, was a missionary aviator with the Summer Institute of Linguistics who pioneered aerial evangelism among Ecuador's Waorani people before his death during Operation Auca.
