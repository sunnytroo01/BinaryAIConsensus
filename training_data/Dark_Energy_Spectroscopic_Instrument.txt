Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument

 The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) is a ground-based astronomical survey instrument mounted on the 4-meter Mayall telescope at Kitt Peak National Observatory in Arizona, designed to probe the nature of dark energy by measuring the redshifts of tens of millions of galaxies and quasars across 14,000 square degrees of the sky, creating a three-dimensional map of the universe extending to nearly 11 billion light-years. Over its five-year primary survey, which began in May 2021, DESI uses a novel robotic fiber-positioning system with 5,000 fiber-optic positioners to capture spectra simultaneously from thousands of celestial objects per exposure, enabling precise measurements of baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO) and the growth of cosmic structure to test models of cosmic acceleration. Managed by the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory under the U.S. Department of Energy's Office of Science, DESI represents a Stage IV dark energy experiment, building on prior surveys like the Baryon Oscillation Spectroscopic Survey (BOSS) with unprecedented scale and precision.
DESI's scientific mission focuses on quantifying dark energy's influence on the universe's expansion history and large-scale structure evolution, potentially revealing deviations from the standard Lambda-CDM cosmological model through analyses of redshift-space distortions and galaxy clustering. Key innovations include its wide-field corrector optics for imaging faint targets up to magnitude 22.5 and a spectrograph array that resolves wavelengths from 360 to 980 nanometers, allowing classification of galaxies, quasars, and luminous red galaxies across cosmic epochs.
In March 2025, the DESI collaboration released baryon acoustic oscillation measurements from Data Release 2 (DR2), based on three years of observations (20212024) and incorporating nearly 15 million galaxies and quasars. Cosmology chains and data products from DR2 were subsequently released in October 2025. These results provide precise BAO constraints that refine measurements of cosmological parameters, including the dark energy equation of state. The BAO data alone are consistent with the flat CDM model but exhibit a 2.3 tension with CMB-derived parameters. When combined with CMB data, the results favor a dynamical dark energy model with a time-evolving equation of state (parametrized by w and w, with preferred values w > -1 and w < 0) over a constant cosmological constant at 3.1 significance. Including supernova data strengthens this preference to 2.84.2 depending on the supernova sample, supporting models such as non-phantom quintessence-like scenarios over the standard CDM model.
The project involves an international collaboration of over 900 scientists from more than 70 institutions, with construction completed in 2019 after a decade of development funded by a consortium including the DOE, NSF, and international partners. Early milestones include the creation of the largest 3D cosmic map in January 2022 from initial data, encompassing nearly 7 million objects, and the Early Data Release in June 2023 with spectra for nearly 2 million objects, which have already contributed to breakthroughs in understanding cosmic voids and the Lyman-alpha forest. By late 2024, DESI had reached a milestone of 50 million spectra. DESI's observations, expected to conclude in November 2025, promise to deliver transformative insights into the universe's fate, potentially confirming or challenging the dominance of dark energy as the driver of accelerated expansion.

Overview

Instrument Description

The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) is a multi-year survey instrument mounted on the Mayall 4-meter telescope at Kitt Peak National Observatory, designed to collect optical spectra from tens of millions of galaxies and quasars.
Its core function involves measuring redshifts of these celestial objects through optical spectroscopy, allowing scientists to trace the history of cosmic expansion as shaped by dark energy. One key observable in this effort is baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO), which serve as a cosmic standard ruler to gauge the universe's expansion over time.
DESI targets over 40 million objects, including galaxies, quasars, and stars, across 14,000 square degrees of the sky, encompassing roughly 11 billion years of cosmic history from the nearby universe to distant epochs. The survey's main operations began in 2021 and are scheduled to continue through 2026, producing a vast three-dimensional map of the universe.

Location and Operations

The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) is hosted at Kitt Peak National Observatory in Arizona, USA, where it is integrated with the Nicholas U. Mayall 4-meter Telescope. This site, managed by the National Optical-Infrared Astronomy Research Laboratory (NOIRLab), provides optimal conditions for ground-based astronomical observations in the northern sky. The project is led by Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, involving over 900 scientists from more than 70 institutions worldwide.
DESI conducts nights-only observations, leveraging a robotic fiber positioning system to align 5,000 optical fibers across an 8-square-degree field of view on the telescope's focal plane. This setup enables the simultaneous collection of spectra from up to 5,000 astronomical objects per exposure, with positioners adjusting autonomously between exposures to target new fields. On-site operations involve a team of researchers coordinating nightly scheduling, data acquisition, and real-time quality checks to ensure efficient survey progress.
The main survey encompasses approximately 1,000 nights of observation over five years, targeting tens of millions of galaxies and quasars to map cosmic structure. Spectra are obtained across a wavelength range of 360980 nm, with resolutions varying from R 2000 at shorter wavelengths to R 5000 at longer ones. Target selection for these observations integrates photometry from the Legacy Imaging Surveys.

Instrument Design

Spectrograph and Optics

The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) employs ten identical spectrographs to process light collected from 5,000 optical fibers, with each spectrograph handling 500 fibers via fiber-optic cables that integrate with the focal plane system. These spectrographs are housed in a thermally controlled enclosure to maintain stability, enabling simultaneous acquisition of high-quality spectra over a broad wavelength range. The design prioritizes high throughput and resolution to support precise measurements of galaxy redshifts and cosmological probes.
Each spectrograph divides incoming light using dichroic beamsplitters into three channels: blue (360555 nm), red (555656 nm), and infrared (656980 nm). The optical train includes a collimator to parallelize the light, a volume phase holographic (VPH) grating for dispersion, and a camera lens assembly to focus the spectrum onto the detector. The blue channel achieves a spectral resolution $ R = \lambda / \Delta\lambda $ of 2,0003,200, while the red and infrared channels reach 3,2004,100 and 4,1005,000, respectively, ensuring adequate separation of emission lines for target classification. Detectors are 4,096 4,096 pixel CCDs with 15 m pixels, cooled in cryostats to minimize noise, and the system delivers an overall throughput exceeding 70% across the operational wavelengths.
The wavelength coverage is optimized for key emission lines essential to DESI's science goals, including the Lyman- forest in quasars (z 1.92.7) detected in the blue channel, the doublet in emission-line galaxies (z 0.50.8) in the red channel, and H in bright galaxies (z 00.5) in the infrared channel. Quantum efficiency of the detectors surpasses 50% across the full band, with peaks above 85% in the 600900 nm range and over 60% beyond 900 nm, enhancing sensitivity for faint targets.
Calibration of the spectrographs relies on dedicated sky fibers and standard stars to achieve flux accuracy and minimize systematics. 20 dedicated sky fibers in total (approximately 2 per spectrograph) enable precise sky subtraction by modeling zodiacal and atmospheric emission, while 100 standard stars per field (10 per petal) provide spectrophotometric calibration, ensuring relative flux errors below 5% across the wavelength range. Internal LED illuminators further support fiber-to-fiber throughput uniformity checks during observations.

Fiber Positioning System

The Fiber Positioning System of the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) features 5,020 robotic positioners arrayed across a approximately 1-meter-diameter focal plane at the prime focus of the 4-m Mayall Telescope, enabling simultaneous spectroscopy of up to 5,000 targets over a 3.2-degree field of view. Each positioner, known as a "cobra" due to its serpentine actuation mechanism, employs two independent motorsone for azimuthal rotation and one for polar elevationto steer an optical fiber within a patrol region of roughly 12 mm in diameter. These motors, typically piezoelectric or DC brushless gear types, achieve a positioning accuracy better than 5 micrometers root-mean-square (RMS), far exceeding the <10-micrometer requirement for reliable target acquisition while minimizing focal ratio degradation in the fiber optics.
The positioners are organized in a hexapod-supported focal plane assembly, divided into 10 wedge-shaped petals with 500 positioners per petal in a dense hexagonal packing pattern averaging 10.4 mm center-to-center spacing. This configuration optimizes coverage density (about 667 fibers per square degree on-sky) while accommodating the instrument's wide-field corrector optics. Each fiber has a 107-micrometer core diameter, equivalent to 1.5 arcseconds on the sky, balancing light collection from point-like galaxies and quasars against inclusion of excess sky background. The hexapod mount provides six degrees of freedom for sub-micrometer adjustments to the entire focal plane, compensating for gravitational flexure and thermal variations to maintain alignment throughout observations.
Repositioning occurs in coordinated 30-minute cycles between exposures, with the robotic array reconfiguring all fibers in under 2 minutes to target new fields, followed by a typical 1,000-second science integration. This sequence supports DESI's high-throughput survey strategy, allowing over 376,000 total moves across the 5-year mission without significant duty-cycle loss. For redundancy in telescope pointing and focus, the system dedicates 80 guide fibers, back-illuminated for imaging by dedicated cameras, to provide real-time metrology and corrections during observations. The positioned fibers route captured light via 47.5-meter cable assemblies to the spectrographs for dispersion and detection.

Development and History

Project Origins

The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) project emerged as a response to the limitations of earlier galaxy surveys, particularly the Baryon Oscillation Spectroscopic Survey (BOSS), which demonstrated the power of baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO) for probing dark energy but required larger samples for Stage IV precision. In April 2009, the BigBOSS concept was proposed as a DOE-NSF Stage IV ground-based experiment to extend BAO measurements to higher redshifts and volumes, targeting spectra for over 20 million galaxies and quasars to achieve sub-percent accuracy on dark energy parameters.
Building on this, early 2011 saw unsolicited proposals submitted to the DOE: BigBOSS, led by Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL), and the competing DESpec concept from Fermilab, both aiming to upgrade an existing telescope for massively multiplexed spectroscopy. In September 2012, the DOE approved the mission need (CD-0) for a merged Mid-scale DESI (MS-DESI) project and selected the Nicholas U. Mayall 4-meter Telescope at Kitt Peak National Observatory for the upgrade, citing its dark skies, accessibility, and prior NSF investment as key advantages over alternatives like the Blanco Telescope. LBNL was assigned project management in December 2012, with initial leadership from the laboratory's physics division.
The scientific rationale was further detailed in LBNL-led white papers, including a submission to the 2013 Snowmass Community Planning Meeting, which positioned DESI as essential for measuring the universe's expansion history and growth of structure through redshift surveys of emission-line galaxies, luminous red galaxies, and quasars.
Construction funding reached approximately $75 million, including $56 million from the DOE Office of Science for instrument development and telescope modifications, supplemented by NSF grants for operations and imaging support, plus contributions from international partners and private foundations. International collaborations formalized in 2014, incorporating over 70 institutions from more than 20 countries to bolster expertise in instrumentation, data analysis, and theory.
These origins paved the way for DESI's evolution into full-scale construction starting in 2015.

Construction and Milestones

The construction of the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) began with major upgrades to the Nicholas U. Mayall 4-meter Telescope at Kitt Peak National Observatory, initiated in 2015 and spanning through 2019. These modifications centered on replacing the telescope's original prime focus instrumentation with a new prime focus cage and a sophisticated six-lens corrector optics system, designed to deliver a wide 3.2-degree field of view with high image quality across an 8-square-degree focal plane. The corrector, featuring lenses up to 1 meter in diameter, transformed the telescope's focal ratio from f/2.8 to f/3.8 (average over the field of view), enabling precise targeting for DESI's 5,000 robotic fiber positioners. First light for this upgraded optical system was achieved on April 1, 2019, marking a key step in integrating the instrument with the telescope.
Instrument assembly and integration occurred primarily at Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, led by the DESI collaboration under U.S. Department of Energy funding, from 2018 onward through early 2021. This phase involved fabricating and testing the core components, including the barrel assembly housing 10 spectrographs, the focal plane with its robotic positioners for fiber placement, and the extensive fiber optic system linking the telescope to the spectrographs. The positioners, each capable of sub-10-micron accuracy, underwent rigorous actuation testing to ensure reliable targeting of astronomical objects, addressing challenges in mechanical precision and thermal stability. Full instrument first light was attained on October 22, 2019, when the 5,000 fiber-optic "eyes" successfully captured spectra from the night sky, initiating on-site validation.
The COVID-19 pandemic introduced significant delays, halting on-site operations at Kitt Peak from March to November 2020 during the validation phase, which required remote adaptations for hardware and software testing. Commissioning resumed in December 2020 and concluded successfully by March 2021, allowing the main five-year spectroscopic survey to commence on May 17, 2021. In 2022, observations were paused from June to September due to the Contreras fire at Kitt Peak, but the project remained on track. Ongoing observations continue to build toward the survey's completion in 2026.

Surveys and Observations

Legacy Imaging Surveys

The Legacy Imaging Surveys consist of three complementary photometric projects designed to provide wide-field optical imaging over the northern and southern Galactic caps, serving as precursors to the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) observations. These surveysthe Dark Energy Camera Legacy Survey (DECaLS), the Mayall z-band Legacy Survey (MzLS), and the Beijing-Arizona Sky Survey (BASS)collectively cover approximately 14,000 square degrees in the DESI footprint, with imaging in the g, r, and z bands, and additional i-band data from DECaLS.
DECaLS utilizes the Dark Energy Camera on the Blanco 4 m telescope at Cerro Tololo Inter-American Observatory, imaging the region south of declination +32 (including both north and south Galactic caps) to depths of 5 = 24.7 mag in g, 23.9 mag in r, 23.0 mag in z, and approximately 22.5 mag in i for point sources under typical seeing conditions. In the northern Galactic cap, BASS employs the 90Prime camera on the Steward Observatory 2.3 m Bok telescope to observe in g and r bands to 5 depths of 24.0 mag and 23.4 mag, respectively, while MzLS uses the Mosaic-3 camera on the Nicholas U. Mayall 4 m telescope at Kitt Peak National Observatory for z-band imaging to a 5 depth of 22.5 mag; both cover declinations +32. This multi-instrument approach ensures uniform depth and calibration across the DESI footprint, with data processed using the Legacy Surveys pipeline to produce catalogs of galaxies and stars via multi-wavelength forced photometry.
The primary purpose of these surveys is to enable photometric redshift estimation and target selection for DESI's spectroscopic program, identifying millions of objects across key classes: bright galaxies (BGS) at low redshift (z < 0.4) for large-scale structure tracing, luminous red galaxies (LRGs) at intermediate redshifts (0.4 < z < 1.0), emission-line galaxies (ELGs) at higher redshifts (0.6 < z < 1.6) via lines like , and quasars (QSOs) spanning z < 2.1 for baryon acoustic oscillations and z > 2.1 for Lyman- forest analysis. These imaging data thus provide the foundational target lists that feed into DESI's fiber assignment for the main spectroscopic survey.
All data from the Legacy Imaging Surveys are publicly released through the NOIRLab Astro Data Archive and the dedicated Legacy Surveys portal, with the latest Data Release 10 (DR10, completed in 2023) including processed images, catalogs with over 1 billion sources, and software tools for analysis; earlier releases like DR9 covered the core DESI area.

Main Spectroscopic Survey

The Main Spectroscopic Survey of the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) constitutes the primary observational campaign, aimed at acquiring high-quality spectra for tens of millions of objects to trace the large-scale structure of the universe across cosmic time. This program leverages the instrument's capacity to simultaneously position 5000 optical fibers on targets within a 3.2-degree field of view on the Mayall 4-m telescope, enabling efficient mapping over one-third of the extragalactic sky. Target selection draws from photometric catalogs provided by the Legacy Imaging Surveys, ensuring precise identification of objects suitable for redshift measurement.
The survey's target breakdown emphasizes three main classes of galaxiesbright galaxies (BGS), luminous red galaxies (LRGs), and emission-line galaxies (ELGs)totaling approximately 34 million galaxies, selected for their utility in probing baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO) at low to intermediate redshifts. Additionally, about 5 million quasars are targeted to extend the BAO signal and map intergalactic medium absorption via the Lyman-alpha forest, while approximately 7 million stars from the Milky Way Survey (MWS) serve as calibration sources to refine spectrophotometric standards and study Galactic dynamics. These allocations prioritize high-density tracers for statistical power: BGS and LRGs offer robust samples at lower redshifts for nearby structure, ELGs provide volume-limited tracing at higher redshifts, and quasars enable absorption-line tomography of the cosmic web. Redshift coverage focuses on z < 1 for the galaxy samples to capture expansion history in the recent universe, and z > 2 for quasars to access the Lyman-alpha forest at earlier epochs.
Observations follow a tile-based strategy, dividing the survey footprint into ~11,000 hexagonal tiles of 7 square degrees each, with deliberate overlaps between adjacent tiles to achieve uniform coverage and mitigate fiber assignment inefficiencies. Each tile receives multiple exposurestypically 3-4 passes per targetduring dark and bright time allocations, enhancing signal-to-noise ratios and enabling robust redshift classification for faint objects. This redundancy is crucial for achieving >90% success rates in fiber targeting and spectral extraction, particularly for the fainter ELG and high-z quasar samples.
As of November 2025, DESI has collected over 50 million unique spectra from the Main Survey, approaching the planned total and enabling advanced cosmological analyses. By November 2025, the survey has observed nearly the full footprint, with ongoing data processing for future releases expected in 2026. This progress reflects efficient operations since the survey's start in May 2021, with ongoing refinements to fiber positioning and data processing ensuring high-fidelity redshifts for the majority of targets.

Data Releases

Early Data Release

The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument's Early Data Release (EDR), made public on June 13, 2023, provided the first batch of spectroscopic data from its survey validation phase conducted between December 2020 and June 2021. This release encompassed nearly two million spectra, totaling 1,769,157 useful spectra from 1,712,004 unique targets, including approximately 1.13 million galaxies, 90,000 quasars, and 496,000 stars. The data were derived from 2,480 exposures spanning 80 terabytes, focusing on validating the instrument's performance and target selection pipelines.
The EDR covered a partial area of the sky, approximately 1,390 square degrees, overlapping with regions from the Legacy Imaging Surveys Data Release 9 for photometric validation and target selection confirmation. Redshifts extended up to z 2.1 for key galaxy samples such as luminous red galaxies (LRGs) and emission-line galaxies (ELGs), with quasar spectra reaching higher redshifts up to z 3.5 to probe intervening absorption features. This coverage enabled initial tests of the DESI fiber positioning system and spectrograph resolution, confirming high data quality with minimal systematic errors in redshift assignments across diverse target classes.
These findings demonstrated the instrument's readiness for the main survey without significant deviations from predicted clustering patterns.
Access to the EDR is provided through the DESI data portal at data.desi.lbl.gov/public/edr, including raw spectra, reduced data products, and catalogs of derived properties such as redshifts and classifications. Redshift estimation and classification are handled by the Redrock pipeline, which assigns quality flags (e.g., ZWARN=0 for high-confidence redshifts) based on template fitting across 10 spectral channels per fiber. Users can query the data via the Astro Data Lab or download full datasets using Globus transfers, supporting community analyses of the validated Legacy Survey photometry integrated with DESI spectra.

Data Release 1

Data Release 1 (DR1) of the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) was made publicly available on March 19, 2025, marking the first comprehensive release of main survey data from the instrument's initial year of operations, spanning observations from May 2021 to June 2022. This release encompasses high-confidence redshifts for 18.7 million unique objects, including 13.1 million galaxies, 1.6 million quasars, and 4 million stars, derived from over 18.6 million useful spectra. Building briefly on the Early Data Release, DR1 significantly expands the dataset with Year 1 main survey observations, enabling detailed analyses of cosmic structure.
The survey coverage in DR1 extends across approximately 11,000 square degrees of the sky, primarily in the Northern Galactic Cap, with redshift measurements reaching up to z 3.5 for quasar targets and lower for galaxies. This includes contributions from bright-time (targeting brighter, low-redshift objects) and dark-time (focusing on fainter, higher-redshift targets) observations, processed through DESI's spectroscopic pipeline to ensure data quality. Redshift success rates exceed 95% for the primary targets, reflecting robust performance in secure redshift identification via the Redrock fitting algorithm.
Key validations from DR1 include precise baryon acoustic oscillation (BAO) measurements at multiple redshifts, achieving approximately 0.5% precision, which provides stringent constraints on cosmological parameters and supports investigations into dark energy evolution. Data processing pipelines facilitated spectra fitting for redshift estimation and the creation of clustering catalogs optimized for large-scale structure studies, such as galaxy and quasar clustering analyses. All DR1 products, including raw spectra, redshifts, and value-added catalogs, are accessible to the public via the National Energy Research Scientific Computing Center (NERSC), promoting widespread scientific use.

Data Release 2

Data Release 2 (DR2) incorporates observations from the second year of the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument's main survey, adding to the existing dataset and achieving a total of approximately 25 million spectra. Released on October 6, 2025, this update provides derived data products, including cosmology chains generated via Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods for analyzing baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO) and other cosmological parameters. As of November 2025, only the derived data products and cosmology chains have been released, with the full spectra and redshifts expected in subsequent months.
Key new elements in DR2 feature enhanced analysis of the Lyman- forest, utilizing over 820,000 quasar spectra to measure the BAO scale with improved statistical precision of 0.65% isotropically at an effective redshift of z = 2.33. Additionally, it introduces galaxy clustering catalogs extending to redshift z = 4, derived from more than 14 million galaxies and quasars across six samples, enabling robust measurements of large-scale structure evolution.
Improvements in DR2 include refined corrections for fiber collisions in the galaxy and quasar samples, which mitigate observational biases and enhance the accuracy of clustering statistics. These advancements yield a BAO precision of 0.4% when combining galaxy/quasar and Lyman- forest results, surpassing previous releases. The release also integrates DR2 BAO measurements with external datasets, such as the Planck cosmic microwave background likelihoods, to constrain cosmological models more tightly.
Access to DR2 products is facilitated through an updated data portal at the National Energy Research Scientific Computing Center (NERSC), which includes mock catalogssuch as 100 Saclay and 300 CoLoRe-QL simulationsfor validation and user simulations of the dataset. These resources support external researchers in exploring cosmological inferences prior to the full DR2 spectrum release. Building briefly on refinements from Data Release 1, DR2 doubles the Lyman- forest sample size for greater high-redshift sensitivity.

Key Scientific Results

Cosmic Mapping Achievements

In April 2024, the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) unveiled the largest three-dimensional map of the universe to date, charting the positions of nearly 6 million galaxies and quasars spanning 11 billion light-years of cosmic history. This map, derived from the instrument's first year of observations, captures the large-scale structure of the cosmos with unprecedented detail, enabling precise measurements of baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO) that serve as a cosmic ruler. Subsequent data releases, including Data Release 1 in early 2025, expanded this to over 18 million objects, with approximately 13 million galaxies, pushing the total beyond 20 million in later analyses.
DESI's mapping resolves the evolution of cosmic structure from low redshifts (z 0.1) to high redshifts (z 3), tracing how galaxies clustered over billions of years as the universe expanded. This range allows for the measurement of the acoustic scale, the comoving sound horizon at the drag epoch, fixed at r_d = 147 Mpc in fiducial analyses, which calibrates distances across cosmic epochs with percent-level precision. By quantifying the clustering patterns imprinted by these oscillations, DESI achieves resolutions that reveal the hierarchical assembly of filaments, walls, and voids in the cosmic web.
These achievements extend to testing models of gravity beyond general relativity through measurements of the linear growth factor f(z), which describes how density perturbations amplify over time. DESI's high-fidelity maps constrain parameters in modified gravity theories, such as f(R) and scalar-tensor models, by comparing observed growth rates against predictions, often yielding bounds at the few-percent level across redshift bins. Additionally, the maps provide indirect hints toward resolving the Hubble tension, as discrepancies in expansion history inferred from structure growth align with potential early-universe physics modifications, though full reconciliation remains ongoing.
Visual representations of DESI's maps, such as fan plots projecting galaxy distributions in angular wedges and density slices slicing through the volume, vividly illustrate the cosmic web's architecture. These depictions highlight vast voids spanning hundreds of megaparsecs alongside dense filaments connecting galaxy clusters, underscoring the map's role in visualizing the universe's skeleton.

Implications for Dark Energy

The March 2025 baryon acoustic oscillation (BAO) measurements from Data Release 2 (DR2) of the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI), based on three years of observations encompassing more than 14 million galaxies and quasars, combined with cosmic microwave background (CMB) and supernova (SNe) data, indicate a preference for dynamical dark energy models over the constant vacuum energy (cosmological constant) in the CDM model. These results, derived from BAO and redshift-space distortion analyses, show deviations in the equation-of-state parameter w(z)w(z)w(z) from the value -1, with favored parameters in the w0waw_0 w_aw0waCDM parametrization lying in the quadrant where w0>1w_0 > -1w0>1 and wa<0w_a < 0wa<0. This is consistent with non-phantom quintessence models involving evolving scalar fields. The dynamical model is preferred over CDM at 3.1 significance for the combination of DESI BAO and CMB data, with the preference increasing to 2.84.2 when SNe datasets are included, depending on the specific SNe sample. Official communications describe these as strengthened hints for evolving dark energy, noting that the results do not yet reach definitive levels (such as 5) to rule out CDM, and that unknown systematic effects in one or more datasets remain possible.
In the CDM framework, DESI DR2 constraints yield =0.690.01\Omega_\Lambda = 0.69 \pm 0.01=0.690.01, consistent with prior CMB results but with reduced uncertainties from the expanded dataset. However, dynamic models are preferred, as the data exhibit tensions with a constant at up to 3 in low-redshift bins, supporting alternatives like quintessence fields that allow w>1w > -1w>1. Some analyses link this evolution to astrophysical processes, such as non-singular black hole formation converting matter into dark energy, which aligns with DESI's observed growth in structure and eases neutrino mass constraints.
Updates in November 2025 from a Yonsei University-led study, incorporating DESI BAO and corrected supernova progenitor age biases, indicate that the universe's expansion has transitioned to deceleration at the present epoch, with dark energy weakening over time at 99.999% confidence. This finding partially resolves the Hubble constant (H0H_0H0) tension by reconciling local measurements with CMB-derived values, suggesting a dynamic dark energy model where acceleration peaked in the past and is now fading.
Projections for DESI's Years 3-5 data anticipate reaching 1\sigma precision on dark energy evolution, enabling definitive tests of w(z)w(z)w(z) deviations and further discrimination between constant and dynamic models through enhanced BAO and RSD coverage across 40 million galaxies.
