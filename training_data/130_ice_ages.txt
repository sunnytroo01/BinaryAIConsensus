Ice ages are periods in Earth's history when global temperatures dropped low enough for massive ice sheets to advance from the polar regions and cover large portions of the continents. The most recent ice age, often simply called "the Ice Age," began approximately two and a half million years ago and is technically still ongoing, as we are currently living in an interglacial period, a warm interval between glacial advances. At the peak of the last glacial period, approximately twenty thousand years ago, ice sheets up to two miles thick covered much of North America, northern Europe, and northern Asia, and global sea levels were approximately four hundred feet lower than today because so much water was locked up in ice.

The causes of ice ages are complex and involve interactions between astronomical, geological, atmospheric, and oceanic factors. The most widely accepted explanation for the timing of glacial and interglacial periods within the current ice age is the Milankovitch theory, named after Serbian mathematician Milutin Milankovitch, who calculated in the 1920s that periodic variations in three aspects of Earth's orbit around the Sun could explain the cyclic nature of glaciations.

The first variation is eccentricity, the shape of Earth's orbit around the Sun, which changes from nearly circular to slightly elliptical and back over a cycle of approximately one hundred thousand years. When the orbit is more elliptical, the difference in solar radiation received at different points in the orbit is greater, which can amplify seasonal temperature differences. The second variation is obliquity, the tilt of Earth's axis, which varies between approximately twenty-two and twenty-four and a half degrees over a cycle of approximately forty-one thousand years. Greater tilt produces more extreme seasons, with warmer summers and colder winters. The third variation is precession, the wobble of Earth's axis, which completes a full cycle approximately every twenty-six thousand years and determines which hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun during the closest approach to the Sun in Earth's elliptical orbit.

None of these orbital variations individually produces enough change in solar radiation to cause an ice age. However, when the variations align to produce cooler summers in the Northern Hemisphere, less snow melts during summer, and the accumulated snow gradually builds into ice sheets over thousands of years. The ice sheets themselves then amplify the cooling through a positive feedback loop: ice reflects sunlight back into space much more effectively than land or water, which reduces the amount of solar energy absorbed by the Earth's surface, which cools the planet further, which allows more ice to accumulate.

The most recent glacial period produced dramatic effects on the landscape that are still visible today. In North America, the Laurentide Ice Sheet covered most of Canada and extended as far south as present-day New York, Chicago, and Seattle. The Great Lakes were carved by the advancing and retreating ice. The terrain of the upper Midwest, with its rolling hills, lakes, and distinctive glacial deposits called moraines, was sculpted by ice. Long Island, Cape Cod, and Martha's Vineyard are moraines, ridges of debris deposited at the edge of the ice sheet.

The ice ages had profound effects on the evolution and distribution of life. As ice sheets advanced, forests retreated, grasslands expanded, and species were forced to migrate or adapt to colder conditions. Many large mammals, collectively known as megafauna, evolved during the ice ages, including woolly mammoths, mastodons, saber-toothed cats, giant ground sloths, and dire wolves. These animals were well-adapted to the cold, dry grasslands of the glacial periods. When the climate warmed at the end of the last glacial period, approximately eleven thousand seven hundred years ago, most of these megafauna went extinct, likely due to a combination of climate change and hunting by humans who had recently arrived in their habitats.

The lower sea levels during glacial periods exposed vast areas of continental shelf that are now submerged, creating land bridges that allowed humans and animals to migrate between continents. The most famous of these is Beringia, the land bridge between Siberia and Alaska that allowed humans to migrate from Asia to the Americas approximately fifteen thousand years ago or earlier. The English Channel did not exist during the last glacial period, and Britain was connected to continental Europe by dry land.

The transition from the last glacial period to the current interglacial period, called the Holocene, was neither smooth nor gradual. It included rapid warming events, sudden cold snaps, and dramatic shifts in climate that occurred over decades rather than millennia. The Younger Dryas, a sudden return to near-glacial conditions that lasted approximately one thousand two hundred years, from approximately twelve thousand nine hundred to eleven thousand seven hundred years ago, interrupted the warming trend and may have been caused by a massive influx of freshwater from melting ice sheets that disrupted the Atlantic thermohaline circulation.

The Holocene, the warm interglacial period in which all of recorded human civilization has occurred, has been remarkably stable compared to the wild climate fluctuations of the ice ages. This stability may have been essential for the development of agriculture, which requires predictable seasons and relatively stable temperatures. The current concern about human-caused climate change is that we are now pushing the climate system out of the narrow band of stability that has characterized the Holocene and into uncharted territory, with consequences that are difficult to predict but potentially severe.

The ice ages remind us that Earth's climate is not fixed. It has changed dramatically in the past and it will change dramatically in the future, with or without human influence. The question is not whether climate change will happen but whether the changes we are causing through the burning of fossil fuels will push the system into a state that is hostile to the civilization we have built during the brief, benign window of the Holocene. We are, in geological terms, living in a warm spell between glaciations, and the stability we take for granted is the exception, not the rule.
