Antiquities Act

 The Antiquities Act of 1906 (54 U.S.C. 320301 et seq.) is a United States federal statute enacted on June 8, 1906, and signed by President Theodore Roosevelt, empowering the President to unilaterally proclaim national monuments on federal lands to safeguard prehistoric, historic, and scientific objects or localities of public interest, with designations limited to "the smallest area compatible with the proper care and management" of such features. The law responded to widespread looting and destruction of archaeological sites on public lands, establishing requirements for federal permits to excavate or remove antiquities and imposing penalties for unauthorized actions.
Over its history, the Act has enabled 18 presidents to issue nearly 300 proclamations protecting more than 800 million acres across diverse landscapes, from Theodore Roosevelt's inaugural designation of Devils Tower in 1906 to expansive marine and terrestrial reserves, often preserving areas later incorporated into national parks like the Grand Canyon. While facilitating swift conservation amid congressional inaction, the statute's vague criteria and absence of explicit revocation authority have fueled persistent controversies over executive overreach, with critics arguing that massive designationssuch as the 1.35-million-acre Bears Ears National Monument created by President Obama in 2016bypass legislative input, restrict local economic uses like grazing, mining, and energy development, and encroach on states' interests in managing vast federal landholdings in the West. These tensions peaked when President Trump reduced Bears Ears and Grand Staircase-Escalante by over 2 million acres in 2017 to restore multiple-use opportunities, a move later reversed by President Biden, underscoring the Act's potential for partisan swings in land policy without judicial limits on presidential discretion to modify prior designations.

Origins and Enactment

Background and Motivations

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, archaeological sites in the American Southwest faced severe threats from unregulated looting and vandalism, primarily driven by commercial demand for prehistoric artifacts among collectors, dealers, and museums. Public lands lacked any systematic protection or permitting requirements, allowing pot hunters to excavate indiscriminately, often destroying architectural features and contextual data essential for scientific study. For instance, at Mesa Verde in Colorado, the Wetherill brothers discovered the Cliff Palace in 1888 and conducted extensive excavations, removing pottery, tools, and human remains for sale, while vandals used explosives to breach walls and extracted wooden beams for firewood, leaving kivas and structures in partial ruin by the early 1900s.
Similar desecration occurred at Chaco Canyon in New Mexico, where Richard Wetherill homesteaded near Pueblo Bonito in the 1890s, facilitating the Hyde Exploring Expedition's operations from 1896 to 1899; this effort dismantled entire rooms and shipped thousands of artifacts, including timbers, to the American Museum of Natural History, contributing to structural instability without regard for site preservation. Other sites, such as Hovenweep's burial mounds, were ravaged by cattlemen scattering remains, and Palatki in Arizona saw walls stripped for commercial gain and fuel. These activities not only depleted collections of movable objects but also accelerated erosion and collapse, as evidenced by reports from explorers like J. Walter Fewkes, who in 1896 highlighted the irreversible loss to hasty, profit-motivated digging.
Archaeologist Edgar Lee Hewett, who began surveying southwestern ruins in 1896, played a pivotal role in highlighting these threats through detailed documentation, including his 1904 report on New Mexico's historic and prehistoric sites, which cataloged widespread vandalism and urged federal intervention due to states' inability to enforce protections on federal lands. Influenced by such empirical observations rather than abstract environmental ideals, Hewett joined a 1905 American Anthropological Association committee to advocate for legislation, emphasizing that unchecked smuggling and desecrationoften by both amateurs and purported scientific expeditions exporting artifacts abroadnecessitated centralized authority to safeguard ruins as irreplaceable records of prehistoric cultures. This focus on causal threats from human exploitation, rather than generalized conservation, formed the core impetus for federal action.

Legislative Process and Original Intent

The Antiquities Act emerged from congressional efforts starting in the late 1890s to address widespread looting and destruction of archaeological sites on federal lands in the American Southwest, where pothunters were excavating and selling prehistoric artifacts from ruins of ancient pueblos and cliff dwellings. Representative John F. Lacey (R-IA), chairman of the House Committee on Public Lands, introduced multiple bills between 1900 and 1906, drawing on reports from archaeologist Edgar Lee Hewett documenting the threats; earlier versions failed due to concerns over federal overreach and insufficient penalties. The final bill, H.R. 11016, passed the House on June 5, 1906, and the Senate on June 8, 1906, before President Theodore Roosevelt signed it into law that same day as Chapter 3061 of the 34th Statutes at Large.
Congressional debates emphasized targeted protection of discrete "ruins, articles, or objects of antiquity" and sites of scientific interest, such as fossil beds or prehistoric villages, rather than expansive land management or habitat preservation; sponsors like Lacey argued the measure would enable scientific study and prevent commercialization, with no discussion of withdrawing vast territories for ecological purposes. The Act's text granted the President authority to declare national monuments "situated upon the lands owned or controlled by the Government of the United States" but explicitly required that any reserved parcels "be confined to the smallest area compatible with the proper care and management of the objects to be protected." This limitation reflected intent to safeguard specific features, often measured in acres rather than millions, excluding private lands entirely and focusing penaltiesfines up to $500 or imprisonment up to three monthson unauthorized excavation, injury, or removal of antiquities without permits issued by the relevant department secretary.
While broader conservation debates, including the ongoing Hetch Hetchy Valley controversy over damming in Yosemite, highlighted tensions between preservation and development, the Act's architects prioritized archaeological integrity over landscape-scale interventions, viewing presidential proclamations as administrative tools subordinate to congressional oversight rather than unilateral conservation policy. Regulations for permit-based examinations, requiring reports to the Secretary of the Interior or Agriculture, underscored the legislative goal of regulated scientific access to federal antiquities, not indefinite land lockup.

Core Provisions and Mechanisms

Scope of Presidential Authority

The Antiquities Act of 1906 empowers the President to unilaterally proclaim national monuments on federal lands through public proclamation, bypassing the need for congressional approval or legislative processes typical in other land management statutes such as those establishing national parks. This authority stems from Section 2 of the Act, which vests discretion in the President to protect resources without prior consultation or environmental review beyond the proclamation itself.
Under the Act, national monuments encompass areas containing "historic landmarks, historic and prehistoric ruins, and any other objects of historic or scientific interest" located on lands owned or controlled by the federal government. This broad definition extends to both cultural artifacts, such as ruins and structures, and natural features deemed scientifically significant, allowing proclamations to safeguard diverse sites from threats like looting or commercial exploitation.
The President's proclamation may reserve adjacent federal lands necessary for protection, but limits such reservations to "the smallest area compatible with the proper care and management of the objects to be protected." Post-designation, the Secretary of the Interior assumes administrative control, with authority to issue regulations governing access, use, and preservation activities within the monument to prevent injury or destruction. This framework originally placed monuments under the Department of the Interior's jurisdiction, though subsequent executive actions have occasionally involved other agencies for management.

Limitations and Administrative Oversight

The Antiquities Act mandates that any national monument proclaimed by the President shall be limited to "the smallest area compatible with the proper care and management of the objects to be protected," a provision designed to constrain designations to discrete sites of historic, prehistoric, or scientific interest rather than expansive territories. This language reflects an intent to prevent overreach by tying reservations directly to the tangible objects warranting protection, such as ruins or landmarks, as evidenced by the Act's emphasis on "historic landmarks, historic and prehistoric structures, and other objects of historic or scientific interest." Early implementations adhered to this site-specific focus, with initial monuments typically encompassing limited acreage around particular features.
To enforce protections, the Act imposes criminal penalties on individuals who appropriate, excavate, injure, or destroy protected antiquities without authorization, including fines of up to $500, imprisonment for not more than 90 days, or both. It further establishes a permit system administered by the Secretaries of the Interior, Agriculture, and the Smithsonian Institution (or their designees), requiring federal land managers' approval for legitimate scientific examinations, excavations, or removals of objects, subject to regulations ensuring preservation and public access to findings. These mechanisms prioritize controlled scholarly access while prohibiting commercial or unauthorized activities, with collections from permitted work to be deposited in designated public institutions.
The Federal Land Policy and Management Act of 1976 (FLPMA) incorporated the Antiquities Act into modern federal land policy by repealing outdated withdrawal authorities but explicitly preserving presidential proclamation powers under the 1906 law, without altering or broadening its core constraints. FLPMA's multiple-use and sustained-yield mandates apply to retained public lands generally, yet defer to Antiquities Act designations for monument management, maintaining the original Act's emphasis on limited, object-specific reservations amid broader resource planning. This integration reinforced administrative oversight through coordinated agency roles, such as those of the Bureau of Land Management, without introducing mechanisms to expand monument scale or authority.

Historical Applications

Initial Designations Under Roosevelt and Early Presidents

President Theodore Roosevelt issued the first proclamation under the Antiquities Act on September 24, 1906, designating Devils Tower in Wyoming as a national monument to preserve a unique geologic formation and surrounding landscape of scientific interest. The designation covered approximately 1,153 acres, reflecting the Act's intent to protect specific objects of antiquity or scientific value with the smallest compatible area. Roosevelt followed with additional early designations focused on discrete archaeological ruins and natural features, such as El Morro National Monument in New Mexico (proclaimed December 8, 1906, about 1,279 acres) for its prehistoric inscriptions and Montezuma Castle in Arizona (proclaimed December 8, 1906, roughly 847 acres) to safeguard cliff dwellings. These initial monuments averaged around 3,300 acres, adhering closely to the preservation of targeted historic and prehistoric sites rather than expansive territories.
Subsequent presidents continued this pattern of designating smaller, site-specific areas. President William Howard Taft proclaimed Jewel Cave National Monument in South Dakota on February 16, 1908 (initially about 1,485 acres), emphasizing its karst formations as objects of scientific import. Under President Woodrow Wilson, Casa Grande Ruins National Monument in Arizona was established on August 3, 1918 (approximately 473 acres), to protect the largest surviving structure of the Hohokam civilization's prehistoric ruins. Wilson's designations, including Aztec Ruins in New Mexico (proclaimed January 24, 1923, about 507 acres, though initiated earlier), prioritized archaeological integrity over broad land withdrawals.
Presidents Warren G. Harding and Calvin Coolidge extended protections to additional modest prehistoric and cultural sites, such as Pipe Spring National Monument in Arizona (proclaimed May 31, 1923, by Harding, 40 acres) for its Mormon pioneer and Native American history, and Tuzigoot National Monument in Arizona (proclaimed July 25, 1939, but planning under Coolidge era, about 834 acres). Coolidge's proclamations included smaller archaeological areas like Sunset Crater in Arizona (1928, initial 3,040 acres), maintaining the emphasis on antiquities protection without large-scale reservations. Collectively, these early designations through the 1920s totaled under several million acres across dozens of sites, but individual monuments remained focused on compact areas necessary for the care of specific historic landmarks, prehistoric structures, and scientific objects, aligning with the Act's original parameters for limited federal intervention.

Mid-20th Century Uses and Shifts Toward Conservation

Following Franklin D. Roosevelt's ascension to the presidency, designations under the Antiquities Act remained relatively modest in scale and primarily focused on preserving specific historic, prehistoric, or natural features, such as the Channel Islands National Monument established on April 26, 1938, encompassing Anacapa and Santa Barbara islands for their archaeological ruins, endemic flora, and marine ecosystems of scientific interest. Roosevelt proclaimed nine such monuments between 1933 and 1943, including Joshua Tree in 1936 for its unique desert flora and geology, and Capitol Reef in 1937 for prehistoric ruins and fossil-bearing strata, aligning closely with the Act's emphasis on discrete "antiquities" or objects of historic and scientific value.
A notable deviation occurred with Roosevelt's proclamation of Jackson Hole National Monument on March 15, 1943, covering 221,773 acres adjacent to Grand Teton National Park to safeguard the Teton Range's scenic landmarks, geological formations, and wildlife habitats, including elk migration corridors, under the Act's "objects of scientific interest" clause. This larger-scale application, motivated by conservation needs amid declining wildlife populations, prompted critiques from Wyoming officials and ranchers who argued it deviated from the Act's original intent to protect limited archaeological or historic sites, effectively enabling broader federal land withdrawals that hindered local grazing and development.
Under Harry S. Truman, the Act saw limited use, with the designation of Effigy Mounds National Monument on October 25, 1949, to preserve prehistoric Native American burial mounds shaped like animals in Iowa, maintaining a focus on archaeological antiquities. Dwight D. Eisenhower similarly prioritized historic sites, proclaiming the Thomas Edison National Monument (now part of Edison National Historical Park) on July 14, 1956, for its laboratories and inventions of scientific and historic significance, though his 1961 Chesapeake and Ohio Canal National Monument a 184-mile linear corridorrevived congressional opposition, particularly from Eastern representatives, over perceived overreach in reserving extensive public lands beyond strict antiquities protection.
By 1960, presidents had designated approximately 61 national monuments under the Act, reflecting a cumulative expansion from Theodore Roosevelt's initial 18, with several mid-century examples incorporating wildlife and ecological features justified as scientific objects, indicative of emerging post-World War II priorities in natural resource preservation amid growing public awareness of environmental degradation, yet without the vast scales seen in later decades.

Expansions and Interpretations

Late 20th and Early 21st Century Designations

In December 1978, President Jimmy Carter invoked the Antiquities Act to designate 13 national monuments in Alaska, encompassing over 56 million acres of federal lands, primarily to safeguard them from potential mineral and oil development amid stalled congressional negotiations over the Alaska National Interest Lands Conservation Act. These included sites like Wrangell-St. Elias, later enlarged to 10.95 million acres, marking the first substantial expansion in monument scale beyond the smaller, site-specific protections typical under earlier presidents. The action withdrew vast tracts from multiple-use considerations, aligning with Carter's environmental priorities but prompting backlash from Alaskan stakeholders favoring resource extraction.
President Bill Clinton continued this trend of larger designations with the proclamation of Grand Staircase-Escalante National Monument on September 18, 1996, covering 1.7 million acres in southern Utah to preserve paleontological, geological, and biological features. The monument's creation effectively precluded a proposed coal mine by Andalex Resources, reflecting Clinton administration goals to prioritize conservation over extractive industries without congressional approval. At roughly 1.7 million acres, it exceeded prior monuments in scope, shifting focus from discrete historic objects to expansive landscapes.
The Obama administration accelerated designations, proclaiming 29 new national monuments totaling approximately 5.6 million acres between 2009 and 2017, alongside enlargements of existing ones. Notable among these was Bears Ears National Monument, established on December 28, 2016, spanning 1.35 million acres in southeastern Utah to protect cultural artifacts, sacred tribal sites, and ecosystems through collaboration with five Native American tribes. Proclamations emphasized co-benefits for indigenous heritage and biodiversity, yet encompassed broad public lands far larger than the "smallest area" compatible with the objects of scientific interest stipulated in the Act's original language.
This era saw average monument sizes swell from early 20th-century norms of thousands of acressuch as Theodore Roosevelt's 1,192-acre Devils Tower in 1906to millions, often enveloping entire regions without detailed delineation of minimal boundaries justified solely by antiquities protection. Carter's Alaska actions set the precedent for mega-designations tied to policy agendas like thwarting development, a pattern amplified under Clinton and Obama to advance ecological and cultural preservation at the expense of local resource access. Empirical data from federal records indicate over 60 million acres added via these three administrations alone, dwarfing cumulative prior uses and highlighting a causal shift toward using the Act for de facto wilderness withdrawals.

Scaling of Monument Sizes Beyond Original Parameters

The Antiquities Act of 1906 mandates that national monument designations be limited to "the smallest area compatible with the proper care and management of the objects to be protected." This provision reflected the original intent to preserve discrete objects of historic or scientific interest, such as archaeological sites or unique geologic features, without encompassing vast landscapes.
Early designations adhered closely to this size constraint, with the first eleven monuments averaging approximately 3,300 acres. While exceptions like the Grand Canyon National Monument (800,000 acres in 1908) occurred, the typical scale remained under 10,000 acres, focusing on specific artifacts or formations rather than expansive territories. In contrast, recent proclamations frequently exceed 1 million acres, as seen with the 506,814-acre Avi Kwa Ame National Monument designated in 2023.
This scaling deviation stems from interpretive expansions broadening "objects of scientific interest" to include entire ecosystems and habitats, justifying larger areas for comprehensive protection and management. Such shifts prioritize ecological integrity over isolated features, enabling designations that reserve broad swaths of land to safeguard biodiversity and environmental processes.
Cumulative designations under the Act have encompassed hundreds of millions of acres by the 2020s, with one president alone protecting over 550 million acres, contributing to federal oversight of substantial portions of U.S. territory and amplifying concerns regarding centralized land control.

Legal Framework and Challenges

Presidential Power to Modify or Revoke Monuments

The Antiquities Act of 1906 contains no explicit provision authorizing a president to modify or revoke a national monument proclaimed by a predecessor. This statutory silence has fueled ongoing debate over whether such authority is implied through the Act's grant of presidential discretion to proclaim monuments "as he deems necessary and proper." Proponents of revocation power argue that the absence of prohibition, combined with the executive's general authority to undo prior administrative actions, permits alteration or abolition, while opponents contend that the one-way nature of protective designations precludes unilateral reversal absent congressional intent.
Historical precedents demonstrate instances of presidential modifications to earlier designations, though full revocations remain rare and contested. For example, presidents including Woodrow Wilson and Calvin Coolidge issued proclamations adjusting the boundaries or status of monuments established under Theodore Roosevelt, effectively reducing protected areas without formal challenge at the time. These actions were implicitly upheld through continued administrative practice, suggesting an early executive understanding of flexible authority under the Act, though they involved partial adjustments rather than outright abolition.
Interpretations by the Department of Justice's Office of Legal Counsel (OLC) have evolved, revealing inconsistency in executive branch views. A 1938 opinion from the Attorney General, issued during Franklin D. Roosevelt's administration, concluded that the president lacks authority to revoke a monument once proclaimed, emphasizing the Act's protective purpose. In contrast, a 2025 OLC memorandum affirmed that the Act permits presidents to alter prior declarations, including by reducing or eliminating protections if the original "historic landmarks, historic and prehistoric structures, and other objects of historic or scientific interest" no longer warrant monument status. This shift highlights interpretive reliance on textual silence and historical practice over fixed prohibition.
The Federal Land Policy and Management Act (FLPMA) of 1976 reinforces deference to the Antiquities Act while limiting subordinate officials. FLPMA prohibits the Secretary of the Interior from modifying or revoking withdrawals, including national monuments, without specific congressional authorization, but carves out exceptions for actions under preexisting laws like the Antiquities Act. Section 204(c) of FLPMA implicitly acknowledges presidential prerogative by barring secretarial revocations "except as specifically authorized by Act of Congress," thereby preserving executive discretion under the 1906 statute without extending it to non-presidential actors. This framework underscores the Act's delegation to the president alone for both establishment and potential adjustment of monuments.

Judicial Rulings and Interpretations

In Utah Association of Counties v. Bush (2003), the Tenth Circuit Court of Appeals upheld President Bill Clinton's 1996 designation of the 1.7 million-acre Grand Staircase-Escalante National Monument, ruling that the Antiquities Act grants the president broad discretion to determine the "smallest area compatible" with protection of objects of historic or scientific interest, without requiring exhaustive analysis of alternatives or public input. The court rejected arguments that the designation exceeded statutory bounds due to its size or exclusion of local interests, affirming that judicial review is limited to whether the proclamation rationally connects to the Act's purposes.
Federal district courts have similarly upheld President Joe Biden's 2021 restoration and expansion of Bears Ears National Monument to approximately 1.36 million acres, dismissing challenges by the state of Utah and counties in 2023 on grounds that the Antiquities Act imposes no explicit size or acreage limits and allows presidential judgment on protective needs. The U.S. District Court for the District of Utah granted the federal government's motion to dismiss, emphasizing the Act's delegation of authority without mandating consideration of economic impacts or state consent, though appeals continued into 2024 with arguments questioning potential extremes in scope. These rulings preserved the expansions while leaving open federalism-based claims in ongoing litigation.
The U.S. Supreme Court declined to review challenges to presidential designations in Murphy Co. v. Biden (2024), rejecting certiorari in a timber industry suit against President Barack Obama's 2017 expansion of the Cascade-Siskiyou National Monument by 148,000 acres, thereby maintaining lower court affirmations of expansive authority without addressing underlying debates over executive overreach. Justices Neil Gorsuch and Brett Kavanaugh dissented, signaling interest in examining whether such actions align with the Act's original intent for limited, site-specific protections rather than broad land withdrawals impacting resource use. This denial preserved the status quo of judicial deference but highlighted unresolved tensions regarding monument scale and interference with prior land management statutes like the Oregon and California Railroad Act.

Controversies and Critiques

Claims of Executive Overreach and Federalism Concerns

Critics contend that the Antiquities Act of 1906 grants presidents excessive unilateral authority, contravening the separation of powers by allowing routine land withdrawals without congressional approval or defined limits on scope. Originally enacted to protect archaeological sites and "objects of historic or scientific interest" in the "smallest area compatible with proper care and management," the Act was envisioned as an emergency measure rather than a mechanism for broad, ongoing federal land policy. Originalist interpretations emphasize that its framers did not intend perpetual executive dominance over vast territories, viewing such uses as a departure from constitutional checks where Congress holds primary authority over public lands under Article IV, Section 3. This has led to accusations of executive overreach, as presidents have designated monuments encompassing millions of acres without veto mechanisms, size caps, or requirements for public input, effectively substituting presidential fiat for legislative deliberation.
Federalism concerns arise from the Act's tendency to preempt state sovereignty over resource management and local planning. In Utah, for instance, President Bill Clinton's 1996 proclamation of the 1.7 million-acre Grand Staircase-Escalante National Monument withdrew federal lands containing significant coal and uranium deposits, overriding state-endorsed development plans and local economic priorities without gubernatorial consent. Similarly, designations like Bears Ears in 2016 under President Barack Obama encompassed sacred sites but also disrupted Utah's multi-use land strategies, prompting state lawsuits arguing that the Act enables federal imposition on state jurisdiction, diminishing cooperative federalism. Utah lawmakers have repeatedly sought exemptions or reforms, asserting that such actions ignore state input and centralize control in Washington, eroding the balanced federal-state partnership envisioned in the Property Clause.
The absence of procedural safeguards in the Act facilitates partisan oscillations in land policy, amplifying overreach risks. Empirical patterns show Democratic presidents disproportionately expanding monuments since the late 20th centurysuch as Obama's 553 million acres designatedwhile Republican administrations, like Donald Trump's 2017 reductions of Bears Ears and Grand Staircase-Escalante, attempt reversals, creating policy instability without congressional guardrails. This swing enables ideologically driven uses, where incoming executives alter prior designations absent thresholds for acreage or scientific justification, undermining consistent governance and inviting abuse across administrations. Critics from both conservative and progressive circles highlight this potential for executive excess, urging reforms to restore balance.

Economic and Resource Development Impacts

The designation of national monuments under the Antiquities Act has imposed restrictions on mining, grazing, timber harvesting, and energy development across millions of acres of federal lands, withdrawing them from multiple-use management and precluding potential economic activities in those sectors. For instance, monuments generally prohibit new mineral entry and leasing, limit grazing allotments, and restrict oil and gas development, thereby forgoing revenues from extractive industries that could otherwise contribute to local and national economies. Opponents of such designations argue that these constraints result in lost tax revenues and employment opportunities, particularly in rural western communities dependent on resource extraction.
A prominent example is the 1996 establishment of the Grand Staircase-Escalante National Monument, encompassing about 1.7 million acres in southern Utah, which included the Kaiparowits Plateau coal field. This field contains an estimated 11.36 to 16 billion tons of recoverable subbituminous and bituminous coal, with a potential market value of $221 to $312 billion based on contemporaneous pricing. The U.S. Geological Survey assessed the resources as high-quality, low-sulfur coal suitable for power generation, but noted that approximately 55% may be unmineable due to geological factors; however, the monument's creation halted advanced-stage development plans by Andalex Resources, including a proposed mine and associated slurry pipeline and power plant that would have extracted up to 90 million tons annually. Utah state officials and industry analyses projected that full development could have generated over $1 trillion in long-term economic value from the reserves, alongside direct mining employment and ancillary jobs in transportation and energy production.
Similar restrictions have impacted grazing and other uses; for example, monument management plans often reduce animal unit months (AUMs) available for livestock, affecting ranching operations that support rural livelihoods across the West, where public lands grazing contributes to approximately $1.5 billion in annual economic activity and over 10,000 jobs nationwide. In affected counties near large monuments like Grand Staircase-Escalante, critics documented declines in related sectors post-designation, including reduced timber processing and school enrollments tied to population outflows from forgone resource jobs, though broader economic studies attribute net growth to tourism offsets. These opportunity costs prioritize preservation over dynamic resource utilization, locking substantial mineral and energy potentialssuch as uranium and oil under Bears Ears National Monumentaway from development amid rising national demands for critical minerals and affordable energy.

Recent Developments and Reforms

Actions Under Trump, Biden, and Ongoing Litigation

In December 2017, President Donald Trump invoked the Antiquities Act to reduce the size of Bears Ears National Monument in Utah by approximately 85%, from 1.35 million acres designated by President Obama in 2016 to about 201,876 acres, dividing the remaining area into two smaller units to align with the Act's requirement for the "smallest area compatible" with protecting objects of historic or scientific interest. The reductions were justified by the administration as restoring the Act's original intent of limited, targeted protections rather than broad land withdrawals that bypassed local input and economic considerations, emphasizing consultations with Utah officials and stakeholders who opposed the original expansive boundaries. Similarly, Grand Staircase-Escalante National Monument was halved from nearly 1.9 million acres to about 1 million acres, with the proclamation citing excessive scope beyond core antiquities and the need to balance federal preservation with multiple-use access for grazing, energy development, and recreation.
President Joe Biden reversed these modifications on October 8, 2021, restoring Bears Ears to roughly its original Obama-era boundaries plus an additional 81,000 acres, totaling about 1.36 million acres, and reinstating Grand Staircase-Escalante to approximately 1.87 million acres, arguing the Trump cuts unlawfully diminished protections for cultural, archaeological, and natural resources central to the Act's purpose. In March 2023, Biden designated Avi Kwa Ame National Monument in Nevada, encompassing 506,814 acres of desert landscape sacred to multiple Tribal Nations, to safeguard geological formations, archaeological sites, and wildlife habitats from potential mining and energy extraction. These actions continued a pattern of expansive designations, with the administration defending them as faithful exercises of presidential discretion under the Act to address modern threats like climate change and development pressures.
As of October 2025, ongoing litigation challenges Biden's restorations and new designations, primarily from states, local governments, and industry groups alleging violations of the Act's "smallest area compatible" clause through oversized boundaries that exceed historic or scientific necessities and infringe on state sovereignty and resource rights. The Department of Justice has defended the designations in federal courts, asserting broad presidential authority to interpret and apply the Act's criteria without judicial second-guessing, while separate opinions from the Office of Legal Counsel affirm that subsequent presidents may revoke or modify prior monuments if prior protections are deemed unwarranted. Cases involving Bears Ears, Grand Staircase-Escalante, and monuments like Chuckwalla remain pending in U.S. district courts, with appeals likely to test the Act's boundaries on reversal powers amid conflicting precedents from earlier challenges to Trump's reductions.

Congressional Efforts to Limit or Reform the Act

In 1976, Congress enacted the Federal Land Policy and Management Act (FLPMA), which imposed procedural requirements on federal land withdrawals, including those effected through national monument designations under the Antiquities Act. Section 204 of FLPMA mandates that the Secretary of the Interior provide notice to Congress and consult with state governors for proposed withdrawals exceeding 5,000 acres, alongside public hearings to assess impacts. These provisions aimed to curb unilateral executive action by ensuring state and local input, reflecting bipartisan concerns over unchecked presidential authority; however, successive administrations have frequently proceeded with designations via direct proclamation, bypassing full FLPMA processes on the grounds that Antiquities Act authority supersedes.
Subsequent legislative efforts have sought to impose stricter limits, often driven by western states' objections to large-scale designations lacking gubernatorial concurrence. For instance, bills like H.R. 3990 (National Monument Creation and Protection Act) in earlier Congresses proposed caps on monument sizes and mandatory environmental reviews to prevent "abuse of executive authority in the designation of excessively large monuments." Similarly, reform proposals have included default size limits of 640 acres with escalating requirements for larger areas, such as congressional ratification and state approval, as advanced in 2017 House measures.
In the 119th Congress, momentum for reform intensified with the introduction of H.R. 521 and S. 220 (Ending Presidential Overreach on Public Lands Act) on January 16 and 23, 2025, respectively, which amend the Antiquities Act to eliminate presidential authority for designating or expanding monuments, reserving that power exclusively to Congress. H.R. 2645 (Congressional Oversight of the Antiquities Act), introduced April 3, 2025, further advances oversight by requiring enhanced congressional involvement in the designation process. These bills respond to data indicating frequent state-level oppositionsuch as in Utah and Nevada, where governors have protested multiple designations for restricting resource development without adequate consultationfueling arguments that the Act's original intent for limited protections has been exceeded. House hearings in February 2025 highlighted these abuses, with witnesses testifying on the need for size caps (e.g., default 5,000 acres) and congressional approval for areas over 100,000 acres to restore federalism principles.
