 Actinium is a radioactive chemical element with the symbol Ac and atomic number 89, classified as the first member of the actinide series in the periodic table. It appears as a soft, silvery-white metal that slowly tarnishes in air due to oxidation and is intensely radioactive, with its most stable isotope, actinium-227, having a half-life of 21.77 years. Chemically, actinium behaves similarly to the rare earth elements, particularly lanthanum, exhibiting a predominant +3 oxidation state and an electron configuration of 6d 7s.
Discovered in 1899 by French chemist Andr-Louis Debierne while studying pitchblende (a uranium ore), actinium was independently confirmed in 1902 by Friedrich Oskar Giesel through similar extraction methods from rare earth oxides. The name derives from the Greek word "aktinos," meaning "ray," reflecting its intense radioactivity. Actinium occurs naturally only in trace amounts in uranium ores, yielding approximately 0.1 milligrams per tonne of pitchblende, and is about 150 times more radioactive than radium.
Physically, actinium is a solid at room temperature with a density of 10 g/cm, a melting point of 1050C, and a boiling point of 3200C. It forms cubic crystals and reacts with air to produce actinium(III) oxide, while in water it liberates hydrogen gas. All known isotopes are radioactive, with actinium-225 (half-life 9.92 days) being particularly notable for its applications.
The primary use of actinium is in medicine, specifically the isotope actinium-225 in targeted alpha therapy for cancer treatment, where it delivers localized radiation to tumors such as prostate cancer, leukemia, and neuroendocrine tumors with minimal damage to healthy tissue. As of 2025, commercial-scale production of Ac-225 has begun, supporting expanded clinical trials including for breast cancer. Due to its extreme radioactivity and toxicity, actinium requires specialized handling and has no significant industrial applications beyond research. Ongoing efforts focus on improving production methods for medical isotopes like Ac-225 to support clinical trials.

Discovery and History

Discovery

Actinium was first identified in 1899 by French chemist Andr-Louis Debierne while working as an assistant to Pierre and Marie Curie at the Sorbonne in Paris. Debierne examined the residues left after extracting polonium and radium from pitchblende, a uranium-rich ore, and detected a new radioactive substance that exhibited strong activityapproximately 100,000 times greater than uraniumand co-precipitated with rare earth elements during fractional precipitation processes. He formally announced this discovery on October 16, 1899, initially associating the activity with titanium impurities, but further investigations revealed it as a distinct element resembling thorium in chemical behavior. However, later scholarly analysis has debated whether Debierne's 1899 sample truly contained actinium or was instead a low-activity thorium-uranium compound, with some historians attributing primary credit for the element's identification to Giesel's later work.
In 1900, Debierne named the element "actinium," derived from the Greek word aktis meaning "ray" or "beam," reflecting its intense radioactivity. His work was published in the Comptes Rendus de l'Acadmie des Sciences, detailing the separation methods involving repeated precipitations with ammonia and ammonium sulfide to isolate the new radioactive material from associated elements like iron and titanium.
Independently, German chemist Friedrich Oskar Giesel rediscovered actinium in 1902 while purifying radium preparations from pitchblende at the University of Berlin. Giesel isolated a strongly radioactive fraction that behaved like the lanthanides and initially named it "emanium" in 1904, believing it to be a new rare-earth element associated with radium emanation (radon). A brief controversy arose over priority and identity, as Giesel's samples showed identical radioactive propertiessuch as a 3.7-second half-life for its emanation and 41-minute activityto Debierne's actinium, confirmed through phosphorescence tests in Paris in 1904. The element's name was ultimately standardized as actinium, with Debierne receiving primary credit for the discovery.

Naming and Etymology

Actinium derives its name from the Greek word aktinos, meaning "ray" or "beam," a reference to the element's intense radioactivity, which causes it to emit alpha particles that can be detected as luminous rays on a fluorescent screen. The name was proposed by French chemist Andr-Louis Debierne, who announced the discovery of the element in 1900 after isolating it from pitchblende residues during his collaboration with Pierre and Marie Curie. Debierne chose the term to highlight the radioactive emanations observed in the new substance, drawing an analogy to the Greek root used in words like "actinism," which denotes the property of radiation.
The naming of actinium was not without controversy, stemming from an independent discovery by German chemist Friedrich Oskar Giesel in 1902. Giesel isolated a similar radioactive element from the same source and initially named it emanium in 1904, derived from "emanation," reflecting its ability to produce gaseous radioactive decay products that emit rays capable of illuminating zinc sulfide screens. By 1904, spectroscopic and chemical analyses confirmed that actinium and emanium were the same element, prompting a debate over nomenclature. After discussions in the scientific community, Debierne's original name, actinium, was adopted in 1907, as it had priority due to the earlier announcement and better encapsulated the element's radiant properties; Giesel himself relinquished emanium in favor of the consensus.
This resolution established actinium (symbol Ac) as the official name, recognized by the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC). The etymological choice underscores the era's fascination with radioactivity, linking the element to the pioneering work on X-rays and radium, though actinium's rays were subtler and primarily alpha emissions rather than the more visible beta or gamma types.

Occurrence and Production

Natural Occurrence

Actinium occurs naturally only in trace amounts as the radioactive isotope actinium-227 (Ac), which has a half-life of 21.77 years and is a member of the uranium-235 decay series. This isotope forms through the beta decay of thorium-227, itself a descendant of uranium-235, and exists in secular equilibrium with its short-lived decay products, including thorium-227, radium-223, and radon-219. In nature, Ac is not found as a free element but is present in minute concentrations within uranium- and thorium-bearing minerals, primarily due to the ongoing decay processes in these ores.
The element's abundance in the Earth's crust is extremely low, estimated at 5.5 10 mg/kg, making it one of the rarest elements. Actinium is associated with uranium ores such as pitchblende (uranium dioxide, UO), where it constitutes about 0.15 mg per metric ton of ore (0.15 ppb) in typical uranium mineral deposits. These traces arise from the natural decay chain of primordial uranium-235, which comprises about 0.72% of natural uranium, ensuring a steady but minuscule production of Ac over geological timescales. No significant deposits of actinium as a primary mineral exist, and its separation from ores is impractical due to the low yields and chemical similarities to other actinides and lanthanides.

Production Methods

Actinium occurs naturally only in trace amounts, primarily as the isotope actinium-227 in uranium ores, but its production for research and medical applications relies almost exclusively on artificial methods due to extremely low natural concentrations (on the order of 10 of uranium content by mass).
Historically, actinium was first isolated in pure form in the mid-20th century through chemical separation from neutron-irradiated radium targets. In 1950, French Hagemann at Argonne National Laboratory developed a method involving the bombardment of radium-226 with neutrons in a nuclear reactor to produce actinium-227 via the reaction ^{226}Ra(n,)^{227}Ra ^{227}Ac + ^-, followed by ion-exchange chromatography and precipitation to separate it from radium and other impurities, yielding milligram quantities of high-purity actinium. This reactor-based approach remains a foundational technique for producing actinium-227, the longest-lived isotope (half-life 21.77 years), with modern facilities like those at Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL) using similar neutron irradiation of radium-226 to generate parent nuclides for subsequent decay chains.
For the medically important isotope actinium-225 (half-life 9.92 days), production has expanded significantly since the 2010s to support targeted alpha therapy. The primary method involves the decay of thorium-229 (half-life 7,340 years), extracted from legacy stockpiles of uranium-233 produced during Cold War-era nuclear programs; actinium-225 is periodically "milked" from thorium-229 generators using cation-exchange chromatography, providing a global supply of approximately 63 GBq per year (as of 2021) from facilities in the US (ORNL), Russia, and Europe. An alternative accelerator-based route uses high-energy proton irradiation (70200 MeV) of thorium-232 targets to induce spallation reactions, directly yielding actinium-225 along with minor actinium-227; this process, scaled up by the US Department of Energy's Tri-Lab effort at Brookhaven, Los Alamos, and ORNL, has distributed over 40 GBq since 2015 through chemical separation via anion- and cation-exchange resins, achieving batches of 1.853.7 GBq with high purity (>99.9%).
Since 2021, Ac-225 production has increased with new commercial and accelerator methods. In 2024, TerraPower Isotopes began market supply using Th-229 decay, free of long-lived contaminants. SpectronRx initiated commercial-scale production of high-purity Ac-225 in October 2025 to bolster global availability for cancer therapies. NorthStar Medical Radioisotopes is developing an electron beam accelerator method irradiating Ra-226 targets for scalable, domestic production. A groundbreaking clinical trial using accelerator-produced Ac-225 began in summer 2025, marking the first human use of this source. IAEA projections indicate these advances will meet growing demand through 2030.
Emerging methods address supply limitations and co-produced contaminants. Proton bombardment of radium-226 at medium energies (around 1625 MeV) via the reaction ^{226}Ra(p,2n)^{225}Ac produces carrier-free actinium-225 with minimal isotopic impurities, though radium handling and radon gas management pose challenges; pilot-scale production at TRIUMF in Canada yields test quantities suitable for preclinical studies. Similarly, photon irradiation of radium-226 using linear accelerators (peak cross-section at 16.8 MeV) generates actinium-225 through ^{226}Ra(,n)^{225}Ra ^{225}Ac + ^-, offering a non-proton alternative under development for higher throughput. Actinium metal itself can be prepared in microgram amounts by reducing actinium fluoride (AcF_3) with lithium or magnesium vapor at 1,2001,400 K, or actinium chloride with calcium at 1,000 K, but such preparations are rare due to the element's intense radioactivity and are typically performed only for fundamental studies.

Physical and Atomic Properties

Physical Characteristics

Actinium is a soft, silvery-white radioactive metal that exhibits a characteristic blue glow in the dark due to its intense radioactivity, a phenomenon similar to that observed in radium. This luminescence arises from the excitation of air molecules by alpha particles emitted during its decay.
The element has a density of approximately 10 g/cm at room temperature, making it comparable in density to lead. Its melting point is 1050C (1323 K), and the boiling point is estimated at 3200C (3473 K), indicating high thermal stability typical of early actinides. Actinium adopts a face-centered cubic (fcc) crystal structure with a lattice constant of 5.311 , confirming its close-packed metallic arrangement as determined by early crystallographic studies.
Due to its scarcity and radioactivity, detailed measurements of mechanical properties are limited, but actinium is known to be malleable and ductile, akin to lanthanum, with no reported hardness values exceeding those of soft metals. Thermal conductivity is low at about 0.12 W/cmK, reflecting poor heat transfer characteristic of heavy actinides. Electrical resistivity data is sparse, but actinium behaves as a typical metal conductor, though its reactivity with air necessitates handling in inert atmospheres to prevent oxidation.

Atomic and Electronic Structure

Actinium (Ac) is a chemical element with atomic number 89, placing it as the first member of the actinide series in the periodic table. It resides in period 7, group 3, and the d-block, serving as a bridge between the transition metals and the f-block lanthanides and actinides, though its chemistry initiates the 5f electron series in heavier congeners.
The ground-state electron configuration of neutral actinium atoms is 6d 7s, consisting of the closed-shell radon core (, Z=86) plus three valence electrons distributed across the 6d and 7s orbitals. This arrangement yields a shell structure of 2.8.18.32.18.9.2 electrons from the K through O shells, with the outermost shell featuring one 6d electron and two 7s electrons. The term symbol for the ground state is D/, reflecting the unpaired 6d electron's spin-orbit coupling. Unlike the lanthanides, which fill 4f orbitals early, actinium's configuration emphasizes 6d involvement, influenced by relativistic effects that contract inner orbitals and stabilize the valence shells in heavy elements.
Actinium's atomic radius is empirically estimated at 188195 pm, while its covalent radius measures 215 pm and the van der Waals radius is 260 pm; these values indicate a relatively large atomic size consistent with its position at the bottom of group 3. The first ionization energy, required to remove the 7s valence electron and form Ac, is 5.17 eV (499 kJ/mol), with subsequent energies rising sharply: 12.1 eV (1170 kJ/mol) for the second and approximately 19.7 eV (1900 kJ/mol) for the third, facilitating the stable +3 oxidation state by completing the octet in the 6d orbital. Electronegativity is low at 1.1 on the Pauling scale, underscoring actinium's electropositive, metallic character and tendency to form ionic compounds.
Theoretical calculations reveal that actinium's electronic structure involves multi-configurational mixing in low-lying excited states, with energies below 35,000 cm dominated by 6d7s transitions; relativistic effects, including spin-orbit coupling, play a key role in orbital energies and bonding properties. In ionic forms like Ac, the configuration simplifies to 6d, with an ionic radius of about 112 pm for coordination number 6, influencing its coordination chemistry in aqueous solutions.

Chemical Properties and Compounds

Reactivity and Oxidation States

Actinium is a highly reactive, electropositive metal that tarnishes slowly in dry air but oxidizes rapidly in moist air, forming a dense, adherent layer of actinium(III) oxide (AcO) that provides some protection against further corrosion. The reaction with oxygen proceeds as 4Ac + 3O 2AcO, and the metal can ignite when heated in air. This oxide formation highlights actinium's affinity for oxygen, similar to other early actinides and lanthanides.
The predominant and most stable oxidation state of actinium is +3, resulting in the colorless Ac ion, which dominates its aqueous chemistry and closely mirrors the behavior of La due to comparable ionic radii (approximately 1.12 for six-coordinate Ac) and electronic structure. Theoretical calculations confirm that higher oxidation states, such as +4, are unstable in aqueous solution, with the Ac/Ac redox potential being highly negative, preventing their formation under standard conditions. No stable +2 state has been observed, though transient lower states may occur in specific reducing environments.
In terms of reactivity with water, actinium shows limited interaction at room temperature, remaining largely inert to cold water, but it reacts more readily with hot water or steam to produce actinium(III) hydroxide and hydrogen gas: 2Ac + 6HO 2Ac(OH) + 3H. The metal dissolves readily in dilute mineral acids, such as hydrochloric or nitric acid, liberating hydrogen and yielding the soluble Ac ion: Ac + 3HCl AcCl + (3/2)H. This acid reactivity underscores its strong metallic character and positions it as a powerful reducing agent. Actinium does not react with bases under normal conditions.
Actinium also combines directly with halogens at elevated temperatures to form trihalides, including AcF, AcCl, AcBr, and AcI, all of which are hygroscopic and soluble in water, facilitating coordination with ligands in solution. Its overall chemical profile, dominated by the +3 state and lanthanide-like properties, limits applications but is crucial for targeted alpha therapy where Ac coordination chemistry is exploited for bioconjugation.

Inorganic Compounds

Actinium, as the first member of the actinide series, predominantly exhibits the +3 oxidation state in its inorganic compounds, reflecting its ionic radius and electronic configuration akin to the lanthanides. These compounds are typically ionic solids with high coordination numbers, often 9, due to the large size of the Ac cation (effective ionic radius of approximately 1.22 for CN=9). Preparation of pure actinium compounds is challenging owing to its radioactivity and scarcity, but structural data have been obtained from early crystallographic studies and more recent spectroscopic analyses.
The most studied inorganic compound is actinium(III) oxide, AcO, which adopts the hexagonal LaO-type structure (space group P-3m1) with a coordination number of 7 for Ac. In this structure, each actinium atom is surrounded by seven oxygen atoms, with Ac-O bond lengths averaging 2.502.78 , longer than expected for pure AcO possibly due to impurities like radium. AcO is formed by ignition of actinium oxalate or hydroxide in air at high temperatures (around 1000C) and appears as a white powder that is stable in air but hydrolyzes slowly in water.
Actinium halides are well-characterized and follow the UCl-type hexagonal structure (space group P6/m) for chlorides, bromides, and iodides, with a coordination number of 9. Actinium(III) fluoride, AcF, however, crystallizes in the LaF-type hexagonal structure (space group P6/m), also with CN=9 and Ac-F bond distances of approximately 2.10 . AcCl exhibits anisotropic bonding, with apical Ac-Cl distances of 3.09 and equatorial ones of 3.03 , prepared by reacting AcO with HCl gas or ammonium chloride. Similarly, AcBr and AcI show comparable structures but with longer bond lengths, and all halides are hygroscopic, white to pale yellow solids that dissolve in water to form acidic solutions.
Actinium(III) sulfide, AcS, forms a cubic structure of the CeS type with lattice parameter a = 0.899 nm, synthesized by heating actinium oxalate in hydrogen sulfide at 1400C. It is a black, air-stable powder, unlike the more reactive lanthanide sulfides, and decomposes at higher temperatures to yield actinium metal and sulfur.
Other notable inorganic compounds include actinium(III) phosphate, AcPO, which adopts a monazite-type structure similar to lanthanide phosphates, and actinium nitrate, Ac(NO)xHO, a hydrated salt used in early separations due to its solubility. In aqueous solutions, the actinium aquo ion ^{3+} predominates with an average hydration number of approximately 11, as determined by extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) spectroscopy, exhibiting Ac-O distances of 2.63 . These compounds highlight actinium's preference for hard ligands and high coordination, influencing its potential in radiochemical applications.

Organometallic Compounds

Organometallic compounds of actinium, defined as those featuring direct actinium-carbon - or -bonds, remain unknown and unexplored experimentally. This lack of development stems from the extreme challenges associated with handling actinium, whose isotopes are all radioactive with short half-livesthe most stable, actinium-227, has a half-life of 21.77 yearsnecessitating specialized facilities and limiting synthetic efforts. In contrast, organometallic complexes have been synthesized and studied for subsequent actinides from thorium through californium, including notable examples such as thorium and uranium metallocenes that exhibit reactivity toward small molecules like CO and H.
Actinium's coordination chemistry with organic ligands, while not organometallic, has advanced primarily in the context of radiopharmaceutical applications. These involve chelating agents like DOTA (1,4,7,10-tetraazacyclododecane-1,4,7,10-tetraacetic acid) and its derivatives, which bind Ac through nitrogen and oxygen donors to stabilize the ion for targeted alpha therapy against cancer. Such complexes demonstrate high thermodynamic stability but do not involve direct metal-carbon bonding. Theoretical investigations into potential actinium organometallics, such as hypothetical tris(cyclopentadienyl)actinium species, predict predominantly ionic AcC interactions with minimal 5f orbital involvement, aligning with actinium's large ionic radius and +3 oxidation state dominance. However, no experimental verification exists due to the aforementioned practical barriers.

Nuclear Properties and Isotopes

Isotopic Composition

Actinium possesses no stable isotopes, with all 36 known isotopes being radioactive and spanning mass numbers from 203 to 236. The isotopic composition of naturally occurring actinium is dominated by the radioisotope $ ^{227}\mathrm{Ac} $, which constitutes essentially 100% of the element found in nature. This isotope arises primarily from the decay chain of $ ^{235}\mathrm{U} $ in uranium ores, where actinium occurs in trace amounts, typically around 0.1 grams per ton of ore.
$ ^{227}\mathrm{Ac} $ has an atomic mass of 227.027750(3) u and a half-life of 21.77 years, decaying mainly via beta minus emission (98.62%) to $ ^{227}\mathrm{Th} $ or, to a lesser extent (1.38%), via alpha decay to $ ^{223}\mathrm{Fr} $. Its relatively long half-life compared to other actinium isotopes allows it to accumulate in secular equilibrium within the uranium-235 decay series, making it the characteristic isotope for natural samples.
Trace quantities of $ ^{228}\mathrm{Ac} $ also occur naturally as an intermediate in the thorium-232 decay chain, but its short half-life of 6.15 hours prevents significant buildup, resulting in negligible contribution to the overall isotopic composition. Other isotopes, such as $ ^{225}\mathrm{Ac} $ (half-life 9.92 days), are either artificially produced or present in extremely low natural abundances from rare decay pathways and do not factor into the representative terrestrial composition. As a result, the standard atomic weight of actinium is conventionally given as , reflecting the mass of $ ^{227}\mathrm{Ac} $.

Nuclear Stability and Decay

Actinium possesses no stable isotopes, with all known isotopes being radioactive and exhibiting half-lives ranging from nanoseconds to 21.77 years. The element's nuclear instability arises from its position in the actinide series, where proton-to-neutron ratios lead to excess neutrons or energy states favoring radioactive decay. Thirty-six isotopes of actinium have been characterized, spanning mass numbers from 203 to 236, but only a few have half-lives exceeding hours, underscoring the element's inherent radioactivity. In 2022, the neutron-deficient isotope $ ^{204}\mathrm{Ac} $ (half-life ~50 ms) was discovered, expanding knowledge of light actinium isotopes.
The longest-lived isotope, actinium-227, has a half-life of 21.77 years and serves as the primordial isotope in natural decay chains. It undergoes primarily beta minus decay to thorium-227 with a branching ratio of 98.62% and an average decay energy of 0.045 MeV, while a minor branch (1.38%) proceeds via alpha decay to francium-223 with an energy of 5.042 MeV. This dual decay mode positions 227Ac as a key intermediate in the uranium-235 decay series (4n+3 chain), where it contributes to the production of subsequent alpha- and beta-emitting daughters.
Other significant isotopes include actinium-225, which has a half-life of 9.92 days and decays exclusively by alpha emission to francium-221, initiating a chain of four alpha decays that release substantial energy for targeted applications. Heavier isotopes such as 228Ac (half-life 6.15 hours) decay predominantly by beta minus emission to thorium-228, while lighter ones like 224Ac (half-life 2.78 hours) favor a mix of electron capture and alpha decay. In general, neutron-deficient isotopes below mass 225 tend toward alpha decay or electron capture, whereas neutron-rich ones above 227 prefer beta minus decay, reflecting trends in actinide nuclear structure where alpha emission dominates for odd-odd nuclei seeking even-even stability. These decay patterns contribute to actinium's role in natural thorium and uranium series, with no evidence of spontaneous fission as a primary mode.

Applications

Medical Uses

Actinium-225 (Ac), an alpha-emitting radioisotope with a half-life of approximately 10 days, is primarily utilized in targeted alpha therapy (TAT) for cancer treatment due to the high linear energy transfer (LET 80 keV/m) of its alpha particles, which induce irreparable double-strand DNA breaks in tumor cells while limiting damage to adjacent healthy tissue owing to their short range (50100 m). In TAT, Ac is chelated to targeting vectors such as monoclonal antibodies or small molecules that bind to cancer-specific antigens, enabling selective delivery to malignant sites. This approach has shown promise in preclinical and early clinical studies for its ability to achieve sustained tumor regression, particularly in metastatic settings resistant to conventional therapies.
The most advanced applications of Ac involve prostate cancer, where it is conjugated to prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) inhibitors like Ac-PSMA-617, leading to significant prostate-specific antigen (PSA) reductions in patients with metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC). For instance, phase I/II trials have reported PSA declines of 50% in approximately 60% of treated patients, with some achieving undetectable PSA levels and radiographic responses, alongside manageable toxicity profiles primarily involving xerostomia due to salivary gland uptake. Ongoing multicenter trials, such as the ACCEL study evaluating Ac-PSMA-62, aim to optimize dosing and assess long-term outcomes in PSMA-positive advanced prostate cancer.
Beyond prostate cancer, Ac-based TAT is being investigated for hematologic malignancies, including acute myeloid leukemia (AML), where Ac-lintuzumab targets CD33-positive blasts, demonstrating complete remissions in relapsed/refractory cases within phase I trials. Applications extend to solid tumors such as neuroendocrine tumors, glioblastoma, and colorectal cancer, with conjugates like Ac-DOTA-anti-CEA for CEA-expressing metastases showing tumor uptake and preliminary antitumor effects in early-phase studies. The decay daughter Bi, with a 46-minute half-life, is also employed in similar TAT regimens for shorter-range applications, enhancing therapeutic precision in these contexts.
Production challenges, including limited supply from cyclotron irradiation of thorium-232 or radium-226 generators, have historically constrained widespread adoption, but recent advancements in accelerator-based methods and commercial-scale production as of 2025 (e.g., facilities by SpectronRx and Cardinal Health) are supporting expanded clinical trials that commenced in 2025. Despite these hurdles, Ac's potency positions it as a leading radionuclide for next-generation radiopharmaceuticals, with over a dozen active trials worldwide evaluating its safety and efficacy across diverse oncologic indications.

Industrial and Research Applications

Due to its intense radioactivity and scarcity, actinium has limited industrial applications, primarily centered on its historical use as a neutron source in specialized equipment. Actinium-227, the longest-lived isotope, is mixed intimately with beryllium to form compact neutron sources, where alpha particles emitted during its decay interact with beryllium-9 nuclei to produce neutrons via the (, n) reaction. These sources have been used in research settings for nuclear calibration and radiation detection experiments, where its predictable alpha emission profile helps standardize detectors and instruments used in particle physics and materials analysis. Beyond these, actinium plays a key role in fundamental studies of actinide chemistry, particularly in understanding coordination behavior and ionic radii within the f-block elements. For instance, recent experiments have utilized protein scaffolds to isolate and crystallize actinium ions, revealing unexpected eight-coordinate geometries that challenge prior models of its chemical similarity to lanthanides. Such investigations, often conducted at national laboratories, contribute to broader actinide science, including separation techniques and environmental behavior of radioactive elements. Actinium is typically produced in milligram quantities for these purposes through neutron irradiation of radium-226 in nuclear reactors, underscoring its niche but essential role in advancing nuclear and chemical research.

Safety and Precautions

Health Hazards

Actinium presents significant health hazards primarily due to its high radioactivity, with isotopes such as ^{227}Ac and ^{225}Ac emitting alpha particles that pose minimal external risk but severe internal dangers upon ingestion or inhalation. Once internalized, actinium exhibits biokinetics similar to other trivalent actinides, with low gastrointestinal absorption (f_1 5 10^{-4}) and limited lung absorption to blood (type M, ~5%), but the fraction that enters the bloodstream distributes primarily to the skeleton (~45% retention) and liver (~30%), where it persists with long biological half-lives of approximately 50 years in bone. This prolonged retention amplifies the risk of chronic radiation exposure, leading to cellular damage through ionization, DNA strand breaks, chromosomal aberrations, and oxidative stress via reactive oxygen species.
The primary long-term health effect is carcinogenesis, particularly osteosarcoma, liver cancer, and other malignancies in radiosensitive organs, as alpha particles deposit high energy over short ranges within cells. Internalized alpha- and beta-emitting radionuclides like actinium contribute to ionizing radiation, classified as carcinogenic to humans (Group 1) by the International Agency for Research on Cancer. Lifetime cancer risk coefficients for actinium-227 exposure are estimated at 6.1 10^{-10} per Bq ingested and 2.1 10^{-9} per Bq inhaled (moderate absorption), underscoring its potency even at trace levels. Historical cases, such as liver tumors observed in patients administered thorotrast (containing decay products akin to actinium), highlight the potential for hemopoietic disorders and fibrosis as well.
Acute high-dose exposures, though rare outside laboratory or production settings, can induce radiation syndrome, including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, skin erythema, hair loss, and hematopoietic suppression, potentially progressing to multi-organ failure and death; ingestion of even microgram quantities of ^{227}Ac may be fatal due to its specific activity of approximately 73 Ci/g. External exposure from gamma emissions is negligible for shielding purposes, but contamination risks emphasize the need for strict controls to prevent dust formation or aerosol generation during handling. No specific chemical toxicity independent of radiation has been documented, with hazards dominated by radiological effects.

Handling and Storage

Due to its intense radioactivity, primarily from alpha particle emission, actinium must be handled exclusively by trained personnel in controlled laboratory environments equipped for radiological work. All individuals involved require specific radiation safety training, such as that mandated by institutional environmental health and safety (EHS) programs, to ensure compliance with principles like ALARA (as low as reasonably achievable) for minimizing exposure. Annual limits on intake are 0.3 Ci for ^{225}Ac and 0.0004 Ci for ^{227}Ac. Handling typically employs indirect methods, including long-handled tools, shielded syringes, and enclosed systems like gloveboxes or hot cells, to avoid direct contact and reduce the risk of inhalation, ingestion, or skin contamination.
Personal protective equipment (PPE) is essential and includes disposable gloves (often double-layered), lab coats, and safety glasses, which must be removed and discarded if contaminated before leaving the work area. Work surfaces should be covered with plastic-backed absorbent paper, and operations conducted within spill-containment trays or operational fume hoods to capture potential releases. Continuous monitoring with tools like alpha probes, wipe tests, hand/foot monitors, and air samplers is required during and after handling to detect contamination, with surveys conducted at least monthly or post-experiment. Radiation risk assessments, aligned with international standards such as IAEA GSR Part 4, must precede any procedure, emphasizing time, distance, and shielding to limit doses from actinium-225 and its daughters like francium-221 and bismuth-213.
For storage, actinium isotopes, particularly Ac-225 (half-life 10 days) and Ac-227 (half-life 21.7 years), are kept in secure, shielded containers within dedicated refrigerators or freezers to block emissions from daughter products, maintaining dose rates below regulatory limits. No food or beverages may be stored in these areas, and inventories must be meticulously tracked for receipts, transfers, and disposals. Waste contaminated with actinium is segregated, shielded, and stored for decaytypically 100 days to reduce activity by a factor of 1,000 or up to one year for greater reductionbefore final disposal in accordance with local regulations and IAEA guidelines. Facilities should incorporate engineering controls like secondary containment and impervious surfaces to prevent environmental release, with spill kits and contingency plans readily available.
