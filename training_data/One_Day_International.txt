 A One Day International (ODI) is a form of limited-overs cricket contested between two teams holding international status, in which each side bats for a maximum of 50 overs under the regulations set by the International Cricket Council (ICC). These matches are scheduled to be completed in a single day, typically lasting around eight hours, and blend elements of traditional Test cricket technique with faster-paced scoring to create an engaging, spectator-friendly format.
The ODI format originated in response to weather-disrupted Test matches, with the inaugural game played on 5 January 1971 between Australia and England at the Melbourne Cricket Ground, where each team faced 40 overs due to rain interruptions in a preceding Test. The standard 50-over structure was established soon after, and the format gained widespread popularity in the 1980s, fueled by aggressive batting innovations like the reverse sweep and the introduction of colored uniforms and floodlights for day-night games. The first ICC Men's Cricket World Cup in 1975, held in England, marked a pivotal moment, transforming ODIs into a global spectacle and establishing it as one of cricket's three core international formats alongside Tests and T20Is.
ODIs are governed by the ICC's Standard Playing Conditions, which mandate one innings per team, with a minimum of 20 overs bowled to the second batting side for a valid result, subject to weather adjustments via methods like Duckworth-Lewis-Stern. Fielding restrictions apply during powerplays, and recent updates include using two new balls for the first 34 overs to maintain seam movement and visibility. Official ODIs involve Full Members like India and Australia or Associate Members with ODI status, such as the Netherlands or USA, and are classified only if played in sanctioned tournaments or bilaterals. As of 2025, 20 ICC members hold men's ODI status and 16 hold women's, enabling over 4,000 ODIs to have been played historically.
The format's enduring appeal lies in its balance of strategy and excitement, underpinning major events like the quadrennial Men's and Women's Cricket World Cups, the biennial ICC Champions Trophy, and annual bilateral series that contribute to ICC team rankings. Iconic records highlight its legacy: India's Sachin Tendulkar amassed 18,426 runs across 463 matches, while Pakistan's Wasim Akram took 502 wickets, underscoring the format's demand for versatile all-round skills. Despite the rise of T20 cricket, ODIs remain a cornerstone of the international calendar, with India leading the 2025 ICC rankings at 122 points.

Overview

Definition and Format

A One Day International (ODI) is a form of limited-overs cricket contested between two teams holding international status, in which each side bats for a maximum of one innings consisting of up to 50 overs. The format emphasizes balanced scoring opportunities, with the team accumulating the most runs declared the winner upon completion of both innings.
The core objective of an ODI is for the batting team to score as many runs as possible while preserving wickets, as an innings ends prematurely if 10 wickets are lost before the full allocation of overs. Matches are structured to conclude within a single day, typically around 8 hours of playing time to accommodate both innings, a lunch or tea interval, and any brief delays.
Each ODI features two inningsone per teamwith the captain winning the pre-match toss electing to bat first or bowl first, thereby setting the order of play. An over consists of six legal deliveries bowled by a member of the fielding side, yielding a potential maximum of 300 deliveries per innings; however, extras such as no-balls and wides contribute additional runs to the batting team's total but do not count toward the over limit.
In the event of weather interruptions reducing available playing time, the Duckworth-Lewis-Stern (DLS) method is used to set a revised target for the second innings, ensuring fairness by calculating the relative run-scoring resources available to each team based on remaining overs and wickets. Resources are quantified as percentages from pre-defined tables relative to a full 50-over innings. The target (T) is determined as follows: if the second team's resources (R2) are less than the first team's resources at the interruption (R1), T = + 1 (rounded down), where S is the first team's score; if R2 = R1, T = S + 1; if R2 > R1, T = S + + 1 (rounded down), where G50 is the average score in a full 50-over innings (typically 245 for senior men's internationals).
Recent updates to ODI playing conditions, effective July 2025, include using two new balls from each end for the first 34 overs (with the fielding captain selecting one for overs 35-50 thereafter) to enhance seam movement and visibility, and a minimum 7-day stand-down period for players with concussion.

Distinction from Other Cricket Formats

One Day International (ODI) cricket differs fundamentally from Test cricket in its structure and strategic demands. While Tests feature unlimited overs spread across up to five days with each team playing two innings, ODIs restrict each side to a single innings of 50 overs, completed within one day to encourage aggressive batting and faster-paced play over endurance and defensive technique. This limited-overs approach shifts the focus from prolonged attrition to rapid scoring and tactical adaptability, as teams must build innings efficiently without the luxury of multiple days or additional opportunities.
In contrast to Twenty20 International (T20I) cricket, which confines teams to 20 overs per innings and emphasizes explosive, high-risk batting in matches lasting approximately three hours, ODIs provide more time for strategic buildup and nuanced gameplay across their 50 overs. This extended duration allows for higher team totals, typically ranging from 250 to 300 runs per innings, fostering a blend of calculated aggression and pressure management rather than the short-burst intensity of T20Is.
The ODI format uniquely balances batting flair with bowling variety, promoting both innovative shot-making and diverse bowling tactics in a way that bridges the complexity of Tests and the dynamism of T20Is. Matches generally last around 8 hours of playing time, enabling deeper tactical layers such as partnerships and bowling rotations, while maintaining the single-innings-per-team structure shared with T20Is but distinct from the double-innings setup in Tests. This equilibrium has solidified ODIs as a format that rewards versatility across skills.

History

Origins and Early Development

The origins of One Day International (ODI) cricket trace back to efforts in the 1960s to revitalize the sport amid declining attendance at traditional multi-day matches, particularly in England where spectator numbers had fallen significantly from post-World War II highs. In response, English county cricket introduced limited-overs experiments to create faster-paced, more spectator-friendly games. A key example was the inaugural Gillette Cup in 1963, the first domestic knockout tournament featuring 65 overs per side, which proved popular and demonstrated the potential for one-day formats to draw crowds despite initial skepticism from traditionalists. These trials laid the groundwork for international adoption, as similar domestic competitions emerged elsewhere, such as Australia's one-day tournament in the 1969-70 season, further highlighting the format's appeal for scheduling flexibility and entertainment value.
The official birth of ODIs occurred on 5 January 1971, when Australia faced England at the Melbourne Cricket Ground in a hastily arranged match to salvage a rain-ruined third Test of the Ashes series. With the Test abandoned after three days, officials feared substantial financial lossesestimated at 80,000prompting the boards to organize a one-day substitute modeled on the Gillette Cup, consisting of 40 eight-ball overs per innings and sponsored by Rothmans for 5,000. Australia won by five wickets, chasing 190 in front of a surprising 46,006 spectators, exceeding expectations and proving the format's viability. This ad-hoc fixture was retroactively recognized by the International Cricket Conference (now the ICC) as the first official ODI, marking the transition from domestic trials to international play.
Formalization followed swiftly, with the format integrated into bilateral schedules despite initial resistance from conservative administrators wary of diluting Test cricket's prestige. The first dedicated ODI series came during the 1972 Australian tour of England, featuring three matches after rain-affected Tests, where England won the opener at Old Trafford. Early ODIs, however, faced challenges including limited global interest and sporadic scheduling, confined primarily to full-member nations like England, Australia, and West Indies, with the latter joining in 1973 against England. This exclusivity reflected the format's tentative beginnings, as it took years for broader adoption beyond these core teams. The 50-over structure, adjusted from the eight-ball overs, became standard in bilateral series through the mid-1970s.

Key Milestones and Global Expansion

The inaugural ICC Cricket World Cup, known as the Prudential Cup, was held in England from June 7 to 21, 1975, featuring eight teams in a 60-over-per-side format and marking the first major international tournament dedicated to One Day Internationals. West Indies clinched the title by defeating Australia by 17 runs in the final at Lord's, with Clive Lloyd's century proving pivotal in setting a total of 291 for 8. This event represented a commercial breakthrough for ODIs, attracting significant attendanceover 18,000 for the finaland generating revenue through sponsorships like Prudential's 100,000 contribution, which helped stabilize cricket's finances amid growing professional demands.
In the late 1970s, Kerry Packer's World Series Cricket (1977-79) revolutionized the format by emphasizing player professionalism through higher salaries and better contracts, drawing top talent and pressuring traditional boards to modernize compensation structures. Building on this, the 1979-80 season introduced colored clothing, white balls, and floodlights for day-night matches, first trialed in a WSC fixture on November 28, 1978, at Sydney Cricket Ground, which enhanced visibility and spectator appeal under artificial lighting. These innovations, initially controversial, became standard by the early 1980s, boosting ODI attendance and television viewership globally.
Global expansion accelerated in the 1980s with the inclusion of associate nations; Sri Lanka gained full ICC membership in July 1981, earning permanent ODI status and playing their first such match as a full member against England in February 1982. The 1983 World Cup, hosted across England, saw underdog India defeat defending champions West Indies by 43 runs in the final, an upset that ignited widespread enthusiasm for ODIs in Asia. India-Pakistan rivalries, exemplified by intense clashes in tournaments like the 1980s Asia Cups, further amplified popularity, drawing massive crowds and media attention in the subcontinent due to the matches' high-stakes drama and cultural significance.
The 1990s and 2000s solidified ODIs through structured competitions like tri-series, particularly Australia's annual VB/CB Series involving the hosts, New Zealand, and a rotating third team such as India or South Africa, which ran from 1981 but peaked in popularity during this era with formats blending round-robin play and finals. Women's ODIs, which began informally in the early 1970s, achieved a milestone with the first Women's World Cup in 1973, hosted in England and won by the host nation after a round-robin among seven teams, laying the foundation for the format's growth parallel to the men's game.
Into the 2020s, ODIs sustained momentum through annual bilateral series between full members, with the ICC scheduling over 200 such matches yearly pre-pandemic to foster competitive depth. The ICC intensified efforts to include associate nations, granting temporary ODI status to teams like Ireland and Afghanistan for select cycles and expanding their participation in qualifiers and interleague fixtures to build global pathways. The COVID-19 pandemic disrupted this growth, postponing key tours such as Australia's planned 2020 visit to Bangladesh and India's series against South Africa in early 2021, though rescheduling and bio-secure bubbles enabled a partial recovery by 2022, with bilateral calendars rebounding to near pre-2020 levels by 2025. The 2023 ICC Men's Cricket World Cup in India, won by Australia, highlighted the format's continued global appeal, with expanded associate involvement in qualifiers.

Rules and Regulations

Core Playing Rules

In One Day International (ODI) cricket, each team bats for a single innings limited to a maximum of 50 overs, with the objective of scoring more runs than the opposing team while preventing their dismissal. The innings concludes when the batting team is all out (losing 10 wickets) or has faced 50 overs, whichever occurs first. Runs are primarily scored by the batsmen running between the two wickets after hitting the ball, with each completed run counting as one; if the ball reaches the boundary rope after touching the ground, four runs are awarded, and if it clears the boundary without touching the ground, six runs are scored. Additional runs can be added through extras, such as byes, leg byes, no-balls, wides, or penalties for fielding infringements.
A batsman can be dismissed in several ways, ending their innings and contributing to the team's total of 10 wickets. Common modes of dismissal include being bowled, where the ball delivered by the bowler directly hits and dislodges the bails on the stumps (Clause 32 of the Laws of Cricket as modified by ICC playing conditions); caught, when a fielder catches the ball after it has touched the bat or glove without bouncing (Clause 33); leg before wicket (LBW), if the ball strikes the batsman's leg (or body) in line with the stumps and would have hit them had it not been deflected, provided it pitched in line and was not a no-ball (Clause 36); run out, occurring when a batsman is out of their ground and the fielding side breaks the wicket with the ball (Clause 38); and stumped, a form of run out specific to the wicket-keeper removing the bails while the striker is out of their crease during a delivery that is not a no-ball (Clause 39). These dismissals are adjudicated by the on-field umpires, with the Laws of Cricket serving as the foundational framework adapted by the International Cricket Council (ICC) for ODIs.
Bowling in ODIs follows strict mechanics to ensure fair play and pace. Each over consists of six legal deliveries bowled from one end by a single bowler, after which the bowler rotates ends or is replaced, with no bowler permitted to deliver consecutive overs from the same end (Clause 17). No individual bowler may exceed 10 overs in an innings (Clause 17.4). Illegal deliveries, such as no-balls (e.g., overstepping the crease or high bouncers) or wides (deliveries too wide for the batsman to reach reasonably), are signaled by the umpire, do not count toward the six legal balls of the over, and award one extra run to the batting team, plus any additional runs scored off the delivery (Clauses 21 and 22). Umpires also signal boundaries and other scoring events to maintain accurate tallying. Two new balls, one from each end, are used alternately for the first 34 overs to aid seam movement and visibility; thereafter, one ball is selected by the umpires to continue the innings. For matches reduced to 25 overs or fewer, only one new ball is used.
The match result is determined by the team with the higher total runs at the end of both innings; if scores are level after regular play, a Super Over tiebreaker has been used since 2011, where each team faces one over and the higher score wins, with further Super Overs if tied (Appendix G of ICC playing conditions). In cases of rain or other interruptions, the Duckworth-Lewis-Stern method adjusts targets to ensure a fair outcome based on overs remaining. Two on-field umpires oversee the game, making real-time decisions on dismissals, extras, and play progression (Clause 2.1), while a third umpire, located off-field, reviews contentious calls via video replays. The Decision Review System (DRS), introduced for ODIs in 2011, allows each team two reviews per innings to challenge umpire decisions, primarily using Hawk-Eye technology for LBW predictions and UltraEdge for edge detection (Appendix D).
Player substitutions are limited to maintain team balance. General substitutes for injury or illness may enter the field but cannot bat, bowl, or captain unless approved otherwise (Clause 24). Concussion substitutes, permitted since 2019 following a 2017 trial, allow a like-for-like replacement if a concussion is medically confirmed by the team doctor; as of July 2025, teams must nominate five designated substitutes (a batter, bowler, all-rounder, wicketkeeper, and like-for-like option) before the toss, with the concussed player subject to a minimum 7-day stand-down period (Clause 8). Fielding restrictions, including limits on fielders outside the inner circle during powerplays, further shape strategy but are enforced separately from these core mechanics.

Fielding Restrictions and Powerplays

In One Day International (ODI) cricket, fielding restrictions are implemented through three distinct powerplay phases to balance attacking opportunities for batsmen and defensive strategies for the fielding side, thereby enhancing the tactical depth of the 50-over format. These restrictions limit the number of fielders permitted outside a marked 30-yard (27.43 meters) circle on the field, which consists of two semicircles with a radius of 30 yards from the center of the pitch, connected by parallel lines behind the popping and bowling creases. The circle is enforced by umpires, who use measurements marked by white lines or dots at five-yard intervals to ensure compliance at the instant of delivery.
The first powerplay, covering overs 1 to 10, is mandatory and allows a maximum of two fielders outside the 30-yard circle, compelling the fielding team to pack the inner field and encouraging aggressive batting from the outset to capitalize on reduced protection. This phase aims to promote high run rates early in the innings by restricting the fielding captain's ability to spread the field widely. Any violation, such as more than two fielders being outside the circle when the ball is bowled, results in the umpire signaling a no-ball, awarding one run to the batting team plus an extra delivery, with a free hit on the subsequent ball if applicable.
Following the first powerplay, overs 11 to 40 constitute the second phase, during which up to four fielders may be positioned outside the 30-yard circle, providing the fielding side with greater flexibility to contain the scoring rate in the middle overs while still limiting fully defensive setups. This fixed restriction, introduced to streamline gameplay, allows batsmen to rotate the strike more freely compared to the opening phase but maintains pressure through partial inner-field crowding. Enforcement remains with the umpires, who call a no-ball for breaches, applying the same penalties as in the first powerplay to uphold the rules' integrity.
The third powerplay, encompassing overs 41 to 50, permits a maximum of five fielders outside the 30-yard circle, enabling the fielding team to adopt more protective positions against big hitting in the death overs and heightening the drama of boundary attempts. This phase shifts the balance toward bowling containment, as the extra fielder outside helps prevent easy runs but still requires careful placement to avoid gaps. Umpires continue to monitor positions strictly, with no-ball penalties for non-compliance ensuring fair play throughout the innings' climax.
Fielding restrictions were first introduced in ODI cricket during the 1992 World Cup, initially limiting two fielders outside the circle for the opening 15 overs to inject excitement into the format. The system was refined in 2008 with the addition of an optional batting powerplay, allowing the batting captain to select a five-over block with tighter restrictions for a scoring boost. By 2011, the first 10-over powerplay became mandatory across all ODIs to standardize aggressive starts. Further evolution occurred in 2015, when the ICC abolished optional powerplays, establishing the current fixed three-phase structure to simplify tactics and promote consistent run-scoring patterns.

Additional Trial and Umpire Regulations

The Decision Review System (DRS) allows each team two unsuccessful player reviews per innings in ODIs, a protocol established to assist umpires in making accurate decisions on specific dismissals. Introduced in international cricket in 2008 and standardized for ODIs by 2011, DRS covers on-field calls for leg before wicket (LBW), caught behind, stumped, and other dismissals, but excludes wides and no-balls, which are handled separately by umpires or the third umpire without consuming a team review. The system relies on technologies such as ball-tracking for trajectory predictions and UltraEdge for detecting bat-pad edges through audio and visual analysis, enabling the TV umpire to overturn or uphold decisions based on evidence.
Umpire protocols in ODIs emphasize impartiality, with mandatory neutral umpiresone from the ICC's Elite Panel, not affiliated with either participating teamintroduced experimentally in the late 1990s and made compulsory starting in 2002 to eliminate home bias. The TV umpire, also neutral, oversees DRS referrals, consulting footage and data within a 15-second window for player-initiated reviews, while on-field umpires signal referrals with a T-shape gesture. These measures ensure consistency, with the third umpire empowered to intervene proactively on clear errors like boundary catches or run-outs without a team review.
Trial regulations in ODIs have tested innovations to enhance competitiveness, such as the Impact Player rule, first implemented in the 2023 Indian Premier League (IPL) to permit one mid-innings substitution of a batter or bowler from a pre-named list of reserves, aiming to add tactical depth without altering the 11-player format permanently. The rule remains limited to domestic T20 and limited-overs leagues like the IPL and has not been adopted in international ODIs. Earlier experiments included the Super Sub rule from 2005-2006, allowing one permanent substitution before the toss for any player, which was discontinued after one year due to strategic imbalances and lack of excitement.
Another key trial, the free hit rule introduced in October 2007, awards the batter a "free" delivery after a no-ball, during which they cannot be dismissed except by run-out or obstructing the field, to deter overstepping and boost scoring rates in limited-overs play. This applies to all foot-fault no-balls in ODIs and has become a permanent fixture, influencing bowler discipline without referencing powerplay restrictions.
Anti-corruption measures under the ICC Code of Conduct apply uniformly to ODIs, prohibiting spot-fixingmanipulating specific events like no-balls or boundariesand imposing severe penalties to maintain integrity. Violations, such as those in the 2010 Pakistan spot-fixing scandal involving deliberate no-balls, result in bans ranging from five years to life, with the Anti-Corruption Unit monitoring matches via player education, reporting protocols, and investigations. The code mandates immediate reporting of corrupt approaches, with fines or suspensions for non-compliance, ensuring ODIs remain free from undue influence.

Participating Teams

Permanent ODI Status Teams

The International Cricket Council (ICC) grants permanent One Day International (ODI) status to its 12 full member nations, which automatically qualify them to participate in all bilateral ODI series and ICC ODI events, including the Cricket World Cup, without needing additional qualification processes. These teams represent the core of international ODI cricket, fostering consistent competition and global development of the format. As of 2025, the full members are Afghanistan, Australia, Bangladesh, England, India, Ireland, New Zealand, Pakistan, South Africa, Sri Lanka, West Indies, and Zimbabwe.
Full membership, and thus permanent ODI status, is achieved through ICC recognition based on criteria including sustained international performance, infrastructure development, and administrative governance, granting these teams voting rights in ICC decisions and priority access to funding and scheduling. This status ensures their ongoing involvement in the ODI ecosystem, enabling them to host and tour extensively, which has stabilized the format's structure since the 1980s with no full members ever demoted. For instance, Zimbabwe, a full member since 1992, faced a brief suspension in 2019 due to governance issues but was swiftly readmitted, maintaining its ODI eligibility.
Among these teams, Australia stands out for its dominance, holding the record for the most ODI victories with over 600 wins and securing six Cricket World Cup titles (1987, 1999, 2003, 2007, 2015, and 2023), which underscores their pivotal role in elevating ODI standards through consistent excellence. India, meanwhile, drives the format's economic and spectator growth as a powerhouse, with ODI events in the country attracting record crowds, such as the 2023 Cricket World Cup that drew 1.25 million attendees across matches, highlighting their influence on global viewership and revenue. These teams collectively shape ODI scheduling, often prioritized in high-profile bilateral series informed by ICC rankings to balance competitiveness and market appeal.

Temporary and Associate Status Teams

In addition to the 12 full members of the International Cricket Council (ICC) that hold permanent One Day International (ODI) status, the organization recognizes 98 associate members as of July 2025, providing a structured pathway for these nations to earn temporary ODI status through competitive performance. These associate teams, which represent emerging cricket-playing countries across regions like Africa, Asia, Europe, and the Americas, gain ODI recognition primarily via the ICC's 50-over qualification pathway, including the Men's Cricket World Cup League 2 and associated qualifiers. For the 2023-2027 cycle, the top-performing associate teams in League 2such as those securing positions in the upper echelons of the standingsearn temporary ODI status for a four-year period, enabling them to play official ODIs against full members and other qualified associates. This status is limited to eight associate teams at any time, ensuring a competitive balance while promoting global development.
Temporary ODI status allows associate teams intermittent access to the format, often resulting in 10-15 matches per year, typically tied to bilateral series, tri-nations, or qualification events rather than a full Future Tours Programme schedule afforded to permanent teams. Examples include the Netherlands, Scotland, and United Arab Emirates (UAE), which have secured this status through consistent results in League 2 and qualifiers, allowing them to host and participate in ODIs sporadically. For instance, the Netherlands earned qualification for the 2023 Men's Cricket World Cup via the ICC Cricket World Cup Qualifier, highlighting how temporary status facilitates high-profile exposure despite limited fixtures. This provisional access contrasts with the unconditional ODI privileges of full members and is reviewed cyclically, with teams like Scotland maintaining status through strong League 2 performances in 2025.
Notable special cases illustrate pathways from associate to full membership, culminating in permanent ODI status. In June 2017, Afghanistan and Ireland were unanimously granted full ICC membership and Test status, elevating them from associate ranks after years of sustained development and qualification successes, including temporary ODI access since 2009 and 2000, respectively. This transition provided both nations with ongoing ODI opportunities, marking a milestone for non-traditional cricket powerhouses. More recently, the Netherlands received enhanced temporary ODI access for the 2023-2027 World Cup cycle following their qualifier performance, allowing fuller integration into the international calendar without immediate full membership.
The qualification process for temporary ODI status emphasizes merit-based progression through structured tournaments like the World Cricket League and its successors. Associate teams compete in League 2a round-robin series involving eight nationswhere points accumulated over matches determine advancement to the Cricket World Cup Qualifier; the top two from League 2 proceed, while overall performance influences ODI eligibility for the next cycle. For example, in the 2023 Cricket World Cup Qualifier, full member West Indies participated due to a dip in rankings but were eliminated after losses, underscoring how even established teams must navigate qualifiers, though associates like the Netherlands capitalized on such events to affirm their temporary status. This system, active through 2026 for the 2027 World Cup pathway, ensures associates play at least 24 League 2 matches each, fostering skill growth and occasional upsets against permanent teams.

Rankings and Tournaments

ICC ODI Rankings System

The ICC ODI Rankings System is a points-based rating method developed by statistician David Kendix to evaluate the relative strength of men's national teams in One Day International cricket. Introduced in its current form in May 2017, it employs an Elo-inspired algorithm that updates rankings after every international ODI match, providing a dynamic measure of team performance based on recent results against opponents of varying strengths. This system replaced an earlier weighted average approach, emphasizing ongoing competition rather than fixed periods, and ensures neutrality regarding home or away conditions.
The primary purpose of the rankings is to determine seeding for major ICC events, such as the Cricket World Cup, where the top-ranked teams receive favorable draws and automatic qualification pathways; for instance, the top 10 full-member teams typically auto-qualify for the ODI World Cup, while lower-ranked sides enter qualifiers. Additionally, the rankings influence bilateral tour scheduling, with higher-rated teams often facing similarly ranked opponents to maintain competitive balance in the international calendar.
Under the Elo-based formula, a team's rating is calculated as the sum of points accumulated from recent matches divided by the number of completed ODIs in the assessment period (typically the last 3-4 years, with all matches weighted equally post-2017). After each match, the rating update follows: new rating = previous rating + points gained (or minus points lost), where points exchanged depend on the pre-match rating difference between teams (R) and the match outcome. For rating differences under 40 points, a win yields approximately +50 points to the winner and -50 to the loser, adjusted slightly by R; for differences of 40 or more, the stronger team gains +10 for a win (or loses -90 for a defeat), while the weaker team gains +90 for an upset (or loses -10). Ties or no-results result in no net change. This opponent strength factor rewards victories over higher-rated teams more generously, promoting upsets as key ranking movers. Series context does not alter per-match calculations in ODIs, unlike in Test cricket where series bonuses apply, and win margins (runs or wickets) do not directly influence team points, focusing instead on binary outcomes.
As of November 17, 2025, India tops the rankings with a rating of 122 points from 39 matches, followed closely by New Zealand at 112 (42 matches) and Australia at 109 (38 matches). Historical shifts illustrate the system's responsiveness; for example, Pakistan climbed to as high as 5th in mid-2019 with 106 points after strong performances in bilateral series, before dropping amid inconsistent results in subsequent years. These fluctuations underscore how the rankings reflect evolving global competitiveness in ODI cricket.

Major ODI Tournaments and Events

The ICC Cricket World Cup stands as the pinnacle of One Day International (ODI) cricket, held every four years since its inception in 1975. The tournament typically features 10 to 14 teams competing in a round-robin group stage, where each team plays the others in their group, followed by knockout semifinals and a final to determine the champion. This structure emphasizes strategic depth and high-stakes elimination matches, making it the most prestigious event in the ODI calendar and a global showcase for the format's evolution. The 2023 edition, hosted across 10 venues in India, exemplified its scale with Australia defeating India in the final to claim their sixth title, drawing over 1 trillion global live viewing minutes.
The ICC Champions Trophy complements the World Cup as a quadrennial event for the top eight teams based on ODI rankings. It was last held in 2017, with the 2025 edition taking place from February 19 to March 9 across Pakistan and the United Arab Emirates, where India defeated New Zealand in the final to claim their third title. Its hybrid format divides the teams into two groups of four for initial round-robin play, with the top two from each advancing to semifinals and a final, ensuring a compact yet intense competition over two weeks. This tournament highlights elite one-day prowess among the strongest nations, fostering rivalries and serving as a key preparatory milestone in the international calendar.
Bilateral ODI series form the backbone of the annual global schedule, typically consisting of three to five matches between two nations during tours, often integrated into broader multi-format itineraries. These series, such as the high-profile India-Pakistan encounters or the ODI component of the Ashes rivalry between England and Australia, allow for sustained competition and bilateral trophy contests. Triangular series, involving three teams in a round-robin format followed by finals, add variety; historical examples include the Australian Tri-Series featuring teams like India, England, and hosts Australia.
Regional events like the Asia Cup, organized biennially by the Asian Cricket Council, bring together six full-member teamsAfghanistan, Bangladesh, India, Pakistan, and Sri Lankaplus qualifiers, in a group-stage to knockout structure that promotes Asian cricket development. Women's ODI equivalents have been integrated into multi-format bilateral series since 2017, combining ODIs with Tests and T20Is to streamline tours and enhance competitiveness. Looking ahead, the 2027 Men's World Cup will expand to 14 teams across 54 matches, with two groups of seven advancing the top three each to a Super Six stage before semifinals and final, aiming to broaden participation while maintaining the event's intensity.

Records and Statistics

Team Performance Records

Australia holds the record for the most ODI victories, with 617 wins from 1,019 matches played as of November 2025. India follows with 568 wins in 1,069 matches as of November 2025. In terms of win percentage, Australia leads slightly at 62.70%, closely followed by South Africa at 62.31%.
Australia dominates ODI World Cup history, securing six titles in 1987, 1999, 2003, 2007, 2015, and 2023. The highest team total in ODI history is England's 498/4 against Pakistan in 2019.
Australia achieved the longest unbeaten streak in ODIs with 21 consecutive wins from January 2003 to May 2003. India leads in bilateral series victories, with 73 series wins overall.
Notable defensive records include the United States successfully defending 122 against Oman in February 2025, the lowest such total in ODI history. South Africa's chase of 438/9 against Australia in 2006 remains the highest successful pursuit.

Individual Player Records

In One Day Internationals (ODIs), individual player records highlight exceptional personal achievements in batting, bowling, and all-round performance. Sachin Tendulkar of India holds the record for the most runs scored in ODIs, amassing 18,426 runs across 463 matches from 1989 to 2012. The highest individual score remains Rohit Sharma's unbeaten 264 off 173 balls for India against Sri Lanka in Kolkata in November 2014. Tendulkar also leads in most centuries, with 49 in his ODI career.
For bowling, Wasim Akram of Pakistan tops the wicket-takers list with 502 wickets in 356 matches between 1984 and 2003. The best bowling figures in an innings are Chaminda Vaas's 8/19 for Sri Lanka against Zimbabwe in Colombo in December 2001. Joel Garner of West Indies boasts the lowest career bowling average of 18.84, achieved over 98 matches from 1977 to 1987 with a minimum of 2,000 deliveries bowled.
All-round contributions are epitomized by Jacques Kallis of South Africa, who scored 11,579 runs and took 273 wickets in 328 ODIs from 1996 to 2014, making him one of only two players to exceed 10,000 runs and 250 wickets in the format. Sachin Tendulkar of India earned the most Player of the Match awards with 62 across 463 ODIs from 1989 to 2012, underscoring his match-winning impact.
Key milestones include AB de Villiers's fastest ODI century, reached in 31 balls for South Africa against West Indies in Johannesburg in January 2015. In women's ODIs, Mithali Raj of India holds the record for most runs with 7,805 in 232 matches from 1999 to 2022.
