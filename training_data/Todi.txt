 Todi is a comune and hilltop town in the Province of Perugia, Umbria region, central Italy, situated on a ridge at approximately 400 meters elevation overlooking the Tiber River valley.
With a population of 15,682 residents as of 2023, the town maintains a compact historic core encircled by three concentric rings of walls from its Umbrian-Roman, Roman, and medieval eras.
Its origins trace to Umbrian settlements around 2700 BC, followed by Etruscan influences and Roman development after gaining municipium status in 89 BC, which spurred urban expansion around the present-day Piazza del Popolo.
Todi exemplifies a "Citt Slow," prioritizing deliberate pace and cultural preservation amid its Renaissance landmarks, such as the Ingegno Church of Santa Maria della Consolazione, and serves as the birthplace of Jacopone da Todi, the 13th-century Franciscan mystic and proto-Renaissance poet.
The town's enduring appeal stems from its intact medieval fabric, including the Palazzo del Popolo and Cathedral of San Lorenzo, alongside agricultural heritage in wine and olive production, though it has occasionally been highlighted in media for quality of life without formal international designations like UNESCO status.

Geography

Location and topography

Todi is a comune in the Province of Perugia within the Umbria region of central Italy, situated at approximately 4247N 1224E. The town lies on the east bank of the Tiber River, overlooking the river valley and surrounded by the characteristic rolling hills of Umbria. The municipal territory spans 223 square kilometers, encompassing varied terrain from river plains to elevated ridges.
The historic center of Todi occupies the summit of Nidoli hill at an elevation of 398 meters above sea level, positioned on a prominent two-crested ridge that provides expansive views of the surrounding countryside. This hilltop location, with elevations in the municipality ranging from a minimum of 126 meters to a maximum of 639 meters, exemplifies the typical topography of Umbrian hill towns, where settlements developed on defensible heights amid fertile valleys. The terrain features gentle slopes covered in vineyards, olive groves, and woodlands, contributing to the area's agricultural productivity and scenic landscape.

Climate and natural environment

Todi experiences a Mediterranean climate (Kppen Csa classification), characterized by hot, dry summers and mild, wetter winters. The average annual temperature is approximately 14C, with July and August as the warmest months, reaching daily highs of 3031C (8688F) and lows around 1516C (5961F). Winters are cooler, with January averages fluctuating between lows of 0.8C (33F) and highs of 7.2C (45F), occasionally dipping below freezing. Annual precipitation totals about 9001,000 mm, concentrated in autumn and winter, with September being the wettest month at around 90100 mm.
The town's natural environment is shaped by its position on a prominent two-crested hill rising from the Tiber River valley, at an average elevation of 343 meters, with clastic sedimentary formations underlying the terrain. Surrounding landscapes feature rolling hills covered in olive groves, vineyards, and mixed woodlands typical of Umbria's agrarian mosaic. Common flora includes olive trees (Olea europaea), strawberry trees (Arbutus unedo), and blackthorn (Prunus spinosa), supporting a biodiversity influenced by Mediterranean and continental elements.
Wildlife in the vicinity includes species such as the golden eagle (Aquila chrysaetos), wild cat (Felis silvestris), and gray wolf (Canis lupus), though urban proximity limits large predator populations within Todi proper. Nearby protected areas, like the WWF Oasis La Malcontenta e il Lupo, encompass wetlands, evolving forests, and climatic inversion zones, fostering habitats for diverse flora and fauna including rare amphibians and birds. The Tiber River Park, accessible from Todi, adds riparian ecosystems with archaeological and vegetative richness, contributing to regional conservation efforts.

History

Ancient origins and Roman period

Archaeological evidence indicates that Todi, known anciently as Tuder to the Umbrians, was settled by the late 7th century BC, with early graves in the San Raffaele and Le Logge necropoles on the southern hill slopes revealing a wealthy noble class. Further necropoles at Peschiera and San Stefano were active from the 5th to 3rd centuries BC, supporting an urban settlement by the late 5th century BC. The site, strategically positioned on a defensible hill with springs, shows continuous occupation from Neolithic times, though organized village cores date to the 8th-6th centuries BC among farmers and shepherds. Primarily Umbrian in culture, Todi bordered Etruscan territories, leading to cultural exchanges evidenced by coins minted from the early 3rd century BC inscribed with "Tutere" or "Tular" in an Etruscan-influenced system.
The first circuit of defensive walls, fragmentary and partially incorporated into later structures, dates to the 6th or 5th century BC, enclosing the pre-Roman town. A notable artifact is the "Mars of Todi," a hollow-cast bronze statue of a warrior (likely the Etruscan war god Laran) standing 141 cm tall in contrapposto pose, dated to the late 5th or early 4th century BC and discovered near Todi in 1835. Inscribed in Umbrian using Etruscan characters"Ahal Trutitis dunum dede" (a gift from Ahal Trutitis, possibly Celtic-named)it highlights multicultural interactions at regional sanctuaries like Montesanto, where it was likely dedicated. Ditch tombs from the 8th to 3rd centuries BC near Santa Maria della Pace and votive warrior figurines further attest to martial and religious practices.
Todi integrated into the Roman sphere gradually rather than through direct conquest, with Roman expansion into Umbria around 295 BC following the Battle of Sentinum, though a treaty likely existed by the 3rd century BC when coin minting began during the First Punic War (264241 BC). Full Roman citizenship was granted in 89 BC after the Social War, conferring municipium status and prompting urban reorganization around the forum at what is now Piazza del Popolo, including a Capitolium temple on the site of the present cathedral. A second wall circuit was constructed in the Roman period, and infrastructure like underground cisterns and roadssuch as those linked to gates like Porta Aureasupported development, with Todi assigned to Augustan Regio VI by the late 1st century BC.

Medieval expansion and conflicts

In the 12th century, Todi emerged as an independent commune, receiving privileges from Emperor Frederick I in 1171, which formalized its self-governance and marked the beginning of territorial expansion. The construction of the Palazzo del Comune in 1206 and the appointment of a podest by 1201 underscored its administrative consolidation, while the institution of the Capitano del Popolo in 1255 and the Consiglio delle Arti in 1258 reflected growing popular and guild influences. By the 13th century, the town walls were extended to encompass northern and southern buttresses, incorporating new gates such as Porta Orvietana, Porta Perugina, Porta Romana, and Porta Amerina, which facilitated control over surrounding rural areas and enhanced defensive capabilities. This expansion allowed Todi to assert authority over territories including Amelia, Terni, Alviano, Guardea, and the Nera valley, often at the expense of rival Orvieto.
Todi's medieval period was dominated by protracted conflicts with Orvieto, a rivalry fueled by territorial disputes in the Nera valley and control of strategic castles like Montemarte. A notable war erupted in 1207, escalating longstanding hostilities that persisted until Pope Boniface VIII mediated an alliance in 1301, temporarily aligning the two cities against common threats. With ecclesiastical support, Todi seized Montemarte Castle from Orvieto's influence, subduing local lordships such as the Counts of Montemarte, Arnolfi, and Atti families, who resisted communal authority through feudal strongholds. These victories bolstered Todi's economic and military reach but also invited reprisals, embedding the town in a web of regional power struggles.
As a predominantly Ghibelline stronghold favoring imperial over papal authority, Todi clashed repeatedly with the Guelph-dominated Perugia, despite an initial defensive pact in 1239 against Emperor Frederick II. Tensions boiled over in the early 14th century, culminating in the siege of Massa Martana from 1303 to 1306 and a decisive battle at Montemolino in 1310, where Todi forces repelled Perugian advances. The town defected to Frederick II in 1241 and joined a broader Ghibelline uprising during Henry VII's Italian descent in 1308, aiding figures like Corrado da Montefeltro and hosting Louis IV in 13271328, which provoked papal interdicts and further sieges. Internal revolts, such as the 1238 uprising, compounded these external pressures, fracturing communal unity amid Guelph-Ghibelline factionalism that persisted into the 14th century.

Renaissance to contemporary developments

During the early Renaissance, Todi experienced political instability marked by factional feuds between Guelph and Ghibelline families, such as the Atti and Chiaravalle, contributing to a period of slow decline beginning in the 15th century. In 1408, condottiero Braccio da Montone captured the town for King Ladislas of Naples, leading to its sacking in 1414 and the rebuilding of the Rocca fortress in 1423. Frequent changes in rulers continued, including control by figures like Biondo Michelotti, Pandolfo Malatesta, Francesco Sforza in 1434, and condottiero Niccol Piccinino, amid ongoing Guelph-Ghibelline conflicts. By 1474, the murder of Gabriele degli Atti prompted papal intervention, and in 1486, the Chiaravalle faction was expelled, with the Rocca rebuilt again. Architecturally, the period saw the initiation of Renaissance-style developments, notably the construction of Santa Maria della Consolazione, a centralized pilgrimage church begun in 1508 and completed in 1607, exemplifying symmetric planning and a prominent dome influenced by early Renaissance ideals. 
In the 16th century, Cesare Borgia subdued remaining local powers in 1500, destroying the fortress at Acquasparta, while papal campaigns under Pope Julius II reclaimed Todi for the Papal States, formalized by Pope Paul III's incorporation in 1540. Plagues ravaged the population, with over half perishing in 1523 and further devastation in 1630 during the 17th century. Internal conflicts persisted, including the 1553 murder of Angelo Atti amid Cesi family disputes, and by 1607, the Atti family sold significant properties to Bartolomeo Corsini. Urban enhancements included the development of Via Cesia, Palazzo Vescovile, and Tempio del Santissimo Crocifisso in the 16th century, reflecting papal administrative influence. Under sustained Papal rule through the 17th and 18th centuries, Todi remained a peripheral ecclesiastical territory with limited growth, focused on religious institutions like the 1608 diocesan seminary.
The 19th century brought Todi into the Risorgimento, with Giuseppe Garibaldi passing through in 1849 during his campaigns, and a plebiscite in 1860 announcing its integration into the Kingdom of Italy, ending Papal temporal authority. In the 20th century, Todi shared Umbria's trajectory under unified Italy, experiencing relative stability amid national events like World War I and II, with no major battles documented locally but participation in fascist-era policies and post-war reconstruction. The region faced seismic challenges, including the 1979 Norcia earthquake and the 1997 Umbria-Marche sequence, which caused minor damage to Todi's structures but prompted preservation efforts for its historic core.
Contemporary Todi emphasizes heritage preservation and sustainable development, designated a Citt Slow in recognition of its unhurried lifestyle and culinary traditions since the early 2000s. Tourism has driven economic shifts from agriculture to services, leveraging Renaissance and medieval sites, while the population stabilized around 17,000 by 2021, supported by wine production and olive oil. Local governance focuses on seismic retrofitting and cultural events, maintaining the town's hilltop integrity amid modern Italy's regional dynamics.

Demographics

Population trends and statistics

As of 2023, the resident population of Todi stood at 15,572. Independent estimates for the same year report 15,682 residents. The municipality encompasses 222.86 km, yielding a low population density of 69.9 inhabitants per km, characteristic of rural Umbrian communes.
The demographic profile features a gender imbalance favoring females at 51.5% (versus 48.5% males) and an average age of 49.6 years, signaling an aging populace amid Italy's nationwide fertility decline. Households number around 7,100, with foreigners accounting for 9.9% of residents, primarily from Eastern Europe and North Africa based on regional patterns.
Population trends indicate steady depopulation, dropping from 16,851 in 2016 to current levelsa roughly 7.5% reduction over seven years. The average annual variation rate from 2018 to 2023 was -0.23%, driven by vital statistics showing a birth rate of 5.2 per 1,000, death rate of 14.3 per 1,000, and net migration rate of 1.1 per 1,000; negative natural increase outweighs modest inflows, mirroring structural challenges in Italy's inland hill towns where youth outmigration to urban centers persists. Projections suggest continuation to approximately 15,500 by 2025 absent policy interventions.

Socially, Todi exhibits a traditional small-town structure typical of inland Umbria, with limited socioeconomic stratification data available at the municipal level. Foreigners are integrated primarily through low- to mid-skilled employment in agriculture, tourism support, and domestic services, reflecting Italy's national trends where immigrants fill labor gaps in rural areas. The overall population's age distribution shows a higher proportion of working-age adults (57% aged 18-64), with foreigners contributing to a younger demographic profile compared to the aging native cohort. No significant ethnic enclaves or social tensions have been documented in official statistics, though regional reports note occasional integration challenges related to employment and housing for non-EU migrants.

Economy

Agriculture and traditional industries

Todi's agricultural sector centers on olive cultivation and viticulture, reflecting the hilly terrain and Mediterranean climate of the Umbria region. The area produces high-quality extra virgin olive oil from varieties such as Frantoio and Leccino, with local farms emphasizing traditional pressing methods and organic practices. Vineyards yield wines under Umbrian DOC designations, including Orvieto and Todi-specific blends of Grechetto and Trebbiano grapes, supporting both local consumption and exports. The Istituto Agrario Ciuffelli, Italy's oldest agricultural school established in the early 20th century, specializes in olive and grape cultivation techniques, training students amid working groves and contributing to regional innovation in sustainable farming.
Livestock and dairy complement crop production, with farms rearing pigs fed on acorns, barley, and corn to produce cured hams and salumi using outdoor, low-density methods. Cheese-making traditions include fresh varieties like caciotta and ricotta, often incorporating local black truffles, as demonstrated in artisanal workshops near Todi. Cereals such as spelt and barley, alongside legumes like lentils and chickpeas, are grown on smaller scales, frequently under organic certification to meet demand for traceable, minimally processed foods.
Traditional industries in Todi emphasize artisanal craftsmanship, with historic shops specializing in restoration, tailoring, carpentry, shoemaking, and pottery. The textile sector stands out, featuring knitwear, embroidery, and cashmere production in a district employing hundreds of skilled workers who hand-process fine wool fibers into luxury garments. These activities preserve pre-industrial techniques, often family-run, and integrate with agriculture by utilizing local wool or plant-based dyes, though they face challenges from mechanized competition elsewhere in Italy.

Tourism impacts and modern services

Tourism plays a pivotal role in Todi's contemporary economy, driving revenue through visitor spending on accommodations, dining, and local products while fostering ancillary jobs in hospitality and guiding services. In May to August 2025, the town hosted 333,344 unique visitors, a figure nearly identical to the prior year's 332,535, indicating sustained appeal amid regional growth. Foreign arrivals accounted for 30% of total arrivals in 2023, with marked increases in subsequent years, including a 14.5% rise in foreign arrivals and 14.9% in presences during the second quarter of 2025, positioning Todi as the fourth-leading Umbrian municipality for tourist inflows. This activity generated approximately 15,000 monthly visitors by mid-2025, bolstering fiscal inflows without evidence of severe overcrowding, given Todi's relative seclusion from mass tourism circuits.
Economic benefits include heightened demand for short-term rentals and property investments, fueled by a 15% regional uptick in overnight stays in 2023, which elevated real estate values in Todi and environs. July 2025 saw presences climb 15.5% year-over-year, with foreign arrivals surging 47%, amplifying spending on wine tours and artisanal goods tied to Todi's Grechetto production and agritourism initiatives established since 2002. However, average stays remain briefoften day tripscurtailing per-visitor expenditure and deeper integration with local supply chains, a pattern observed in stable but non-explosive visitor metrics from 2024 onward. Environmentally, the low-volume, niche focus mitigates strains like resource depletion seen in denser Italian destinations, though broader Umbrian tourism growth (21% arrivals increase from 20132023) underscores needs for sustainable infrastructure to preserve Todi's hilltop ecology.
Modern services have evolved to accommodate this influx, emphasizing boutique and experiential offerings over high-volume chains. Todi hosts around 1015 prominent hotels and guesthouses, including the Roccafiore Wine Resort & Spa (with over 600 reviews averaging high satisfaction) and agriturismi like Castello Di Meleto, which integrate lodging with on-site farming and wellness facilities. Culinary services feature family-run restaurants specializing in Umbrian specialties, such as those in the historic center praised for authenticity, supporting farm-to-table models that leverage local truffles and olive oil. Accessibility relies on regional bus links to Perugia (about 50 km away) and the Umbria Airport near Bastia Umbra, with limited on-site public transport reflecting the town's compact, pedestrian-friendly layout; car rentals and guided tours fill gaps for exploring surrounding vineyards and Etruscan sites. These services, while not expansive, align with Todi's profile as an understated alternative to overtouristed peers, prioritizing quality over quantity in visitor experiences.

Government and administration

Local governance structure

Todi, as an Italian comune with a population of approximately 15,440 residents, follows the governance model outlined in the Testo Unico delle Leggi sull'Enti Locali (TUEL, Legislative Decree No. 267/2000). The structure centers on directly elected political organs: the sindaco (mayor), who exercises executive powers including policy implementation, public administration oversight, and representation of the comune; and the consiglio comunale (city council), the deliberative body responsible for approving budgets, land-use plans, regulations, and holding the executive accountable.
Elections for both the mayor and council occur every five years via a majoritarian system with proportional elements for council seats; for a comune of Todi's size (over 15,000 inhabitants), the council consists of 24 members, including the mayor, with seats allocated based on vote shares among lists supporting the winning candidate or opposition. The mayor appoints the giunta comunale (municipal executive board), typically comprising 4 to 8 assessori (assessors) delegated to specific sectors such as finance, urban development, social services, and culture, which proposes policies and manages daily operations under the mayor's direction.
Supporting the political organs is a professional administrative apparatus divided into specialized units, including the Area Amministrativa for human resources, budgeting, and legal affairs; other sectors cover public works, environmental services, tourism, and citizen welfare. The comune maintains transparency obligations under Italian law, publishing organ compositions, meeting minutes, and financial data via its official portal. Municipal headquarters are situated at Piazza del Popolo 29, with contact facilitated through a central office reachable at +39 075 89561.

Political history and recent elections

Following Italian unification in 1861, Todi integrated into the Kingdom of Italy as a comune in the province of Perugia, governed under the municipal statutes that emphasized local autonomy within a centralized state framework. In the post-World War II republican period, local politics reflected broader Umbrian trends, with initial dominance by Christian Democratic and centrist forces giving way to center-left coalitions aligned with the Italian Communist Party and later the Democratic Party of the Left.
The introduction of direct mayoral elections in 1993 marked a shift toward personalized leadership and civic lists. From 1998 to 2006, Catiuscia Marini of the center-left (supported by civic lists) served two terms, focusing on administrative modernization amid Umbria's regional economic growth. In 2007, Antonino Ruggiano, representing a center-right coalition with civic lists, won the mayoralty, signaling an early local challenge to left-leaning dominance, though he was defeated in 2012 by Carlo Rossini of a center-left alliance including the Democratic Party (PD), Italian Socialist Party (PSI), and left-wing groups.
Ruggiano reclaimed the office in 2017 via runoff, backed by a coalition featuring Fratelli d'Italia-Alleanza Nazionale, Forza Italia, and family-oriented civic lists, defeating the center-left incumbent. This victory aligned with Umbria's gradual political realignment toward center-right forces, evident in subsequent regional contests.
In the June 12, 2022, municipal elections, Ruggiano was reelected outright in the first round with 58.1% of the vote (4,520 ballots), supported by a center-right coalition including Forza Italia, Lega Salvini Premier, Fratelli d'Italia, and civic lists like Per Todi con Ruspolini and Todi Tricolore. His main challenger, Fabio Catterini of a center-left coalition (PD, civic lists, and Sinistra per Todi), received 27.7%, while independent center candidate Floriano Pizzichini garnered 14.2%. Voter turnout was approximately 60%, reflecting sustained engagement in a town of about 16,000 residents. Ruggiano, an independent lawyer born in 1970, continued as mayor into 2025, though he resigned from Forza Italia's regional coordination in January amid internal party tensions.

Culture and society

Traditions, festivals, and cuisine

Todi's traditions are deeply rooted in its medieval heritage, particularly evident in historical reenactments that emphasize archery and communal gatherings. The town is known as the "City of Archers" due to longstanding practices of crossbow competitions, which symbolize its defensive past and are preserved through annual events.
The primary traditional festival is the Disfida di San Fortunato, held in mid-October to honor the city's patron saint, Fortunatus, a 4th-century bishop. This event features medieval costumes, archery tournaments between districts, flag-throwing displays, and markets under historic vaults, recreating 13th-century atmospheres with participants in period attire parading through streets. Organized by local quarters (contrade), it fosters community rivalry and culminates in a procession to the saint's basilica, blending religious devotion with secular spectacle.
Complementing these is the Todi Festival, an annual cultural event from late August to early Septemberscheduled for August 30 to September 7 in 2025focusing on theater, music, dance, visual arts, and literature across the town's historic venues like Piazza del Popolo and communal porticos. While more contemporary, it integrates local artistic traditions, including sacred music performances in September's Festival di Musica Sacra.
Local cuisine emphasizes simple, seasonal ingredients reflective of Umbria's agrarian economy, with Todi specialties highlighting game and flatbreads. The signature dish is palomba alla ghiotta (or alla todina), a wood pigeon stewed with olives, capers, and tomatoes, braised in its own fat for tenderness, tracing to medieval hunting practices. Torta al testo, a dense unleavened bread made from flour and water, grilled and split to sandwich cured meats, cheeses, or wild greens, serves as a staple for street food and accompanies soups or roasts. Regional influences include pastas like pappardelle with wild boar rag or tagliatelle with black truffles from nearby hills, paired with Orvieto Classico wines, underscoring Todi's reliance on foraged and farmed produce.

Sports and community life

Todi's sports scene emphasizes amateur and recreational activities, with football as the primary organized pursuit. The Associazione Sportiva Dilettantistica Todi Calcio, the town's main football club, competes in regional Umbrian leagues, such as the Eccellenza, with home games at the Franco Martelli Municipal Stadium, which accommodates local matches and youth training. The club, registered with the Italian National Olympic Committee (CONI), draws community support through seasonal fixtures that engage residents in spectator and participatory roles.
Tennis holds prominence via the Tennis Club Todi 1971, a facility offering courts, padel, gym, and sauna services at the town's outskirts. It annually hosts the Internazionali di Tennis Citt di Todi, an ATP Challenger 100 event from August 10 to 16, 2025, attracting professional players and boosting local economy through tourism. Other offerings include athletics via Atletica Todi, which has operated for over 25 years to promote fitness and territorial events, alongside facilities like the municipal swimming pool, Tashunka Equestrian Center for horseback riding, and a bowling green for leisure.
Community life integrates sports with traditions through associations and events that strengthen social bonds. The La Disfida di San Fortunato, a historical re-enactment from October 6 to 14 honoring the town's patron saint, features archery competitions, medieval games, and parades involving flag-throwers and falconers, drawing participants from local districts. Nonprofit groups like Friends of Todi, comprising local and international residents, facilitate gatherings and shared interests among second-home owners and expatriates. Sports clubs further this by organizing youth programs and inclusive activities, countering urban isolation in the hilltop setting.

Architecture and landmarks

Religious sites and structures

Todi's religious sites reflect the town's medieval and Renaissance heritage, with churches serving as focal points for worship and pilgrimage. The Concattedrale della Santissima Annunziata, known as Todi Cathedral, stands as the principal ecclesiastical structure in the historic center. Constructed primarily in the 12th century on the site of an earlier church destroyed by fire in 1190, it features a 13th-century facade with a rose window added in 1515 and a central portal from the 16th century. The interior includes a Gothic high altar dating to 1343 and remnants of 13th-century frescoes, underscoring its evolution through Gothic influences.
The Church of San Fortunato, perched on the highest point of the town, exemplifies Gothic architecture with Renaissance elements. Begun in the late 13th century by Franciscan friars, it was expanded in the 14th and 15th centuries, though its facade remains incomplete. The crypt houses the tomb of Jacopone da Todi (c. 12301306), a Franciscan mystic and poet who contributed to the Laudesi movement, with the tomb itself erected in 1596. Fragments of frescoes attributed to Masolino da Panicale adorn the interior, dating to the early 15th century. Dedicated to San Fortunato, Todi's patron saint, the church hosts annual celebrations on October 14.
Outside the medieval walls, the Tempio di Santa Maria della Consolazione represents a pinnacle of Renaissance design, constructed between 1508 and 1607 as a pilgrimage site. Its Greek cross plan, surmounted by a 70-meter-high dome, symbolizes harmony and was built to enshrine a 14th-century icon depicting the mystical marriage of Saint Catherine. The structure's symmetric proportions and fusion of Renaissance and Baroque details highlight its role in reconciling local factions post-conflict.

Secular and civic architecture

The historic core of Todi centers on Piazza del Popolo, a medieval square originating from the Roman forum and renowned for its cohesive ensemble of Gothic civic structures representing communal governance. The square's pavement rests on an underlying system of Roman-era cisterns that once supported public infrastructure.
Dominating the southern side is the Palazzo del Popolo, a Lombard-Gothic palace initiated around 12131214 as one of Italy's earliest documented communal town halls, featuring a porticoed ground floor for public assembly and administrative functions. Construction progressed in phases, with upper stories added in the 13th century, and the facade oriented definitively by the 16th century; 19th-century restorations introduced crenellated merlons for aesthetic enhancement while preserving its medieval core. Today, it houses the Civic Museum and art gallery, showcasing local archaeological and artistic collections.
Adjacent stands the Palazzo del Capitano del Popolo, completed in 1293 in Italian Gothic style to serve as residence for a captain-magistrate advocating lower-class interests amid factional politics. Further along, the Palazzo dei Priori, erected by 1347 with Gothic elements and early 16th-century Renaissance modifications, functioned as the priors' administrative seat, underscoring Todi's self-governing medieval institutions.
Todi's defensive perimeter comprises three concentric walls: the innermost, attributed to Etruscans around the 3rd century BCE; a middle Roman layer; and an outermost medieval circuit from the 13th14th centuries, incorporating gates like Porta Catena and remnants of bastions for fortification against regional conflicts. These enclosures delineate the town's expansion across its hilltop site, blending ancient engineering with later reinforcements.

Archaeological and natural sites

Todi's archaeological record reflects its ancient Umbrian-Etruscan origins, with the settlement known as Tutere on pre-Roman coins, indicating an early urban center influenced by Etruscan culture through trade and grave goods found in nearby tombs. Roman integration began gradually in the 3rd century BC, leading to infrastructure development evidenced by surviving structures.
Key Roman-era remains include the Todi Underground, a subterranean complex featuring over 30 cisterns from pre-Roman, Roman, and medieval periods, alongside approximately 500 wells of diverse forms and dates, used for water storage and distribution. The Nicchioni, two large brick niches from the 1st century AD, stand as monumental features possibly linked to a temple podium or public building, though their exact function remains debated beyond earlier misidentification as a forum. The town's defensive evolution is marked by three concentric wall circuits: the innermost from the Umbrian-Roman phase, a middle Roman ring, and an outer medieval enclosure, with remnants like Porta Marzia preserving elements of the ancient perimeter.
Natural sites near Todi emphasize the region's fluvial and hilly landscapes. The Tiber River Park encompasses 7,295 hectares along 50 kilometers of the Tiber from the Montemolino bridge to Alviano Lake, supporting diverse ecosystems with riparian forests, wetlands, and wildlife habitats that extend to Todi's vicinity. Parco della Rocca, situated atop Todi's central hill, functions as a green public space integrated with historical fortifications, offering elevated vistas over the surrounding Umbrian countryside and serving as an accessible natural area amid the town's medieval core.
