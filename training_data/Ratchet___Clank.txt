 Ratchet & Clank is an action-adventure video game series developed by Insomniac Games and published by Sony Interactive Entertainment, primarily for PlayStation consoles. The franchise centers on the titular duo: Ratchet, a courageous Lombax mechanic from the planet Veldin, and Clank, his small but highly intelligent robotic companion, as they embark on interstellar quests to thwart evil forces threatening the galaxy.
Debuting on November 4, 2002, with the original Ratchet & Clank for the PlayStation 2, the series blends platforming, shooting, and puzzle-solving gameplay with humorous narratives, inventive weapons like the Groovitron, and exploration of diverse alien worlds. Over two decades, it has grown to encompass nine mainline titles, including sequels such as Ratchet & Clank: Going Commando (2003), Ratchet & Clank: Up Your Arsenal (2004), and the dimension-hopping Ratchet & Clank: Rift Apart (2021) for PlayStation 5. Recurring characters like the boastful superhero Captain Qwark and the villainous Dr. Nefarious add depth to the stories, which often span multiple sagas involving time travel, alternate dimensions, and epic battles.
The series is renowned for its cinematic presentation, accessible yet challenging mechanics, and signature blend of comedy and sci-fi action, evolving from third-person shooters with platform elements to showcase advanced hardware features in later entries. A 2016 reimagining of the original game revitalized the franchise, while spin-offs and a 2016 animated film expanded its universe, with a new mobile title, Ratchet & Clank: Ranger Rumble, announced in November 2025. This has solidified Ratchet & Clank as a beloved pillar of PlayStation's gaming legacy.

History

Origins and development

The Ratchet & Clank franchise originated at Insomniac Games, which developed the inaugural title for the PlayStation 2 in 2002 under the leadership of founder and CEO Ted Price and creative officer Brian Hastings. Following the completion of their third Spyro the Dragon game in 2001, the studio sought a new intellectual property to sustain its momentum as an independent developer partnered with Sony Computer Entertainment. Hastings sketched the core concept on graph paper, envisioning a duo adventure to revitalize the team amid creative challenges.
The idea drew inspiration from the adventurous spirit of Spaceman Spiff in the Calvin and Hobbes comic strip, the quirky antics of Marvin the Martian from Looney Tunes, and contemporary platformers such as Jak and Daxter: The Precursor Legacy, which influenced the genre-blending approach. Development began in earnest in 2001, with rapid prototyping that refined Ratchet as a resourceful mechanic-type charactera feline-like Lombax skilled with toolsand Clank as his inventive robot sidekick, emphasizing themes of humor, fast-paced action, and interstellar exploration across diverse worlds. This partnership dynamic allowed for collaborative gameplay, where Clank's abilities complemented Ratchet's agility, fostering a lighthearted narrative tone.
A key innovation was the seamless integration of platforming, third-person shooting, and light RPG elements, such as weapon upgrades funded by collected currency, which distinguished it from pure platformers and shooters of the era. Voice performances enhanced the characters' charm, with Mikey Kelley providing Ratchet's enthusiastic and youthful delivery, while David Kaye lent Clank a precise, witty robotic inflection. The game launched on November 4, 2002, in North America, published by Sony Computer Entertainment, and was marketed as a fresh, family-friendly action-adventure to showcase the PS2's capabilities. This debut paved the way for the series' expansion into multiple sequels.

Series evolution and hiatus

Following the success of the original Ratchet & Clank in 2002, Insomniac Games expanded the series across the PlayStation 2 with three sequels released between 2003 and 2005, refining gameplay mechanics and broadening appeal. Ratchet & Clank: Going Commando (2003) introduced customizable starships for space combat, an expanded arsenal of over 30 weapons, and challenge arenas that encouraged replayability through skill-based trials. These additions shifted the formula toward greater RPG-like progression, allowing players to upgrade gadgets and armor for deeper customization. The subsequent Ratchet & Clank: Up Your Arsenal (2004) further evolved the series by incorporating online multiplayer for up to eight players in deathmatch and capture-the-flag modes, marking the first such implementation in an action-platformer on consoles. This title also emphasized narrative depth through a more cinematic story involving interstellar politics and media satire, while Ratchet: Deadlocked (2005) leaned into arena-based gladiatorial combat with vehicular battles and team-based multiplayer, adopting a darker tone to explore themes of exploitation in entertainment.
The franchise transitioned to the PlayStation 3 in 2007 with the Future saga, a tetralogy that relocated the action to the Polaris Galaxy and prioritized emotional storytelling alongside technical advancements. Ratchet & Clank Future: Tools of Destruction (2007) served as the saga's launch, introducing high-fidelity graphics optimized for the new hardware, dynamic planet-hopping via a sentient ship, and lore expanding on Ratchet's Lombax heritage, which added personal stakes to the adventure. Subsequent entries, including the downloadable Ratchet & Clank Future: Quest for Booty (2008), Ratchet & Clank Future: A Crack in Time (2009), and Ratchet & Clank: Into the Nexus (2013), delved into time travel mechanics using the Great Clock, enabling puzzle-solving across timelines and fostering character-driven drama, particularly Clank's quest for identity. This era marked a stylistic shift toward mature themes of loss and redemption, with upgraded combat systems featuring gravity manipulation and seamless level transitions, while maintaining the series' humorous core.
Between 2006 and 2012, Insomniac experimented with spin-offs to test new formats, including handheld titles and cooperative multiplayer experiences, often developed in collaboration with secondary studios. Portable games like Ratchet & Clank: Size Matters (2007, PSP) and Secret Agent Clank (2008, PS2) adapted the platforming for smaller screens, emphasizing puzzle elements and stealth mechanics respectively, though they received mixed reception for scaled-down scope. On consoles, Ratchet & Clank: All 4 One (2011) pioneered four-player co-op as a core mechanic, requiring synchronized actions like shared ziplines and combo attacks in a linear campaign, dropping traditional solo exploration in favor of team-based progression. The following year's Ratchet & Clank: Full Frontal Assault (2012) blended tower defense with multiplayer battles, where players built fortifications during missions, aiming to innovate on competitive play but resulting in polarizing reviews due to repetitive structure.
The series entered a hiatus from 2013 to 2016 after Ratchet & Clank: Into the Nexus (2013), the final mainline entry of the Future saga, as Insomniac redirected resources to other projects amid declining sales and internal priorities. Later PS3 titles like All 4 One and Full Frontal Assault underperformed commercially compared to PS2 peaks, with unit sales dropping below 2 million each, prompting a reevaluation of the formula. Insomniac shifted focus to the Resistance trilogy's conclusion and the multiplayer shooter Fuse (2013), the latter a cross-studio collaboration that failed to meet expectations and contributed to financial strain. Developer burnout from rapid iteration across multiple franchises also factored in, as the studio's expansion to multiplatform efforts like Sunset Overdrive (2014, Xbox One) strained creative bandwidth. This pause ended the Insomniac-exclusive era for new mainline content until a 2016 reimagining, though the 2019 acquisition by Sony Interactive Entertainment for $229 million integrated the studio fully into PlayStation's ecosystem, retaining the core development team to sustain long-term franchise oversight.

Recent developments and future prospects

The Ratchet & Clank franchise experienced a significant revival starting in 2016 with the release of a reimagining of the original 2002 game, developed by Insomniac Games for the PlayStation 4. This title served as both a remake and a soft reboot, incorporating updated visuals and gameplay mechanics tailored to modern hardware while tying directly into the narrative of the 2016 animated film to attract a new generation of players.
In 2021, Ratchet & Clank: Rift Apart launched as a flagship title for the PlayStation 5, showcasing the console's SSD technology to enable seamless dimension-hopping mechanics that allowed for rapid transitions between worlds without traditional loading screens. The game received critical acclaim for its innovative use of hardware capabilities and expansive platforming action. A PC port followed in July 2023, developed by Nixxes Software and Insomniac Games, expanding accessibility to non-PlayStation platforms while maintaining high-fidelity graphics and performance options like ultrawide support.
To mark the franchise's 20th anniversary in 2022, Insomniac Games released a free "20th Anniversary Armor Pack" for Rift Apart on November 4, featuring five cosmetic armors inspired by classic entries in the series, available to all players via an in-game update. Later that month, on November 15, PlayStation Plus Premium added streaming access to five additional Ratchet & Clank titles from the PS3 era, including Ratchet & Clank Future: Tools of Destruction and Ratchet & Clank Full Frontal Assault, further preserving and promoting the series' legacy. In September 2024, Secret Agent Clank joined the PlayStation Plus Premium catalog as a playable title for PS4 and PS5, enhanced with up-rendering, rewind functionality, quick save, and custom video filters to modernize the 2008 PSP spin-off.
A previously lost mobile game, Ratchet & Clank: Clone Home, originally developed for J2ME platforms around 2006, was leaked and fully archived in October 2025 after nearly two decades of preservation efforts by enthusiasts. Intended as a side-scrolling adventure but canceled before release, the complete build has since been made playable via emulation, offering new insight into early mobile experiments within the franchise.
On November 12, 2025, Sony Interactive Entertainment announced Ratchet & Clank: Ranger Rumble, a free-to-play multiplayer arena shooter developed by OhBibi for iOS and Android devices, marking a new mobile entry in the franchise.
Looking ahead, a 2023 data breach at Insomniac Games revealed internal roadmaps indicating a new mainline Ratchet & Clank title targeting fiscal year 2029, positioned after ongoing Marvel projects such as Wolverine and Spider-Man 3, with development emphasizing next-generation console features. No new franchise releases occurred between 2023 and 2025, allowing Insomniac to focus on these external commitments while sustaining interest through ports and re-releases.

Setting

Universe overview

The Ratchet & Clank universe is a sprawling science fiction setting that encompasses multiple galaxies, including the Solana and Polaris systems, where advanced interstellar societies coexist with quirky, consumer-driven worlds filled with gadgets and alien cultures. This galactic framework supports epic adventures blending high-tech exploration with lighthearted escapades, emphasizing a vibrant cosmos of bustling space stations, diverse planets, and interdimensional phenomena.
At its core, the universe explores themes of unlikely friendship between organic and robotic lifeforms, heroic stands against corporate overlords and imperial tyrants, and the perils and wonders of venturing into alternate dimensions. These narratives highlight resilience and camaraderie in the face of existential threats, such as environmental exploitation or robotic uprisings, often underscoring the value of diverse alliances across species like the inventive Lombaxes. The satirical edge pokes fun at sci-fi conventions, portraying a universe where heroism emerges from everyday mechanics and malfunctions alike.
The timeline unfolds non-linearly across the series, beginning in the Solana Galaxy during the year 5354 marked by industrial expansion and villainous schemes, then shifting to the Polaris Galaxy in a post-time rift future altered by temporal anomalies. Later entries, such as those involving the Dimensionator device, introduce a multiverse structure, allowing cross-dimensional travel that connects disparate realities and timelines. This structure enables overlapping sagas, where past events influence future conflicts without a strict chronological progression.
Technological elements define the universe's adventurous spirit, featuring iconic tools like the OmniWrench for combat and repairs, hoverboots for agile traversal, and customizable spaceships for interstellar navigation, alongside rift-based portals for leaping between dimensions. These innovations facilitate dynamic gameplay in a tone that mixes exhilarating action with comedic villainy and colorful, ever-shifting environments, creating a satirical homage to classic space operas.

Lombaxes and key species

The Lombaxes are a feline-like humanoid species originating from the planet Fastoon in the Polaris Galaxy, renowned for their exceptional ingenuity in technology and engineering. Biologically, they possess heightened intelligence and agility, with physical traits including fur, ears, and tails, enabling them to excel as inventors and mechanics. Their culture emphasizes innovation, loyalty to kin, and a post-war commitment to peace following the Great War against the Cragmites, during which they developed advanced devices like the Dimensionator for dimensional travel. However, betrayal by the Cragmite scientist Percival Tachyon, whom they had integrated into their society, led to a devastating siege on Fastoon, prompting most Lombaxes to flee to a parallel dimension using the Dimensionator. Subsequent threats from the robotic dictator Dr. Nefarious further contributed to their near-extinction, leaving them as a nomadic, scattered race focused on survival and technological preservation. In the Solana Galaxy, Ratchet represents the last known Lombax, while in Polaris, Alister Azimuth was among the few who remained to combat the invasion.
Other prominent species in the Ratchet & Clank universe include the Blarg, a diminutive, green-skinned race of industrialists native to the polluted, overpopulated planet Orxon in the Solana Galaxy. The Blarg prioritize mass production and resource exploitation, often at environmental cost, as seen in their construction of massive fleets and factories to address planetary habitability issues. Robots, particularly those originating from Blarg factories on planets like Quartu, form a synthetic species integral to galactic conflicts; Clank, a defective sentry-bot model, exemplifies their role as companions and warriors despite initial programming flaws. The Zoni are ethereal beings composed of pure bio-energy, capable of manipulating time and space, who oversee the Great Clocka monumental device regulating the universe's timeline. Lacking physical form in their natural state, the Zoni integrate with machinery and value cosmic balance, often intervening in temporal disruptions. The Grungal, monkey-like primates featured in spin-off titles, hail from remote worlds and exhibit tribal, agile behaviors suited to forested or rugged terrains, serving as opportunistic allies or foes in multi-species adventures.
Interspecies dynamics within the series highlight alliances forged against common threats, such as Lombaxes collaborating with robots like Clank to thwart invasions, underscoring themes of unity across biological and synthetic divides. However, arcs reveal underlying prejudices, particularly against synthetics, where organic races view robots as disposable tools, leading to tensions resolved through shared heroism.

Planets and factions

The Ratchet & Clank series is set across diverse planets that function as central explorable locations, each with distinct environmental biomes influencing gameplay and enemy encounters. Veldin serves as Ratchet's homeworld, a arid desert planet dotted with junkyards and mechanical debris, where Ratchet maintains his garage amid vast, dusty plateaus. Kerwan represents a bustling urban center and hub for robotic societies, characterized by towering skyscrapers, elevated walkways, and a dense metropolis that highlights advanced technological infrastructure. Ryllus is a lush, jungle-covered world that hosts mercenary operations, featuring thick foliage, ancient ruins, and hazardous terrain teeming with wildlife. Quantos acts as a mystical realm tied to ethereal beings, encompassing swampy forests and foggy woodlands that evoke an otherworldly, ancient atmosphere. Sargasso, a primordial bog planet, is riddled with acidic swamps, prehistoric flora, and massive carnivorous creatures, creating a treacherous, untamed ecosystem in the Polaris Galaxy.
Key factions in the series drive interstellar conflicts and alliances, often embodying themes of order, chaos, or conquest. The Galactic Rangers operate as the primary law enforcement body in the Solana Galaxy, functioning as an elite superhero team dedicated to protecting planets from threats through coordinated patrols and defensive operations. Thugs-4-Less is a notorious mercenary gang that provides hired muscle for various criminal enterprises, operating from hidden bases and deploying customizable enforcers for sabotage and intimidation. The Blarg Empire, under its polluting regime, pursues aggressive expansion by exploiting planetary resources, leading to widespread environmental degradation through industrial weaponry and fleet deployments. Nefarious forces represent a robotic tyranny, enforcing authoritarian control via automated armies and advanced war machines aimed at subjugating organic life across dimensions.
These planets and factions are integral to the series' lore, with locations providing biome-specific challenges like desert traversal on Veldin or swamp navigation on Sargasso, while groups such as the Galactic Rangers offer cooperative defensive roles and antagonists like the Blarg Empire or Nefarious forces propel narratives of invasion and ecological harm. Inhabitants often include diverse species adapted to these environments, such as robotic denizens on Kerwan.

Characters

Protagonists

Ratchet is a Lombax, a feline-like alien species, who grew up as an orphan on the desert planet Veldin, where he worked as a mechanic and aspiring pilot dreaming of galactic adventure. Initially driven by a desire for fame and glory, inspired by figures like Captain Qwark, Ratchet matures through his experiences into a responsible hero who prioritizes saving others over personal acclaim. He is voiced by Mikey Kelley in the original 2002 game and by James Arnold Taylor from Ratchet & Clank: Going Commando (2003) onward, including the 2016 remake and later titles.
Clank, designated XJ-0461, is a small, defective robot originally produced in a Blarg factory on the planet Quartu, intended for war but malfunctioning due to his inherent Zoni soul, crafted by the Zoni leader Orvus within the Great Clock. As an analytical and logical sidekick, Clank begins as a more mechanical entity but develops emotions and deeper self-awareness through his bond with Ratchet and encounters with the Zoni race. He has been consistently voiced by David Kaye across the series, from the 2002 debut through the 2016 remake and Rift Apart (2021).
Rivet is a female Lombax from an alternate dimension, introduced in Ratchet & Clank: Rift Apart (2021) as a resistance fighter who allies with Ratchet to combat Dr. Nefarious's multiversal threat. Voiced by Jennifer Hale, Rivet shares Ratchet's adventurous spirit but carries the burden of her dimension's hardships, including the loss of her own Lombax heritage. Her hammer weapon and acrobatic style complement Ratchet's arsenal, emphasizing themes of parallel lives and unity across dimensions.
Kit is Rivet's robotic companion, a Warbot similar to Clank but initially more timid and inexperienced, providing flight assistance, gadgets, and emotional support. Voiced by Alanna Ubach, Kit grows in confidence through her partnership with Rivet, mirroring Clank's evolution, and aids in dimension-rifting mechanics central to Rift Apart.
The duo's core dynamic revolves around their complementary strengths, with Ratchet's physical prowess and combat skills pairing seamlessly with Clank's intellect, gadgetry, and thruster capabilities that enable enhanced mobility and puzzle-solving. This partnership underscores themes of enduring friendship, where Clank serves as the voice of reason to temper Ratchet's impulsiveness, evolving from a simple alliance into a profound, brotherly bond that drives their adventures. In Rift Apart, these dynamics extend to Rivet and Kit, forming a multiversal team.
Together, Ratchet and Clank's shared narrative arc transforms them from accidental heroesunited by chance to thwart Chairman Drek's planetary destruction in the original gameinto seasoned guardians of the multiverse, confronting interdimensional threats in Rift Apart while reflecting on their growth and the vast universe they protect, alongside Rivet and Kit.

Supporting allies

Captain Qwark, full name Copernicus Leslie Qwark, is a prominent supporting ally in the Ratchet & Clank series, portrayed as an egotistical intergalactic superhero and former member of the Galactic Rangers. Initially introduced as a self-serving antagonist who betrays the protagonists for personal gain, Qwark undergoes a redemption arc, evolving into a loyal, if comically incompetent, companion who offers leadership and humorous relief during missions. His character frequently highlights themes of heroism and ego, serving as a foil to Ratchet's more grounded heroism while contributing to team dynamics in multiple entries.
Talwyn Apogee emerges as a key ally in the Future saga, depicted as a skilled resistance fighter hailing from the planet Sargasso and an expert pilot who joins Ratchet and Clank in their battles against threats like Emperor Tachyon. As Talwyn's mentors, she provides tactical support, ship piloting, and emotional depth to the narrative, developing a romantic relationship with Ratchet that Insomniac Games has officially confirmed through developer discussions. Her background as a Markazian underscores themes of interstellar resistance and personal loss, making her an integral part of the protagonists' extended family.
Angela Cross serves as a pivotal supporting character in Ratchet & Clank: Going Commando, introduced as a brilliant female Lombax scientist and former MegaCorp researcher who invents the powerful Disintegrator weapon. Disillusioned with corporate ethics, she steals the prototype to prevent its misuse, eventually allying with Ratchet and Clank while offering insights into Lombax history and technology. Her appearances hint at broader Lombax lore, though Insomniac Games has expressed uncertainty about her canonical status in later timelines, positioning her as a mysterious figure tied to the species' enigmatic past.
Cronk and Zephyr form a memorable duo of elderly warbots, acting as Talwyn Apogee's loyal mentors and bodyguards with a history of fighting in nineteen galactic wars. Known for their bickering comic banter and contrasting combat stylesCronk with slower, heavier firepower and Zephyr with rapid burst shotsthey provide essential support in battles during Ratchet & Clank Future: A Crack in Time. Their senile yet endearing personalities add levity, while their sacrifices highlight the emotional stakes of the Future saga.
Other recurring allies include Big Al, a shady green-skinned vendor and robotics engineer who supplies gadgets and hacks systems for Ratchet across multiple games; Skid McMarx, a laid-back hoverboard champion and Q-Force recruit who offers racing expertise and morale boosts; Sasha Phyronix, the capable Cazar commander of the Starship Phoenix who coordinates galactic defenses in Up Your Arsenal; and The Plumber, a enigmatic Solanian handyman who mysteriously aids the heroes with repairs and cryptic guidance whenever plumbing issues arise in the Solana Galaxy.

Antagonists

The primary antagonist of the original Ratchet & Clank is Chairman Alonzo Drek, the tyrannical leader of the Blarg species and CEO of Drek Industries. Motivated by overpopulation on their polluted homeworld Orxon, Drek launches a scheme to construct a new planet for the Blarg by stripping habitable worlds across the Solana Galaxy using his fleet's Deplanetizer technology, rendering the targeted planets uninhabitable and causing widespread ecological devastation. His plan culminates in assembling pieces from eleven planets into New Orxon, but he is ultimately defeated when Ratchet reprograms the Deplanetizer to target Drek's ship, destroying both the chairman and his artificial world in a reversal of his own weapon.
Emperor Percival Tachyon serves as the central villain in Ratchet & Clank Future: Tools of Destruction, portrayed as a self-proclaimed crown prince of the Cragmite race and a former exile raised by the advanced Zoni species on their homeworld. Harboring deep resentment toward the Zoni for banishing him after discovering his destructive ambitions, Tachyon seeks vengeance by harnessing Zoni technology to travel through time and eradicate the Lombax race, whom he blames for previously defeating the Cragmites and exiling them to the planet Reepor. He rebuilds his Imperial Army, invades planets in the Polaris Galaxy to gather resources, and targets Ratchet as the last known Lombax, but his schemes are thwarted in a battle on Fastoon, where he is banished to dimension A2-66 when the Dimensionator malfunctions.
Doctor Nefarious emerges as a recurring archenemy across multiple entries, beginning in Ratchet & Clank Up Your Arsenal and prominently featured in the Future saga (Quest for Booty, A Crack in Time) and Rift Apart. Originally an organic inventor who attended high school with Captain Qwark, Nefarious was transformed into a robot after a prison escape attempt gone wrong, fueling his pathological hatred for organic lifeforms and a desire to replace them with machines. He constructs vast robotic armies, including the Tyrhannosaurus and clones like Klank, and manipulates time via the Great Clock to rewrite history in favor of robotic dominance, such as erasing the Galactic Rangers or conquering dimensions. Voiced by David Kaye throughout the series, Nefarious is repeatedly defeated, including by destruction of his space station in Up Your Arsenal, prevention of his temporal alterations in A Crack in Time, and dimensional exile after a final battle in Rift Apart.
Alister Azimuth appears as the antagonist in Ratchet & Clank Future: A Crack in Time, depicted as a Lombax general and close friend of Ratchet's father, Kaden, who once led the defense against the Cragmites. Believing the Lombaxes' self-imposed exile through dimensional rifts was a cowardly abandonment rather than survival, Azimuth betrays his people by allying with Emperor Tachyon during the Great War, providing the Cragmite leader with a dimensional map that enables the near-extinction of the Lombaxes, including the murder of Kaden to prevent Ratchet's escape. Surviving into exile, Azimuth later seeks to use the Great Clock to reverse time and restore the Lombax race for what he views as the "greater good," manipulating Ratchet as a surrogate son before revealing his treachery. Following his defeat in battle, Azimuth sacrifices himself atop the Great Clock to halt the time shift, dying in the process to save the universe.
Several secondary antagonists appear across the franchise, often with redemptive arcs or limited roles. In Going Commando, Angela initially opposes Ratchet and Clank by hiring the Thugs-4-Less to steal Gadgetron technology, aiming to prevent the weaponization of her Protopet invention, but she ultimately allies with the heroes and vanishes with inventor Max Apogee. In Quest for Booty, the pirate Grimroth Razz serves as a brief adversary amid the search for Clank, clashing with Ratchet over treasures in the Azorean Sea before his defeat aids the quest's progression. Helga von Streissenburgen, a robotic aide originally Qwark's fitness trainer, is reprogrammed to assist Emperor Tachyon in Tools of Destruction, managing his operations until the emperor's downfall. In spin-offs like Size Matters, Skrunch the cycloptic monkey acts as a playful rival to Ratchet in racing challenges on Dayni Moon, competing for artifacts while ultimately aiding the protagonists against larger threats.

Games

Mainline series overview

The mainline Ratchet & Clank series comprises ten titles developed primarily by Insomniac Games and released exclusively on PlayStation platforms from 2002 to 2021. These games are structured into the Original trilogy (Ratchet & Clank , Going Commando , Up Your Arsenal , and Deadlocked ), which centers on escalating threats within the Solana Galaxy; the Future saga (Tools of Destruction , Quest for Booty , A Crack in Time , and Into the Nexus ), set in the Polaris Galaxy with elements of time manipulation; and later entries including the 2016 reimagining of the original game and Rift Apart , which introduces multiversal dimensions. This progression reflects Insomniac's evolution from PS2-era action adventures to more narrative-driven experiences on subsequent hardware.
Central to the series are third-person action-platforming mechanics, where protagonists Ratchet and Clank traverse vibrant planets, collect bolts as currency for weapon and gadget upgrades, and engage in boss battles against tyrannical overlords like Chairman Drek and Dr. Nefarious. Narratively, the storyline advances from environmental destruction in the Original trilogyexemplified by Drek's planet-polluting schemesto explorations of Ratchet's Lombax heritage and familial bonds in the Future saga, before shifting to interdimensional rifts and alternate realities in Rift Apart, emphasizing themes of identity, friendship, and cosmic consequence. These arcs build a cohesive yet expansive lore, with recurring motifs of heroism against galactic oppression.
The series' chronology operates non-linearly, as Rift Apart interconnects the Future saga's timeline with an alternate iteration of the Original trilogy via the Dimensionator artifact, allowing seamless jumps across eras and realities without strict adherence to release order. Developmentally, the games transitioned from PS2's focus on expansive worlds and humor-infused action to PS3's cinematic storytelling and time-bending mechanics, then to PS4's refined combat visuals inspired by CG animation, and finally PS5's exploitation of ray-tracing, ultra-fast SSD loading, and DualSense haptics for heightened immersion and emotional resonance. As of 2023, the mainline titles had collectively sold over 40 million units, underscoring their enduring commercial and critical impact.

Original trilogy

The original trilogy consists of four mainline entries developed by Insomniac Games exclusively for the PlayStation 2, establishing the core formula of action-platforming adventures in a vibrant sci-fi universe while evolving the narrative partnership between protagonists Ratchet and Clank. Released between 2002 and 2005, these games follow the duo as they confront increasingly sophisticated threats across galaxies, blending humor, exploration, and combat in a way that garnered widespread praise for their polish and wit.
Ratchet & Clank (2002) introduces the titular characters on the desert planet Veldin in the Solana Galaxy, where ambitious Lombax mechanic Ratchet encounters the defective robot Clank, who has escaped a Blarg factory. Clank reveals that Chairman Alonzo Drek, leader of the polluted Blarg homeworld, plans to destroy other planets and reassemble their fragments around his world to save it from environmental collapse, prompting the pair to embark on a galaxy-hopping quest to thwart him. The game innovates with melee combat using Ratchet's OmniWrench tool for close-quarters attacks and bolt collection, alongside piloting ship sections for space dogfights between planetary levels, setting a template for seamless transitions between ground and aerial action.
Ratchet & Clank: Going Commando (2003), also known as Locked and Loaded in some regions, shifts the action to the Bogon Galaxy, where celebrity status post-Drek leads Ratchet and Clank to be recruited by Abercrombie Fizzwidget, CEO of the arms manufacturer Megacorp, to retrieve a stolen biological experiment called the Protopet. Their investigation uncovers a conspiracy involving the thief Angela Cross and the mercenary group Thugs-4-Less, who seek to weaponize the seemingly cute creatures, culminating in a twist revealing deeper corporate machinations. Building on the original, it introduces RPG-style weapon upgrades that evolve gadgets through usage for enhanced power and effects, as well as diverse mini-games like hoverbike races and robo-ship challenges to break up the platforming.
Ratchet & Clank: Up Your Arsenal (2004) brings the heroes back to the Solana Galaxy after a distress signal reveals an invasion by the robotic Tyhrranoid army under the command of the mad scientist Dr. Nefarious, who aims to eradicate all organic life and replace it with machines using his "Thermostat" device. Ratchet joins the Galactic Rangers led by Captain Qwark to fight back, while Clank stars in a spy holovision parody called Secret Agent Clank, adding layers of media satire to the conflict. The title expands with arena battle modes for competitive combat trials, hoverboard races on tracks like those on Metalis for high-speed challenges, and deeper weapon customization, refining the series' blend of shooting and traversal.
Ratchet: Deadlocked (2005), known as Gladiator in some PAL regions, sees Ratchet, Clank, and their ally Al captured by the ruthless media mogul Gleeman Vox and forced to compete as gladiators in the illegal Dreadzone tournament, a deadly reality show featuring abducted heroes. To survive and overthrow Vox's operation, Ratchet battles in intense arena matches, liberates fellow combatants, and upgrades modular weapons while Clank hacks systems from the sidelines. The game emphasizes multiplayer arenas, online play, and vehicular combat with customizable ships, shifting toward competitive elements while retaining platforming and shooting core. It earned a Metascore of 82, commended for its refined combat and variety but critiqued for reduced exploration focus.
Across the trilogy, the narrative arcs emphasize the evolving bond between Ratchet's impulsive heroism and Clank's logical caution, progressing from a reluctant alliance against a corporate polluter to confronting mercenary syndicates and ultimately a genocidal AI, mirroring the duo's maturation amid escalating galactic perils. All four titles were PS2 exclusives, leveraging the console's capabilities for fluid animations and expansive worlds. Critically, they achieved Metascores of 88 for the debut, 90 for Going Commando, a series-high 91 for Up Your Arsenal, and 82 for Deadlocked, with reviewers lauding the sharp humoroften through pop culture parodies and witty dialogueas a standout element that elevated the platformer genre.

Future saga

The Future saga of the Ratchet & Clank series, spanning the PlayStation 3 era from 2007 to 2013, marks a shift toward more mature storytelling, expanded lore, and innovative gameplay mechanics centered on time manipulation and dimensional travel. Developed by Insomniac Games and published by Sony Computer Entertainment, these titles build on the duo's adventures in the Polaris Galaxy, delving into Ratchet's Lombax origins and the consequences of past conflicts. The arc introduces key antagonists and allies while incorporating co-op elements and new wrench-based abilities, emphasizing emotional depth in the protagonists' bond.
Ratchet & Clank Future: Tools of Destruction (2007) launches the saga in the Polaris Galaxy, where Emperor Percival Tachyon, a vengeful Cragmite leader, orchestrates an invasion of Ratchet's homeworld to enact genocide against the Lombaxes, the feline-like species responsible for imprisoning the Cragmites during the Great War. Ratchet, discovering his true Lombax heritage, flees with Clank and encounters Alister Azimuth, a battle-scarred Lombax and former Praetorian Guard general who reveals the threat of dimension keysartifacts that could open portals to the Lombax home dimension, allowing Tachyon to complete his extermination. The narrative follows their quest across planets like Cobalia and Kerwan to gather these keys, blending high-stakes platforming with wrench combat innovations, such as the OmniWrench's grappling hook for traversal. Azimuth's introduction adds layers to Lombax history, portraying them as advanced inventors who once dominated galactic technology but now face extinction, setting a tone of personal discovery amid galaxy-spanning warfare. The game culminates in a confrontation that separates the duo, with Clank abducted by the ethereal Zoni race, teasing deeper cosmic mysteries.
Serving as a narrative bridge, Ratchet & Clank Future: Quest for Booty (2008) is a compact digital title focusing on Clank's absence through Ratchet's solo pirate-themed odyssey on the planet Igliak (also known as Merdegraw). One year after the events of Tools of Destruction, Ratchet survives a spaceship crash and battles seafaring pirates led by Captain Darkwater, using enhanced wrench mechanics like close-quarters brawling and environmental puzzles to navigate treacherous islands and ships. His search for Clank leads to ancient Zoni technology, including a communication device that briefly connects him to his companion, while uncovering hints about the Zoni's role in Lombax lore as enigmatic guardians of advanced tech. The short adventure innovates with focused, non-linear exploration and puzzle-solving tied to pirate lore, emphasizing Ratchet's isolation and determination without multiplayer elements.
Ratchet & Clank Future: A Crack in Time (2009) reunites the protagonists in a time-bending epic, with Ratchet scouring the galaxy for Clank, who is imprisoned within the Great Clocka colossal device at the universe's center that regulates time itself. Clank, aided by the Zoni, solves intricate time-manipulation puzzles inside the clock, learning of his creator Orvus and the device's vulnerability to Dr. Nefarious, a robotic villain plotting universal domination. Meanwhile, Ratchet allies with Azimuth, whose revelations about KadenRatchet's father and a Lombax hero who sacrificed himself during Tachyon's genocidedeepen the emotional stakes and expose Azimuth's hidden agenda tied to reversing Lombax losses. Innovations include Clank's time-rewind abilities for platforming and Ratchet's chrono-pilot spaceship for open-galaxy exploration, alongside co-op time-slowing wrench powers that enhance combat fluidity. The story arc explores themes of regret and legacy, concluding the core Future trilogy by resolving the duo's separation while hinting at lingering dimensional threats.
As a post-saga epilogue, Ratchet & Clank: Into the Nexus (2013) provides emotional closure, set after A Crack in Time with Ratchet and Clank escorting the dimension-shifting siblings Vendra and Nevo Prog to a maximum-security prison. An attack unleashes the Progs, who flee through unstable rifts threatening the galaxy with a Nether-based force, forcing the duo to pursue them across five planets like Ardolis and Silox. The narrative reflects on Ratchet's growth post-Azimuth, emphasizing the unbreakable bond with Clank through heartfelt moments amid chaotic dimension-hopping. Innovations feature gravity-manipulating gadgets and rift-based puzzles, building on time mechanics with co-op zero-gravity combat and upgraded weapons like the Photon Blaster. Pollyx, a new antagonist, embodies the saga's lingering Lombax extinction theme by exploiting rifts for power, offering final introspection on the duo's adventures without introducing major new lore.
Overall, the Future saga deepens Lombax lore by chronicling their near-genocide at Tachyon's hands and the survivors' exile, portraying them as innovative yet flawed creators whose dimensional retreat stemmed from hubris in aiding the Cragmites. Time manipulation emerges as a core innovation, from the Great Clock's puzzles to rift traversal, enabling narrative flashbacks that reveal Kaden's fate and Azimuth's betrayal. The series evolves gameplay with co-op wrench time powersallowing synchronized slow-motion attacksand a shift toward emotional, character-driven arcs, solidifying Ratchet and Clank's partnership as the franchise's heart.

Rift Apart and remakes

The 2016 reimagining of Ratchet & Clank, developed by Insomniac Games and published by Sony Interactive Entertainment as a PlayStation 4 exclusive, reworks the original 2002 game's story into a fresh narrative that expands on the duo's origins while incorporating new levels, boss encounters, and gameplay segments such as jetpack flight sequences and expanded Clank-focused puzzles. This version bridges longtime fans with newcomers by blending faithful elements from the PS2 classiclike iconic weapons and planetary explorationwith modernized visuals and controls, serving as both a soft reboot and a tie-in to the 2015 animated film.
Ratchet & Clank: Rift Apart, released in 2021 for PlayStation 5, advances the series with a multiverse-spanning plot where Ratchet and Clank confront Dr. Nefarious from an alternate dimension, navigating dimensional rifts that allow seamless transitions between worlds without loading screens. The game introduces Rivet, a female Lombax resistance fighter, as a co-protagonist who wields a unique hammer weapon and explores themes of alternate realities and identity through her parallel backstory to Ratchet's. A free demo was offered prior to launch, showcasing the rift mechanics, while a PC port developed by Nixxes Software arrived in July 2023, supporting NVIDIA DLSS and ray tracing for enhanced performance.
The title leverages PS5 hardware innovations, including ray-traced lighting for realistic shadows and reflections, haptic feedback via the DualSense controller to simulate weapon recoil and environmental vibrations, and adaptive triggers for nuanced input during combat. These features underscore the game's emphasis on female representation through Rivet and emotional depth in Ratchet and Clank's bond amid multiversal threats. Building on the Future saga, Rift Apart includes references to prior events like Clank's Zoni connections, hinting at future stories involving the Lombax race. Commercially, it sold over 1.1 million units worldwide within its first few weeks, contributing to the series' enduring success.

Spin-offs

The Ratchet & Clank series expanded beyond its mainline platforming entries with several spin-off titles between 2007 and 2012, developed primarily by High Impact Games and Sloclap. These games experimented with new genres and formats, such as handheld adventures, stealth-action parodies, co-operative multiplayer, and tower defense hybrids, while featuring core characters like Ratchet, Clank, and Captain Qwark in supporting or altered roles. Released for the PlayStation Portable (PSP), PlayStation 3 (PS3), and PlayStation Vita, the spin-offs often emphasized multiplayer or unique mechanics over the series' traditional third-person shooting and exploration, leading to varied integration with the overarching narrative.
Ratchet & Clank: Size Matters, released in 2007 for PSP and later ported to PS2, marks the duo's debut on handheld hardware as a 3D platformer with mini-game elements. The story begins with Ratchet and Clank on a tropical vacation interrupted by their encounter with a young Chimponian girl named Luna, who is kidnapped by the robotic Technomites seeking to create infant Lombaxes for experimentation. The heroes pursue the culprits across new planets like Dreamtime and Metalis, utilizing shrinking technology to navigate the Technomite universe and even explore inside Clank's head for puzzle-solving segments. Weapons such as the Nursing Ray and armor customization return from prior entries, alongside arena challenges and vehicle sections, blending action with light RPG progression. The game ties into the original trilogy's timeline, occurring after Ratchet & Clank: Up Your Arsenal and introducing the Technomites as recurring foes in later titles.
Secret Agent Clank, launched in 2008 for PSP with a 2009 PS2 port, shifts focus to Clank as the protagonist in a stealth-spy parody inspired by James Bond tropes. Framed for stealing the Eye of Infinity gem during a museum heist, Ratchet is imprisoned, prompting Clank to adopt his "Secret Agent" personacomplete with tuxedo and gadgetsto investigate the true culprits, including the villainous Lady Von something and a network of spies. Gameplay emphasizes stealth mechanics like gadget-based infiltration, Clank-fu martial arts combos, and puzzle-solving across levels set in glamorous locales, with Ratchet and Qwark playable in prison brawl mini-games and co-op segments. The humorous narrative inverts the duo's dynamic, portraying Clank as suave and capable while Ratchet plays the bumbling sidekick, though it stands somewhat apart from core continuity due to its comedic exaggeration.
Ratchet & Clank: All 4 One, a 2010 PS3 exclusive, pivots to four-player co-operative gameplay, forcing Ratchet, Clank, Qwark, and arch-nemesis Dr. Nefarious into an uneasy alliance. Tricked by Nefarious into traveling to the planet Ignitus for a supposed weapon, the group is infected with a gelatonium virus that mutates local creatures into aggressive monsters, compelling them to team up against hordes while seeking a cure. Levels span diverse biomes like the Terawatt Forest, featuring upgradeable weapons such as the Cryo Blaster and co-op mechanics like rocket-jumping between characters or combined attacks. The story emphasizes ensemble dynamics and lighthearted banter, bridging the gap between A Crack in Time and subsequent entries by rehabilitating Nefarious temporarily.
Ratchet & Clank: Full Frontal Assault, released in 2012 for PS3 and Vita, hybridizes third-person action with real-time tower defense across three self-contained campaigns. Prompted by Qwark's boredom with retirement, the heroes defend planets from a resurgent threat by the Pest Control organizationrevealed as a front for alien invadersusing deployable turrets, barricades, and hero abilities to repel waves while exploring hub worlds for bolts and upgrades. Playable as Ratchet, Clank, or Qwark, the game incorporates classic weapons like the Heavy Lancer alongside strategic base-building, supporting split-screen co-op and competitive modes. Set after Ratchet & Clank: Deadlocked, it fills narrative gaps in the post-arena era by showcasing the team's return to planetary defense duties.
These spin-offs integrate into the broader lore by exploring side stories and character developments, such as the origins of threats like the Technomites or Nefarious' uneasy alliances, often bridging events between mainline installments like the original trilogy and Future saga. However, their departure from pure platformingfavoring stealth, co-op, or strategydrew mixed critical reception, with praise for innovative formats and humor but criticism for diluted core mechanics and shorter campaigns. For instance, Size Matters earned acclaim as a strong handheld entry (Metacritic 82/100 for PSP), while Full Frontal Assault faced backlash for its grindy defense elements (Metacritic 64/100). Overall, the titles expanded the franchise's experimental side without overshadowing the main series' focus on duo-led adventures.

Mobile titles

The Ratchet & Clank franchise expanded to mobile platforms in the mid-2000s to capitalize on emerging portable gaming trends, with subsequent efforts in the 2010s amid lulls in console releases. These titles adapted the series' core platforming and action elements to simpler hardware and touch interfaces, prioritizing accessibility over the depth of mainline games.
Ratchet & Clank: Going Mobile, developed by Handheld Games and published by Sony Pictures Mobile, launched on November 2, 2005, for Java (J2ME)-enabled cell phones. As a 2D side-scrolling platformer and shooter, it serves as a prequel to the 2002 original, depicting Ratchet and Clank's early adventures against robotic foes across 12 levels with basic jumping, shooting, and enemy encounters. The game's mechanics were simplified due to mobile hardware limitations, such as pixelated graphics and limited controls via phone keypads, yet it faithfully captured the series' humor and gadgetry in a compact format. Two versions existed: a basic edition for lower-end Series 40 devices and an enhanced one for Series 60 phones with improved visuals and content.
In 2013, during a transitional period following the Future saga, Ratchet & Clank: Before the Nexus was released on December 19 for iOS and Android by Darkside Game Studios. This free-to-play 3D endless runner is set shortly before the events of Ratchet & Clank: Into the Nexus, where players control Ratchet swiping to dodge obstacles, battle enemies, and collect bolts in procedurally generated levels leading to boss fights. Free-to-play elements include in-app purchases for extra bolts to upgrade weapons like the Buzz Blades and Constructo Pistol, blending quick-session gameplay with progression incentives.
Ratchet & Clank: Clone Home, intended as a direct sequel to Going Mobile, entered development in 2005 but was canceled in 2006 just before launch. Planned for J2ME mobile phones, the title was envisioned as a 2D RPG emphasizing exploration and combat with Clank clones, centered on a plot where Gleeman Vox creates digital replicas of Ratchet and Clank, forcing the duo to navigate cloned dimensions to thwart the scheme. A limited beta was briefly available in select regions, but the project was shelved due to shifting priorities. In October 2025, a complete, playable build surfaced through fan preservation efforts via archived files, allowing emulation and revealing its innovative clone mechanics.
These mobile entries, including promotional tie-ins like brief Qwark-themed mini-games in early Java demos, reflected Sony's strategy to broaden the franchise's audience during console development gaps post-2007. Overall reception highlighted their niche appeal for on-the-go play, with Going Mobile earning praise for its solid 2D adaptation (8/10 from Pocket Gamer) despite technical constraints. Before the Nexus garnered mixed feedback for engaging runs but clunky touch controls (4.3/5 average on app stores), while Clone Home's recovery sparked enthusiast interest without formal reviews. Commercial performance remained modest, with downloads paling against console hits due to fragmented mobile markets and limited marketing.
On November 12, 2025, Sony announced Ratchet & Clank: Ranger Rumble, a free-to-play multiplayer shooter for iOS and Android devices, developed as a battle royale-style game featuring Ratchet and other characters in team-based combat. It is slated for release in 2026.

Collections and ports

The Ratchet & Clank Collection, released for PlayStation 3 in 2012, bundles the original trilogyRatchet & Clank (2002), Ratchet & Clank: Going Commando (2003), and Ratchet & Clank: Up Your Arsenal (2004)as an HD remaster developed by Idol Minds, featuring upgraded visuals at 1080p resolution, stereoscopic 3D support, and online multiplayer for the third title. The collection also introduces full Trophy support for each game, enabling PlayStation Network achievements on the remastered versions. It launched in North America on August 28, 2012, providing newcomers access to the foundational stories while preserving the classic platforming and shooting mechanics.
In 2013, Sony offered a digital bundle combining Ratchet & Clank Future: Quest for Booty (2008) and Ratchet & Clank: Into the Nexus (2013), the latter serving as an epilogue to the Future saga; purchasing Into the Nexus for $29.99 included Quest for Booty at no extra cost, enhancing value for fans of the dimension-hopping narrative arc. This PS3-exclusive package, released on November 12, 2013, in North America, focused on shorter, puzzle-infused adventures without the full open-world scope of earlier entries.
Ratchet & Clank: Rift Apart expanded beyond PlayStation hardware with its PC port on July 26, 2023, available via Steam and the Epic Games Store, marking the first mainline release for non-PlayStation platforms and optimized for high-end systems with support for NVIDIA DLSS 3 upscaling and ray tracing. The port retains the PS5 version's interdimensional rift mechanics and haptic feedback equivalents through controller adaptations, broadening accessibility to PC gamers.
PlayStation Plus has facilitated further access, adding Secret Agent Clank (2008) to its Premium Classics catalog on September 17, 2024, with enhanced features like up-rendering and rewind functionality for the PSP-originated spy adventure. Rift Apart joined the Extra tier Game Catalog in May 2023, offering a full playthrough for subscribers, while Premium users can access time-limited trials for select titles in the series. Additionally, PlayStation 5 provides backward compatibility for all PS4 Ratchet & Clank titles, including the 2016 remake and Ratchet & Clank (2015), with boosted frame rates and resolutions on compatible models.
These re-releases and ports have revived interest in the series by enabling cross-generation and cross-platform play, allowing players to experience legacy content on modern hardware without fragmentation. As of November 2025, however, no comprehensive collection bundles the entire mainline series into a single package.

Gameplay

Core mechanics

The Ratchet & Clank series utilizes a third-person perspective to deliver an action-platformer experience that integrates precise platforming, third-person shooting, and open exploration within vibrant, sci-fi environments. Players primarily control Ratchet, who performs acrobatic jumps, rail swings, and ledge grabs to navigate multi-tiered levels, while simultaneously firing weapons at enemies in fluid combat sequences. Exploration is a key component, with players hunting for boltsthe game's currencyhidden in off-path areas, crates, and destructible objects to incentivize thorough level traversal and replayability. This foundational loop was established in the 2002 original, where Insomniac Games prototyped over 35 weapons and gadgets to ensure seamless integration of movement and shooting, discarding designs that disrupted pacing, such as the slower Revolverator.
Controls have evolved to prioritize intuitive analog movement and responsive actions, adapting to advancing hardware while maintaining core accessibility. The left analog stick handles Ratchet's omnidirectional movement, with the right stick controlling free-look camera for aiming and environmental scanning; melee attacks use the OmniWrench for wrench-throwing combos or ground pounds. Clank functions as a modular backpack, enabling specialized modes like thruster packs for high jumps, glides, and smash attacks to access elevated platforms, or the pilot's helmet for commandeering vehicles such as swingships in aerial dogfight sections. These mechanics received refinements in later entries, such as haptic feedback for weapon handling in Rift Apart via the DualSense controller, enhancing immersion without altering the dual-analog foundation.
Levels are structured as interconnected linear hubs, featuring semi-open planetary zones with collectible-laden side paths that encourage backtracking via newly acquired gadgets, balanced against a forward-driven narrative progression. Vehicle segments break up on-foot gameplay, including swingship flights for rail-grinding and bombing runs or hoverbike races for high-speed chases through obstacle courses, often leading into expansive boss arenas that demand pattern recognition and weapon switching. This design philosophy, honed through focus testing with over 200 players during the original's development, ensures levels like Metropolis evolve dynamically without scope creep, promoting a sense of discovery amid structured challenges.
Difficulty options span casual modes for beginners, which lower enemy aggression and boost health pickups, to hardcore variants that amplify damage taken and reduce ammo efficiency, catering to varied skill levels across the series. Post-game challenge modes unlock upon completion, ramping up enemy density and introducing time trials or survival waves for replay value, as seen in Rift Apart's Renegade Legend setting.
Multiplayer elements were added in sequels to expand the solo formula, beginning with split-screen co-op in Up Your Arsenal for shared campaign progression and arena battles. Ratchet: Deadlocked further innovated with online multiplayer supporting up to eight players in competitive modes, including team-based arenas and bot-assisted vehicular combat, marking Insomniac's second foray into networked play.

Weapons and gadgets

The Ratchet & Clank series is renowned for its diverse arsenal of weapons and gadgets, which blend high-tech firepower with inventive utility to enhance both combat and exploration. These tools, often featuring exaggerated sci-fi designs and humorous effects, allow players to tackle enemies in creative ways while solving environmental puzzles. Weapons typically include ranged firearms with various ammo types, while gadgets focus on mobility and support functions, all integrated seamlessly into the platforming gameplay.
Central to Ratchet's kit is the OmniWrench, an upgradeable melee tool that serves as both a combat weapon and a versatile gadget for interacting with the world. It enables close-range attacks, such as wrench throws and combos, and is essential for turning screws, loosening bolts for currency collection, and manipulating machinery in puzzles. The OmniWrench evolves across titles, with variants like the OmniWrench 8000 in early games providing basic functionality that grows more powerful through use.
Ranged weapons form the bulk of the arsenal, encompassing over a dozen categories like blasters, launchers, and beam emitters, each with distinct ammo types such as plasma, missiles, or energy bolts. Signature examples include the RYNO seriesshort for "Rip You a New One"a mega-missile launcher renowned for its devastating, auto-targeting barrages that clear groups of foes in seconds; variants like the RY3NO fire multiple explosive projectiles for area denial. Another standout is the Groovitron, a throwable disco ball that forces enemies to dance uncontrollably, disabling them temporarily and exposing vulnerabilities for follow-up attacks. Weapons often come in rarities from common to gold, with higher tiers offering enhanced damage, ammo efficiency, and visual flair, such as golden models with infinite ammo in certain modes. Humorous staples like the Sheepinator, which transmogrifies enemies into harmless sheep via a transmutation beam, exemplify the series' whimsical tone, turning battles into chaotic spectacles.
Gadgets complement weapons by emphasizing traversal and utility, often equipping Clank for backpack-based abilities. The Thruster Pack upgrades Clank's propulsion, enabling glide jumps and boosted flight to reach high platforms or cross gaps, acquired early in most titles for enhanced aerial mobility. Hoverboots provide high-speed dashing across surfaces, destroying obstacles on contact and facilitating rapid navigation in open areas. The RY3NO, while primarily a weapon, includes drone-summoning modes in some iterations for automated support fire. In Ratchet & Clank: Rift Apart, dimension anchors allow Clank to stabilize rifts, pulling in debris or enemies to manipulate dimensional tears during puzzles and combat. Across the series, dozens of such gadgets appear, promoting fluid movement akin to core platforming but tailored to specific tools.
Players acquire weapons and gadgets primarily through vendors scattered across planets, such as Gadgetron kiosks for standard arms, Megacorp emporiums in later games, or Ms. Zurkon's arsenal in Rift Apart, where purchases are made with collected bolts. Specialized shops like Big Al's Roboshack offer gadgets and upgrades, while rare items like RYNO variants require holocards or skill points earned from challenges. Additional sources include mini-games, boss rewards, and free pickups in levels; upgrades occur at R&D terminals using bolts, improving stats without altering core mechanics. This system ensures a steady progression of tools, with over 50 unique weapons documented across the franchise's history.

Progression systems

The Ratchet & Clank series employs a variety of progression systems that encourage exploration, combat efficiency, and replay through resource collection and upgrades. Central to player advancement is the bolt economy, where bolts serve as the primary currency obtained by destroying environmental crates, defeating enemies, and completing missions. These bolts are spent at vendors to acquire new weapons, replenish ammunition, and purchase nanotech upgrades that increase Ratchet's maximum health. In challenge modes available after completing the main story, an infinite bolt supply allows for unrestricted farming and testing of fully upgraded loadouts.
Skill points represent another key advancement mechanic, first introduced in the 2002 original game as rewards for fulfilling optional level challenges, such as eliminating all enemies or navigating without damage. In early titles, collecting enough skill points unlocks extras like concept art and cheats in the game's menu. The system evolves in the Future saga, where skill points are invested in Captain Qwark's upgrade tree to purchase perks enhancing core stats, including additional health segments, expanded ammo capacity for weapons, and bolt multipliers for faster economy growth. Later remakes, such as the 2016 reimagining, replace skill points with gold bolts, which are hidden collectibles used solely for cosmetic and gameplay cheats without direct stat perks.
Weapon progression introduces RPG-like depth starting with Going Commando (2003), where frequent use in combat fills an experience meter, enabling levels that boost attributes like damage output, firing rate, and capacity. At higher levels, weapons unlock alternate fire modes or visual evolutions, and players can apply modspurchased with boltsfor specialized effects such as homing projectiles or reduced recoil. This system persists and expands in subsequent entries, with Up Your Arsenal (2004) adding a wider array of mods and arena-based weapon testing for balanced progression. In more recent titles like Rift Apart (2021), weapons feature rarity tiers (common to legendary) that influence base stats, with leveling tied to use and further enhancements via raritanium-infused RYNO upgrades for global improvements across the arsenal.
Replayability is enhanced by modes like New Game+ (often branded as Challenge Mode), unlocked post-story, which retains all weapons, levels, upgrades, and bolts while escalating enemy difficulty and introducing infinite ammo or score multipliers for bolt farming. Time trials challenge players to beat level records using optimized loadouts, while mod unlocks and hidden collectibles like titanium or gold bolts provide long-term goals. From the PS3 era onward, integrated trophies and achievements reward full progression, such as maxing all weapons or earning every skill point, fostering multiple playthroughs.
Over the series' evolution, progression shifts from the original's straightforward collection of bolts and gold bolts for basic unlocks to more intricate systems in mid-era games like Up Your Arsenal, incorporating weapon mods and ship/armor upgrades for RPG depth. The Future trilogy peaks this with skill point-driven perk trees and customizable weapons via the Gadgetron vendor's create-a-weapon lab. Remakes and Rift Apart streamline these for accessibility, emphasizing use-based leveling and rarities over extensive modding, while preserving the bolt economy and challenge modes as core replay hooks.

Reception

Critical response

The Ratchet & Clank series has received generally positive critical reception across its mainline entries, with Metacritic scores typically ranging from the mid-80s to low-90s, reflecting acclaim for its engaging gameplay and production values. The original 2002 title earned an 88, praised as an innovative 3D action-platformer that revitalized the genre with its blend of precise platforming, shooting mechanics, and humorous storytelling. Subsequent PS2 entries, Ratchet & Clank: Going Commando (90) and Up Your Arsenal (91), built on this foundation, earning universal acclaim for refined combat systems and inventive level designs that combined exploration with chaotic weapon-based battles. In contrast, spin-off titles like All 4 One scored lower at 70, with critics noting its co-op focus diluted the solo adventure appeal central to the series.
Critics consistently highlight the series' strengths in fluid, satisfying combat and a vast array of creative weapons, from the RYNO rocket launcher to the Pixelizer, which turn enemies into blocky hazards. Voice acting, featuring talents like David Kaye as Clank and James Arnold Taylor as Ratchet, adds charm and wit, enhancing the buddy dynamic and satirical humor that pokes fun at sci-fi tropes. Visuals have evolved impressively, with the PS3 Future sagaTools of Destruction (89) and A Crack in Time (87)lauded for deeper narrative elements, such as Clank's time-manipulating segments that added emotional weight and puzzle variety to the action. The 2016 remake, scoring 85, was commended for modernizing accessibility with smoother controls and vibrant graphics tailored for broader audiences, serving as an effective entry point while tying into the animated film. Ratchet & Clank: Rift Apart (91) stands out as a PS5 showcase, earning praise for seamless dimension-hopping mechanics that leverage the console's SSD for instant loading and haptic feedback in combat.
Despite these praises, common criticisms include repetitive mission structures, where planet-hopping often follows a collect-and-shoot formula that can feel formulaic in later entries. Many titles are faulted for easy difficulty, even on higher settings, lacking robust challenge for veteran players and reducing tension in boss fights. Plot inconsistencies emerge in post-PS2 games, with some reviewers noting underdeveloped character arcs and lore retcons that undermine the series' early cohesion.
The series' critical evolution mirrors its innovation: early PS2 games were hailed as saviors of the platformer genre amid a market shift toward realism, offering joyful escapism post-3D pioneers like Super Mario 64. The Future saga deepened storytelling with themes of identity and loss, earning higher regard for narrative ambition. The 2016 remake emphasized accessibility through intuitive design and family-friendly tweaks, broadening appeal without alienating fans.
Ratchet & Clank entries have garnered multiple Game of the Year nominations, including Rift Apart's nods at The Game Awards 2021 and D.I.C.E. Awards 2022 for overall excellence, art direction, and animation. The franchise also received a BAFTA Games Award for Best Animation for Rift Apart in 2022, recognizing its fluid character movements and dimensional effects.

Commercial performance

The Ratchet & Clank series has achieved significant commercial success, with cumulative sales exceeding 26 million units worldwide by 2021, driven primarily by its status as a PlayStation exclusive that fostered strong platform loyalty among Sony's user base. By 2023, the franchise's total sales had surpassed 40 million units, reflecting sustained demand through remakes, new entries, and collections. The original 2002 title sold over 4 million copies, establishing the series as a key pillar of PlayStation 2's lineup.
Key milestones include Ratchet & Clank: Up Your Arsenal, which sold 3.21 million units on PlayStation 2, and Ratchet & Clank Future: A Crack in Time, which reached 1.83 million units on PlayStation 3. In contrast, spin-off titles such as Ratchet & Clank: Size Matters and Ratchet & Clank Future: Quest for Booty each sold under 1 million units, highlighting the stronger performance of mainline releases. The 2021 entry, Ratchet & Clank: Rift Apart, sold nearly 4 million units by mid-2023, making it one of the series' top performers and generating approximately $145 million in revenue.
The franchise's PlayStation exclusivity contributed to its market dominance, though the 2023 PC port of Rift Apart expanded its reach to over 800,000 expected units, adding roughly $20 million in projected profit despite initial underperformance compared to console sales. Inclusion in PlayStation Plus bundles has boosted player engagement and streaming metrics but negatively impacted traditional unit sales for first-party titles like those in the series. A development hiatus following the 2013 spin-off All 4 One slowed momentum, but the 2016 remake revitalized interest, selling over 7 million units and setting a franchise record for launch speed.
Revenue streams extend beyond game sales to include merchandise tie-ins, such as action figures and apparel, though the primary economic driver remains the core titles. Sony's 2019 acquisition of Insomniac Games for $229 million further secured the series' future by integrating it fully into the company's first-party portfolio. In November 2025, Sony announced Ratchet & Clank: Ranger Rumble, a free-to-play multiplayer arena shooter for iOS and Android devices, developed by Oh Bibi.

Legacy and influence

The Ratchet & Clank franchise pioneered the action-platformer hybrid genre by integrating fluid platforming with expansive third-person shooting mechanics, redefining expectations for 3D platformers in the early 2000s. This innovative blend influenced subsequent titles, including later entries in Naughty Dog's Jak and Daxter series, which shifted toward darker tones and increased combat focus following the 2002 debut of Ratchet & Clank. The series' emphasis on diverse, upgradeable weapons also contributed to the evolution of looter-shooter designs seen in games like Borderlands, where procedural weapon variety and humorous gadgetry echo Ratchet's arsenal experimentation. More recent platformers, such as Astro Bot, incorporate homages to the duo through cameos and level designs that celebrate PlayStation's platforming heritage.
Culturally, Ratchet and Clank have become enduring icons, frequently appearing in memes that highlight Clank's deadpan wit and the duo's banter, fostering a lighthearted online presence among fans. The characters inspire widespread cosplay at conventions, with detailed recreations of Ratchet's Lombax design and Clank's robotic form showcasing the franchise's visual appeal. The 20th anniversary in 2022 featured official celebrations, including a free armor pack for Ratchet & Clank: Rift Apart and the addition of five classic titles to PlayStation Plus Premium, underscoring the series' lasting popularity.
Within the industry, Insomniac Games adopted an "adapt or die" philosophy to sustain the franchise across console generations, evolving gameplay while preserving core elements like exploration and humor to avoid stagnation. This approach culminated in Ratchet & Clank: Rift Apart (2021), which served as a technical showcase for the PlayStation 5's SSD, enabling seamless dimension-hopping without loading screens and demonstrating next-gen capabilities.
The fanbase remains highly active, with communities creating mods for the 2023 PC port of Rift Apart, including unofficial tools for custom content and visual enhancements. Enthusiasts continue to call for new sequels, fueled by rumors from the 2023 Insomniac data breach suggesting a follow-up targeting 2029. While underrepresented in esports, the series thrives in speedrunning circles, with events like Awesome Games Done Quick featuring marathon runs that highlight precise mechanics and glitches.

Other media

Comics and manga

The Ratchet & Clank franchise has seen limited adaptations in printed comics and manga formats, primarily consisting of a Japanese manga series and an American miniseries published by DC Comics' WildStorm imprint. These works expand on the video games' narratives with added comedic elements and side adventures, targeting fans of the original platformers.
A Japanese manga adaptation titled Ratchet & Clank: Gagaga! Ginga no Gakeppuchi Densetsu (translated as Ratchet & Clank: Bang Bang Bang! Critical Danger of the Galaxy Legends from the Edge of the Galaxy) was serialized in Shogakukan's CoroCoro Comic magazine starting in February 2004. Spanning two volumes released between 2005 and 2008, the manga retells elements of the original game's storyline with exaggerated humor and caricatural depictions of the characters, emphasizing slapstick comedy and galactic escapades involving Ratchet and Clank. Published exclusively in Japan, it captures the series' whimsical tone while adapting the duo's battles against Drek and other foes for a younger audience through vibrant, dynamic artwork.
In the United States, the primary comic series was a six-issue miniseries published by WildStorm from September 2010 to February 2011, bridging the events of Ratchet & Clank Future: A Crack in Time and Ratchet & Clank: All 4 One. Written by T.J. Fixman, who contributed to the games' scripts, and illustrated by Adam Archer with covers by CreatureBox, the story follows Ratchet and Clank during a brief vacation disrupted by the villainous Artemis Zogg and his planet-stealing plot, incorporating familiar allies like Captain Qwark and Talwyn Apogee. The narrative introduces new threats while maintaining the franchise's blend of action, gadgetry, and humor, though it deviates slightly from game canon in character motivations and timelines.
Additional tie-in comics appeared as promotional inserts in gaming magazines and art books, often featuring short side stories focused on character exploits such as Captain Qwark's bumbling superhero antics or Clank's inventive mishaps. These one-off pieces, distributed alongside game releases like Ratchet & Clank: Going Commando, served to build hype and provide lighthearted vignettes without advancing the main canon. Examples include ads with embedded comic strips in titles like Batman comics and brief tales in promotional bundles.
Reception for these adaptations has been niche, appealing mainly to dedicated fans of the video games. The WildStorm series received mixed reviews, with praise for its faithful humor, vibrant character designs, and Archer's detailed artwork that evokes the games' style, but criticism for a slow-building plot in early issues and occasional deviations from established lore. IGN awarded the first issue a 7/10, noting the engaging characters and potential despite pacing issues. Comic Revolution lauded it as a solid entry for newcomers and veterans alike, highlighting the action sequences. The manga, while less reviewed internationally due to its Japan-only release, was appreciated in domestic circles for its comedic exaggeration but noted for simplifying the games' plots.
Both the manga and WildStorm comics are out of print in physical form, with copies commanding high prices on secondary markets due to rarity; digital versions of the American series remain available through platforms like Amazon Kindle and Comixology. No new comic or manga adaptations have been produced since 2011.

Film and animations

The Ratchet & Clank franchise expanded into feature-length animation with the 2016 CGI film Ratchet & Clank, a reimagining of the original 2002 video game's origin story. Directed by Kevin Munroe and produced by Rainmaker Entertainment in collaboration with Insomniac Games and Sony Pictures, the movie follows Ratchet, a young Lombax mechanic, and Clank, a diminutive robot, as they team up with the Galactic Rangers to thwart Chairman Drek's plan to destroy planets using the Deplanetizer superweapon. The film deviates from the source material by incorporating elements from later games, such as expanded roles for the Rangers and new supporting characters, while emphasizing themes of heroism and unlikely alliances. With a production budget of $20 million, it was released theatrically on April 29, 2016, by Gramercy Pictures (a Focus Features imprint) in North America and internationally through various distributors.
The voice cast featured returning actors from the video games, including James Arnold Taylor as Ratchet and David Kaye as Clank, alongside high-profile additions like Paul Giamatti as Chairman Drek, John Goodman as Grimroth, and Sylvester Stallone as the villainous Victor Von Ion, a disguised operative tied to the plot. Jim Ward reprised his role as Captain Qwark, the boastful leader of the Galactic Rangers. Critics gave the film mixed-to-negative reviews, praising the voice performances and action sequences but criticizing the predictable plot, uneven pacing, and generic animation style that failed to capture the games' inventive humor and world-building. It holds a 22% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 82 reviews, with consensus noting it as a "cheerfully noisy" but ultimately forgettable adaptation. The film's box office performance was underwhelming, grossing $13.4 million worldwideless than the budgetcontributing to financial losses for Rainmaker, estimated at around $10 million.
In addition to the feature film, the franchise has produced a handful of animated shorts that extend its narrative outside the main games. The 2021 short Ratchet & Clank: Life of Pie, produced by Mainframe Studios (Rainmaker's successor), depicts Ratchet and Clank on a pizza delivery mission that uncovers Dr. Nefarious's interdimensional portal scheme, serving as a lighthearted tie-in to broader lore elements like Nefarious's recurring villainy. Running about 23 minutes, it premiered exclusively on Crave in Canada before wider digital release, showcasing updated character designs and gadget-based humor. Earlier promotional content tied to the 2016 film included brief animated vignettes, but no full prequel short focused solely on the Deplanetizer was released. Other holiday-themed animations, such as episodic specials inspired by games like Size Matters, have appeared in limited promotional formats, though none achieved standalone theatrical or streaming distribution. As of 2025, no sequels to the 2016 film have been produced or announced, despite ongoing interest from Insomniac Games in potential PlayStation Productions adaptations. In January 2025, Insomniac Games expressed interest in further PlayStation Productions adaptations, specifically mentioning Ratchet & Clank.
The film's distribution strategy emphasized cross-promotion with the concurrent PlayStation 4 remake of the original game, bundling movie tickets with game demos and releasing both in April 2016 to capitalize on franchise nostalgia. It was made available on video-on-demand platforms shortly after theaters, including iTunes and Amazon Prime Video, and later on Blu-ray/DVD. This synergy aimed to introduce the series to new audiences but highlighted the challenges of synchronizing game and film releases. In terms of impact, the movie slightly expanded the Ratchet & Clank lore by formalizing early backstories for characters like Qwark and the Rangers, while introducing sleeker, more cinematic designs for weapons and environments that influenced promotional art but were not integrated into the core game canon. It remains a notable, if divisive, entry that underscored the difficulties of adapting interactive adventures to linear storytelling.

Merchandise and crossovers

The Ratchet & Clank franchise has inspired a range of consumer merchandise, including toys, apparel, and books, produced by various licensees since the series' debut in 2002. Action figures featuring Ratchet and Clank, along with supporting characters like Captain Qwark, were first released by Playmates Toys in 2003 to coincide with the original game's launch, offering articulated models with accessories such as weapons and vehicles. Later lines, including those tied to Ratchet & Clank Future: Tools of Destruction, continued production through the mid-2000s, emphasizing play features like transforming elements for Clank. In 2020, Funko released a PlayStation-exclusive two-pack of vinyl figures depicting Ratchet and Clank, measuring approximately 3.75 inches tall and capturing their iconic designs from the series.
Apparel items, such as T-shirts featuring character artwork and game logos, have been widely available through official PlayStation merchandise channels and third-party retailers, often highlighting key moments like Ratchet's wrench or Clank's backpack form. Art books provide deeper insights into the series' development, with Dark Horse Comics publishing The Art of Ratchet & Clank in 2018, a hardcover collection of concept art, character designs, and commentary from Insomniac Games spanning the franchise's history up to that point. Strategy guides, essential for players navigating the games' complex levels and bolt collection, were produced by publishers like Prima Games and BradyGames; for instance, Prima's Official Strategy Guide for the 2002 original game detailed weapon upgrades, enemy encounters, and hidden items.
The franchise has appeared in several video game crossovers, expanding its reach beyond standalone titles. In PlayStation All-Stars Battle Royale (2012), developed by SuperBot Entertainment, Ratchet and Clank serve as a single playable fighter duo, utilizing weapons like the RYNO and Clank's hover mode in arena battles against other Sony characters such as Kratos and Sackboy. A cameo appearance occurs in Astro's Playroom (2020), a PS5 tech demo by Team Asobi, where a Ratchet-inspired Lombax bot and Clank-like robot feature as collectible V-Shaped Successors in levels referencing PlayStation history.
Events like Comic-Con panels have promoted the series and its merchandise, with Insomniac Games hosting sessions at San Diego Comic-Con in 2009 to reveal weapons for Ratchet & Clank Future: A Crack in Time and in 2016 at WonderCon to showcase clips from the animated film. For the 20th anniversary in 2022, Sony released a free Ratchet & Clank: Rift Apart update featuring an Armor Pack with five cosmetic sets inspired by past games, such as the Nova armor from the 2003 sequel, available to all owners without additional cost. In November 2025, Youtooz announced new Ratchet & Clank merchandise figures for release on November 17.
Merchandise sales for Ratchet & Clank have remained steady but secondary to the core video games, with a notable boost in 2016 from the animated film's release, which spurred new toy lines, apparel, and collectibles tied to the reimagined story. The film's promotion, including theater tie-ins, contributed to expanded availability of figures and books during that period, though specific sales figures for non-game products are not publicly detailed.
