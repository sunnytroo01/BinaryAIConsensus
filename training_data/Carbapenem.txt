 Carbapenems are a class of broad-spectrum -lactam antibiotics distinguished by their bicyclic nucleus, consisting of a -lactam ring fused to a five-membered carbacycle with a carbon atom replacing the sulfur found in penicillins, along with a double bond between C-2 and C-3 for enhanced stability against -lactamases. They are highly potent bactericidal agents effective against a wide range of Gram-positive, Gram-negative, and anaerobic bacteria, including many multidrug-resistant strains such as extended-spectrum -lactamase (ESBL)-producing Enterobacteriaceae and Pseudomonas aeruginosa (though ertapenem has limited activity against the latter). Primarily administered intravenously due to their poor oral bioavailability, carbapenems are reserved for treating severe, life-threatening infections like complicated intra-abdominal infections, hospital-acquired pneumonia, complicated urinary tract infections, sepsis, and skin and soft tissue infections.
The origins of carbapenems trace back to the 1970s, when natural products like the olivanic acids were identified as -lactamase-stable compounds, followed by the isolation of thienamycin from Streptomyces cattleya in 1976, which became the foundational structure for synthetic derivatives. Imipenem, the first clinically approved carbapenem in 1985, is combined with cilastatin to prevent its rapid degradation by renal dehydropeptidase-I, ensuring effective dosing. Later developments expanded the class, including meropenem (approved 1996) and ertapenem (2001), each featuring modifications like a 1-methyl group for improved metabolic stability and reduced seizure risk compared to imipenem. These agents have become cornerstones of antimicrobial therapy, particularly in hospital settings, but their overuse has contributed to the global rise of carbapenem-resistant organisms. As of 2024, antimicrobial resistance is projected to cause 39 million attributable deaths globally from 2025 to 2050, with carbapenem resistance contributing significantly; in October 2025, the WHO warned of its widespread increase.
Carbapenems act by diffusing through bacterial outer membrane porins, where they irreversibly acylate multiple penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs), disrupting transpeptidation and inhibiting peptidoglycan cross-linking essential for cell wall integrity, ultimately causing osmotic lysis. Their broad activity stems from high affinity for PBPs across diverse pathogens and resistance to hydrolysis by most serine-based -lactamases, though they remain susceptible to metallo--lactamases. Common formulations include imipenem-cilastatin, meropenem (often with vaborbactam for enhanced activity against serine carbapenemases), and ertapenem for once-daily dosing in outpatient settings.
Despite their efficacy, carbapenem resistance poses a major public health threat, primarily through production of carbapenemase enzymes (e.g., KPC, NDM-1, OXA-48), reduced outer membrane porin expression, efflux pump overexpression, and alterations in PBPs, particularly in Enterobacterales and non-fermenters like Acinetobacter baumannii. Adverse effects are generally mild but include gastrointestinal disturbances such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, hypersensitivity reactions (with low cross-reactivity to penicillins), and rare neurotoxicity like seizures, especially with imipenem at high doses or in patients with renal impairment. They may also disrupt normal intestinal flora, leading to Clostridium difficile-associated diarrhea or overgrowth of resistant pathogens. Ongoing research focuses on novel carbapenem--lactamase inhibitor combinations and stewardship programs to preserve their utility.

Overview and Classification

Definition and History

Carbapenems are a class of intravenous beta-lactam antibiotics characterized by a core structure featuring a beta-lactam ring fused to a five-membered pyrroline ring, originally derived from the natural product thienamycin. They exhibit broad-spectrum activity against a wide range of Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, including many multidrug-resistant strains, due to their stability against most beta-lactamases.
The history of carbapenems began with the discovery of thienamycin in 1976 by researchers at Merck & Co., isolated from the soil bacterium Streptomyces cattleya. Thienamycin's instability prompted the development of more stable analogs, leading to imipenem, the first clinically viable carbapenem, which was approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 1985 for treating severe infections. Subsequent advancements yielded meropenem, approved by the FDA in 1996; ertapenem in 2001; and doripenem in 2007, each offering improved pharmacokinetics or spectra tailored to specific clinical needs. An oral prodrug, tebipenem pivoxil, was approved in Japan in 2009 for pediatric use in respiratory and ear, nose, and throat infections.
As a subclass of beta-lactam antibiotics, carbapenems are grouped alongside penicillins, cephalosporins, and monobactams, but they are distinguished by their exceptional resistance to hydrolysis by many beta-lactamases, enabling treatment of infections caused by extended-spectrum beta-lactamase-producing organisms. Since the 1990s, carbapenems have played a critical role in managing multidrug-resistant bacterial infections, particularly in hospital settings. Various carbapenems, such as imipenem-cilastatin and meropenem, have been included on the World Health Organization's Model List of Essential Medicines since the early 2000s, underscoring their importance in global antimicrobial therapy.

Chemical Structure

Carbapenems possess a bicyclic core structure consisting of a four-membered -lactam ring fused to a five-membered pyrroline ring, forming a 1-azabicyclohept-2-ene system with a 7-oxo group in the -lactam. This core includes a carboxylate group at position 2 and a double bond between C-2 and C-3, distinguishing it from other -lactams by replacing the sulfur atom found in penicillins with a carbon at the fusion point. A key substituent is the (1R)-hydroxyethyl group at C-6, typically in the configuration, which is conserved across major carbapenems like imipenem and contributes to their potency.
Unlike penicillins, which feature a thiazolidine ring with sulfur at C-1 and extensive substitution at C-6, carbapenems have a sulfur-free pyrroline ring and minimal or no substitution at C-2 beyond the carboxylate, enhancing their rigidity and resistance profile. Compared to cephalosporins, the more constrained bicyclic framework of carbapenems provides superior binding affinity to penicillin-binding proteins due to reduced conformational flexibility. Side-chain variations further differentiate agents; for instance, imipenem includes a thio group at C-3, while meropenem features a 1-methyl group at C-1 and a (1S)-2-(dimethylcarbamoyl)pyrrolidin-3-ylthio substituent at C-3.
The 1-methyl substituent in meropenem improves stability against renal dehydropeptidase-I, reducing the risk of nephrotoxicity compared to imipenem, which requires co-administration with cilastatin to mitigate this issue. Overall, the fused ring system and strategic substituents confer resistance to hydrolysis by many -lactamases, including extended-spectrum -lactamases (ESBLs), as the rigid structure and hydroxyethyl group hinder enzymatic deacylation. This stability arises from the strained -lactam ring and the trans configuration at the C-5C-6 bond, which limits access to the acyl-enzyme intermediate.

Pharmacology

Mechanism of Action

Carbapenems are a class of -lactam antibiotics that primarily inhibit bacterial cell wall synthesis by binding covalently to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs), which are essential transpeptidase enzymes involved in the final stages of peptidoglycan cross-linking. This binding acylates the active site serine residue of PBPs, preventing the formation of peptide cross-links between peptidoglycan strands and disrupting the structural integrity of the bacterial cell wall. As a result, the cell becomes susceptible to osmotic lysis, particularly in Gram-negative bacteria where autolysins continue to degrade the weakened wall.
Carbapenems exhibit high binding affinity for a broad range of PBPs, including PBP2 in Gram-negative bacteria, which is crucial for cell elongation and septation, and high-molecular-weight PBPs such as PBP1a and PBP1b in Gram-positive bacteria, which are involved in cell wall elongation and division. This multi-PBP affinity allows for synergistic inhibition of cell wall assembly, contributing to their potent bactericidal activity against actively dividing bacteria through a time-dependent killing mechanism, where efficacy depends more on the duration of exposure above the minimum inhibitory concentration than on peak concentrations.
A key feature enhancing their effectiveness is the inherent stability of carbapenems against many serine-based -lactamases produced by bacteria, owing to structural elements like the -hydroxyethyl side chain at the C-2 position, which sterically hinders enzymatic hydrolysis and enables the drugs to act as slow substrates or transient inhibitors. This resistance facilitates penetration through the outer membrane porins into the periplasmic space, where PBPs are located, without prior degradation.
While highly bactericidal against growing bacterial populations, carbapenems demonstrate only a minimal post-antibiotic effectthe persistent suppression of bacterial regrowth after drug removaltypically lasting less than 1-2 hours, in contrast to the more prolonged effects observed with aminoglycosides. This characteristic underscores the importance of maintaining adequate drug levels over time for optimal therapeutic outcomes.

Spectrum of Activity

Carbapenems exhibit a broad spectrum of antibacterial activity, primarily due to their stability against most -lactamases and high affinity for penicillin-binding proteins across diverse bacterial species. This makes them particularly valuable for treating polymicrobial infections involving both aerobic and anaerobic pathogens.
Against Gram-negative bacteria, carbapenems demonstrate excellent coverage, including Enterobacteriaceae such as Escherichia coli and Klebsiella pneumoniae. They are also highly effective against non-fermenters like Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Acinetobacter baumannii, with imipenem, meropenem, and doripenem showing potent activity (low minimum inhibitory concentrations) against these organisms. Ertapenem, however, provides weaker coverage against P. aeruginosa. This intrinsic activity is enhanced by their resistance to hydrolysis by many -lactamases produced by Gram-negative pathogens.
For Gram-positive bacteria, carbapenems offer good activity against most streptococci, including Streptococcus pneumoniae, but limited activity against enterococci, with moderate susceptibility in penicillin-susceptible Enterococcus faecalis and intrinsic resistance in Enterococcus faecium (including non-vancomycin-resistant strains). They are effective against methicillin-susceptible Staphylococcus aureus, but coverage is limited against methicillin-resistant S. aureus (MRSA) due to poor binding to altered penicillin-binding protein 2a (PBP2a).
Carbapenems also provide broad coverage against anaerobic bacteria, including Bacteroides fragilis, making them suitable for intra-abdominal and polymicrobial infections. Regarding other organisms, they show activity against certain atypical pathogens like Legionella pneumophila, as demonstrated by successful clinical outcomes with imipenem. However, they lack reliable activity against Mycobacteria without combination therapy, and they have no effect on fungi or viruses.

Clinical Use

Indications

Carbapenems are primarily indicated for the treatment of severe, complicated bacterial infections caused by susceptible multidrug-resistant or polymicrobial pathogens, particularly in hospitalized patients. Their broad-spectrum activity makes them suitable for conditions such as complicated intra-abdominal infections, including peritonitis and appendicitis due to pathogens like Escherichia coli and Bacteroides fragilis. They are also approved for complicated urinary tract infections (cUTIs), encompassing pyelonephritis, often involving extended-spectrum beta-lactamase (ESBL)-producing Enterobacterales. Additionally, carbapenems treat hospital-acquired pneumonia (HAP) and ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP), as well as sepsis and bloodstream infections, where rapid coverage of Gram-negative and anaerobic bacteria is essential.
In empirical therapy, carbapenems serve as first-line agents for suspected multidrug-resistant Gram-negative infections in critically ill patients, such as those with recent hospitalization, prior antibiotic exposure, or high risk for ESBL-producing organisms. The Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA) 2024 guidance recommends carbapenems (e.g., meropenem or imipenem-cilastatin) as preferred therapy for non-urinary ESBL-Enterobacterales infections and high-risk cases of carbapenem-resistant Enterobacterales (CRE), including critically ill patients with sepsis or pneumonia. For CRE urinary tract infections, combinations like meropenem-vaborbactam are specifically endorsed if susceptibility is confirmed.
Specific uses include complicated skin and soft tissue infections, such as diabetic foot infections without osteomyelitis, and febrile neutropenia in cancer patients where broad coverage is needed pending culture results. However, carbapenems are not first-line for community-acquired pneumonia or uncomplicated urinary tract infections, where narrower agents like cephalosporins or fluoroquinolones suffice.
Recent expansions include the 2017 FDA approval of meropenem-vaborbactam for CRE-associated cUTIs, providing targeted therapy against beta-lactamase-producing strains. Carbapenems, particularly ertapenem, also play a role in post-surgical prophylaxis, such as preventing infections following elective colorectal surgery.

Administration and Dosing

Carbapenems are primarily administered via intravenous (IV) infusion due to their low oral bioavailability, which prevents effective absorption through the gastrointestinal tract. Ertapenem is unique among approved agents in also being available for intramuscular (IM) administration, offering flexibility in outpatient settings. An oral prodrug form, tebipenem pivoxil, is approved in Japan for pediatric use in treating urinary tract infections and other susceptible infections, marking the only oral carbapenem available clinically; as of November 2025, an adult oral formulation (tebipenem HBr) is in late-stage development in the US with planned FDA submission following positive phase 3 results.
Pharmacokinetically, carbapenems exhibit rapid distribution to various tissues following IV administration, with extensive penetration into body fluids and organs. Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) penetration varies by agent and patient factors but typically ranges from 15% to 40% in the presence of meningeal inflammation, supporting their use in central nervous system infections. Plasma half-lives differ across agents: imipenem and meropenem have short half-lives of approximately 1 hour, necessitating frequent dosing, while ertapenem's longer half-life of about 4 hours allows for once-daily administration. Primarily eliminated via renal excretion, carbapenem dosing requires adjustment in patients with impaired kidney function to prevent accumulation and toxicity.
Dosing guidelines are tailored to infection severity, patient renal function, and pathogen susceptibility, with representative regimens including imipenem 500 mg IV every 6 hours for moderate infections and meropenem 1 to 2 g IV every 8 hours for severe cases such as pneumonia or intra-abdominal infections. Ertapenem is typically dosed at 1 g IV or IM once daily for community-acquired infections. To optimize pharmacodynamic efficacyparticularly the time above the minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC)prolonged infusions over 3 hours are recommended for agents like meropenem in critically ill patients with resistant pathogens.
Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM) is advised for carbapenems in critically ill patients due to heightened pharmacokinetic variability from factors like augmented renal clearance or organ dysfunction, enabling dose adjustments to achieve target exposures. A notable drug interaction occurs with valproic acid, where co-administration with carbapenems can reduce valproate serum levels by up to 77%, potentially leading to subtherapeutic effects and seizures, thus requiring alternative anticonvulsants or close monitoring.

Specific Agents

Approved Carbapenems

Imipenem, the first carbapenem approved for clinical use, received U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval in 1985 in combination with cilastatin as imipenem/cilastatin (Primaxin). Cilastatin serves as a dehydropeptidase inhibitor to prevent the renal metabolism of imipenem, thereby reducing its nephrotoxicity while preserving its broad-spectrum activity against aerobic and anaerobic Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria. This combination exhibits potent activity against many multidrug-resistant pathogens but requires dosing every 6-8 hours due to its short half-life.
Meropenem (Merrem) was approved by the FDA in 1996, offering improved stability against human renal dehydropeptidase compared to imipenem, which eliminates the need for a companion inhibitor. It demonstrates a lower risk of seizures and is particularly effective for central nervous system infections, including bacterial meningitis, due to its ability to penetrate the blood-brain barrier. Meropenem provides broad-spectrum coverage similar to imipenem but with enhanced safety for prolonged use in serious infections like sepsis and intra-abdominal infections.
Ertapenem (Invanz) gained FDA approval in 2001 and is distinguished by its prolonged half-life, enabling once-daily administration, which facilitates outpatient therapy. It offers reliable activity against many community-acquired pathogens, including extended-spectrum beta-lactamase-producing Enterobacterales, but shows reduced potency against Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Acinetobacter species, limiting its use in hospital-acquired infections involving these organisms. This profile makes ertapenem suitable for skin and soft tissue infections, complicated urinary tract infections, and community-acquired pneumonia.
Doripenem (Doribax) was approved by the FDA in 2007 as an injectable agent with spectrum and potency comparable to meropenem, including enhanced activity against Pseudomonas aeruginosa. It was initially indicated for complicated intra-abdominal and urinary tract infections, with a focus on its favorable pharmacokinetic profile for critically ill patients. However, following safety concerns from clinical trials showing higher mortality in ventilator-associated pneumonia, doripenem was discontinued in the U.S. market after 2014 and had its approval withdrawn for certain formulations in 2018, though it remains available in some international markets.
Faropenem, an oral carbapenem, has limited approvals worldwide, including in Japan since 2000 for respiratory and urinary tract infections and in India since 2008 for similar indications. It is absorbed well after oral administration and provides broad-spectrum coverage against respiratory pathogens, but its use is restricted due to lack of approval in major markets like the U.S. and Europe, where development was halted after a non-approvable FDA decision in 2006.
Tebipenem pivoxil, another oral carbapenem, was approved in Japan in 2009 under the brand Orapenem for pediatric respiratory tract infections, ear/nose/throat infections, and urinary tract infections. It features a prodrug formulation for improved bioavailability and is particularly valued for its activity against beta-lactamase-producing pathogens in children, though it remains under investigation for broader adult indications elsewhere.
Recent combinations of carbapenems with beta-lactamase inhibitors have expanded options for multidrug-resistant infections. Meropenem-vaborbactam (Vabomere) was approved by the FDA in 2017 specifically for complicated urinary tract infections, including pyelonephritis, due to its enhanced activity against Klebsiella pneumoniae carbapenemase-producing strains. Imipenem-cilastatin-relebactam (Recarbrio) received FDA approval in 2019 for complicated urinary tract and intra-abdominal infections, with later expansion in 2020 to hospital-acquired and ventilator-associated pneumonia, targeting similar resistant Enterobacterales.

Investigational and Experimental Agents

Sanfetrinem cilexetil, an oral prodrug of the tricyclic carbapenem sanfetrinem, was initially developed in the 1990s but discontinued after phase II trials in the early 2000s due to commercial considerations. Recent efforts have revisited its potential, particularly for intracellular infections like tuberculosis, where it shows promising activity against Mycobacterium tuberculosis with a minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC)50 of 2 g/mL when combined with -lactamase inhibitors.
Recent developments emphasize oral carbapenems to enable non-intravenous outpatient treatment for multidrug-resistant (MDR) infections. Sulopenem etzadroxil, approved by the FDA in October 2024 in combination with probenecid (Orlynvah) for uncomplicated urinary tract infections (uUTIs) in adult women, represents a limited but significant step, with clinical success rates of 65.9% in phase III trials against Escherichia coli not susceptible to other oral agents; commercial launch is anticipated mid-2025. Tebipenem HBr, another oral carbapenem, met its primary endpoint in the 2025 PIVOT-PO phase III trial, demonstrating non-inferiority to intravenous imipenem-cilastatin (60.2% success rate) with a success rate of 58.5%; the trial was stopped early for efficacy in May 2025, with NDA filing planned for Q4 2025, potentially reducing hospitalization needs for MDR cases.
Development of these agents faces substantial challenges, including high costs estimated at over $1 billion per new antibiotic due to low commercial returns and regulatory hurdles. As of 2025, trends favor dual-action agents like -lactam/-lactamase inhibitor combinations to combat resistance, amid WHO reports highlighting a shrinking pipeline of just 90 antibacterial candidates, underscoring shortages for critical Gram-negative threats.

Safety Profile

Adverse Effects

Carbapenems are generally well-tolerated, but common adverse effects include gastrointestinal disturbances such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, occurring in approximately 1-5% of patients. Injection site reactions, including phlebitis, thrombophlebitis, and inflammation, are also frequently reported, with incidences around 0.9-2.4%. Dermatological reactions, particularly rash, pruritus, and urticaria, affect 1.8-3.5% of recipients.
Serious adverse effects, though less common, can include seizures, with an overall incidence of 0.4-2.4% across carbapenems, rising to 3% or higher with imipenem, especially in patients with renal impairment. Clostridium difficile-associated diarrhea represents a notable risk due to the broad-spectrum activity disrupting gut flora, contributing to antibiotic-associated diarrhea in up to 7% of hospitalized patients receiving carbapenems. Hypersensitivity reactions occur in about 0.3-1% of cases, with cross-reactivity to penicillins estimated at less than 1% in confirmed allergic patients.
Hematologic effects such as neutropenia and thrombocytopenia are rare, particularly with prolonged therapy or in patients with renal impairment. Elevations in liver enzymes, typically transient and asymptomatic, are commonly observed but rarely lead to clinically significant hepatotoxicity.
Management of adverse effects involves dose adjustments, particularly reducing imipenem doses in patients with epilepsy or renal failure to mitigate seizure risk. For gastrointestinal issues like diarrhea, supportive measures such as probiotics may help restore gut microbiota balance. Incidence data from post-marketing surveillance, including FDA adverse event reports, continue to inform monitoring, with serious events like seizures and hypersensitivity comprising a small proportion of notifications (as of 2023).

Contraindications and Precautions

Carbapenems are contraindicated in patients with known hypersensitivity to any component of the specific agent or to other drugs in the same class, as well as in those with a history of anaphylactic reactions to beta-lactam antibiotics.
Relative contraindications include a history of epilepsy or seizures, particularly with imipenem/cilastatin, due to its higher association with convulsive activity compared to other carbapenems. Severe renal impairment requires dose adjustment, and carbapenems should be avoided without modification in such cases to prevent toxicity. In pregnancy, available data for meropenem, ertapenem, and doripenem show no evidence of fetal harm in animal studies at exposures up to 3.2 times the maximum recommended human dose, though human data are limited; for imipenem/cilastatin, animal studies indicate potential maternal and fetal toxicity at high doses, with limited human data, requiring careful benefit-risk assessment. Note that doripenem (Doribax) has been discontinued in the United States since 2014 and is no longer available.
Precautions are advised for patients with central nervous system disorders, where seizures may occur more frequently, necessitating close monitoring and potential dose reduction. Elderly patients and those with renal impairment require vigilant monitoring of renal function, as carbapenems are primarily excreted by the kidneys. Drug interactions include reduced valproic acid levels leading to breakthrough seizures when co-administered with any carbapenem, and increased seizure risk with ganciclovir, particularly imipenem. In patients with a reported penicillin allergy, carbapenems should be avoided without skin testing due to a cross-reactivity risk of less than 1%, though higher in cases of confirmed IgE-mediated reactions.
In special populations, carbapenems are approved for pediatric use starting from neonates for certain indications with imipenem/cilastatin and from 3 months for meropenem and ertapenem, though efficacy data are limited in infants under 3 months. Doripenem lacks established pediatric safety data. For breastfeeding, carbapenems exhibit minimal excretion into breast milk, with meropenem levels undetectable in some cases, and are generally considered compatible, though infants should be monitored for gastrointestinal effects or altered microbiota.

Resistance

Mechanisms of Resistance

Bacterial resistance to carbapenems primarily arises through enzymatic inactivation, reduced drug influx, active efflux, and alterations in target sites, enabling pathogens to evade the antibiotics' bactericidal effects on cell wall synthesis. These mechanisms are particularly prevalent in Gram-negative bacteria such as Enterobacteriaceae, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Acinetobacter baumannii, though Gram-positive species like methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) and Enterococcus also exhibit resistance.
The most significant enzymatic mechanism involves the production of carbapenemases, which are -lactamases capable of hydrolyzing the -lactam ring central to carbapenem structure. In Enterobacteriaceae, class A serine-based carbapenemases like Klebsiella pneumoniae carbapenemase (KPC), particularly variants KPC-2 and KPC-3, are plasmid-mediated and hydrolyze carbapenems such as imipenem and meropenem, often in association with the ST258 clone. Class B metallo--lactamases (MBLs), exemplified by New Delhi metallo--lactamase (NDM-1), utilize zinc ions for hydrolysis and confer resistance across a broad range of -lactams, including carbapenems, in species like Escherichia coli and Klebsiella pneumoniae. Class D oxacillinases, such as OXA-48 and its variants, predominate in Enterobacteriaceae and Acinetobacter baumannii, exhibiting weak carbapenem hydrolytic activity but resistance to many -lactamase inhibitors. The spread of these carbapenemase genes occurs via plasmids and transposons, facilitating horizontal transfer among bacterial populations.
Non-enzymatic mechanisms reduce intracellular carbapenem accumulation by limiting entry or enhancing expulsion. Loss or downregulation of outer membrane porins, such as OmpK35 and OmpK36 in Klebsiella pneumoniae, decreases permeability to hydrophilic carbapenems, often combining with low-level -lactamase activity to achieve clinical resistance. Overexpression of efflux pumps, like MexAB-OprM in Pseudomonas aeruginosa or AcrAB-TolC in Enterobacteriaceae, actively pumps carbapenems out of the cell, further amplified in multidrug-resistant strains. In Acinetobacter baumannii, intrinsic impermeability arises from sparse porin expression and robust efflux systems, contributing to high baseline resistance levels.
Alterations in penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs), the molecular targets of carbapenems, reduce antibiotic affinity and impair cell wall cross-linking inhibition. In Gram-positive bacteria, MRSA acquires the mecA gene encoding low-affinity PBP2a, which maintains peptidoglycan synthesis despite carbapenem exposure. Enterococcus faecium exhibits resistance through mutations in intrinsic PBP5, such as at residue 485 (e.g., M485T or M485A), increasing protein dynamics and accelerating deacylation of the acyl-enzyme intermediate, thereby enhancing hydrolysis of -lactams including carbapenems.
Additional mechanisms include biofilm formation, where extracellular polymeric substances in pathogens like Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Acinetobacter baumannii create barriers that limit carbapenem penetration and sublethal concentrations promote tolerance. These diverse pathways often combine, such as porin loss with carbapenemase production, to yield multidrug resistance profiles.

Epidemiology and Clinical Impact

Carbapenem resistance among Enterobacterales, particularly carbapenem-resistant Enterobacterales (CRE), exhibits significant regional variation, with prevalence rates exceeding 50% in high-burden areas such as Greece and India, where endemic transmission drives widespread dissemination. Globally, CRE infections have surged, with the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) reporting a more than 460% increase in New Delhi metallo-beta-lactamase-producing CRE (NDM-CRE) cases between 2019 and 2023, a trend persisting into 2025. For carbapenem-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii (CRAB), rates in intensive care units (ICUs) can reach up to 80% in outbreak-prone settings, underscoring its persistence in critical care environments. Carbapenem-resistant Pseudomonas aeruginosa (CRPA) shows global resistance rates of 20-30%, with a pooled prevalence estimate of 34.7% across clinical and screening samples from 2014 to 2024, varying by region and contributing to its classification as a high-priority pathogen.
Key risk factors for carbapenem-resistant infections include prolonged hospital stays, prior exposure to broad-spectrum antibiotics such as carbapenems, and invasive procedures like mechanical ventilation or central line catheterization, which facilitate colonization and outbreaks. These risks are amplified in ICU and long-term care facilities, where previous CRE colonization increases the odds of subsequent infection by up to 10-fold, often leading to clustered outbreaks. In low-resource settings, inadequate infection control exacerbates transmission, particularly among vulnerable populations like the elderly and immunocompromised.
The clinical consequences of carbapenem resistance are severe, with CRE bacteremia associated with 30-day mortality rates of 40-50%, compared to approximately 20% for susceptible strains, driven by limited treatment options and delayed appropriate therapy. CRAB infections in ICUs carry even higher mortality, reaching 56.5% at 28 days, alongside prolonged hospital stays averaging 10-15 additional days per case. Economically, the burden in the United States exceeds $1 billion annually, encompassing direct healthcare costs, lost productivity, and excess bed-days, with global estimates projecting trillions in cumulative impact by 2050 if unchecked.
Recent trends from 2024 to 2025 reveal accelerating resistance, prompting WHO warnings of widespread carbapenem non-susceptibility narrowing therapeutic pipelines and increasing reliance on toxic last-resort agents like colistin. The Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA) has reinforced stewardship guidelines, advocating for rapid diagnostics and restricted carbapenem use to mitigate spread, particularly in rising low-resource regions where surveillance gaps hinder containment. Production and Development

Biosynthesis

Carbapenems are naturally produced by certain soil bacteria, with thienamycin serving as the archetypal example isolated from Streptomyces cattleya. This actinomycete synthesizes thienamycin through a complex enzymatic pathway that constructs the characteristic bicyclic -lactam core and appends distinctive side chains, including a cysteaminyl moiety at C-2 and a 1-hydroxyethyl group at C-6. The process draws on elements of non-ribosomal peptide synthesis but lacks involvement of polyketide synthases, relying instead on specialized synthetases and modifying enzymes to assemble the antibiotic from simple precursors like acetyl-CoA, glutamate, and cysteine.
The formation of the bicyclic core begins with the condensation of acetyl-S-CoA and -glutamyl phosphate, catalyzed by ThnE, to generate a pyrroline-5-carboxamide intermediate that establishes the five-membered ring. This is followed by closure of the -lactam ring via the -lactam synthetase ThnM, which cyclizes the structure into (3S,5S)-carbapenam, analogous to CarA and CarB enzymes in simpler carbapenem pathways from Enterobacteriaceae. Subsequent modifications include epimerization at C-5, likely mediated by an oxidoreductase such as ThnA, to achieve the correct stereochemistry, and hydroxylation steps, with ThnG introducing the hydroxyl at the 1-position of the ethyl side chain. For the C-2 cysteaminyl side chain, pantetheinederived from coenzyme Aserves as the sulfur donor unit; it is first attached to the carbapenam core by the ligase ThnJ, forming a pantetheinylated intermediate. Radical S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) enzymes ThnK and ThnL then perform sequential methylations on the adjacent carbon to build the ethyl extension, while ThnP, a thioesterase, cleaves the pantetheine linker to yield the free cysteamine group, incorporating sulfur from the original pantetheine unit.
The biosynthetic machinery is encoded by the thn gene cluster, spanning approximately 26.5 kb and comprising 22 genes (thnA through thnV) in S. cattleya. Core genes like thnE and thnM direct bicyclic ring assembly, while thnJP handle side-chain elaboration via non-ribosomal peptide-like mechanisms, and accessory genes such as thnG and thnO encode hydroxylases and oxidoreductases for maturation. Regulation occurs through transcriptional activators like ThnI and ThnU, which respond to global antibiotic production pathways in streptomycetes, ensuring coordinated expression under nutrient-limiting conditions. Similar thn-like clusters are present in other streptomycetes, such as S. argenteolus (producing MM4550 and MM1270) and S. griseus, where conserved thnE/M/K/L homologs facilitate analogous core and side-chain formation, though with variations in side-chain substituents. Natural yields of thienamycin remain low (typically <100 mg/L), prompting reliance on semi-synthetic derivatization for therapeutic agents like imipenem.

Synthetic Production and Recent Advances

The synthetic production of carbapenems relies on total synthesis strategies, often starting from readily available chiral precursors such as L-aspartic acid to construct the core bicyclic -lactam structure. A seminal approach involves the conversion of L-aspartic acid to a 3-acetoxy-2-azetidinone intermediate, followed by lead tetraacetate-mediated oxidative decarboxylation and insertion of a four-carbon diazo unit to form the five-membered pyrroline ring fused to the -lactam. The -lactam ring is typically closed via a Staudinger cycloaddition between a ketene and an imine, ensuring the trans configuration at the C5-C6 bridgehead. More recent enantioselective formal syntheses, such as a nine-step route to thienamycin completed in 2024, employ asymmetric reduction using NaBH in the presence of D-tartaric acid to establish stereochemistry at the C-1 hydroxyethyl substituent early in the sequence.
Industrial manufacturing predominantly uses semi-synthetic routes derived from fermented thienamycin, the natural progenitor, to produce commercial agents like imipenem and meropenem. Thienamycin is enzymatically protected at the C-2 amino group during purification, followed by deprotection and side-chain modification; for imipenem, the Merck process introduces the cysteaminyl thio group at C-2 via a late-stage displacement, with final purification achieved through hydrogenation to remove protecting groups without isolating intermediates. This approach enhances yield and scalability, as full total synthesis from glucose derivatives or aspartic acid remains cost-prohibitive for bulk production due to multiple stereocontrolled steps.
Recent advances emphasize greener and more efficient methods, including biocatalytic resolutions and continuous flow processes to address environmental and scalability issues. Biocatalysis has been integrated for stereoselective modifications, such as enzyme-mediated inversions in carbapenam intermediates, enabling production of non-natural stereoisomers with high enantiopurity (>99% ee) using engineered carbapenem biosynthesis proteins. Continuous flow synthesis has gained traction for key intermediates; for instance, a 2020 two-stage flow protocol for a carbapenem pyrroline core achieved GMP compliance with reduced solvent use and improved safety for azide handling. As of early 2025, flow chemistry has been extended to the synthesis of carbapenem intermediates, such as protected azetidinone cores, employing vertical dynamic reactors for multikilogram scales with >95% purity. Advances in boronate chemistry, relevant to companion -lactamase inhibitors like vaborbactam, include enantioselective methods for -aminoboronates using NiH catalysis.
Challenges in carbapenem synthesis center on achieving stereoselectivity at the C-1 position and reducing costs, particularly for generics. The trans-3S,4R configuration at C-1 requires precise control during side-chain installation, often via chiral auxiliaries or catalytic hydrogenation, but side reactions like epimerization can lower yields to below 70% without optimized ligands. Post-patent expiry of meropenem in 2010, generic production has focused on streamlined semi-synthetic routes, leading to price reductions of 20-30% within markets through efficient deprotection and crystallization steps, enhancing global accessibility amid rising resistance.
