 Top Rank, Inc. is an American boxing promotion company founded in 1973 by Bob Arum, a Hall of Fame promoter and Harvard Law School graduate who began his career in the sport by staging Muhammad Ali's fights starting in 1966. Based in Las Vegas, Nevada, Top Rank has established itself as the preeminent force in professional boxing over more than five decades, having promoted nearly 2,500 fight cards worldwide.
Under Arum's leadership as CEO, the company pioneered the broadcasting of boxing events through closed-circuit television and pay-per-view formats in the 1970s and 1980s, forging landmark partnerships with major networks such as HBO and Showtime that elevated the sport's global visibility. Key historical achievements include promoting 27 of Muhammad Ali's bouts from 1966 to 1978, among them the iconic "Thrilla in Manila" against Joe Frazier in 1975, as well as eight of the nine legendary "Four Kings" welterweight title fights in the 1980s featuring Roberto Durn, Sugar Ray Leonard, Thomas Hearns, and Marvin Hagler. Top Rank has guided the careers of numerous Hall of Famers and modern stars, including Oscar De La Hoya, Floyd Mayweather Jr., Manny Pacquiao (for whom it staged 20 fights, culminating in the record-breaking 2015 superfight against Mayweather that generated over $400 million), and former heavyweight champion Tyson Fury.
In the digital age, Top Rank has adapted by securing an exclusive multi-year deal with ESPN in 2017 to produce and broadcast its events, debuting with Pacquiao's victory over Jeff Horn, and expanding into streaming platforms like its own Top Rank Classics FAST channel available on Pluto TV, Roku, and Tubi for archival and live content. The company continues to stage high-profile international events, representing a diverse roster of elite fighters and maintaining its dominance in matchmaking and promotion amid evolving industry dynamics, including recent shifts away from long-term ESPN partnerships announced in early 2025.

History

Founding and Predecessor Organizations

Top Rank was founded in 1973 by prominent boxing promoter Bob Arum and Jabir Herbert Muhammad, Muhammad Ali's longtime business manager, as a promotional company specializing in boxing events and headquartered in the Las Vegas area of Nevada. The incorporation marked a formal venture into independent promotion for Arum, building directly on his experiences with Ali's career, and was structured to capitalize on the growing commercial potential of heavyweight boxing during that era. Early financial backing stemmed from Arum's earnings and networks developed through prior promotional work, including partnerships with investors who supported Ali-related ventures, though specific funding details remain tied to the profitability of those initial efforts.
A key predecessor to Top Rank was Main Bout, Inc., a promotional entity established in January 1966 primarily to handle Muhammad Ali's fight rights and negotiations, with Arum serving as vice president and secretary alongside white partners Mike Malitz and black investors including Ali himself, Jim Brown, and Jabir Herbert Muhammad. Main Bout emerged from discussions during the promotion of Ali's 1965 heavyweight title rematch against Sonny Liston, where Arum and Malitz had collaborated on event logistics and publicity, hiring figures like Jim Brown to enhance outreach. The company formalized Ali's control over his promotions, enabling events such as the November 1966 bout against Cleveland Williams, and emphasized black economic empowerment with 60% ownership allocated to African American stakeholders, reflecting the structure's ties to the Nation of Islam.
Jabir Herbert Muhammad, born Herbert E. Poole in 1929 as the third son of Nation of Islam leader Elijah Muhammad, brought significant influence to both Main Bout and Top Rank through his role in Ali's management starting in 1966, when he replaced the Louisville Sponsoring Group as the boxer's primary advisor and handler. Having managed Nation of Islam-affiliated businesses like bakeries and dry cleaners in Chicago, Muhammad's involvement ensured that early promotions under these entities maintained strong connections to the Nation of Islam, particularly in heavyweight boxing centered on Ali's career and its cultural resonance. This foundational emphasis on heavyweight divisions and Ali's fights provided Top Rank with its initial identity upon incorporation, setting the stage for broader operations while honoring the communal and ideological networks that supported black athletic talent.

Early Promotions and Growth

Top Rank was incorporated in 1973 by Bob Arum and Jabir Herbert Muhammad, initially focusing on heavyweight boxing promotions while leveraging Arum's prior experience with Muhammad Ali's post-exile comeback fights. Arum had promoted Ali's 1970 bout against Jerry Quarry in Atlanta, marking Ali's return after a three-and-a-half-year ban, which drew significant attention despite Quarry's competitiveness and helped establish Arum's reputation in the sport. By 1974, shortly after Top Rank's founding, the company co-promoted Ali's iconic "Rumble in the Jungle" against George Foreman in Zaire through its handling of U.S. closed-circuit television rights, contributing to the event's global reach and generating substantial revenue streams via pay-per-view distribution.
To diversify beyond boxing and enhance brand visibility, Top Rank ventured into non-traditional events, notably promoting daredevil Evel Knievel's attempted rocket-powered jump across Idaho's Snake River Canyon on September 8, 1974. The stunt, broadcast via closed-circuit TV, aimed for an $8 million gate but fell short due to a parachute deployment that prevented a full crossing, yet it underscored Top Rank's innovative approach to spectacle-driven promotions and attracted millions of viewers. Early operations faced financial hurdles, including high production costs for TV distribution and competition from broadcast networks. Arum engaged in legal disputes with ABC in the mid-1970s over pay-TV boxing rights, particularly amid a 1977 scandal involving kickbacks in ABC's heavyweight tournament that led the network to temporarily withdraw from the sport, forcing Top Rank to pivot toward CBS and other outlets for its packages.
As Ali's career waned in the late 1970s, Top Rank shifted from a heavyweight-centric model to multi-weight class promotions to sustain growth, signing rising stars across divisions. The company established a promotional contract with Puerto Rican prodigy Wilfred Bentez in 1978, backing his welterweight title defenses and high-profile bouts, such as his 1979 loss to Sugar Ray Leonard, which expanded Top Rank's portfolio into lighter weights. Similarly, in the early 1980s, Top Rank secured a deal with junior welterweight contender Aaron Pryor following his initial title win under another promoter, supporting his defenses and marquee fights like the 1982 wars against Alexis Argello that solidified the company's presence in the 140-pound division. This expansion helped Top Rank navigate economic pressures and build a broader roster, setting the stage for its dominance in the 1980s.

Major Milestones and Transitions

In the 1980s, Top Rank solidified its position as a leading boxing promoter through high-profile events featuring stars like Sugar Ray Leonard, Thomas Hearns, and Hctor Camacho, capitalizing on the "Four Kings" era of welterweight and middleweight rivalries. Notable bouts included the 1981 WBC/WBA welterweight unification fight between Leonard and Hearns at Caesars Palace, which drew over 1.2 million pay-per-view buys and showcased Top Rank's ability to stage blockbuster events that elevated the sport's commercial appeal. Top Rank's promotions of Puerto Rican talent, such as Wilfred Bentez's bouts, helped establish the company's reputation for nurturing dynamic fighters from Latin American markets.
The 1990s marked a period of intense rivalry and internal turbulence for Top Rank, exemplified by founder Bob Arum's escalating feud with rival promoter Don King, which highlighted broader tensions over fight rights and sanctioning body influences that threatened independent promoters. This "stormy" era saw legal clashes, including Arum's 1990 testimony in federal court against King's business tactics during a racketeering trial. Arum refocused Top Rank on rebuilding through strategic fighter signings, but the fallout underscored the precarious power dynamics in professional boxing promotion.
Entering the late 1990s and 2000s, Top Rank drove the megafight landscape, notably through Oscar De La Hoya's early career promotionssuch as his 1997 upset of Pernell Whitakerand later collaborations that enabled events like the 2007 De La Hoya-Mayweather Jr. bout, which generated a record 2.4 million pay-per-view buys despite Golden Boy Promotions' lead role. Concurrently, Manny Pacquiao's ascent under Top Rank's banner from 2006 onward transformed the company, with 20 promoted fights including his 2008 featherweight unification against Juan Manuel Marquez and 2009 welterweight conquest of Miguel Cotto, establishing Pacquiao as an eight-division champion and boosting Top Rank's global revenue through high-stakes rivalries.
The 2010s represented a pivotal digital transition for Top Rank, marked by a 2017 multiyear deal with ESPN that shifted broadcasts to streaming and linear TV hybrids, enabling broader accessibility and youth engagement amid declining traditional cable viewership. This era also saw thawing relations with Golden Boy Promotions, leading to co-promotions like the landmark 2015 Pacquiao-Mayweather "Fight of the Century," which shattered records with 4.6 million pay-per-view sales and over $400 million in revenue, fostering cross-promotional stability after years of antagonism.
The 2020s brought unprecedented challenges, including the COVID-19 pandemic's suspension of live events from March 2020 onward, forcing Top Rank to postpone major cards like Pacquiao's title defenses and pivot to closed-door bouts upon resumption, which reduced gate revenues by an estimated 70% in the initial year. Compounding this, the long-standing ESPN partnership concluded in July 2025 after eight years, ending linear TV exposure for Top Rank cards and prompting a search for new streaming platforms amid evolving media landscapes. As of November 2025, no new broadcasting deal has been announced.
A key legal milestone influencing Top Rank's operational framework was the 1959 U.S. Supreme Court decision in United States v. International Boxing Club of New York, Inc., which dismantled the IBC's monopoly on championship promotions by ruling its exclusive contracts and venue controls violated antitrust laws, paving the way for independent entities like Top Rank to thrive without centralized dominance.

Organization and Operations

Leadership and Structure

Bob Arum founded Top Rank in 1973 and has served as its CEO and chairman since inception, overseeing the promotion of over 2,500 boxing events during his tenure. Inducted into the International Boxing Hall of Fame in 1999, Arum remains actively involved in the company's strategic decisions at age 93.
Top Rank operates as a privately held company owned by the Arum family, with integrated divisions managing event production, fighter development, and promotional activities. Key executives include President Todd duBoef, who handles day-to-day operations; Vice President of Boxing Operations Carl Moretti, responsible for coordinating fight cards and logistics; and matchmakers Brad Goodman and Bruce Trampler, the latter having shaped fighter careers since joining in 1981 and inducted into the Hall of Fame in 2010.
In February 2025, Top Rank and duBoef faced a $25 million lawsuit filed by former employee William "Billy" Keane, alleging breach of contract, withholding of promised compensation, and failure to disclose business dealings with Daniel Kinahanaccused by U.S. authorities of leading a transnational crime organization involved in drug traffickingto ESPN during partnership negotiations. The suit claims these actions risked the broadcasting deal and raises questions about internal governance. Top Rank has denied the allegations, stating they are without merit, and the case remains ongoing as of November 2025. This development has intensified industry discussions on leadership succession amid Arum's age.
The company's business model centers on securing exclusive promotional contracts with professional boxers, enabling Top Rank to control matchmaking and event staging while generating revenue through ticket sales at live venues, pay-per-view distributions, and corporate sponsorships. This approach has sustained the promoter's position as a leading force in boxing, with sponsorship income rising 110% year-over-year as of 2022 and media rights forming a core revenue stream.
Headquartered at 748 Pilot Road in Las Vegas, Nevada, Top Rank employs approximately 175 people as of 2025, supporting its global operations in talent scouting and event execution. Amid Arum's advancing age, industry observers have raised questions about succession planning, noting duBoef's expanded role in negotiations and long-term strategy as a potential bridge to the next generation of leadership.

Broadcasting Partnerships

Top Rank established its foundational broadcasting partnership with ESPN in 1980, launching the weekly "Top Rank Boxing" series that aired fights promoted by the company and became the longest-running boxing program in U.S. cable television history, continuing without interruption until a hiatus in 1996. The partnership resumed in the 2000s through ESPN's "Friday Night Fights" series, which frequently featured Top Rank events and helped sustain the promoter's presence on the network amid a shifting media landscape. In 2017, Top Rank and ESPN signed a landmark multi-year agreement, making ESPN the exclusive U.S. broadcaster for Top Rank content, including live events on ESPN, ESPN Deportes, and streaming via the newly launched ESPN+ platform, with the deal encompassing up to 54 annual events and extending through 2025.
The ESPN partnership featured prominent announcer teams, notably Joe Tessitore handling play-by-play duties alongside analyst Teddy Atlas, whose insightful commentary became a hallmark of the broadcasts during the early 2000s and into the 2010s, though Atlas was removed from live event coverage by ESPN in late 2017. This era significantly boosted Top Rank's visibility, integrating traditional television with digital streaming to reach broader audiences and support pay-per-view (PPV) events distributed through ESPN platforms.
In August 2025, the ESPN partnership concluded after eight years, prompting Top Rank to pursue split media rights negotiations with multiple platforms to diversify its distribution and adapt to the rise of streaming services. Historically, Top Rank had collaborated with broadcast networks like NBC, which aired its events on NBCSN starting in 2006, and CBS, which televised high-profile bouts such as the 2011 Manny Pacquiao-Shane Mosley fight in prime time. Internationally, Top Rank's co-promotional deals, particularly for heavyweight Tyson Fury, have included partnerships with UK-based Sky Sports, which has broadcast Fury's major fights to European audiences since 2019.
Top Rank has also ventured into PPV events through various platforms, including ESPN+ for select marquee matchups during the recent partnership, and has explored streaming options like DAZN for global distribution of premium content. As of late 2025, ongoing discussions point to potential new streaming alignments, such as with DAZN or other services, to facilitate PPV and live events amid the post-ESPN transition.

International Expansion

Top Rank's international expansion gained momentum in the 1980s and 1990s through its focus on Latin America, particularly Mexico, where it promoted the career of Julio Csar Chvez, one of the country's most iconic fighters. The company organized numerous bouts in Mexico featuring Chvez, including his 1984 super featherweight title win against Mario Martinez in Culiacn and his 1993 WBC super lightweight title fight against Greg Haugen at the Estadio Azteca in Mexico City, which drew a record-breaking crowd of 132,274 spectators. These events not only solidified Top Rank's presence in the Mexican market but also highlighted the company's ability to stage large-scale promotions tailored to regional audiences and boxing traditions.
In the 2000s, Top Rank extended its reach into Asian markets by signing Manny Pacquiao in 2001, leveraging his Filipino heritage to boost popularity across the Philippines and surrounding regions, including Japan. This partnership facilitated promotional activities and increased visibility in Asia, with Pacquiao's fights drawing massive followings and contributing to Top Rank's strategy of promoting local stars to penetrate new territories. By the 2010s, this laid the groundwork for direct events in Asia, such as the 2023 Naoya Inoue vs. Marlon Tapales undisputed junior featherweight title bout in Tokyo, Japan.
European operations expanded significantly in 2019 through a groundbreaking co-promotional agreement with Frank Warren's Queensberry Promotions, focusing on Tyson Fury's heavyweight bouts in the UK. This deal enabled Top Rank to co-promote Fury's fights, including domestic UK events compliant with the British Boxing Board of Control regulations via Queensberry's local licensing. In the 2020s, Top Rank further grew in the Middle East by co-promoting high-profile cards in Saudi Arabia, such as the 2024 undisputed heavyweight championship between Tyson Fury and Oleksandr Usyk at Kingdom Arena in Riyadh. These ventures reflect Top Rank's model of operating without dedicated international offices, instead relying on strategic partnerships to navigate regional regulatory frameworks and host events abroad.
To support its global activities, Top Rank has diversified revenue through sponsorships with multinational brands, including Anheuser-Busch, which renewed its deal in 2022 to make Bud Light the official beer and exclusive hard seltzer sponsor of Top Rank promotions worldwide. Similarly, long-term venue partnerships with MGM Resorts have facilitated events at international MGM properties, enhancing brand exposure beyond the U.S.

Promoted Fighters

Current Roster

Top Rank's current roster as of November 2025 comprises around 50-60 active boxers under exclusive or co-promotional contracts, spanning from flyweight to heavyweight divisions and emphasizing pay-per-view headliners, world titleholders, and international talent. The stable features a mix of established champions and rising stars, with many secured through multi-year exclusive agreements designed to maximize global exposure and revenue potential, particularly via high-profile bouts on platforms like DAZN and Amazon Prime following the end of the ESPN partnership in summer 2025. These transitions have enabled broader distribution for roster events.
Among the roster's world champions, Terence Crawford holds the undisputed super middleweight titles with a 42-0 record (31 KOs) as of November 2025. Naoya Inoue remains the undisputed super bantamweight kingpin with a 31-0 record (28 KOs), having unified all four major belts in 2023 and continuing to dominate with defenses in 2025. Tyson Fury, co-promoted by Top Rank for U.S.-based events, has a 34-2-1 record (24 KOs) as of November 2025, following losses in his trilogy with Oleksandr Usyk. Other titleholders include Teofimo Lopez (WBO and Ring super lightweight, 22-1, 13 KOs), Shakur Stevenson (WBC lightweight, 24-0, 11 KOs), and Jesse "Bam" Rodriguez (unified WBC, WBO, and Ring super flyweight, 22-0, 15 KOs).
Emerging prospects bolster the roster's depth, such as lightweight Keyshawn Davis (15-0-1, 10 KOs), an Olympic silver medalist who captured the WBO lightweight title in 2025. In the women's division, featherweight Alyssa Mendoza (5-0, 2 KOs) signed a multi-fight deal in 2024, winning her pro debut and positioning as a PPV draw in the growing female boxing scene. Additional standouts include lightweight contender Albert Puello (23-0, 10 KOs), super featherweight Bruce "Shu Shu" Carrington (6-0, 4 KOs), and heavyweight prospect Moses Itauma (10-0, 8 KOs), the latter signed post-2023 for his explosive potential.
The roster's diversity reflects Top Rank's global strategy, with strong American representation alongside international stars like Japanese champions Inoue, Junto Nakatani (undisputed bantamweight, 31-0, 24 KOs), and Kosei Tanaka (super flyweight contender, 23-1, 14 KOs), as well as British talents Fury and super middleweight Zach Parker (prospect, 24-1, 18 KOs). Mexican and Puerto Rican fighters add regional flavor, including featherweight Abraham Nova (23-2, 16 KOs) and junior welterweight Cristian Baez (prospect). Recent post-2023 signings, such as Nakatani in 2024 and potential 2025 additions like cruiserweight prospects amid broadcasting transitions, underscore efforts to refresh the stable for multi-platform distribution.

Notable Alumni

Top Rank has promoted numerous legendary fighters whose careers under the company elevated its status in the boxing world. Among the most iconic is Muhammad Ali, whose association with Top Rank founder Bob Arum began in 1966 and spanned 27 fights through 1978, including pivotal heavyweight title defenses. Notable bouts during this tenure include the "Thrilla in Manila" against Joe Frazier in 1975, a brutal 14-round war that solidified Ali's legacy as a three-time champion and drew global attention to Top Rank's promotional prowess. Ali's fights helped establish Top Rank as a major force in heavyweight boxing, contributing to the company's early growth through high-profile events that attracted massive audiences.
Oscar De La Hoya's partnership with Top Rank in the 1990s transformed him from an Olympic gold medalist into a multi-division superstar, with Arum promoting 37 of his bouts. De La Hoya captured titles in six weight classes during this era, including victories over Julio Csar Chvez in 1996 and Pernell Whitaker in 1997, which showcased his speed and power while setting pay-per-view benchmarks. His fights generated record-breaking revenue for Top Rank, such as the 2007 bout against Floyd Mayweather Jr., co-promoted by the company, which sold 2.4 million PPV units and earned over $136 million, underscoring De La Hoya's role in commercializing boxing on a massive scale.
Manny Pacquiao's tenure with Top Rank from the early 2000s through the 2010s marked one of the company's most successful chapters, encompassing 20 promoted fights and his achievement as the only eight-division world champion in history. Key highlights include the trilogy with Juan Manuel Mrquez, culminating in a 2012 knockout, and triumphs over Ricky Hatton in 2009 and Miguel Cotto in 2009, where Pacquiao claimed welterweight gold. The 2015 superfight against Mayweather, co-promoted by Top Rank, shattered records with 4.6 million PPV buys and over $400 million in revenue, while Pacquiao's overall run boosted Top Rank's international profile and financial dominance in the sport.
Floyd Mayweather Jr.'s early career from 1996 to 2006 was exclusively under Top Rank, where Arum promoted 35 of his undefeated fights, building the foundation for his rise to pound-for-pound supremacy. During this period, Mayweather won titles at super featherweight, lightweight, and super lightweight, including a dominant 2002 decision over Genaro Hernndez and a 2005 unification against Arturo Gatti. His technical mastery in these bouts helped Top Rank cultivate a new generation of defensive wizards, though he departed in 2006 to form his own promotion.
George Foreman's comeback in the 1990s, promoted by Top Rank, exemplified the company's ability to revive legends, culminating in his 1994 knockout of Michael Moorer to claim the heavyweight title at age 45the oldest in boxing history. Arum orchestrated 20 fights in this second act, including punishing knockouts of Ken Norton in 1995 and Axel Schulz in 1996, which preserved Foreman's status as a knockout artist with 68 career stoppages. These events not only extended Foreman's Hall of Fame career but also demonstrated Top Rank's strategic promotion of veteran fighters for broad appeal.
Modern standout Vasyl Lomachenko, signed to Top Rank since his 2013 professional debut, amassed three-division titles and an 18-3 record (12 KOs) under the banner before retiring in June 2025, earning acclaim as one of the most skilled technicians in recent boxing history. His tenure includes amateur-like dominance, such as a 2018 stoppage of Jorge Linares for lightweight gold and a 2020 unification against Tefimo Lpez, contributing to Top Rank's emphasis on high-IQ fighters from international talent pools.

Events and Promotions

Flagship Boxing Series

Top Rank's flagship boxing series, known as Top Rank Boxing, originated as a weekly television program on ESPN from 1980 to 1996, featuring a structured format that included a main event headline bout typically involving established contenders or champions, supported by an undercard of 4-6 preliminary fights showcasing rising prospects and occasional title eliminators. This series debuted on April 10, 1980, and became a cornerstone of cable boxing programming, with events often scheduled on Friday nights to align with prime-time viewership, drawing consistent audiences through live broadcasts from venues like casinos and arenas.
The partnership was revived in 2017 under a multi-year agreement that extended through 2025, producing 54 live events annually on ESPN platforms, including ESPN, ESPN2, and ESPN+, while maintaining the core format of main events paired with multi-fight undercards focused on talent development and championship contention. These cards emphasized a mix of heavyweight clashes, lightweight title defenses, and prospect showcases, with undercards averaging 4-6 bouts to build narratives around emerging stars like Teofimo Lopez and Shakur Stevenson.
In the 1980s, Top Rank Boxing events in Atlantic City, New Jersey, set record attendance milestones, capitalizing on the city's emergence as a boxing hub with over 500 cards hosted between 1982 and 1985, including high-profile Top Rank-promoted fights that routinely drew 10,000-15,000 fans per event at venues like the Trump Plaza.
Following the conclusion of the ESPN deal in July 2025, Top Rank transitioned to a hybrid model emphasizing streaming and pay-per-view distribution across multiple networks, beginning with the October 2025 Mikaela Mayer-Mary Spencer women's super welterweight main event on a new streaming platform, while retaining the traditional undercard structure for broader accessibility. This shift prioritizes digital platforms like DAZN for non-linear delivery, allowing for global reach without reliance on traditional television.
Over time, the flagship series has evolved to incorporate women's boxing, with dedicated main events and undercard slots for female fighters since the mid-2010s, exemplified by Seniesa Estrada's 2023 minimumweight title unification on ESPN, which highlighted growing parity in promotion and viewership. Additionally, the format has adapted to include multi-sport crossovers, blending boxing with broader combat sports appeal to attract diverse audiences.

Other Ventures and Collaborations

Top Rank has pursued co-promotions with rival boxing promoters to enable marquee matchups that might otherwise be hindered by contractual rivalries. A prominent example is the 2008 "Dream Match" between Oscar De La Hoya and Manny Pacquiao at the MGM Grand Garden Arena in Las Vegas, which was jointly promoted by Top Rank and Golden Boy Promotions, drawing over 1.25 million pay-per-view buys and generating record revenue for the sport.
In more recent years, Top Rank collaborated with Premier Boxing Champions (PBC) on the 2021 welterweight title bout between Manny Pacquiao and Yordenis Ugas at T-Mobile Arena, where Ugas retained his WBA welterweight title via unanimous decision; this event marked a rare alignment between Top Rank and PBC, emphasizing shared interests in elevating global boxing profiles.
Top Rank has also explored alliances with Matchroom Boxing, particularly through purse bid competitions and potential joint ventures, though direct co-promotions remain limited; for instance, in October 2025, Matchroom outbid Top Rank for promotional rights to the IBF lightweight title fight between Raymond Muratalla and Andy Cruz, highlighting competitive dynamics in the promoter landscape.
Beyond core boxing, Top Rank has forged sponsorship and merchandising partnerships to enhance brand visibility and fighter support. Bud Light has served as the official beer sponsor of Top Rank events since at least 2022, extending to exclusive hard seltzer designation and integrating branding into live broadcasts and ring walks. In December 2024, Miller Lite announced a multi-year partnership with Top Rank, focusing on athlete endorsements, event sponsorships, and targeted merchandising to promote responsible consumption and elevate emerging talents.
These deals have facilitated apparel and accessory lines featuring Top Rank fighters, often through licensed collaborations that include branded gear for events and fan merchandise, though specific video game integrations remain tied to individual athlete appearances rather than company-wide ventures.
Philanthropic efforts linked to Top Rank, primarily driven by founder Bob Arum, include support for fighter welfare initiatives, such as advocating for improved medical protocols during the COVID-19 era to ensure safety in closed-door events; Arum has emphasized the need for rigorous health measures equivalent to hospital standards in high-stakes promotions.
As of 2025, Top Rank has shown interest in diversification amid streaming shifts, with exploratory discussions around virtual events and esports-adjacent boxing simulations, though no formal launches have been confirmed; this aligns with broader industry trends toward digital engagement.
