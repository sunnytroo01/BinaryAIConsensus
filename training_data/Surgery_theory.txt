Surgery theory
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Surgery theory is a collection of techniques in geometric topology for modifying one finite-dimensional manifold to produce another in a controlled manner, primarily by excising embedded spheres and replacing them with disks of complementary dimensions, enabling the classification of manifolds up to homotopy equivalence, diffeomorphism, or homeomorphism, especially in dimensions five and higher.
The theory originated in the 1960s with John Milnor's introduction of surgery on differentiable manifolds to study exotic spheres, and was rapidly extended to piecewise-linear (PL) and topological categories by William Browder, Michel Kervaire, Sergei Novikov, Dennis Sullivan, and C.T.C. Wall, who developed the foundational surgery exact sequence and obstruction groups. Their work disproved the manifold Hauptvermutung, showing that homotopy equivalent manifolds may not be homeomorphic, and built on earlier results like the h-cobordism theorem by Stephen Smale. Later contributions by Frank Quinn, Andrew Ranicki, and Wolfgang Lck incorporated algebraic tools, including L-theory and quadratic forms, to handle more general settings such as Poincar complexes and infinite fundamental groups.
At its core, surgery theory revolves around normal mapsdegree-one maps from a manifold to a Poincar complex equipped with stable bundle dataand the surgery obstruction, an algebraic invariant in the L-groups $ L_n(\mathbb{Z}, w) $ that determines whether a homotopy equivalence can be realized by surgery. The process involves iterative surgery steps to kill homotopy groups below half the dimension, using tools like the Whitney trick for transversality and Spivak normal fibrations to ensure compatibility. The surgery exact sequence relates the structure set $ S_{TOP}(X) $, which parametrizes homotopy manifolds over a complex $ X $, to normal invariants in $ $ and L-groups, providing a computable framework for obstructions.
Applications of surgery theory include the classification of exotic spheres via the stable homotopy of spheres, resolutions of the topological rigidity problem, and connections to broader conjectures like the Farrell-Jones and Baum-Connes assemblies in equivariant homology. It has also influenced the study of manifold structures on homotopy types and the enumeration of diffeomorphism classes in high dimensions.

Surgery on Manifolds

Fundamental Observation

A manifold is a topological space that is locally Euclidean, meaning that around every point there exists a neighborhood homeomorphic to an open subset of Euclidean space Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn. In the context of surgery theory, compact smooth manifolds of dimension nnn are particularly amenable to decomposition into handlebodies or CW-complexes, where the space is constructed iteratively by attaching cells (disks DkD^kDk) along their boundaries via maps from spheres Sk1S^{k-1}Sk1. This handle decomposition provides a framework for understanding the manifold's structure, as handles of various indices capture both the geometric and topological features of the space.
The core insight of surgery theory, often termed the fundamental observation, is that in dimensions n5n \geq 5n5, a degree-1 normal map from a manifold to a Poincar complex can be modified through local surgeriesspecifically, excising the tubular neighborhood of an embedded sphere SkMnS^k \subset M^nSkMn (with k<n/2k < n/2k<n/2) and attaching a (k+1)(k+1)(k+1)-handle along the boundary SkSnk1S^k \times S^{n-k-1}SkSnk1to kill non-trivial classes in k(M)\pi_k(M)k(M) below the middle dimension, yielding a highly connected map while producing an h-cobordism to the original manifold. This process preserves the homotopy type relative to the target complex but alters the diffeomorphism class of the manifold, enabling the classification of manifold structures on homotopy types. This observation is underpinned by the h-cobordism theorem, which equates h-cobordant manifolds (those connected by such handle attachments with trivial Whitehead torsion) with diffeomorphisms relative to the boundary in dimensions at least 5.
Central to this observation is the embedding of spheres into the manifold interior, where a smoothly embedded sphere SkMnS^k \subset M^nSkMn (with k<n/2k < n/2k<n/2) admits a tubular neighborhood diffeomorphic to a disk bundle in its normal bundle. In dimensions n5n \geq 5n5, the Whitney trick ensures that such embeddings can be made transverse (hence disjoint from other submanifolds of complementary dimension), and the normal bundle can often be trivialized stably, facilitating the handle attachment without affecting compatibility with the ambient structure. The role of the normal bundle is pivotal, as it determines the framing for the attaching map of the replacement handle, ensuring the surgery aligns smoothly with the original manifold.
Consequently, in dimension n5n \geq 5n5, surgery enables controlled local modifications that relate homotopy equivalent manifolds, allowing algebraic classification of diffeomorphism types via obstructions in L-groups and normal invariants, as surgeries produce h-cobordisms. This fact stems directly from the stable range of embeddings and handle cancellation lemmas, which hold reliably above dimension 4.

Definition of the Surgery Operation

The surgery operation in manifold topology is a precise technique for modifying an n-dimensional manifold MnM^nMn by excising a neighborhood of an embedded sphere and reattaching a handle, thereby altering its topological structure in a controlled manner. This operation, introduced as a fundamental tool in the classification of manifolds, relies on the existence of an embedding of a k-dimensional sphere SkS^kSk into MnM^nMn with a trivial normal bundle.
The process begins with selecting a smoothly (or PL) embedded sphere SkMnS^k \subset M^nSkMn, where 0k<n/20 \leq k < n/20k<n/2 typically, ensuring the embedding is framed, meaning the normal bundle (Sk)\nu(S^k)(Sk) is trivialized. This construction preserves the orientability and dimension of the original manifold while modifying its homotopy type, particularly by killing the homotopy class represented by the embedded sphere.
For the surgery to be well-defined in the smooth or PL category, the normal bundle (Sk)\nu(S^k)(Sk) must be trivial, which is guaranteed in high dimensions by the existence of framings for spheres in stable range, and the manifold dimension satisfies n5n \geq 5n5 to allow the Whitney trick for disentangling intersections. This codimension condition prevents singularities in the attachment and enables the operation to be performed without altering the fundamental group or lower-dimensional topology unexpectedly.

Formally, an nnn-dimensional kkk-handle is the product DkDnkD^k \times D^{n-k}DkDnk, attached to the boundary via an embedding of its "attaching region" Sk1DnkS^{k-1} \times D^{n-k}Sk1Dnk. Basic Examples

One of the simplest illustrations of the surgery operation occurs in dimension 3, where an embedded circle S1S^1S1 (an unknot) in the 3-sphere S3S^3S3 serves as the submanifold for excision. Removing a tubular neighborhood of this S1S^1S1, which is diffeomorphic to a solid torus S1D2S^1 \times D^2S1D2, leaves another solid torus as the complement. Gluing back a solid torus via a specific framingknown as 0-surgery, where the meridian of the new solid torus is attached to the longitude of the excised neighborhoodyields the 3-manifold S1S2S^1 \times S^2S1S2. This operation demonstrates how surgery can alter the fundamental group from trivial (as in S3S^3S3) to Z\mathbb{Z}Z, while preserving the homology in higher degrees.
Performing the same 0-surgery simultaneously on two disjoint unknotted embeddings of S1S^1S1 in S3S^3S3 produces the connected sum (S1S2)#(S1S2)(S^1 \times S^2) \# (S^1 \times S^2)(S1S2)#(S1S2), as the surgeries act independently on disjoint neighborhoods. This example highlights the additive nature of surgery under connected sums, transforming the simply connected S3S^3S3 into a manifold with fundamental group ZZ\mathbb{Z} * \mathbb{Z}ZZ and Betti number b1=2b_1 = 2b1=2. In both cases, the boundary tori from the handle attachments (referencing the cobordism structure) play a key role in the gluing, but the resulting closed manifolds exhibit distinct toroidal factors absent in the original S3S^3S3.
In contrast, surgery with framing 1\pm 11 on a non-trivial knot in S3S^3S3 often produces a homology 3-sphere, a manifold with the same homology as S3S^3S3 but potentially non-trivial fundamental group. For instance, +1 surgery on the right-handed trefoil knota (2,3)-torus knotyields the Poincar homology sphere, first described by Henri Poincar in 1904 as the boundary of a dodecahedral orbifold. This manifold has fundamental group the binary icosahedral group of order 120 and is a classic example of a homology sphere that is not diffeomorphic to S3S^3S3, illustrating how surgery can introduce finite non-abelian fundamental groups while preserving integral homology. Michel Kervaire provided early constructions of such homology spheres via successive surgeries: starting from a connected sum of copies of S1Sn1S^1 \times S^{n-1}S1Sn1 (for n>4n > 4n>4), embedding spheres to kill relators in a presentation, and performing additional 2-surgeries on S2Dn2S^2 \times D^{n-2}S2Dn2 to achieve vanishing homology in dimensions 1 and 2. These examples underscore surgery's role in generating exotic homotopy types from standard manifolds.
A notable dimension-specific illustration arises in 4-manifold topology, where surgery along an embedded RP2\mathbb{RP}^2RP2 (real projective plane) in CP2\mathbb{CP}^2CP2 (complex projective plane) reveals challenges unique to dimension 4, such as obstructions to smooth structures and alterations to the intersection form. The embedding of RP2\mathbb{RP}^2RP2 in CP2\mathbb{CP}^2CP2 has a twisted normal bundle, and performing 2-surgeryexcising RP2D2\mathbb{RP}^2 \times D^2RP2D2 and gluing in D3S1D^3 \times S^1D3S1can produce manifolds with altered Kirby-Siebenmann invariants, potentially non-smoothable in the topological category. This operation, akin to twisting along the RP2\mathbb{RP}^2RP2, modifies the second Stiefel-Whitney class and highlights how middle-dimensional surgeries in 4-manifolds can lead to exotic phenomena, like failure of the smooth h-cobordism theorem, unlike in higher even dimensions where such issues diminish.

Comparison to CW-Cell Attachments

In CW-complex theory, attaching an n-cell to a space X involves specifying a map from the boundary sphere $ S^{n-1} $ to X and gluing the disk $ D^n $ along this attaching map, which effectively kills the homotopy class represented by that map in $ \pi_{n-1}(X) $, thereby modifying the homotopy type of the resulting complex. This process builds CW-complexes inductively, preserving cellular structure while altering fundamental groups and higher homotopy groups in a controlled algebraic manner.
Surgery on manifolds provides a smooth analog to this cell attachment mechanism, where an n-surgery on an m-manifold M (with m > n + 1) removes the interior of an embedded disk bundle $ S^{n-1} \times D^{m-n+1} $ and attaches a handle $ D^n \times D^{m-n+1} $ along the boundary, mimicking the attachment of an n-cell while ensuring the result remains a manifold. The homotopy effect parallels that of CW-attachment: the surgery kills the class in $ \pi_{n-1}(M) $ represented by the attaching sphere, quotienting the homotopy group accordingly, much like the reduction observed in cell attachments. This analogy extends to the dual operation, where surgery detaches a complementary cell to restore Poincar duality, unlike pure CW-attachments which do not inherently preserve such geometric duality.
Despite these similarities in homotopy modificationboth processes aim to achieve homotopy equivalences by targeted alterationsthe frameworks differ fundamentally in their categorical settings. CW-cell attachments operate in the combinatorial or piecewise-linear (PL) category, applicable to general topological spaces without embedding obstructions, whereas surgery resides in the smooth (differentiable) category for manifolds, necessitating the existence of smoothly embedded spheres with trivial normal bundles to ensure the handle attachment yields a smooth manifold. In low dimensions, smooth embeddings may fail due to knotting phenomena, but the Haefliger-Weber theorem guarantees that for embeddings of k-spheres in $ \mathbb{R}^m $ with 2m 3k + 3 (the metastable range), such embeddings exist up to isotopy, enabling surgery in sufficiently high dimensions without smoothing obstructions that plague CW methods in the smooth realm.
J.H.C. Whitehead's development of simple homotopy theory in the 1950s bridges these approaches, establishing that h-cobordismscobordisms where the inclusions of the boundary manifolds induce homotopy equivalencescan be analyzed via Whitehead torsion, a measure of deviation from simple homotopy equivalence that links manifold surgeries to CW-complex deformations. This connection underscores how surgery refines CW-attachment ideas geometrically, with trivial torsion implying the cobordism is diffeomorphic to a product, thus preserving the homotopy type across categories.

Classification of Manifolds

Overview of the Surgery Program

The surgery program in topology seeks to classify smooth or topological manifolds up to homotopy equivalence or diffeomorphism by systematically modifying a given homotopy equivalence f:MNf: M \to Nf:MN between closed nnn-manifolds into a diffeomorphism or homeomorphism through controlled geometric alterations. The core strategy begins by approximating fff with a normal map, which is a degree-one map equipped with a bundle monomorphism from the stable normal bundle of MMM to that of NNN, ensuring the map respects the embedding in Euclidean space up to homotopy. Subsequent steps involve performing surgeriesprecise excisions and gluings of handlebodies along embedded spheresto successively kill elements in the relative homotopy groups i(f)\pi_i(f)i(f) for i<n/2i < n/2i<n/2, reducing obstructions until the map becomes a homotopy equivalence between simply connected manifolds. Finally, the h-cobordism theorem (for topological category) or s-cobordism theorem (for smooth category) is invoked to confirm whether the resulting homotopy equivalence is isotopic to a diffeomorphism or homeomorphism, provided no further obstructions arise.
This program was initiated by John Milnor in his 1961 paper, where he introduced the basic surgery technique to kill homotopy groups in differentiable manifolds, laying the groundwork for altering manifold structures while preserving homology. It was rapidly developed in the 1960s by Michel Kervaire, C.T.C. Wall, and Dennis Sullivan, who extended the method to classify simply connected manifolds in high dimensions, incorporating algebraic invariants to measure the success of these modifications. Their contributions, building on Milnor's ideas, established surgery as a primary tool for resolving the Hauptvermutung and related classification problems in dimensions greater than or equal to 5.
The program succeeds in dimensions n5n \geq 5n5, where the stable range for homotopy groups and the h/s-cobordism theorems apply effectively, allowing complete classification up to diffeomorphism or homeomorphism for simply connected cases. However, in dimension 4, significant challenges persist due to the existence of exotic smooth structures on manifolds like CP2#CP2\mathbb{CP}^2 \# \overline{\mathbb{CP}^2}CP2#CP2, as demonstrated by Simon Donaldson's gauge-theoretic constructions in the 1980s, which reveal that smooth and topological categories diverge dramatically. These exotic phenomena, arising from Yang-Mills invariants, indicate that the surgery program remains incomplete for 4-manifolds, motivating ongoing research into gauge theory and symplectic geometry.

Key Theorems and Obstructions

The h-cobordism theorem, established by Stephen Smale in 1962, asserts that if two simply-connected smooth closed n-manifolds with n 5 are h-cobordant via a compact smooth cobordism, then the cobordism is diffeomorphic to the product of one manifold with the interval . This result forms a cornerstone of high-dimensional manifold classification, implying the generalized Poincar conjecture in dimensions greater than four.
The s-cobordism theorem, developed by Dennis Barden, Barry Mazur, and John Stallings around 1964-1965, extends the h-cobordism theorem to non-simply-connected cases by incorporating Whitehead torsion invariants; specifically, an s-cobordism between manifolds of dimension at least 6 is a product if and only if the torsion vanishes. This generalization, building on the h-cobordism framework, enables the study of homotopy equivalences up to diffeomorphism in broader settings.
In surgery theory, primary obstructions to realizing homotopy equivalences as diffeomorphisms lie in the algebraic L-groups L_n(), where for even dimensions they are captured by the signature invariant and for odd dimensions by the Arf invariant. These invariants, rooted in quadratic forms over rings with involution, detect whether a normal map can be surgically modified to a homotopy equivalence. For non-simply-connected manifolds, secondary obstructions arise in the algebraic K-group SK_1(), measuring deviations from stable isomorphism in the fundamental group ring.
Surgery theory remains incomplete in dimension 4, where Michael Freedman's 1982 classification of topological 4-manifolds by their intersection form and Kirby-Siebenmann invariant succeeds, but smooth counterparts fail due to exotic phenomena. As of 2025, questions about exotic smooth structures on ^4 persist, with infinitely many known but unresolved issues regarding their diffeomorphism types and relations to the smooth Poincar conjecture.
Rokhlin's theorem from 1952 demonstrates that no closed smooth spin 4-manifold has signature congruent to 8 modulo 16, implying signature divisible by 16 and obstructing certain surgeries that would produce such manifolds. This invariant provides a fundamental barrier in low-dimensional surgery, linking topological and analytic constraints.
