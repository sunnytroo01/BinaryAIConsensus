Algebraically closed field

 In mathematics, an algebraically closed field is a field $ F $ such that every non-constant polynomial with coefficients in $ F $ has at least one root in $ F $. This property is equivalent to the condition that every non-constant polynomial in $ F $ factors completely into a product of linear factors over $ F $.
Prominent examples include the field of complex numbers $ \mathbb{C} $, which is algebraically closed by the fundamental theorem of algebra stating that every non-constant polynomial with complex coefficients splits into linear factors over $ \mathbb{C} $. Another example is the field of algebraic numbers, the algebraic closure of the rational numbers $ \mathbb{Q} $, consisting of all complex numbers algebraic over $ \mathbb{Q} $.
For any field $ K $, there exists an algebraic closure, defined as an algebraic extension $ \overline{K} $ of $ K $ that is itself algebraically closed. Such an algebraic closure is unique up to isomorphism as a field extension of $ K $.
Algebraically closed fields play a central role in abstract algebra, particularly in Galois theory, where they serve as the ambient setting for studying splitting fields and Galois groups. In algebraic geometry, working over an algebraically closed field simplifies the study of varieties, enabling key results like Hilbert's Nullstellensatz, which establishes a correspondence between radical ideals in the polynomial ring and algebraic varieties.

Definition and Motivation

Definition

In mathematics, a field $ F $ is defined to be algebraically closed if every non-constant polynomial in the polynomial ring $ F $ has at least one root in $ F $. Formally, for any polynomial $ p(x) \in F $ of degree $ n \geq 1 $, there exists $ \alpha \in F $ such that $ p(\alpha) = 0 $. This property ensures that $ F $ contains solutions to all its own polynomial equations within the field itself.
An equivalent characterization is that $ F $ is algebraically closed if and only if every non-constant polynomial in $ F $ factors completely into linear factors over $ F $. As a direct consequence, the only irreducible polynomials in $ F $ are the linear ones (of degree 1), up to multiplication by units in $ F $.
Since the polynomial ring $ F $ over any field $ F $ is a Euclidean domainand hence a unique factorization domainthis implies that every element of $ F $ admits a unique factorization into irreducible elements, all of which are linear polynomials in the algebraically closed case. Thus, non-constant polynomials factor uniquely (up to units and ordering of factors) as products of linear terms, reflecting the complete solvability of polynomial equations over $ F $.

Historical Motivation

The study of algebraically closed fields emerged in the 19th century as mathematicians sought to understand the solvability of polynomial equations over different number systems, driven by the fundamental theorem of algebra. This theorem asserts that every non-constant polynomial with complex coefficients has at least one complex root, thereby establishing the complex numbers as a field where all such polynomials factor completely into linear terms. Carl Friedrich Gauss demonstrated the fundamental theorem of algebra in his 1799 doctoral dissertation at the University of Helmstedt, providing a geometric proof using the intermediate value theorem that, while groundbreaking, contained gaps by modern standards. He later offered additional proofs in 1816 (two versions) and 1849 to address these issues.
The motivation intensified with variste Galois's groundbreaking work in the 1830s, which linked the solvability of polynomials by radicals to the structure of field extensions, highlighting the need for fields where polynomials could be fully resolved without adjoining extraneous roots. Galois's theory demonstrated that certain quintic equations resist radical solutions due to the Galois group of their splitting fields, prompting deeper inquiry into extensions that "close" under root extraction. This framework underscored the desire for a universal field property ensuring all polynomials split completely, influencing subsequent efforts to generalize beyond the complexes.
In the early 20th century, the transition to abstract algebra formalized these ideas, with Ernst Steinitz's seminal 1910 paper "Algebraische Theorie der Krper" providing the first axiomatic treatment of fields and proving that every field admits an algebraic closurean extension that is algebraically closed and unique up to isomorphism over the base field. Steinitz's construction relied on transfinite induction to adjoin roots iteratively, marking a pivotal shift from concrete number fields to abstract structures. Concurrently, Emmy Noether advanced the formalization through her work on ring and ideal theory, such as her 1921 paper on primary decomposition in commutative rings, which provided tools to analyze field extensions within broader algebraic contexts.
The term "algebraically closed field" gained prominence in the 1920s and 1930s amid developments in algebraic geometry and number theory, as seen in Bartel van der Waerden's 1930 textbook Moderne Algebra, which synthesized these concepts for a new generation of mathematicians. This period solidified the notion's role in studying polynomial factorization universally, bridging classical equation-solving with modern abstract methods.

Algebraic Closures

The algebraic closure of a field $ K $, denoted $ \overline{K} $, is defined as the smallest algebraically closed field that contains $ K $ as a subfield; equivalently, it is the algebraic field extension of $ K $ in which every polynomial in $ K $ splits into linear factors. This extension is algebraic over $ K $, meaning that every element $ \alpha \in \overline{K} $ satisfies a polynomial equation with coefficients in $ K $. In particular, for any such $ \alpha $, the degree of the extension $ $ is finite, and the minimal polynomial of $ \alpha $ over $ K $ splits completely into linear factors within $ \overline{K} $.
Key properties of $ \overline{K} $ include its infinitude unless $ K $ is already algebraically closed, as adjoining roots iteratively extends beyond any finite degree if necessary. Moreover, any two algebraic closures of $ K $ are isomorphic as field extensions over $ K $, ensuring a canonical structure up to this equivalence. For any field $ K $, an algebraic closure exists and can be constructed as the direct limit of the directed system of all finite algebraic extensions of $ K $, or alternatively by first adjoining a transcendence basis to form a purely transcendental extension and then taking its algebraic closure.
A concrete example is the algebraic closure of the rational numbers $ \mathbb{Q} $, denoted $ \overline{\mathbb{Q}} $, which comprises all algebraic numbersroots of non-constant polynomials with rational coefficientsand forms a countable field of characteristic zero. This field serves as a universal algebraic extension of $ \mathbb{Q} $, embedding into the complex numbers while remaining strictly smaller.

Absence in Finite Fields

Finite fields, which are fields with a finite number of elements, cannot be algebraically closed. Suppose $ F_q $ is a finite field with $ q = p^n $ elements, where $ p $ is prime and $ n \geq 1 $. If $ F_q $ were algebraically closed, every non-constant polynomial in $ F_q $ would factor completely into linear factors over $ F_q $, implying that $ F_q $ has no proper algebraic extensions. However, it is a well-established result that every finite field admits proper algebraic extensions of every positive integer degree $ m $; specifically, there exists a unique (up to isomorphism) extension $ F_{q^m} $ of degree $ m $ over $ F_q $, which properly contains $ F_q $ for $ m > 1 $. This contradicts the assumption, as the algebraic closure of $ F_q $ would be an infinite union of these finite extensions, properly extending $ F_q $.
The existence of such proper extensions stems from the presence of irreducible polynomials of every degree greater than 1 in $ F_q $. For instance, the polynomial $ x^q - x $ factors completely over $ F_q $ as $ x^q - x = \prod_{a \in F_q} (x - a) $, accounting for all elements of $ F_q $ as roots. Yet, the ring $ F_q $ contains monic irreducible polynomials of each degree $ d \geq 2 $, whose roots generate the extensions $ F_{q^d} $. The number of such irreducibles of degree $ d $ is given by $ \frac{1}{d} \sum_{e \mid d} \mu(e) q^{d/e} > 0 $, ensuring their existence.
In the specific case of characteristic $ p $, no finite field satisfies the condition that polynomials like the Artin-Schreier form $ x^p - x - a $ factor completely into linears without roots outside the field for all $ a $. For appropriate choices of $ a \in F_q $ (where $ q $ is not a power of $ p $ in certain cases, but generally in char $ p $), $ x^p - x - a $ is irreducible over $ F_q $, generating a proper extension of degree $ p $. This irreducibility follows from the fact that if it had a root $ \alpha \in F_q $, then all translates $ \alpha + i $ for $ i = 1, \dots, p-1 $ would also be roots, implying complete splitting, but for suitable $ a $, no roots exist in $ F_q $.
As a consequence, all algebraically closed fields of characteristic $ p $ must be infinite. The algebraic closure $ \overline{\mathbb{F}_p} $ of the prime field $ \mathbb{F}p $ is a countable infinite union of the finite fields $ \mathbb{F}{p^k} $ for $ k \geq 1 $.

Equivalent Characterizations

Polynomial Factorization Properties

A field FFF is algebraically closed if and only if every nonconstant polynomial in FFF has a root in FFF. This condition implies that the splitting field of any polynomial over FFF is FFF itself, as all roots lie within FFF.
An equivalent characterization is that the only irreducible polynomials in FFF are the linear ones. If there existed an irreducible polynomial of degree greater than 1, adjoining a root would yield a proper algebraic extension of FFF, contradicting algebraic closedness. Conversely, if all irreducibles are linear, every nonconstant polynomial factors into linears, ensuring all roots are in FFF.
Another equivalent condition is that every polynomial of prime degree in FFF has a root in FFF. A polynomial of prime degree is either irreducible or has a linear factor; irreducibility would imply a proper extension, which cannot occur in an algebraically closed field. This prevents the existence of irreducibles of prime degree and extends to all degrees by composition.
In an algebraically closed field FFF, the polynomial ring FFF is a unique factorization domain where every nonconstant polynomial factors uniquely (up to units and ordering) into linear factors, accounting for multiplicities. Specifically, for any p(x)Fp(x) \in Fp(x)F of degree n1n \geq 1n1,

Field Extension Properties

A field $ F $ is algebraically closed if and only if it has no proper algebraic extensions, meaning that every algebraic extension $ E $ of $ F $ satisfies $ E = F $. This characterization implies that every element algebraic over $ F $ already belongs to $ F $, as adjoining any such element outside $ F $ would yield a proper algebraic extension.
Equivalently, an algebraically closed field $ F $ admits no proper finite field extensions, since all finite extensions are algebraic./21%3A_Fields/21.01%3A_Extension_Fields) As a consequence, any proper extension of $ F $ must be transcendental, involving elements not algebraic over $ F $, and $ F $ is maximal among algebraic fields in the sense that no larger field containing $ F $ can be algebraic over it.
This extension property is closely tied to the factorization of polynomials over $ F $: $ F $ is algebraically closed if and only if every irreducible polynomial in $ F $ splits completely into linear factors in $ F $. To see the connection to extensions, suppose toward contradiction that $ L/F $ is a proper algebraic extension. Let $ \alpha \in L \setminus F $; then the minimal polynomial $ m(x) $ of $ \alpha $ over $ F $ is irreducible of degree at least 2. However, since $ F $ is algebraically closed, $ m(x) $ must split into linear factors in $ F $, contradicting the irreducibility of $ m(x) $ unless its degree is 1 (which would imply $ \alpha \in F $). Thus, no such proper algebraic extension exists, and the simple extensions $ F(\alpha)/F $ for algebraic $ \alpha $ are trivial.
In fields of positive characteristic $ p $, algebraically closed fields have the additional property that all purely inseparable extensions are trivial. This follows because such fields are perfect, meaning every element of $ F $ is a $ p $-th power in $ F $, precluding nontrivial purely inseparable extensions of degree $ p^k > 1 $ for $ k \geq 1 $.

Constructions

Existence of Algebraic Closures

The existence of an algebraic closure for every field is a fundamental result in field theory. For any field KKK, an algebraic closure K\overline{K}K is an algebraic field extension of KKK that is itself algebraically closed, satisfying KKK \subseteq \overline{K}KK and the property that every K\alpha \in \overline{K}K is algebraic over KKK, meaning \alpha satisfies a non-constant polynomial equation with coefficients in KKK.
This existence was established by Ernst Steinitz in 1910, who proved that every field KKK admits an algebraic closure K\overline{K}K, unique up to isomorphism over KKK. Steinitz's theorem provides the cornerstone for studying algebraic extensions, ensuring that such closures exist without relying on specific constructions for particular fields.
One standard proof of existence uses Zorn's lemma from set theory. Consider the set E\mathcal{E}E of all algebraic field extensions of KKK, partially ordered by inclusion. This poset is nonempty (as KKK itself is in E\mathcal{E}E) and inductive, since the union of any chain of algebraic extensions is again an algebraic extension of KKK. By Zorn's lemma, E\mathcal{E}E has a maximal element LLL, which must be algebraically closed: if not, adjoining a root of some irreducible polynomial over LLL would yield a strictly larger algebraic extension, contradicting maximality.
An alternative, more constructive proof avoids full reliance on Zorn's lemma by building the closure via transfinite induction or direct limits. Enumerate all monic irreducible polynomials over KKK as {f<}\{f_\alpha \mid \alpha < \kappa\}{f<}, where \kappa is the cardinality of KKK. Start with K0=KK_0 = KK0=K, and at each successor stage +1\beta + 1+1, adjoin all roots of ff_\betaf to KK_\betaK to form K+1K_{\beta+1}K+1; at limit ordinals, take the direct limit. The resulting field at stage \kappa is an algebraic closure of KKK. This approach uses the axiom of choice only for well-ordering but provides an explicit iterative construction.
The proofs must account for the characteristic of the field. In characteristic 0, all algebraic extensions are separable, so the above constructions directly yield the algebraic closure. In characteristic p>0p > 0p>0, inseparable extensions may arise, so first form the perfect closure K1/pK^{1/p^\infty}K1/p by iteratively adjoining ppp-th roots of all elements until every element has a ppp-th root; this perfect field has the same algebraic closure as KKK, reducing the problem to the perfect case where extensions are separable.
Specific examples illustrate cardinality aspects: the algebraic closure Q\overline{\mathbb{Q}}Q of the rationals Q\mathbb{Q}Q is countable, as it is the union of the splitting fields of all countably many polynomials over Q\mathbb{Q}Q, each of finite degree. In contrast, the algebraic closure R\overline{\mathbb{R}}R of the reals R\mathbb{R}R is the complex numbers C\mathbb{C}C, which is uncountable.

Uniqueness up to Isomorphism

An algebraically closed field LLL is called an algebraic closure of a field KKK if LLL is algebraic over KKK. If LLL and MMM are algebraic closures of the same field KKK, then there exists a KKK-isomorphism LML \cong MLM. This uniqueness up to isomorphism over KKK was established by Steinitz in 1910.
The proof proceeds by showing that any two such closures are isomorphic over KKK. Since both LLL and MMM are algebraically closed and algebraic over KKK, there exists a KKK-embedding i:LMi: L \hookrightarrow Mi:LM. As MMM is algebraically closed, and LLL algebraic over KKK implies no proper algebraic extensions within MMM, the embedding iii must be surjective, hence an isomorphism. The non-uniqueness of the isomorphism arises from the possible KKK-automorphisms of MMM.
A key implication of this uniqueness is that all algebraic closures of KKK share the same absolute Galois group up to isomorphism, defined as Gal(Ksep/K)\mathrm{Gal}(K^\mathrm{sep}/K)Gal(Ksep/K), where KsepK^\mathrm{sep}Ksep is the separable closure of KKK contained in any algebraic closure. This group captures the non-uniqueness of the isomorphisms between closures.
While the full uniqueness theorem relies on Zorn's lemma (or the axiom of choice), separable closures in characteristic zero are unique up to isomorphism without choice, as separability coincides with algebraicity there; in general, however, choice is necessary. In characteristic p>0p > 0p>0, ensuring the closure is perfect (i.e., every element has a ppp-th root) guarantees the isomorphism property holds uniformly.
For a concrete example, all algebraic closures of the function field Fp(t)\mathbb{F}_p(t)Fp(t) over Fp\mathbb{F}_pFp are isomorphic over Fp(t)\mathbb{F}_p(t)Fp(t), reflecting the general theorem despite the transcendental nature of the base field.
More generally, any two algebraically closed fields of the same characteristic and the same transcendence degree over their prime subfields are isomorphic. This follows from mapping transcendence bases of the same cardinality to establish an isomorphism between the corresponding purely transcendental extensions over the prime subfield, then extending to the algebraic closures using the uniqueness of algebraic closures up to isomorphism. This generalizes the uniqueness for algebraic closures of a fixed field.

Coprime Polynomial Behaviors

In an algebraically closed field FFF, two polynomials f,gFf, g \in Ff,gF are coprime, meaning their greatest common divisor is 1, if and only if they share no common roots in FFF. This follows from the fact that every irreducible polynomial in FFF is linear, so any common root would imply a shared linear factor, contradicting coprimeness.
The ideal generated by coprime polynomials fff and ggg, denoted (f,g)(f, g)(f,g), equals the unit ideal (1)(1)(1) in FFF, which implies that the set of common zeros of fff and ggg is empty. This is a direct consequence of the weak form of Hilbert's Nullstellensatz, which states that over an algebraically closed field, a proper ideal corresponds to a nonempty affine variety, so the unit ideal corresponds to the empty variety.
