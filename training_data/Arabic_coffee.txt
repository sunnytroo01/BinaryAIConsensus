Arabic coffee
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Arabic coffee, known as qahwa or gahwa, is a traditional hot beverage central to Arab hospitality and social customs, prepared by lightly roasting coffee beans to a reddish-brown color, coarsely grinding them, and boiling the grounds in water often infused with spices such as cardamom, without added sugar for a bitter, aromatic profile. Originating in 15th-century Yemen, where Sufi mystics first brewed it to aid prolonged nighttime prayers and spiritual focus, qahwa spread across the Arabian Peninsula and beyond, evolving into a ritualistic drink that embodies generosity, respect, and communal bonding.
This unfiltered coffee is typically served from a distinctive long-spouted pot called a dallah, poured into small, handleless cups known as finjan, with the host using the right hand to offer it first to elders and guests in settings like the majlistraditional gathering spaces for conversation, poetry recitation, and conflict resolution. The serving ritual emphasizes etiquette: portions are small to encourage multiple refills, typically up to three, and drinkers gently shake their cup from side to side to signal they have had enough. In 2015, qahwa was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity by Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, Oman, and Qatar, recognizing its role in fostering social cohesion, cultural identity, and peaceful interactions across generations and communities. Beyond daily greetings and family meals, it features prominently in weddings, funerals, and tribal assemblies, often accompanied by dates or sweets to balance its robust flavor, underscoring its enduring status as a vessel for Arab values of warmth and unity.

Origins

Etymology

The term "qahwa," the Arabic word for coffee, derives from the Semitic root q-h-w, linked to the verb qahiya, meaning "to have no appetite," as the beverage was believed to suppress hunger and act as an appetite suppressant. Originally, in classical Arabic, "qahwa" referred to wine or any dark-colored intoxicating brew, a usage attested in pre-Islamic poetry and early lexicographical works, before being repurposed for the coffee infusion by Sufi mystics in Yemen during the 15th century. This shift highlighted coffee's role as a non-alcoholic alternative that induced alertness without inebriation, aligning with Islamic prohibitions on alcohol.
The earliest surviving written references to "qahwa" as coffee appear in 16th-century Arabic literature, notably in the treatise 'Umdat al-safwa fi hill al-qahwa (The Reliance of the Righteous on the Legality of Coffee) by the Yemeni scholar Abd al-Qadir al-Jaziri, who chronicled its introduction, medicinal uses, and the theological debates surrounding its permissibility in Islamic law. Al-Jaziri's work, drawing on earlier oral traditions among Sufis, describes how the term was adopted for the boiled coffee beverage to distinguish it from other concoctions, marking a pivotal moment in its nomenclature within Arabic scholarly discourse.
As coffee spread through trade routes, "qahwa" influenced European languages via Ottoman Turkish "kahve," which entered Italian as "caff" in the early 17th century and subsequently Dutch "koffie" and English "coffee" around 1600, retaining the Arabic phonetic core while adapting to local pronunciations. In regional Arabic dialects, the term evolved with qualifiers like "qahwa sada" (plain or unsweetened coffee), denoting the traditional bitter preparation served in hospitality rituals across the Arabian Peninsula and Levant, without additives like sugar or milk.

Historical Development

The origins of Arabic coffee trace back to Yemen in the 15th century, where Sufi monks first cultivated and domesticated the coffee plant, originally from Ethiopia, to brew a beverage that helped them stay awake during extended night prayers and religious rituals. This practice transformed coffee from a wild forest product into a cultivated crop, with Yemen becoming the center of its early production and consumption in the Arab world.
By the 16th century, coffee spread rapidly from Yemen through Islamic trade routes and the expanding Ottoman Empire, reaching the Levant, North Africa, and other regions as pilgrims, merchants, and Sufi orders carried the custom during travels and conquests. A notable early controversy arose in 1511 when religious authorities in Mecca banned coffee consumption, viewing it as an intoxicant that fostered unauthorized gatherings; coffee stocks were seized and burned, but the prohibition was short-lived, overturned within a year, paving the way for the legalization and proliferation of coffeehouses across the Ottoman territories.
In 2015, UNESCO inscribed Arabic coffee on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity, recognizing it as a symbol of hospitality and generosity shared across Arab societies from the United Arab Emirates, Saudi Arabia, Oman, and Qatar; the inscription was extended in 2024 to include Jordan. The inscription met criteria for community participation in its transmission, its viability through ongoing social practices, and the existence of safeguarding measures like cultural education programs, highlighting its role in fostering intercultural dialogue and social cohesion.
Post-colonial revivals in the late 20th and early 21st centuries have seen Arabic coffee regain prominence in Arab nations, with initiatives in Saudi Arabia and the Gulf states promoting traditional gahwa preparation amid modernization, leading to economic boosts through heritage tourism and exports by the 2020s. Global recognition has grown, evidenced by expanded UNESCO protections and international festivals celebrating its cultural significance up to 2025.

Cultivation and Production

Coffee Growing Regions

Yemen serves as the historical birthplace of coffee cultivation in the Arab world, where the crop was first domesticated from Ethiopian origins and remains the primary growing region today. The Haraz Mountains in the northwest, along with areas like Sa'ada and Sana'a governorates, feature high-altitude terraces that support small-scale farming at elevations reaching up to 2,400 meters. These terraced landscapes, carved into steep slopes, enable the production of renowned heirloom varieties that define Arabic coffee.
In Saudi Arabia, coffee production occurs on small-scale farms concentrated in the southwestern highlands, particularly in the Jazan, Asir, Al-Baha, and Najran regions, yielding over 800 tons annually from approximately 500,000 Arabica trees as of 2025. The Saudi Reef Program aims to boost production to 7,000 tons annually by increasing self-sufficiency. Emerging cultivation efforts are also underway in Oman, focused on the Dhofar region's monsoon-influenced terrains and highland areas like Jabal Shams, where the first coffee farm launched in 2025 aims to plant 6,000 seedlings by 2027 to establish high-quality Arabica production. The United Arab Emirates supports limited farming through innovative greenhouses and shade-grown projects, such as the Elite Agro Projects greenhouse, though the country relies heavily on imports. Ethiopia exerts significant influence on Arab coffee supplies, exporting substantial volumesvalued at $304 million to Saudi Arabia alone in 2023to meet regional demand.
Arabic coffee predominantly features Arabica beans, with Yemen's unique heirloom varieties, such as the celebrated Mocha, thriving in tropical highland conditions between 1,000 and 2,000 meters. These varieties require well-drained, fertile, slightly acidic soils and consistent moisture, ideally from 1,000 millimeters of annual rainfall, though Yemeni regions often receive only 200 to 350 millimeters, necessitating traditional irrigation systems. Shade-grown practices under native trees are common, promoting biodiversity and protecting plants from intense sun.
Cultivation faces significant challenges, including acute water scarcity exacerbated by depleting groundwater and erratic rainfall patterns. As of 2025, climate change has intensified impacts on yields through rising temperatures, soil degradation, and increased pest prevalence, threatening traditional farming in Yemen and Saudi Arabia. In response, producers are shifting toward sustainable shade-grown methods to enhance resilience and mitigate environmental pressures.

Harvesting and Processing

In Yemen, the primary region for Arabic coffee cultivation in highland areas, coffee cherries are harvested by hand-picking from October to December to ensure only ripe fruit is selected, preserving the beans' quality and flavor profile. This selective method involves workers returning to the same trees multiple times during the season, as cherries ripen unevenly on the Coffea arabica plants grown at elevations between 1,000 and 2,400 meters.
Following harvest, Yemeni coffee undergoes a traditional dry processing method, where whole cherries are sun-dried on rooftops or raised mats for several weeks, allowing the fruit to naturally ferment and impart earthy, fruity notes to the beans. In contrast, other Arab regions with limited local production, such as parts of Saudi Arabia's Asir highlands, employ dry processing techniques, resulting in robust, traditional flavors.
Once processed, the green beans for Arabic coffee are typically roasted lightly to medium in small batches at home or by local roasters using shallow pans over open flames, which helps retain the beans' aromatic compounds. Cardamom is commonly infused during the grinding stage post-roasting, creating a spiced blend that is central to Arabic coffee's distinctive taste.
Yemen's annual coffee production stands at approximately 30,000 metric tons as of 2023, with the majority consumed locally and only a small portion exported due to infrastructural challenges and high domestic demand.

Preparation Techniques

Arabian Peninsula Methods

In the Arabian Peninsula, traditional Arabic coffee, known as qahwa, is prepared through a simple yet ritualistic boiling method that emphasizes the natural flavors of lightly to medium-roasted coffee beans infused with aromatic spices such as cardamom (primary), saffron, cloves, and occasionally rose water, without any filtering to remove the grounds. The core recipe involves coarsely ground coffee, water, and cardamom as the primary spice, with common additions of saffron and cloves, and occasional use of rose water for floral notes, resulting in a bitter, full-bodied brew served hot. This unfiltered approach allows the coffee to retain a thick texture and earthy sediment, distinguishing it from filtered Western styles.
The preparation process begins with roasting green Arabica beans to a light or medium roast over an open fire or in a pan, achieving a cinnamon-like hue that preserves the beans' mild acidity and subtle sweetness. The roasted beans are then ground fresh into a coarse powder using a mortar and pestle, a step performed immediately before brewing to ensure optimal aroma and freshness. In a traditional dallah pottypically crafted from brass or copper with a curved spout and ornate handlewater is brought to a boil, after which 23 tablespoons of the ground coffee and 1 tablespoon of crushed cardamom pods (or ground cardamom) are added per 34 cups of water. The mixture is gently simmered and brought to a full boil three times without stirring, allowing the spices and coffee to infuse gradually over about 1520 minutes; between boils, it rests briefly off the heat to develop foam and depth. After the third boil, the dallah is removed from the heat and left undisturbed for 510 minutes, enabling the grounds to settle at the bottom.
The dallah's design facilitates precise pouring from a height of about 3050 cm into small, handleless porcelain or glass cups called finjan, which hold just 3050 ml to encourage sipping in small amounts and multiple refills as a gesture of hospitality. This pouring technique aerates the coffee slightly, enhancing its aroma without disturbing the settled grounds, and the brew is traditionally offered three times to guestsaccepting all three rounds signifies respect for the host. No sugar or milk is added, preserving the coffee's inherent bitterness and the spices' prominence.
Regional variations within the Peninsula reflect local tastes and resources. In Saudi Arabia, the coffee is often lightly roasted for a smoother profile and may incorporate a pinch of saffron threads during boiling to impart a golden hue and floral note, aligning with the emphasis on elegance in social rituals. Yemeni styles, by contrast, favor a slightly darker medium roast for bolder body and frequently include fresh or ground ginger alongside cardamom, valued for its warming and digestive medicinal properties in traditional Yemeni culture. Cloves may appear in both but are more common in Saudi preparations for their mild sweetness. In the United Arab Emirates, traditional qahwa may include saffron or rose water for floral notes. In contemporary settings across the UAE, including Dubai and Abu Dhabi, these traditional spices inspire modern beverages such as saffron and rose-infused lattes or cold brews, which emphasize natural flavors from real spices and infusions, in contrast to many syrup-based Western-style coffees. These differences highlight the Peninsula's shared yet adaptable brewing heritage, rooted in Yemen's historical role as coffee's origin.

Levantine Variations

In the Levant, which includes Syria, Lebanon, and Jordan, Arabic coffee preparation builds on the foundational boiling technique but incorporates finer grinds and subtler flavor profiles suited to local tastes. The coffee beans are ground to a very fine consistency, similar to powder, enabling a quick infusion that yields a smooth, bold brew without excessive sediment. Water is brought to a boil in a small pot, with the ground coffee and ground cardamom added, followed by a short simmering period of 5 to 15 minutes to extract flavors without prolonged bitterness; this contrasts with longer boils in other regions by emphasizing clarity and balance.
Optional enhancements like a dash of rosewater introduce delicate floral aromas, evoking the Levant's herbal traditions, while influences from neighboring Turkish coffee styles occasionally include a touch of milk for creaminess or a focus on thick foam. The foam, known as raha, is prized for its velvety texture and formed by gentle heating and pouring techniques that trap air in the brew. Preparation relies on a rakweh or jezveh, a narrow-necked pot akin to a cezve with a long handle for precise control over heat, paired with small demitasse cups called finjan or fenjein that hold just 50-60 ml to encourage mindful sipping and foam appreciation.
Regional nuances highlight hospitality's role: in Syria, sadaha plain, unsweetened versionsymbolizes genuine welcome and is offered to guests during social visits, underscoring simplicity and respect. Jordanian qahwa typically features a medium-roast blend spiced heavily with cardamom, often served alongside nuts like almonds or pistachios to offset its intensity, reflecting communal sharing in daily life. In Lebanon, the emphasis mirrors Turkish methods with multiple short boils to build foam, prioritizing the coffee's aromatic depth over sweetness.
Contemporary urban households in the Levant blend tradition with convenience, employing electric Arabic coffee makers or percolators that automate the boiling process while preserving the cardamom's essential pungency and foam formation for everyday use.

North African Adaptations

In North Africa, Arabic coffee adaptations emphasize sweetness and thickness, distinguishing them from lighter regional styles. In Morocco, qahwa is prepared using coarsely ground dark roast coffee beans boiled with generous amounts of sugar to create a rich, indulgent brew served very hot. The traditional process involves adding the grounds and sugar to boiling water in a plain pot, bringing the mixture to a boil multiple timestypically two or threeto extract bold flavors, then allowing it to settle for 5-10 minutes so the grounds sink before pouring. Unlike spice-heavy preparations elsewhere, cardamom is typically omitted, though small amounts of cinnamon or black pepper may be incorporated for subtle warmth and aroma.
Algerian and Tunisian variants build on this boiled method but incorporate lighter sugar levels for a less intense sweetness, often using about one tablespoon per serving compared to Morocco's heavier dosing. In Algeria, occasional additions like ground almonds or a touch of honey enhance the texture and nutty notes, while Tunisian preparations might include orange blossom water or honey for floral depth, reflecting local flavor preferences. These coffees are boiled in a zezoua potsimilar to a Turkish cezvethree times before settling, ensuring a thick consistency without filters.
These adaptations integrate with North African beverage traditions, where mint tea dominates daytime hospitality, but coffee is reserved for evenings to promote relaxation and conversation. This distinction underscores coffee's role in winding down after meals, influenced briefly by Ottoman trade routes that spread boiling techniques across the region.

Cultural Role

Social and Ceremonial Uses

Arabic coffee serves as a cornerstone of hospitality in Arab cultures, where offering it to guests embodies generosity and respect. Traditionally, it is served in small cups, often in three rounds: the first cup as a welcome to honor the guest's arrival, the second to facilitate social conversation and bonding, and the third as a gesture of farewell or ongoing protection, signaling the end of the immediate visit while affirming enduring goodwill. This ritual, prepared in a dallah pot and poured with the right hand, reinforces communal ties and is never refused as a sign of politeness.
In family and social contexts, Arabic coffee highlights distinct yet complementary gender roles. Women typically prepare it in home settings, passing down the knowledge through observation to younger family members, while men often handle its service in public spaces like coffeehouses, which historically served as male-dominated venues for discussion. It plays a role in family-oriented ceremonies, such as marriage proposals where coffee is presented alongside sweets during engagement meetings to symbolize harmony between families, and in tribal gatherings for dispute resolution, where sharing cups promotes reconciliation and peace.
Religiously, Arabic coffee holds significance in enhancing spiritual and communal practices. Among Sufi orders, it originated in Yemen in the 15th century to promote alertness during dhikr (remembrance of God) rituals, aiding prolonged worship and mystical experiences. During Ramadan, it is commonly consumed in the evenings after iftar to sustain energy for night prayers and family gatherings, fostering a sense of shared endurance and reflection.
As a cultural symbol, Arabic coffee represents community and connection in Arabic literature and oral traditions. It appears in proverbs like "A cup of coffee commits one to forty years of friendship," underscoring its role in forging lasting bonds, and in poetry, such as Al-Qadi's "Al-Qahwa," which celebrates its sensory and social allure as a unifying force in Arab identity.

Coffeehouses and Gatherings

The first coffeehouses, known as qahveh khaneh, emerged in the 16th century across the Islamic world, with notable establishments appearing in cities like Cairo and Damascus, where they served as vibrant centers for intellectual debate, storytelling, poetry recitations, and music performances among scholars, merchants, and artists. These venues transformed coffee from a Sufi ritual aid into a communal beverage, fostering egalitarian social interactions that transcended class boundaries in urban settings.
During the Ottoman era, coffeehouses faced periodic bans, such as the 1633 decree by Sultan Murad IV, who viewed them as breeding grounds for sedition and political dissent, punishable by execution to curb gatherings that challenged authority. Despite these suppressions, the institutions persisted and evolved into key hubs for political discourse by the 19th century, where patrons openly discussed governance, reform, and revolutionary ideas, laying the groundwork for broader social movements. In modern Gulf cities such as Dubai, Abu Dhabi, and Riyadh, contemporary coffeehouses blend traditional Arabic coffee culture with global trends and amenities such as shisha lounges and free Wi-Fi, attracting diverse crowds for extended socializing amid air-conditioned comfort. These venues offer Western-style flavored coffees such as caramel macchiato, vanilla latte, mocha, and hazelnut varieties alongside innovative fusions that incorporate traditional elements like cardamom, saffron, or rose in lattes, cold brews, and espressos. Natural flavors dominate traditional preparations via real spices and infusions, while artificial flavors may appear in commercial syrup-based drinks, reflecting the coexistence of heritage and modernity in the region's coffee scene.
Regional variations highlight the adaptability of these spaces: in Yemen, open-air majlis gatherings emphasize communal hospitality in village settings, where boiled Arabic coffee accompanies informal discussions and news-sharing under the stars. In contrast, Lebanese urban cafes in Beirut function as lively social venues, often doubling as discreet spots for young people to meet and date, reflecting the city's cosmopolitan vibe.
Coffeehouses have profoundly shaped Arab cultural life, serving as catalysts for pivotal events like the 2011 Arab Spring, where Egyptian cafes hosted activist planning sessions akin to their role in the 1919 Revolution, enabling anonymous exchanges of revolutionary ideas. As of 2025, they continue to facilitate daily male bonding rituals, particularly in Gulf societies, where men gather to sip coffee, smoke shisha, play games like backgammon, and strengthen ties through unhurried conversations that reinforce community and tradition.

Serving and Rituals

Traditional Serving Practices

Arabic coffee, known as qahwa, is traditionally served from a dallah pot into small, handleless finjan cups, with the server holding the dallah in the left hand and extending the cup with the right hand to present it to the guest. The pouring begins with the eldest or most distinguished guest and proceeds in a social order, often clockwise around the group, with each cup filled only one-third full to allow for the aromatic steam to rise. The pour is done slowly in a fine, continuous thread from a slight height to aerate the coffee without spilling, emphasizing precision and respect in the ritual.
Finjan cups are held by the guest using the thumb and forefinger, with the right hand only, as receiving or offering with the left is considered impolite. Guests typically receive up to three cups, symbolizing hospitality, and signal the end of serving by gently shaking the cup between the thumb and forefinger or saying "shukran" (thank you) after the third. Refusing the first cup is seen as disrespectful unless due to valid reasons like fasting, while stirring the coffee is prohibited to preserve its traditional bitterness and flavor profile.
To balance the coffee's bold, cardamom-infused bitterness, it is accompanied by dates or halwa sweets, which are offered alongside without altering the qahwa itselfno sugar or milk is added in authentic preparations. Hygiene is paramount in presentation; the dallah must be impeccably clean, and the host often tastes the first pour (fenjan el-heif) to verify quality before serving, ensuring the ritual reflects generosity and purity. This meticulous approach underscores the cultural emphasis on communal harmony during daily servings.

Occasions and Customs

In Arabic coffee traditions, tasseography involves interpreting the patterns formed by coffee grounds remaining in the cup after drinking, a practice used to divine omens about the future, love, or personal circumstances. This form of fortune-telling is particularly prevalent in Levantine countries like Lebanon, where the cup is divided into sectionsthe area near the handle signifying love life, the left side the present, and the bottom the home lifewith symbols such as birds indicating good news or snakes warning of deceit. In Gulf regions, similar readings occur during social gatherings, often by elders or women, blending entertainment with cultural insight into one's destiny.
During funerals and wakes, Arabic coffee is served bitter and unsweetened, known as qahwa sada or qahwa mar, to symbolize the endurance and bitterness of loss while consoling mourners through its warmth and communal sharing. This custom is observed in funeral houses across Arab societies, where it is often the primary beverage offered, reflecting solemnity and solidarity among family and tribe members.
For joyous occasions like weddings, Arabic coffee is prepared sweetened to evoke celebration and harmony, served abundantly to guests as a gesture of shared happiness and unity. During Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha, it is often spiced with cardamom, saffron, or clovesknown as qahwa helwato enhance festive aromas, accompanying family gatherings and reinforcing communal bonds through its aromatic presence.
Certain taboos govern the serving of Arabic coffee to maintain etiquette and respect cultural boundaries. Guests who overstay are subtly signaled to depart by adhering to the three-cup hospitality rule, where the first cup welcomes, the second offers pleasure, and the third concludes the visitrefusing a fourth indicates departure without offense. In mourning periods beyond immediate wakes, such as extended family grief observances, sweetened or spiced variations are typically withheld, with only bitter coffee provided to honor the somber mood and avoid frivolity.

Nutritional Profile

Ingredients and Composition

Arabic coffee is traditionally prepared using lightly roasted Coffea arabica beans, which constitute the primary flavor base and contain approximately 1.2% caffeine by dry weight. These beans are coarsely ground to facilitate brewing while retaining essential oils that contribute to the beverage's aromatic profile. Water serves as the solvent, typically in a ratio that yields a concentrated yet clear infusion, with no milk added in authentic recipes to preserve the pure, spiced character.
Cardamom (Elettaria cardamomum) is a staple spice, particularly in Arabian Peninsula preparations, where it is incorporated at a ratio of about 1 part ground cardamom to 3-4 parts coffee by volume for every 500 ml of water, imparting a subtle eucalyptus-like aroma and balancing the coffee's bitterness. The use of ground cardamom allows for quicker infusion compared to whole pods, which release flavors more gradually and may result in a milder spice note during short boiling times.
Regional additives enhance color, aroma, or sweetness without altering the core composition. In some Gulf traditions, a pinch of saffron (Crocus sativus)approximately 0.1 g per literis added for its golden hue and floral undertones. North African variations, such as Moroccan qahwa, may include up to 15-20 g of sugar per 100-150 ml cup to create a sweeter profile, though unsweetened versions align more closely with Peninsula styles.
Nutritionally, a typical 50 ml serving of unsweetened Arabic coffee provides approximately 1 calorie, primarily from trace amounts in spices, along with negligible protein (<0.1 g). It contains negligible fat and offers minute amounts of minerals like potassium and magnesium from the cardamom and saffron, contributing to its low-macronutrient status as a beverage. The light roasting process referenced in traditional methods helps retain these volatile compounds without introducing significant additional calories.

Health Considerations

Arabic coffee offers several potential health benefits primarily derived from its key components. Chlorogenic acids, abundant in coffee beans, function as potent antioxidants that help mitigate oxidative stress and reduce inflammation by activating pathways like Nrf2. The inclusion of cardamom in traditional preparations supports digestive health, as it soothes the stomach lining, alleviates bloating, and promotes better gut motility through its anti-inflammatory properties. With a relatively low caffeine content of approximately 15-40 mg per typical small serving (30-60 ml), Arabic coffee provides a mild boost to alertness and cognitive function without the jitteriness associated with higher-caffeine beverages.
Despite these advantages, excessive consumption carries risks. The inherent acidity of coffee can exacerbate gastrointestinal issues, such as heartburn or gastroesophageal reflux, particularly when consumed on an empty stomach or in large amounts, by stimulating excess gastric acid production. High intake may also contribute to insomnia, as caffeine blocks adenosine receptors, delaying sleep onset and reducing sleep quality even when consumed hours before bedtime. Additionally, the unfiltered brewing method retains diterpenes like cafestol and kahweol, which have been shown to elevate serum cholesterol levels, including LDL, by inhibiting bile acid synthesis in the liver.
In cultural contexts, Arabic coffee has been employed medicinally in traditional Arab practices, including in Yemen, to relieve headaches through caffeine's vasoconstrictive effects that narrow dilated blood vessels. Islamic teachings emphasize moderation in all consumables to maintain bodily balance and health, aligning with prophetic traditions that advocate avoiding excess to prevent harm. Recent research up to 2025 in Arab populations, such as studies on Saudi coffee consumption, suggests that regular intake of spiced varieties may lower blood glucose levels and correlate with reduced type 2 diabetes risk, potentially enhanced by synergistic effects of coffee's polyphenols and spices like cardamom on insulin sensitivity.
