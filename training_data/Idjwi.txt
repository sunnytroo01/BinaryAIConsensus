 Idjwi is a large inland island situated in Lake Kivu, in the South Kivu province of the eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo, near the border with Rwanda. Measuring approximately 340 square kilometers and stretching about 70 kilometers in length, it ranks as the second-largest lake island in Africa and the tenth largest in the world. With a population estimated at around 300,000 as of 2017, Idjwi features high population density, primarily inhabited by Bantu-speaking groups such as the Havu people, alongside a small Pygmy minority.
Geographically, Idjwi is characterized by lush green hills, volcanic soil, and limited infrastructure, which contribute to its relative isolation from the mainland conflicts that have plagued eastern DRC. The island's terrain supports extensive agriculture, but rapid population growth has led to environmental challenges like deforestation and soil erosion. Demographically, the population has expanded significantly since the mid-1990s, driven by natural increase and influxes of refugees; for instance, it grew by 41% in 1994 alone due to 46,000 Rwandan refugees fleeing the genocide. This growth has resulted in one of the highest fertility rates globally, at 8.3 births per woman as recorded in 2010 surveys.
Historically, Idjwi served as a haven of relative peace amid regional turmoil, avoiding the direct impacts of the DRC's civil wars and ethnic conflicts through its insular position and traditional governance structures until 2025. Since the 1990s, waves of Congolese and Rwandan refugees had sought refuge there, straining resources but also fostering a culture of local resilience and community-driven security; however, in early 2025, over 100,000 displaced persons fled to the island to escape ongoing violence, and in March 2025, it was captured by M23 rebels. The island operates semi-autonomously under the authority of two traditional kings, known as mwamis, who oversee local affairs alongside limited national administration, promoting stability through customary laws and dispute resolution.
Economically, Idjwi relies heavily on subsistence agriculture, fishing, and small-scale trade, with coffee as the primary cash crop supporting cooperatives like the Kivu Cooperative of Coffee Planters and Traders. Over 80% of economic activity occurs in the informal sector, with average household incomes remaining lowaround $61 PPP monthly in 2010exacerbated by food insecurity affecting about 26% of households. Recent development initiatives, including UNDP-supported reforestation and terracing projects, aim to address malnutrition and environmental degradation while creating temporary employment for hundreds of residents.

Geography

Location and physical features

Idjwi Island is situated in Lake Kivu, within South Kivu Province in the eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo, positioned near the international border with Rwanda and approximately halfway between the Congolese cities of Bukavu and Goma. The island lies at coordinates approximately 210S 2900E.
Measuring about 70 kilometers (43 miles) in length with a total area of 340 square kilometers (131 square miles), Idjwi is the second-largest lake island in Africa and ranks among the tenth largest inland islands globally. Its elongated form stretches north-south, dividing Lake Kivu into eastern and western basins.
The island's terrain features rolling green hills rising from 1,400 to 2,400 meters in elevation, with an average slope of 24 percent and steeper gradients exceeding 30 percent in places, contributing to its asymmetrical relief with more pronounced eastern slopes. Dense forests and lush vegetation, including banana groves and pineapple plantations, cover much of the landscape, supported by fertile soils along a varied shoreline fringed by the lake's waters. These soils, primarily andosols derived from basaltic volcanic rocks, are young, friable, and nutrient-rich, enhancing agricultural productivity.
Geologically, Idjwi formed as part of the Albertine Rift, the western branch of the East African Rift system, where ongoing tectonic divergence at 6-7 millimeters per year has shaped the region's faulted basins and volcanic landscapes since the Miocene epoch (around 25 million years ago). Volcanic influences from nearby activity, including basaltic eruptions, have enriched the island's soils and contributed to its integration within the rift's dynamic topography.

Climate and environment

Idjwi experiences a tropical monsoon climate characterized by high humidity and bimodal rainfall patterns, with a long wet season from September to May and a short dry season from June to August. Average annual temperatures range from 20C to 25C, with yearly highs around 24C and lows near 14C, moderated by the island's elevation of approximately 1,460 meters above sea level. Annual precipitation averages about 1,400 mm, supporting lush vegetation but also contributing to seasonal flooding risks during peak rainy months like November, when rainfall can exceed 260 mm.
The surrounding Lake Kivu significantly influences Idjwi's microclimate by providing thermal moderation, reducing temperature extremes and increasing local humidity through evaporation, which fosters a stable environment for agriculture on the island's fertile volcanic soils. However, the lake's deep waters contain substantial methane depositsestimated at 55-60 kmalong with carbon dioxide, posing potential water quality challenges and risks of limnic eruptions that could release toxic gases and affect island ecosystems, though direct exploitation of these gases on Idjwi remains unfeasible due to its isolation. These geological features, combined with volcanic activity in the Virunga region, contribute to occasional seismic influences on the local environment.
Idjwi's biodiversity includes native forests historically covering significant portions of the island, alongside diverse flora such as bamboo and agricultural-adapted plants, and fauna like the endemic Idjwi blue monkey subspecies, various bird species, and edible insects that play a role in local ecosystems. However, land cover changes have severely impacted this diversity; a 33-year analysis from 1990 to 2023 reveals forest loss from 57.16 km (20.61% of land area) to just 3.25 km (1.17%), primarily driven by agricultural expansion and sand extraction, leading to habitat fragmentation and reduced carbon storage. These alterations have accelerated biodiversity decline, with species like primates facing habitat loss since the 1990s.
Conservation efforts on Idjwi focus on mitigating these environmental pressures, including community-based initiatives to protect blue monkey habitats in areas like the 247-acre Bulolero forest reserve and shoreline bamboo planting to combat erosion. Local groups have also protested deforestation and advocated for sustainable land use to preserve ecosystem services such as water regulation. Climate change exacerbates these challenges, with observed irregular precipitation, extended droughts, and decreasing annual rainfall trendsparticularly in Idjwiintensifying soil erosion and further reducing forest carbon sequestration capabilities. Tree-planting campaigns since 2022 have aimed to restore vegetation and enhance resilience against these impacts. As of March 2025, the island came under control of M23 rebels, which may pose new challenges to conservation efforts amid regional conflict.

History

Pre-colonial period

The pre-colonial history of Idjwi Island is marked by the arrival of Bantu-speaking communities, primarily the Havu people, who established initial settlements along the island's shores starting in the late 18th century. These early inhabitants, known collectively as BanyIdjwi, formed small communities focused on subsistence activities, leveraging the fertile volcanic soils for farming and the abundant waters of Lake Kivu for fishing. Oral traditions suggest that these groups migrated from regions in present-day Rwanda and the eastern Congo mainland, arriving independently or in tandem with royal lineages, which contributed to the island's cultural and ethnic heterogeneity.
By the early 19th century, Idjwi had coalesced into a single independent kingdom under the Basibula dynasty, which originated on the mainland before a schism prompted the junior line to settle the island around 18151825. The dynasty's founder and first mwami (king), Mwendanga, established centralized authority over the island's clans, ruling from the southern region near Nyabihungwe and extending influence northward. This political structure emphasized royal control while integrating clan leaders, creating a unified governance that distinguished Idjwi from the more decentralized societies on the surrounding mainland.
Social organization on Idjwi was fundamentally clan-based, with the Havu (also referred to as Bashi or Bahavu) forming the dominant ethnic group among approximately a dozen clans. These clans maintained social differentiation through kinship ties and roles in governance, while the mwami held symbolic and administrative power, often delegating to royal kin in provincial areas. Oral histories preserve accounts of migrations from interlacustrine regions, portraying the Havu as descendants of Rwandan and Congolese groups who adapted to the island's lacustrine environment, fostering a shared identity as "people of the Lake."
The pre-colonial economy of Idjwi was predominantly subsistence-oriented, centered on seed agriculturecultivating crops like bananas, beans, and sorghumand fishing in Lake Kivu, which provided staples such as tilapia and sardines. Cattle keeping supplemented these activities, reflecting interlacustrine cultural influences. Trade was limited but vital, involving inter-island exchanges of fish, agricultural goods, and iron tools with communities on the eastern and western shores of the lake, integrating Idjwi into broader regional networks without extensive long-distance commerce.

Colonial era

The colonial era on Idjwi began with German involvement in the late 19th century, as the island was incorporated into German East Africa following territorial claims during the Berlin Conference of 1884-1885. In 1899, German forces physically occupied Idjwi amid border disputes with Belgium, establishing a presence that included early missionary activities by German Protestants who sought to evangelize the local population. These disputes, spanning 1899 to 1910, ultimately assigned Idjwi to the Belgian sphere, but Germany remained reluctant to fully relinquish control after World War I, partly due to the established German missionary foothold on the island.
Belgian rule commenced effectively in 1912 with the establishment of a military post on Idjwi, integrating the island into the Belgian Congo administration after the 1910 border resolution. To consolidate control and undermine the island's pre-existing unified kingdom under the Basibula dynasty, Belgian authorities divided Idjwi into two chiefdoms: the northern Rubenga chiefdom and the southern Ntambuka (or Buhoro) chiefdom, creating parallel chiefly structures that fragmented traditional authority. Initially, the Belgians recognized Mwami Ndogosa as chief of the island and surrounding areas in 1913 via an official decree, but his resistance to resource extraction and labor demands led to his deportation in 1926, after which they appointed Berainitially as chief of the entire islandfollowed by further subdivisions and replacements like Rutishereka and Muhamiriza to ensure compliance.
Under Belgian indirect rule, administration relied on appointed or deposed local chiefs to govern, collect taxes, and mobilize labor, often prioritizing collaboration over traditional legitimacy, which sparked resistance and periodic military occupations. Land reallocations during this period favored colonial priorities, including the settlement of Hutu migrants from Masisi in areas like Buzi-Ziralo in the 1920s and 1930s to support agricultural labor demands, while missions received concessions that altered indigenous land use patterns. Missionary influence expanded with the arrival of Catholic White Fathers in 1936, who established a mission station and reported administrative abuses to Belgian authorities, thereby shaping education, religion, and social structures; earlier Protestant efforts, linked to the German era, had laid groundwork through sporadic visits. This divided governance framework persisted beyond decolonization in 1960, influencing local administrative legacies.

Post-independence developments

Following independence in 1960, Idjwi integrated into the newly formed Democratic Republic of the Congo, later renamed Zaire under Mobutu Sese Seko's regime, as part of South Kivu province, with minimal direct involvement from the central government in Kinshasa. Traditional governance structures persisted, centered on customary chiefdoms such as Ntambuka in the south and Rubenga in the north, which handled local administration and dispute resolution amid the national political turmoil of the era. In 1974, Idjwi was elevated to an independent administrative territory, separate from Kalehe, reinforcing its reliance on indigenous leadership while aligning with national ethnic and territorial policies.
The 1994 Rwandan genocide profoundly affected Idjwi when approximately 46,000 refugees, predominantly women and children, arrived on the island, causing a 41% surge in its population over a few months. This influx strained local resources, leading to widespread deforestation as refugees cleared forests for shelter and fuel, and exacerbating food insecurity due to heightened population pressure on limited arable land. The refugees remained on the island until 1996, when most were repatriated or displaced following the advance of the AFDL rebels during the First Congo War, allowing Idjwi to regain stability without descending into prolonged ethnic violence.
During the First Congo War (19961997) and Second Congo War (19982003), Idjwi notably avoided the incursion of armed groups that ravaged much of eastern DRC, preserving its status as a peaceful enclave through community-driven mechanisms. This resilience stemmed from a "culture of peace" rooted in grassroots conflict resolution, where customary leaders and communal dialogues mediated disputes over land, resources, and ethnic identities, preventing escalation into broader violence. Scholars attribute this success to Idjwi's social cohesion and traditional governance, which emphasized inclusive consultation over armed confrontation, even as regional wars displaced millions elsewhere.
In the 2010s, Idjwi gained international recognition for its peacebuilding efforts, being designated an International City of Peace by the International Cities of Peace initiative, highlighting its model of local stability amid eastern DRC's ongoing instability. Recent local initiatives, including community partnerships for sustainable development and gender parity programs led by figures like liaison Kashali Bamenyirwe, have further bolstered this reputation, fostering resilience against external threats while influencing the island's semi-autonomous political status.

Government and administration

Local governance

Idjwi's local governance operates through a semi-autonomous traditional system dominated by two mwami, or kingsone ruling the northern section (Idjwi-Nord) and the other the southern section (Idjwi-Sud)who trace their authority to pre-colonial dynasties established in the late 18th and 19th centuries. These leaders maintain significant influence in a society where customary institutions persist alongside modern administration, reflecting the island's historical isolation and cultural continuity.
Administratively, Idjwi forms its own territory within South Kivu Province, subdivided into sectors and chiefdoms where local chiefs adjudicate disputes using customary law, which governs matters like inheritance, marriage, and minor conflicts. This system emphasizes restorative justice and community consensus, with chiefs serving as intermediaries between clans and the broader population. The mwami, as paramount authorities, play central roles in land allocationdistributing arable plots among families based on lineage and needand in dispensing justice for serious offenses, often convening councils to resolve inter-clan tensions. They also guide community decisions on resource management and social welfare, reinforcing social cohesion in a predominantly agrarian society.
To address contemporary challenges, traditional leaders have participated in capacity-building initiatives; for instance, in March 2011, the United Nations Joint Human Rights Office (UNJHRO) under MONUSCO trained 25 chiefs from Idjwi on human rights, the limits of customary courts in handling sexual violence cases, and victim counseling protocols, aiming to align local practices with international standards.
Oversight from the central government in Kinshasa remains limited due to logistical challenges and ongoing instability in eastern DRC, leading Idjwi to rely heavily on local councils and mwami for day-to-day administration, including tax collection and basic security. This decentralized approach has contributed to the island's relative stability amid regional conflicts.

Political status

Idjwi Territory is administratively part of South Kivu Province in the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC), established as a distinct territory in 1974 to address local governance needs. Despite this formal subordination, the island has historically enjoyed de facto autonomy stemming from its geographical isolation in Lake Kivu and the DRC central government's limited control over remote eastern regions.
This isolation has facilitated a "security from below" model, where community-led micro-political solutions have maintained stability without reliance on national institutions, allowing Idjwi to avoid the armed group incursions plaguing neighboring areas amid Rwanda-DRC border tensions. Until March 2025, no rebel militias operated on the island, making it a rare haven of peace in eastern DRC. However, on March 12, 2025, the M23 rebel group captured Idjwi without resistance, extending its control over Lake Kivu shorelines and marking a significant shift in the island's security dynamics. As of November 2025, a framework agreement signed on November 15 between the DRC government and M23 in Qatar, mediated by Qatar, the United States, and the African Union, aims to end the conflict in eastern DRC, potentially affecting the status of M23-held areas including Idjwi.
In recognition of its pre-2025 peacebuilding efforts, Idjwi was designated an International City of Peace, highlighting its bottom-up approaches as an inspirational model for fostering stability in fragile states where top-down governance fails.
Despite these achievements, Idjwi remains overlooked by major development agencies and receives minimal support from the DRC government, exacerbating its vulnerability to regional spillovers such as the recent rebel advances. This marginalization underscores the island's precarious integration into national politics, even as local initiatives continue to shape its response to external pressures.

Demographics

Population statistics

Idjwi Island's population is estimated at approximately 300,000 residents as of the mid-2010s, with no official census data available beyond earlier estimates, though steady growth continues due to high fertility and limited migration outflows. Recent conflicts have driven further population growth, with over 100,000 people displaced to the island since January 2025, likely pushing the total above 400,000 as of November 2025. This figure aligns with projections from a 2010 estimate of 220,000, applying an annual growth rate of about 2.2% observed since the mid-1990s. The island's high total fertility rate of 8.3 children per woman contributes significantly to this natural increase, one of the highest globally.
Historical population trends reflect rapid expansion, particularly post-1994. In 1986, the population stood at around 50,000, rising sharply due to natural growth rates of 2.9% to 3.3% annually from the late 1920s through the early 1990s, followed by a 41% surge in 1994 from an influx of approximately 46,000 Rwandan refugees (pre-influx ~112,000; post-influx ~158,000). By 2010, it had reached 220,000, driven by ongoing high birth rates and some internal migrations, with further increases to over 290,000 by 2017.

With an island area of 340 km, Idjwi's population density is approximately 850 people per km as of the mid-2010s, among the highest for lake islands, with concentrations in coastal villages and terraced hillsides suitable for agriculture (rising above 1,175 per km with 2025 influx). The population remains predominantly rural, with over 95% in small, dispersed settlements focused on subsistence farming and fishing; there are no major urban centers, only minor administrative hubs like Kahuzi and Kibirizi.

Ethnic composition and languages

The Havu (also known as Bashi or Bahavu), a Bantu ethnic group, form the dominant population on Idjwi, comprising over 90% of the island's approximately 300,000 residents as of the mid-2010s and organized into subgroups associated with traditional chiefdoms such as Ntambuka and Kyafula-Mpinga.
Minority groups include the indigenous Batwa (Pygmy) community, numbering around 7,000 and concentrated in coastal camps, who face ongoing marginalization due to historical land clearance by Bantu settlers and lack of legal rights to land, fishing, or hunting. Smaller numbers of Rwandan refugees and Congolese migrants from the mainland, who arrived following the 1994 genocide and subsequent conflicts, have integrated into the population, contributing to demographic growth without forming distinct ethnic enclaves. Recent 2025 displacements have added diverse groups from South Kivu, further diversifying the population.
The primary language spoken by the Havu majority is Kihavu (also referred to as Shi or Kishi), a Bantu tongue used in daily household and community interactions across the island. Swahili serves as the lingua franca for trade and intergroup communication, particularly with mainland Congolese, while French functions as the language of administration, education, and formal governance; despite Idjwi's proximity to Rwanda, Kinyarwanda holds no dominant role in local usage.
Historical Bantu expansions, including the arrival of the Havu, displaced earlier indigenous groups like the Batwa from forest interiors, fostering a society that is ethnically diverse yet cohesive through shared subsistence practices and identification as "BanyIdjwi" (people of Idjwi).

Economy

Agriculture and fishing

Agriculture on Idjwi primarily consists of smallholder farming on plots typically smaller than 0.5 hectares, benefiting from the island's fertile volcanic soil that supports a variety of subsistence and cash crops. Subsistence crops dominate, with cassava (manioc) being the most prevalent, cultivated by approximately 89.6% of the island's farming households, alongside bananas, beans, tomatoes, and soy, which provide staple foods for local consumption. Cash crops include Arabica coffee grown on hilly terrain, pineapples as a key export-oriented produce, and fruits such as avocados and oranges, often intercropped with staples like bananas and cassava to maximize land use.
Fishing in Lake Kivu forms a vital complement to agriculture, with Idjwi's waters yielding species such as tilapia and sambaza (a small sardine-like fish) that supply local markets in eastern Democratic Republic of Congo. Traditional methods, including canoe-based netting, are widespread, as most island households engage in fishing to supplement incomes. In 2017, a United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) initiative established six mixed fishing crews on the island, each comprising 11 members equipped with full supplies, to promote equitable resource access and job generation.
Productivity in both sectors faces significant hurdles, including crop diseases like mosaic virus and bacterial wilt that have severely reduced manioc and banana yields, turning Idjwi from a former breadbasket for nearby Bukavu into an area grappling with hunger. Limited mechanization persists due to the predominance of smallholder systems and lack of infrastructure, while seasonal transport disruptions via wooden canoes or motorcycles constrain exports beyond local markets. In fishing, crews earn around US$80 per trip, with revenues split to cover equipment (25%), cooperative operations (25%), and family incomes (50%), yet broader environmental pressures like habitat degradation in Lake Kivu limit long-term viability.
Sustainability efforts emphasize social inclusion and resource management, notably through the UNDP program that integrates Pygmy fishermen with Bantu crews, fostering cooperation and improving livelihoodsfor instance, enabling Pygmy participants to earn $2 daily compared to $0.50 as farmhandswhile aiming to mitigate overfishing through community-based equitable practices. These initiatives also support Pygmy inclusion in agriculture-related activities, addressing historical marginalization and promoting balanced ecosystem use. Coffee and pineapple plantations occasionally attract tourism visits, highlighting the island's agricultural heritage without diverting from primary production.

Other economic activities

In addition to agriculture and fishing, the economy of Idjwi includes artisanal crafts such as pottery, basket weaving, and woodworking, primarily produced by local women and Pygmy communities for household use and sale as souvenirs. These crafts are often created using traditional techniques and materials sourced from the island's forests and lake, contributing to supplementary income through small-scale production.
Local markets facilitate inter-village exchange of crafts, agricultural surplus, and fish, serving as hubs for daily barter and cash transactions among the island's population, estimated at over 300,000 as of 2017 and further increased by more than 100,000 refugees arriving since January 2025. This influx has strained resources, exacerbating food insecurity and poverty while placing additional pressure on markets and livelihoods. Limited cross-border trade occurs via boat with nearby Rwandan ports like Rubavu and Rusizi, involving handicrafts, bananas, and other goods, though transportation challenges restrict its scale.
Tourism is an emerging sector, focusing on eco- and cultural experiences that leverage the island's isolation and Lake Kivu's scenery. Visitors engage in village walks to observe daily life and traditions, tours of pineapple plantations, and boat trips for birdwatching and swimming. Cultural activities include interactions with local artisans for pottery demonstrations and coffee processing experiences, with basic eco-lodges and guesthouses accommodating small groups at low costs. However, poor access via infrequent ferries from Goma or Bukavu hinders growth, limiting visitor numbers despite the potential for sustainable tourism tied to conservation efforts like the Idjwi Nature Reserve.
Small-scale energy projects provide another avenue for economic diversification, with solar mini-grids installed in communities like Bugarula to power households, businesses, and institutions, enabling extended productive hours and reducing reliance on diesel generators. Initiatives such as the ENRU project have constructed hybrid solar-hydroelectric systems, training local entrepreneurs in maintenance and distribution to foster inclusive employment. Since 2017, UNDP-supported development programs have acted as incubators, promoting livelihood diversification through skills training in soap-making, market gardening, and community cooperatives to bridge ethnic divides and combat poverty.
The island's geographic isolation exacerbates high poverty levels, with average household incomes around 63 USD per month, though projects emphasize inclusive opportunities for marginalized groups like Pygmy artisans. Efforts continue to integrate crafts and tourism with energy access to build resilience against these challenges.

Culture

Traditions and society

Idjwi's society is organized around clan-based communities, where extended family ties form the core of social organization, and traditional leaders known as mwami oversee communal affairs. Clan heads, or bakonde, manage small domains, emphasizing collective decision-making in matters of land use and resource allocation, while chiefs maintain authority rooted in historical kingship traditions. Gender roles are distinctly divided, with men primarily responsible for fishing and heavy agricultural labor, and women handling crop cultivation, household management, and child-rearing, reflecting broader Bantu cultural patterns among the Havu.
Customs among the Havu on Idjwi center on oral storytelling as a primary means of preserving history and imparting moral lessons, often shared during evening gatherings to reinforce community bonds and cultural identity. Marriage rites integrate the bride's family deeply into the groom's lineage, with traditions treating the mother-in-law as a biological mother post-union, underscoring lifelong familial obligations. Beliefs in sorcery persist, with rumors of protective rituals invoking spiritual forces to safeguard against misfortune, though these are often whispered due to external scrutiny. High fertility rates are a longstanding social norm, promoting large families as a symbol of prosperity and security within the clan's extended network.
Community life on Idjwi emphasizes peace and resilience, with bottom-up mediation processes led by elders and chiefs resolving disputes through dialogue to maintain harmony in this isolated setting. The island's reputation as a haven of peace stems from flexible societal mechanisms that adapt to external pressures, fostering a culture of non-violence amid regional instability. Following the 1994 Rwandan genocide, local Havu communities demonstrated hospitality by integrating thousands of refugees, providing shelter and shared resources despite resource strains, which strengthened inter-ethnic ties over time.
Religion on Idjwi blends Christian influences from early 20th-century missions with enduring animist practices. Catholic and Protestant denominations, including Adventist and other evangelical groups, dominate formal worship, introduced via missionary stations since the 1930s, yet many incorporate animist elements such as ancestral veneration and rituals for protection against evil spirits. This syncretism reflects the Havu's adaptation of colonial-era Christianity to pre-existing spiritual beliefs centered on nature and community spirits.

Arts and festivals

The arts of Idjwi reflect the island's rich Havu heritage, deeply rooted in Bantu traditions with subtle synergies from neighboring Rwandan cultural exchanges due to its Lake Kivu border location. Traditional music features rhythmic drumming and wooden flutes, often accompanied by chants that accompany daily life and rituals, creating an immersive auditory landscape that emphasizes community harmony. Dances are energetic and narrative-driven, with performers using fluid movements to recount ancestral stories and historical events, typically performed in circles to foster collective participation.
Crafts on Idjwi, particularly pottery, hold regional recognition as a vital expression of cultural identity, often crafted by artisans drawing from ancient techniques passed down through generations. These earthenware pieces, shaped from local clay, feature intricate patterns symbolizing fertility and protection, and are commonly used in households or sold at island markets to support livelihoods. Weaving complements this tradition, producing baskets and mats with geometric designs that echo Bantu motifs, blending utility with artistic value.
Festivals on Idjwi are tied closely to agricultural cycles and communal life, with annual harvest celebrations marking the bounty of bananas, cassava, and beans through vibrant gatherings. These events include drumming ensembles, dance troupes, and shared feasts featuring banana beer (kasiksi), serving as platforms to honor Havu history and reinforce social bonds. Performances during these festivals often highlight storytelling through song and movement, drawing participants from across the island's villages.
Cultural preservation efforts on Idjwi focus on community-led initiatives to sustain these arts amid modernization pressures, such as evening circles where elders teach youth drumming, dancing, and pottery skills to maintain distinct island identity. These programs, often supported by local cooperatives, emphasize oral transmission of ancestral narratives, ensuring that Bantu-rooted practices evolve without losing their essence, while adapting to contemporary contexts like eco-tourism.

Infrastructure and transport

Access to the island

Idjwi Island, located in Lake Kivu, is primarily accessible by boat from the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) mainland or neighboring Rwanda. The main departure point from the DRC is Goma, where ferries and speedboats depart from Goma Port, with the journey typically taking 2 to 3 hours to reach ports like Bugarula or Minembwe on the island. From Rwanda, boats operate from ports such as Karongi (formerly Kibuye) or Cyangugu (now Rusizi), with travel times ranging from 1 to 2 hours, though these routes often require coordination with local operators. Local residents frequently use smaller motorized canoes for shorter crossings, which are more informal and cater to daily commuters. In February 2025, M23 rebels lifted a ban on Lake Kivu navigation, allowing resumption of boat services after earlier disruptions.
Ferry services to Idjwi operate on irregular schedules, often once or twice daily, and are highly dependent on weather conditions on Lake Kivu, which can lead to cancellations during storms or high winds. Recent infrastructure enhancements, including the Lake Kivu Harbor Transport Project supported by TradeMark Africa, have improved connectivity by upgrading ports in Karongi and facilitating better links to DRC ferry routes serving Idjwi, aiming to boost trade and passenger reliability. Additionally, the 2024 inauguration of Rwanda's Rubavu Port has introduced more structured ferry operations across the lake, potentially easing access from the Rwandan side.
Cross-border access from Rwanda involves passing through the Gisenyi-Goma border, where travelers must obtain a DRC tourist visaavailable on arrival for a fee of around $100 for single entry (1 month validity) or via e-visa applicationalong with a yellow fever vaccination certificate. Security checks are stringent due to ongoing regional tensions, including conflicts involving armed groups like M23 in North Kivu province, which can disrupt border operations and require advance planning for safe passage. Since late January 2025, over 100,000 people have fled to Idjwi as refugees amid escalating conflicts, further impacting access and services.
Access to Idjwi faces several challenges, including high transportation costssuch as $50 for a one-way speedboat ticket from Gomaand frequent overcrowding on ferries, particularly during market days when locals transport goods. There is no air access to the island, as it lacks an airport, making boat travel the sole option and exacerbating vulnerabilities to lake conditions and service limitations.

Internal infrastructure

Idjwi's internal transportation network consists primarily of unsealed dirt tracks spanning the island's length, with no paved roads available for vehicular use. These rudimentary paths, often narrow and winding through hilly terrain, facilitate movement between villages but are predominantly traversed by foot, bicycles, or motorcycles, as four-wheeled vehicles remain scarce and limited to officials or emergencies. During the rainy season, heavy downpours transform the tracks into muddy quagmires, rendering them largely impassable and isolating remote communities for days at a time.
Access to utilities on the island is severely constrained, exacerbating daily challenges for its approximately 300,000 residents as of 2017, with an additional influx of over 100,000 refugees since January 2025. Electricity is not supplied by the national grid; instead, isolated solar mini-grids and community-based systems, introduced in the 2010s and expanded with new sustainable solar installations in 2024, provide intermittent power to a fraction of households, primarily supporting essential services like health centers and information hubs. Clean water shortages persist, with safe drinking sources limited to a handful of gravity-fed schemes serving select areas, forcing many to draw from Lake Kivu and contributing to prevalent waterborne illnesses. Internet access arrived in 2013 via Pamoja Net, a locally owned mesh WiFi network that connects thousands across the island, fostering education and economic links despite ongoing coverage gaps.
Health and education infrastructure reflects the island's isolation and resource limitations, with services largely sustained by missionary and church legacies. Basic clinics, operated predominantly by religious organizations, offer primary care but face chronic shortages of personnel, equipment, and supplies, compounded by a high total fertility rate of approximately 8.3 that overwhelms available facilities; the 2025 refugee influx has intensified these pressures, contributing to food insecurity and health crises. Schools are similarly rudimentary, often consisting of single-room structures run by faith-based groups, providing foundational education amid challenges like overcrowding and inadequate materials that hinder broader learning opportunities.
Development efforts on Idjwi have been largely self-directed, as major international agencies have historically overlooked the island in favor of mainland priorities. Local initiatives, including community-managed solar installations for productive uses and expanded water access points, have emerged to bridge gaps in utilities and livelihoods, though these remain small-scale and funding-dependent. Such grassroots projects highlight resilience but underscore persistent vulnerabilities, including to climate variability that affects water availability and track usability and the ongoing strain from the 2025 refugee arrivals.
