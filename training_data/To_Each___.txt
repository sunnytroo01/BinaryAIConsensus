 To Each... is the second studio album and debut full-length LP by the English post-punk band A Certain Ratio, released in April 1981 on Factory Records. Produced by Martin Hannett, the album features nine tracks blending elements of funk, punk, and new wave, including standout songs like "The Fox" and the 12-minute closer "Winter Hill." Recorded during a creative peak for the band, it marked a shift from their earlier mini-album The Graveyard and the Ballroom toward more experimental and groove-oriented sounds influenced by Manchester's post-punk scene. The double LP spent 20 weeks on the UK Indie Charts, reaching number one, and is regarded as a cult classic for its innovative fusion of genres and icy, desolate production.

Background and Development

Band Context

A Certain Ratio (ACR) is an English post-punk band formed in Manchester in late 1977, emerging from the vibrant late-1970s punk and club scene. The band originated when multi-instrumentalist Jez Kerr joined an embryonic lineup featuring Simon Topping on vocals and trumpet, Pete Terrell on guitar and effects, and Martin Moscrop on guitar and trumpet, after Kerr encountered them performing at Pips Disco. Kerr, who took up bass for the group, and Moscrop had previously been involved in Manchester's experimental music circles, drawing initial influences from punk acts like Wire and Throbbing Gristle, as well as electronic pioneers such as Brian Eno and Kraftwerk. The quartet's sound quickly evolved to incorporate funk and disco elements, inspired by artists like James Brown, George Clinton, and northern soul, setting them apart in the post-punk landscape.
By early 1979, ACR had expanded with drummer Donald Johnson, recommended by Factory Records founder Tony Wilson, completing a core lineup that emphasized rhythm-driven experimentation over traditional song structures. Introduced to Wilson by Joy Division manager Rob Gretton, the band secured a deal with the influential Factory Records label after early gigs supporting acts like Joy Division and A Forest. Their debut single, "All Night Party"/"The Thin Boys" (FAC 5), released in May 1979, showcased a raw post-punk edge with dubby effects and angular rhythms, marking their entry into the UK indie scene. This was followed by the cassette-only release The Graveyard and the Ballroom in 1980, a compilation of demos and live recordings that captured their no wave and post-disco fusion, though it was not a conventional studio album.
Leading into To Each..., ACR's breakthrough came with the 1980 single "Shack Up," a cover of Banbarra's 1975 track that highlighted their percussive funk grooves and dual-trumpet arrangements, peaking at number 46 on the Billboard Hot Dance/Disco chart and solidifying their reputation for blending post-punk austerity with danceable energy. Vocalist Martha Tilson joined in September 1980, adding a new dimension to their live performances just before the album's recording. By 1981, ACR had become a key Factory act, known for their innovative use of electronics, tape loops, and jazz-inflected improvisation, influencing the Manchester music scene alongside contemporaries like New Order. To Each... represented their first full studio LP, produced by Martin Hannett, and encapsulated the band's maturation into a distinctive post-punk funk outfit.

Album Conception

The conception of To Each... emerged in the late 1970s amid A Certain Ratio's rapid evolution within Manchester's post-punk scene, following their formation in late 1977 and early singles like "All Night Party" on Factory Records. The band, initially influenced by the raw energy of punk and the experimental sounds of groups such as Throbbing Gristle and the Velvet Underground, sought to expand their sound into funk, minimalism, and global rhythms after supporting acts like Talking Heads on a brief UK tour. This period marked a shift toward incorporating elements from James Brown, Kraftwerk, and Brazilian musicians like Hermeto Pascoal and Airto Moreira, reflecting the band's desire to blend industrial punk-funk with danceable grooves.
Factory Records founder Tony Wilson played a pivotal role in shaping the album's direction, envisioning it as the band's debut full-length LP to capture their growing sophistication after a string of EPs and singles. Motivated by the vibrant New York club scene and its potential to inspire, Wilson funded the project using inheritance from his mother's death, arranging for the band to record in a New Jersey studio during their temporary relocation to the United States in 1980. This decision stemmed from Wilson's belief that immersion in New York's musical environmentincluding exposure to no-wave acts and early hip-hop crews like the Rock Steady Crewwould infuse the album with fresh energy, distinguishing it from Manchester's insular Factory sound. The band members, including vocalist Jez Kerr and guitarist Martin Moscrop, embraced this opportunity to refine their hybrid style, drawing on the city's samba bands and underground venues during their stay.
However, logistical challenges during preparations underscored the album's precarious beginnings; the band lost much of their equipment at Manchester Airport en route, forcing them to acquire new percussion in New Jersey upon arrival. Despite these setbacks, the conception emphasized collaboration and serendipity, with Wilson securing producer Martin Hannettknown for his work with Joy Divisionto helm the sessions, aiming to translate the band's live intensity into a studio format. Band members later reflected mixed feelings on this choice, viewing To Each... as a transitional work that captured their punk-funk ambitions but sometimes diverged from their raw performance energy due to Hannett's atmospheric production style.

Recording Process

Studio Sessions

The recording of To Each... took place in late 1980 at Eastern Artists Recording Studio (E.A.R.S.) in East Orange, New Jersey, marking A Certain Ratio's first trip to the United States and their debut full-length studio album. The band, seeking to expand their sound with influences from New York City's vibrant music scene, including Latin and funk elements, traveled with an augmented lineup consisting of core members Jez Kerr (vocals, bass), Martin Moscrop (guitar, trumpet), Donald Johnson (drums), Simon Topping (alto saxophone, percussion), and Peter Terrell (bass, tape), alongside vocalist Martha Tilson. This expansion allowed for a richer, more percussive approach, with the group acquiring additional instruments like congas during their stay to incorporate Brazilian and Latin rhythms.
Produced by Martin Hannett, the renowned Factory Records collaborator known for his work with Joy Division, the sessions were self-financed in part by Factory founder Tony Wilson, who accompanied the band and intended to purchase percussion equipment but was mugged at gunpoint, losing 10,000. Hannett's production emphasized cavernous echoes and dub-like effects, though the band later expressed mixed feelings about his heavy-handed style, which they felt overshadowed their raw energy. Engineering duties were handled by Bruce Gerstein and Chris Nagle, with the band themselves credited as co-producers and performers on all instruments.
The sessions were fraught with challenges, including an incident where an engineer accidentally erased the final mixes, forcing a complete redo and exacerbating tensions with Hannett, who reacted by destroying studio equipment in frustration. Upon departure, the band faced further delays at U.S. customs, where they were detained for 12 hours and accused of drug smuggling due to suspicions of being a religious cult. Despite these setbacks, the basic tracks were completed in New Jersey, with final mixing occurring back in the UK at Strawberry Studios in Stockport, also under Hannett's supervision. The tumultuous process ultimately shaped the album's murky, atmospheric sound.

Production Techniques

The production of To Each... marked A Certain Ratio's first full studio album effort, recorded in late 1980 at Eastern Artists Recording Studio (E.A.R.S.) in East Orange, New Jersey, under the guidance of producer Martin Hannett. The choice of location stemmed from Factory Records founder Tony Wilson's desire to immerse the band in the vibrant New York City club scene, which influenced their incorporation of funk, jazz, and Latin elements into the post-punk framework. Sessions lasted approximately one month, concluding ahead of schedule and allowing the band ESG to utilize the remaining time for their debut EP. Mixing occurred subsequently at Strawberry Studios in Stockport, England, where Hannett refined the tracks to emphasize spatial depth and textural layering.
Hannett, renowned for his experimental approach honed on Factory Records releases like Joy Division's Unknown Pleasures, applied dub-inspired techniques to To Each..., including extensive reverb on drums and horns to create a cavernous, echoing atmosphere that lent the album its signature murkiness. He favored plate and spring reverbs alongside tape echo units like the Melos, often layering subtle delays and compression via tools such as the Marshall Time Modulator to produce groaning, metallic basslines and a sense of vast, immersive spaceevident in tracks like "Felch" and "Oceans," where guitars and brass emerge as reverb-soaked specters. This method prioritized atmospheric tension over raw energy, transforming the band's wiry funk grooves into a "world-devouring morass," though it sometimes obscured instrumental clarity. Hannett's process involved isolating elementssuch as treating percussion with gated reverb for a stark, prominent punchdrawing from reggae dub's emphasis on rhythm section isolation while integrating post-punk's angularity.
The collaboration was fraught, with drummer Donald Johnson later recalling limited communication and stylistic clashes: "Outside the studio we got along great, but me and Martin Hannett were simply not adaptable to one another." Band members felt Hannett's heavy-handed effects smothered their intended snap and individuality, prompting them to self-produce the follow-up Sextet for greater control. Despite these tensions, Hannett's innovations elevated the album's sonic profile, blending Manchester's industrial edge with New York's funk pulse to forge a distinctive "cistine funk" hybrid, as described by Wilson.

Musical Style and Content

Genre Characteristics

To Each... exemplifies the post-punk genre with strong infusions of funk and dub, creating a distinctive sound characterized by stark, atmospheric production. Produced by Martin Hannett, the album features a cavernous, reverb-heavy mix that lends an eerie, desolate quality to its grooves, often described as "freezer-burned" and "alienated funk." The band's rhythm section, anchored by robust basslines from Jez Kerr and kinetic drumming from Donald Johnson, drives syncopated, rubbery patterns that blend primal funk urgency with post-punk tension, evoking influences from James Brown and George Clinton while incorporating Latin percussion and warped vocals for a sense of unease.
Horns play a pivotal role, delivering petrified, reverb-soaked blasts that shriek and bend, adding a ghoulish layer to tracks like "Choir" and "The Fox," where adrenalized surges meet scathing guitars reminiscent of the Contortions. This fusion extends to jazz elements, with afro-jazz-punk rhythms and deep, funk-inspired bass creating a chilling, tribal atmosphere that fluctuates between danceable vitality and sinister abstraction. Hannett's approachsucking the marrow from the sound and encasing it in a bubbleresults in a skeletal, immaterial quality, contrasting the band's earlier work and pushing boundaries toward a futuristic nightmare infused with dub's echoing spaces.
The album's genre characteristics also highlight its genre-bending nature, swagging between post-punk's angularity and odd funk's lubricious motion, as seen in the percussion-led voodoo of the 13-minute closer "Winter Hill," which layers martial-funk drumming with murky guitar feedback and zombie-like chants. Overall, To Each... stands as a seminal example of Manchester's post-punk scene evolving into experimental dance-punk and funk rock, prioritizing conceptual immersion over conventional accessibility.

Track Analysis

The album To Each... comprises nine tracks that exemplify A Certain Ratio's fusion of post-punk austerity with funk rhythms and experimental textures, produced by Martin Hannett to create a stark, desolate atmosphere. The songs feature syncopated drumming by Donald Johnson, rubbery basslines from Jez Kerr, and Martin Moscrop's plangent trumpet, often layered with eerie effects and off-key vocals by Simon Topping to evoke unease and alienation. The sequencing builds from clipped grooves to extended percussive explorations, blending danceable urgency with industrial bleakness, as heard in the shift from adrenalized funk to hypnotic epics.
Opening with "Felch," the album introduces a clipped, minimalist groove that merges post-punk tension with subtle funk propulsion, setting a tone of restrained experimentation. "My Spirit" follows with its sparse arrangement, emphasizing echoing percussion and detached vocals to convey emotional isolation amid rhythmic drive. "Forced Laugh" escalates the intensity through Donald Johnson's jazzy drum signature, incorporating feedback-laden guitar effects that build to explosive trumpet-led bursts, mimicking a chaotic, electric-shock release.
"Choir" delivers adrenalized funk reminiscent of the Contortions' urgency, driven by scathing Pop Group-style guitars and urgent bass, creating a raw, confrontational energy suitable for dancefloors haunted by post-punk edge. "Back to the Start," clocking in at nearly eight minutes, unfolds as a relentless percussive jam blending Latin-inflected rhythms, cowbell overload, ill female vocals, and horn charts for a funky yet disorienting exploration of repetition and groove.
The second side begins with "The Fox," a manic and panicked track featuring sudden drum rhythm changes infused with irresistible Caribbean touches, its frenetic pace evoking a desperate, heart-reviving urgency. "Loss" shifts to a stealthy, rubbery funk with grunty basslines funkier than Sly Stone's silkier efforts, maintaining a brooding undercurrent through subtle electronic textures.
Closing with the 13-minute "Winter Hill," the album culminates in a mesmerizing epic of martial-funk drumming, polyrhythmic layers, murky guitar feedback, eerie drones, whistles, groans, and zombie-like chants, delivering a throbbing, hypnotic trance-dance that bookends the record's voodoo percussion with ambitious, anti-climactic sprawl.

Release and Promotion

Commercial Launch

To Each... was released commercially in April 1981 by Factory Records in the United Kingdom as a gatefold vinyl LP under catalog number FACT 35. The album marked A Certain Ratio's first full-length studio release on the label, distributed primarily through independent channels typical of Factory's roster, emphasizing artistic autonomy over mainstream promotion.
The launch garnered attention within the post-punk and indie scenes, bolstered by the band's growing reputation from prior singles and EPs. It quickly rose to prominence, peaking at number 1 on the UK Independent Albums Chart compiled by NME and spending a total of 20 weeks on the listing. This chart success underscored Factory Records' influence in alternative music distribution during the early 1980s.
International editions followed shortly after the UK debut, with vinyl pressings issued in France (Celluloid), Italy (Materiali Sonori), Spain (Polydor Iberica), and New Zealand (Jayrem) between 1981 and 1982, expanding the album's reach beyond domestic indie networks. No cassette format was produced for the original run, aligning with Factory's focus on premium vinyl packaging.

Singles and Marketing

No singles were released from To Each... to promote the album, distinguishing it from A Certain Ratio's earlier EPs and 12-inch releases like the 1980 Do the Du, which included the track "The Fox" later featured on the LP.
Factory Records handled the album's marketing through its established network in the Manchester post-punk scene, emphasizing grassroots distribution and live performances rather than traditional radio play or major advertising campaigns. The label's innovative approach, including distinctive sleeve design by Peter Christopherson, contributed to the record's visibility among indie audiences.
Despite the absence of singles, To Each... performed strongly in the independent market, topping the UK Indie Chart and charting for 20 weeks, reflecting effective word-of-mouth promotion and the band's touring efforts in the UK and US around the release.

Critical Reception

Initial Reviews

Upon its release in April 1981, To Each... garnered mixed critical reception, with reviewers divided on the band's shift toward a more expansive, percussion-heavy sound influenced by funk, post-punk, and jazz elements under producer Martin Hannett's stark guidance. While some praised its innovative grooves and atmospheric production, others criticized the album's deliberate pacing and experimental leanings as overly monotonous. For example, Trouser Press noted that "the studied tedium of To Each..., however, snuffed the early promise, as the band buried itself in dreary rhythms and astonishing self-indulgence," comparing it unfavorably to Joy Division's more evocative despair while highlighting Simon Topping's subdued trumpet reworkings.
Commercially, the album resonated strongly within the independent music circuit, underscoring its cult appeal despite the uneven press. It spent 20 weeks on the UK Independent Chart, ultimately peaking at number one, a testament to A Certain Ratio's growing influence in Manchester's post-punk scene and Factory Records' roster.

Retrospective Views

In the decades following its 1981 release, To Each... has been reevaluated by critics as a pivotal work in post-punk's evolution toward funk and experimental rhythms, often hailed for its innovative fusion of genres. Produced by Martin Hannett, the album's productioncharacterized by eerie effects and atmospheric textureshas been praised for capturing a sense of unease and rhythmic propulsion that distinguishes A Certain Ratio from contemporaries like Joy Division, while foreshadowing the band's later dance-oriented sound. Retrospective analyses highlight its role as a "landmark" album, blending bleak industrial elements with "monstrously powerful" grooves, as noted in reviews of its 2017 Mute reissue, which positioned it alongside classics like Sextet for its enduring intensity.
Later appreciations emphasize the album's cult status within post-punk and funk circles, crediting its syncopated drumming, rubbery basslines, and warped vocals for pioneering "punk funk"a term that encapsulates A Certain Ratio's genre-bending influence on Manchester's music scene. Reissues, including the 2004 edition by Soul Jazz and the 2017 Mute reissue, have renewed interest, with commentators describing To Each... as a "classic" that transformed the band's raw early style into something more hypnotic and Latin-inflected, despite production challenges like tape wipes and logistical hurdles during recording in New Jersey. Critics have noted how tracks like "Back to the Start" exemplify this shift, offering percussive jams that feel both tribal and futuristic, contributing to the album's reputation as an "absolute killer" in retrospective overviews.
By the 2010s, To Each... was frequently cited in broader discussions of Factory Records' legacy, with reviewers reflecting on its initial underappreciation due to the band's outsider status but now celebrating its visionary qualities. A 2014 Quietus piece on the Sextet reissue revisited To Each... as a "quintessential Factory artefact," acknowledging its dour post-punk rigidity while affirming a growing adoration for its hypnotic core amid the label's innovative ethos. Similarly, a 2019 Paste Magazine profile on the band's career underscored the album's reissues as key to rediscovering forgotten gems, boosting confidence in A Certain Ratio's unique identity and influence on subsequent indie-dance acts. Into the 2020s, amid the band's resurgence with albums like 1982 (2023) and It All Comes Down to This (2024), To Each... continues to be praised as a foundational text in experimental music's rhythmic explorations, with a 2020 limited edition reissue further highlighting its enduring appeal.

Legacy and Reissues

Cultural Influence

A Certain Ratio's 1981 album To Each... played a pivotal role in bridging post-punk's angular experimentation with funk's rhythmic propulsion, influencing a generation of artists who sought to expand the genre's boundaries. The record's production by Martin Hannett, recorded in New Jersey, forged connections between Manchester's Factory Records scene and New York's no wave movement, introducing dub echoes and percussive grooves that prefigured the dance-oriented evolution of post-punk. This fusion helped establish ACR as genre-bending pioneers, with the album's tracks like "Forced Laugh" and "Back to the Start" exemplifying a "punky-funky" aesthetic that encouraged subsequent bands to incorporate Latin rhythms, jazz elements, and electronic textures into indie music.
The album's cultural footprint extends to its impact on key figures in alternative music. James Murphy of LCD Soundsystem has frequently cited ACR's early work, including To Each..., as a major influence on his blend of post-punk revival and dance grooves, crediting their ability to merge raw energy with sophisticated rhythms. Similarly, bands like Franz Ferdinand and Radio 4 drew from ACR's model of angular guitars overlaid with bass-driven funk, helping revive interest in the band's sound during the early 2000s post-punk resurgence. The Red Hot Chili Peppers and The Rapture also echoed elements of To Each...'s percussive intensity and improvisational feel in their own hybrid rock-funk explorations, underscoring the album's role in normalizing cross-genre experimentation within rock.
In Manchester's musical ecosystem, To Each... contributed to the groundwork for the Madchester scene of the late 1980s and early 1990s. By infusing post-punk with danceable funkoften described as teaching "white kids to dance"ACR's sound on the album anticipated the ecstatic grooves of Happy Mondays and Primal Scream, while their Factory affiliation amplified the label's reputation for innovative, boundary-pushing releases. The record's cult status grew through reissues, such as the 2017 remastered edition by Mute Records, which introduced its tracks to new audiences via streaming platforms and festival circuits, sustaining ACR's legacy as influencers on indie dance and electronic acts like The Invisible. The band's continued activity, including albums 1982 (2023) and It All Comes Down to This (2024) and ongoing tours as of 2025, further reinforces To Each...'s role as a foundational work. Overall, To Each... remains a touchstone for its demonstration of post-punk's potential as a launchpad for multicultural, rhythm-centric innovation in British music.

Later Editions

In the years following its 1981 debut, To Each... underwent several reissues that preserved its double-album format and introduced it to new audiences through expanded formats and regional markets. A CD edition was released in Japan by Factory Records in 1989, marking one of the earliest digital transfers of the album. This was followed by a UK CD reissue from Rev-Ola in 1994, which emphasized the album's punk-funk elements for compact disc collectors.
The early 2000s saw further accessibility efforts with Universal Sound's 2004 reissues, including a digipak CD and a vinyl LP bundled with a bonus 12-inch single containing additional tracks, both aimed at UK listeners seeking expanded content. In Japan, Birdsong and Hayabusa Landings issued paper-sleeve CDs in 2008 and a remastered version in 2011, the latter enhancing audio fidelity for audiophiles in the region. These editions highlighted the album's enduring appeal in niche markets, often with packaging that replicated or updated the original Factory Records aesthetic.
A significant revival occurred through Mute Records' partnership with A Certain Ratio, announced in 2016, which led to a series of remastered reissues beginning in 2017. The 2017 European 2xLP edition, pressed at 45 RPM on limited red vinyl, featured remastering to improve clarity and dynamics, accompanying CD and digital formats released on November 24 of that year as part of the label's catalog campaign. This batch, including To Each... alongside other early works, underscored the band's influence on post-punk and dance genres. A U.S. numbered red vinyl variant followed in 2018, maintaining the remastered audio.
More recently, Mute issued a 2020 limited-edition 2xLP on white vinyl, restricted to 1000 copies worldwide, to commemorate the band's album ACR Loco; this pressing converted the original artwork to black and white while including a high-definition audio download code for enhanced playback options. Digital reissues also appeared on platforms like Bandcamp in 2016, broadening access for streaming audiences. These later editions, particularly the Mute remasters, have contributed to renewed critical appreciation by delivering the album's experimental trackssuch as "The Fox" and "Oceans"in superior sound quality.
