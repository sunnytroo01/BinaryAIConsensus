Biproduct
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 In category theory, a biproduct is a construction that simultaneously serves as both a product and a coproduct of two objects in a category, equipped with compatible inclusion and projection morphisms that satisfy the universal properties of both.
More precisely, for objects aaa and bbb in a category with zero morphisms, the biproduct aba \oplus bab is an object isomorphic to both the product aba \times bab and the coproduct aba \sqcup bab, via a canonical isomorphism that aligns the structure maps: the inclusions i1:aabi_1: a \to a \oplus bi1:aab and i2:babi_2: b \to a \oplus bi2:bab act as coproduct injections, while the projections p1:abap_1: a \oplus b \to ap1:aba and p2:abbp_2: a \oplus b \to bp2:abb serve as product projections, satisfying p1i1=idap_1 \circ i_1 = \mathrm{id}_ap1i1=ida, p2i2=idbp_2 \circ i_2 = \mathrm{id}_bp2i2=idb, and p1i1+p2i2=idabp_1 \circ i_1 + p_2 \circ i_2 = \mathrm{id}_{a \oplus b}p1i1+p2i2=idab. This structure implies the existence of zero morphisms and makes the category semiadditive when all finite biproducts exist, meaning the hom-sets are equipped with commutative monoid structures under composition. In additive categories, where hom-sets form abelian groups, biproducts coincide with direct sums and are universal for diagrams involving the objects.
Biproducts generalize familiar operations like the direct sum in abelian groups or vector spaces, where ABA \oplus BAB captures both the Cartesian product structure (for projections) and disjoint union (for inclusions). Examples abound in algebraic settings, such as the category of abelian groups (Ab), modules over a ring (R-Mod), or finite-dimensional vector spaces (fdVect_k), all of which are additive and thus possess all finite biproducts. However, categories like Set (sets with functions), Top (topological spaces), or Grp (groups) typically have products and coproducts separately but lack biproducts, as their products and coproducts are not naturally isomorphic.
The concept was formalized in the foundational work of category theory, notably appearing in Saunders Mac Lane's Categories for the Working Mathematician (1971), where biproducts are discussed in the context of abelian and additive categories on pages 194 and 196. Modern developments, such as those in enriched category theory, explore biproducts without assuming pointedness (zero objects), relying instead on commuting idempotents for the structure, as detailed in works like Karvonen's analysis of biproducts in arbitrary categories. These generalizations highlight biproducts' role in unifying dual constructions and enabling abelian-like behavior in broader categorical frameworks.

Fundamentals

Definition

In category theory, a biproduct of two objects AAA and BBB in a category C\mathcal{C}C equipped with zero morphisms is an object ABA \oplus BAB together with inclusion morphisms iA:AABi_A: A \to A \oplus BiA:AAB, iB:BABi_B: B \to A \oplus BiB:BAB, and projection morphisms pA:ABAp_A: A \oplus B \to ApA:ABA, pB:ABBp_B: A \oplus B \to BpB:ABB, such that ABA \oplus BAB serves simultaneously as the categorical product of AAA and BBB (with projections pA,pBp_A, p_BpA,pB) and as the categorical coproduct of AAA and BBB (with inclusions iA,iBi_A, i_BiA,iB). The existence of zero morphisms 0A,B:AB0_{A,B}: A \to B0A,B:AB (for any objects A,BA, BA,B) is required to define the mediating maps that ensure compatibility between the product and coproduct structures.
The product conditions ensure that the projections compose appropriately with the inclusions: pAiA=idAp_A i_A = \mathrm{id}_ApAiA=idA, pBiB=idBp_B i_B = \mathrm{id}_BpBiB=idB, pAiB=0B,Ap_A i_B = 0_{B,A}pAiB=0B,A, and pBiA=0A,Bp_B i_A = 0_{A,B}pBiA=0A,B. These commutativity relations imply that the diagram for the product commutes, with the inclusions acting as the unique mediating morphisms from AAA and BBB to the product object. Dually, the coproduct conditions require that the inclusions and projections satisfy
iApA+iBpB=idAB,
i_A p_A + i_B p_B = \mathrm{id}_{A \oplus B},
iApA+iBpB=idAB,
where +++ denotes the addition in the Hom-sets, confirming that the diagram for the coproduct commutes and the projections act as the unique mediating morphisms to AAA and BBB.
Such biproducts typically arise in preadditive categories (Ab-categories), where the Hom-sets form abelian groups and composition is bilinear, allowing the necessary zero morphisms and addition of morphisms. In fully additive categories, which possess a zero object and binary biproducts for all pairs of objects, this structure extends naturally to finite direct sums.

Historical Context

The concept of the biproduct emerged as part of the foundational developments in category theory during the 1940s, when Samuel Eilenberg and Saunders Mac Lane introduced categories, functors, and natural transformations to unify concepts across algebraic topology and algebra. Their work provided the abstract framework necessary for later structures like biproducts, emphasizing duality and universal properties in mathematical systems, with Mac Lane introducing direct sums via universal mapping properties.
In the 1950s, the idea appeared in the context of abelian categories, closely tied to advancements in homological algebra. David A. Buchsbaum introduced a notion of exact categories in a 1955 paper, laying groundwork for the later definition of abelian categories by Alexander Grothendieck in his 1957 Tohoku paper, where finite direct sums function simultaneously as products and coproducts in exact sequences and resolutions. Henri Cartan and Samuel Eilenberg expanded on this in their 1956 monograph Homological Algebra, integrating direct sums into the study of derived functors and chain complexes within module categories, thereby motivating biproducts through homological duality.
Barry Mitchell provided a rigorous formalization of biproducts in his 1965 book Theory of Categories, defining them in semiadditive categories as objects that are both finite products and coproducts. Subsequent refinements occurred in the study of additive categories, where biproducts underpin abelian structures and homological computations, with further details in Saunders Mac Lane's 1971 Categories for the Working Mathematician (pp. 194, 196).
The evolution of biproducts drew significant influence from direct sums in module theory, a concept rooted in 19th-century algebra but recast categorically in the mid-20th century, and from tensor products, which gained prominence in the 1960s and 1970s as bifunctors in additive and monoidal settings.

Construction in Abelian Categories

In an abelian category, which is a preadditive category equipped with a zero object such that every morphism admits both a kernel and a cokernel, and moreover every monomorphism is the kernel of its cokernel while every epimorphism is the cokernel of its kernel, finite biproducts exist and coincide with both binary products and binary coproducts. These biproducts provide the additive structure essential for the category's operations, ensuring that the Hom-sets form abelian groups under pointwise addition defined via the zero morphisms and the biproduct diagrams.
The explicit construction of a finite biproduct for objects AAA and BBB relies on the category's kernel and cokernel structure to realize the direct sum ABA \oplus BAB, along with canonical inclusions i1:AABi_1: A \to A \oplus Bi1:AAB, i2:BABi_2: B \to A \oplus Bi2:BAB and projections p1:ABAp_1: A \oplus B \to Ap1:ABA, p2:ABBp_2: A \oplus B \to Bp2:ABB satisfying the relations p1i1=idAp_1 i_1 = \mathrm{id}_Ap1i1=idA, p2i2=idBp_2 i_2 = \mathrm{id}_Bp2i2=idB, i1p1+i2p2=idABi_1 p_1 + i_2 p_2 = \mathrm{id}_{A \oplus B}i1p1+i2p2=idAB, and the orthogonality conditions p1i2=0p_1 i_2 = 0p1i2=0, p2i1=0p_2 i_1 = 0p2i1=0. This diagram arises from split exact sequences involving the zero object, where the direct sum serves dually as both the product (via projections) and coproduct (via inclusions). In particular, the product ABA \times BAB exists if and only if the biproduct exists, with the biproduct object and its projections forming the product; dually for the coproduct.
Biproducts in abelian categories further enable constructions of pullbacks and pushouts via kernels and cokernels. Specifically, given morphisms f:XZf: X \to Zf:XZ and g:YZg: Y \to Zg:YZ, their pullback is the kernel of the difference map (f,g):XYZ(f, -g): X \oplus Y \to Z(f,g):XYZ, yielding an object WWW with morphisms to XXX and YYY such that the diagram commutes and is universal. Dually, the pushout of a:YXa: Y \to Xa:YX and b:YZb: Y \to Zb:YZ is the cokernel of the difference map (a,b):YXZ(a, -b): Y \to X \oplus Z(a,b):YXZ. These realizations confirm that every pair of objects admits a biproduct precisely when the category is additivemeaning composition is bilinear and there is a zero morphismand possesses binary products and coproducts that coincide via the canonical comparison morphism. Such constructions justify the universal properties of biproducts by embedding them within the abelian framework's exact sequences.

Examples

Finite Biproducts in Vector Spaces

In the category FinVectk\mathbf{FinVect}_kFinVectk of finite-dimensional vector spaces over a field kkk, equipped with linear maps as morphisms, the biproduct of two objects VVV and WWW is given by their direct sum VWV \oplus WVW, which consists of ordered pairs (v,w)(v, w)(v,w) with vVv \in VvV and wWw \in WwW, under componentwise addition and scalar multiplication. This category is abelian and semiadditive, meaning it has a zero object (the trivial vector space {0}\{0\}{0}) and binary biproducts for all objects. The inclusion maps are defined as iV:VVWi_V: V \to V \oplus WiV:VVW by iV(v)=(v,0)i_V(v) = (v, 0)iV(v)=(v,0) and iW:WVWi_W: W \to V \oplus WiW:WVW by iW(w)=(0,w)i_W(w) = (0, w)iW(w)=(0,w), while the projection maps are pV:VWVp_V: V \oplus W \to VpV:VWV by pV(v,w)=vp_V(v, w) = vpV(v,w)=v and pW:VWWp_W: V \oplus W \to WpW:VWW by pW(v,w)=wp_W(v, w) = wpW(v,w)=w.

Infinite Biproducts in Abelian Groups

In the category of abelian groups, denoted Ab, biproducts exist for finite families of objects through the direct sum, which is isomorphic to the direct product in such cases. For infinite families indexed by a set III, the situation differs fundamentally: the product iIAi\prod_{i \in I} A_iiIAi consists of all tuples with arbitrary entries from each AiA_iAi, while the coproduct, or direct sum iIAi\bigoplus_{i \in I} A_iiIAi, is the subgroup of the product comprising only those tuples with finite support (i.e., all but finitely many components are zero). These constructions coincideand thus form a biproductonly if all but finitely many of the AiA_iAi are the zero group, reducing the infinite case to a finite biproduct.
This coincidence condition highlights the rarity of infinite biproducts in Ab. In general, the category possesses both infinite products and infinite coproducts, but their isomorphism fails for nontrivial infinite families, as the direct sum embeds properly into the direct product without being surjective. For instance, consider a countable infinite family of trivial groups {0}n=1\{0\}_{n=1}^\infty{0}n=1; here, both the product and the sum are the zero group, yielding a biproduct that is simply the terminal object in Ab.
The direct sum iIAi\bigoplus_{i \in I} A_iiIAi is inherently a restricted direct sum, enforcing finite support to satisfy the universal property of the coproduct, in contrast to the unrestricted nature of the product. This distinction underscores why infinite biproducts are exceptional in Ab, occurring solely under the finite-support condition, and emphasizes the category's limitations compared to settings like finite-dimensional vector spaces where biproducts are more readily available.

Properties and Relations

Uniqueness and Isomorphisms

In categories where biproducts exist, they are unique up to isomorphism in the sense that if PPP and PP'P are both biproducts of objects AAA and BBB, equipped with inclusions iA:APi_A: A \to PiA:AP, iB:BPi_B: B \to PiB:BP and projections pA:PAp_A: P \to ApA:PA, pB:PBp_B: P \to BpB:PB, and similarly iA:APi_A': A \to P'iA:AP, iB:BPi_B': B \to P'iB:BP, pA:PAp_A': P' \to ApA:PA, pB:PBp_B': P' \to BpB:PB, then there exists a unique isomorphism :PP\phi: P \to P':PP such that iA=iA\phi \circ i_A = i_A'iA=iA, iB=iB\phi \circ i_B = i_B'iB=iB, pA=pAp_A' \circ \phi = p_ApA=pA, and pB=pBp_B' \circ \phi = p_BpB=pB.
This uniqueness follows from the universal properties defining biproducts as both products and coproducts. Specifically, the product universal property ensures a unique morphism $\phi: P \to P' $ mediating the projections (i.e., pA=pAp_A' \circ \phi = p_ApA=pA and pB=pBp_B' \circ \phi = p_BpB=pB), while the coproduct universal property ensures a unique morphism :PP\psi: P' \to P:PP mediating the inclusions (i.e., iA=iA\psi \circ i_A' = i_AiA=iA and iB=iB\psi \circ i_B' = i_BiB=iB). The biproduct axioms, including the relations pAiA=idAp_A \circ i_A = \mathrm{id}_ApAiA=idA, pBiB=idBp_B \circ i_B = \mathrm{id}_BpBiB=idB, pAiB=0p_A \circ i_B = 0pAiB=0, and pBiA=0p_B \circ i_A = 0pBiA=0 (where 0 denotes the zero morphism), imply that \phi and \psi are inverses, hence isomorphisms, and the structure preservation follows from naturality.
For finite collections of more than two objects, biproducts admit canonical isomorphisms ensuring associativity and commutativity. The associator isomorphism A,B,C:A(BC)(AB)C\alpha_{A,B,C}: A \oplus (B \oplus C) \to (A \oplus B) \oplus CA,B,C:A(BC)(AB)C is the unique morphism induced by the universal properties that aligns the inclusions and projections for the ternary structures, satisfying the pentagon identity in the coherence theorem for such categories. Similarly, the commutator A,B:ABBA\sigma_{A,B}: A \oplus B \to B \oplus AA,B:ABBA is the unique isomorphism swapping the roles of AAA and BBB while preserving the structure maps. These isomorphisms render the biproduct operation coherent, forming a strict monoidal structure up to equivalence.
The presence of a zero object plays a crucial role in ensuring this uniqueness by providing the necessary zero morphisms that distinguish biproducts from mere products or coproducts. The zero object serves as the empty biproduct (i.e., 0A00A0 \cong A \oplus 0 \cong 0 \oplus A0A00A), which induces unique zero morphisms 0A,B:AB0_{A,B}: A \to B0A,B:AB as the composite iB!i_B \circ !iB! where $ ! : A \to 0 $ is the unique morphism to the zero object, thereby enforcing the orthogonality conditions (e.g., pAiB=0p_A \circ i_B = 0pAiB=0) that make the mediating morphisms isomorphisms. Without a zero object, biproducts may exist in a point-free sense, remaining unique up to unique isomorphism compatible with the structure, as in generalizations to arbitrary categories without pointedness.

Connection to Products and Coproducts

In category theory, a biproduct of objects AAA and BBB is a special case of a categorical construction where the product ABA \times BAB, equipped with its projection morphisms, is naturally isomorphic to the coproduct A+BA + BA+B, equipped with its inclusion morphisms, through the mediation of zero morphisms. This isomorphism arises from the unique morphism r:A+BABr: A + B \to A \times Br:A+BAB defined by composing the inclusions into the coproduct with the projections from the product, adjusted by zero morphisms to ensure compatibility; when rrr is an isomorphism, the shared object serves as the biproduct.
In additive categories, where the hom-sets form abelian groups and composition is bilinear, this coincidence is enforced by the underlying structure: the existence of zero morphisms and the abelian group operations on morphisms imply that finite products and coproducts must coincide as biproducts, with the zero object acting as the nullary case. This unification simplifies many constructions, as the biproduct diagrams satisfy both the universal properties of products and coproducts simultaneously.
In contrast, non-additive categories such as the category of sets (Set\mathbf{Set}Set) generally feature distinct products and coproductsthe former as Cartesian products and the latter as disjoint unionswithout the required additivity to induce an isomorphism between them, precluding biproducts unless additional structure is imposed. Biproducts thus demand the presence of additive features, distinguishing them from purely universal constructions in general categories.
While biproducts are often defined binary for two objects, they extend to finite nnn-ary versions for n3n \geq 3n3 by iterated application of binary biproducts, yielding an object i=1nAi\bigoplus_{i=1}^n A_ii=1nAi with inclusions iki_kik and projections plp_lpl satisfying ikpl=kli_k \circ p_l = \delta_{k l}ikpl=kl (the Kronecker delta) and kpkik=id\sum_k p_k \circ i_k = \mathrm{id}kpkik=id. This iterative construction preserves the dual universal properties in categories supporting biproducts.

Applications

In Linear Algebra

In the category of finite-dimensional vector spaces over a field, the biproduct coincides with the direct sum, providing a canonical way to decompose spaces into orthogonal components while preserving linear structure. This structure allows for the unique representation of elements in the sum as pairs from each summand, with inclusions and projections defined componentwise.
A fundamental application arises in direct sum decompositions of vector spaces. For finite-dimensional subspaces V1V_1V1 and V2V_2V2 of a vector space VVV, VVV decomposes as the direct sum V=V1V2V = V_1 \oplus V_2V=V1V2 if and only if V1V2={0}V_1 \cap V_2 = \{0\}V1V2={0} and V1+V2=VV_1 + V_2 = VV1+V2=V, ensuring every vector in VVV can be uniquely expressed as a sum of elements from V1V_1V1 and V2V_2V2. This decomposition is dimensionally additive, with dim(V)=dim(V1)+dim(V2)\dim(V) = \dim(V_1) + \dim(V_2)dim(V)=dim(V1)+dim(V2), and extends to multiple summands for complete bases.
Matrix representations leverage this biproduct structure for endomorphisms on direct sums. If V=V1V2V = V_1 \oplus V_2V=V1V2, an endomorphism T:VVT: V \to VT:VV that preserves the decompositionmapping each summand to itselfhas a matrix representation that is block-diagonal with respect to bases adapted to V1V_1V1 and V2V_2V2, consisting of the blocks for the restrictions TV1T|_{V_1}TV1 and TV2T|_{V_2}TV2. Such block-diagonal forms simplify computations, as operations like inversion or powers act independently on each block.
Invariant subspaces further illustrate the role of biproducts in spectral theory. For a linear operator TTT on VVV, the generalized eigenspaces for distinct eigenvalues are invariant under TTT and form a direct sum decomposition of VVV, enabling the Jordan canonical form as a block-diagonal matrix of Jordan blocks within each generalized eigenspace. This decomposition relies on the biproduct to ensure the space splits without overlap, with the Jordan form capturing the structure of nilpotent parts.
Change of basis operations preserve these direct sum decompositions when the new basis respects the summands. Selecting bases for each ViV_iVi in a multi-summand decomposition V=ViV = \bigoplus V_iV=Vi yields a basis for VVV whose change-of-basis matrix is block-diagonal, maintaining the biproduct structure in coordinate representations. This compatibility ensures that linear maps and their invariants, such as traces or determinants, decompose additively across the summands.

In Homological Algebra

In the category of chain complexes over an abelian category, biproducts are given by the direct sum of complexes. For two chain complexes CC_\bulletC and DD_\bulletD, their biproduct CDC_\bullet \oplus D_\bulletCD is the chain complex with components (CD)n=CnDn(C_\bullet \oplus D_\bullet)_n = C_n \oplus D_n(CD)n=CnDn in each degree nnn, equipped with the componentwise differential dnCD=dnCdnD:CnDnCn1Dn1d_n^{C \oplus D} = d_n^C \oplus d_n^D: C_n \oplus D_n \to C_{n-1} \oplus D_{n-1}dnCD=dnCdnD:CnDnCn1Dn1. This construction preserves the exactness of differentials since dn1CDdnCD=(dn1CdnC)(dn1DdnD)=00=0d_{n-1}^{C \oplus D} \circ d_n^{C \oplus D} = (d_{n-1}^C \circ d_n^C) \oplus (d_{n-1}^D \circ d_n^D) = 0 \oplus 0 = 0dn1CDdnCD=(dn1CdnC)(dn1DdnD)=00=0.
Short exact sequences of objects in an abelian category split via biproducts when the middle term is isomorphic to the direct sum of the outer terms. Specifically, for a short exact sequence 0ABC00 \to A \to B \to C \to 00ABC0, it splits if and only if there exists a retraction CBC \to BCB or a section BCB \to CBC, in which case BACB \cong A \oplus CBAC as a biproduct. In the context of chain complexes, this extends degreewise: a short exact sequence of chain complexes 0ABC00 \to A_\bullet \to B_\bullet \to C_\bullet \to 00ABC0 splits if each degree-nnn sequence 0AnBnCn00 \to A_n \to B_n \to C_n \to 00AnBnCn0 splits, yielding BACB_\bullet \cong A_\bullet \oplus C_\bulletBAC. Such splittings are preserved under homotopy equivalence in the homotopy category of complexes.
Derived functors like Extn\operatorname{Ext}^nExtn and Torn\operatorname{Tor}_nTorn exhibit additivity with respect to biproducts, facilitating decompositions in homological computations. For finite direct sums, Extn(AB,C)Extn(A,C)Extn(B,C)\operatorname{Ext}^n(A \oplus B, C) \cong \operatorname{Ext}^n(A, C) \oplus \operatorname{Ext}^n(B, C)Extn(AB,C)Extn(A,C)Extn(B,C) and Torn(AB,C)Torn(A,C)Torn(B,C)\operatorname{Tor}_n(A \oplus B, C) \cong \operatorname{Tor}_n(A, C) \oplus \operatorname{Tor}_n(B, C)Torn(AB,C)Torn(A,C)Torn(B,C), reflecting the additivity of the Hom and tensor functors in the appropriate variables. This isomorphism arises because projective (or injective) resolutions of direct sums are direct sums of resolutions, and the homology of the resulting complexes decomposes accordingly.
Biproducts play a key role in projective and injective resolutions, where they preserve exactness and enable modular constructions. A projective resolution of a direct sum MNM \oplus NMN is the direct sum of projective resolutions of MMM and NNN, since direct sums of projectives remain projective in abelian categories with enough projectives. Similarly, for injective resolutions, the direct sum preserves injectivity and exactness, allowing the computation of derived functors on decomposed modules without loss of information. This property ensures that long exact sequences from short exact sequences remain exact after applying biproduct decompositions.
