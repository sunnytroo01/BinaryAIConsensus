The Commune
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Commune (Danish: Kollektivet) is a 2016 Danish drama film directed by Thomas Vinterberg. It was selected to compete for the Golden Bear in the main competition section of the 66th Berlin International Film Festival. The film stars Trine Dyrholm, Ulrich Thomsen, Helene Reingaard Neumann, and Martha Sofie Wallstrm Hansen.
In 1970s Denmark, television presenter Erik and his schoolteacher wife Anna inherit Erik's childhood home, a large villa, and invite a group of friends and students to form a commune. As the communal living experiment unfolds, tensions arise from personal relationships, including Erik's growing attraction to his student Emma, testing the ideals of solidarity and openness.

Synopsis and Analysis

Plot Summary

In 1970s Denmark, Erik, an ambitious architecture professor, and his wife Anna, a prominent television news anchor, inherit a spacious family home from Erik's late father, prompting them to transform it into a communal living space amid the era's countercultural spirit. Eager to embrace ideals of openness and shared responsibility, the couple invites a diverse group of friends and university students to join them, including the boisterous leftist Ole, the shy musician Allon, and others, along with their teenage daughter Freja, who navigates the changes with adolescent curiosity. The commune begins with enthusiastic harmony, establishing rules through group discussions, chore rotations, and communal meals that foster a sense of collective joy and experimentation.
As the household settles into its rhythm, Erik's midlife aspirations at the university lead him to develop a passionate affair with one of his young students, the 24-year-old Emma, whom he eventually invites to live in the commune after Anna reluctantly consents, testing the group's commitment to transparency and non-possessiveness. Tensions simmer during emotional house meetings where members debate rules on infidelity, jealousy, and personal boundaries, revealing cracks in the utopian facade as individual desires clash with communal expectations. Freja, meanwhile, experiences her own budding romance with a schoolmate, offering a youthful perspective on the household's evolving chaos, while Anna grapples with increasing professional pressures and a sense of isolation within the once-welcoming home.
The affair escalates conflicts, drawing the commune into heated confrontations over openness versus emotional security, with specific incidents like Freja's accidental discovery of her father's infidelity amplifying the familial strain. Anna's growing depression manifests in moments of withdrawal and crisis, highlighting her journey from supportive architect of the experiment to a figure confronting personal vulnerability amid the group's dynamics. Erik's pursuit of fulfillment abroad the commune's ideals further complicates relationships, leading to profound emotional reckonings that challenge the very foundation of their shared life.

Themes and Motifs

The film The Commune delves into the tension between communal solidarity and individual desires, portraying how the idealism of shared living often unravels under personal pressures. In the story, the characters' commitment to collective harmony is tested by selfish impulses, such as romantic attractions that prioritize personal fulfillment over group cohesion, reflecting the broader challenges of balancing autonomy with interdependence in experimental social structures. This conflict underscores the fragility of utopian visions, where the pursuit of equality exposes underlying hierarchies and resentments.
Central to the narrative is the impact of 1970s idealism on personal relationships, drawing from the era's countercultural experiments in Denmark, where free love and collective ownership promised liberation but frequently led to emotional turmoil. The film illustrates how these ideals, inspired by real-life communes like the one in which director Thomas Vinterberg grew up, clashed with the realities of jealousy and emotional dependency, transforming a dream of radical openness into a source of relational strain.
Infidelity and jealousy emerge as pivotal forces in the open-living experiment, highlighting the brutality of love when boundaries dissolve. The introduction of an external romantic partner into the household amplifies insecurities, forcing characters to confront the limits of tolerance and the pain of non-possessive affection, ultimately revealing how such arrangements can exacerbate rather than alleviate relational fractures. Gender roles and women's emotional labor are critiqued through the experiences of female characters, who bear the brunt of maintaining communal harmony while navigating identity loss and unacknowledged sacrifices in a setting that ostensibly promotes equality.
Recurring motifs reinforce these themes, with the inherited house serving as a symbol of both opportunity and entrapment, representing the weight of familial legacy that enables the commune but also confines its inhabitants in escalating conflicts. Group meals function as microcosms of fragile unity, where shared rituals of eating and discussion initially foster bonding but devolve into arenas of confrontation, echoing the commune's overall instability. The film draws parallels to actual 1970s Danish communes, capturing their vibrant yet flawed dynamics through Vinterberg's semi-autobiographical lens, which emphasizes the virtues of camaraderie alongside inevitable discord.
Symbolic elements further illuminate the narrative's emotional core, as Anna's descent into depression metaphorically embodies the commune's structural failure, her unraveling triggered by the erosion of personal agency amid collective demands and external stressors like the Vietnam War. Freja's youth, in contrast, symbolizes lost innocence, positioning her as a silent observer whose coming-of-age mirrors the broader disillusionment with the adults' idealistic experiment.

Development

Conception and Inspiration

Thomas Vinterberg, the director of The Commune, drew deeply from his own upbringing in a 1970s Danish commune to shape the film's depiction of communal living. Born in 1969, Vinterberg moved with his family at age seven into a large house north of Copenhagen, where his intellectual parentshis father a film criticand their professional friends established a collective household that lasted until he was 19. This environment, characterized by an optimistic, free-spirited atmosphere among left-leaning academics, fostered a sense of equality and shared experiences, though it was not the stereotypical hippie enclave of rampant drugs or unchecked sexuality; instead, it involved occasional excesses like "a few too many beers" amid a largely normal, atheist lifestyle. Vinterberg's immersion in this setting provided the authentic emotional texture for the film's portrayal of group dynamics, blending warmth with underlying tensions.
The initial concept for The Commune emerged from Vinterberg's reflections on his parents' idealistic experiment and its profound emotional consequences. As the commune eventually dissolved amid separations, divorces, and the harsh realities of aging, Vinterberg grappled with the loss of that communal innocence and the personal toll it exacted on relationships, including themes of replacement and impermanence that echoed his family's experiences. He described the film as "based on a true feeling" rather than a direct autobiography, using these memories to explore how open-minded ideals could lead to breakdowns, particularly for women like the protagonist Anna, whose generosity unravels under strain. This personal introspection transformed anecdotal recollections into a narrative examining love's fragility within collective ideals.
The film's historical backdrop was inspired by Denmark's 1970s counterculture movement, a period of widespread experimentation with alternative living as a rebellion against conservative societal norms and housing shortages. This era saw the rise of squatting and collective households, most notably the establishment of Freetown Christiania in 1971, when hippies and activists occupied an abandoned military barracks in Copenhagen to create an autonomous, self-governing enclave emphasizing consensus and anti-authoritarianism. Such initiatives reflected broader leftist pushes for communal solidarity and freedom from nuclear family constraints, influencing Vinterberg's portrayal of idealism clashing with reality.
Vinterberg conceived the idea in the early 2010s, initially developing it as a stage play premiered in Vienna in 2011, before expanding it into a screenplay that broadened personal stories into a commentary on utopian aspirations. This evolution allowed him to defend the commune's legacy against clichs, highlighting its role in fostering camaraderie while acknowledging inevitable conflicts.

Screenplay

The screenplay for The Commune was co-written by director Thomas Vinterberg and Tobias Lindholm, adapting an original stage play of the same name co-authored by Vinterberg and Mogens Rukov. The play, first developed through improvisational workshops with actors in Vienna, served as the foundation, capturing initial ensemble interactions in a confined setting.
In transitioning the material to film, Vinterberg and Lindholm expanded the narrative's spatial dynamics to suit cinematic storytelling, moving beyond the play's single-kitchen focus to incorporate multiple rooms and locations within the commune house. This adaptation amplified the ensemble's interpersonal tensions, allowing for a broader exploration of group living while retaining the core emotional authenticity derived from Vinterberg's personal history. The revisions emphasized fluid character movements across the house, revealing hidden conflicts that the stage version could only imply through dialogue.
The resulting script runs 111 minutes, striking a balance between extended, dialogue-driven commune assembliessuch as house meetings and shared mealsand quieter, intimate family exchanges that highlight individual vulnerabilities. Vinterberg and Lindholm prioritized naturalistic dialogue, drawing on 1970s Danish vernacular to evoke the era's communal idealism and interpersonal candor, often sidestepping overt dramatics for subtle, overlapping conversations.
Key revisions during development integrated Vinterberg's own childhood recollections from growing up in a 1970s academic commune near Copenhagen, lending realism to the script's depictions of conflict escalation and resolution. These personal elements, including memories of communal fights, a family divorce, and the impermanence of group bonds, were fictionalized but informed adjustments to character arcs, particularly shifting narrative emphasis from the teenage daughter to the central couple's unraveling marriage for heightened emotional depth. This infusion ensured the screenplay's resolutions felt grounded in lived experience rather than contrived theatrics.

Production

Pre-production and Casting

Pre-production for The Commune (original Danish title: Kollektivet) began with development rooted in director Thomas Vinterberg's personal experiences growing up in a 1970s commune, which he first adapted into a stage play for Vienna's Burgtheater in 2011. The screenplay, co-written with Tobias Lindholm, evolved from this theatrical foundation, emphasizing ensemble dynamics and period authenticity. Produced by Sisse Graum Jrgensen and Morten Kaufmann under Zentropa Entertainment, the film received support from the Danish Film Institute as part of its national funding for Danish features, alongside international backing including the EU MEDIA Programme's Prix Media award in 2013, which recognized its potential for European distribution. Set design focused on recreating 1970s Danish aesthetics, with principal locations filmed in a large villa in the upscale Hellerup suburb of northern Copenhagen, inspired by the era's communal living spaces from Vinterberg's childhood.
Casting prioritized actors capable of conveying familial chemistry and emotional nuance, drawing from Vinterberg's prior collaborations to foster natural ensemble interactions. Trine Dyrholm was selected as Anna, the TV anchor navigating personal crisis, for her proven emotional depth in Vinterberg's earlier work Festen (1998), where their longstanding rapport had matured over nearly two decades. Ulrich Thomsen reprised a leading role as Erik, the ambitious professor, reuniting with Dyrholm and Vinterberg from Festen to leverage their established on-screen tension and intimacy. Helene Reingaard Neumann, Vinterberg's wife and a stage actress, was cast as Emma, the young student, bringing theatrical precision to the role. Newcomer Martha Sofie Wallstrm Hansen portrayed Freja, the teenage daughter, selected for her fresh perspective in a "loveable family" dynamic. Supporting roles included Lars Ranthe as Ole, Fares Fares as Allon, and Julie Agnete Vang as Mona, with several performers chosen from Denmark's theater scene to align with the project's stage origins and enhance improvisational group scenes.
Rehearsals underscored the film's emphasis on collective improvisation within the script, with lead actors Dyrholm and Thomsen requiring about 48 hours to refine their character arcs due to the clarity of their roles. The ensemble commune members, including Ranthe, Fares, and Vang, underwent 10 days of intensive sessions involving discussions, scene work, and even shared drinks to build cohesion and spontaneity reflective of 1970s communal life. This preparatory approach allowed Vinterberg to prioritize interpersonal chemistry over rigid blocking, ensuring the cast embodied the film's exploration of solidarity and conflict.

Principal Photography

Principal photography for The Commune commenced in November 2014 and lasted eight weeks, wrapping in early 2015. The production filmed on location in Copenhagen, Denmark, utilizing real sites such as a large villa in the upscale Hellerup suburb to authentically evoke the 1970s setting of communal living. Some scenes were also captured in Sweden to support the narrative's scope.
Cinematographer Jesper Tffner employed a post-Dogme 95 aesthetic, relying heavily on handheld camerawork to foster intimacy and spontaneity among the ensemble cast. This approach was complemented by an emphasis on natural lighting, which enhanced the film's naturalistic tone and captured the organic flow of group interactions. Production designer Niels Sejer meticulously recreated period-specific details, including 1970s furniture, clothing, and interior decor, to immerse viewers in the era's domestic and social environment. Costume designer Ellen Lens contributed to this authenticity with era-appropriate attire that reflected the characters' bohemian yet middle-class sensibilities. The final edit, handled by Anne sterud and Janus Billeskov Jansen, resulted in a runtime of 110 minutes, streamlining the footage to highlight key emotional beats.
Filming presented challenges in coordinating the ensemble dynamics, particularly in extended roundtable dinner scenes that required synchronizing multiple actors' performances to convey the commune's evolving tensions. Director Thomas Vinterberg addressed these by favoring long takes, allowing natural overlaps in dialogue and improvisation to mirror the unpredictable nature of communal life. This method demanded precise blocking in the villa's confined spaces but yielded raw, unfiltered portrayals of group harmony and discord.
The production was overseen by producers Sisse Graum Jrgensen and Morten Kaufmann of Zentropa Entertainments, ensuring a collaborative Danish-led process. Post-production, including editing and sound design, was completed in Denmark, finalizing the film for its early 2016 release.

Release

World Premiere

The Commune had its international premiere at the 66th Berlin International Film Festival on February 17, 2016, where it competed in the main competition for the Golden Bear award. Directed by Thomas Vinterberg, the film marked a significant return to Danish-language cinema following his English-language project Far from the Madding Crowd (2015), drawing on Vinterberg's own childhood experiences in a 1970s Copenhagen commune.
Following its Berlin debut, the film continued its festival run with a screening as a Special Presentation at the 41st Toronto International Film Festival in September 2016. It also featured prominently on the European Film Awards circuit, earning nominations including for Best Actress (Trine Dyrholm) and a win for Best Editing. These festival appearances provided early international exposure, emphasizing the film's exploration of communal living and personal relationships.
The selection for Berlin generated buzz around Vinterberg's reconnection with his Danish roots and the film's semi-autobiographical elements, positioning it as a poignant return to intimate, character-driven storytelling after more commercial ventures. Initial audience and critic reactions at the premiere highlighted the movie's emotional intensity, particularly in its portrayal of familial tensions and the bittersweet unraveling of utopian ideals, with performances by Trine Dyrholm and Ulrich Thomsen noted for their raw depth.
Amid the festival circuit, distribution deals were announced to broaden the film's reach: Magnolia Pictures acquired North American rights shortly after the Berlin premiere, while Nordisk Film handled distribution in Denmark and Scandinavia, aligning with the production's regional focus. These agreements underscored the film's appeal as a prestige drama with universal themes of love and community.

Commercial Performance

The Commune was released theatrically in Denmark on January 14, 2016, marking its commercial debut in its home market. International rollouts followed, including Italy on March 31, 2016; the United Kingdom on July 29, 2016; and France on January 18, 2017. In the United States, it received a limited release on May 19, 2017, primarily in art-house theaters.
The film achieved a worldwide box office gross of $4.41 million. It performed strongly in Denmark, earning $2.82 million, and saw solid returns in other Nordic countries due to regional familiarity with director Thomas Vinterberg's work and the film's cultural resonance. In contrast, its U.S. performance was modest at $38,737, limited by the subtitled Danish-language presentation and competition within the independent drama market.
Distribution was managed by Nordisk Film across Scandinavia, leveraging the company's established network for Danish productions. Magnolia Pictures handled U.S. rights, acquiring the film following its festival circuit and releasing it through limited theatrical engagements. Home media releases, including DVD and Blu-ray editions, became available in 2017 via Magnolia Pictures in North America. As of 2025, the film is accessible for streaming on platforms such as Netflix.
The film's market performance benefited from initial buzz generated by its Berlin International Film Festival competition entry, which heightened interest in key European territories. However, it navigated a crowded drama genre landscape, where audience preferences for subtitled foreign films remained niche outside of festival-driven audiences.

Reception

Critical Reviews

"The Commune" received generally positive reviews from critics, earning a 71% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 92 reviews, with an average score of 6.4/10. On Metacritic, the film holds a score of 60 out of 100 from 22 critics, indicating mixed or average reviews. Critics frequently praised the strong ensemble performances, particularly Trine Dyrholm's nuanced portrayal of Anna's struggle with depression and marital strain, which anchored the film's emotional depth. Vinterberg's direction was commended for effectively building tension within the group dynamics, while the film's recreation of 1970s Copenhagen aesthetics, including period costumes and set design, added authenticity to the communal experiment.
However, some reviewers criticized the narrative for its predictable plot progression and uneven pacing, particularly in the drawn-out commune scenes that occasionally veered into melodrama. Comparisons to Vinterberg's earlier work, such as "The Celebration" (1998), highlighted the film as less innovative and ferocious, with a sweeter, soapier tone that diluted its dramatic impact.
Notable critiques included Sheila O'Malley's review for RogerEbert.com, which awarded the film 2 out of 4 stars and faulted its sentimentality as suspect, despite praising the brilliant acting ensemble. Peter Bradshaw of The Guardian offered a positive take, appreciating the relational drama and Dyrholm's magnetic performance that elevated the fluent family melodrama. Guy Lodge in Variety commended the emotional core and Dyrholm's gutsy emoting, though noted the film's unevenness and occasional manipulative subplots.

Accolades

At the 66th Berlin International Film Festival in 2016, The Commune competed in the main competition, earning a nomination for the Golden Bear for Best Film, while Trine Dyrholm received the Silver Bear for Best Actress for her portrayal of Anna.
In 2017, The Commune garnered five nominations at the 70th Bodil Awards, Denmark's oldest and most prestigious film honors, including Best Film, Best Actress (Dyrholm), Best Actor (Ulrich Thomsen), Best Supporting Actress (Martha Sofie Wallstrm Hansen), and Best Supporting Actor (Lars Ranthe); Dyrholm won the Best Actress award, marking her sixth Bodil in that category.
The film also received recognition at the 34th Robert Awards, the Danish equivalents organized by the Danish Film Academy, where Dyrholm won Best Actressher ninth Robert overalland the film secured Best Adapted Screenplay for Thomas Vinterberg and Tobias Lindholm.
At the 29th European Film Awards in 2016, Dyrholm was nominated for European Actress, and the film won the European Film Award for Editing for Anne sterud and Janus Billeskov Jansen, highlighting its technical achievements alongside its acting accolades.
The Commune was among the three films shortlisted by the Danish Oscar Committee for Denmark's submission to the 89th Academy Awards in the Best Foreign Language Film category but was not selected; Denmark ultimately submitted Land of Mine.
