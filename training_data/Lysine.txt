Lysine

1x
 Lysine is an essential -amino acid with the molecular formula CHNO, featuring a positively charged side chain that terminates in a primary amine group, making it one of the basic amino acids critical for protein structure and function. As a proteinogenic amino acid, it serves as a building block for proteins and is indispensable for human nutrition because the body cannot synthesize it, requiring dietary intake to support growth, development, and various metabolic processes.
In biological systems, lysine plays key roles beyond protein synthesis, including post-translational modifications such as acetylation and methylation that regulate gene expression and histone function, as well as serving as a precursor for the synthesis of carnitine, which is vital for fatty acid transport and energy production in mitochondria. It also contributes to collagen crosslinking through the formation of hydroxylysine, essential for connective tissue integrity and wound healing. Lysine deficiencies, often limiting in cereal-based diets, can impair growth and immune function, highlighting its status as a nutritionally critical nutrient.
Dietary sources of lysine are abundant in high-quality proteins such as meat, fish, eggs, dairy, and soybeans, while plant-based options like beans, peas, lentils, and quinoa provide substantial amounts, though bioavailability may vary. Supplementation is sometimes used to address deficiencies in populations reliant on low-lysine staples like corn or wheat, supporting overall protein quality and health outcomes.

Chemical Properties

Structure and Nomenclature

Lysine is an -amino acid with the molecular formula C6_66H14_{14}14N2_22O2_22, characterized by a central -carbon atom bonded to a hydrogen atom, a carboxyl group, an amino group, and a side chain consisting of a butylamine moiety, -(CH2_22)4_44NH2_22. This side chain extends the carbon backbone to six atoms, distinguishing lysine from shorter-chain amino acids.
The systematic IUPAC name for the biologically active form is (2S)-2,6-diaminohexanoic acid, reflecting the positions of the two amino groups on the hexanoic acid chain. In biochemical contexts, it is commonly abbreviated as Lys or K, following the standard three-letter and one-letter codes for amino acids.
Lysine exhibits chirality at the -carbon (position 2), existing as two enantiomers: the L-isomer, which is the naturally occurring form incorporated into proteins, and the D-isomer, a rare enantiomer primarily associated with microbial metabolites rather than standard protein synthesis. The L-configuration corresponds to the (S) absolute configuration in the Cahn-Ingold-Prelog priority rules.
In the molecular structure, the hexanoic acid chain is numbered starting from the carboxyl carbon as position 1, with the -amino group attached to carbon 2 and the -amino group (a primary amine) at the terminal carbon 6, enabling lysine's basic properties and roles in ionic interactions. These functional groupsthe -carboxyl (-COOH), -amino (-NH2_22), and -amino (-NH2_22)confer distinct reactivity, particularly under physiological conditions where protonation states vary with pH.

Physical and Chemical Characteristics

Lysine appears as a white crystalline solid at room temperature. It has a melting point of 224.5 C, at which point it decomposes rather than fully melting. Lysine exhibits high solubility in water, approximately 584 g/L at 25 C, owing to its polar and ionic character, while it shows low solubility in common organic solvents such as ethanol, acetone, and ether.
The chemical properties of lysine are dominated by its basic nature, stemming from the -amino group on the side chain, which contributes an additional protonation site compared to neutral amino acids. The pKa values are approximately 2.18 for the -carboxylic acid group, 8.95 for the -ammonium group, and 10.53 for the -ammonium group, reflecting stepwise deprotonation in aqueous solution. This results in an isoelectric point (pI) of about 9.74, the pH at which the net charge is zero, making lysine positively charged at physiological pH.
In terms of reactivity, lysine readily forms salts with acids due to its basic amino groups; a common example is lysine hydrochloride, which enhances its stability and solubility in certain formulations. The - and -amino groups are susceptible to acylation reactions, where they react with acylating agents to form amides, altering the molecule's charge and reactivity. Lysine also participates in peptide bond formation through its -amino and -carboxylic groups, enabling polymerization under appropriate conditions.
Lysine demonstrates good stability under neutral pH and moderate temperatures but decomposes upon heating above its melting point or under strongly acidic or basic conditions, where protonation or deprotonation extremes can lead to side reactions. It remains stable in aqueous solutions at room temperature for extended periods, provided oxidative or hydrolytic agents are absent.

Biosynthesis

Diaminopimelate (DAP) Pathway

The diaminopimelate (DAP) pathway serves as the primary biosynthetic route for L-lysine in most bacteria, including Escherichia coli, certain archaea, and plants, where it diverges from the aspartate amino acid family at L-aspartate--semialdehyde. This pathway not only produces L-lysine for protein synthesis but also generates meso-diaminopimelic acid (meso-DAP), an essential component of peptidoglycan in bacterial cell walls, highlighting its dual role in growth and structural integrity. Unlike the -aminoadipate pathway predominant in fungi and higher eukaryotes, the DAP route relies on aspartate and pyruvate as initial substrates, underscoring metabolic diversity across kingdoms.
The pathway comprises 10 enzymatic steps leading to meso-DAP, followed by an 11th step to form L-lysine. It initiates with the ATP-dependent phosphorylation of L-aspartate by aspartokinase (LysC in E. coli) to yield L-4-aspartyl phosphate, which is then reduced by aspartate--semialdehyde dehydrogenase (Asd) using NADPH to produce L-aspartate--semialdehyde. The lysine-specific branch commences with the condensation of L-aspartate--semialdehyde and pyruvate, catalyzed by dihydrodipicolinate synthase (DapA), forming 4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate; this intermediate is promptly reduced by dihydrodipicolinate reductase (DapB) with NADPH to 2,3,4,5-tetrahydrodipicolinate. In the succinylase variant employed by E. coli and many Gram-negative bacteria, subsequent modifications include N-succinylation of tetrahydrodipicolinate by N-succinyldiaminopimelate aminotransferase/succinyltransferase (DapD) using succinyl-CoA, yielding N-succinyl-2-amino-6-oxopimelatic acid. This is followed by transamination via a dedicated aminotransferase (often DapC) to N-succinyl-L,L-diaminopimelate, desuccinylation by N-succinyl-L,L-diaminopimelate desuccinylase (DapE) to L,L-diaminopimelate, epimerization by diaminopimelate epimerase (DapF) to meso-DAP, and finally decarboxylation of meso-DAP by diaminopimelate decarboxylase (LysA) to L-lysine, releasing CO. Variants like the acetylase or dehydrogenase routes occur in some Gram-positive bacteria, such as Bacillus subtilis, but the core steps remain conserved. Regulation of the DAP pathway primarily occurs through feedback inhibition, where L-lysine allosterically inhibits aspartokinase isozymes (e.g., LysC in E. coli), preventing overproduction and conserving precursors for other aspartate-derived amino acids like threonine and methionine. Additional control involves transcriptional attenuation and repressor binding to operons like lysC and dap genes in response to lysine levels. This tight regulation ensures balanced flux in nutrient-limited environments.
Evolutionarily, the DAP pathway is a hallmark of prokaryotic metabolism, likely emerging early in bacterial evolution to support peptidoglycan synthesis for cell wall rigidity, a trait absent in eukaryotes. Its conservation across diverse bacterial phyla and acquisition by plants via endosymbiosis reflects adaptive pressures for robust amino acid and cell envelope biosynthesis. Targeting enzymes like DapA or LysA has been explored for antibiotic development due to this prokaryote-specific feature.

-Aminoadipate (AAA) Pathway

The -aminoadipate (AAA) pathway represents an alternative route for the de novo biosynthesis of L-lysine, primarily utilized by fungi and certain bacteria, but absent in animals and higher plants. This pathway initiates with the condensation of -ketoglutarate and acetyl-CoA to form homocitrate, diverging from the aspartate-derived diaminopimelate (DAP) pathway common in prokaryotes. In eukaryotes such as the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae, the process involves approximately seven dedicated enzymes that convert these precursors through a series of condensations, isomerizations, oxidations, transamination, and reductions to yield L-lysine.
Key steps begin with homocitrate synthase catalyzing the ATP-dependent condensation of -ketoglutarate and acetyl-CoA to homocitrate, a reaction subject to feedback inhibition by lysine. Subsequent isomerization of homocitrate to homoisocitrate requires two enzymes from the aconitase family: homoaconitate hydratase, which dehydrates homocitrate to cis-homoaconitate, and homoisocitrate hydratase, which rehydrates it to homoisocitrate. Homoisocitrate dehydrogenase then oxidatively decarboxylates homoisocitrate to 2-oxoadipate using NAD+ as a cofactor. Transamination by 2-oxoadipate aminotransferase converts 2-oxoadipate to -aminoadipate, followed by reduction of -aminoadipate to -aminoadipate--semialdehyde via -aminoadipate reductase, an NADPH-dependent enzyme encoded by LYS2 in yeast. The final steps proceed through saccharopine intermediates: saccharopine reductase (LYS5) condenses the semialdehyde with glutamate to form saccharopine, and saccharopine dehydrogenase (LYS1) cleaves saccharopine to L-lysine and -ketoglutarate, regenerating the initial precursor. These enzymes are encoded by the LYS gene cluster in S. cerevisiae, including LYS1, LYS2, LYS3, LYS4 (homocitrate synthase), LYS5, LYS12 (homoisocitrate dehydrogenase), and LYS20 (an additional homocitrate synthase isoform).
Regulation of the AAA pathway is primarily transcriptional and post-translational, with less stringent feedback inhibition compared to the DAP pathway. In S. cerevisiae, the LYS4 and LYS20 gene products (homocitrate synthases) are repressed by lysine-mediated activation of the Lys14p transcription factor, which binds to upstream activating sequences in the LYS gene promoters. However, the pathway exhibits lower sensitivity to end-product inhibition, allowing sustained flux under varying nutritional conditions. This contrasts with the DAP pathway's prokaryotic counterpart, which features multiple feedback-sensitive isozymes.
The overall reaction can be summarized as: acetyl-CoA + 2 glutamate + 2 NADPH + 2 NAD + ATP L-lysine + 2 -ketoglutarate + CoA + 2 NADP + 2 NADH + ADP + P_i + CO (net balance, with initial -ketoglutarate recycled). The absence of the AAA pathway in animals underscores lysine's essentiality in mammalian diets, as vertebrates lack the machinery for its de novo synthesis and rely instead on dietary intake.

Metabolism

Catabolism

In mammals, lysine catabolism primarily proceeds via the saccharopine pathway, which predominates in the liver and kidney mitochondria and accounts for the majority of lysine degradation in extracerebral tissues. This pathway initiates with the irreversible condensation of L-lysine and -ketoglutarate to form saccharopine, catalyzed by the lysine-ketoglutarate reductase activity of the bifunctional enzyme lysine-ketoglutarate reductase/saccharopine dehydrogenase (LKR/SDH). The subsequent step involves the saccharopine dehydrogenase activity of the same enzyme, which hydrolyzes saccharopine to yield -aminoadipate semialdehyde (AASA) and glutamate, accompanied by the reduction of NAD to NADH.

Further -oxidation-like reactions convert crotonyl-CoA through 3-hydroxybutyryl-CoA and acetoacetyl-CoA to two molecules of acetyl-CoA, mediated by enoyl-CoA hydratase, 3-hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydrogenase, and thiolase, respectively.

Glutamate can be further metabolized or used in transamination reactions.
An alternative route, the pipecolate pathway, operates mainly in the brain and some peripheral tissues during development and adulthood, where lysine undergoes -deamination to -keto--aminocaproate (which spontaneously cyclizes to -piperideine-2-carboxylate), potentially catalyzed by kynurenine aminotransferase III, then reduced to L-pipecolate, and subsequently oxidized to -piperideine-6-carboxylate by peroxisomal pipecolate oxidase before conversion to AASA, joining the saccharopine pathway downstream.
Regulation of the saccharopine pathway occurs at the level of LKR/SDH expression, which increases in the liver under conditions of starvation or acute lysine loading, potentially influenced by hormonal signals such as glucagon to mobilize amino acid breakdown for gluconeogenesis.

Absorption and Transport

Lysine is primarily absorbed in the small intestine through active transport mechanisms in enterocytes. Apical uptake occurs via the sodium-independent system b0,+, formed by the heterodimer of SLC3A1 (rBAT) and SLC7A9 (b0,+AT), which transports lysine along with other dibasic amino acids like arginine and ornithine, as well as cystine. This system exhibits high affinity for lysine and operates efficiently at the brush border membrane, facilitating its entry from the intestinal lumen. Basolateral efflux involves the y+L system, comprising SLC7A7 (y+LAT1) paired with SLC3A2 (4F2hc), which exchanges lysine for neutral amino acids in a sodium-dependent manner. Absorption is pH-sensitive, with optimal transport at slightly acidic luminal conditions typical of the proximal small intestine.
Once absorbed, lysine enters the portal bloodstream either as free amino acid or bound to plasma proteins such as albumin. In circulation, free lysine constitutes the majority, with typical plasma concentrations ranging from 0.15 to 0.3 mM in healthy adults under fasting conditions. These levels reflect a balance between dietary intake, intestinal absorption, and tissue utilization, with bound forms providing a reservoir during periods of high demand.
At the cellular level, lysine uptake into various tissues is mediated by cationic amino acid transporters of the y+ system, particularly CAT-1 (SLC7A1), which facilitates Na+-independent, electrogenic transport driven by the membrane potential. CAT-1 exhibits high affinity for lysine (Km in the micromolar range) and is ubiquitously expressed, enabling efficient accumulation in cells like hepatocytes and myocytes. The y+ system predominates in non-epithelial cells, ensuring lysine availability for protein synthesis and other functions.
Lysine distribution favors protein-rich tissues such as skeletal muscle and liver, where it supports anabolism and metabolic processes. In the kidney, nearly all filtered lysine is reabsorbed in the proximal tubule via the apical b0,+ transporter (SLC3A1/SLC7A9), preventing urinary loss and maintaining systemic homeostasis. Renal reabsorption efficiency exceeds 99% under normal conditions, underscoring the transporter's role in conserving this essential amino acid.
Transport of lysine is subject to competition from structurally similar dibasic amino acids, notably arginine and ornithine, which share the same carrier systems like b0,+ and y+. High luminal or plasma concentrations of arginine can reduce lysine uptake by up to 50% in intestinal and renal epithelia, potentially impacting bioavailability during imbalanced diets. This competitive inhibition highlights the interconnected regulation of cationic amino acid homeostasis.

Nutritional Value

Essentiality and Requirements

Lysine is classified as an essential amino acid in humans and most animals because it cannot be synthesized de novo, owing to the absence of the diaminopimelate (DAP) and -aminoadipate (AAA) biosynthetic pathways.
The World Health Organization (WHO), in collaboration with the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) and United Nations University (UNU), recommends a dietary lysine intake of 38 mg/kg body weight per day for healthy adults as the recommended dietary allowance (RDA), based on the estimated average requirement of 30 mg/kg/day plus two standard deviations to account for variability. For infants aged 3-6 months, the requirement is higher at 97 mg/kg/day due to rapid growth demands, while needs for other age groups, such as children and adolescents, fall between these values. Athletes and individuals with high physical activity may require elevated lysine intakes, proportional to increased overall protein needs of up to 1.2-2.0 g/kg/day, to support muscle repair and recovery.
In animals, lysine requirements vary by species and production stage; for example, growing pigs typically need 1.0-1.2% standardized ileal digestible lysine in their diets to optimize growth and feed efficiency, as outlined in the National Research Council (NRC) guidelines.
Deficiency of lysine is rare in humans due to diverse diets but can occur in populations relying on lysine-poor staples like maize, leading to symptoms such as fatigue, nausea, dizziness, and impaired growth in children. In animals, lysine deficiency commonly manifests as stunted growth, reduced feed intake, anemia, and weakened immune function, particularly in monogastric species like pigs and poultry.
Lysine contributes to high protein digestibility-corrected amino acid score (PDCAAS) values of approximately 1.0 in high-quality proteins such as eggs, milk, and meat, where it meets or exceeds the reference pattern without being the limiting factor.

Dietary Sources

Lysine is obtained primarily through dietary protein sources, with animal-derived foods generally providing higher concentrations per serving compared to most plant-based options. Meats such as beef contain approximately 2.6 g of lysine per 100 g of cooked lean tenderloin, while poultry like chicken and turkey offers around 2.53.5 g per 100 g of roasted breast. Fish, including tuna (2.5 g per 100 g of fresh bluefin tuna), salmon (2.53.5 g per 100 g), cod (3.03.8 g per 100 g), and sardines, supplies high levels, and eggs provide 0.91.2 g per 100 g of whole raw eggs. Dairy products, particularly cheeses like parmesan (3.3 g per 100 g), mozzarella, ricotta, and cottage cheese (up to 1.5 g per 100 g), as well as yogurt and milk (0.81.5 g per 100 g), deliver significant amounts. Pork also provides 2.53.5 g per 100 g of lean cuts.
Plant-based sources of lysine are typically lower in concentration and often limiting in grains, necessitating diverse intake to meet nutritional needs. Legumes such as soybeans are among the richest, with mature seeds providing 2.7 g per 100 g, while cooked soybeans, tofu, and tempeh offer 1.02.0 g per 100 g. Other legumes like lentils, black beans, kidney beans, peas, and chickpeas provide 0.61.2 g per 100 g. Quinoa offers 0.70.9 g per 100 g of cooked grain. Nuts and seeds, such as pumpkin seeds (1.01.5 g per 100 g), almonds, and chia seeds, are also notable sources. In contrast, grains like wheat contain only 0.3 g per 100 g of whole wheat flour, highlighting lysine as a bottleneck in cereal-heavy diets.
The bioavailability of lysine varies by source, with animal proteins exhibiting higher absorption rates of 85-95% due to their complete amino acid profiles and minimal interfering compounds, whereas plant proteins range from 70-80% bioavailability, reduced by dietary fiber and antinutritional factors like phytates. To address deficiencies in lysine-poor staples, fortification of cereals with synthetic lysine is common, enhancing protein quality in products like wheat flour and bulgur, and lysine supplements are available in doses of 500-1000 mg per tablet for targeted intake. Most individuals obtain sufficient lysine from a varied diet; however, consulting a healthcare professional is recommended for precise nutritional needs.

Biological Roles

Protein Synthesis and Structural Functions

Lysine serves as one of the 20 standard amino acids incorporated into proteins during translation, where it is encoded by the codons AAA and AAG in the genetic code. These codons are recognized by transfer RNA molecules charged with lysine (tRNALys), which act as adaptors to deliver the amino acid to the ribosome's A-site during polypeptide chain elongation. The lysyl-tRNA synthetase enzyme specifically aminoacylates tRNALys with L-lysine, ensuring accurate decoding of mRNA sequences into protein primary structure.
In human proteins, lysine constitutes approximately 5.7% of all amino acid residues by frequency, reflecting its prevalence across the proteome and contributing to the overall charge and functionality of polypeptides. Beyond incorporation, lysine's -amino group enables key structural roles, particularly in extracellular matrix proteins. In collagen, lysyl oxidase oxidizes specific lysine residues to allysine (allysine), an aldehyde intermediate that undergoes spontaneous condensation to form covalent intermolecular cross-links, such as aldimine or aldol types, essential for fibril tensile strength and stability. Similarly, in elastin, lysine-derived allysine residues facilitate cross-linking to generate desmosine and isodesmosine bridges, conferring the protein's elastic recoil properties in tissues like arteries and lungs.
Lysine also plays a critical role in blood clotting through its involvement in fibrin stabilization. During coagulation, activated factor XIII (a transglutaminase) catalyzes the formation of -(-glutamyl)lysine isopeptide bonds between glutamine and lysine residues in adjacent fibrin monomers, cross-linking the polymer network to enhance clot mechanical strength and resistance to fibrinolysis.
Post-translational modifications of lysine further diversify its structural contributions. Acetylation of lysine residues on histone tails neutralizes their positive charge, reducing affinity for negatively charged DNA and promoting chromatin unfolding to facilitate gene transcription in epigenetic regulation. Conversely, ubiquitination involves the covalent attachment of ubiquitin to lysine side chains by E3 ubiquitin ligases, marking proteins for proteasomal degradation and thus controlling cellular protein turnover.

Specialized Metabolic Roles

Lysine serves as a critical precursor in the biosynthesis of carnitine, a compound essential for the transport of long-chain fatty acids into mitochondria for -oxidation. The process begins with the post-translational methylation of protein-bound lysine residues to form -N-trimethyllysine, primarily in muscle tissues, followed by its release and subsequent enzymatic conversion to butyrobetaine and then L-carnitine, with the final hydroxylation step occurring predominantly in the liver and kidneys. This pathway underscores lysine's role in energy metabolism, as carnitine deficiency can impair fatty acid utilization and lead to metabolic disorders.
In collagen maturation, specific lysine residues undergo 5-hydroxylation to form hydroxylysine, a modification catalyzed by lysyl hydroxylases that enhances the thermal stability of the collagen triple helix and enables subsequent O-glycosylation with galactose and glucose. These glycosylated hydroxylysines contribute to intermolecular cross-linking and interactions within the extracellular matrix, including associations with hyaluronan that support tissue architecture and resilience. The extent of lysine hydroxylation varies by collagen type, influencing fibril formation and overall structural integrity.
Lysine indirectly influences nitric oxide (NO) signaling through competition with arginine for uptake via cationic amino acid transporters, potentially limiting substrate availability for both arginase and nitric oxide synthase (NOS) enzymes. Elevated lysine levels can thus modulate arginase activity, which depletes arginine and reduces NO production, affecting vascular tone and immune responses.
In mammals, lysine can serve as a precursor for the polyamine cadaverine via lysine decarboxylase, supporting certain cellular processes, though cadaverine is less prominent in mammalian tissues compared to bacterial systems.
Although in vitro studies suggest lysine may inhibit herpes simplex virus replication by antagonizing arginine incorporation into viral capsid proteins, clinical evidence in humans remains inconclusive and lacks robust support for therapeutic use.

Health Implications

Roles in Disease

Lysine plays a critical role in several genetic disorders stemming from defects in its metabolism or transport. Hyperlysinemia type I results from biallelic pathogenic variants in the AASS gene, which encodes -aminoadipic semialdehyde synthase, a bifunctional enzyme catalyzing the first two steps in lysine degradation; this leads to elevated serum lysine levels due to impaired conversion to saccharopine. Saccharopinuria, or hyperlysinemia type II, arises from isolated deficiency in the saccharopine dehydrogenase domain of AASS, causing accumulation of saccharopine alongside hyperlysinemia and potential neurological symptoms such as developmental delay. Lysinuric protein intolerance (LPI) is an autosomal recessive disorder caused by pathogenic variants in SLC7A7, encoding the y+LAT1 transporter subunit essential for dibasic amino acid uptake in intestinal and renal epithelia; defective transport results in urinary loss of lysine, arginine, and ornithine, leading to hyperammonemia and multi-organ involvement including pulmonary alveolar proteinosis.
In infectious diseases, lysine has been investigated for its potential antagonistic effects against herpes simplex virus (HSV) replication, primarily through competition with arginine, which is required for viral capsid protein synthesis and DNA packaging. Early studies demonstrated that arginine deprivation suppresses HSV growth in cell culture, with lysine acting as a structural analog to inhibit this process. However, clinical trials from the late 1970s and 1980s yielded inconclusive results on lysine's efficacy for HSV prophylaxis or treatment; while some small-scale studies reported accelerated lesion healing with doses around 1 g/day, a systematic review concluded that supplementation below 1 g/day shows no consistent benefit in preventing recurrences of oral or genital herpes. A 2015 Cochrane systematic review found insufficient evidence for preventing herpes simplex labialis. As of 2026, evidence remains limited and mixed, with L-lysine supplementation appearing ineffective for prophylaxis at doses below 1 g/day without a low-arginine diet; higher doses may offer subjective benefits in some cases, but no universally recommended dosage exists for HSV prevention due to inconclusive data. Common suggested prophylactic regimens include 5001,000 mg daily, though some sources propose 1,0003,000 mg daily. Individuals considering L-lysine supplementation should consult a healthcare provider before use.
Dysregulation of lysine residues in histones contributes to oncogenesis in certain leukemias via altered acetylation patterns that affect gene expression. In acute myeloid leukemia (AML), overexpression or mutation of lysine acetyltransferases like KAT6A promotes aberrant histone H3 and H4 acetylation at specific sites, enhancing oncogenic transcription and leukemogenic potential. Similarly, in B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (B-ALL), histone acetylation imbalances mediated by HBO1 (KAT7) activate Wnt/-catenin signaling, driving proliferation and survival of leukemic cells.
Cardiovascular manifestations linked to lysine involve disruptions in carnitine biosynthesis, as carnitine is derived from lysine via trimethyllysine intermediate and is essential for fatty acid transport into mitochondria. Secondary carnitine deficiency from impaired lysine availability, as seen in transport defects like LPI, can impair myocardial energy metabolism, contributing to dilated cardiomyopathy through lipid accumulation and contractile dysfunction. Primary carnitine deficiencies, indirectly tied to lysine-dependent synthesis, similarly present with progressive cardiomyopathy in affected individuals.
Neurological involvement of lysine occurs in peroxisomal disorders where defects in -oxidation lead to accumulation of pipecolic acid, a lysine catabolite normally oxidized in peroxisomes. In Zellweger spectrum disorders, such as Zellweger syndrome, absence of functional peroxisomes impairs L-pipecolic acid oxidase activity, resulting in elevated pipecolic acid levels that may contribute to neurodegeneration, seizures, and hypotonia through neurotoxic effects or disrupted myelin formation.

Supplementation and Deficiency

Lysine deficiency is uncommon in developed countries due to the prevalence of lysine-rich animal proteins in typical diets, but it can occur in populations relying on low-protein or plant-based foods without adequate variety. Symptoms of deficiency primarily manifest as fatigue, anemia, decreased appetite, weight loss in adults, growth retardation in children, and weakened immune function, with severe cases potentially leading to mood disturbances and reproductive issues.
Supplementation with lysine is often recommended for specific groups to address potential imbalances. Athletes may benefit from lysine to support muscle repair and hypertrophy, as it activates satellite cells via mTORC1 signaling, promoting protein synthesis and recovery after exercise. Vegans, who may have up to 25% lower plasma lysine concentrations compared to omnivores due to lower intake from plant sources, can use supplements to bridge gaps in essential amino acid profiles. Typical supplemental doses range from 1 to 3 grams per day, which are generally safe for most adults, though intakes exceeding 10 grams daily may cause gastrointestinal upset such as stomach pain or diarrhea. In Japan, as of 2025, L-lysine supplements are widely available over-the-counter. They are sold online through platforms such as Amazon.co.jp and Rakuten, and in physical drugstores and pharmacies, including chains like Matsumoto Kiyoshi and Tsuruha. Many products are manufactured in Japan, with brands such as JAY&CO., NICHIGA, and Julia offering doses of 10001500 mg.
Supplementation during breastfeeding requires caution. According to the NIH's Drugs and Lactation Database (LactMed), L-lysine supplements are probably low risk during breastfeeding, as L-lysine is an essential amino acid naturally present in breast milk and there is no correlation between dietary intake and milk lysine levels. No information is available on the use of high-dose supplementation during breastfeeding, such as for herpes treatment, and caution is warranted when using any single amino acid at levels significantly above those normally found in food. An alternate drug may be preferred while nursing newborns or preterm infants due to limited data. Drugs.com advises against using lysine products while breastfeeding.
In therapeutic contexts, lysine serves as an adjunct for managing certain conditions. For herpes simplex virus infections, there is no universally recommended dosage for L-lysine supplementation due to mixed and limited evidence. Common suggestions for prophylaxis include 5001,000 mg daily, with some sources recommending 1,0003,000 mg daily. Evidence indicates that lysine may be ineffective at doses below 1 g/day unless combined with a low-arginine diet. Doses of 1 to 3 grams per day have shown mixed results in reducing outbreak frequency and severity, with some 2020s reviews supporting efficacy in preventing recurrences while others, including meta-analyses, find insufficient evidence for routine use. Individuals should consult a healthcare provider before use. Lysine may also aid osteoporosis management by enhancing collagen formation and supporting bone health through improved calcium utilization.

Applications

Use in Animal Feed

Lysine serves as the first limiting amino acid in corn-soybean meal diets commonly used for pigs and poultry, where natural sources often fail to meet growth demands, necessitating supplementation to achieve dietary requirements of approximately 0.9% to 1.1% for optimal performance in growing animals. For promoting intramuscular fat (IMF) in finishing pigs, a lower lysine to digestible energy (DE) ratio of 0.6-0.7 g/Mcal is recommended in the finishing phase, while maintaining 0.9-1.0 g/Mcal in earlier phases; supplement essential amino acids such as lysine, methionine, threonine, and tryptophan to meet ideal protein needs. In swine, lysine deficiency limits protein synthesis and muscle development, while in poultry, it ranks as the second limiting amino acid after sulfur-containing ones like methionine, impacting overall feed utilization in these monogastric species.
Feed-grade synthetic L-lysine hydrochloride (L-lysine HCl), containing about 78% lysine, is widely supplemented to address these shortfalls and is primarily produced through microbial fermentation using Corynebacterium glutamicum, a bacterium engineered for high-yield biosynthesis from glucose or other carbohydrates. As of 2024, global production exceeded 3.6 million metric tons, driven by the expanding livestock industry and demand for efficient protein sources. This fermentation process, refined over decades, ensures cost-effective scalability while maintaining the L-isomer essential for animal metabolism.
Supplementation with L-lysine HCl enhances feed efficiency and growth rates in livestock; for instance, in broilers, it can increase body weight gain by 10-20% and improve feed conversion ratios by optimizing nitrogen retention and protein accretion without excess crude protein in diets. Similar benefits occur in pigs, where adequate lysine levels support lean tissue growth and reduce feed costs by allowing formulation with lower soybean meal inclusion.
Economically, feed-grade lysine trades at approximately $1-2 per kg, with major production centered in China, which dominates global supply, followed by facilities in the USA operated by companies like Cargill and ADM. In 2025, prices experienced volatility, collapsing in July due to aggressive Chinese exports before booming in November; additionally, the EU imposed anti-dumping duties on Chinese imports in early 2025 to protect domestic producers. Regulatory oversight by the FDA in the United States and the European Commission in the EU imposes strict limits on impurities, such as heavy metals (e.g., arsenic below 2 ppm) and microbiological contaminants, to safeguard animal health and prevent residues in meat products. These standards ensure the additive's safety as authorized under frameworks like EU Regulation 1831/2003 for feed additives.

Industrial and Cultural Uses

Lysine serves as a key precursor in the industrial production of polyamides, particularly -poly-L-lysine (-PL), a biodegradable homopolymer synthesized via microbial fermentation that exhibits antimicrobial properties and is used in food preservation and biomedical applications. This nylon-like polyamide is valued for its natural origin and eco-friendly degradation profile compared to synthetic alternatives.
In the pharmaceutical sector, lysine is incorporated into various drug formulations to enhance solubility and bioavailability, such as in lysine theophyllinate, which is employed for treating respiratory conditions like asthma and bronchitis by providing a stable salt form of theophylline. Additionally, lysine-derived compounds contribute to antifibrinolytic agents that inhibit blood clot breakdown, aiding in surgical and trauma care.
Cosmetics leverage lysine derivatives for hair care, with lauroyl lysine acting as a conditioning agent that improves hair smoothness, shine, and manageability by forming a protective film on the hair shaft without weighing it down. Quaternary ammonium salts derived from lysine, such as cationic surfactants, further enhance these effects by providing antistatic and softening properties in shampoos and conditioners.
In biotechnology, lysine is a critical component of cell culture media for producing monoclonal antibodies, typically supplemented at concentrations of 1-5 mM to support cell growth and protein synthesis in Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cell lines, though higher levels up to 10 mM can influence product quality attributes like charge variants.
Lysine-based materials are increasingly utilized in environmental applications through the development of biodegradable polymers, such as polyurethanes synthesized from lysine diisocyanate, which offer tunable mechanical properties and complete hydrolytic degradation, making them suitable for sustainable packaging and biomedical implants.
Culturally, lysine has appeared in science fiction literature, notably in Michael Crichton's 1990 novel Jurassic Park, where the "lysine contingency" is a genetic modification rendering cloned dinosaurs dependent on external lysine supplies to prevent their survival beyond controlled environments. In contemporary media, lysine features in vegan diet discussions during the 2020s, often highlighted as a potentially limiting essential amino acid in plant-based nutrition, prompting debates on supplementation to achieve balanced protein intake.
As of 2024, the global lysine market reflects its industrial significance, with production exceeding 3.6 million metric tons, of which approximately 10-15% is allocated to non-feed applications like those in pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, and polymers, driven by demand for bio-based materials.
