 The Neretva is a 225-kilometer-long karst river originating beneath Lebrnik Mountain in Bosnia and Herzegovina at an elevation of about 1,100 meters above sea level, flowing northwest through deep canyons and the city of Mostar before crossing into Croatia for its final 22 kilometers and discharging into the Adriatic Sea near Ploe, where it forms a biodiverse delta.
Renowned for its emerald waters and status as one of Europe's last relatively untamed rivers despite multiple upstream hydroelectric dams, the Neretva supports exceptional biodiversity, including endemic fish species like the softmouth trout and marble trout, and serves as a critical wetland habitat in its delta for migratory birds and fisheries.
The river's course through the Dinaric Alps has historically facilitated human settlement, trade, and conflict, notably the World War II Battle of the Neretva, while its ecological integrity faces ongoing threats from damming, pollution, and development that have fragmented habitats and altered natural flows.

Geography and Hydrology

Course and Physical Characteristics

The Neretva River originates from five springs on the steep, forested slopes of the Lebrnik and Zelengora mountains in southeastern Bosnia and Herzegovina, at an elevation of 1,227 meters above sea level. It flows southward for a total length of 225 kilometers, with approximately 203 kilometers in Bosnia and Herzegovina and 22 kilometers in Croatia before emptying into the Adriatic Sea near the port of Ploe via a deltaic estuary. The river's basin covers 11,798 square kilometers, predominantly within the Dinaric Alps karstic terrain, which influences its hydrological regime through high permeability and subterranean flows.
In its upper course, from the source to Konjic (about 90 kilometers), the Neretva exhibits a steep gradient, carving deep canyons and gorges through limestone bedrock, with elevations dropping rapidly from over 1,200 meters to around 300 meters. This section features turbulent rapids and limited sediment load due to the karst substrate, maintaining cold water temperatures rarely exceeding 7C even in summer. The middle course, passing through Jablanica and Mostar, transitions to broader valleys interrupted by additional gorges, where the river's slope moderates but still supports high velocities; here, hydroelectric dams like those at Jablanica and Grabovica have impounded reservoirs, altering natural flow dynamics while the underlying physical morphology retains narrow, incised channels flanked by steep slopes.
The lower course enters Croatia near Metkovi, widening into a marshy floodplain with reduced gradient, forming a complex delta of approximately 20,000 hectares characterized by tidal influences, karst springs, and braided channels mixing freshwater with seawater. The river's mean annual discharge at the estuary measures 325 cubic meters per second, with significant seasonal variabilitypeaking in winter floods up to several thousand cubic meters per second and dropping to 40 cubic meters per second in dry summersdriven by pluvial recharge in the mountainous headwaters rather than glacial melt. Overall, the Neretva's morphology reflects a high-energy, erosive system in the upstream reaches transitioning to depositional lowlands, with minimal alluvial buildup due to karst dissolution and coarse bedload.

Tributaries and Human Settlements

The Neretva receives over 30 tributaries along its 225 km course, with notable ones including the Rakitnica in the upper reaches, which spans 26 km and joins near Glavatievo after traversing a deep canyon. In the middle section, tributaries such as the Dreanka and Drenica contribute to the flow before the river widens into Jablanica Lake. 
Lower Neretva tributaries include the Trebiat on the right bank, which feeds the system through approximately 50 karst springs; the Bregava; the Krupa, originating in the Hutovo Blato wetlands; the Buna; and the Norin, which flows through extensive reedbeds. These inflows support the river's high discharge, peaking at 1,500 m/s during floods, and sustain downstream ecosystems.
Human settlements cluster primarily in the middle and lower valleys, where the terrain broadens, enabling agriculture and urban development. In Bosnia and Herzegovina, Konjic (estimated population 30,000) marks the upper-middle transition, followed by Jablanica near the reservoir lake, and Mostar (over 105,000 residents), a historical trade hub straddling the river. Downstream, apljina (around 19,000) precedes the Croatian border.
In Croatia, settlements intensify in the deltaic lower reaches, including Opuzen (3,600 inhabitants), where the river branches into channels, and Metkovi (13,400), a key town supporting fisheries and tourism near the Adriatic outlet. Smaller villages like Poitelj and Vid feature Ottoman-era architecture and Roman archaeological sites, respectively, reflecting millennia of human adaptation to the riverine landscape. Hydropower dams, such as those at Jablanica and Grabovica, have altered flows, displacing communities like Ulog (now reduced to 12 residents from 400).

Upper Neretva

Geological Features and Rakitnica Confluence

The Upper Neretva basin lies within the Dinaric Alps, featuring a karst landscape primarily composed of Mesozoic limestones and dolomites that facilitate extensive dissolution and erosion processes. This karstic terrain, part of the broader Dinaride orogenic belt, includes tectonic structures such as thrusts and folds resulting from Alpine compression, which control the river's incision and valley morphology. The Neretva's upper course cuts through narrow canyons with steep limestone cliffs, exemplifying fluvial-karst interaction where surface runoff rapidly erodes soluble bedrock, forming deep gorges and intermittent underground drainage.
The Rakitnica River, the principal right-bank tributary, confluences with the Neretva after traversing a 26-kilometer canyon that constitutes most of its 32-kilometer length, separating the Bjelanica and Visoica massifs in central Bosnia's Dinaric Karst. This canyon, incised into thick Cretaceous limestones, exhibits morphostructures influenced by neotectonic activity, including fault-guided valleys and inner gorges formed by accelerated headward erosion during Pleistocene climatic shifts. At the confluence, the Rakitnica's high-gradient flow merges with the Neretva, amplifying downstream erosive capacity in a tectonically fractured zone that enhances gorge deepening and lateral undercutting of carbonate walls.
Geological surveys indicate that the confluence area features periadriatic flysch intercalations amid dominant carbonates, contributing to heterogeneous erosion rates and localized instability, such as rockfalls along fault lines. These features underscore the region's vulnerability to seismic activity, given its position in a seismically active segment of the Dinarides, while preserving endemic karst aquifers recharged by the rivers' catchments.

Middle Neretva

Lakes and Hydrological Features

The middle course of the Neretva River, extending from Konjic to Mostar, is dominated by artificial reservoirs forming a hydropower cascade that regulates the river's hydrology. These include Jablanica Lake, Grabovica Lake, and Salakovac Lake, constructed sequentially downstream to harness the river's steep gradient in its canyon sections for electricity generation and flow management.
Jablanica Lake, the uppermost and largest reservoir in this sequence, was created in 1953 by the Jablanica Hydroelectric Power Plant dam, impounding the Neretva over a length of approximately 30 kilometers with a surface area of 13 square kilometers and maximum depths up to 80 meters. Water levels in the lake fluctuate by as much as 25 meters annually due to operational demands, enabling storage for peak power production while attenuating floods from upstream karst inflows.
Downstream, Grabovica Lake follows, serving as an intermediate storage basin in the cascade, before Salakovac Lake, impounded since 1981 by the Salakovac Hydroelectric Power Plant with a capacity supporting 210 megawatts of generation. These reservoirs collectively transform the middle Neretva's natural torrential, karst-influenced regimecharacterized by high spring and autumn dischargesinto a controlled flow that reduces seasonal variability and sediment delivery to lower reaches, though at the cost of ecological discontinuities.
Hydrological monitoring indicates that the cascade stabilizes discharges for downstream users but amplifies vulnerabilities to drought-induced low flows in summer, when natural Adriatic basin rivers typically recede. The system's design prioritizes energy output over mimicking pre-dam dynamics, resulting in altered water quality gradients and reduced peak flows essential for maintaining channel morphology.

Lower Neretva and Delta

Wetlands and Estuarine Systems

The wetlands of the lower Neretva River primarily encompass Hutovo Blato Nature Park in Bosnia and Herzegovina, a shallow Mediterranean wetland situated approximately 20 km upstream from the Adriatic Sea. This area features marshlands formed by the underground aquifer system of the Krupa River, including major lakes such as Deransko, Jelinsko, Svitavsko, and Skrkino, which support diverse aquatic ecosystems.
Hutovo Blato serves as a critical component of the broader Neretva Delta ecosystem, characterized by sub-Mediterranean hydrology with abundant water resources fostering high biodiversity, including significant avian populations and endemic fish species like the Neretvan roach (Rutilus basak). Designated as a Ramsar wetland of international importance, it has experienced historical human modifications such as irrigation barriers, though recent efforts focus on barrier removal to restore natural flows.
The estuarine systems of the Neretva Delta, located in Croatia, form a transitional zone where freshwater meets seawater, creating brackish habitats that retain sediments and mitigate flooding for the coastal hinterland. This delta hosts at least 313 bird species, with around 193 breeding or regularly occurring, underscoring its role as a key ornithological reserve amid ongoing threats like hydrological alterations and saline intrusion exacerbated by climate change.
Conservation challenges in these systems include habitat loss from past reclamation and the need for permanent monitoring to preserve endemic biodiversity, as outlined in IUCN assessments emphasizing integrated management across the Bosnia-Croatia border.

Krupa River and Local Hydrology

The Krupa River originates from Lake Deran in the Hutovo Blato Nature Park and functions as the main drainage channel for the wetlands, discharging into the Neretva River as a left tributary approximately 20 kilometers upstream from the Adriatic Sea. It measures 6 kilometers in length, with widths of 10 to 30 meters and depths ranging from 1 to 5 meters, classifying it as a Type 3c lowland river with a slope under 1 meter per kilometer and an organogenic substrate. Flow rates typically vary between 2 and 20 cubic meters per second, supporting the water regime of the surrounding 7,824 hectares of aquatic surfaces, including lakes, wetlands, and reservoirs.
A defining hydrological feature is the river's bidirectional flow, where under normal conditions water moves from Hutovo Blato toward the Neretva, but reverses direction during elevated Neretva levels, allowing upstream inundation of depressions like Deransko Blato and Svitavsko Blato. This reversal, documented in hydrological measurements, enables the wetlands to serve as a natural flood retention basin, reducing peak flows in the lower Neretva and mitigating downstream flood risks. The riverbed elevation of about 1.5 meters below sea level facilitates this exchange, integrating karst groundwater inputs from springs like Londa, with minimum discharges of 60 to 100 liters per second, alongside surface inflows from sinkholes in Popovo Polje. 
Local hydrology in the Hutovo Blato area depends on this dynamic interplay, with the Krupa channeling waters from a compensating basin like Svitavsko Blato, which holds up to 44 million cubic meters and buffers fluctuations from the nearby apljina Pumped Storage Hydroelectric Power Plant. Upstream hydropower developments on the Neretva, including five dams in Bosnia and Herzegovina, influence the Krupa by altering seasonal inflows, potentially reducing freshwater delivery and exacerbating salinization in the wetlands during low-flow periods. These modifications, combined with flood control infrastructure, have narrowed the Krupa's floodplain, affecting water quality and levels, though the system retains good ecological status under the Water Framework Directive.

Biodiversity and Ecology

Endemic and Threatened Species

The Neretva River basin supports 34 native freshwater fish species, 17 of which are endemic to the drainage. Among these, salmonids represent a significant component of endemism, with three species unique to the basin: the softmouth trout (Salmothymus obtusirostris oxyrhinchus), marble trout (Salmo marmoratus), and dentex trout (Salmo dentex). These species inhabit fast-flowing upper reaches and tributaries, adapted to the karstic, oligotrophic conditions of the Dinaric Alps.
The dentex trout (Salmo dentex), endemic to the Neretva and adjacent Adriatic drainages, reaches lengths up to 70 cm and is characterized by a silverish body with compressed form and prominent teeth. Classified as critically endangered in regional assessments due to habitat fragmentation from dams and overfishing, populations are now restricted to isolated segments of the main stem. The softmouth trout (Salmothymus obtusirostris oxyrhinchus), distinguished by reduced dentition and a maximum size of 50 cm, persists primarily in the Neretva system as its global stronghold, facing endangerment from hydroelectric barriers that block migration routes.
Marble trout (Salmo marmoratus) in the Neretva exhibit the distinctive marbled patterning and high growth potential of the Adriatic lineage, though hybridization with introduced brown trout (Salmo trutta) threatens genetic purity. This species, capable of reaching 120 cm and 50 kg, is vulnerable basin-wide due to river regulation and pollution. Cyprinid endemics include the Neretva nase (Chondrostoma knerii), a rheophilic species distributed across Croatian and Bosnian tributaries, assessed as vulnerable from habitat degradation. Similarly, the Neretva chub (Squalius svallize), confined to headwaters, relies on clear, oxygenated streams but contends with anthropogenic pressures.
The basin's endemic fauna extends beyond fish to include specialized invertebrates and amphibians, though data gaps persist; multiple species feature on IUCN Red Lists, underscoring the Neretva's status as a hotspot for threatened Adriatic endemics amid ongoing infrastructure development.

Fish Fauna: Salmonids, Cyprinids, and Others

The Neretva River basin supports a diverse salmonid fauna, dominated by endemic species adapted to its fast-flowing, oligotrophic waters. Key species include the softmouth trout (Salmo obtusirostris), an endangered form restricted primarily to the upper Neretva upstream of Glavatievo, where it coexists with brown trout (Salmo trutta) and occasional non-native salmonids. The marble trout (Salmo marmoratus), characterized by its distinctive marbled patterning and potential to reach weights exceeding 20 kg, occurs in the Neretva basin, including tributaries, though populations have declined due to habitat fragmentation. The dentex trout (Salmo dentex), another Adriatic endemic, inhabits the main river channels in Bosnia and Herzegovina, with specimens up to 70 cm recorded in the Neretva, confirming its presence despite rarity. These three endemics, alongside brown trout, represent the core salmonid diversity, with the Neretva serving as a key habitat in the western Balkans Adriatic drainage.
Cyprinids form the most speciose family in the basin, with numerous endemics reflecting the region's karstic hydrogeology and isolation. The Neretva nase (Chondrostoma knerii), endemic to the Neretva in Croatia and Bosnia and Herzegovina, migrates for spawning and faces threats from river regulation. The spotted minnow (Delminichthys adspersus), widespread in right-bank tributaries like the Tihaljina-Trebiat system, exhibits underground migration during droughts, underscoring adaptations to intermittent flows. Other notable cyprinids include species from endemic genera such as Phoxinellus and Aulopyge huegelii, contributing to over 20 endemic fish in the broader Dalmatian ecoregion encompassing the Neretva. These benthic and rheophilic forms thrive in riffles and pools but are vulnerable to sedimentation and barriers.
Among other fish, lower Neretva and delta zones host euryhaline species like mullets (Mugilidae), which support local fisheries alongside cyprinids and salmonids. Endemic gobies, including Knipowitschia radovici in the Adriatic basin's Neretva catchment, occupy interstitial habitats in the estuary. Overall, the fauna reflects high endemism, with salmonids in upstream oligotrophic zones transitioning to cyprinid-rich mid-reaches and diverse estuarine communities downstream, though hydropower dams have fragmented distributions since the mid-20th century.

Invasive Species and Ecosystem Threats

The Neretva River basin, renowned for its high endemism, faces escalating threats from invasive alien species that disrupt native aquatic communities through competition, predation, and habitat alteration. The European catfish (Silurus glanis), a large predatory fish native to central and eastern Europe, has established populations in the Neretva Delta since at least 2021, with records confirmed via angler reports and social media verification; this marks its first documented incursion into an Adriatic Sea drainage, where it preys on endemic cyprinids and salmonids, potentially reducing their abundances. Similarly, pikeperch (Sander lucioperca), another voracious piscivore introduced from Ponto-Caspian origins, invaded the Neretva watershed by the early 2010s, achieving high densities in reservoirs and river sections; diet analyses reveal it consumes up to 70% native prey, including threatened species like the marble trout (Salmo marmoratus), exacerbating local extinctions in fragmented habitats.
Smaller alien fishes compound these pressures, with spirlin (Alburnoides sp.), originating from adjacent Black Sea basins, detected at two Bosnian localities in the upper Neretva in 20182019, constituting the 32nd non-native fish species in the basin; its rapid upstream dispersal likely stems from aquaculture escapes or bait releases, enabling competition for spawning grounds with endemic minnows. In the estuarine delta, the Atlantic blue crab (Callinectes sapidus), introduced via ship ballast from North American waters around 2004, proliferates in brackish habitats, preying on bivalves, crustaceans, and juvenile fish while outcompeting native crabs; by 2021, it had decimated local stocks of the prized eel (Anguilla anguilla) and reduced trap catches by over 50% in commercial fisheries.
Benthic and macrophyte invaders further degrade ecosystems: the zebra mussel (Dreissena polymorpha), a filter-feeding bivalve from Ponto-Caspian regions, was first recorded in the Croatian Neretva in January 2024, fouling substrates and altering nutrient cycles in a system previously free of such dreissenids; its potential for explosive population growth threatens oligotrophic upper reaches by promoting eutrophication. The submerged aquatic plant Egeria densa, naturalized since the 2010s via ornamental trade, forms dense mats in the lower Neretva, reducing water flow, oxygen levels, and light penetration, which suffocates native macrophytes and fish spawning sites; climate projections indicate warmer temperatures could accelerate its spread, heightening invasion risks across Mediterranean rivers.
These invasives, often vectored by dams, aquaculture, and maritime traffic, synergize with hydrological alterations to amplify biodiversity loss, with over 30 alien fishes now documentedmany unmonitoredimperiling the basin's 50+ endemic species; peer-reviewed surveys underscore the need for barrier enforcement and eradication pilots, though enforcement lags due to transboundary governance challenges.

Economic Utilization

Hydropower Potential and Infrastructure

The Neretva River's hydropower potential arises primarily from its steep gradient in the upper and middle reaches, where the river descends rapidly from elevations exceeding 1,200 meters in the Dinaric Alps, creating high hydraulic head suitable for efficient turbine operation. In Bosnia and Herzegovina, where most of the exploitable potential lies, the river's flow regimedriven by snowmelt and precipitationsupports both run-of-river and storage-based generation, though exact technical potential figures for the Neretva basin remain estimates within the national total of approximately 6,100 MW for all rivers. The middle Neretva cascade alone demonstrates this capacity, with dams enabling regulated discharge for peak power during high-demand periods, while upper tributaries offer opportunities for smaller installations amid rugged terrain.
Existing infrastructure centers on a series of four large dams forming the middle Neretva cascade in Herzegovina-Neretva Canton, Bosnia and Herzegovina, constructed between 1955 and 1982 to exploit the river's narrow gorges and stable inflows. The upstream Jablanica Hydroelectric Power Plant (HPP), commissioned in 1955 and revitalized in 2012, features a 95-meter-high arch-gravity dam impounding Jablanica Lake (volume 300 million m) and six Francis turbines with a total installed capacity of 196.98 MW, generating an average of 1,100 GWh annually. Downstream, the Grabovica HPP (1982) has a 102-meter dam and two 58.5 MW turbines for 117 MW total capacity, with a reservoir of 19.77 million m focused on power peaking. Further downstream, Salakovac HPP (1981) employs a 70-meter dam and three turbines yielding 208.5 MW, producing about 356 GWh yearly from its elongated reservoir. The cascade concludes at Mostar HPP (1957), a 28-meter run-of-river facility with three turbines totaling 72 MW and annual output near 200 GWh.
Operated jointly by Elektroprivreda Bosne i Hercegovine (EPBiH) for Jablanica, Grabovica, and Salakovac, and Elektroprivreda Hercegovako-Neretvansko kantona (EPHZHB) for Mostar, this infrastructure provides flood control, irrigation storage, and baseload renewable energy equivalent to roughly 20-25% of Bosnia and Herzegovina's hydropower output. The plants' combined capacity exceeds 594 MW, with interlinked reservoirs allowing coordinated water management for optimized generation efficiency exceeding 80% in favorable hydrology. In the lower reaches in Croatia, hydropower development is minimal due to the river's meandering course and ecological priorities for the delta, limiting infrastructure to minor run-of-river facilities or none on the main stem.
Untapped potential persists in the pristine upper Neretva, where steep canyons support additional run-of-river projects; for instance, the Ulog HPP (35.1 MW), licensed in 2025 and under construction with Chinese financing, includes a 53-meter dam to capture head from glacial-fed flows. Complementary small hydropower plants (under 10 MW each) in the Gornja Neretva system total about 15 MW planned capacity, targeting remote tributaries for decentralized supply. These developments reflect economic incentives from the river's average discharge of 135 m/s and untapped gradients, though constrained by environmental regulations and transboundary water agreements with Croatia.

Irrigation, Fisheries, and Tourism

The lower Neretva Valley and delta support irrigated agriculture across approximately 5,200 hectares of reclaimed land within the 12,000-hectare delta, primarily for high-value crops such as mandarin oranges, whose annual production ranges from 20,000 to 30,000 tonnes. Irrigation relies on a network of canals drawing from river water, which is essential due to the region's low rainfall and sandy soils, but often involves saline sources that elevate soil salinity risks and necessitate drainage systems. Historical reclamation efforts since the early 20th century have expanded arable land but reduced wetland extent, altering hydrology and contributing to flood vulnerabilities in the transboundary delta shared by Bosnia and Herzegovina and Croatia.
Fisheries in the Neretva delta and associated wetlands, such as Hutovo Blato, target migratory species including European eel (Anguilla anguilla), sea mullet, and gilthead sea bream, with the estuary historically supporting commercial traps and nets. Annual eel catches peaked at around 75 tonnes in the mid-20th century but have collapsed, with recent official purchases averaging under 500 kg per year in Croatia due to overexploitation, habitat loss from reclamation, and barriers like dams. The system hosts nearly 150 fish species across its stages, including endemic salmonids upstream, but delta production remains marginal economically, supplemented by sport fishing in reserves where spawning grounds persist. Management challenges include illegal gear, estimated at over 10,000 eel traps, prompting regulatory closures and restoration efforts to reconnect migratory paths.
Tourism leverages the Neretva's scenic gorges and delta ecosystems for adventure and eco-activities, with whitewater rafting on the upper river near Konjic attracting around 10,000 participants annually as of the mid-2000s, generating local income through guided tours and supporting post-war economic recovery. In the lower reaches, birdwatching, angling, and boat safaris in Hutovo Blato draw visitors to observe over 240 bird species and wetlands, contributing to broader regional tourism revenues exceeding 600 million in Dubrovnik-Neretva County by 2016, though Neretva-specific figures are integrated into adventure segments. Preservation of free-flowing sections enhances potential for sustainable growth in rafting and trekking, outweighing hydropower alternatives in long-term economic value according to basin analyses.

Hydroelectric Development

Existing and Planned Projects

The Neretva River features three major existing hydroelectric power plants (HPPs) on its main stem in Bosnia and Herzegovina, constructed primarily during the Yugoslav era to harness the river's steep gradient and high flow for electricity generation. The Jablanica HPP, the uppermost and largest, is a diversion-type facility with an installed capacity of 196.98 MW, a head of 111 m, and average annual production of 693 GWh; its arch-gravity dam, completed between 1947 and 1955, created Jablanica Lake, a reservoir extending approximately 36 km upstream toward Konjic. Downstream, the Grabovica HPP, operational since 1982, has an installed capacity of 114 MW and a reservoir volume of 19.77 million cubic meters formed by a 60 m high concrete dam spanning about 11 km in the Srednja Neretva canyon. Further downstream near Mostar, the Salakovac HPP, built from 1977 to 1981, provides 208.5 MW capacity with a 70 m dam and average annual output of 356.6 GWh, regulating flows for flood control and power peaking.

Overall, over 50 hydropower initiatives are in various planning stages across the Neretva and its tributaries, driven by regional energy needs but facing scrutiny for cumulative basin impacts.

Economic and Energy Benefits

The hydroelectric facilities on the Neretva River, primarily the cascade operated by Elektroprivreda BiH d.d. Sarajevo, generate renewable electricity that bolsters Bosnia and Herzegovina's energy supply. In 2020, these plants produced 1,024.1 GWh, representing a key component of the country's hydroelectric output, which can comprise up to 50% of total electricity generation in years with favorable hydrology. The Jablanica HPP alone, with an installed capacity of 196.98 MW following revitalization, contributes substantially through Francis turbines exploiting a net head of 111 meters. Similarly, the Grabovica HPP adds 117 MW via Kaplan turbines with a 36-meter head, enabling efficient peaking and baseload support in the national grid.
These developments enhance energy security by diversifying away from coal-fired plants, which dominate BiH's thermal generation, and mitigating import risks amid variable regional supply. The Neretva's exploited potentialless than 40% of BiH's overall 6,100 MW hydropower capacityyields low marginal costs and high reliability, facilitating electricity exports to Croatia and the EU market, thereby generating foreign exchange. Economic analyses affirm that expanded hydropower utilization, including planned upper Neretva projects like the 35 MW Ulog facility projected at 76 GWh annually, is justified by positive net returns compared to fossil alternatives, with reduced greenhouse gas emissions and wastewater outputs.
Operationally, the cascade sustains employment for hundreds in maintenance, engineering, and ancillary roles, while construction phases for new dams create temporary jobs in underserved Herzegovina-Neretva regions, countering rural depopulation. Hydropower activities in the basin yield disproportionate revenue and job value relative to other water-dependent sectors like agriculture, underpinning local economies in the Federation of BiH entity. By regulating flows, the infrastructure also stabilizes downstream water availability, indirectly aiding irrigation and reducing flood damages, though quantifiable economic gains stem predominantly from energy sales. Overall, the Neretva's hydropower aligns with BiH's untapped economic potential of up to 18,000 GWh annually across major basins, promoting fiscal revenue for utilities like EPBiH amid efforts to modernize the sector.

Environmental Impacts and Criticisms

Hydropower development on the Neretva River, including existing facilities like the Jablanica and Grabovo dams, has fragmented aquatic habitats and altered natural flow regimes, leading to reduced sediment transport and downstream erosion. These changes disrupt the river's ecological connectivity, particularly in its upper canyon sections, which support high levels of endemism. Proposed additional dams, potentially numbering over 50 across the basin, risk further inundation of pristine valleys and canyons, exacerbating habitat loss for both aquatic and riparian species.
The construction and operation of these projects pose severe threats to the river's endemic fish fauna, notably salmonids such as the critically endangered softmouth trout (Salmo obtusirostris), marble trout (Salmo marmorata), and dentex trout (Salmo dentex). Dams create impassable barriers that prevent upstream migration for spawning, fragment populations, and increase vulnerability to local extinctions through genetic isolation and reduced genetic diversity. Fluctuating water releases from reservoirs have caused deoxygenation and stranding, as evidenced by a mass die-off of fishincluding softmouth troutalong a 7 km stretch below the Ulog Dam in September 2025. Such incidents underscore the acute mortality risks to rheophilic species adapted to the river's high-gradient, oxygen-rich flows.
Broader ecosystem impacts include declines in invertebrate communities, crayfish populations (Austropotamobius torrentium), and otter (Lutra lutra) habitats due to reduced prey availability and altered hydraulic conditions. Downstream effects extend to the Neretva Delta and associated wetlands like Hutovo Blato, where diminished sediment deposition threatens coastal stability and bird nesting sites. Small hydropower plants, often less regulated, contribute to riverbed drying and flow diversion, further degrading side channels and tributaries critical for biodiversity refugia.
Criticisms from environmental NGOs, scientists, and international bodies center on the inadequacy of environmental impact assessments (EIAs), which reportedly fail to fully evaluate cumulative effects, transboundary impacts, and alternatives to damming. Complaints lodged with the Bern Convention in 2020 and subsequent investigations highlight non-compliance with protections for endangered species and insufficient mitigation measures, such as ineffective fish passes. Activists argue that the push for hydropower, driven by energy needs and foreign investment, overlooks the Neretva's status as a European biodiversity hotspot, potentially leading to irreversible loss of one of the continent's last free-flowing rivers. While proponents cite renewable energy benefits, opponents contend that the ecological costs outweigh gains, given viable alternatives like solar and wind in the region.

Recent Incidents and Regulatory Responses

In September 2025, a mass die-off of fish occurred along a 7-kilometer stretch of the upper Neretva River immediately downstream of the newly operational Ulog hydropower plant in Bosnia and Herzegovina, with local fishermen reporting thousands of dead specimens including endemic species. Scientists attributed the incident to operational factors such as rapid water level fluctuations, sediment disruption, and potential deoxygenation from the plant's impoundment and turbine releases, exacerbating vulnerabilities in the river's pristine canyon ecosystem. This event followed earlier reports of water discoloration from carbonates near the Ulog site in 2023, linked to construction activities, though no structural failures were recorded.
In response to the fish kill, over 190 international river ecologists issued an open letter to Bosnian authorities in October 2025, demanding an immediate suspension of Ulog operations, a comprehensive review of its environmental impact assessment (EIA) for failing to predict cumulative ecological risks, and stricter enforcement of water quality monitoring. The letter highlighted deficiencies in BiH's regulatory framework, including inadequate transboundary consultations with Croatia and insufficient mitigation for downstream effects on biodiversity hotspots like the Neretva Delta.
Broader regulatory scrutiny intensified via the Bern Convention on the Conservation of European Wildlife and Natural Habitats, where a 2021 complaint against BiH prompted a 2022 Standing Committee decision (Resolution No. 217) mandating a halt to eight planned upper Neretva hydropower projects, designation of protected zones, and a fact-finding mission to assess violations of habitat directives for endangered salmonids and invertebrates. Despite this, BiH granted an energy license to Ulog in March 2025, prompting criticism from NGOs for prioritizing development over compliance, with ongoing calls for EU-aligned reforms in EIA processes and small hydropower permitting under the Energy Community Treaty. No major dam structural incidents, such as breaches, have been documented on the Neretva since 2020, though flash floods in October 2024 amplified debris accumulation, underscoring vulnerabilities in basin management without direct ties to hydropower failures.

Historical Significance

Pre-Modern History: Ancient to Ottoman Eras

The Neretva River valley was inhabited by Illyrian tribes, notably the Daorsi, whose fortified center Daorson was located near modern Stolac, approximately 20 kilometers northeast of the river's upper reaches. Archaeological evidence from sites like Desilo, an underwater settlement near the Neretva delta, indicates an Illyrian trading post with harbor facilities dating to the 4th-3rd centuries BCE, facilitating commerce along the Adriatic coast. During the Illyrian Wars, Roman forces overran settlements in the Neretva valley in 229 BCE and 219 BCE, incorporating the region into their expanding Adriatic provinces.
Under Roman administration from the 1st century CE, Narona emerged as a key emporium and colony at the river's mouth, near present-day Vid, serving as an administrative and economic hub for trade in timber, metals, and agricultural goods. Historical records, including those from Pseudo-Scylax in the 4th century BCE, describe Narona as a navigation port on the Neretva, with Roman-era developments including rebuilt city walls and a temple complex uncovered in excavations from 1995-1996. The site's strategic position supported fluvial trade routes through the valley's agricultural areas, linking inland resources to coastal ports until the decline of Roman control in the 4th-5th centuries CE.
Following Slavic migrations in the 6th-7th centuries CE, the Neretva valley integrated into early medieval South Slavic polities, with the region forming part of the principality of Hum (Zachlumia), extending from the Neretva to the Adriatic. By the 12th century, Hum's territories along the Neretva fell under competing influences from Hungary and Serbia; in 1216, Hungarian king Andrew II asserted control over Hum up to the Neretva, installing a puppet prince before Serbian ruler Stefan Nemanji replaced him with a governor. Local sclaviniassemi-independent Slavic communitiespersisted between the Neretva and Cetina rivers, blending with residual Romanized and Vlach populations amid feudal fragmentation.
The Ottoman Empire's expansion reached the Neretva region in the mid-15th century, conquering most of Bosnia by 1463 and Herzegovinaincluding the valleyby 1482, transforming strategic crossings like the pre-existing hamlet at Mostar into frontier outposts. Ottoman forces advanced along the river after securing Herzegovina, bypassing fortified Croatian towns to consolidate control, with full integration of the area occurring amid prolonged campaigns ending by the early 16th century. This conquest facilitated the empire's southward push, exploiting the valley's navigable routes for military logistics and settlement.

World War II: Battle of the Neretva

The Battle of the Neretva, part of the Axis Case White (Fall Weiss) offensive, unfolded from 20 January to 9 March 1943 in western Bosnia and Herzegovina, targeting the Yugoslav Partisan main force trapped in the Neretva River valley. German-led Axis forces, comprising roughly 100,000 troops including Wehrmacht divisions, Italian units, Independent State of Croatia (NDH) forces, and collaborating Chetnik militias under Draa Mihailovi, aimed to encircle and destroy Josip Broz Tito's headquarters and up to 25,000 Partisans, many of whom were wounded or ill from prior operations. The Partisans, communist-led guerrillas fighting both Axis occupiers and rival royalist Chetniks, faced near annihilation amid harsh winter terrain that favored the better-equipped attackers.
By early February, the Partisans were compressed into a shrinking pocket south of the Neretva near Jablanica, with over 4,000 wounded requiring evacuation; Tito's command prioritized breaking north across the river under intense artillery and air bombardment. On 12 February, Partisan engineers constructed a pontoon bridge amid combat, enabling the "Battle for the Wounded," where units fought rearguard actions against pursuing Germans and Chetniks positioned to block escape routes. Feinting westward to draw Axis reserves, Tito's forces then pivoted eastward, crossing back and evading full encirclement, though at the cost of abandoning initial plans for safer Montenegrin territory. Chetnik forces, nominally anti-Axis but operationally allied with Germans against the Partisans in this engagement, inflicted additional pressure but failed to coordinate effectively with regular Axis troops.
The operation ended in tactical Axis success, with Partisans suffering approximately 11,000 casualtiesover 40% of their engaged strength, including killed, wounded, and capturedwhile evacuating most wounded via improvised means. Axis losses totaled 6,500 to 8,500, disproportionately borne by Italians and NDH units in ground assaults. Strategically, the Partisans' survival preserved their command structure, contributing to Allied reassessment at the Tehran Conference later in 1943, shifting support from Chetniks to Tito's movement; however, the battle highlighted the communists' tactical resilience amid internecine Yugoslav conflicts, where mutual royalist-communist hostilities enabled Axis exploitation. Yugoslav communist historiography later framed it as a heroic victory, but empirical assessments emphasize the disproportionate Partisan attrition and Axis inflicting of decisive blows, tempered by the guerrillas' evasion of total destruction.

Yugoslav Dissolution Wars: Operations and Divisions

During the initial phase of the Yugoslav Dissolution Wars in 1992, the Neretva valley served as a critical theater against Yugoslav People's Army (JNA) and subsequent Army of Republika Srpska (VRS) forces, with joint Croatian Defence Council (HVO) and Army of the Republic of Bosnia and Herzegovina (ARBiH) operations securing Mostar by mid-June after recapturing the river's east bank on June 14. The valley's strategic bridges and terrain facilitated supply lines from central Bosnia to Herzegovina, enabling defenders to push Serb forces into surrounding mountains while retaining control over key industrial sites in Mostar.
Tensions escalated into direct Croat-Bosniak conflict on May 9, 1993, when HVO forces launched a coordinated assault on ARBiH positions in eastern Mostar, establishing frontlines along the Neretva River and Bulevar, effectively besieging the Bosniak-held east bank and aiming to sever ARBiH links to Jablanica and Konjic. This operation, involving artillery, mortars, and small arms, reflected HVO ambitions to incorporate Mostar into a Croat entity, resulting in over 1,200 Bosniak deaths during the ensuing siege through early 1994, compared to approximately 600 on the Croat side. ARBiH counteractions included capturing Bijelo Polje on June 29-30, 1993, which temporarily disrupted the HVO encirclement, though the river remained a de facto ethnic divide with HVO dominating the west bank and ARBiH the east.
In September 1993, ARBiH initiated Operation Neretva '93 to break the Mostar blockade and advance southward along the valley, reaching Vrdi by October 9 before political directives halted further gains amid reports of civilian and POW mistreatment. HVO responses included retaking Malo Polje on October 26, 1993, and the destruction of the Stari Most bridge on November 9, 1993, which severed ARBiH crossings and symbolized the entrenched divisions, exacerbating humanitarian crises with restricted aid access to eastern Mostar until UN interventions in August 1993. The conflict's ethnic fault lines manifested in detention camps on both sides, such as HVO facilities at Dretelj and Gabela holding up to 2,000 Bosniaks, and ARBiH sites like Musala, with releases pressured by international observers.
Hostilities subsided following a February 23, 1994, ceasefire and the March 3 Washington Agreement, which formalized a Bosniak-Croat federation and ended major operations along the Neretva, though the river's valley persisted as a marker of unresolved territorial claims in Herzegovina. VRS shelling from elevated positions continued intermittently, underscoring the valley's multi-front role, but Croat-Bosniak clashes defined the primary operational and divisional dynamics, with control of the Neretva enabling or denying access to Adriatic outlets and internal corridors.

Transboundary Management

Bosnia-Croatia Water Sharing and Disputes

The Neretva River basin spans approximately 10,000 km, with about 80% in Bosnia and Herzegovina (BiH) and the remainder primarily in Croatia, necessitating transboundary cooperation for water allocation, flood control, and environmental sustainability. A bilateral framework for water management exists under the 1996 Agreement between BiH and Croatia on Water Management Regulation, supplemented by the Helsinki Convention on the Protection and Use of Transboundary Watercourses, which provides legal basis for joint monitoring and equitable sharing. In 2006, a Memorandum of Cooperation on the Neretva River Delta was signed involving BiH, Croatia, and Monaco, focusing on conservation and sustainable use of shared delta resources.
Hydroelectric development in upstream BiH, including four major dams (Jablanica, Grabovica, Salakovac, and Mostar) operational since the mid-20th century, has altered the river's flow regime, reducing peak discharges and sediment transport to Croatia's downstream delta. This has raised concerns in Croatia over diminished ecological health in the Neretva Delta, a Ramsar-listed wetland, where sediment deficits exacerbate coastal erosion, saltwater intrusion, and habitat loss for species like the softmouth trout. Croatian authorities have noted that power-peaking operations from BiH dams contribute to fluctuating water levels, increasing flood risks during storms while lowering base flows that support agriculture and fisheries in the delta.
To address these transboundary effects, the World Bank-supported Neretva and Trebisnjica River Basin Management Project (20042013) established joint BiH-Croatia commissions for integrated basin planning, emphasizing data sharing on discharges and pollution to prevent unilateral actions. Despite progress, tensions persist over proposed new BiH hydropower projects, such as the Ulog dam, which environmental groups warn could further impair downstream sediment delivery and biodiversity, prompting calls from Croatian NGOs for stricter enforcement of equitable utilization principles under international law. No formal arbitration has occurred, but ongoing dialogues highlight BiH's prioritization of energy security against Croatia's focus on delta preservation.

Integrated Basin Management Efforts

The Neretva River Basin, spanning Bosnia and Herzegovina and Croatia, has prompted transboundary integrated basin management initiatives primarily through international funding and bilateral frameworks to address water allocation, pollution reduction, and ecosystem conservation. A pivotal effort was the Neretva and Trebisnjica Management Project (NTMP), launched on May 29, 2008, and completed on December 30, 2013, with $8 million from the Global Environment Facility and a total budget of $21.15 million. This World Bank-supported initiative aimed to catalyze an integrated approach to water resource management, enhancing ecosystem integrity by conserving water, land, and biodiversity while demonstrating the benefits of binational cooperation. 
Key components of the NTMP included developing joint strategies for shared issues such as nutrient pollution and habitat degradation, establishing implementation mechanisms, and piloting a comprehensive River Basin Management Plan (RBMP) as a central activity to guide equitable water use and environmental protection across borders. The project focused on reducing nitrogen pollution through agricultural best practices, monitoring water quality, and fostering institutional collaboration between Bosnian and Croatian authorities, thereby improving transboundary ecosystem functioning. Outcomes emphasized practical tools for binational decision-making, including nitrogen management plans and enhanced monitoring networks, which served as models for aligning with the European Union Water Framework Directive in both countries' EU accession processes. 
Subsequent efforts have built on this foundation, such as bilateral proposals for a joint management plan covering the Neretva Delta in Croatia and the adjacent Hutovo Blato wetland in Bosnia and Herzegovina to protect shared coastal and riparian ecosystems. In Bosnia and Herzegovina, the Agency for the Adriatic Sea Watershed in Mostar has pursued sustainable management updates, including decision support systems for flood and water quality risks, while Croatia's 20162021 Adriatic Sea River Basin District plan incorporated Neretva-specific measures for pollution control and habitat restoration. These initiatives underscore ongoing, albeit incremental, progress in data-driven, cooperative governance amid hydropower pressures and climate variability.

Cultural Representations

Folklore, Literature, and Media

In Bosnian folklore, the Neretva River is associated with a legend observed particularly in Mostar, where locals traditionally avoid swimming on Saint Elijah's Day (Ilija's Dan, corresponding to August 2 in the Julian calendar). This belief holds that the river becomes exceptionally treacherous on this date, claiming lives due to sudden currents or other perils, with anecdotal reports of drownings reinforcing the taboo.
The river's name itself is linked to etymological myths tracing back to ancient Indo-European roots meaning "to dive" or "plunge," evoking imagery of the Neretva's dramatic descents through gorges, as interpreted in regional oral traditions. Additionally, the high-diving tradition from the Stari Most bridge in Mostar embodies folkloric elements of bravery and ritual, where divers perform leaps into the Neretva's depths as a test of skill and heritage, a practice dating to Ottoman times and symbolizing mastery over the river's power.
In literature, the Neretva has inspired Bosnian and Croatian poets, notably Aleksa anti (18681924), a Mostar native whose verses romanticize the river's beauty and the surrounding Herzegovina landscape, earning praise from contemporaries like Tin Ujevi for their evocative quality. Works such as Odjek Neretve (The Echo of the Neretva) by Enerika Bija compile poetry, ethnography, and reflections on the river's cultural resonance in the region. Historical fiction like Robert Michel's Crescent over the Neretva (original 1920s, translated into Bosnian in 2023) depicts Ottoman-era life along the river, drawing on archival details of Herzegovina's multicultural past.
The Neretva features prominently in media, most notably the 1969 Yugoslav film Bitka na Neretvi (Battle of Neretva), directed by Veljko Bulaji, which dramatizes the 1943 World War II partisan offensive along the river, featuring an international cast including Yul Brynner and Orson Welles, and nominated for the Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film. The film's score, composed by Mikls Rzsa and others, incorporates orchestral suites evoking the river's role in the battle. Later works, such as Jean-Luc Godard's 2004 film Notre musique, include footage of the rebuilt Stari Most over the Neretva, symbolizing post-war reconciliation amid the river's divisive history. Regional sevdah music from Mostar ensembles like Mostar Sevdah Reunion often references the Neretva in lyrics and themes of longing and heritage, bridging folk traditions with modern recordings.
