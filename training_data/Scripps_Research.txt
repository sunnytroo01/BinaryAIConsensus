 Scripps Research is an independent, nonprofit biomedical research organization dedicated to advancing scientific understanding of the human body and developing innovative medicines to improve health worldwide. Founded in 1924 by philanthropist Ellen Browning Scripps as the Scripps Metabolic Clinic in La Jolla, California, it has grown into a leading institution in the life sciences and chemistry, with campuses in La Jolla, California, and Jupiter, Florida. The organization employs over 175 faculty members and trains the next generation of scientists through highly ranked graduate and postdoctoral programs.
Over its century-long history, Scripps Research has evolved from a clinic focused on metabolic diseases to a global leader in fundamental and translational research, seamlessly integrating basic science with drug discovery to accelerate medical breakthroughs. Key milestones include the establishment of its Florida campus in 2007 to expand interdisciplinary work and the formation of specialized institutes like the Scripps Research Translational Institute and Calibr-Skaggs Institute for Innovative Medicines, which bridge laboratory discoveries to clinical applications. The institute's research is organized into six departmentsChemistry, Immunology and Microbiology, Integrative Structural and Computational Biology, Molecular and Cellular Biology, Translational Medicine, and Neurosciencefostering collaborations across biology, chemistry, and computational sciences to address major health challenges such as cancer, infectious diseases, and neurodegenerative disorders.
Scripps Research's impact is profound, with faculty earning five Nobel Prizes, five Wolf Prizes, and one Breakthrough Prize in Life Sciences, alongside 27 members in the National Academies of Sciences, Medicine, and Engineering. Its scientists have contributed to 17 FDA-approved drugs and vaccines, hold over 1,100 U.S. patents, and have launched more than 50 spin-off companies, while annual federal and state grants exceed $295 million to support research affecting 85% of the world's population. Ranked among the world's most influential research organizations, Scripps Research continues to drive innovation in precision medicine and biotechnology.

History

Founding and early development

Scripps Research originated as the Scripps Metabolic Clinic, founded on December 11, 1924, by philanthropist Ellen Browning Scripps in La Jolla, California. The institution was established with a focus on advancing research into nutrition and metabolism, particularly inspired by the 1921 discovery of insulin as a treatment for diabetes, aiming to integrate patient care with scientific investigation into metabolic disorders. Initially housed on Prospect Street as part of the newly opened Scripps Memorial Hospital, the clinic began operations with a 57-bed facility dedicated to treating chronic illnesses through dietary and metabolic approaches.
Under the early leadership of James W. Sherrill Sr., MD, selected as the first director by an advisory board of physicians, the clinic emphasized clinical applications of metabolic research, including diabetes management and nutritional therapies. Following Ellen Browning Scripps' death in 1932 at age 95, she bequeathed approximately $300,000 (equivalent to about $5.3 million today) to the clinic, ensuring continued funding for research initiatives and solidifying its commitment to scientific inquiry alongside patient treatment. During the 1930s and 1940s, the clinic evolved by deepening the integration of clinical practice and research, with physicians conducting studies on metabolic conditions while providing care, which helped build its reputation in endocrinology and nutrition.
A pivotal milestone occurred in 1946 when the Scripps Metabolic Clinic separated from Scripps Memorial Hospital, enabling greater autonomy to expand into basic biomedical science beyond immediate clinical needs. This period also saw contributions to wartime research efforts, including studies on nutrition and metabolism relevant to military health during World War II, which broadened the clinic's scope and attracted additional talent. By the mid-20th century, these developments laid the groundwork for more fundamental investigations into disease mechanisms.
In 1956, the institution was renamed the Scripps Clinic and Research Foundation, reflecting its formal nonprofit structure and renewed emphasis on both clinical and basic biomedical research. This reorganization marked a key step in institutional evolution, culminating in further advancements such as the 1961 arrival of Frank J. Dixon, MD, and his team from the University of Pittsburgh, who established the Division of Experimental Pathology and propelled the foundation into immunology and molecular biology.

Expansion and institutional evolution

In 1991, the institution underwent a significant renaming to The Scripps Research Institute (TSRI), marking its evolution into an independent entity focused on basic biomedical research rather than solely clinical applications. This change separated TSRI from its affiliations with Scripps Memorial Hospital and emphasized its expanding role in fundamental scientific inquiry.
The presidency of Richard Lerner from 1987 to 2012 catalyzed substantial institutional growth, particularly in chemical biology and immunology during the 1980s and 1990s. Lerner tripled the scale of research operations, recruited leading scientists, and built TSRI into a global leader in these disciplines by fostering interdisciplinary collaborations and establishing a premier chemistry department. His leadership transformed the institute into a biomedical powerhouse, with notable advancements in antibody research and molecular mechanisms of disease.
A pivotal development in 1996 was the establishment of the Skaggs Institute for Chemical Biology, funded by a landmark $100 million gift from philanthropists Aline and L.S. "Sam" Skaggs. This institute accelerated research at the intersection of chemistry and biology, enabling breakthroughs in drug discovery and molecular therapeutics. Concurrently, the completion of the Arnold and Mabel Beckman Center in La Jolla provided state-of-the-art facilities to support this expansion.
The 2000s brought further infrastructural growth, including new research buildings in La Jolla to accommodate burgeoning programs in structural biology and neurosciences. In 2004, amid incentives from the state of Florida and Palm Beach County, TSRI announced plans to establish a second campus in Jupiter, Florida, on 30 acres dedicated to advancing biomedical innovation and diversifying its geographic footprint. This decision reflected the institute's strategy to scale operations and attract talent beyond California.

Recent mergers and milestones

In 2022, Scripps Research completed the transfer of its Florida campus operations to the University of Florida, a pivotal organizational milestone that streamlined the institute's focus on its La Jolla headquarters while preserving collaborative ties in biomedical research. This transition, finalized after agreements signed in 2021, involved handing over the 30-acre Jupiter facility, research programs, and approximately 500 staff to UF's academic health center, enabling Scripps Research to concentrate resources on core scientific innovation without the bicoastal administrative demands.
Under the continued leadership of President and CEO Peter G. Schultz, who assumed the role in 2016, Scripps Research has emphasized integrated biomedical advancements, fostering a model that bridges fundamental discovery with therapeutic translation. Schultz's tenure has prioritized strategic alliances and internal restructuring to enhance efficiency and impact.
The institute marked its 2024 centennial with a year-long celebration of 100 years of contributions to fields like immunology, infectious diseases, and neuroscience, featuring events such as public discovery days, proclamations from the City of San Diego, and reflections on transformative discoveries including COVID-19 research.
In 2024, Scripps Research recruited nine new faculty members specializing in areas such as drug discovery, neuroscience, chemistry, and immunity to bolster interdisciplinary research. In 2025, faculty members Andrew Su and Chunlei Wu secured a $1.7 million grant from the National Institutes of Health's National Center for Advancing Translational Sciences to develop AI-driven tools for analyzing biomedical data, accelerating personalized medicine applications. In November 2025, eleven Scripps Research scientists were named to Clarivate's Highly Cited Researchers 2025 list.

Organization and structure

Campuses and facilities

Scripps Research's primary campus is located in La Jolla, California, equipped with state-of-the-art facilities to support biomedical research. The La Jolla campus, the institute's original and largest site, spans 35 acres on the Torrey Pines Mesa and encompasses more than 1 million square feet of research space. This campus houses numerous faculty laboratories, core research facilities, and specialized centers, including the Worm Institute for Research and Medicine, which focuses on using model organisms like C. elegans to study human diseases. Additionally, it features the Cell-Based High-Throughput Screening Core and the Scripps Research Molecular Screening Center, equipped with over $22 million in specialized automation for screening large libraries of small molecules, such as the Scripps Drug Discovery Library containing more than 665,000 compounds.
Scripps Research formerly operated a second campus in Jupiter, Florida, at 130 Scripps Way in Palm Beach County, which consisted of a 350,000-square-foot biomedical research facility that opened in February 2009. This site emphasized translational research, with key infrastructure including the Scripps Research Translational Institute, established in 2007 to integrate genomic data analysis, digital health monitoring, and clinical applications for advancing therapies. In April 2022, the Florida campus was integrated into the University of Florida as the Herbert Wertheim UF Scripps Institute for Biomedical Innovation & Technology, with the University purchasing the facilities for a symbolic $100. Its proximity to the Florida Atlantic University campus and the growing biotech cluster in the region had facilitated partnerships and access to additional resources for drug development and innovation.
Following the 2016 strategic affiliation and 2018 full merger with Calibr (now Calibr-Skaggs Institute for Innovative Medicines), Scripps Research has enhanced shared infrastructure, including a unified board of directors, administrative resources, and collaborative platforms to accelerate drug discovery from basic science to preclinical trials. Ongoing collaborations with the former Florida campus, now part of the University of Florida, are supported through academic ties such as shared graduate programs via the Skaggs Graduate School of Chemical and Biological Sciences, fostering interdisciplinary work.
In 2025, sustainability efforts advanced with the certification of the new 83,000-square-foot Chi-Huey Wong Laboratories for Biomedical Research building on the La Jolla campus, which earned LEED Gold status for features like drought-tolerant landscaping, water-efficient systems, a reflective cool roof, EV charging stations, low-emission materials, and high indoor air quality. This facility, opened in August 2024 at the intersection of Genesee Avenue and John Jay Hopkins Drive, supports drug discovery labs while prioritizing energy efficiency and environmental protection, including the relocation of a native Torrey pine tree during construction.

Departments and research centers

Scripps Research maintains an interdisciplinary organizational structure comprising six interrelated departments that collaborate to advance biomedical discoveries. These units integrate expertise across chemistry, biology, and computational sciences, enabling seamless cooperation among faculty to address complex health challenges.
The Department of Molecular Medicine emphasizes the molecular underpinnings of immunology, virology, and disease mechanisms, with research aimed at elucidating immune responses, viral pathogenesis, and therapeutic interventions for infectious and immune-related disorders. Faculty in this department employ advanced techniques to explore protein interactions and cellular signaling pathways, fostering collaborations with other units to translate findings into clinical applications.
The Department of Integrative Structural and Computational Biology concentrates on determining protein structures and macromolecular complexes using methods such as cryo-electron microscopy, X-ray crystallography, and nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy. It integrates artificial intelligence and bioinformatics for predictive modeling of biological systems, supporting drug design by simulating molecular interactions and identifying potential therapeutic targets. This department's work bridges structural insights with computational tools to accelerate discoveries in biomolecular dynamics.
The Department of Chemistry and Chemical Biology drives innovations at the interface of organic synthesis and biological applications, developing chemical tools to probe cellular processes and design novel therapeutics. Researchers pioneer synthetic methodologies, including "click" chemistry, to create functional molecules for drug discovery, RNA engineering, and targeted therapies against diseases like cancer and neurodegeneration. The department's efforts in chemical biology extend to natural product synthesis and the creation of probes for high-throughput screening.
Key research centers at Scripps Research enhance these departmental activities through focused initiatives. The Skaggs Institute for Chemical Biology, established in 1996 with a $100 million endowment from Aline W. and L.S. Skaggs, supports investigations at the chemistry-biology nexus, including macromolecular structure determination, catalyst design, and molecular recognition studies. Institute members, holding dual appointments in departments, have contributed to advancements in RNA chemistry, drug design, and protein misfolding research, producing over 3,600 publications and facilitating clinical developments like angiogenesis inhibitors.
Medicinal chemistry efforts are prominently integrated within the Department of Chemistry, where scientists advance small-molecule design and synthesis for therapeutic applications, often in collaboration with centers like Calibr-Skaggs for translational drug discovery. These initiatives emphasize structure-based drug design and optimization of compounds for unmet medical needs.
Following the 2022 integration of the former Florida campus into the University of Florida as the Herbert Wertheim UF Scripps Institute, Scripps Research's core departments and centers on the La Jolla campus have continued to operate as a unified structure, with ongoing academic ties such as shared graduate programs to maintain interdisciplinary momentum.

Research activities

Core focus areas

Scripps Research pursues interdisciplinary research that integrates chemistry, biology, and computational methods to address complex biomedical challenges, fostering collaborations across its departments to advance fundamental understanding and therapeutic innovation. This approach emphasizes the development of novel tools and methodologies to probe biological mechanisms, with a strong commitment to translating discoveries into clinical applications. The institution's core focus areas reflect its dedication to solving pressing health issues through rigorous, collaborative science.
In chemical biology, researchers develop molecular tools to investigate and manipulate biological processes at the molecular level. This includes pioneering synthetic methods like click chemistry to create functional molecules for drug discovery and probing cellular functions. The Department of Chemistry leads these efforts, focusing on organic synthesis and bioorganic approaches to design probes for diseases such as cancer and neurodegeneration.
Immunology and infectious diseases represent another pillar, where studies elucidate immune system dynamics, pathogen evolution, and host responses to develop protective strategies. Key methodologies involve analyzing antibody production, vaccine design, and immune cell interactions to combat infections, autoimmunity, and cancer. The Department of Immunology and Microbiology drives this work, employing advanced imaging and genomic techniques to model disease spread and therapeutic interventions.
Neuroscience and aging research centers on unraveling brain function, neurodegeneration, and pathways influencing longevity. Investigations explore neural circuits underlying learning, memory, and movement, while examining molecular disruptions in conditions like Alzheimer's and schizophrenia. Approaches include cellular imaging, genetic modeling, and pathway analysis to understand how aging affects neural stem cells and sensory systems. The Department of Neuroscience spearheads these studies, integrating computational biology for deeper insights into nervous system resilience.
Translational drug discovery bridges basic research to clinical outcomes, emphasizing the progression from molecular targets to patient therapies. This involves high-throughput screening, bioinformatics, and personalized medicine strategies to identify and validate drug candidates for diseases including cardiovascular disorders and addiction. AI integration accelerates target identification by predicting protein interactions and optimizing lead compounds, enhancing efficiency in therapeutic development. The Department of Translational Medicine coordinates these initiatives, supported by the Scripps Research Translational Institute.
Emerging emphases in 2025 highlight genomics and wireless health technologies to enable precision medicine. Genomic research utilizes next-generation sequencing and bioinformatics to map disease susceptibility and viral evolution, informing individualized treatments. Wireless health leverages digital sensors and mobile platforms for real-time monitoring, combining with AI to analyze phenotypic data for proactive care. These areas build on the Scripps Research Translational Institute's expertise in integrating genomics with digital tools for scalable health solutions.

Key scientific contributions

Scripps Research has made pioneering contributions to chemical biology through the development of click chemistry by K. Barry Sharpless, who received the 2022 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for this work alongside bioorthogonal chemistry innovations. This modular approach enables precise and efficient assembly of complex molecules under mild conditions, mimicking natural processes while avoiding interference with living systems. Its applications in drug development have revolutionized the synthesis of bioconjugates, such as antibody-drug conjugates and targeted therapeutics, facilitating faster and more reliable production of candidates for treating diseases like cancer and infectious conditions. For instance, click chemistry has been instrumental in creating stable linkages for imaging agents and prodrugs, enhancing precision medicine outcomes.
In the field of targeted protein degradation, Scripps Research scientists have advanced PROTAC (proteolysis-targeting chimera) technology, which hijacks the cell's ubiquitin-proteasome system to selectively eliminate disease-causing proteins. This approach has led to novel cancer therapies by degrading oncoproteins that are otherwise "undruggable" with traditional inhibitors, such as selective degradation of cytosolic epoxide hydrolase isoforms to modulate inflammation and tumor growth. Researchers at Scripps have optimized PROTACs for spatiotemporal control, enabling applications in oncology where they promote tumor cell death while sparing healthy tissue, paving the way for clinical candidates in treating hematologic malignancies and solid tumors.
During the COVID-19 pandemic from 2020 to 2022, Scripps Research accelerated antibody discovery efforts, identifying potent neutralizing antibodies like SARD and J08 from survivor samples to inform therapeutic and vaccine strategies. These antibodies targeted conserved SARS-CoV-2 spike protein sites, evading variants and enabling broad protection against coronaviruses, with J08 demonstrating sub-nanomolar potency independent of known escape mutations. This work supported rapid platform development for monoclonal antibody cocktails and mRNA vaccine enhancements, contributing to global pandemic response by shortening discovery timelines from months to weeks.
In 2025, Scripps Research received a $6.9 million NIH grant to advance HIV antibody research for long-term protection, focusing on durable immune responses through long-lived plasma cell generation. Led by Bryan Briney and collaborators, the project uses mouse models, single-cell multi-omics, and structural biology to map antibody evolution and test adjuvants that sustain broadly neutralizing antibodies against diverse HIV strains, potentially enabling preventive vaccines with multi-year efficacy. Complementing this, a $14.2 million NIH award supported brain mapping initiatives to decode neural pathways for interoception, the body's internal sensing mechanism. Under Ardem Patapoutian and Li Ye, the effort employs 3D imaging and genetic profiling to atlas sensory neuron connections to organs, revealing disruptions in neurological disorders like chronic pain and neurodegeneration, which could inform targeted interventions for improved sensory-motor function.

Rankings and global impact

Scripps Research's graduate programs maintain a strong position in national rankings, reflecting the institution's emphasis on cutting-edge biomedical education. According to the U.S. News & World Report's 2024-2025 graduate school assessments, the Skaggs Graduate School ranks 9th (tie) in biological sciences, 7th (tie) in chemistry, 4th (tie) in biochemistry, and 2nd (tie) in organic chemistry, with sustained top-10 placements across these categories. These rankings underscore the program's rigorous training in chemical and biological sciences, attracting top talent and fostering interdisciplinary research.
In terms of citation impact, Scripps Research demonstrates exceptional influence through its faculty's prolific output. In 2025, Clarivate named 11 Scripps Research scientists as Highly Cited Researchers, placing them in the top 1% globally by citations in their fields from 2014 to 2024. This recognition highlights the broad reach of their work in areas like immunology, structural biology, and drug discovery, with high citation rates signaling foundational contributions that drive global scientific progress.
The institution's innovation extends to tangible economic and therapeutic outcomes via intellectual property. Scripps Research holds over 1,100 patents, many of which have been licensed to industry partners, generating substantial revenueexceeding $296 million in fiscal year 2023 aloneand supporting more than 50 spin-off companies. Notable examples include licensing agreements with Fate Therapeutics, which has leveraged Scripps-derived stem cell technologies to advance clinical-stage immunotherapies for cancer and autoimmune diseases.
Scripps Research exerts significant global health influence through international collaborations and prestigious awards. The institute partners with organizations like the International AIDS Vaccine Initiative (IAVI) and the National Institutes of Health (NIH) on vaccine development, including first-in-human trials for HIV broadly neutralizing antibodies and efforts toward universal alphavirus vaccines, enhancing equitable access to preventive therapies worldwide. In 2025, Professor David Nemazee received the Crafoord Prize in Polyarthritis from the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences for discovering mechanisms of B cell tolerance that prevent autoimmune diseases, affirming Scripps' role in advancing immunology with broad societal benefits.

Education and training

Graduate degree programs

The Skaggs Graduate School of Chemical and Biological Sciences at Scripps Research offers a PhD program focused on interdisciplinary training at the chemistry-biology interface, encompassing fields such as chemical biology, immunology, microbiology, neurobiology, computational biology, cell biology, biochemistry, genetics, genomics, molecular medicine, and structural biology. This bicoastal program, available at the La Jolla, California, and Jupiter, Florida, campuses, emphasizes intensive laboratory research and customizable education, with students able to access faculty and resources across both locations through technologies like live broadcasting. The program, established in 1989, has awarded nearly 1,000 doctoral degrees and currently enrolls approximately 400 PhD students.
The curriculum is designed for flexibility, beginning with laboratory rotations in up to three different labs during the first year to explore research interests and foster interdisciplinary exposure. Students then complete a minimum of 12 credits in core coursework, taken for letter grades, with examples including molecular biology courses like BIOL 531 (Advanced Molecular Biology) and synthetic chemistry courses such as CHEM 520 (Organic Reaction Mechanisms). Electives, which can be taken pass/fail, allow further customization across more than 10 research areas, promoting training beyond traditional disciplinary boundaries. The program culminates in original dissertation research under a faculty advisor, leading to a PhD defense; the 33rd commencement ceremony on May 16, 2025, awarded degrees to 61 graduates.
Admissions are competitive and holistic, requiring a strong undergraduate background in biomedical sciences or related fields, demonstrated research commitment, and submission of an online application by December 1 for fall entry. Applications are reviewed by a bicoastal committee of faculty from both campuses, with selected candidates invited for interviews at their preferred location; offers of admission maintain identical standards across sites. The program is tuition-free, with full coverage of tuition and benefits, and provides annual stipends of $45,760 for students on the California campus and $43,680 on the Florida campus for the 2025-26 academic year, paid in monthly installments to support living expenses. Additionally, Scripps Research offers the Skaggs-Oxford Programme, a five-year DPhil in Biochemistry in partnership with the University of Oxford, based at either campus.

Postdoctoral and early-career opportunities

Scripps Research provides extensive postdoctoral training opportunities across more than 160 laboratories led by internationally renowned faculty, allowing candidates to pursue research in diverse areas such as chemistry, immunology, and neuroscience. Postdoctoral positions are secured by directly contacting principal investigators or applying through the institute's careers portal, with an emphasis on fostering independent research projects that can lead to faculty appointments. Trainees receive competitive salaries starting at approximately $68,000 for postdoctoral associates, along with comprehensive benefits including full medical coverage, internal travel awards, and access to over 50 annual career development events.
The Scripps Research Fellows Program targets exceptional early-career scientists who have recently completed their PhD or equivalent degree, offering three years of full salary support, funding for two lab members, and principal investigator status to enable independent, innovative research programs. Additional early-career initiatives include the NIH-funded T32 Postdoctoral Training Program, which supports up to three scholars for three years with a focus on building research independence through skill development in areas like translational medicine. The K12 Scholars Program provides mentored training for recent PhD holders and physicians, emphasizing grant writing, bioinformatics, and career transitions to industry or academia via protected research time and multidisciplinary mentorship.
In 2025, the K12 program opened applications on November 1 for appointments starting July 2026, enhancing opportunities in digital medicine and informatics. Recent NIH grants, including $1.7 million for AI-powered biomedical data integration and $6.9 million for HIV vaccine development, bolster training by funding collaborative research environments that incorporate postdoctoral roles.
Postdoctoral alumni at Scripps Research demonstrate strong career progression, with published outcomes data indicating transitions to academic faculty positions, biotech leadership roles, and industry opportunities following a median postdoctoral tenure. The institute's Career and Professional Development Office supports this high placement rate through resources for manuscript preparation, grant proposals, and networking, contributing to alumni success in competitive fields.

Outreach and community programs

Scripps Research engages the public and underrepresented groups through a variety of educational initiatives aimed at fostering interest in STEM fields and promoting scientific literacy. These efforts include hands-on research opportunities for students, partnerships with educators, and public events that highlight cutting-edge discoveries in biomedical research.
The institute's undergraduate research programs provide college students with immersive summer experiences in laboratory settings. The Summer Undergraduate Research Fellows (SURF) program, a 10-week internship held on the La Jolla campus, pairs participants with faculty-led labs for full-time research projects tailored to their interests, offering hands-on experience in areas such as chemistry, biology, and neuroscience. Interns receive daily mentorship from graduate students or postdoctoral researchers, along with professional development workshops on topics like graduate school applications and career panels, with a stipend, housing, and travel support provided to ensure accessibility.
For K-12 outreach, Scripps Research collaborates with local San Diego County schools and community centers to deliver STEM enrichment programs, including science lessons and laboratory demonstrations for underserved K-8 students. High school students can participate in the Summer High School Internship Program, a collaboration with the REACH initiative, which exposes participants to biomedical research through hands-on projects and mentorship. Additionally, teacher training workshops enable educators to co-develop lesson plans with Scripps scientists, integrating real-world research into classroom curricula to inspire the next generation of scientists.
Diversity initiatives at Scripps Research focus on increasing representation in STEM by targeting underrepresented minorities through targeted recruitment and support programs. The STEM Diversity and Inclusion Initiative accelerates these efforts by expanding internships and educational opportunities for students from diverse backgrounds, including those historically excluded from academia. The Post-Baccalaureate Research Education Program (PREP), offered on the Florida campus, provides a one-year immersive research experience for recent graduates from underrepresented groups, preparing them for competitive PhD or MD-PhD programs through intensive lab work and mentorship.
Community engagement includes public events such as the Front Row Lecture Series, which features scientists discussing breakthroughs in health and longevity. In May 2025, executive vice president Eric Topol delivered a lecture on the science of longer, healthier lives, exploring advances in aging research and their implications for vitality, as part of the institute's ongoing centennial celebrations marking a century of scientific contributions. These events, held in-person and online, draw local audiences to foster dialogue on pressing health issues.

Leadership and people

Current administration

Scripps Research is led by President and Chief Executive Officer Peter G. Schultz, PhD, who assumed the role in 2021. A renowned chemist and biotech entrepreneur, Schultz holds the Scripps Family Chair in Chemistry and Biological Sciences and has pioneered advancements in synthetic and chemical biology, including the development of novel molecular assemblies with tailored properties. His leadership emphasizes integrating fundamental research with translational applications to address global health challenges.
Serving as Executive Vice President is Eric Topol, MD, who oversees the Scripps Research Translational Institute and drives initiatives in individualized medicine, artificial intelligence, and digital health innovations. Topol, a cardiologist and prolific author, was recognized in the inaugural 2024 TIME100 Health list for his influential work in advancing global health through technology and precision medicine. Under his guidance, the institute has expanded efforts to bridge basic science with clinical applications, particularly in AI-driven diagnostics and therapeutics.
Key educational and operational roles include Keary Engle, PhD, as the John and Susan Diekman Dean of Graduate and Postdoctoral Studies, responsible for overseeing academic programs and training the next generation of scientists. Caroline Moon serves as Chief Financial Officer, providing strategic oversight in financial planning, corporate development, and operational efficiency to support the institute's research mission. These positions ensure robust support for education, fiscal management, and institutional growth.
The Board of Trustees, which provides strategic direction and governance, is chaired by John D. Diekman, PhD, founding partner of 5AM Ventures, with Vice Chair Peter G. Schultz. Comprising 20 members with expertise in biotechnology, finance, and philanthropysuch as Ron Burkle, Gerald Chan, ScD, and Benjamin Cravatt, PhDthe board fosters innovation and resource allocation. In a 2025 update, Douglas W. Phillips, a retired senior vice president and chief investment officer with decades in asset management, and Steven M. Paul, MD, a neuroscientist and biopharma leader who co-founded multiple therapeutics companies, joined the board to enhance financial and biomedical perspectives.

Keck Professor of Chemistry, received the 2001 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for his work on chirally catalyzed oxidation reactions and shared the 2022 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for the development of click chemistry and bioorthogonal chemistry. Phil S. David Nemazee, professor of Immunology and Microbiology, won the 2025 Crafoord Prize in Polyarthritis for discovering fundamental mechanisms of B cell tolerance that prevent autoimmune diseases like polyarthritis.
In 2024, ten Scripps Research scientists were named to Clarivate's Highly Cited Researchers list, recognizing their significant influence across fields such as chemistry, microbiology, immunology, and medicine. In 2025, eleven Scripps Research scientists were named to the list. Notable among them are Ardem Patapoutian for identifying protein sensors of touch and pain, Eric Topol for advancing AI and genomics in personalized medicine, and Nemazee for contributions to immune tolerance and vaccinology. These researchers exemplify the institute's impact in areas from viral structural biology to innovative drug discovery.
Alumni of Scripps Research's graduate programs have ascended to prominent leadership roles in academia, biotechnology, and science policy. Benjamin F. Funding and resources

Primary funding sources

Scripps Research's primary funding derives from federal grants, particularly those awarded by the National Institutes of Health (NIH), which support a wide array of biomedical research initiatives. In 2025, notable NIH awards included a $14.2 million grant to a Scripps-led team for mapping the body's interoceptive system, often referred to as the "hidden sixth sense," to advance understanding of sensory integration in health and disease. Additional funding encompassed $6.9 million over five years for developing effective HIV vaccines through multidisciplinary collaborations. A further $1.7 million supported AI-powered biomedical data integration to enhance discovery processes. These grants represent key components of NIH's broader allocation, underscoring Scripps Research's role in national priorities like precision medicine and infectious disease research.
Philanthropic contributions form another cornerstone, with endowments from the Skaggs family providing sustained support for graduate fellowships and research programs. The Skaggs Institute for Chemical Biology, established through family gifts, has enabled foundational work in chemical and biological sciences, including recent endowments like the Farfy Foundation Graduate Fellowship. In FY2024, philanthropic support totaled $18.75 million. In FY2021, total private support encompassing philanthropy, technology licensing, and related donations reached $146 million, including over $40 million in direct philanthropic gifts.
Industry partnerships, especially with pharmaceutical companies, generate significant licensing revenue through collaborative drug discovery efforts. These collaborations, such as those via the Calibr-Skaggs Institute for Molecular Medicine, have advanced over 12 medicines into clinical development, including antivirals and regenerative therapies, with licensing deals reinvested to fuel ongoing research. In fiscal year 2021, technology licensing revenues surpassed $100 million, more than tenfold the amount from five years prior, highlighting the model's self-sustaining impact on innovation.
State and other sources include incentives following the 2022 integration of Scripps Florida with the University of Florida, which facilitated access to state-backed resources for biomedical expansion in Jupiter. This merger unlocked additional funding, such as a $100 million lead gift from the Wertheim Foundation to name and support the institute. International grants further bolster global health projects, including support from the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation for therapeutics targeting diseases like malaria, tuberculosis, and HIV in low- and middle-income countries. In FY2024, Scripps Research's total revenue was approximately $641 million, primarily from federal grants, philanthropy, and licensing.

Financial support for education and research

Scripps Research provides comprehensive financial support for its graduate students, ensuring tuition-free education and covering health insurance along with a competitive stipend to cover living expenses. For the 2025-26 academic year, the base annual stipend is set at $45,760 for students on the California campus and $43,680 on the Florida campus, with potential supplements of up to $10,000 for those securing eligible external fellowships that cover at least 50% of the base amount. The institute fully funds the first year of support, while subsequent years are typically secured through the student's advisor's research grants or national fellowships, such as those from the National Science Foundation, promoting independence and alignment with ongoing projects.
Postdoctoral researchers at Scripps Research receive competitive salary packages that align with National Institutes of Health (NIH) National Research Service Award (NRSA) scales, with starting salaries often ranging from $62,000 to $75,000 annually as of 2025 and annual increases to reflect experience and cost-of-living adjustments. These positions include comprehensive benefits such as health insurance and retirement contributions, fostering a supportive environment for early-career scientists transitioning to independent research.
The institute supports research projects through targeted seed grants, particularly for early-career faculty pursuing innovative, high-risk ideas. For instance, assistant professor Ahmed Badran received a seed grant from the Hypothesis Fund to advance novel chemical biology approaches. Additionally, diversity-focused fellowships, such as the Summer Undergraduate Research Fellowship (SURF) program, provide stipends and housing for undergraduates from underrepresented backgrounds, enabling hands-on projects in biomedical research and prioritizing accessibility for first-generation students.
Sustainability of these educational and research supports is bolstered by a growing endowment, highlighted by the 2018 campaign led by the Skaggs family, which has established over 60 three-year endowed fellowships covering tuition, stipends, and professional development for approximately 177 trainees annually as of 2024. This endowment-driven model ensures long-term tuition-free PhD education for all students, reducing reliance on short-term grants and enhancing program stability.
