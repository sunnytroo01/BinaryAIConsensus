Calcium oxide
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Calcium oxide, commonly known as quicklime or lime, is an inorganic compound with the chemical formula CaO and a molecular weight of 56.077 g/mol. It appears as an odorless, white or gray-white solid, often in the form of hard lumps or powder, and is highly caustic and alkaline, reacting vigorously with water to form calcium hydroxide and generate significant heat. This compound is a strong irritant to skin, eyes, and mucous membranes, necessitating careful handling in industrial and laboratory settings.
Calcium oxide is primarily produced industrially through the calcination of calcium carbonate (limestone, CaCO) in rotary kilns at temperatures between 900C and 1,200C, following the reaction CaCO CaO + CO. This process, known as lime burning, has been utilized for millennia and remains the dominant method due to the abundance of limestone deposits worldwide. The resulting quicklime is then processed into various forms, including hydrated lime (Ca(OH)), for diverse applications.
Key physical properties include a high melting point of approximately 2,613C, a boiling point around 2,850C, and a density of 3.34 g/cm, making it suitable for high-temperature processes. Chemically, it exhibits basic properties with a pH of about 12.5 in aqueous suspension and is sparingly soluble in water (about 1.65 g/L at 20C), but it readily absorbs moisture and carbon dioxide from the air, reverting to calcium carbonate over time.
In industry, calcium oxide serves as a fundamental material in construction, where it is a key ingredient in Portland cement and lime mortar for binding aggregates. It is also essential in steelmaking as a flux to remove impurities like phosphorus and sulfur, and in water treatment for softening, pH adjustment, and sludge conditioning. Additional applications include agriculture as a soil amendment and fertilizer, pulp and paper production for bleaching, and chemical manufacturing for processes like the Solvay soda production. Historically, it has been used in incendiary devices, and in modern contexts for self-heating food packaging due to its exothermic hydration reaction.

Properties

Physical properties

Calcium oxide is an odorless, white or gray-white crystalline solid, typically appearing as hard lumps, fine powder, or granules in its pure form. It exhibits a caustic and alkaline nature due to its basic properties, with a density of 3.34 g/cm at 20 C.
This compound demonstrates high thermal stability, with a melting point of 2613 C and a boiling point of 2,850 C, at which it tends to sublime rather than boil in a conventional sense. Its specific heat capacity is approximately 0.75 J/gK for the solid phase near room temperature.
Regarding solubility, calcium oxide is practically insoluble in waterreacting instead to form calcium hydroxide with significant heat evolutionbut it dissolves in glycerol while remaining insoluble in ethanol and acetone. In commercial quicklime products, particle morphology varies by processing, often featuring irregular crystalline granules or lumps with sizes ranging from fine powder (less than 0.1 mm) to larger aggregates up to 150 mm, influencing handling and reactivity.

Chemical properties

Calcium oxide, with the chemical formula CaO, has a molar mass of 56.077 g/mol. It is an ionic compound consisting of calcium cations (Ca) and oxide anions (O), where calcium exhibits an oxidation state of +2 and the oxide ion serves as a strong Lewis base.
The crystal structure of calcium oxide is face-centered cubic, adopting the rock salt (NaCl) structure with space group Fmm, in which each Ca ion is octahedrally coordinated to six O ions and vice versa. This ionic lattice contributes to its high melting point and stability under standard conditions. Calcium oxide is a strong base owing to the basicity of the oxide ion, which readily accepts protons; an aqueous suspension exhibits a pH of approximately 12.8.

Production

Industrial production

Calcium oxide, commonly known as quicklime, is primarily produced on an industrial scale through the calcination of limestone (calcium carbonate, CaCO) in large rotary kilns at temperatures ranging from 900C to 1,200C, following the reaction CaCO CaO + CO. This endothermic process decomposes the carbonate, releasing carbon dioxide gas and leaving behind the oxide, with rotary kilns favored for their efficiency in handling high volumes of material in continuous operation.
The primary raw material is high-purity limestone, often sourced from quarries, though marble can also serve as a suitable alternative due to its similar calcium carbonate composition; impurities such as silica or clay in the feedstock can lead to reduced product purity and affect downstream applications by forming silicates or other contaminants.
Various kiln types are employed depending on scale and desired product characteristics, including shaft kilns for smaller or intermittent operations and advanced flash calcination processes that rapidly heat fine limestone particles to produce highly reactive quicklime with greater surface area. Energy consumption for these processes typically ranges from 5 to 7 GJ per ton of lime produced, primarily from fuels like natural gas or coal used to sustain the high temperatures required.
Global production of lime, including quicklime, reached 424 million metric tons in 2023 and an estimated 420 million metric tons in 2024, underscoring its status as a major commodity chemical essential to industries like steelmaking and construction; leading producers include China (310 million tons), India (17 million tons), the United States (16 million tons), and several European countries.
A significant by-product of calcination is CO, with the lime industry accounting for about 1% of global industrial CO emissions, predominantly from the inherent process reaction; mitigation efforts include carbon capture and storage technologies integrated into kiln operations to reduce these emissions.
Produced lime is classified into quality grades based on reactivity, with quicklime offering high reactivity for applications requiring rapid slaking and dead-burned lime, achieved through prolonged high-temperature exposure, exhibiting lower reactivity and suited for refractory materials due to its denser structure.

Calcium oxide is commonly prepared in the laboratory through the thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate, a method analogous to but scaled down from industrial calcination processes. Applications

Construction and materials

Calcium oxide, commonly known as quicklime, plays a pivotal role in the production of Portland cement, where it reacts with silica and alumina from clay or shale at high temperatures to form clinker, the primary component of cement. This clinker is then ground with gypsum to produce the final cement powder used extensively in concrete for building foundations, bridges, and infrastructure. In lime mortar applications, quicklime is slaked with water to form calcium hydroxide, which is mixed with sand to create a breathable binder suitable for historic restorations and modern eco-friendly buildings, allowing moisture vapor to pass through walls and prevent damage from trapped water. The high reactivity of calcium oxide enables rapid setting and strong adhesion in these mortars, contributing to their durability and flexibility compared to rigid cement-based alternatives.
In steel manufacturing, calcium oxide serves as a flux in basic oxygen furnaces (BOF), where it combines with impurities such as phosphorus and sulfur to form slag, which is then removed to purify the molten steel. This process, essential for producing high-quality steel used in construction beams, rebar, and structural components, consumes significant quantities of quicklime to maintain the basicity of the slag and facilitate efficient impurity removal.
Calcium oxide is also employed in water treatment processes within construction to soften hard water used for concrete mixing, where it reacts with calcium and magnesium ions to precipitate them as insoluble carbonates, preventing adverse effects on cement hydration and concrete strength.
In glass and ceramics production, calcium oxide acts as a flux to lower the melting point of silica, enabling the formation of stable soda-lime glass through the lime-soda process, which combines limestone (source of CaO), soda ash, and sand for windows, bottles, and containers used in building materials. Similarly, in ceramics, it stabilizes glazes and bodies by adjusting viscosity and promoting vitrification at lower temperatures, enhancing the quality of tiles and sanitary ware for construction interiors.
The construction industry is the largest end-user of calcium oxide, accounting for over half of global demand as of 2025. The material's advantages, such as its high reactivity for quick setting and its historical application in Roman concretewhere unslaked quicklime created self-healing propertiescontinue to influence modern sustainable construction practices.

Chemical manufacturing

Calcium oxide serves as a versatile reagent and catalyst in numerous industrial chemical processes, leveraging its strong basicity and reactivity to facilitate the synthesis of key compounds. In the production of calcium carbide, a critical intermediate for acetylene and other chemicals, calcium oxide reacts with carbon at high temperatures. The reaction proceeds as follows:
CaO+3CCaC2+CO
\mathrm{CaO + 3C \rightarrow CaC_2 + CO}
CaO+3CCaC2+CO
This endothermic process typically occurs in electric arc furnaces at approximately 2,000C, with lime derived from calcined limestone providing the calcium source and petroleum coke or anthracite serving as the carbon input. The resulting calcium carbide is then hydrolyzed to produce acetylene gas, which is essential for welding and organic synthesis.
In the pulp and paper industry, calcium oxide plays a pivotal role in the kraft process's chemical recovery cycle, specifically during the causticizing step. Here, quicklime (CaO) reacts with sodium carbonate in green liquor to regenerate sodium hydroxide, the active cooking agent for wood pulping:
CaO+Na2CO3CaCO3+2NaOH
\mathrm{CaO + Na_2CO_3 \rightarrow CaCO_3 + 2NaOH}
CaO+Na2CO3CaCO3+2NaOH
This reaction precipitates calcium carbonate mud, which is subsequently calcined in a lime kiln to recover CaO, enabling a closed-loop system that minimizes waste. The process efficiency depends on the slaking of CaO to calcium hydroxide prior to reaction, ensuring high conversion rates of sodium carbonate to hydroxide.
Calcium oxide is widely employed as a heterogeneous base catalyst in biodiesel production via the transesterification of vegetable oils or animal fats with methanol or ethanol. Typically, 1-2% CaO loading by weight of the oil is used, promoting the conversion of triglycerides to fatty acid methyl esters (biodiesel) and glycerol at temperatures around 60-65C. Yields exceeding 90% are achievable under optimized conditions, such as a 6:1 methanol-to-oil ratio and 2-3 hours reaction time, with the catalyst's activity stemming from its Lewis basic sites. Waste-derived CaO, like from eggshells, enhances sustainability while maintaining efficacy.
In petroleum refining, calcium oxide functions as a desulfurization agent to remove sulfur compounds from fuels and as a drying agent to eliminate moisture from hydrocarbons. It neutralizes acidic impurities and absorbs water in processes like caustic washing or additive formulations, with specialized grades like PetroCal O tailored for these applications. Its role supports compliance with low-sulfur fuel standards by facilitating the conversion of sulfur oxides or mercaptans.
For organic synthesis, calcium oxide acts as a dehydration agent to remove water from reaction mixtures and as a base in esterification reactions, promoting the formation of esters from acids and alcohols. In transesterification variants, it catalyzes the exchange of alkyl groups, often at mild conditions, while its dehydrating properties prevent hydrolysis side reactions. These applications benefit from CaO's mild basicity, avoiding over-alkalinity issues seen with homogeneous catalysts.
High-grade calcium oxide, with purity exceeding 95%, is essential for catalytic applications to ensure minimal impurities that could deactivate sites or contaminate products. Commercial lime for these uses typically contains 90-95% free CaO when properly stored, but catalytic processes demand refined grades approaching 99% to optimize activity and selectivity.

Agriculture and environmental uses

Calcium oxide, commonly known as quicklime, plays a vital role in agriculture by neutralizing acidic soils through liming, which raises soil pH and enhances nutrient availability for crops. This process counters soil acidity caused by factors such as acid rain, fertilizer application, and crop nutrient uptake, thereby improving plant growth and yield. Application rates typically range from 1 to 5 tons per hectare, determined by soil tests that assess buffering capacity, target pH, and crop needs; quicklime's high neutralizing value (150-175% calcium carbonate equivalent) allows for lower quantities compared to agricultural limestone.
In animal feed, calcium oxide serves as a supplemental source of calcium for livestock and poultry, supporting bone health, eggshell formation, and overall metabolic functions. It is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) when incorporated into formulations at low levels, such as 1% of dry matter in cattle rations to improve feed digestibility and reduce acidity in by-products like distillers grains. Quicklime is often slaked to calcium hydroxide for safer integration into feed blocks or supplements containing molasses, minerals, and non-protein nitrogen.
Calcium oxide is widely employed in water and wastewater treatment for its ability to precipitate phosphates and heavy metals, aiding in pollution control. In phosphate removal, lime raises pH to form insoluble calcium phosphate, effectively reducing phosphorus levels in effluents from sources like agricultural runoff or sewage. For heavy metals such as lead, cadmium, and zinc, quicklime precipitates them as hydroxides at concentrations exceeding 1000 mg/L, enabling efficient separation from industrial wastewater. Additionally, in flue gas desulfurization (FGD) systems at power plants, calcium oxide reacts with sulfur dioxide to form calcium sulfite, mitigating acid rain precursors:
CaO+SO2CaSO3
\text{CaO} + \text{SO}_2 \rightarrow \text{CaSO}_3
CaO+SO2CaSO3
This dry or wet scrubbing process captures up to 90% of SO emissions.
In sugar refining, particularly for beet juice clarification, calcium oxide is added to raw juice after heating, where it neutralizes organic acids and facilitates impurity removal through subsequent carbonation. The addition of CO converts excess lime to calcium carbonate precipitates, which trap proteins, colorants, and other non-sugars, producing clear juice for evaporation and crystallization. This step enhances sugar purity and yield, with quicklime preferred for its rapid reactivity in industrial-scale operations.
For odor control in sewage treatment, calcium oxide neutralizes ammonia and reduces volatile compounds like hydrogen sulfide by elevating pH above 12, creating an alkaline environment inhospitable to odor-producing bacteria. In biosolids stabilization, quicklime addition minimizes off-gassing of amines and ammonia during processing and storage, improving handling safety and land application viability. This application also aids pathogen reduction, aligning with regulatory standards for wastewater residuals.
The use of calcium oxide in agriculture and environmental applications promotes sustainability by enhancing soil fertility and reducing reliance on synthetic fertilizers, as improved pH optimizes natural nutrient cycling and microbial activity. These sectors represent a significant portion of global calcium oxide production, underscoring its role in eco-friendly practices that lower chemical inputs and mitigate environmental pollution.

Historical and miscellaneous uses

Calcium oxide, known historically as quicklime, has been utilized since ancient times in construction for its binding properties when slaked to form lime mortar. Archaeological evidence indicates its use in Egyptian pyramids around 2600 BCE, where lime-based mortars helped bond limestone blocks, contributing to the enduring structures of the Old Kingdom. The Romans advanced this application by combining quicklime with pozzolana, a volcanic ash, to create hydraulic concrete that set underwater and exhibited superior durability, as described in Vitruvius's engineering texts and evidenced in structures like the Pantheon.
In military contexts, quicklime is speculated to have served as an incendiary component in Byzantine Greek fire, a petroleum-based weapon deployed from the 7th century onward, where its exothermic reaction with water intensified flames and caused burns upon contact. During the medieval period, lime mortars incorporating quicklime were widely employed in European fortifications, such as castle walls and cathedrals, providing flexible yet strong bonding that accommodated structural shifts over time.
For applications requiring adjusted reactivity, substitutes like magnesium oxide have been used in place of calcium oxide, particularly in steelmaking fluxes where higher refractoriness is needed, and dolomitic lime (containing both calcium and magnesium oxides) offers slower slaking for controlled pH adjustments in soil treatment. Magnesium oxide provides similar basicity but with greater thermal stability, making it preferable in high-temperature processes.
Among miscellaneous uses, quicklime plays a key role in leather tanning through depilation, where it swells and loosens hair from hides via alkaline hydrolysis, a process dating back to ancient civilizations and still employed today. Slaked lime, derived from calcium oxide, is essential in nixtamalization, the traditional Mesoamerican processing of corn, where it enhances nutritional bioavailability by breaking down cell walls and adding calcium.
Recent research since 2010 has explored calcium oxide nanoparticles for catalytic applications, particularly in biodiesel production via transesterification, where their high surface area enables efficient conversion of triglycerides with yields exceeding 95% under mild conditions. These nanomaterials also show promise in CO2 capture and organic synthesis, leveraging their basic sites for enhanced reactivity.
Culturally, quicklime held symbolic significance in alchemy as a emblem of transformation, representing the calcination stage where base materials were purified into higher forms, akin to spiritual enlightenment in medieval esoteric texts. This metaphor of rebirth through fire underscored its role in alchemical pursuits of the philosopher's stone.

Safety and environmental considerations

Health and safety hazards

Calcium oxide, commonly known as quicklime, is highly corrosive and acts as a severe irritant to the skin, eyes, and respiratory tract. Upon contact with moist tissues, it undergoes exothermic hydration, generating significant heat that can cause chemical burns and tissue damage. This reactivity makes it particularly hazardous in handling, as even brief exposure can lead to painful blisters, ulceration, or perforation of affected areas.
Primary exposure routes include inhalation of dust particles, which can irritate the upper respiratory tract and, in cases of impure calcium oxide containing silica, pose a risk of silicosisa chronic lung disease characterized by scarring of lung tissue. Skin contact often results in dermatitis, with symptoms ranging from redness and itching to severe burns, while eye exposure can cause immediate pain, swelling, and potential vision impairment. Ingestion is less common but can lead to gastrointestinal irritation and burns in the mouth and esophagus.
Toxicity assessments indicate low acute oral toxicity, with an LD50 greater than 2000 mg/kg in rats, suggesting it is not highly poisonous when swallowed in small amounts. The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) does not classify calcium oxide as a carcinogen. However, chronic inhalation exposure has been associated with lung issues, including pneumoconiosis and nasal septum ulceration, due to prolonged irritation of respiratory passages.
To mitigate risks, personal protective equipment (PPE) such as chemical-resistant gloves, safety goggles, protective clothing, and respirators approved for particulate matter is essential during handling. Storage should occur in cool, dry, well-ventilated areas to prevent moisture absorption and spontaneous reactions, and workplaces must adhere to the OSHA permissible exposure limit (PEL) of 5 mg/m as an 8-hour time-weighted average for calcium oxide dust. Engineering controls like local exhaust ventilation further reduce airborne concentrations.
In case of exposure, immediate first aid is critical: for skin or eye contact, flush thoroughly with water for at least 15-20 minutes while removing contaminated clothing, and seek medical attention; for inhalation, move the affected person to fresh air and provide oxygen if breathing is difficult; if ingested, do not induce vomiting but rinse the mouth and contact poison control.
Historical incidents in lime kilns frequently involved severe burns from hot calcium oxide or dust exposure, contributing to worker injuries before modern safeguards. Under EU REACH regulations, calcium oxide is classified as causing skin irritation (Category 2), serious eye damage (Category 1), and specific target organ toxicity from single exposure (respiratory tract irritation, Category 3), mandating strict handling protocols across member states.

Environmental impact

The production of calcium oxide through calcination of limestone releases approximately 0.78 tons of CO per ton of CaO, primarily due to the decomposition of calcium carbonate, contributing significantly to global greenhouse gas emissions and climate change.
Airborne lime dust from production and handling processes can degrade local air quality by forming fine particulates that scatter sunlight and deposit on ecosystems, while alkaline runoff from sites elevates water pH levels, potentially causing oxygen depletion and disrupting aquatic habitats.
Limestone quarrying for calcium oxide feedstock results in habitat fragmentation and loss, leading to biodiversity declines as vegetation is cleared and soil structures are altered, with long-term effects on local flora and fauna populations.
To mitigate these impacts, carbon capture and storage (CCS) technologies are being integrated into lime kilns, capturing up to 99% of process CO for geological sequestration, while recycling waste materials such as eggshellscalcined to produce CaOreduces reliance on virgin limestone and diverts organic waste from landfills.
Regulatory measures like the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) impose caps on CO emissions from lime production facilities, requiring allowances for verified outputs and incentivizing reductions through trading, while life-cycle assessments indicate a global warming potential of approximately 1.0 kg CO equivalent per kg of calcium oxide, encompassing extraction, production, and transport stages.
On a positive note, calcium oxide's application in SO scrubbing processes neutralizes sulfur dioxide emissions from industrial sources, thereby reducing acid rain formation and associated ecosystem damage.

Natural occurrence

Associated minerals

Calcium oxide, mineralogically known as lime (IMA symbol: Lm), is an extremely rare naturally occurring mineral due to its high reactivity, which causes it to rapidly form hydrated or carbonated compounds in the presence of water or atmospheric CO. It has been documented in trace amounts as a product of high-temperature processes, such as coal-seam fires where limestone decomposes, and in altered limestone xenoliths within volcanic ejecta or sublimates. Additionally, pure CaO has been identified in calcium-aluminum-rich inclusions within meteorites like Allende.
A related mineral is portlandite (Ca(OH)), the hydrated form of calcium oxide, which occurs more frequently in natural environments such as altered ultramafic rocks and combustion metamorphosed limestones, though it differs from pure CaO by incorporating water of hydration. This hydration reflects the instability of anhydrous CaO under typical terrestrial conditions.
When naturally occurring quicklime is found, it is typically impure, often containing significant magnesia (MgO) in dolomitic varieties derived from magnesium-bearing limestones, along with minor traces of silica (SiO) and alumina (AlO) from the parent rock composition. Over 99% of calcium oxide in existence is anthropogenic, produced via industrial calcination, underscoring the negligible role of natural sources.
The identification of calcium oxide in potential natural samples relies on techniques like X-ray diffraction (XRD), which detects its cubic crystal structure through characteristic peaks at 2 values of approximately 32, 37, 54, 64, and 67; such analyses often reveal it in industrial residues or slags that may be erroneously classified as minerals.

Geological formation

Calcium oxide, commonly known as lime, primarily forms in geological settings through the metamorphism of precursor minerals such as limestone (calcium carbonate, CaCO), where heat and pressure drive decarbonation reactions that release carbon dioxide. This process occurs during contact or regional metamorphism, typically at temperatures exceeding 700900C under low-pressure conditions, decomposing calcite into CaO and CO. Similarly, in meteoritic contexts, CaO appears as a minor oxide phase within calcium-aluminum-rich inclusions (CAIs) in chondritic meteorites, representing early condensates from the solar nebula formed at high temperatures over 4.5 billion years ago.
The geological stability of calcium oxide is limited by its rapid hydration in the presence of atmospheric moisture, forming calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)) and preventing the accumulation of large natural deposits. Exploration for potential CaO sources focuses on geophysical surveys targeting limestone precursors, such as seismic and magnetic methods to map carbonate layers, given the rarity of pure lime, which constitutes less than 0.01% of Earth's crust as a distinct mineral phase.
