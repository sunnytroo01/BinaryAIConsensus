 True Religion is an American lifestyle and apparel brand specializing in premium denim jeans and casual clothing, founded in 2002 by entrepreneur Jeff Lubell and designer Kym Gold in Los Angeles, California. Headquartered in Gardena, California, the company is renowned for its distinctive horseshoe stitching on pocket backs, premium fabric quality, and the iconic Buddha logo, which disrupted the traditional five-pocket jean design during the early 2000s denim boom.
The brand's mission emphasizes denim as a universal fabric that transcends boundaries of faith, class, ethnicity, and orientation, promoting inclusivity through bold, high-end sportswear for men, women, and children. True Religion quickly gained prominence in the mid-2000s as a celebrity favorite, with endorsements from A-listers like 50 Cent, Jessica Alba, and Paris Hilton, driving its expansion into a global premium label sold at high-end retailers. Despite facing challenges including multiple ownership changessuch as its 2013 acquisition by TowerBrook Capital Partners, a 2017 bankruptcy and sale to Global Brands Group, and a 2020 restructuringthe brand experienced a resurgence in the 2020s, including a January 2025 acquisition by ACON Investments and SB360 Capital Partners, leveraging nostalgia, collaborations, and Gen Z-targeted marketing to reclaim its status in streetwear culture. As of 2025, True Religion continues to innovate with sustainable materials and limited-edition collections, maintaining its influence in the approximately $87 billion global denim market (as of 2024).

Overview

Company profile

True Religion Apparel, Inc. is an American fashion company founded in 2002 by entrepreneur Jeffrey Lubell and designer Kym Gold in Los Angeles, California, with an initial focus on premium denim products designed to redefine casual style through superior quality and unique aesthetics. The brand quickly established itself as a key player in the apparel industry, emphasizing craftsmanship and innovation in its early offerings.
Headquartered in Gardena, California, the company operates globally as a premium lifestyle brand that produces high-quality apparel, accessories, and fragrances for men, women, and children. True Religion's signature Buddha logo and heritage in premium denim underscore its commitment to distinctive, culturally resonant designs.
In January 2025, ACON Investments, L.L.C., along with affiliates including Schottenstein & Company, acquired a controlling stake in the company, marking a new phase of strategic growth. Following its 2020 restructuring, the brand reported annual revenue of approximately $280 million in 2023, reflecting steady recovery and expansion in its product lines.

Brand identity and market position

True Religion's brand identity is deeply rooted in its distinctive visual elements that emerged in the early 2000s, setting it apart in the premium denim landscape. The brand's signature horseshoe stitching on back pockets, often in high-contrast colors, became an iconic hallmark of luxury craftsmanship. This stitching, in the form of the oversized "Super T" or "Big T" patterns, adorns the back pockets of its jeans, contributing to the brand's recognizable silhouette. Complementing this is the embroidered Buddha logo patch, typically placed on the waistband or front pockets, which evokes a sense of spiritual enlightenment and laid-back California cool, drawing from founder Jeffrey Lubell's vision of blending Eastern philosophy with Western streetwear. Additionally, True Religion utilized distressed wash techniques from its inception in 2002, employing stonewashing and whiskering methods to create worn-in, vintage aesthetics that mimicked high-end, lived-in luxury without compromising durability.
The target audience for True Religion has evolved significantly since its inception, reflecting shifts in cultural trends and consumer preferences. Initially appealing to affluent millennials in the 2000s seeking status-symbol denim, the brand has repositioned itself to capture Gen Z through Y2K nostalgia marketing campaigns that revive early-2000s aesthetics like low-rise fits and bold graphics. This strategy leverages social media influencers and collaborations to emphasize flirtatious, body-hugging silhouettes, resonating with younger consumers' embrace of retro revival.
In the competitive premium denim segment, True Religion holds a strong position as a revival brand, rivaling established players like Citizens of Humanity and AG Jeans through its emphasis on artisanal detailing and cultural relevance. The brand reported annual revenue of approximately $280 million in 2023, primarily from U.S.-based wholesale partnerships and direct-to-consumer channels, including a growing e-commerce presence. This positioning underscores its focus on high-quality, trend-driven apparel that balances heritage with contemporary appeal.
True Religion has incorporated sustainability initiatives to align with modern consumer values, announcing eco-friendly denim sourcing practices in 2023 that include repurposing existing materials for new collections. Through efforts like the Stitch Remixed campaign, the brand integrates recycled denim and deadstock fabrics, aiming to reduce waste and promote circularity. These steps reflect a commitment to ethical production while maintaining the premium quality central to its identity.

History

Founding and early development

True Religion was founded in 2002 in Los Angeles by Jeffrey Lubell and designer Kym Gold, a denim industry veteran with prior experience in textiles and apparel. Lubell, who served as the company's chairman, CEO, and creative director, drew inspiration from spiritual themes, naming the brand "True Religion" to reflect the idea that there is only one true religion in the world: humanity, as symbolized by everyone wearing jeans. The brand's logo, featuring a smiling Buddha figure, reflected Lubell's personal spiritual outlook, representing peace, happiness, and enlightenment.
The debut collection launched in 2002, centering on premium five-pocket jeans crafted from high-quality denim with innovative construction. Lubell reengineered standard sewing machines to produce the signature "Super T" stitchinga bold, five-needle, two-stitches-per-inch patternand introduced the distinctive horseshoe embroidery on the back pockets, setting the jeans apart in a crowded market. Priced at $200 or more, these jeans positioned True Religion as a luxury denim option from the outset, emphasizing superior fit, comfort, and artisanal details. The company bootstrapped its operations with Lubell's personal investment, achieving modest initial sales of $2.4 million in 2003 while recording a small net loss of $10,700 amid efforts to build production and distribution.
Early growth relied on strategic wholesale partnerships and targeted marketing, including gifting jeans to celebrities to generate buzz in Hollywood circles. By 2004, True Religion secured distribution in upscale department stores such as Bloomingdale's and Nordstrom, fueling a surge in sales to $27.7 million and turning the prior year's loss into a $4.2 million profit. This milestone validated the brand's premium positioning, with products appearing on high-profile figures like Courteney Cox and David Beckham. The company marked further progress in late 2005 by opening its first retail storea 900-square-foot flagship in Manhattan Beach, Californiaexpanding beyond wholesale channels amid projected sales exceeding $90 million for the year.

Rise to prominence in the 2000s

During the mid-2000s, True Religion gained significant visibility through endorsements by high-profile celebrities, who frequently wore the brand's distinctive jeans in public appearances, music videos, and on red carpets. Stars such as Jennifer Lopez, Ashton Kutcher, Paris Hilton, and Jessica Simpson were often photographed in True Religion's premium denim, which helped position the brand as a staple of early 2000s celebrity fashion and boosted its appeal among consumers seeking luxury streetwear.
The brand experienced rapid revenue growth during this period, driven by strong wholesale demand and expanding retail presence. Sales increased from approximately $173 million in 2007 to $467 million by 2012, reflecting the surge in popularity of its signature styles amid the premium denim boom. International expansion played a key role, with licensing agreements facilitating entry into markets in Europe and Asia, alongside the opening of dedicated stores; by the end of 2012, True Religion operated 122 stores in the United States and 30 internationally.
Product diversification further fueled the brand's ascent, broadening its appeal beyond core men's jeanswhich featured the iconic curved pocket stitchingto include women's apparel and accessories. In 2008, True Religion introduced a dedicated women's line alongside its first handbag collection, designed to complement the denim with leather goods bearing the brand's signature hardware. The company expanded into footwear in 2010 through a licensing partnership, launching a women's shoe range that incorporated similar stylistic elements. Additionally, the fragrance line debuted in 2008 with the women's scent True Religion, a floral woody musk composition, followed by a men's version in 2009.
By 2012, True Religion's success epitomized the era's luxury streetwear trend, culminating in a valuation exceeding $800 million ahead of its privatization. The brand's $835 million acquisition by TowerBrook Capital Partners in 2013 underscored its peak market position, built on celebrity-driven hype and strategic global scaling.

2013 acquisition by TowerBrook Capital Partners

In May 2013, True Religion Apparel Inc. agreed to be acquired by private equity firm TowerBrook Capital Partners in a transaction valued at approximately $835 million, with TowerBrook offering $32 per share in cash for all outstanding common stock. This represented a premium of about 9 percent over the company's closing stock price of $29.44 on May 9, 2013, and came amid declining sales in the premium denim sector following a peak of roughly $490 million in revenue for fiscal year 2013 (ended January 2014). The deal, approved by shareholders on July 29, 2013, closed on July 30, 2013, taking True Religion private and delisting its shares from Nasdaq. TowerBrook, known for investments in consumer brands like Jimmy Choo, aimed to reposition the company through operational improvements and growth in a challenging retail environment.
The acquisition prompted immediate leadership transitions to support strategic realignment. David Conn, a marketing executive with prior roles at VF Corporation (owner of brands like The North Face) and Iconix Brand Group, was appointed CEO effective July 30, 2013, replacing interim leader Lynne Koplin. Conn's mandate focused on enhancing brand positioning and international opportunities, building on True Religion's established U.S. retail footprint of over 200 stores. In June 2015, John Ermatinger, a retail veteran from Macy's Inc. and Liz Claiborne Inc., succeeded Conn as CEO to accelerate operational efficiencies, supply chain optimizations, and digital initiatives.
Key strategic shifts under TowerBrook emphasized digital expansion and cost management to counter slowing domestic demand for high-end jeans. In September 2015, True Religion launched its dedicated e-commerce site on the Demandware (now Salesforce Commerce Cloud) platform, integrating online sales with in-store inventory for a seamless omnichannel experience and aiming to boost direct-to-consumer revenue. The company also introduced in-store technologies like the Endless Aisle system in late 2014, allowing customers to access full online catalogs via tablets for immediate fulfillment. Concurrently, efforts to streamline operations included inventory reductions and selective store closures to address overexpansion, though the brand continued modest retail growth internationally through wholesale partnerships.
Performance under TowerBrook's ownership showed short-term stabilization but mounting pressures from market shifts toward athleisure and fast fashion. Revenue declined from $469 million in fiscal 2014 (ended January 2015) to $410 million in fiscal 2015 and further to about $377 million for the year ended October 2016, reflecting weaker comparable-store sales and reduced wholesale demand. Aggressive store openings in the early post-acquisition years contributed to over $400 million in long-term debt by late 2016, including a $400 million first-lien term loan issued at closing that traded at distressed levels of around 27 cents on the dollar amid liquidity concerns. Despite these challenges, TowerBrook's involvement enabled targeted marketing refreshes, such as the 2014 "Fearless Style" campaign featuring bold imagery to reaffirm the brand's premium appeal.

2017 bankruptcy and acquisition by Global Brands Group

True Religion Apparel Inc. filed for voluntary Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection on July 5, 2017, in the U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the District of Delaware. The petition estimated assets and liabilities each in the range of $100 million to $500 million.
The filing stemmed from weakening demand for premium denim amid the rise of athleisure wear and competition from fast-fashion retailers, compounded by heavy reliance on U.S. brick-and-mortar stores and approximately $471 million in debt built up during expansion under TowerBrook Capital Partners' ownership since 2013. Fiscal 2016 net sales fell to $369.5 million, accompanied by a $78.5 million net loss.
The pre-packaged restructuring agreement with lenders enabled a swift resolution, with the court confirming the reorganization plan on October 5, 2017, and the company emerging from bankruptcy on October 25. This slashed debt by more than $350 million via loan-to-equity conversions, reducing term loans to $113.5 million payable through 2022, while securing $60 million in debtor-in-possession financing from Citizens Bank to support operations. As part of cost-cutting, True Religion closed 27 U.S. stores, shrinking its retail footprint from about 140 locations.
Post-restructuring, the brand shifted emphasis to revitalizing its wholesale channels and streamlining retail operations under lender-backed management, positioning it for improved financial stability alongside other apparel holdings in the reorganized portfolio.

2020 bankruptcy and restructuring

True Religion Apparel, Inc., filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection on April 13, 2020, in the United States Bankruptcy Court for the District of Delaware, marking the company's second such filing in three years. The petition listed assets and liabilities each in the range of $100 million to $500 million, with approximately $182 million in funded debt as of the filing date. The bankruptcy was precipitated by the COVID-19 pandemic, which led to widespread store closures and a sharp decline in retail traffic, compounding pre-existing challenges including unappealing product designs and a 25% drop in sales during 2019 to about $259 million, resulting in a net loss of $50 million for that year.
The filing occurred amid escalating financial distress at True Religion's parent company, Global Brands Group (GBG), which had acquired the brand out of its 2017 bankruptcy but was itself grappling with liquidity shortages and operational pressures from the pandemic. GBG's woes, including delayed payments to suppliers and mounting debt, accelerated True Religion's path to insolvency, as the parent could no longer provide adequate support. Sales plummeted further in 2020 to $151 million, a decline of over 40% from the prior year, largely due to the enforced shutdown of physical retail operations.
Under a prepackaged restructuring support agreement with key lenders and GBG, True Religion's reorganization plan was confirmed by the court on October 7, 2020, allowing the company to emerge from bankruptcy on October 19, 2020. The plan reduced the company's debt load by approximately 80%, converting much of its nearly $500 million in obligations into equity and eliminating unsecured claims, while extending maturities on remaining secured debt. Majority ownership shifted to a consortium of existing lenders, positioning the reorganized entity for streamlined operations free from GBG's influence.
Following emergence, True Religion pivoted aggressively toward digital channels to offset the impacts of reduced physical presence, with e-commerce accounting for about 35% of total sales by late 2021 and projected to reach 50% of revenue the following year. The company rationalized its store footprint, closing 37 locations during the proceedings and retaining approximately 49 U.S. stores alongside a limited global network totaling around 100 outlets, focusing on high-performing sites in key markets. This leaner structure, combined with cost reductions, enabled the brand to report profitability in its initial post-bankruptcy quarters.

2025 acquisition by ACON Investments

On January 7, 2025, ACON Investments, LLC, alongside strategic partner SB360 Capital Partners led by Jay Schottenstein, acquired a controlling stake in True Religion for an undisclosed amount. This transaction marked the latest chapter in the brand's ownership evolution, building on the stabilization achieved following its 2020 restructuring.
The acquisition was driven by True Religion's renewed appeal through Y2K-era nostalgia, particularly among Gen Z consumers who have embraced the brand's signature denim styles in contemporary fashion trends. ACON highlighted the opportunity to invest in an established lifestyle brand at a pivotal growth inflection point, leveraging its post-2020 recovery where annual revenue reached $280 million in 2023, up from $151 million in 2020. This financial momentum, coupled with $80 million in EBITDA for 2023, positioned True Religion as a prime candidate for accelerated expansion under private equity guidance.
In the first quarter of 2025, ACON outlined strategic initiatives to enhance True Religion's global footprint, including international e-commerce expansion and deepened celebrity collaborations to broaden product diversification beyond core denim. Key partnerships featured artists such as Anitta, Megan Thee Stallion, Saweetie, GloRilla, Duke Dennis, and Ciara, aligning with the brand's hip-hop roots and cultural relevance. No store closures were announced, reflecting a commitment to maintaining physical retail presence while prioritizing digital channels.
As of November 2025, True Religion continues to emphasize digital growth, with e-commerce projected to comprise half of its sales by year-end, supported by stable U.S. wholesale partnerships. The brand's holiday campaign, "Wrapped in True," launched in October with Ciara, underscores ongoing momentum in community-building and performance-driven marketing.

Products and Design

Core product lines

True Religion's core product lines center on premium denim as the foundation of its offerings, with expansions into broader apparel and accessories to appeal to diverse consumer segments. The brand's denim collection includes men's, women's, and children's jeans in various silhouettes, such as straight-leg (e.g., Ricky fit), skinny (e.g., Rocco), bootcut (e.g., Billy), baggy (e.g., Bobby), and slim fits, available in light and dark washes. These jeans represent approximately 40% of the company's business, reflecting a strategic balance with non-denim categories while maintaining denim's iconic status.
Beyond denim, True Religion has developed apparel extensions including T-shirts, hoodies, joggers, button-down shirts, and jackets, with the brand reporting sales of millions of units in these categories. Activewear lines, such as joggers and statement sets, were integrated post-2010 as part of broader lifestyle expansions, with seasonal collections like the 2025 ICONS line featuring activewear elements targeted at Gen Z through collaborations with influencers like GloRilla and Duke Dennis.
The accessories range complements the apparel with handbags like denim totes and sling bags, belts featuring signature horseshoe buckles in embossed or monogram designs, and eyewear including sunglasses and prescription frames. Fragrances form another key category, with the debut of True Religion for Women Eau de Parfum in 2008, a floral woody musk scent that remains available alongside newer options like the fruity-floral Always True Femme.
In terms of sizing and inclusivity, True Religion offers extended sizes through its dedicated Plus Shop, providing women's jeans and apparel up to 3X to broaden accessibility. The brand has also incorporated vegan alternatives, such as faux leather in products like the Horseshoe Satchel Bag and Becca Ponte Bootcut Jeans, aligning with contemporary sustainability preferences.

Signature design elements

True Religion's signature design elements revolve around bold, artisanal details that emphasize premium craftsmanship and visual distinction in denim apparel. Central to this aesthetic is the back pocket embroidery, featuring oversized horseshoe or Buddha-inspired stitching in red, white, and blue threads, introduced in 2002 as the brand's inaugural design feature. This motif, achieved through a proprietary five-needle threading process at two stitches per inch known as the Super T stitch, creates a prominent, curved pattern that has been trademarked to protect its uniqueness.
The brand's denim undergoes specialized washing and distressing techniques to enhance texture and longevity, utilizing premium fabrics often sourced from Japanese mills for superior quality and weight. Hand-sanded fades and 3D whiskering simulate organic wear patterns, adding depth without compromising structural integrity; these methods have been refined over time to include eco-conscious washes that reduce water usage by up to 50% compared to traditional processes, a practice adopted as part of broader sustainability initiatives starting in the mid-2010s and continued into recent collections.
Complementing these fabric treatments are distinctive hardware and branding accents, including custom-engraved rivets, embossed leather patches bearing the Buddha emblem on the rear waistband, and contrasting thread along visible seams for added dimension. These elements underscore a fusion of luxury and streetwear, ensuring each garment bears the brand's identifiable stamp.
In response to shifting fashion preferences, True Religion has transitioned from the exaggerated distressing and embellishments dominant in its 2000s heyday to sleeker, minimalist profiles in 2025 lines, such as streamlined washes and subtler hardware placements, while preserving the core pocket and stitching signatures to maintain brand heritage and attract a wider demographic.

Business Operations

Retail strategy and distribution

True Religion employs a hybrid retail model that combines owned brick-and-mortar stores with extensive wholesale distribution. As of early 2025, the brand operates 51 owned retail locations primarily in the United States, with plans to expand this footprint to approximately 150 stores in the coming years. In Europe, True Religion maintains a presence through a distribution partnership with UNIFA Premium, which manages branded stores and wholesale operations across Germany, Austria, Switzerland, and the Netherlands. The wholesale channel forms the core of its distribution, reaching over 6,000 doors in North America and 1,000 internationally, including major department stores such as Macy's, Nordstrom, Dillard's, and Saks Off 5th.
E-commerce represents a significant and growing portion of True Religion's sales, projected to account for nearly 50% of total revenue by the end of 2025, up from about 35% three years prior. The brand's direct-to-consumer platform at truereligion.com supports international shipping to multiple countries, facilitating global access. Additionally, True Religion partners with major online marketplaces to broaden its digital reach, including sales through platforms like Amazon and European sites such as Zalando.
The company's supply chain emphasizes cost-effective manufacturing across international facilities, with production primarily in Mexico, Vietnam, and China to serve global demand. Some premium lines are produced domestically in the United States using high-quality denim. Logistics operations are centered in the Los Angeles area, leveraging the Port of Los Angeles as a key import hub for shipments arriving from overseas suppliers.
Following its 2020 bankruptcy, True Religion underwent significant store rationalization, closing dozens of locations and reducing its physical retail footprint by roughly 50% to streamline operations and focus on profitability. In 2025, the brand has shifted emphasis toward agile distribution in emerging markets, particularly through pop-up shops in Asia, such as activations in Seoul and planned expansions in China, India, and Indonesia to test demand and build brand awareness.

Marketing and celebrity endorsements

True Religion has maintained strong ties to hip-hop and celebrity culture since its early days, with notable endorsements from artists like 50 Cent in the 2000s, who helped cement the brand's status as a status symbol in rap music. In recent years, the brand has revitalized these connections through high-profile partnerships aimed at younger audiences, including social media influencers targeting Gen Z consumers. Following its 2020 restructuring, True Religion appointed its first chief marketing officer in 2023 to oversee these efforts, focusing on authentic collaborations that blend nostalgia with contemporary appeal.
Post-2020 campaigns have emphasized celebrity ambassadors to drive relevance, such as Grammy-winning rapper Megan Thee Stallion, who starred in the brand's 2024 holiday campaign "Where Holiday Wishes Come True," showcasing signature denim pieces. This was followed by partnerships with Saweetie for the Spring 2024 collection, highlighting streetwear elements, and Brazilian pop star Anitta launching the "Own Your True" platform in early 2025, which promotes individuality through bold styling. In 2025, the ICONS collection featured GloRilla and Duke Dennis, while the holiday campaign "Wrapped in True" spotlighted Ciara, emphasizing confidence and self-expression across fashion and entertainment.
Key promotional initiatives have leaned into Y2K nostalgia to recapture early 2000s vibes, including a 2025 limited-edition holiday collection with Von Dutch that revives low-rise denim and bold graphics, integrated with TikTok content for viral reach among younger demographics. Earlier efforts, like the Fall 2024 "Catch Us While You Can" campaign, expanded global influence through music and sports tie-ins, building on the brand's hip-hop roots. These campaigns often incorporate digital platforms, with True Religion allocating approximately 10% of its turnoveraround $28 million based on 2023 revenuesto marketing, prioritizing influencer-generated content on Instagram and YouTube for optimal return on ad spend.
The brand has shifted toward experiential marketing to boost engagement, moving away from traditional print ads toward immersive events that enhance celebrity-driven visibility. Notable activations include Los Angeles pop-ups like the 2025 Michaeladas collaboration with Bella Doa, fostering community through cultural events, and broader initiatives such as a college campus tour and drone shows tied to the 2025 holiday campaign. During New York Fashion Week 2025, a mobile truck pop-up offered interactive shopping and loyalty sign-ups, aligning with endorsements to drive direct consumer interaction. These strategies have contributed to revenue growth, with sales rising from $255 million in 2021 to $280 million in 2023, over $370 million in 2024, and projected to approach $500 million in 2025.

Cultural and Market Impact

Influence on fashion trends

True Religion played a pivotal role in the early 2000s denim revolution by introducing premium, logo-emblazoned jeans that emphasized bold aesthetics and high-end craftsmanship, setting a new standard for luxury casual wear. Founded in 2002, the brand's innovative designs, including thick white stitching and prominent horseshoe emblems, quickly gained traction among celebrities and influenced the shift toward designer denim as a fashion statement, often priced at around $400 per pair. This premium positioning helped elevate denim from everyday staple to aspirational item, inspiring widespread adoption in Hollywood and urban scenes.
The brand's smiling Buddha logo emerged as a key cultural symbol, particularly within hip-hop and celebrity circles, where it signified status and individuality during the mid-2000s. Worn by artists like those referenced in Fergie's 2005 track "My Humps" and later embraced by rappers such as Chief Keef and 2 Chainz, the logo became synonymous with the era's flashy, expressive style, contributing to True Religion's revenue surge to $173 million in 2007 through hip-hop endorsements. Its visibility in noughties party culture and 2010s rap aesthetics solidified the brand's place in streetwear lore.
True Religion's emphasis on distinctive, embroidered details also spurred broader trends, including the proliferation of affordable imitations by fast fashion retailers that democratized logo-heavy and distressed denim looks in the 2000s. Recognized alongside contemporaries like Seven for All Mankind, the brand is frequently cited in fashion media as an enduring 2000s icon, with its low-rise, embellished styles fueling the Y2K revival among Gen Z in the 2020s through nostalgic reinterpretations on platforms like TikTok and high-profile endorsements, such as Timothe Chalamet's appearances. This resurgence has highlighted True Religion's lasting contribution to blending denim with contemporary casual hybrids, though some observers have critiqued the Buddha motif's commercialization as potentially exploitative of Asian spiritual heritage since the early 2010s.

Recent revival efforts

Following its 2020 bankruptcy and restructuring, True Religion implemented targeted strategies to revitalize the brand amid post-pandemic market shifts. In 2024 and 2025, the company focused on appealing to Gen Z consumers through nostalgia-driven campaigns that leveraged the resurgence of Y2K fashion trends, featuring collaborations with influencers and artists like GloRilla and Duke Dennis in the ICONS 2025 collection. These efforts included lowering entry-level pricing, with average jeans now at around $100$150 compared to $172$350 in the 2000s, making the brand more accessible to younger buyers. This approach contributed to sales growth, with the company exceeding first-quarter 2025 targets and projecting approximately $500 million in total revenue for 2025 as of November, with a long-term goal of $1 billion in volume.
Digital transformation played a central role in the revival, with the launch of a mobile app in late 2022 providing exclusive access to collections, offers, and personalized shopping experiences, which evolved to drive deeper customer engagement by 2025. E-commerce became a key growth engine, representing nearly 50% of total business by the end of 2025, up from one-third three years prior, helping diversify revenue streams and recover from pandemic-induced losses that had slashed physical retail sales. The 2025 acquisition by ACON Investments further supported these initiatives, providing capital to accelerate digital and international expansion.
On the sustainability front, True Religion committed to eco-friendly practices, including water-saving denim production techniques and guidance for customers to repurpose old garments, aligning with broader industry demands for circularity. While specific quantitative targets like recycled material percentages were not publicly detailed in 2025 announcements, the brand's vendor code of conduct emphasized compliance with environmental regulations, and earlier efforts like deadstock utilization informed ongoing adaptations. These measures addressed challenges from the pandemic era, where store closures eliminated much of the revenue base, by prioritizing resilient, diversified operations that balanced heritage appeal with modern consumer priorities.
