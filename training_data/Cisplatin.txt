Cisplatin
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Cisplatin is a platinum-based chemotherapeutic agent classified as an alkylating drug, widely used in the treatment of various solid tumors and hematologic malignancies. It functions by forming covalent bonds with DNA, primarily at guanine bases, leading to intra- and inter-strand crosslinks that inhibit DNA replication and transcription, ultimately causing cell death in rapidly dividing cancer cells. First synthesized in 1845 by Michele Peyrone, its anticancer properties were serendipitously discovered in 1965 by Barnett Rosenberg during experiments on bacterial cell division, revealing its ability to halt cell proliferation. Approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration in 1978, cisplatin revolutionized cancer therapy and remains a cornerstone in regimens for testicular, ovarian, bladder, lung, head and neck, and cervical cancers, often improving survival rates significantly.
Despite its efficacy, cisplatin is associated with dose-limiting toxicities, including nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity, neurotoxicity, and emetogenicity, necessitating supportive measures like hydration and antiemetics during administration. Its intravenous delivery requires careful monitoring, and resistance mechanisms, such as enhanced DNA repair and reduced cellular uptake, pose ongoing challenges in clinical use. Over the decades, cisplatin has inspired the development of less toxic analogs like carboplatin and oxaliplatin, yet it continues to be integral to combination therapies, underscoring its enduring impact on oncology.

Medical Applications

Indications

Cisplatin is approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of advanced ovarian cancer, either as a single agent or in combination with other approved chemotherapeutic agents in patients who have already received appropriate surgical and/or radiation therapy. It is also FDA-approved for metastatic testicular tumors and metastatic ovarian tumors in established combination therapy regimens. Additionally, cisplatin is indicated for the treatment of advanced bladder cancer.
In clinical practice, cisplatin is a cornerstone of first-line therapy for several other solid tumors, including head and neck cancers, where it is commonly administered concurrently with radiation therapy to improve locoregional control and survival in locally advanced squamous cell carcinoma. For non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), particularly in advanced stages, cisplatin-based doublets such as cisplatin plus pemetrexed are recommended by the National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) as preferred regimens for nonsquamous histology. In cervical cancer, cisplatin is a standard component of chemoradiation for locally advanced disease and is also used in combination regimens for metastatic settings, per NCCN guidelines. For osteosarcoma, cisplatin is incorporated into multi-agent neoadjuvant and adjuvant protocols, often combined with doxorubicin and high-dose methotrexate, as endorsed by ESMO-PaedCan-EURACAN guidelines to enhance antitumor activity.
Notable efficacy has been demonstrated in testicular cancer, where cisplatin-based combination chemotherapy, such as with etoposide and bleomycin, achieves cure rates exceeding 90% in advanced disease, transforming it from a frequently fatal condition to one with high survivability. Similar high response rates are observed in other primary indications; for instance, in advanced ovarian cancer, cisplatin regimens yield complete response rates of approximately 40-60% when combined with paclitaxel, contributing to prolonged progression-free survival.
Off-label applications of cisplatin include esophageal, gastric, and brain cancers, though evidence is more limited and typically involves combination therapies. In esophageal cancer, cisplatin with fluorouracil is used for neoadjuvant or palliative intent, with response rates around 30-40% but variable overall survival benefits. For gastric cancer, cisplatin-based regimens like with capecitabine are employed in advanced cases, per some international protocols, with response rates of approximately 30-50%. In brain cancers such as glioblastoma, cisplatin has been explored in multi-modal approaches, but its role remains investigational due to limited penetration of the blood-brain barrier and inconsistent efficacy data.
Major oncology guidelines reinforce cisplatin's role as first-line therapy across these indications. The NCCN recommends cisplatin-containing regimens as category 1 options for testicular, ovarian, bladder, head and neck, NSCLC, cervical, and osteosarcoma treatments, emphasizing combinations to optimize outcomes; however, carboplatin is often preferred over cisplatin in patients with higher toxicity risks. Similarly, the European Society for Medical Oncology (ESMO) guidelines advocate cisplatin-based chemotherapy for advanced urothelial (bladder), cervical, and head and neck cancers, as well as in osteosarcoma protocols, while noting alternatives like carboplatin for patients ineligible due to toxicity risks.

Administration and Dosage

Cisplatin is administered exclusively via intravenous infusion, as it is not suitable for oral or other routes due to its chemical properties and potential for severe local toxicity if extravasated. The infusion is typically diluted in 0.9% sodium chloride or other compatible solutions and administered over 1 to 4 hours, though durations ranging from 15 minutes to 24 hours have been employed depending on the regimen and patient factors. An in-line filter is recommended to prevent particulate contamination during administration.
Dosages are individualized based on body surface area (in mg/m) and the specific cancer being treated, with cycles typically repeated every 3 to 4 weeks to allow recovery from potential toxicities. For single-agent use in advanced bladder cancer, the recommended dose is 50 to 70 mg/m per cycle. In ovarian cancer, doses of 75 to 100 mg/m every 3 to 4 weeks are common. For heavily pretreated patients, initial doses may be reduced to 50 mg/m every 4 weeks, with subsequent adjustments based on tolerance. Maximum cumulative doses are generally limited to avoid long-term complications, though exact limits vary by protocol.
In combination regimens, cisplatin dosing is adjusted to complement other agents. For metastatic testicular cancer in the BEP regimen (with bleomycin and etoposide), it is given at 20 mg/m daily for 5 consecutive days, repeated every 3 weeks. Similar fractionated dosing applies in regimens for other germ cell tumors. These combinations enhance efficacy while distributing the cisplatin load to minimize peak exposure.
Hydration protocols are critical to support renal function during treatment. Patients receive 1 to 2 liters of intravenous normal saline or balanced electrolyte solution before infusion, with additional post-infusion hydration to maintain urine output above 100 mL/hour. Mannitol or furosemide may be included in some protocols to promote diuresis. Emerging short hydration strategies, delivering 2 to 4 liters over 4 to 5 hours, have demonstrated comparable safety to traditional longer regimens in clinical studies.
Prior to each cycle, renal function must be assessed, with creatinine clearance exceeding 60 mL/min required for full dosing; reduced clearance prompts dose adjustments or alternative therapies. Premedication with antiemetics, such as 5-HT3 receptor antagonists and corticosteroids, is standard to prevent acute nausea and vomiting associated with infusion. Electrolyte levels, including magnesium and potassium, are monitored and supplemented as needed throughout treatment.

Side Effects and Toxicity

Cisplatin is associated with a range of significant adverse effects, primarily due to its mechanism of DNA cross-linking and accumulation in various tissues, leading to dose-limiting toxicities that necessitate careful monitoring and supportive care during therapy.
Among the major toxicities, nephrotoxicity is the primary dose-limiting effect, occurring in 20-30% of patients and manifesting as acute kidney injury through proximal tubular damage, oxidative stress, and inflammation. Ototoxicity, characterized by high-frequency hearing loss and tinnitus, affects 30-50% of patients, with higher rates in pediatric populations (40-60%) due to cochlear hair cell apoptosis and vascular changes in the inner ear. Neurotoxicity, particularly peripheral neuropathy, develops in up to 50% of patients after cumulative doses exceeding 350 mg/m, presenting as symmetrical sensory deficits from dorsal root ganglion neuron damage. Emetogenicity is profound, with severe acute and delayed nausea and vomiting occurring in 80-100% of patients receiving high-dose regimens, driven by serotonin and substance P release in the central nervous system.
Other notable effects include myelosuppression, which impacts 25-30% of patients with nadirs in platelets and leukocytes around days 18-23, increasing infection risk. Electrolyte imbalances, such as hypomagnesemia, arise in 40-90% of cases from renal tubular dysfunction and can persist long-term, often accompanied by hypokalemia or hypocalcemia. Alopecia is generally mild and reversible, affecting a smaller proportion of patients compared to other toxicities.
Management strategies focus on prevention and mitigation: vigorous hydration with mannitol-induced diuresis reduces nephrotoxicity incidence by promoting cisplatin excretion, while amifostine provides cytoprotective effects against renal damage when administered prior to infusion. For ototoxicity, baseline and serial audiology monitoring is essential to detect early hearing loss and guide dose adjustments. Antiemetic regimens combining 5-HT3 antagonists, NK1 receptor antagonists, and corticosteroids effectively control emetogenicity in most cases. Electrolyte disturbances require supplementation, particularly magnesium repletion to prevent cardiac and neuromuscular complications.
Emerging data from 2024-2025 studies highlight cisplatin's potential for cognitive impairment, linked to hippocampal neurotoxicity involving neuroinflammation, oxidative stress, and glutamatergic receptor overexpression, which may contribute to long-term "chemobrain" effects in survivors.

Pharmacology

Mechanism of Action

Cisplatin, chemically cis-, is a neutral, lipophilic compound that enters cells via passive diffusion. Inside the cell, where chloride ion concentration is low (approximately 4 mM compared to 100 mM in extracellular fluid), it undergoes sequential aquation reactions to form reactive electrophilic species capable of binding to nucleophilic sites on biomolecules. The first aquation step replaces one chloride ligand with a water molecule:
PtCl2(NH3)2+H2O++Cl
\text{PtCl}_2(\text{NH}_3)_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons ^{+} + \text{Cl}^{-}
PtCl2(NH3)2+H2O++Cl
This monoaquated species then undergoes a second aquation to yield the highly reactive diaqua form , which is the primary species responsible for DNA platination.
The activated platinum complexes predominantly target DNA, forming covalent adducts at the N7 position of purine bases, especially guanine. Approximately 90% of these adducts are intrastrand cross-links, including the major 1,2-d(GpG) and 1,2-d(ApG) types, with about 5-10% being interstrand cross-links such as 1,4-adducts between guanine residues on opposite strands. These cross-links distort the DNA helix, bending it by up to 40 and unwinding it, which impedes the progression of DNA and RNA polymerases. As a result, cisplatin inhibits DNA replication by stalling replication forks and blocks transcription by preventing RNA polymerase II from elongating past the lesions, leading to accumulation of stalled transcription complexes.
The DNA damage induced by cisplatin triggers cellular DNA damage response (DDR) pathways, resulting in cell cycle arrest primarily at the G/M phase to allow time for repair attempts. If the damage is irreparable, it activates apoptosis through the p53 tumor suppressor pathway; p53 is stabilized and phosphorylated in response to the cross-links, transactivating pro-apoptotic genes such as BAX and PUMA, which promote mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization and caspase activation. In p53-deficient cells, alternative pathways like MAPK signaling may contribute to cell death, but p53-dependent apoptosis is the dominant mechanism in many cisplatin-sensitive tumors.

Pharmacokinetics

Cisplatin is administered intravenously due to its lack of oral bioavailability, resulting in 100% systemic bioavailability upon IV infusion.
Following intravenous administration, cisplatin rapidly distributes throughout the body, with more than 90% binding to plasma proteins such as albumin and gamma-globulins. The volume of distribution at steady state is approximately 11-12 L/m, indicating moderate tissue penetration. Cisplatin crosses the blood-brain barrier poorly, limiting its distribution to the central nervous system.
Cisplatin does not undergo significant enzymatic metabolism in the liver; instead, it is activated through non-enzymatic aquation, where chloride ligands are replaced by water molecules in aqueous environments.
Excretion of cisplatin occurs primarily via the kidneys, with 50-70% of the administered dose eliminated unchanged in the urine within 24 hours. The pharmacokinetics follow a biphasic pattern, featuring an initial alpha half-life of 20-30 minutes and a terminal beta half-life of approximately 73 hours for total platinum species. Renal clearance exceeds glomerular filtration rate, at around 50-62 mL/min/m, and is markedly reduced in patients with renal impairment, necessitating dose adjustments.

Resistance Mechanisms

Resistance to cisplatin in cancer cells can manifest as either intrinsic or acquired forms. Intrinsic resistance is present prior to treatment and prevents initial response to the drug, while acquired resistance develops during therapy as a result of selective pressure on surviving cells. In ovarian cancer, intrinsic resistance is often observed in clear cell and mucinous subtypes, where baseline metabolic alterations like enhanced glycolysis contribute to poor responsiveness, whereas acquired resistance emerges in serous subtypes following repeated exposure, involving adaptive changes in DNA repair and drug transport. Similarly, in non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), intrinsic resistance occurs at the onset of platinum-based therapy in a subset of patients due to pre-existing genetic variations, while acquired resistance arises post-treatment through clonal evolution of resistant subpopulations.
Several key molecular pathways underlie cisplatin resistance by counteracting the drug's primary effect of forming DNA cross-links. Enhanced DNA repair, particularly via upregulation of the nucleotide excision repair (NER) pathway, allows cells to excise cisplatin-induced adducts more efficiently; for instance, increased expression of excision repair cross-complementation group 1 (ERCC1) has been correlated with resistance in ovarian and lung cancers by facilitating the repair of intra- and inter-strand cross-links. Reduced drug uptake is mediated by downregulation of the copper transporter 1 (CTR1), which normally facilitates cisplatin influx, leading to lower intracellular accumulation in resistant cells across various tumor types. Additionally, increased efflux and detoxification occur through overexpression of glutathione (GSH) and glutathione S-transferase (GST) enzymes, which conjugate cisplatin to GSH for export via multidrug resistance proteins, thereby minimizing DNA damage.
Epigenetic modifications play a significant role in modulating cisplatin resistance, particularly in lung cancer, by altering gene expression without changing the DNA sequence. Recent 2025 research highlights how aberrant DNA methylation, such as hypermethylation of tumor suppressor genes involved in apoptosis, silences their function and promotes survival of cisplatin-exposed cells in NSCLC models. Histone modifications, including increased H3K27me3 repressive marks on DNA repair gene promoters, further enhance NER activity and resistance; for example, studies have shown that EZH2-mediated histone methylation upregulates ERCC1 expression in resistant lung cancer cell lines. These epigenetic changes are reversible and represent potential therapeutic targets to restore sensitivity.
Clinically, understanding resistance mechanisms has led to the identification of biomarkers for predicting treatment outcomes. ERCC1 expression levels serve as a key predictive biomarker, where high ERCC1 correlates with poorer response to cisplatin-based regimens in NSCLC and ovarian cancer patients, guiding decisions toward alternative therapies to avoid ineffective treatment and associated toxicity. Prospective trials have validated that low ERCC1 expression predicts better progression-free survival with platinum chemotherapy, emphasizing its role in personalized oncology.

Chemistry

Molecular Structure and Properties

Cisplatin, chemically known as cis-diamminedichloroplatinum(II), has the molecular formula PtCl(NH) and a molecular weight of 300.05 g/mol. This coordination compound features platinum in the +2 oxidation state, coordinated to two ammonia (NH) ligands and two chloride (Cl) ligands.
The molecular structure of cisplatin exhibits a square planar geometry, characteristic of d transition metal complexes like Pt(II), with the two chloride ligands and two ammine ligands arranged in a cis configurationmeaning the chlorides occupy adjacent positions around the platinum center. Approximate bond lengths include PtN at 2.05 and PtCl at 2.31 , reflecting the relative strengths of these bonds in the complex.
Physically, cisplatin appears as a yellow crystalline solid that decomposes at around 270C without melting. It has limited solubility in water, approximately 2.53 mg/mL at 25C, and is insoluble in ethanol but soluble in dimethylformamide. The compound remains stable in neutral pH environments with sufficient chloride ions but becomes reactive, undergoing hydrolysis to form aquated species, in low-chloride conditions.
In contrast to its geometric isomer transplatin (trans-diamminedichloroplatinum(II)), which has the chloride ligands in opposite positions and forms pale yellow crystals with higher water solubility, cisplatin's cis arrangement imparts distinct structural properties relevant to its applications.

Synthesis

The historical laboratory synthesis of cisplatin involves the reaction of potassium tetrachloroplatinate(II) (K) with aqueous ammonia:
KX2+2NHX3+2KCl
\ce{K2 + 2 NH3 -> + 2 KCl}
KX2+2NHX3+2KCl
This produces a mixture of cis and trans isomers.
However, the standard modern industrial and laboratory method, known as Dhara's synthesis (developed in 1970), selectively produces the cis isomer. It begins with the reaction of K with potassium iodide to form K, followed by addition of ammonia to yield cis- due to iodide's strong trans-directing effect. The iodo ligands are then removed using silver nitrate to form the diaqua complex, which is treated with hydrochloric acid to give cisplatin. This route achieves high selectivity for the cis isomer with yields over 90%.
Purification is accomplished by recrystallization from hot water containing 0.1 M HCl or 0.9% NaCl solution. For identification or separation in cases of isomer mixtures, the Kurnakov test with thiourea can be employed: the cis isomer forms a soluble tetra(thiourea) complex, while the trans isomer forms an insoluble bis(thiourea) complex.
Alternative optimized approaches include one-pot reactions using ammonium acetate for controlled ammonia release and incorporation of hydrogen peroxide for precursor modification, further enhancing efficiency and purity.
For pharmaceutical applications, cisplatin must meet stringent purity standards, typically greater than 99.0% on an anhydrous basis, with transplatin impurities limited to no more than 0.15% to ensure efficacy and safety. Additional recrystallizations or ion-exchange chromatography ensure the final product is free of significant transplatin contaminants.

History and Development

Discovery

In the early 1960s, biophysicist Barnett Rosenberg at Michigan State University initiated experiments to investigate the effects of electric fields on cellular processes, specifically aiming to understand how such fields might influence bacterial cell division. Using platinum electrodes in a nutrient medium containing Escherichia coli, Rosenberg and his team applied a direct current, expecting the electric field alone to impact growth; however, they observed that the bacteria elongated into filaments without dividing, halting mitosis without affecting overall growth or protein synthesis. This unexpected inhibition was traced not to the field but to electrolysis products from the platinum electrodes forming in the solution.
Further analysis over the following years revealed that the active agent involved platinum complexes, specifically the platinum(IV) complex cis-, which rapidly reduced to the platinum(II) compound cis-PtCl2(NH3)2identified as cisplatin by 1969responsible for blocking cell division at the mitotic stage in E. coli. These findings, initially published in 1965 and expanded in subsequent work, highlighted the compound's specificity in disrupting binary fission while permitting continued cellular elongation, suggesting a targeted interference with chromosomal segregation. The geometric isomer trans-PtCl2(NH3)2 showed no such activity, underscoring the importance of the cis configuration.
Encouraged by these results and noting structural similarities to known antitumor agents, Rosenberg's team tested cisplatin on animal models in 1968. In mice bearing solid Sarcoma 180 tumors, intraperitoneal administration of the compound at doses of 48 mg/kg led to complete tumor regression in over 90% of cases, with treated animals surviving tumor-free for extended periods. These preclinical successes were reported in a seminal 1969 publication, establishing platinum compounds, particularly cisplatin, as a new class of potent antitumor agents effective against solid tumors and leukemias in vivo.

Clinical Introduction

Cisplatin's transition to clinical use began with Phase I trials initiated in 1972 under the auspices of the National Cancer Institute (NCI), marking the first human evaluations of the compound following promising preclinical data. These studies, spanning 1972 to 1974, focused on determining the drug's safety, maximum tolerated dose, and preliminary pharmacokinetics in patients with advanced solid tumors, including ovarian and testicular cancers. Dosing regimens typically involved intravenous administration at escalating levels up to 100 mg/m, with hydration protocols developed to mitigate nephrotoxicity, a key dose-limiting adverse effect identified early. Initial observations revealed antitumor activity, including remissions in patients with ovarian cancer, paving the way for further development despite challenges like renal impairment and nausea.
Phase II trials in the mid-1970s expanded on these foundations, particularly through investigations at Indiana University led by Lawrence Einhorn, which tested cisplatin in combination with vinblastine and bleomycin (PVB regimen) for disseminated testicular and ovarian cancers. A landmark Phase II study published in 1977 enrolled 50 patients with advanced nonseminomatous germ cell tumors and achieved complete remissions in 36 cases (72%), with durable responses in most, representing a dramatic improvement over prior therapies that yielded cure rates below 10%. Subsequent Phase III trials in the late 1970s confirmed these results across larger cohorts, demonstrating superior efficacy compared to non-platinum regimens in both testicular and ovarian malignancies. These successes culminated in U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval on December 19, 1978, under the trade name Platinol by Bristol-Myers Squibb, initially for metastatic testicular and ovarian cancers refractory to standard chemotherapy.
Pivotal trials from this era, such as the Einhorn-Donohue study and related NCI-supported efforts, reported overall response rates of 70-90% in germ cell tumors using cisplatin-based combinations, including partial and complete responses that often led to surgical resection of residual disease and long-term cures. This high responsiveness, particularly in previously intractable advanced cases, underscored cisplatin's role in shifting testicular cancer from a lethal diagnosis to one with over 80% curability in metastatic settings. Global adoption followed swiftly, with approvals in the United Kingdom and other European countries in 1979, and inclusion on the World Health Organization's Model List of Essential Medicines in 1984, affirming its status as a cornerstone of oncology.

Current Research

Novel Formulations and Delivery

Novel formulations of cisplatin have been developed to address limitations of the conventional intravenous administration, such as poor tumor penetration, rapid clearance, and systemic toxicity, particularly nephrotoxicity. These innovations leverage nanotechnology, protein conjugates, and prodrug strategies to enhance targeted delivery, prolong circulation time, and minimize off-target effects. By encapsulating or conjugating cisplatin, these approaches aim to improve therapeutic indices while maintaining its DNA-crosslinking mechanism.
Nanotechnology-based systems, including liposomal and polymeric micelle formulations, represent a major advance in cisplatin delivery. Liposomal cisplatin encapsulates the drug within lipid bilayers, enabling pH-sensitive or sterically stabilized release in tumor microenvironments, which has shown promise in preclinical models for controlled release and reduced renal accumulation. Polymeric micelles, formed by amphiphilic block copolymers, provide stable cores for cisplatin loading and surface modifications for tumor targeting, with recent formulations demonstrating enhanced accumulation in solid tumors via the enhanced permeability and retention effect. In oral and oropharyngeal (ORL) cancers, 2025 advancements in these nanocarriers have enabled targeted delivery, improving local efficacy while mitigating systemic exposure.
Conjugates such as albumin-bound cisplatin exploit the natural tumor affinity of albumin for enhanced penetration and uptake. Human serum albumin-cisplatin complexes have demonstrated potent antitumor activity in ovarian cancer models with significantly lower nephrotoxicity compared to free cisplatin, attributed to altered biodistribution and reduced kidney filtration. Antibody-drug conjugates incorporating cisplatin derivatives further promote specificity, with linker technologies allowing intracellular release and improved tumor selectivity in preclinical studies.
Investigational oral formulations, primarily Pt(IV) prodrugs, aim to overcome intravenous limitations by enabling gastrointestinal absorption and activation in tumors. These prodrugs, often integrated with nanoscale carriers, exhibit stability in plasma and selective reduction to active cisplatin, with recent designs showing oral bioavailability in animal models. Phase II and III trials of liposomal and micellar cisplatin formulations have reported improvements in safety profiles, including lower incidence of nephrotoxicity, while maintaining or enhancing efficacy in cancers like lung and head-and-neck.

Combination Therapies and Resistance Strategies

Combination therapies involving cisplatin have evolved to enhance efficacy in various cancers by synergizing with other chemotherapeutic agents. In advanced biliary tract cancer, a phase III randomized clinical trial demonstrated that adding polymeric micellar paclitaxel to gemcitabine and cisplatin did not improve progression-free survival or overall survival compared to gemcitabine and cisplatin alone. Similarly, the SWOG S1815 phase III trial evaluated gemcitabine, nab-paclitaxel, and cisplatin versus gemcitabine and cisplatin, showing no significant overall survival benefit but highlighting potential in specific subgroups with advanced disease. For nasopharyngeal carcinoma, a phase III multicenter trial reported that four cycles of neoadjuvant docetaxel plus cisplatin followed by concurrent chemoradiotherapy significantly reduced distant metastasis rates to 10% compared to 22% with standard therapy at 5 years, prolonging 5-year disease-free survival to 81.7%.
Immunotherapy combinations represent a promising frontier, particularly in gynecologic malignancies. A phase II study presented at ASCO 2025 investigated cisplatin-sensitized radiation therapy with pembrolizumab for unresectable vulvar cancer, achieving an objective response rate of 75% and a 6-month relapse-free survival of 70%, surpassing historical benchmarks and meeting the primary endpoint. This regimen leverages cisplatin's DNA-damaging effects to enhance PD-1 inhibition, promoting antitumor immunity without excessive toxicity.
Strategies to overcome cisplatin resistance increasingly target epigenetic modifications and DNA repair pathways. In non-small cell lung cancer, histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitors such as vorinostat have been shown to reverse resistance by downregulating ERCC1 expression, a key component of nucleotide excision repair (NER), thereby resensitizing cells to cisplatin in preclinical models. These inhibitors promote chromatin remodeling, reducing DNA repair efficiency and enhancing apoptosis. Complementing this, CRISPR-Cas9 genome-wide screens in 2025 identified NER pathway genes like HELQ as promoting cisplatin resistance in gastric cancer organoid models; knockdown of HELQ restored sensitivity by impairing DNA damage repair. Such approaches have shown potential in preclinical studies to enhance sensitivity in resistant models.
