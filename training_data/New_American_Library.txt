 The New American Library (NAL) is an American publishing house founded in 1948 by Victor Weybright and Kurt Enoch, initially focused on producing affordable paperback editions of literature and scholarly non-fiction to democratize access to quality reading materials.
Established after acquiring the U.S. operations from Penguin Books, NAL introduced the Signet imprint for fiction and Mentor for non-fiction, emphasizing reprints of established classics alongside works by contemporary authors such as William Faulkner and J.D. Salinger.
The company pioneered mass-market paperbacks in the United States, achieving widespread distribution through partnerships with newsstands and bookstores, which significantly expanded readership during the post-World War II era.
Ownership transitioned multiple times, including acquisition by Times Mirror in 1960 and later integration into Penguin Random House following the 2013 merger of Penguin and Random House, after which NAL shifted to publishing exclusively nonfiction titles.

Founding and Early Development

Establishment and Founders' Vision

The New American Library of World Literature, Inc. (NAL) was founded in 1948 by Victor Weybright and Kurt Enoch, who purchased the U.S. assets of Penguin Books to establish an autonomous American paperback publishing house. Enoch, a German-Jewish immigrant and veteran of European paperback ventures including Albatross Books, assumed the presidency, drawing on his experience in producing affordable editions for mass audiences during the interwar period. Weybright, an American publisher previously involved in Penguin's U.S. operations, served as chairman and editor-in-chief, providing strategic direction for the venture. This partnership emerged amid the post-World War II expansion of paperback formats, which enabled wider distribution through newsstands, drugstores, and other retail outlets beyond traditional bookstores.
The founders envisioned NAL as a vehicle for delivering "good reading for the millions," prioritizing inexpensive reprints of quality literature to broaden access for working-class and middlebrow readers previously underserved by hardcover-dominated publishing. This mission reflected Enoch's European-influenced commitment to sleek, marketable designs and Weybright's emphasis on cultural enrichment through diverse genres, including unexpurgated classics and scholarly non-fiction. By launching imprints like Signet for fiction and Mentor for non-fictionadopting the latter's slogan to underscore aspirational readingthey sought to elevate public discourse while capitalizing on the era's demand for portable, budget-friendly books.
NAL's debut catalog exemplified this vision, featuring titles such as D.H. Lawrence's Lady Chatterley's Lover, William Faulkner's Sanctuary, and John Steinbeck's Tortilla Flat, which challenged prior censorship norms and appealed to a maturing readership eager for provocative and mainstream works alike. The company's structure emphasized editorial independence and aggressive marketing, positioning it as a leader in the burgeoning mass-market paperback industry that would soon transform American literary consumption.

Initial Business Model and First Publications

The New American Library (NAL) adopted a business model centered on licensing reprint rights from established hardcover publishers to produce mass-market paperbacks priced at 25 to 35 cents, enabling wide distribution through non-traditional outlets such as newsstands, drugstores, and supermarkets rather than solely bookstores. This approach, pioneered in the post-World War II paperback boom, emphasized high-volume sales of quality reprintsincluding classics, scholarly works, popular fiction, and pulp titlesto democratize access to literature for working-class and middlebrow audiences previously limited by hardcover costs. Founders Kurt Enoch, who handled production, sales, and distribution, and Victor Weybright, who oversaw editorial selections, positioned NAL as an independent American entity after acquiring and separating from the U.S. operations of Penguin Books, relinquishing the Penguin imprint to focus on original branding like Signet for fiction and, later, Mentor for non-fiction. The model prioritized editorial curation of "world literature" alongside commercial viability, avoiding the lowest-common-denominator pulp of competitors while scaling through efficient printing and aggressive marketing.
NAL's first publications launched under the Signet imprint in the summer of 1948, featuring reprints of contemporary fiction and non-fiction titles adapted for paperback format. The company released 40 titles in its inaugural year, expanding to 60 the following year, with early Signet volumes including popular works like Claude Anet's Ariane (Signet #676, 1948 first printing). These initial offerings broke from Penguin's restrained design aesthetic, adopting bolder covers to appeal to mass audiences while maintaining textual fidelity to originals. The Mentor series, introduced shortly thereafter for scholarly and philosophical reprints, complemented Signet by targeting educated readers with affordable editions of enduring non-fiction, such as early volumes on American history and antiques, though specific debut titles like Frank Luther Mott's A Gallery of Americans (M61) emerged in the late 1940s. This dual-imprint strategy underscored NAL's commitment to balancing entertainment with intellectual content from the outset.

20th-Century Expansion

Growth of Mass-Market Paperbacks

The New American Library (NAL) rapidly expanded the mass-market paperback sector following its establishment in 1948, when founders Kurt Enoch and Victor Weybright acquired the U.S. assets of Penguin Books and rebranded operations to emphasize affordable editions sold through non-traditional outlets such as newsstands, pharmacies, and variety stores. Launching with 40 titles priced at 25 cents each, NAL introduced the Signet imprint for fiction and the Mentor imprint for nonfiction, diverging from pulp-heavy competitors by prioritizing reprints of mainstream literature, classics, and scholarly works to broaden readership beyond elite audiences. This strategy capitalized on postwar demand for portable, inexpensive books, building on the paperback boom initiated by Pocket Books in 1939 but extending it to more diverse genres and demographics.
By 1949, NAL increased its output to 60 titles under Signet, achieving early commercial success with reprints like William Faulkner's novels, which sold 800,000 copies by year's end, demonstrating the viability of mass-distributing literary fiction to generate high-volume sales unattainable in hardcover formats. In 1950, annual sales reached 30 million units, positioning NAL as the third-largest mass-market publisher behind Pocket Books (50 million) and Bantam (38 million), with growth driven by aggressive distribution networks and marketing that targeted impulse buys in high-traffic retail environments. The Mentor's focus on nonfiction further fueled expansion by reprinting works in philosophy, history, and sciencesuch as editions of Aristotle and Bertrand Russellmaking intellectual content accessible to working-class readers and contributing to a cultural shift where paperbacks outsold hardcovers in volume by the mid-1950s.
NAL's model emphasized reprints over originals initially, licensing rights from hardcover publishers to minimize risk while maximizing print runs, which often exceeded 100,000 copies per title for bestsellers; this approach sustained double-digit annual growth through the decade, as evidenced by the publisher's pivot to science fiction originals by 1958 and broader genre diversification. Until 1963, NAL adhered strictly to mass-market formats, avoiding higher-priced trade paperbacks or hardcovers to maintain focus on scalability and low barriers to entry, which helped solidify paperbacks' dominance in U.S. book consumption by the late 1950s. This era's innovations in cover design, binding durability, and rack positioning at retail points enhanced visibility and turnover, with NAL's imprints accounting for a significant share of the industry's shift from niche to ubiquitous availability.

Key Imprints and Series Launches

The New American Library (NAL) initiated its core paperback operations in 1948 by launching the Signet imprint for fiction and popular titles, debuting in the summer of that year with an initial output of 40 titles, expanding to 60 the following year. Simultaneously, the Mentor imprint was established for scholarly nonfiction and educational works, rebranding prior Pelican series efforts under NAL's new structure and adopting the slogan "Good reading for the millions" to emphasize accessible quality literature. These imprints capitalized on the post-World War II demand for inexpensive books, drawing from NAL's roots in adapting Penguin's model for the American market.
Signet Classics emerged as a specialized series within the Signet line in 1959, providing mass-market editions of canonical literature such as Homer's Iliad and Herman Melville's Moby-Dick, which complemented Mentor's classical nonfiction focus while broadening NAL's appeal to students and general readers. Additional niche series followed, including Mentor-Omega in the early 1950s for works by Catholic philosophers and theologians, and Signet Key around the same period for young adult readers aged 10 to 14. The Meridian imprint, initially developed by Noonday Press, was integrated into NAL by 1972 for trade paperbacks emphasizing literary and intellectual titles.
By 1965, Signet and Mentor combined annual sales surpassed 50 million volumes, underscoring the imprints' role in democratizing reading through affordable pricingtypically 25 to 50 cents per copyand strategic distribution via newsstands, drugstores, and bookstores. These launches solidified NAL's position in the burgeoning mass-market paperback industry, prioritizing volume over high margins while curating content from bestsellers to enduring classics.

Landmark Publications and Legal Challenges

The New American Library (NAL) played a pivotal role in disseminating controversial literature through its Signet imprint, often facing informal censorship pressures amid post-World War II debates over obscenity standards. A key publication was the 1959 Signet paperback edition of D.H. Lawrence's Lady Chatterley's Lover, which offered an unexpurgated version after U.S. courts, including in Grove Press, Inc. v. Gerstein (1959), had affirmed its non-obscene status under evolving First Amendment interpretations, enabling mass-market access to the novel's explicit depictions of adultery and class dynamics. This edition sold widely, amplifying public discourse on literary freedom but drawing localized distributor hesitancy due to lingering moral concerns.
Another landmark was the Signet paperback of Grace Metalious's Peyton Place (original hardcover 1956), which exposed hypocrisy, incest, and extramarital affairs in a New England town, achieving over 20 million sales by the early 1960s and provoking widespread outrage from religious and civic groups who decried its "salacious" content as corrosive to social norms, though it evaded formal federal prosecution. NAL's editions of Mickey Spillane's hard-boiled detective novels, such as I, the Jury (1947, paperback reprints), further tested boundaries with graphic violence and sexual undertones, fueling congressional scrutiny of mass-market paperbacks as vehicles for "degenerate" material.
NAL confronted legal threats directly in New American Library of World Literature, Inc. v. Allen, 114 F. Supp. 823 (S.D.N.Y. 1953), where it secured an injunction against Ohio officials' informal notices warning distributors against stocking its titles suspected of obscenity, arguing such tactics constituted unconstitutional prior restraint without judicial review. This victory, referenced in subsequent Supreme Court precedents like Bantam Books, Inc. v. Sullivan, 372 U.S. 58 (1963), highlighted NAL's advocacy against extralegal suppression mechanisms employed by commissions and prosecutors to deter paperback sales of borderline works. Company president Victor Weybright's testimony in federal hearings underscored paperbacks' educational value, countering claims of inherent vulgarity and affirming NAL's commitment to affordable access over sanitized content. These efforts bolstered the industry's resilience amid the 1957 Roth v. United States ruling, which refined obscenity criteria but initially heightened risks for publishers like NAL distributing provocative fiction.

Ownership Changes and Corporate Evolution

Acquisitions and Mergers in the 1980s1990s

In November 1983, the Times Mirror Company sold New American Library to Ira J. Hechler, a publishing executive, and Odyssey Partners, a private investment group, for more than $50 million in cash. At the time of the transaction, NAL maintained over 1 billion paperback books in print across its imprints.
Under the new ownership, NAL expanded into hardcover publishing by acquiring E. P. Dutton, a trade publisher established in 1832, in 1985. This purchase strengthened NAL's position in both mass-market paperbacks and higher-end formats amid industry pressures on paperback houses to diversify.
On September 30, 1986, Penguin Publishing Company, a subsidiary of Pearson PLC, agreed in principle to acquire NAL, including the recently added Dutton operations, marking Penguin's entry into the U.S. mass-market segment. The deal was publicly announced the following day, positioning Penguin to leverage NAL's established distribution and backlist. By 1987, the acquisition was completed, reintegrating NAL under Penguin's umbrella after its origins as an affiliate of Penguin Books in the 1940s. No major additional mergers or acquisitions involving NAL occurred through the 1990s, as it operated as an imprint within the expanding Penguin Group.

Integration into Larger Conglomerates

In 1987, New American Library (NAL) was reintegrated into Penguin Publishing Company, its original parent from which it had branched off as an autonomous entity in 1948. This followed Penguin's 1986 agreement to acquire NAL, which included its recent purchase of E.P. Dutton, for an undisclosed sum that positioned Penguin as a major player in the U.S. mass-market paperback sector. The move marked a full-circle return, with NAL's established imprints like Signet and Mentor continuing under Penguin's oversight while benefiting from shared resources in editing, distribution, and marketing.
As part of Penguin Group USA, NAL operated within a structure that emphasized operational synergies, such as coordinated sales forces and expanded backlist management, which bolstered Penguin's annual U.S. revenue from approximately $50 million in 1985 to greater scale post-acquisition. NAL's leadership, including president Victor Weybright's successors, reported into Penguin's executive hierarchy, though the division retained focus on affordable editions to leverage its pre-acquisition print run exceeding 1 billion copies. This integration exemplified broader 1980s trends in publishing consolidation, where independent houses like NAL were absorbed to counter market fragmentation and rising costs.
Penguin itself formed a key pillar of Pearson PLC, a multinational conglomerate with interests in education, media, and publishing, enabling NAL access to global supply chains and international rights management. The arrangement prioritized efficiency over full autonomy, as evidenced by leadership transitions like the 1989 departure of NAL president Richard D. Hofheimer amid Penguin's strategic realignments. By embedding NAL into this corporate framework, Penguin enhanced its competitive edge against rivals like Simon & Schuster, though it also exposed the imprint to conglomerate-level decisions on cost controls and portfolio rationalization.

21st-Century Shifts Under Penguin Random House

Following the July 1, 2013, merger of Penguin Books and Random Houseboth owned respectively by Pearson PLC and Bertelsmann AGto form Penguin Random House, the New American Library (NAL) imprint underwent structural integration within the enlarged entity's Penguin Publishing Group division. This consolidation positioned NAL alongside other mass-market paperback operations, enhancing resource sharing for distribution, marketing, and digital adaptation amid rising e-book adoption, though NAL retained its focus on affordable editions of commercial titles.
In January 2015, Penguin Random House announced a reorganization of its adult divisions, including leadership transitions such as the appointment of a new president for the Penguin Publishing Group, which oversaw NAL and aimed to streamline operations post-merger. This was followed on June 24, 2015, by the formal combination of all Berkley and NAL imprints into a unified Berkley Publishing Group, effective July 1, under president Leslie Gelbman, to consolidate commercial fiction and nonfiction mass-market publishing. The restructuring emphasized efficiency in a competitive landscape dominated by digital formats and chain retailers, with NAL's historical Signet and other series continuing under the broader group while adapting to print-on-demand and audiobook expansions.
These changes reflected broader industry pressures, including the 2010s surge in self-publishing and e-books, prompting Penguin Random House to prioritize scalable mass-market strategies; by 2020, NAL's output contributed to the group's annual publication of over 15,000 print titles, maintaining its role in accessible genre fiction and reprints despite corporate consolidation. No major divestitures or closures affected NAL under PRH, unlike some imprints in prior decades, underscoring its enduring viability within the conglomerate's U.S. mass-market segment.

Publishing Operations and Imprints

Core Imprints: Signet, Mentor, and Others

The Signet imprint, launched in August 1948 as the primary fiction line of the New American Library, focused on affordable mass-market paperbacks featuring popular novels, mysteries, and contemporary works by authors such as Erskine Caldwell, William Faulkner, Ian Fleming, and James Jones. These editions emphasized accessibility, with early volumes priced at 25 cents and distributed through newsstands and drugstores, contributing to the democratization of reading in post-World War II America. By the mid-1950s, under art director Bill Gregory, Signet covers adopted a modern design aesthetic, moving away from earlier illustrative styles to bolder typography and imagery that appealed to a broad audience.
The Mentor imprint, established alongside Signet in 1948, specialized in nonfiction titles, including scholarly works, philosophical texts, historical analyses, and educational content aimed at self-improvement and intellectual engagement. Unlike Signet's entertainment-oriented fiction, Mentor books targeted readers seeking substantive knowledge, with series like Mentor-Omega for specialized topics and Mentor Executive Library for business and leadership materials. Iconic covers from the era, often featuring abstract or symbolic designs, reinforced Mentor's reputation for rigorous, nonfiction-focused publishing, as seen in vintage editions that prioritized content depth over mass appeal.
Other notable imprints under New American Library included Signet Classics, which reprinted literary masterpieces with updated introductions and annotations to make canonical works accessible in paperback form, and Signet Key, oriented toward select genre fiction or experimental titles. Signet Science addressed popular science and technical subjects, broadening the publisher's scope beyond literature. These lines collectively supported NAL's mission of "good reading for the millions," with combined annual sales exceeding tens of millions of copies by the late 1950s through diversified offerings that balanced commercial viability with cultural value.

Evolution to Nonfiction Focus in 2015

In June 2015, Penguin Random House announced a restructuring within its Penguin Publishing Group, integrating New American Library (NAL) as an imprint of the newly formed Berkley Publishing Group, effective July 1, 2015. The changes unified editorial, production, art, marketing, publicity, and advertising teams across NAL and Berkley to eliminate redundancies and improve operational efficiency, resulting in the elimination of some positions in editorial, managing editorial, art, copy, and production departments.
Under the new structure, NAL narrowed its scope beginning with the fall 2016 list, focusing exclusively on mass-market nonfiction paperbacks. Hardcover and trade paperback nonfiction titles previously published by NAL transferred to the Berkley imprint, while hardcover and trade fiction moved to Berkley Books. Mass-market fiction continued under the Signet imprint, which remained associated with the group. This specialization departed from NAL's prior broad emphasis on both fiction and nonfiction mass-market titles, positioning it to concentrate on accessible, lower-cost nonfiction editions amid consolidating industry dynamics.
Leadership transitions supported the shift, with Kara Welsh elevated to senior vice president and publisher of Berkley/NAL, and editorial directors Claire Zion, Tom Colgan, and Cindy Hwang promoted to vice presidents. Leslie Gelbman continued as president of the Berkley Publishing Group, overseeing the integrated operations. The reorganization aligned with Penguin Random House's post-merger strategies to refine imprint roles for targeted market segments, enhancing competitiveness in nonfiction paperback distribution.

Nonfiction and Intellectual Contributors

Economist Eric Hoffer emerged as a key contributor through The True Believer: Thoughts on the Nature of Mass Movements (original 1951; Mentor edition 1958, MD228), a concise examination of fanaticism and ideological fervor drawing from Hoffer's observations as a longshoreman-philosopher. The paperback's wide availability amplified Hoffer's critique of how frustration fuels mass adherence to doctrines, influencing discussions on totalitarianism and social psychology. Similarly, John Kenneth Galbraith's The New Industrial State (original 1967; Mentor edition 1972) dissected corporate planning and technocratic power in advanced economies, challenging orthodox economics with data on managerial hierarchies and government-industry symbiosis. Galbraith's analysis, grounded in empirical observations of U.S. industrial structures, sold extensively via NAL, underscoring the publisher's role in popularizing contrarian economic thought.
Philosophers and social theorists featured prominently, including John Dewey's Reconstruction in Philosophy (Mentor M53), which advocated pragmatic overhaul of metaphysics amid scientific advances, and Alfred North Whitehead's Science and the Modern World (Mentor M28), tracing the interplay of abstraction and empiricism in modern thought. In psychology, Sigmund Freud's The Psychopathology of Everyday Life (Mentor M67, translated by A.A. Historians and scientists rounded out the intellectual roster, with George F. These publications, often reprints of seminal originals, evidenced NAL's commitment to causal explanations over narrative simplification, fostering literacy in complex domains.

Criticisms, Controversies, and Challenges

Debates Over Content Censorship and Trials

In the early 1950s, the New American Library (NAL) encountered significant resistance from local law enforcement attempting to suppress distribution of its paperback reprints through informal threats rather than judicial proceedings. A prominent instance occurred in Youngstown, Ohio, where Police Chief Edward J. Allen in 1953 compiled a list of 108 titles deemed obscene under a local ordinance prohibiting immoral publications, including 11 books published by NAL. Allen warned local distributor Bernard Bloch that continued sale of these titles would result in arrests, prompting NAL to seek a federal injunction against such actions as unconstitutional prior restraint.
The U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Ohio ruled in NAL's favor in New American Library of World Literature, Inc. v. Allen (114 F. Supp. 823, 1953), upholding the ordinance's constitutionality but finding Allen's threats exceeded his authority by bypassing judicial review, thereby depriving NAL of property rights and due process under the First and Fourteenth Amendments. The court issued a permanent injunction barring suppression without a court determination of obscenity, emphasizing that only judicial bodies could legitimately assess literary merit against obscenity standards. This decision underscored debates over administrative overreach in censorship, with NAL arguing its selectionsreputable reprints aimed at broad accessibilitywere not inherently prurient but targeted by moralistic local officials wary of paperbacks' mass-market appeal.
The case influenced subsequent jurisprudence, including the U.S. Supreme Court's Bantam Books, Inc. v. Sullivan (372 U.S. 58, 1963), which cited Allen in striking down Rhode Island's commission-based informal censorship as violative of free expression protections. Critics of NAL's model contended that affordable editions democratized access to borderline content, potentially eroding community standards, while publishers like NAL maintained that such challenges reflected outdated Comstock-era attitudes clashing with post-World War II cultural shifts toward literary freedom. These trials highlighted broader tensions in the paperback revolution, where economic incentives for sensational titles met resistance from authorities seeking to enforce varying local mores without procedural safeguards.

Concerns Regarding Corporate Consolidation

In 1960, the New American Library (NAL) was acquired by the Times Mirror Company, a diversified media conglomerate, which subsequently hired McKinsey & Company consultants to rationalize its operations, signaling a shift toward corporate efficiency metrics in publishing decisions. This acquisition exemplified the 1960s trend of non-publishing conglomerates entering the industry, often imposing financial oversight that prioritized profitability over editorial experimentation.
Times Mirror divested NAL in 1983 for over $50 million to a group of private investors led by Harry E. Hechler, amid broader corporate restructuring. Three years later, in 1986, Penguin Booksa British firm then owned by Pearsonacquired NAL, integrating it into its U.S. mass-market operations and accelerating foreign ownership of American publishers. Penguin's parent, Bertelsmann, later merged with Random House in 2013 to form Penguin Random House (PRH), positioning NAL within one of the industry's "Big Five" conglomerates controlling approximately 80% of the U.S. trade market by revenue.
Critics of such consolidations argue that repeated ownership changes erode editorial independence, as conglomerate prioritiessuch as cost synergies and blockbuster-focused listscan marginalize niche or riskier titles originally central to NAL's affordable classics mission. For instance, the 2015 internal merger of NAL with the Berkley imprint under PRH eliminated an unspecified number of positions and restructured imprints, with NAL shifting primarily to non-fiction while Berkley handled fiction, potentially streamlining but also homogenizing output. Empirical evidence from U.S. Department of Justice analyses of similar deals indicates that heightened concentration reduces author advances by limiting bargaining power and diminishes overall title diversity, as fewer outlets compete for midlist works.
Proponents of mergers, including publishers, claim efficiencies enable larger advances for top authors and broader distribution, yet antitrust scrutinysuch as the 2022 blockage of PRH's Simon & Schuster bidhighlights causal links between consolidation and anticompetitive effects, including stalled innovation in mass-market segments like NAL's historical domain. These dynamics have contributed to industry-wide job reductions and a pivot toward data-driven, low-risk acquisitions, raising questions about sustained access to affordable, diverse literature amid corporate scale.

Evaluations of Editorial Independence Post-Mergers

Following the 1960 acquisition by the Times Mirror Company, New American Library (NAL) implemented a McKinsey & Company-led reorganization that emphasized corporate efficiency, altering editorial processes to align with profitability metrics and diminishing prior autonomy in content selection and risk-taking. This shift exemplified early conglomeratization in publishing, where independent editorial visions yielded to financial oversight, as evidenced by internal managerial disruptions and leadership turnover in the subsequent years.
Analyses of the era, drawing from founder Victor Weybright's accounts and corporate records, portray the Times Mirror merger as a prototype for corporate incursions eroding publishing integrity, though attributions of blame vary; Weybright emphasized external interference, while subsequent scholarship highlights intertwined internal dysfunctions alongside parental company pressures on decision-making. By the mid-1960s, these dynamics contributed to a half-decade of operational instability, with key executives like Weybright departing amid debates over whether autonomy losses stemmed primarily from Times Mirror's conservative media conglomerate structure or pre-existing strategic misalignments.
The 1986 sale to Penguin Books, valued at an undisclosed sum and completing Times Mirror's divestiture after 26 years of ownership, repositioned NAL within a publisher-centric entity originally linked to its 1948 founding via Penguin's U.S. assets. This reintegration ostensibly restored some operational alignment with literary mass-market goals, enabling expanded Signet and Mentor imprints without immediate documented upheavals in editorial policy.
However, NAL's embedding in Penguin's evolving conglomerateculminating in the 2013 Penguin Random House merger and the 2015 consolidation of NAL with Berkley into a unified group under president Leslie Gelbmaninvited parallel industry critiques of attenuated independence. Observers contend such structures foster homogenized lists favoring commercial predictability over diverse or experimental outputs, as conglomerate scale incentivizes advances for anticipated best-sellers while sidelining niche editorial judgments, a pattern traceable to NAL's earlier corporate phases but amplified post-1986. Specific to NAL imprints, this manifested in streamlined operations prioritizing market data-driven acquisitions, though no singular post-Penguin scandal or policy reversal has been singled out as decisively curtailing its pre-merger ethos.
