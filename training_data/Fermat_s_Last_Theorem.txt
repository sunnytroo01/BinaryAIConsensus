 Fermat's Last Theorem states that there are no positive integers aaa, bbb, and ccc such that an+bn=cna^n + b^n = c^nan+bn=cn for any integer n>2n > 2n>2. Proposed by the French mathematician Pierre de Fermat in the 1630s, the claim appeared as a marginal note in his copy of Diophantus's Arithmetica, where he wrote that he had discovered a truly marvelous proof but that the margin was too narrow to contain it. The note was published posthumously in 1670 by Fermat's son Samuel, sparking centuries of mathematical inquiry.
For over 350 years, Fermat's Last Theorem stood as one of the most famous unsolved problems in mathematics, challenging some of the field's greatest minds. Early progress included proofs for specific cases: Fermat himself proved it for n=4n=4n=4 in 1634, Leonhard Euler established it for n=3n=3n=3 in 1770 (despite initial flaws), and by the 19th century, mathematicians like Sophie Germain, Adrien-Marie Legendre, and Gabriel Lam had verified it for exponents up to 7 and beyond for certain classes of primes. Ernst Kummer advanced the field significantly in the 1840s by proving the theorem for all "regular" primes using ideal numbers, though irregular primes like 37, 59, and 67 remained elusive. In the 20th century, computational efforts verified it for all exponents up to 1,000,000, but a general proof seemed out of reach, leading many to doubt Fermat's claimed simple proof ever existed.
The complete proof was finally achieved by British mathematician Andrew Wiles in 1994, after seven years of secretive work at Princeton University. Wiles announced his initial result on June 23, 1993, during a series of lectures at the Isaac Newton Institute in Cambridge, but a flaw was discovered in 1993, prompting collaboration with Richard Taylor to repair it using the 3-5 switch method. The corrected proof, published in 1995 in the Annals of Mathematics, relies on advanced concepts from algebraic geometry, including the modularity theorem for semistable elliptic curves (formerly the Taniyama-Shimura-Weil conjecture), linking elliptic curves to modular forms. Spanning over 100 pages across two papers"Modular elliptic curves and Fermat's Last Theorem" by Wiles and "Ring-theoretic properties of certain Hecke algebras" by Wiles and Taylorthe proof revolutionized number theory and earned Wiles the Abel Prize in 2016.

Statement and Origins

Theorem Statement

Fermat's Last Theorem asserts that there are no nonzero integers aaa, bbb, and ccc such that an+bn=cna^n + b^n = c^nan+bn=cn for any integer n>2n > 2n>2. This result concerns the Diophantine equation
an+bn=cn,
a^n + b^n = c^n,
an+bn=cn,
where solutions are sought among integers aaa, bbb, and ccc, typically taken to be positive without loss of generality due to the even or odd nature of exponents. Diophantine equations are polynomial equations defined over the integers, with the goal of determining whether integer solutions exist.
For n=1n = 1n=1, the equation a+b=ca + b = ca+b=c admits infinitely many trivial integer solutions, such as a=1a = 1a=1, b=1b = 1b=1, c=2c = 2c=2. When n=2n = 2n=2, infinitely many solutions exist in the form of Pythagorean triples, primitive or otherwise, satisfying a2+b2=c2a^2 + b^2 = c^2a2+b2=c2. The theorem specifies that no such nontrivial solutions arise for exponents greater than 2, marking a fundamental boundary in the study of these equations.

Pythagorean Roots

The Pythagorean theorem states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides, expressed as a2+b2=c2a^2 + b^2 = c^2a2+b2=c2, where ccc is the hypotenuse and aaa and bbb are the legs. This relation forms the geometric foundation for understanding integer solutions to the equation, known as Pythagorean triples, which are positive integers aaa, bbb, and ccc satisfying a2+b2=c2a^2 + b^2 = c^2a2+b2=c2.
Evidence of the theorem's recognition dates to ancient Mesopotamia, where Babylonian mathematicians applied it around 18001600 BCE, as demonstrated by clay tablets such as Plimpton 322, which lists 15 rows of Pythagorean triples derived from ratios approximating angles between 30 and 45. These triples, including scaled versions like (45, 60, 75), indicate practical use in surveying and construction, though without a formal proof. A primitive Pythagorean triple, where gcd(a,b,c)=1\gcd(a, b, c) = 1gcd(a,b,c)=1, exemplifies the simplest form, such as (3, 4, 5), where 32+42=9+16=25=523^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5^232+42=9+16=25=52.
Euclid provided the first known rigorous proof of the theorem in his Elements (circa 300 BCE), Book I, Proposition 47, using geometric constructions to show that the area of the square on the hypotenuse equals the sum of the areas of the squares on the legs through congruent triangles and parallelograms. He also described a method to generate all primitive Pythagorean triples in Book X, Proposition 29 (with a preceding lemma), using parameters m>n>0m > n > 0m>n>0 where mmm and nnn are coprime integers not both odd: a=m2n2a = m^2 - n^2a=m2n2, b=2mnb = 2mnb=2mn, c=m2+n2c = m^2 + n^2c=m2+n2. For instance, with m=2m=2m=2 and n=1n=1n=1, this yields the triple (3, 4, 5). All non-primitive triples are integer multiples of these primitives.
This solvable case for exponent 2 provided early insights into Diophantine equations, contrasting with the absence of nonzero integer solutions for higher exponents in Fermat's generalization.

Fermat's Marginal Note

In 1637, Pierre de Fermat, a French lawyer and amateur mathematician, inscribed a notable annotation in the margin of his personal copy of Claude Gaspard Bachet de Mziriac's 1621 Latin edition of Diophantus's Arithmetica. The text, written in Latin, stated: "Cubum autem in duos cubos, aut quadrato-quadratum in duos quadrato-quadratos, et generaliter nullam in infinitum ultra quadratum potestatem in duos eiusdem nominis fas est dividere; cuius rei demonstrationem mirabilem sane detexi. Hanc marginis exiguitas non caperet." This translates to: "It is impossible to separate a cube into two cubes, or a fourth power into two fourth powers, or in general any power higher than the second into two like powers. I have discovered a truly marvellous proof of this, which however the margin is too narrow to contain." Fermat's assertion generalized the known existence of Pythagorean triples for n=2n=2n=2 to claim no positive integer solutions exist for the equation xn+yn=znx^n + y^n = z^nxn+yn=zn when n>2n > 2n>2.
Fermat's interest in this conjecture arose from his broader investigations into Diophantine equations, which seek integer solutions to polynomial equations, a field inspired by Diophantus's work. He actively corresponded with Marin Mersenne, a Minim friar and coordinator of a European mathematical network, discussing topics in number theory including properties of primes and solutions to indeterminate equations during the 1630s and 1640s. This exchange helped disseminate Fermat's ideas, though he rarely provided full proofs in his letters, preferring to challenge others with problems. Prior to the marginal note, Fermat had independently proved the case n=4n=4n=4 using his method of infinite descent, demonstrating no solutions exist for x4+y4=z4x^4 + y^4 = z^4x4+y4=z4. However, he offered no details of the purported general proof in the annotation or elsewhere.
The original annotated copy of Arithmetica has not survived, but Fermat's son, Samuel de Fermat, included the marginal notealong with other annotations from his father's papersin the 1670 edition of the text that he edited and published. This posthumous publication, five years after Fermat's death in 1665, introduced the conjecture to the wider mathematical community without the promised proof, sparking centuries of efforts to verify or disprove it.

Historical Attempts

Early Proofs for Specific Exponents

Following Fermat's claim in the 17th century, mathematicians began verifying the theorem for small exponents using techniques like infinite descent, which assumes a positive integer solution exists and derives a smaller positive integer solution, leading to an infinite regress of decreasing solutions that contradicts the well-ordering of the natural numbers.
Fermat himself provided a proof for the case n=4n=4n=4 around 1637, demonstrating that there are no positive integers aaa, bbb, and ccc satisfying a4+b4=c4a^4 + b^4 = c^4a4+b4=c4. His argument employed infinite descent by first reducing the problem to showing no primitive Pythagorean triples satisfy certain conditions and then assuming a solution to derive a smaller one, ultimately yielding a contradiction. This proof, preserved in Fermat's correspondence, remains one of the few direct contributions from him to the theorem.
In the 18th century, Leonhard Euler established the theorem for n=3n=3n=3 in 1770, proving that a3+b3=c3a^3 + b^3 = c^3a3+b3=c3 has no positive integer solutions. Euler's approach involved factorization in the ring Z\mathbb{Z}Z, where he assumed a primitive solution and factored the equation to show it implied a non-unique factorization, contradicting the ring's properties after establishing unique factorization up to units. Although his proof contained a minor gap regarding the ring's integrality, it was later corrected and stands as a foundational use of algebraic number theory for the theorem.
The case n=5n=5n=5 was resolved in 1825 through independent but complementary efforts by Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet and Adrien-Marie Legendre. Legendre proved that if a solution exists, the prime factors of aaa and bbb must satisfy specific divisibility conditions, while Dirichlet handled the scenario where 5 divides one of the bases, together ruling out all possibilities via descent and properties of quadratic forms.
Gabriel Lam completed the proof for n=7n=7n=7 in 1839, extending techniques from lower exponents by assuming a minimal solution and using infinite descent in conjunction with factorization in cyclotomic fields to derive a contradiction. His work for this exponent relied on detailed analysis of the equation's implications modulo 7 and descent steps that reduced the solution size indefinitely. These early verifications for n=3,4,5,7n=3,4,5,7n=3,4,5,7 lent credibility to the conjecture and spurred further investigations into higher exponents.

19th-Century Advances

In 1816, Sophie Germain developed a criterion that advanced the understanding of Fermat's Last Theorem for prime exponents. Her theorem states that for an odd prime ppp, if there exists an auxiliary prime \theta such that no two nonzero consecutive ppp-th powers are congruent modulo \theta, and ppp is not itself a ppp-th power residue modulo \theta, then any solution to xp+yp=zpx^p + y^p = z^pxp+yp=zp in positive integers must have p2p^2p2 dividing at least one of xxx, yyy, or zzz. This establishes the first case of the theorem (where ppp divides none of x,y,zx, y, zx,y,z) when 2p+12p + 12p+1 is also prime, known today as a Sophie Germain prime. Her work provided the first general method applicable to infinitely many exponents, though it required verifying the auxiliary prime condition.
A significant setback occurred in 1847 when Gabriel Lam announced what he believed was a general proof of the theorem. Lam's approach involved factoring xp+yp=zpx^p + y^p = z^pxp+yp=zp in the cyclotomic field Q(p)\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_p)Q(p), where p\zeta_pp is a primitive ppp-th root of unity, using the binomial theorem to express the factors as linear terms. He assumed unique factorization into prime elements in this ring, leading to a contradiction via infinite descent. However, this assumption failed because Q(p)\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_p)Q(p) is not a unique factorization domain for most odd primes ppp, as Ernst Kummer had already demonstrated in prior work. The Paris Academy rejected Lam's proof shortly after its presentation, highlighting the need for new algebraic tools to handle factorization in number fields.
Kummer responded to this flaw by introducing the concept of ideal numbers in cyclotomic fields during the 1840s, laying the foundation for modern ideal theory. He defined a prime ppp as regular if ppp does not divide the class number of Q(p)\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_p)Q(p), ensuring that certain ideals remain principal and allowing unique factorization to hold in a generalized sense. Using this framework, Kummer proved Fermat's Last Theorem for all regular odd primes p3p \geq 3p3 in 1847, showing that no nontrivial solutions exist to xp+yp=zpx^p + y^p = z^pxp+yp=zp. By 1850, he had verified that all primes less than 100 except 37, 59, and 67 are regular, covering the theorem for 22 of the 25 primes below 100. His 1857 work attempted to extend the proofs to these irregular cases using refined criteria involving Bernoulli numbers, but it contained gaps that were later filled by mathematicians such as H. S. Vandiver in 1926.
By the late 19th century, these prime exponent results enabled proofs for all composite exponents up to 100 through reduction techniques for the regular prime cases. Since the theorem holds for n=4n=4n=4 (proved by Fermat via infinite descent) and for all regular prime factors of a composite nnn, solutions for such composite nnn can be reduced to the regular prime cases under coprimality assumptions, yielding no nontrivial solutions. Legendre had earlier extended Germain's methods to confirm the first case for all primes below 100 by 1823, and Kummer's work solidified the full proofs for the regular primes. However, complete coverage for all exponents up to 100, including those reducible to the irregular primes, was achieved in the early 20th century after the irregular cases were resolved.

20th-Century Computational Efforts

In the 20th century, mathematicians increasingly turned to computers to verify Fermat's Last Theorem for large ranges of exponents n, employing modular arithmetic, sieving techniques, and computations of cyclotomic polynomials to search for potential counterexamples without finding any. These efforts built on 19th-century manual proofs for small n by extending the scope through automated checks, confirming the absence of solutions up to progressively higher bounds. The methods focused on the first case of the theorem (where a, b, and c share no common prime factor) and used Kummer's criteria for irregular primes, where violations could indicate counterexamples.
Early computational work began in 1954 when H. S. Vandiver, D. H. Lehmer, and Emma Lehmer utilized the SWAC computer at the National Bureau of Standards to verify the theorem for all prime exponents less than 4002, marking the first major use of high-speed computing for this purpose. In the 1960s, J. L. Brenner applied similar computing techniques to extend verifications for exponents below 1000, focusing on specific modular constraints to rule out solutions. By 1971, Bryant Tuckerman advanced these efforts, confirming the theorem for all exponents less than 5000 through exhaustive computer searches that incorporated efficient primality testing and factorization algorithms. (Note: This is from AMS notices abstract on Tuckerman's work.)
Samuel S. Wagstaff, Jr., led significant extensions in the 1970s and 1980s, computing the irregular primes up to 125,000 and verifying the theorem for all such exponents by 1978; his approach involved calculating Bernoulli numbers and class numbers in cyclotomic fields to ensure no irregular primes allowed solutions. For composite exponents, computers played a crucial role by reducing the problem to prime power cases via unique factorization in integers, allowing verification by checking the prime factors of n and applying modular reductions to eliminate possible a, b, c triples efficiently.
By 1993, collaborative computational projects had pushed the bound to all exponents up to 4 million, using optimized sieving on supercomputers to confirm no counterexamples existed, providing strong empirical support for the theorem just prior to Andrew Wiles' general proof. These verifications demonstrated the power of computing in number theory, ruling out low-exponent counterexamples and motivating deeper theoretical investigations.

Path to the General Proof

Sophie Germain's Contributions

Sophie Germain, a pioneering French mathematician working in the early 19th century, advanced the study of Fermat's Last Theorem by developing a systematic approach to prove it for prime exponents, despite facing significant barriers as a woman in academia. Lacking formal education and access to institutions, she corresponded anonymously at first under the pseudonym "Monsieur Le Blanc" to engage with leading mathematicians. Her efforts culminated in key insights published in 1823, building on earlier unpublished manuscripts from around 1816.
In 1816, Germain formulated a theorem linking solutions to Fermat's equation for odd prime exponent $ p $ to the arithmetic of the cyclotomic field $ \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_p) $, where $ \zeta_p $ is a primitive $ p $-th root of unity: if there is no integer solution to $ x^p + y^p = z^p $ with $ xyz $ not divisible by $ p $, then $ p $ does not divide the class number of $ \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_p) $. This criterion provided a number-theoretic condition under which the first case of the theorem (no solutions with $ p $ not dividing $ xyz $) could be established, anticipating later algebraic techniques. She further proved this case for specific small primes using auxiliary primes $ \theta = 2Np + 1 $ satisfying a non-consecutive residues condition (no two nonzero $ p $-th power residues differ by 1 modulo $ \theta $, and $ p $ is not a $ p $-th power residue modulo $ \theta $): for $ p = 3 $ with $ \theta = 7, 13 $; $ p = 5 $ with $ \theta = 11, 41, 71, 101 $; $ p = 7 $ and $ p = 11 $ with similar auxiliary primes up to $ N = 10 $. These proofs demonstrated that any potential solutions would involve extraordinarily large numbers, exceeding 39 digits for $ p = 5 $.
Germain's correspondence was instrumental in refining her ideas and overcoming isolation. In 1804 and 1819, she wrote to Carl Friedrich Gauss, sharing her grand plan for a general proof and seeking feedback, though Gauss's responses were limited due to external circumstances like the Napoleonic Wars. Adrien-Marie Legendre served as a mentor, incorporating her results in a 1823 footnote to his Essai sur la thorie des nombres and collaborating to extend proofs to all odd primes $ p < 100 $. These exchanges not only validated her work but also highlighted her resilience against gender-based exclusion, as she was denied prizes and lectureships despite her brilliance.
Despite these advances, Germain's approach had inherent limitations, applying only to primes where suitable auxiliary primes exist, a finite set for each $ p $. Her general plan faltered because the non-consecutive residues condition holds for only finitely many such $ \theta $, and some proofs contained flaws in assuming relative primality or infinitude of auxiliaries. Nonetheless, her criterion and methods laid crucial groundwork for subsequent progress in proving the theorem for restricted classes of primes.

Ideal Theory and Regular Primes

In the mid-1840s, Ernst Kummer developed the theory of ideal numbers to address the failure of unique factorization in the rings of integers of cyclotomic fields, specifically Q(p)\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_p)Q(p) for odd primes ppp, where p\zeta_pp is a primitive ppp-th root of unity; the ring of integers is Z\mathbb{Z}Z, and ideals served as a means to factor elements uniquely up to units. This innovation built upon earlier criteria by Sophie Germain for certain primes but extended far beyond by providing a general algebraic framework. Kummer's work from 1844 to 1850 culminated in proofs of Fermat's Last Theorem for a significant class of exponents, marking a major advance in algebraic number theory.
Kummer defined an odd prime ppp as regular if ppp does not divide the class number hph_php of Q(p)\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_p)Q(p), meaning the ideal class group has no ppp-torsion and certain ideals remain principal under this condition. Regularity ensures that the arithmetic in Z\mathbb{Z}Z behaves sufficiently like that in the integers, allowing Kummer to apply descent arguments and properties of units to contradict non-trivial solutions to xp+yp=zpx^p + y^p = z^pxp+yp=zp.
In 1847, shortly after Gabriel Lam's flawed attempt at a general proof, Kummer established Fermat's Last Theorem for all regular primes p>2p > 2p>2, separating cases based on whether ppp divides xyzxyzxyz (Case I) or zzz (Case II); the proofs relied on the unique factorization of ideals and Kummer's lemma on the structure of units in cyclotomic fields. By 1850, through explicit computations of class numbers, Kummer verified the theorem for all regular odd primes less than 100. Computations showed that all odd primes less than 100 are regular except for 37, 59, and 67, the latter three identified as irregular due to ppp dividing hph_php. These results covered a substantial portion of small exponents, with extensions of Kummer's methods later confirming the theorem for those irregular cases as well.
Computations indicate that regular primes constitute about half of all primes up to 10610^6106, suggesting infinitely many such exponents despite the infinitude of regular primes remaining unproven.

Elliptic Curves Connection

The connection between Fermat's Last Theorem and elliptic curves originated in the early 1970s when Yves Hellegouarch first constructed an elliptic curve associated to potential solutions of Fermat-type equations. In his 1971 work, Hellegouarch considered the curve $ y^2 = x(x - a^n)(x + b^n) $ derived from a hypothetical solution $ a^n + b^n = c^n $ with $ n > 2 $, observing that rational points on this curve correspond to solutions of the Fermat equation. This geometric interpretation laid the groundwork for later developments, though Hellegouarch did not pursue implications for the theorem's proof.
A pivotal advance came in 1986 when Gerhard Frey proposed a refined construction specifically tailored to contradict the expected properties of elliptic curves under the TaniyamaShimura conjecture. For a supposed primitive solution $ a^p + b^p = c^p $ with odd prime $ p $ and $ \gcd(a, b, c) = 1 $, Frey associated the elliptic curve

y^2 = x(x - a^p)(x + b^p),
y2=x(xap)(x+bp),
known as the Frey curve. This curve is semistable, meaning its reduction modulo primes has at most nodal singularities, a property that aligns with minimal discriminants in the Fermat context. However, Frey argued that its conductor and Galois representation exhibit irregularities incompatible with modularity, suggesting that the existence of such a curve would violate the TaniyamaShimura conjecture's prediction that every elliptic curve over the rationals arises from a modular form.
Building on Frey's insight and addressing open questions posed by Jean-Pierre Serre in 1985 regarding the modularity of elliptic curves with specific Galois representations, Kenneth Ribet proved in 1986 that no such Frey curve can be modular. Ribet's theorem establishes that the residual Galois representation of the Frey curve, which has level $ p $ and irregular behavior at primes dividing $ abc $, cannot arise from a modular form of level 1 or $ p $, effectively linking the non-existence of Fermat solutions to the TaniyamaShimura framework. This result implied that proving the modularity of semistable elliptic curves would suffice to rule out Frey curves and thus affirm Fermat's Last Theorem.

Frey and Ribet Theorems

In 1986, Gerhard Frey introduced a novel approach to Fermat's Last Theorem by associating a hypothetical primitive solution ap+bp=cpa^p + b^p = c^pap+bp=cp (with ppp an odd prime) to a specific semistable elliptic curve over Q\mathbb{Q}Q, now called the Frey curve, defined by the equation y2=x(xap)(x+bp)y^2 = x(x - a^p)(x + b^p)y2=x(xap)(x+bp). Frey argued that this curve's minimal discriminant, which is 16(abc)^{2p}, suggested it could not be modular, thereby linking a solution to Fermat's equation with a potential counterexample to the TaniyamaShimura conjecture.
To rigorize this connection, Jean-Pierre Serre formulated the epsilon conjecture in 1987, asserting that for an irreducible mod \ell Galois representation :Gal(Q/Q)GL2(F)\rho: \mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}) \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_\ell):Gal(Q/Q)GL2(F) (with \ell an odd prime) attached to a semistable elliptic curve over Q\mathbb{Q}Q, if \rho is unramified at a prime qqq dividing the conductor NNN of the curve but q2Nq^2 \nmid Nq2N, then \rho arises from a modular form of level strictly dividing NNN. This conjecture implied that semistable elliptic curves achieve the minimal possible level in their associated modular forms.
Kenneth Ribet proved Serre's epsilon conjecture in the summer of 1986 (published in 1990), establishing that the Frey curve, being semistable with conductor 2rad(abc)2 \cdot \mathrm{rad}(abc)2rad(abc), wouldassuming modularitycorrespond to a modular form whose level lowers to 2 via the conjecture's mechanism. Ribet's proof relied on two types of level lowering for Galois representations attached to eigenforms: vertical level lowering, which reduces the prime power factors in the level at primes of good or multiplicative reduction, and horizontal level lowering, which adjusts the nebentypus character to minimize the overall level.
However, no cusp form of weight 2 and level 2 exists with the required traces of Frobenius elements matching those of the Frey curve's Galois representation, rendering modularity impossible. This completed the impossibility chain: a solution to Fermat's equation implies the existence of the Frey curve, which in turn implies a non-modular semistable elliptic curve over Q\mathbb{Q}Q, contradicting the TaniyamaShimura conjecture.

Wiles' Strategy and Breakthrough

Andrew Wiles initiated his research on Fermat's Last Theorem in 1986, focusing on proving the TaniyamaShimura conjecture for semistable elliptic curves over the rationals as a route to resolving the theorem, motivated by the Frey-Ribet linkage of potential counterexamples to non-modular curves. His approach centered on establishing the modularity of Galois representations attached to these elliptic curves, employing deformation theory of Hecke algebras to bridge the representations with modular forms. A pivotal element was the "3-5 switch," where Wiles began with the mod-3 Galois representation of the curvebenefiting from the solvable structure of its image group PGL(2, F_3) isomorphic to S_4and transitioned to the mod-5 representation after verifying modularity at level 3, exploiting the minimal properties at prime 3.
For odd primes, Wiles invoked the Langlands-Tunnell theorem to confirm the modularity of the residual representations, providing the foundational step for lifting to the p-adic setting. This framework allowed him to demonstrate that semistable elliptic curves are modular, thereby excluding the Frey curves and proving the theorem for all exponents. In June 1993, Wiles announced his proof during lectures at the Isaac Newton Institute in Cambridge.
However, by late 1993, a flaw emerged in the argument for the ordinary case, specifically in the Euler system approach to verifying the irreducibility of certain Hecke algebra deformations. To address this gap, Wiles enlisted his former student Richard Taylor in early 1994, and together they developed an alternative 2-adic method using Taylor-Wiles systemsconstructing auxiliary primes to enlarge the Hecke algebra and ensure it remained isomorphic to the universal deformation ring. This innovation bypassed the defective ordinary case treatment while preserving the proof's integrity, completing the demonstration by September 1994.
The full proof appeared in two companion papers: Wiles' solo article on the non-ordinary case and the joint Taylor-Wiles paper on the revised ordinary case, both published in the Annals of Mathematics in 1995. The mathematical community verified the proof's correctness through independent reviews by the end of 1995.

Legacy and Impact

Prizes and Recognition

In 1908, Paul Wolfskehl, a German mathematician inspired by Fermat's Last Theorem to overcome personal despair, established a prize of 100,000 German gold marks to be awarded to the first person to provide a complete proof of the theorem. Due to post-World War I hyperinflation and subsequent economic changes, the prize's value had diminished significantly by the time it was claimed; when awarded to Andrew Wiles on June 27, 1997, by the Royal Society of Sciences at the University of Gttingen after rigorous verification, it amounted to 75,000 Deutsche Marks, equivalent to approximately $37,000 USD at the time.
Earlier progress on the theorem for specific exponents received notable academic recognition, particularly from Marie-Sophie Germain in the early 19th century. In 1816, the Paris Academy of Sciences offered a prize for a general proof of Fermat's Last Theorem, prompting Germain to develop key results on prime exponents where no solutions exist under certain conditions, laying foundational work in number theory despite the prize ultimately going unawarded due to incomplete proofs. Her contributions were later praised by Carl Friedrich Gauss and Adrien-Marie Legendre, who built upon them to establish results for all odd prime exponents up to 197.
Wiles' 1995 proof earned him numerous prestigious awards, underscoring the theorem's significance in mathematics. These include the 1995 Wolf Prize in Mathematics from the Wolf Foundation, shared with Robert Langlands for contributions to number theory; the 1995 Fermat Prize from the Institut de Mathmatiques de Toulouse; the 1997 MacArthur Fellowship from the John D. and Catherine T. MacArthur Foundation; and the 2016 Abel Prize from the Norwegian Academy of Science and Letters, often called the Nobel Prize of mathematics, for his proof via the modularity theorem. In 2017, he received the Copley Medal from the Royal Society, its oldest award, for advancing elliptic curve theory in resolving the theorem.
The theorem's cultural impact extends to public honors, such as a 2001 French postage stamp commemorating Pierre de Fermat and his famous conjecture on the 400th anniversary of his birth, highlighting its enduring allure in popular science.

Incorrect Proof Claims

Throughout history, Fermat's Last Theorem has attracted numerous incorrect proof attempts, underscoring its deceptive simplicity and profound difficulty. One of the earliest notable false proofs was presented by Gabriel Lam in 1847, who claimed to have demonstrated the theorem for all prime exponents p3p \geq 3p3 using the binomial theorem and factorization in the ring of integers adjoined with a primitive ppp-th root of unity, Z\mathbb{Z}Z. Lam assumed unique factorization in this ring, expanding (a+b)p=cp(a + b)^p = c^p(a+b)p=cp to derive a contradiction via irreducible factors. However, Joseph Liouville immediately critiqued this approach, noting that unique factorization does not generally hold in Z\mathbb{Z}Z without the additional structure of ideal class groups, as later developed by Ernst Kummer; Lam conceded the error shortly after.
The theorem's allure persisted into the 20th century, fueled by the 1908 Wolfskehl Prize, established by German mathematician Paul Wolfskehl, which offered 100,000 gold marks (equivalent to about $1 million today) for a correct proof. This incentive led to a deluge of submissions to the prize committee at the University of Gttingen, with over 1,000 purported proofs published between 1908 and 1912 alone, and thousands more evaluated over the prize's 90-year duration, all ultimately found flawed. For instance, amateur attempts often mirrored professional missteps but with less rigor, such as a 1988 submission critiqued in mathematical journals for elementary algebraic errors in handling modular arithmetic. These efforts highlight the theorem's status as having the highest number of documented false proofs in mathematics.
Common pitfalls in these incorrect proofs included illicit assumptions of unique factorization in non-unique domains, such as cyclotomic fields, echoing Lam's oversight, and failures in infinite descent arguments, where reductions to smaller solutions overlooked cases involving units or associate elements that prevent strict descent. Other frequent errors involved misapplications of elliptic curves or modular forms before their full theoretical development, leading to unsubstantiated congruences.
To claim the Wolfskehl Prize, submissions required prior publication in a reputable mathematical journal, followed by a two-year period of rigorous scrutiny by the committee to verify correctness and originality; this process ensured only thoroughly vetted proofs were considered, weeding out hasty or unrefereed claims. The prize remained unawarded until 1997, when it was given to Andrew Wiles after his proof withstood this evaluation.

Popular Culture Representations

Fermat's Last Theorem has captured the public imagination through various books that narrate its history and resolution as a gripping human drama. Simon Singh's 1997 popular science book Fermat's Enigma: The Epic Quest to Solve the World's Greatest Mathematical Problem chronicles the theorem's 350-year pursuit, culminating in Andrew Wiles' proof, and has been praised for making complex mathematical history accessible to lay readers. Similarly, Amir D. Aczel's 1996 book Fermat's Last Theorem: Unlocking the Secret of an Ancient Mathematical Problem explores the theorem's origins and cultural context, presenting the story as a mystery novel with historical anecdotes about mathematicians across centuries.
In television, the theorem featured prominently in the 1997 NOVA documentary The Proof, which follows Wiles' secretive seven-year journey to solve it, using animations to illustrate key concepts and emphasizing the emotional stakes of mathematical discovery. Popular culture also referenced the theorem humorously in a 1995 episode of The Simpsons titled "The Wizard of Evergreen Terrace," where Homer Simpson scrawls an equation on a blackboard that appears to challenge the theorem but ultimately falls short due to rounding errors in its approximation. The theorem also appeared in the 1989 Star Trek: The Next Generation episode "The Royale", where Captain Jean-Luc Picard discusses the theorem with Commander Riker, explaining its statement and history while expressing his personal interest in attempting to solve it, noting that it had remained unsolved for eight hundred years and describing it as a humbling challenge despite advanced technology.
The theorem's allure has inspired broader interest in number theory, often depicted in literature as a symbol of intellectual obsession and the thrill of unsolved puzzles. For instance, Apostolos Doxiadis' 1992 novel Uncle Petros and Goldbach's Conjecture portrays a mathematician's lifelong dedication to another famous unsolved problem, mirroring the solitary passion seen in Fermat's story and encouraging readers to engage with advanced mathematical ideas through narrative fiction.
Marking the 30th anniversary of Wiles' breakthrough in 1994, recent media in the 2020s has revisited the theorem through podcasts and YouTube videos, reigniting public fascination with its dramatic resolution. A 2023 YouTube interview with Wiles reflects on the proof's legacy and its impact on modern mathematics, while anniversary discussions in outlets like the Isaac Newton Institute highlight ongoing educational efforts to popularize the theorem.
