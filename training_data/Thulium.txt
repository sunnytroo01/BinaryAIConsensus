Thulium

1x
 Thulium is a chemical element with the atomic number 69 and symbol Tm, belonging to the lanthanide series of the periodic table and recognized as the least abundant of the stable rare earth elements. It appears as a soft, silver-gray metal that is malleable and ductile enough to be cut with a knife at room temperature.
As a member of the f-block in period 6, thulium has an atomic weight of approximately 168.93 and exists primarily in the +3 oxidation state, forming a basic oxide and exhibiting low electronegativity of 1.25 on the Pauling scale. Its sole stable isotope, Tm, constitutes 100% of naturally occurring thulium, while 25 radioactive isotopes are known, ranging from mass 152 to 176. Thulium was discovered in 1879 by Swedish chemist Per Theodor Cleve through spectroscopic analysis of erbia (erbium oxide) derived from the mineral gadolinite, with the element named after Thule, the ancient name for Scandinavia; it was first isolated in relatively pure form in 1911 by Charles James.
In nature, thulium occurs in trace amountsabout 0.007% in monazite sandsand is roughly as rare as silver, with an average crustal abundance of around 0.3 parts per million, making commercial extraction challenging and typically involving ion-exchange processes from rare earth mineral concentrates. Production involves reducing thulium oxide with lanthanum metal or calcium under vacuum. Physically, it has a density of 9.32 g/cm, melts at 1545C, and boils at 1950C, displaying typical lanthanide properties such as tarnishing in air and reacting with water to release hydrogen.
Thulium's applications remain limited due to its scarcity and cost, but it serves as a radiation source in portable X-ray machines via neutron activation of Tm to produce Tm, which emits soft X-rays and gamma rays; it is also used in ferrites for microwave applications, as a dopant in fiber lasers for telecommunications, and shows potential in high-temperature superconductors and as an energy source through Tm beta decay. In 2025, thulium was added to the U.S. list of critical minerals. Biologically, thulium exhibits low to moderate acute toxicity, requiring careful handling to avoid inhalation or ingestion, though it has no known essential role in living organisms.

Properties

Physical properties

Thulium is a silvery-white, lustrous metal that is soft, malleable, and ductile, allowing it to be cut with a knife, though it slowly tarnishes in air at room temperature.
It exhibits a density of 9.321 g/cm at 20C, positioning it among the denser elements in the lanthanide series. Thulium has a melting point of 1,545C and a boiling point of 1,947C.
The metal demonstrates moderate electrical and thermal conductivity, with a specific electrical resistivity of 79.0 cm at 20C and a thermal conductivity of 16.8 W/(mK).
At room temperature, thulium is paramagnetic, with a molar magnetic susceptibility of 3.36 10^{-7} m/mol; it undergoes antiferromagnetic ordering below a Nel temperature of 58 K and follows a Curie-Weiss law above this point with a paramagnetic Curie temperature of approximately 20 K.
Thulium adopts a hexagonal close-packed (hcp) crystal structure at ambient conditions, characterized by lattice constants a = 3.538 and c = 5.555 . Under high pressure, it undergoes a phase transition to a samarium-type structure (a distorted hcp variant) between 60 and 116 kbar at room temperature.

Chemical properties

Thulium, with the electron configuration 4f 6s, predominantly exhibits the +3 oxidation state (Tm) in its compounds, resulting in a 4f configuration for the ion. The +2 oxidation state (Tm) occurs rarely and is unstable in aqueous environments, often requiring stabilizing ligands or specific conditions such as in the diiodide TmI.
As a reactive lanthanide metal, thulium dissolves readily in dilute acids such as hydrochloric acid (HCl) and sulfuric acid (HSO), liberating hydrogen gas and forming pale green solutions of Tm ions. It does not react with hydrofluoric acid (HF) owing to the formation of an insoluble thulium(III) fluoride (TmF) protective layer on its surface. In air, thulium tarnishes slowly in moist conditions by forming a thin layer of thulium(III) oxide (TmO) via the reaction 4Tm + 3O 2TmO, and it ignites readily above 150C to produce the same oxide.
Thulium reacts with halogens (F, Cl, Br, I) at elevated temperatures above 200C to yield the corresponding thulium(III) halides (TmX, where X = F, Cl, Br, I), which are generally soluble in water except for TmF. In coordination chemistry, Tm ions, with a Shannon ionic radius of 0.880 for coordination number (CN) 6, typically adopt CN 69 in complexes, as seen in the nona-aqua complex in aqueous solutions and various 8- or 9-coordinate structures in solid-state compounds. This high coordination arises from the relatively large size of the Tm ion among lanthanides. The standard reduction potential for Tm + 3e Tm is approximately -2.3 V versus the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE), underscoring its strong reducing nature.

Isotopes

Thulium has a single stable isotope, ^{169}Tm, which accounts for 100% of its natural abundance and has an atomic mass of 168.9342179(22) u. This isotope's nuclear structure, characterized by an odd number of protons (Z=69) and an even number of neutrons (N=100), contributes to its relative stability among lanthanide nuclides, as the even neutron count aligns with enhanced binding near N=82-126 shell closures, though no direct odd-nucleon pairing explanation is uniquely cited beyond general nuclear models. Naturally occurring thulium exhibits no significant primordial radioisotopes, with all radioactive variants produced artificially in reactors or accelerators.
Thirty-eight radioactive isotopes of thulium, spanning mass numbers 144 to 183, have been identified, with decay modes predominantly involving beta minus () emission for isotopes lighter than ^{169}Tm and electron capture (EC) for heavier ones. Among these, ^{170}Tm (half-life 128.6 days, decay) and ^{171}Tm (half-life 1.92 years, decay) stand out for their relatively long half-lives, enabling applications such as ^{170}Tm in portable X-ray sources for nondestructive testing. Lighter isotopes like ^{168}Tm (half-life 93.1 days, primarily EC) exhibit Q-values around 1.679 MeV, while heavier ones like ^{172}Tm (half-life 63.6 hours, ) have energies up to 1.880 MeV.
Radioactive thulium isotopes are typically produced via neutron activation of enriched ^{169}Tm targets, as in the reaction ^{169}Tm(n,)^{170}Tm, which yields high-specific-activity ^{170}Tm suitable for medical and industrial uses under thermal neutron fluxes. This process exploits the 100% abundance of ^{169}Tm, minimizing isotopic impurities, though burn-up and competing reactions like (n,2n) must be managed for purity.

History

Discovery

Thulium was discovered in 1879 by Swedish chemist Per Teodor Cleve at Uppsala University in Sweden. Cleve was investigating erbia, an impure form of erbium oxide derived from the mineral gadolinite, as part of efforts to purify rare earth elements. Through fractional separation techniques involving repeated precipitation, he isolated two new substances from the erbia: a brown material later identified as holmia (the oxide of holmium) and a greenish material he named thulia. This process highlighted the challenges of separating chemically similar lanthanides, which often required dozens of iterative precipitations to achieve purity.
Cleve confirmed the presence of a new element in the green thulia by spectroscopic analysis, observing distinct absorption lines that differed from those of known rare earths like erbium and terbium. Further examination via flame tests revealed bright green emission lines characteristic of thulium, providing key evidence to distinguish it from erbium and holmium. These spectral features, arising from electronic transitions in the lanthanide series, were crucial for identification amid the overlapping properties of the elements.
This discovery occurred within the broader context of separating the "ytterbium group" of rare earthselements with similar chemical behaviorsfrom gadolinite and other minerals extracted from the Ytterby mine near Stockholm, Sweden. The Ytterby deposits had already yielded yttrium, terbium, and erbium, and Cleve's isolation of thulium completed another step in unraveling the complex lanthanide series originating from this prolific site.

Naming and early research

The name thulium derives from Thule, the ancient Greek term for the northernmost region of the known world, often associated with Scandinavia, chosen to honor the Swedish heritage of its discoverer, Per Teodor Cleve. Cleve announced the element's existence in 1879 after separating thulia (TmO), a pale green oxide, from erbia through spectroscopic analysis of impurities in erbium oxide derived from gadolinite. The chemical symbol Tm was adopted by the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC), replacing the provisional Tu.
Early research on thulium faced significant challenges due to its chemical similarity to neighboring lanthanides, particularly ytterbium and lutetium, which complicated separation from mineral sources like monazite and xenotime. Cleve's initial isolation yielded only impure thulia, and subsequent efforts relied on fractional precipitation and crystallization techniques to purify it, as the elements' oxides and salts exhibited nearly identical solubilities and reactivities. These difficulties persisted into the early 20th century, limiting the quantity of material available for study and delaying confirmation of thulium's distinct properties.
A major advancement came in 1911 when American chemist Charles James achieved the first nearly pure separation of thulium oxide at the University of New Hampshire, employing his innovative fractional crystallization method with ammonium bromatesrequiring over 15,000 operations to yield about 2 grams of the oxide. James's technique, later known as the James method, became a standard for rare earth separations until ion-exchange processes emerged in the 1940s. Using this pure sample, James determined thulium's atomic weight as approximately 169, providing one of the earliest reliable values.
Pre-1940s investigations further characterized thulium through its absorption spectra, which displayed unique sharp lines in the visible and ultraviolet regions, enabling purity assessments and distinguishing it from contaminants like holmium and dysprosium. These spectral studies, building on Cleve's spectroscopic detection, confirmed thulium's position in the lanthanide series. The first metallic thulium was produced in 1936 by German chemists Wilhelm Klemm and Heinrich Bommer via reduction of thulium fluoride with calcium, yielding a reactive, silvery metal that tarnished rapidly in air.

Occurrence and production

Occurrence

Thulium is the rarest of the stable lanthanide elements, with an abundance of 0.52 parts per million (ppm) in the Earth's upper crust. In the continental crust, its concentration averages approximately 0.5 ppm, with an abundance similar in order of magnitude to silver (0.08 ppm) but much greater than gold (0.004 ppm). This low abundance underscores thulium's status among the heavy rare earth elements (HREE), where it exhibits geochemical behavior typical of the group, associating closely with other HREEs such as ytterbium and lutetium.
Thulium occurs primarily as a trace constituent in several phosphate and carbonate minerals, substituting for larger ions due to its relatively small trivalent ionic radius of 0.880 . Key sources include monazite ((Ce,La,Nd,Th)PO), where thulium content reaches up to 0.01% by weight, and xenotime (YPO), a phosphate enriched in HREEs and yttrium. It also appears in trace amounts in bastnsite ((Ce,La)COF) and gadolinite, though concentrations are typically lower, often below 0.001%. Thulium's partitioning into these phosphate minerals reflects its compatibility with structures favoring smaller cations, but no thulium-dominant minerals exist due to its extreme rarity.
In cosmic contexts, thulium's abundance is estimated at approximately 0.3 ppm in CI chondritic meteorites, which serve as a proxy for solar system composition. It has been detected in stellar spectra through absorption lines and in meteorites at levels comparable to terrestrial values, confirming its uniform distribution across cosmic materials.
Thulium is present at trace levels in seawater, with concentrations around 1.6 10^{-11} g/L in deep Atlantic waters, reflecting its low solubility and scavenging by particles. In the biosphere, it accumulates minimally in living organisms, occurring only at incidental trace amounts with no significant incorporation into biological systems.

Production

Thulium production begins with the processing of rare earth-bearing ores, primarily monazite and bastnsite, which are digested to yield concentrates rich in rare earth elements. Monazite sands are typically treated with concentrated sulfuric acid or sodium hydroxide in an autoclave at elevated temperatures to decompose the mineral and produce a rare earth phosphate or sulfate concentrate, while bastnsite is roasted with sulfuric acid or fused with NaOH to facilitate leaching of the rare earth carbonates into solution. These initial steps concentrate the rare earth oxides to around 60% purity, removing gangue materials like silica and thorium, though thulium constitutes only about 0.007% of the original monazite ore.
Isolation of thulium from the mixed rare earth concentrate relies on advanced separation techniques, predominantly solvent extraction and ion-exchange chromatography, due to the chemical similarities among lanthanides. Solvent extraction using di-(2-ethylhexyl)phosphoric acid (DEHPA) dissolved in kerosene is a standard method, where the heavy rare earth fraction, including thulium, is selectively extracted from acidic chloride or nitrate solutions through multiple counter-current stages; distribution coefficients favor thulium over lighter elements like neodymium, achieving purities exceeding 99.9% after 20-50 extraction cycles. Ion-exchange chromatography complements this by passing the solution through resin columns loaded with sulfonic acid groups, eluting thulium with ammonium citrate or EDTA solutions in a stepwise manner to separate it from adjacent elements like ytterbium and erbium. These processes are energy-intensive but essential for handling thulium's low concentration in the heavy rare earth group.
The purified thulium oxide (TmO) is then converted to metal via high-temperature reduction or electrolytic methods. Metallothermic reduction of thulium fluoride (TmF) with calcium or lanthanum metal occurs in a vacuum furnace at approximately 1,400C, following the reaction 2TmF + 3Ca 2Tm + 3CaF, yielding dendritic metal chunks that are subsequently distilled under vacuum for further purification. Alternatively, electrolytic reduction from thulium chloride (TmCl) in a molten LiCl-KCl electrolyte at 400-550C deposits thulium at inert cathodes like tungsten, with anodic chlorine evolution; this method allows for continuous production and higher purity (up to 99.99%) but requires careful control to avoid alloying with the salt components. Both approaches are conducted in inert atmospheres to prevent oxidation, given thulium's reactivity.
Global thulium production is limited, with an estimated annual output of around 200 tonnes of oxide equivalent as of 2023, reflecting its scarcity among rare earths. China dominates with approximately 90% of supply, leveraging large-scale operations at facilities like those in Baotou and Jiangyin, while minor contributions come from Australia via Lynas Rare Earths' Mount Weld mine and processing in Malaysia, with limited U.S. involvement in heavy rare earth separation. The supply chain integrates mining, concentration, and separation stages, with Lynas focusing on non-Chinese sources to diversify global availability amid geopolitical tensions.
High-purity thulium (4N-5N, or 99.99-99.999%) typically costs $8003,000 per kilogram, with prices for small research quantities higher due to handling and purity requirements; challenges in scaling production stem from thulium's low crustal abundance (about 0.5 ppm) and the multi-stage refinement required, limiting output and driving costs higher than for more common rare earths.
Post-2020 advancements in liquid-liquid extraction have enhanced thulium recovery efficiencies, incorporating greener solvents like ionic liquids and task-specific extractants (e.g., diglycolamide derivatives) to reduce acid consumption and waste generation in sustainable rare earth processing. These innovations, including non-aqueous solvent systems and bio-based extractants, have improved selectivity for heavy rare earths like thulium by up to 20-30% in separation yields, supporting environmentally friendlier industrial flowsheets while addressing supply chain vulnerabilities.

Applications

Laser technology

Thulium ions (Tm) are commonly doped into yttrium aluminum garnet (YAG) hosts to produce solid-state lasers operating in the 2 m wavelength range, leveraging the electronic transition from the H upper level to the H ground state, which emits broadly between 1.9 and 2.0 m. This transition is pumped typically at around 800 nm to the F level, followed by cross-relaxation that enables efficient two-for-one photon generation, enhancing overall laser performance. Early developments of Tm:YAG lasers in the late 20th century positioned them as alternatives to neodymium-doped YAG systems, offering eye-safe wavelengths suitable for medical and remote sensing applications.
Thulium fiber lasers (TFLs), utilizing Tm-doped silica fibers, have emerged as versatile systems supporting both pulsed and continuous-wave operations, with emissions centered near 2 m. These lasers benefit from a cross-relaxation mechanism that achieves pump quantum efficiencies approaching 2, allowing slope efficiencies up to 37% with respect to absorbed power in continuous-wave configurations. Compared to holmium:YAG lasers, TFLs offer higher ablation ratesup to four times faster in dusting modeand better alignment with water's absorption peak at 1.94 m, which enhances precision in aqueous environments while reducing thermal damage. Beam quality in TFLs is typically near-diffraction-limited, with M values below 1.1, and power scaling has reached kilowatt levels through large-mode-area fiber designs and cascaded amplification.
In medical applications, particularly endourology, TFLs excel in lithotripsy for kidney stone dusting, where their high repetition rates (up to 2000 Hz) and pulse energies reaching 1 J enable finer fragmentation with minimal retropulsion. Studies from 2020 to 2025 demonstrate TFL superiority over holmium:YAG lasers, including shorter operative times, higher stone-free rates (up to 73% clearance), and reduced energy delivery, as evidenced in in vivo porcine models and clinical trials for ureteric calculi. For instance, superpulsed TFL settings at 0.9 J and 10 Hz have proven safe and effective for ureteric stones, producing fragments under 1 mm in 77% of cases.
Industrially, TFLs support material processing tasks such as welding, cutting, and precision machining of metals and composites, capitalizing on their compact design and high beam quality for non-contact operations. The global thulium fiber laser market, driven by these applications, is projected to reach $178 million by 2025, reflecting growth in demand for efficient, eye-safe systems in manufacturing.
Beyond silica and YAG hosts, Tm doping in calcium fluoride (CaF) enables upconversion lasers, where energy transfer processes in Tm,Yb-codoped structures produce near-infrared to visible emissions under 980 nm pumping, suitable for bioimaging and photonic devices. Recent continuous-wave Tm,Ho:CaF lasers at 2.1 m further highlight the host's low phonon energy and broad emission for ultrafast pulse generation.

X-ray technology

Thulium-170 ($ ^{170}\mathrm{Tm} $), an isotope with a half-life of 128.6 days, serves as a beta-emitting radiation source in portable X-ray devices, where its beta particles generate bremsstrahlung X-rays primarily in the 50-100 keV energy range suitable for imaging. This mechanism leverages the deceleration of beta particles (with maximum energies of 0.97 MeV and 0.88 MeV) within the source material or surrounding components to produce a continuous X-ray spectrum, complemented by characteristic X-rays around 52 keV and gamma rays at 84 keV from internal conversion processes. These devices, often used in dental and industrial field radiography, incorporate a sealed $ ^{170}\mathrm{Tm} $ source encapsulated in materials like aluminum within a lead-shielded housing, enabling operation without electrical power and delivering radiation outputs on the order of several roentgens per hour at close distances for typical curie-level sources.
The production of $ ^{170}\mathrm{Tm} $ involves neutron irradiation of stable thulium-169 ($ ^{169}\mathrm{Tm} $) in nuclear reactors through the capture reaction $ ^{169}\mathrm{Tm}(n,\gamma)^{170}\mathrm{Tm} $, yielding high specific activities up to 173 Ci/g with excellent radionuclidic purity exceeding 99.6%. The resulting isotope is processed into compact foils or capsules for integration into portable units, offering a practical alternative to earlier radium-based sources due to its chemical stability and reduced biological hazard from softer radiation. Since the 1950s, $ ^{170}\mathrm{Tm} $-based systems have facilitated on-site radiography in remote or power-limited environments, with early designs achieving portability under 10 pounds and exposure times of 12-190 seconds at 18 inches for diagnostic contrasts.
Key advantages of $ ^{170}\mathrm{Tm} $ in these applications include its compact form factor, extended operational shelf-life relative to shorter-lived isotopes, and elimination of high-voltage generators, making it ideal for military, exploratory, and industrial field use as a safer successor to radium needles. However, the source's relatively low intensity compared to electronic X-ray tubes requires prolonged exposure durations to achieve adequate image density, and its radioactive decay mandates replacement every few months to maintain efficacy. Recent advancements in the 2020s, particularly in brachytherapy contexts adaptable to portable systems, have emphasized improved tungsten or lead-composite shielding and precision dosimetry models to minimize operator exposure while complying with stringent radiation safety standards, though adoption remains constrained by regulatory oversight on radioactive material handling.

Other applications

Thulium serves as a dopant in phosphors, notably in materials like calcium fluoride (CaF), enabling blue upconversion luminescence that finds use in advanced displays and anti-counterfeiting features due to its efficient conversion of near-infrared light to visible emission. This property stems from thulium's electronic transitions, particularly the H H pathway, which produces sharp blue light under low-power excitation, making it suitable for secure inks and optical security elements.
In magnetic applications, thulium alloys and pure metal exhibit unique low-temperature behaviors, becoming ferromagnetic below 32 K, which has led to their incorporation in cryogenic magnetometers for high-sensitivity measurements in scientific instruments. Recent research highlights thulium's potential in advanced ceramics and catalysts, where trace amounts enhance properties in high-temperature superconductors and microwave filters by stabilizing lattice structures and improving dielectric performance. Additionally, thulium oxide (TmO) is utilized in solid oxide fuel cells as a component in electrolyte and electrode materials, contributing to improved ionic conductivity at elevated temperatures.
Emerging uses include thulium chloride in specialized laser systems like the UROLASE+ for precise soft tissue dissection in plastic surgery, as demonstrated in 2024 clinical studies showing reduced thermal damage compared to traditional methods. Thulium-doped quantum dots are also gaining attention in photonics for dual-mode imaging and fluorescence applications, leveraging their near-infrared emission for biomedical and optical devices. Historically, thulium found minor roles in early 20th-century arc lighting as a rare earth additive for spectral enhancement, though this waned with incandescent advancements.
Thulium plays a minor but growing role in fiber optics amplifiers, where thulium-doped fibers (TDFAs) provide gain in the 2 m wavelength band for telecommunications and sensing, offering broad bandwidth though less common than erbium-based systems. Market trends indicate expanding applications of thulium powder in magnetic devices, with projected growth at a CAGR of approximately 5.5% through 2025, driven by demand in high-tech sectors like cryogenics and energy storage.

Biological and safety aspects

Biological role

Thulium has no known biological role and is not essential for any enzymatic, structural, or physiological functions in organisms, including humans, animals, or plants. Unlike essential trace elements, thulium does not participate in metabolic processes or support growth. Small amounts of soluble thulium salts may stimulate metabolism, though this is not an essential function.
The bioavailability of thulium is limited due to the low solubility of its compounds, such as thulium oxide and phosphates, which restricts uptake through ingestion, inhalation, or dermal exposure in biological systems. The Tm ion has an ionic radius of 88 pm (for coordination number 6), similar to Ca at 100 pm, but its trivalent charge impedes substitution into calcium-binding sites in proteins or bone structures, further reducing incorporation into cells or tissues.
In biological distribution, thulium occurs at trace levels via environmental uptake through the food chain, with accumulation primarily in bones and liver due to weak binding affinities. Concentrations in human tissues occur at trace levels. In aquatic animals like the golden grey mullet, tissue levels range from 0.97 pg/g in muscle to 20.3 pg/g in intestine, reflecting minimal retention. Plants exhibit variable bioaccumulation, with hyperaccumulators such as the fern species Dicranopteris linearis and Blechnum orientale capable of concentrating rare earth elements, including thulium, up to several thousand mg/kg dry weight in contaminated soils, as demonstrated in 2020s studies on phytoremediation.
Metabolically, absorbed thulium undergoes primary excretion via urine, with some fecal elimination observed in rat studies using radioactive tracers.

Precautions

Thulium in its metallic form is generally considered non-toxic, with limited evidence of severe health effects from typical exposures, though its compounds exhibit mild irritant properties. Thulium(III) salts, such as thulium chloride, can cause mild irritation to the skin and eyes upon contact, but no cases of acute thulium poisoning have been widely reported in human literature.
Primary exposure routes for thulium include inhalation of dust or fumes, which may lead to respiratory effects resembling pneumoconiosis with prolonged high-level exposure, and ingestion, which can result in gastrointestinal distress such as nausea and diarrhea. The oral LD50 for thulium chloride is approximately 4.3 g/kg in mice, indicating relatively low acute toxicity compared to more hazardous metals.
For radioactive isotopes like Tm, used in industrial applications, significant radiation hazards arise from beta and gamma emissions, necessitating adherence to ALARA (As Low As Reasonably Achievable) principles, along with appropriate shielding using low-atomic-number materials like polyethylene for beta particles and lead for gamma rays.
Environmental risks from thulium primarily stem from rare earth element mining and processing, which can release thulium into soil and water through tailings and wastewater, potentially leading to localized contamination. Bioaccumulation of thulium in aquatic organisms is generally low due to its chemical behavior, but concentrations in water bodies are monitored under broader rare earth element guidelines, with no specific U.S. EPA criterion established for thulium alone.
Safe handling of thulium requires working in well-ventilated fume hoods to minimize dust inhalation, along with personal protective equipment such as gloves, safety goggles, and respirators; OSHA classifies thulium dust as a nuisance particulate with a permissible exposure limit of 15 mg/m (total dust) and 5 mg/m (respirable fraction) in the absence of element-specific standards. As of 2025, NIOSH continues to recommend similar exposure controls for rare earth compounds, emphasizing engineering controls and monitoring for workers in mining and processing.
Disposal of thulium-containing materials follows hazardous waste regulations; non-radioactive forms are typically managed as non-hazardous unless contaminated, while radioisotopes like Tm are classified as hazardous under RCRA due to radiological risks, requiring secure storage and licensed disposal. Recycling thulium from electronic waste is encouraged to reduce environmental releases, with processes recovering it from magnets and phosphors through hydrometallurgical methods.
