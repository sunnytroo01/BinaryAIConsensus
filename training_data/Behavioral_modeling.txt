Behavioral modeling
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Behavioral modeling is an approach within systems theory and control theory that describes dynamical systems in terms of their behaviorsthe sets of all possible trajectories of system variableswithout distinguishing a priori between inputs and outputs. Pioneered by Jan C. Willems in the late 1970s, it emerged to resolve inconsistencies in classical representations such as state-space models, transfer functions, and convolution models, offering a unified, physics-respecting framework for system analysis and design.
This paradigm shifts focus from internal mechanisms to observable signal behaviors, enabling advancements in areas like controllability, system identification, and control by interconnection. It applies particularly to linear time-invariant systems and has influenced modern developments in networked and hybrid systems.

Overview

Definition and scope

In the behavioral approach to systems theory, a dynamical system is formally defined as a triple (T,W,B)(T, W, B)(T,W,B), where TTT denotes the time axis, such as the real numbers R\mathbb{R}R for continuous time or the integers Z\mathbb{Z}Z for discrete time; WWW represents the signal space, typically Rq\mathbb{R}^qRq for qqq variables; and BWTB \subseteq W^TBWT is the behavior, comprising the set of all valid signal trajectories w:TWw: T \to Ww:TW that satisfy the system's constraints. This framework shifts the focus from internal mechanisms to the observable manifestations of the system, treating behaviors as relations imposed directly on signals without requiring a priori distinctions between inputs and outputs.
Unlike classical state-space or transfer function methods, which presuppose causality, state variables, or partitioned input-output structures, the behavioral approach emphasizes constraintssuch as differential or difference equationsthat define the admissible signals, allowing for a more intrinsic description of system dynamics. The core principle is that systems are governed by laws or relations on the signals themselves, eschewing assumptions about hidden states or directional flows, which makes it particularly suitable for modeling interconnected or physical systems where such partitions are artificial or unclear.
The scope of behavioral modeling primarily encompasses linear time-invariant systems over continuous or discrete time domains, though extensions to nonlinear and time-varying cases exist; it originated within control theory but extends to applications in signal processing, network analysis, and multidimensional systems like partial differential equations in physics.

Historical development

The behavioral approach to systems theory was initiated by Jan C. Willems in the mid-1980s to address fundamental inconsistencies in classical input/output and state-space models, such as the non-realizability of certain transfer functions that lacked proper rational forms or corresponding minimal state-space realizations. In a series of foundational papers published in Automatica between 1986 and 1987, Willems introduced the core idea of viewing dynamical systems as sets of signal trajectories, or "behaviors," rather than predefined inputs and outputs, thereby resolving paradoxes like the artificial distinction between manifest and latent variables in physical systems. This shift emphasized a representation-free perspective, allowing for more flexible modeling of systems where causality was not presupposed.
Willems' seminal 1991 paper, "Paradigms and Puzzles in the Theory of Dynamical Systems," further formalized this signal-based framework, outlining a triptych of behavior, behavioral equations, and latent variables to unify diverse representation problems in dynamical systems theory. The paper highlighted how traditional approaches often led to inconsistencies, such as non-causal behaviors being excluded despite their relevance in interconnected or descriptive models, and proposed intrinsic notions of controllability and observability independent of input/output partitioning.
Key milestones in the 1980s included extensions to linear time-invariant systems, building on the initial behavioral definitions to incorporate module theory over polynomial rings for behavioral descriptions. In the 1990s, the development of representation theoremssuch as kernel and image representationsprovided canonical forms for behaviors, integrating deeply with polynomial matrix theory to enable parametrizations like autoregressive and moving-average models. Willems remained the central figure until his death in 2013, with significant contributions from collaborators like Jan Willem Polderman on realization theory and Ivan Markovsky on identification methods, as detailed in their joint 1998 textbook and Markovsky's later works on behavioral system identification.
The approach evolved from resolving early paradoxes in system representations to profoundly influencing modern data-driven control, where behaviors facilitate direct trajectory-based methods without explicit model identification, as seen in extensions like the fundamental lemma for linear systems. Recent advances as of 2025 include extensions to stochastic systems via Willems' lemma and polynomial chaos expansions, and reformulations preserving LTI behaviors in data-driven contexts.

Core Concepts

Dynamical systems as sets of signals

In the behavioral approach to dynamical systems, signals are formalized as functions $ w: T \to W $, where $ T $ denotes the time domaineither the real line $ \mathbb{R} $ for continuous-time systems or the integers $ \mathbb{Z} $ for discrete-time systemsand $ W $ is a finite-dimensional vector space, typically $ \mathbb{R}^q $ for some positive integer $ q $, serving as the signal space for the system's manifest variables such as positions, velocities, or voltages. These signals represent the observable trajectories of the system over time, capturing its evolution without invoking hidden internal mechanisms.
A dynamical system is conceptualized as the behavior $ B $, defined as the subset of all admissible signal trajectories in $ W^T $ that satisfy the external constraints dictated by the system's physical or mathematical laws, eschewing any reliance on internal state variables. Formally, the system is the triple $ \Sigma = (T, W, B) $ with $ B \subseteq W^T $, where the constraints might arise from differential, difference, or algebraic relations among the signal components. This representation emphasizes the collective set of possible evolutions rather than a specific parametric form.
A canonical example is the undamped mass-spring oscillator, modeled solely through the manifest signal $ w: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R} $ representing the displacement of the mass from equilibrium. The behavior $ B $ comprises all twice-differentiable functions $ w $ satisfying the second-order differential constraint $ M w''(t) + k w(t) = 0 $ for all $ t \in \mathbb{R} $, where $ M > 0 $ is the mass and $ k > 0 $ the spring constant; solutions are sinusoidal trajectories with frequencies determined by $ \sqrt{k/M} $, illustrating how the set $ B $ encodes the system's oscillatory dynamics purely via signal relations.
Central prerequisite concepts in this framework include time-invariance, under which the behavior remains unchanged under temporal shiftsformally, $ B $ is shift-invariant if $ w \in B $ implies $ \sigma_\tau w \in B $ for all $ \tau \in T $, with the shift operator defined by $ (\sigma_\tau w)(t) = w(t - \tau) $ensuring the system's laws do not explicitly depend on absolute time. Linearity requires $ W $ to be a vector space and $ B $ to be a subspace thereof, satisfying superposition: if $ w_1, w_2 \in B $, then $ \alpha w_1 + \beta w_2 \in B $ for scalars $ \alpha, \beta $. The full axiomatic structure accommodates both complete systems, where $ B $ fully captures all constraints without latent variables, and incomplete systems, where additional hidden variables may underlie the manifest behavior but are not essential for the signal-set description.
This signal-centric view diverges from classical paradigms, such as state-space or transfer function models, by imposing no a priori distinction between inputs and outputs; all variables are manifest signals treated symmetrically within the behavior $ B $, allowing flexible partitioning based on context rather than fixed roles.

Behaviors and trajectories

In the behavioral approach to systems theory, the behavior $ B $ is defined as the set of all trajectories $ w: \mathcal{T} \to \mathbb{R}^q $ that satisfy the constraints imposed by the system's laws, where $ \mathcal{T} \subseteq \mathbb{R} $ is the time axis and $ \mathbb{R}^q $ is the signal space. Thus, $ B \subseteq (\mathbb{R}^q)^{\mathcal{T}} $, representing the collection of all admissible signal evolutions that comply with physical or mathematical relations, such as differential equations. This formulation treats the system as the behavior itself, without presupposing input-output partitions, allowing for a unified treatment of various dynamical phenomena.
Behaviors exhibit key properties that characterize their structure. Completeness ensures that $ B $ includes all possible trajectories satisfying the system equations, including weak solutions approximable by smooth strong ones, providing a robust framework for modeling. Autonomy describes behaviors with no free variables, where trajectories are fully determined by initial conditions, as in closed systems governed by equations like $ P\left(\frac{d}{dt}\right) w = 0 $ with $ \det P(\xi) \neq 0 $, ensuring the past uniquely predicts the future. In contrast, controlled behaviors incorporate free variables (inputs) that influence the evolution, such as in systems where $ P\left(\frac{d}{dt}\right) y = Q\left(\frac{d}{dt}\right) u $. Shift-invariance, a hallmark of time-invariant systems, requires that if $ w(t) \in B $, then $ w(t - t_1) \in B $ for any shift $ t_1 $, preserving the behavior under temporal translations.
Trajectories within $ B $ can be full, spanning the entire time domain $ \mathcal{T} $ (e.g., in $ L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{R}^q) $), or restricted to subsets like $ (-\infty, t_0] $ or $ For instance, in modeling an electrical resistor, trajectories $ (V, I) $ satisfy $ V = R I $ over the full real line, while restricted versions might focus on transient responses. Interconnections of behaviors arise in networked systems through projections (restricting to shared variables) or direct sums, such as combining autonomous and controlled components via $ B = B_{\mathrm{aut}} \oplus B_{\mathrm{contr}} $, or intersections for compatibility, enabling the modeling of multi-component interactions like fluid tanks linked by pressure and flow constraints.
A fundamental mathematical property of complete behaviors is closure under concatenation: if two trajectories $ w_1 $ and $ w_2 $ are compatible (e.g., matching states at junction time $ t_0 $), their combination forms a valid trajectory in $ B $, supporting the analysis of continuous evolutions. This property is particularly useful in hybrid systems, where behaviors model switches between discrete modes, or in networked systems like electrical circuits, where interconnections via latent variables capture emergent dynamics without explicit state specifications.
Constraint modeling in the behavioral framework represents laws as relations $ R(w) = 0 $, where $ R $ encodes the system's equations (e.g., differential or algebraic), defining $ B $ as the solution set or kernel of $ R $. For example, Newton's second law yields trajectories $ (q, F) $ satisfying $ F = m \frac{d^2 q}{dt^2} $, with $ B $ as those evolutions where this relation holds. This relational view unifies diverse constraints, from physical laws to interconnections, emphasizing behaviors as the primary objects for synthesis and analysis.

Linear Time-Invariant Systems

Kernel representations

In the behavioral approach to linear time-invariant (LTI) systems, kernel representations provide a constraint-based formulation of the system behavior $ B \subseteq (\mathbb{R}^q)^\mathbb{R} $, defined as the set of all trajectories $ w $ satisfying differential or difference constraints without reference to state variables.
For continuous-time LTI systems, the behavior is given by $ B = \ker(R(\sigma)) = { w \in C^\infty(\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{R}^q) \mid R\left(\frac{d}{dt}\right) w(t) = 0 \ \forall t \in \mathbb{R} } $, where $ \sigma = \frac{d}{dt} $ is the differentiation operator and $ R(\xi) \in \mathbb{R}^{g \times q} $ is a polynomial matrix of full row rank $ g $. This representation corresponds to a system of linear constant-coefficient differential equations, with the full row rank condition ensuring a minimal number of independent equations that uniquely characterize $ B $.
In discrete time, the formulation is analogous: $ B = \ker(R(\sigma)) = { w: \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{R}^q \mid R(\sigma) w(t) = 0 \ \forall t \in \mathbb{Z} } $, where $ \sigma $ denotes the left shift operator $ (\sigma w)(t) = w(t+1) $ and $ R(\xi) $ is again a full row rank polynomial matrix. The full row rank property guarantees that the representation is minimal and eliminates redundant constraints.
Kernel representations are unique up to equivalence under left multiplication by unimodular polynomial matrices (invertible over the polynomials with constant nonzero determinant), meaning if $ R_1(\sigma) $ and $ R_2(\sigma) $ both minimally represent $ B $, then $ R_1(\xi) = U(\xi) R_2(\xi) $ for some unimodular $ U(\xi) $. The lag of $ R $, defined as the maximum degree over the rows of $ R(\xi) $, and the degree of $ R $, the highest polynomial degree in its entries, together determine the order of the system, with the McMillan degree of $ B $ equaling the degree of $ \det(R(\xi)) $ for square full rank cases in autonomous systems.
A representative example is the scalar second-order harmonic oscillator, where $ B = \ker\left( \frac{d^2}{dt^2} + 1 \right) = { w \in C^\infty(\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{R}) \mid \left( \frac{d^2 w}{dt^2} + w \right)(t) = 0 \ \forall t } $, with trajectories $ w(t) = a \cos t + b \sin t $ for constants $ a, b $; here, $ R(\xi) = \xi^2 + 1 $ has lag and degree 2, capturing the system's order without introducing latent states.
This kernel form directly encodes high-order differential equations, allowing reduction to first-order equivalents via state-space realizations if needed, but the behavioral kernel inherently avoids explicit state variables, focusing solely on manifest trajectory constraints.

Image representations

In the behavioral approach to linear time-invariant (LTI) systems, an image representation describes the behavior $ B $ as the image of a polynomial matrix operator applied to free variables, specifically $ B = \operatorname{im} M(\sigma) $, where $ M(\xi) \in \mathbb{R}^{q \times (q-m)} $ is a polynomial matrix with full column rank, $ \sigma $ denotes the shift operator (or differential operator $ d/dt $ in continuous time), $ q $ is the total number of variables, and $ m $ is the number of output variables. This formulation generates all trajectories in the behavior by varying the latent (free) variables, emphasizing the generative aspect of the system rather than constraints. The full column rank condition on $ M $ ensures minimality, meaning the representation captures the essential dynamics without redundancy.
Every kernel representation of an LTI behavior admits an equivalent image representation, and vice versa, through the duality established in polynomial matrix theory, particularly via the Smith-McMillan canonical form, which decomposes the matrix into invariant factors that preserve the behavior. This equivalence allows for transformation between constraint-based (kernel) and generative (image) viewpoints, facilitating analysis tailored to the problem at hand; for instance, a kernel representation $ B = \ker R(\sigma) $ with $ R $ full row rank can be converted to an image form by finding a right inverse or using unimodular transformations.
Key properties of the image representation include the number of outputs $ m $, which equals the row rank of $ M $, determining the dimension of the constrained variables, while the cardinality of inputs is $ q - m $, corresponding to the column dimension and reflecting the degrees of freedom in the system. These properties highlight the structural invariants of the behavior, independent of the specific representation chosen, and are invariant under left multiplication by unimodular matrices, preserving the behavioral equivalence.
A representative example is the integrator chain, where the behavior consists of position signals generated by $ B = \operatorname{im} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ \xi \ \xi^2 \end{bmatrix} $, with $ \xi $ as the indeterminate; here, the free variable drives successive integrations, yielding trajectories where the second component is the derivative of the first and the third of the second, illustrating a second-order system with one input and two outputs.
The image representation offers advantages in distinguishing free (input-like) variables, which can be arbitrarily chosen to generate trajectories, from determined (output-like) variables, which are fully specified by the free ones and the matrix $ M $, thereby clarifying causal structures without presupposing an input-output partition. It is particularly useful for realization theory, enabling the construction of state-space models or module-theoretic realizations directly from the polynomial description, bypassing explicit state variables and focusing on trajectory generation for controllable behaviors.

Observability and Identification

Observability of latent variables

In the behavioral approach to systems theory, observability refers to the ability to uniquely determine latent variablesinternal or hidden signals that model system memory or auxiliary dynamicsfrom the observed manifest behavior, where the observed behavior $ B_{\text{obs}} $ is a subset of the full behavior $ B_{\text{full}} $ consisting of all possible trajectories. Latent variables, such as states or unobserved currents in a circuit, are introduced in an extended representation to capture the full dynamics, and observability ensures that these can be reconstructed solely from the manifest signals without ambiguity.
The formulation of observability hinges on the injectivity of the projection map from the full behavior to the observed behavior: latent variables are observable if distinct trajectories in $ B_{\text{full}} $ that agree on the observed components must be identical overall, implying no "hidden freedoms" in the system description. For a partitioned behavior with manifest signal $ w $ and latent signal $ \ell $, this means that if two pairs $ (w, \ell) $ and $ (w, \ell') $ both belong to $ B_{\text{full}} $, then $ \ell = \ell' $.
In linear time-invariant (LTI) systems, observability is characterized through kernel representations, where the full behavior is defined by a polynomial matrix equation $ R(\frac{d}{dt}) \begin{bmatrix} w \ \ell \end{bmatrix} = 0 $. The system is observable if the kernel representation of $ B_{\text{obs}} $, derived by eliminating $ \ell $, implies the full kernel equation, ensuring that the manifest behavior uniquely extends to the latent components. Mathematically, for the latent-variable form $ R(\frac{d}{dt}) w = M(\frac{d}{dt}) \ell $, observability holds if the polynomial matrix $ M(\lambda) $ has full column rank $ d $ (the dimension of $ \ell $) for all complex $ \lambda \in \mathbb{C} $, preventing non-trivial solutions for $ \ell $ that are invisible from $ w $.
This behavioral notion aligns with classical state-space observability (C-observability) in equivalent representations, where the pair $ (A, C) $ in the state-space model $ \dot{x} = A x + B u $, $ y = C x + D u $ yields a full-rank observability matrix $ \mathcal{O} = \begin{bmatrix} C \ CA \ \vdots \ CA^{n-1} \end{bmatrix} $ of rank $ n $ (the state dimension), but the behavioral framework avoids explicit state coordinates by focusing on trajectory relations. For instance, in an RLC circuit modeled with manifest port variables $ w $ (voltage and current) and latent branch variables $ \ell $ (internal voltages/currents), the latent signals are observable from $ w $ if the resistance-capacitance and inductance-resistance ratios satisfy a non-degeneracy condition like $ \frac{L}{R_L} \neq C R_C $, allowing unique reconstruction via the system equations.

System identification techniques

Behavioral identification in the behavioral systems framework involves estimating a kernel representation matrix $ R $ or an image representation generator $ M $ from observed input-output data or system trajectories. This process typically minimizes prediction errors for trajectory fitting or employs subspace methods to parameterize the system's behavior without assuming an underlying state-space structure. For instance, given a dataset of trajectories $ w_d $, the identification seeks a representation that captures the manifest behavior $ \mathcal{B} $ while ensuring consistency with the data under conditions of persistency of excitation.
A key technique for kernel representation identification applies least-squares optimization to the polynomial coefficients of $ R(\sigma) $, where $ \sigma $ denotes the shift operator in discrete time. This approach formulates the problem as fitting a linear difference equation $ R(\sigma) w = 0 $ to the data, often using structured total least-squares to account for errors in both inputs and outputs, yielding a minimal kernel representation. Adaptations of ARMA models are used for scalar behaviors, where the autoregressive-moving-average structure aligns with the polynomial kernel, solved via least-squares for coefficient estimation. The Ho-Kalman algorithm, originally for state-space realizations, is adapted in the behavioral subspace framework to construct minimal representations from Hankel matrices of impulse responses, bypassing explicit state estimation.
Subspace methods enable data-driven computation of behaviors by constructing Hankel matrices from trajectories, providing a non-parametric image representation of the behavior without latent states. These matrices, denoted $ H_L(w_d) $, span the space of all possible trajectories of length $ L $ if the input is persistently exciting of sufficient order. Willems' fundamental lemma establishes that, for a controllable linear time-invariant system, the column space of the Hankel matrix equals the behavior restricted to horizon $ L $, provided the data length satisfies $ T \geq (m+1)(L + n) - 1 $, where $ m $ is the input dimension and $ n $ the state dimension. This lemma underpins kernel-free identification, allowing direct behavioral synthesis from data via low-rank approximations of the Hankel matrix.
For example, identifying a linear system from multiple trajectories involves verifying observability through rank conditions on the data matrices, such as full column rank of the input-output Hankel matrix to ensure the behavior is fully spanned without redundancies. In a simulated scalar system with known dynamics, trajectories are concatenated into a Hankel matrix, and singular value decomposition reveals the minimal order via the number of non-zero singular values, confirming observability when the rank equals the behavioral dimension.
Advances in non-parametric identification extend these methods to behaviors without predefined parametric forms, leveraging the fundamental lemma for direct trajectory parameterization in data-driven control. Handling noise in behavioral systems theory employs regularization techniques, such as nuclear norm minimization on Hankel matrices or bounded uncertainty sets via semidefinite programming, to robustify estimates against measurement errors while preserving theoretical guarantees on approximation quality. These approaches require only mildly persistent excitation and small datasets compared to machine learning alternatives, with empirical validation on benchmarks like the air passengers dataset showing effective noise mitigation through low-rank pre-processing.

Applications and Extensions

In control theory

In the behavioral approach to control theory, the design of controllers is conceptualized through the interconnection of the plant behavior Bp(Rq)T\mathcal{B}_p \subseteq (\mathbb{R}^q)^TBp(Rq)T with a controller behavior Bc(Rp)T\mathcal{B}_c \subseteq (\mathbb{R}^p)^TBc(Rp)T, yielding a closed-loop behavior Bcl=BpBc\mathcal{B}_{cl} = \mathcal{B}_p \cap \mathcal{B}_cBcl=BpBc that restricts trajectories to those satisfying both dynamics simultaneously. This framework treats control as variable sharing or feedback interconnection, where manifest variables (e.g., inputs and outputs) are constrained to achieve desired specifications, such as stability or performance, without presupposing an input/output partition. For linear time-invariant (LTI) systems, the plant is often represented by a kernel equation R(ddt)w=0R(\frac{d}{dt}) w = 0R(dtd)w=0, and the controller enforces compatibility via intersection, as illustrated in feedback loops for systems like mass-spring-dampers.
Controllability within this setting ensures the existence of inputs capable of steering the behavior to any admissible trajectory, defined as the surjectivity of the projection from the full behavior onto the free (input) variables, allowing arbitrary specification of manifest outputs through appropriate input choices. For LTI behaviors, this property holds if the polynomial matrix R()R(\lambda)R() has constant rank for all complex \lambda, enabling the controller to manipulate the system's response freely within the behavioral constraints. In control design, controllability is essential for pole placement, where a dynamic compensator can assign closed-loop poles to stabilize the interconnection.
The Q-design approach parameterizes all stabilizing controllers via doubly coprime factorizations in the behavioral framework, mirroring the classical Youla-Kuera method but adapted to kernel or image representations of behaviors. Here, a stable behavioral parameter QQQ generates the set of all controllers that regularly implement a specified subbehavior while ensuring closed-loop stability, achieved through coprime factorizations of the plant's representation that admit left and right inverses. For instance, in feedback control of LTI systems, the behavioral Youla parameterization constructs controllers C=(X+MQ)1(Y+NQ)C = (X + MQ)^{-1}(Y + NQ)C=(X+MQ)1(Y+NQ) (in transfer function terms adapted to behaviors), where M,N,X,YM, N, X, YM,N,X,Y derive from the plant's doubly coprime factors, allowing systematic tuning of QQQ for desired closed-loop responses like disturbance rejection.
This framework uniformly addresses non-minimum phase or unstable plants by focusing on trajectory restrictions rather than transfer function inverses, avoiding singularities in factorization and facilitating extensions to decentralized control through multi-variable interconnections.

Modern developments and comparisons

Recent advances in behavioral modeling have leveraged Willems' fundamental lemma, which characterizes the behavior of controllable linear time-invariant systems through Hankel matrices of measured trajectories, enabling non-parametric representations without explicit model identification. This lemma underpins data-enabled predictive control (DeePC), a post-2010 method that directly uses input-output data for predictive control, bypassing traditional parametric modeling and facilitating learning-based control strategies in applications like process control and robotics. DeePC has demonstrated robustness in real-time feedback scenarios, achieving performance comparable to model predictive control while reducing computational demands for subspace identification.
Extensions to nonlinear behaviors have incorporated variational principles and manifold constraints, particularly through port-Hamiltonian frameworks that preserve energy dissipation properties. Arjan van der Schaft's work has advanced this by formulating nonlinear systems as interconnections of Dirac structures on manifolds, ensuring geometric consistency and passivity in behavioral descriptions. These approaches address nonlinear dynamics by embedding behaviors in symplectic or Poisson manifolds, allowing for modular composition without relying on local linearizations.
Compared to state-space models, the behavioral approach avoids reliance on unobservable latent variables, focusing instead on manifest trajectories, which enhances robustness to model mismatches in uncertain environments. Relative to input-output paradigms, it dispenses with a priori causality assumptions, treating variables symmetrically to capture bidirectional influences more flexibly. The behavioral framework complements port-Hamiltonian systems for energy-based modeling, where the former provides trajectory-level abstraction while the latter enforces physical structure like dissipation inequalities.
Since 2015, integrations with machine learning have expanded behavioral modeling to data-driven control in robotics, using trajectory datasets for direct policy learning akin to behavioral cloning, improving adaptability in dynamic tasks like manipulation. Scalability to large networks has advanced through behavioral parametrizations of interconnected systems, enabling efficient analysis of graph-structured dynamics in power grids and sensor arrays without exponential complexity. These developments address gaps in hybrid systems via l-complete approximations for supervisory control, merging discrete events with continuous behaviors, and in stochastic settings through series expansions that extend the fundamental lemma to noisy trajectories.
