Evolution is the process by which populations of living organisms change over successive generations through variations in heritable characteristics. It is the central organizing principle of biology, the framework that connects every fact about living things into a coherent story that spans four billion years and encompasses every organism that has ever lived on Earth. Nothing in biology makes sense except in the light of evolution, as the geneticist Theodosius Dobzhansky famously stated, and understanding evolution is essential to understanding medicine, agriculture, ecology, genetics, and the human condition itself.

Charles Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace independently arrived at the theory of evolution by natural selection in the mid-nineteenth century, though Darwin is more widely remembered because of his exhaustive work "On the Origin of Species," published in 1859. The theory rests on four observations that are individually unremarkable but collectively revolutionary.

First, organisms produce more offspring than can survive. A single oak tree produces millions of acorns in its lifetime. A single salmon lays thousands of eggs. A single bacterium can divide every twenty minutes, theoretically producing billions of descendants in a single day. If every offspring survived and reproduced, any species would quickly overwhelm the Earth.

Second, individuals within a population vary. No two organisms are exactly alike, even within the same species. Some are taller, some are faster, some are more resistant to disease, some are better camouflaged, some are more efficient at extracting nutrients from their food. This variation is the raw material of evolution.

Third, much of this variation is heritable. The traits that make one individual different from another are often passed from parents to offspring through genes. Tall parents tend to have tall children. Disease-resistant organisms tend to produce disease-resistant offspring.

Fourth, individuals with traits that are better suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce. This is natural selection: the environment selects which variations are beneficial and which are harmful, and the beneficial variations become more common in the population over time because the individuals who carry them leave more offspring. Over many generations, this process can produce dramatic changes in a population's characteristics. Over millions of years, it can produce entirely new species.

The mechanism of heredity was unknown to Darwin. It was elucidated by Gregor Mendel through his experiments with pea plants in the 1860s, though Mendel's work was not widely recognized until 1900. The modern synthesis, which merged Darwinian natural selection with Mendelian genetics in the 1930s and 1940s, established that evolution occurs through changes in the frequency of alleles, different versions of genes, in a population. Mutations, which are random changes in DNA, produce new alleles. Natural selection, genetic drift, gene flow, and sexual selection change the frequencies of these alleles over time.

The evidence for evolution is overwhelming and comes from multiple independent sources. The fossil record documents the history of life on Earth over billions of years, showing a progression from simple, single-celled organisms to complex, multicellular ones, with transitional forms that bridge major groups. Comparative anatomy reveals that organisms that appear very different share underlying structural similarities, called homologies, that indicate common ancestry. The forelimb of a human, the wing of a bat, the flipper of a whale, and the leg of a horse all contain the same bones arranged in the same pattern, modified for different functions but derived from the same ancestral structure.

Molecular biology provides perhaps the most powerful evidence for evolution. All living organisms use DNA as their genetic material, use the same genetic code to translate DNA into proteins, and share many of the same genes. The degree of genetic similarity between species corresponds to their evolutionary relatedness: humans and chimpanzees share approximately ninety-eight percent of their DNA, humans and mice share approximately eighty-five percent, and humans and bananas share approximately sixty percent. These molecular similarities make no sense except as the product of common descent from shared ancestors.

Biogeography, the study of the geographic distribution of species, provides additional evidence. Oceanic islands have species that are found nowhere else on Earth but are closely related to species on the nearest mainland, consistent with colonization followed by independent evolution. The unique species of the Galapagos Islands, including the finches that inspired Darwin's thinking, are closely related to South American species despite being found on volcanic islands that rose from the ocean floor and were never connected to the mainland.

Evolution is not just a historical process. It is happening right now, all around us, and it affects our daily lives in profound ways. The evolution of antibiotic resistance in bacteria is one of the most pressing public health challenges of the twenty-first century. When antibiotics are used, they kill susceptible bacteria but leave resistant ones to survive and reproduce. Over time, the resistant bacteria become dominant, and the antibiotic becomes ineffective. This is natural selection in action, occurring in hospitals and clinics rather than in nature, and it demonstrates the speed and power of evolutionary processes.

Viruses evolve rapidly because they reproduce quickly and have high mutation rates. The influenza virus evolves so fast that a new vaccine must be developed every year. The HIV virus evolves within individual patients, making it difficult for the immune system to control and challenging to treat. The emergence of new viral strains, including the SARS-CoV-2 variants that drove successive waves of the COVID-19 pandemic, is a direct consequence of viral evolution.

Human evolution is one of the most fascinating chapters in the evolutionary story. The human lineage diverged from the lineage that produced modern chimpanzees approximately six to seven million years ago in Africa. Over the following millions of years, a succession of hominin species evolved, each with progressively larger brains, more upright posture, and more sophisticated tool use. Homo sapiens, our species, emerged approximately three hundred thousand years ago in Africa. We are the last surviving species in a lineage that once included multiple coexisting hominin species, including the Neanderthals and the Denisovans, with whom our ancestors interbred. Modern humans of non-African descent carry approximately one to four percent Neanderthal DNA, a molecular remnant of these ancient encounters.

Evolution does not have a direction. It does not progress toward a goal. It does not make organisms more complex or more intelligent over time as a general rule. It simply adapts populations to their environments through the differential survival and reproduction of individuals with beneficial variations. Bacteria, which have been evolving for nearly four billion years, are not less evolved than humans. They are differently evolved, exquisitely adapted to their particular niches, and they will almost certainly outlast our species by billions of years.

Evolution is the deepest truth biology has discovered. It explains the unity of life, why all organisms share the same genetic code. It explains the diversity of life, why there are millions of species adapted to every conceivable environment. It explains the imperfection of life, why organisms have vestigial structures and suboptimal designs, because evolution works by modifying what already exists rather than designing from scratch. And it explains the beauty of life, the astonishing intricacy of a butterfly wing, the perfect hydrodynamics of a shark, the impossible architecture of a spider web, because natural selection is the most powerful creative force the universe has ever produced, given enough time.
