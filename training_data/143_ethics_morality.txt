Ethics is the branch of philosophy that asks what we ought to do. It is the study of right and wrong, good and evil, virtue and vice, duty and obligation. Every decision you make that affects another person is, at its core, an ethical decision. Should I keep the extra change the cashier gave me by mistake? Should I tell my friend that their partner is cheating? Should I report my colleague for padding their expense report? Should I donate money to charity or save it for my family? These are not just practical questions. They are moral questions, and how you answer them reveals your values, your character, and your understanding of what it means to live a good life.

There are several major ethical frameworks that philosophers have developed over thousands of years, each offering a different lens through which to evaluate moral decisions.

Consequentialism, the most influential version of which is utilitarianism, judges actions by their outcomes. An action is right if it produces the greatest good for the greatest number of people. The moral worth of an act lies not in the act itself or in the intention behind it but in its consequences. Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill, the founders of utilitarianism, argued that the right action in any situation is the one that maximizes overall happiness and minimizes overall suffering. This framework is intuitively appealing because it reduces morality to a simple calculus: add up the benefits, subtract the costs, and choose the option with the highest net benefit.

But utilitarianism has troubling implications. It could justify harming one innocent person to save five, because the total happiness is greater. It could justify lying if the lie produces better outcomes than the truth. It could justify violating individual rights if doing so benefits the majority. And it requires the impossible task of predicting all the consequences of an action, which is often unknowable in advance.

Deontological ethics, most famously articulated by Immanuel Kant, judges actions by whether they conform to moral rules or duties, regardless of their consequences. Kant argued that certain actions are inherently right or wrong, independent of their outcomes. Lying is wrong even if it produces good consequences. Keeping promises is right even if breaking them would produce better outcomes. Kant's categorical imperative states that you should act only according to rules that you could will to be universal laws. In other words: before you act, ask yourself, "What if everyone did this?" If the answer leads to a contradiction or an unacceptable world, the action is wrong.

Virtue ethics, rooted in the philosophy of Aristotle, focuses not on actions or their consequences but on the character of the person performing them. A virtuous person, one who has cultivated qualities like courage, honesty, compassion, justice, temperance, and wisdom, will naturally make good moral decisions because their character disposes them toward the right action. Virtue ethics asks not "What should I do?" but "What kind of person should I be?" and argues that the right actions flow naturally from the right character.

Care ethics, developed by Carol Gilligan and Nel Noddings, emphasizes the importance of relationships and care in moral decision-making. Traditional ethical theories tend to be abstract and impartial, treating moral problems as if they could be solved by applying universal principles to generic situations. Care ethics argues that morality is fundamentally relational: our moral obligations arise from our connections to specific people, and the right action is the one that maintains, strengthens, and honors those connections. A mother's obligation to her child is not the same as her obligation to a stranger, and recognizing this difference is essential to ethical thinking.

Moral dilemmas arise when two or more ethical principles conflict. The trolley problem, perhaps the most famous thought experiment in ethics, presents a scenario in which a runaway trolley is heading toward five people who will be killed unless you divert it to a side track where it will kill one person. Utilitarianism says divert the trolley: five lives saved versus one lost. Deontological ethics says you must not actively cause the death of the one person, even to save five. The thought experiment reveals that our moral intuitions are often inconsistent and that no single ethical framework captures all of our moral beliefs.

The concept of human rights represents an attempt to establish universal moral principles that transcend cultural, religious, and political differences. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights, adopted by the United Nations in 1948, asserts that all human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights, and it enumerates specific rights including the right to life, liberty, and security of person; the right to freedom of thought, conscience, and religion; the right to education; and the right to freedom from torture, slavery, and arbitrary detention. These rights are considered universal, meaning they apply to every person regardless of nationality, ethnicity, religion, gender, or any other characteristic.

Moral progress is a contested concept. Are we morally better than our ancestors? In some ways, clearly yes: slavery has been abolished in every country, women have gained legal equality in most societies, racial discrimination has been outlawed in most democracies, and the concept of universal human rights has been established as a global norm. But in other ways, our moral progress is less clear: we tolerate extreme poverty in the midst of extraordinary wealth, we are destroying the environment that sustains us, we have developed weapons capable of ending civilization, and we often treat the suffering of distant others as less important than the comfort of those nearby.

The fundamental question of ethics is not a question that can be answered once and for all. It is a question that every person must confront repeatedly throughout their life, in every situation where their actions affect another person. Ethics is not about having the right answers. It is about asking the right questions, with honesty, empathy, and the willingness to be uncomfortable with the complexity of a world in which doing the right thing is rarely simple and rarely free of cost.
