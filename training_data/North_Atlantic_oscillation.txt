North Atlantic oscillation

 The North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) is a prominent mode of atmospheric variability over the North Atlantic Ocean, defined by fluctuations in the difference of sea-level pressure between the Azores High near the Azores and the Icelandic Low near Iceland. This oscillation arises from shifts in the strength and position of these semi-permanent pressure centers, influencing the position and intensity of the North Atlantic storm track and jet stream.
The NAO exhibits two primary phases: a positive phase, characterized by a strengthened pressure gradient with below-normal heights over high latitudes and above-normal pressures over the central North Atlantic, eastern United States, and western Europe; and a negative phase, featuring the opposite pattern with a weakened gradient. The index is typically measured using the projection of the NAO loading pattern onto daily or monthly anomalies of 500-millibar geopotential heights from 0 to 90N, derived from rotated empirical orthogonal function analysis of data spanning 19502000. These phases vary on interannual and interdecadal timescales, with prolonged episodes lasting several months influencing broader climate patterns.
In its positive phase, the NAO drives milder, wetter winters in northern Europe and the eastern United States, with above-normal temperatures across these regions and increased precipitation in Scandinavia, while southern Europe and Greenland experience cooler, drier conditions. Conversely, the negative phase promotes colder, stormier weather over northern Europe and the eastern United States, with enhanced snowfall in the mid-Atlantic states, drier conditions in northern Europe, and wetter weather in the Mediterranean. Beyond Europe and North America, the NAO affects storm tracks across the Atlantic, modulates heat and moisture transport, and can influence ecosystems, such as extending growing seasons in Sweden by up to 20 days during positive phases or boosting agricultural yields in southern Europe during negative ones. The phenomenon is most pronounced from December to March, underscoring its role as a key driver of winter climate variability in the North Atlantic sector.

Fundamentals

Definition

The North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) is the dominant mode of atmospheric variability over the North Atlantic sector, characterized by opposing fluctuations in the strength and location of the subtropical high-pressure system, known as the Azores High, and the subpolar low-pressure system, referred to as the Icelandic Low. This large-scale seesaw pattern in sea-level pressure drives variations in the intensity and path of mid-latitude storms and the position of the polar front jet stream.
The NAO alternates between positive and negative phases based on the relative strengths of these pressure centers. In the positive phase, the pressure contrast between the Azores High and Icelandic Low intensifies, strengthening the westerly winds and promoting a more zonal (west-to-east) flow across the North Atlantic. Conversely, the negative phase features a diminished pressure difference, weakening the westerlies and favoring more meridional (north-south) airflow with increased variability in storm tracks.
Spatially, the NAO exerts influence across the mid-latitudes of the Northern Hemisphere, primarily affecting regions from eastern North America through the North Atlantic to western Europe. It represents a regional manifestation of the hemispheric-scale Arctic Oscillation (AO), which involves similar pressure anomalies but extends to the North Pacific as well.

Historical Background

The earliest scientific recognition of the pressure fluctuations characteristic of the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) emerged in the 19th century among Scandinavian meteorologists, who observed a seesaw-like variation in atmospheric pressure between the region near Iceland and the Azores. Swedish meteorologist Hugo Hildebrandsson, in 1897, explicitly linked these pressure anomalies to broader circulation patterns, building on earlier qualitative descriptions of temperature contrasts across the North Atlantic. French meteorologist Teisserenc de Bort had earlier (1883) conceptualized such features as "centres d'action," influencing the understanding of semi-permanent pressure systems. German physicist Heinrich Wilhelm Dove further substantiated the temperature seesaw between Greenland and Europe in 1839 and 1841 through systematic data analysis. These observations laid the groundwork for viewing the NAO as a recurring atmospheric dipole affecting mid-latitude weather.
The formal naming and initial quantification of the NAO occurred in the 1920s as part of broader studies on global atmospheric oscillations. British meteorologist Sir Gilbert Walker coined the term "North Atlantic Oscillation" in 1924 while investigating large-scale pressure variations, and he and Edwin Bliss developed a quantitative index in 1932 based on sea-level pressure differences between Iceland and the Azores. Austrian meteorologist Felix Exner contributed to mapping the spatial structure of this pattern in 1913 and 1924, emphasizing its role in hemispheric circulation. In the 1930s and 1940s, Swedish-American meteorologist Carl-Gustaf Rossby refined the understanding through analyses of North Atlantic storm tracks and zonal circulation intensity, proposing in 1939 that variations in the zonal flow were tied to displacements of semi-permanent pressure centers, including those central to the NAO.
Following World War II, the NAO gained integration into emerging numerical weather prediction models during the 1950s and 1960s, as improved observational networks and computational capabilities allowed for simulations of large-scale teleconnection patterns like the NAO's dipole. A pivotal advancement came in 1978 with the work of Harry van Loon and Jeffery C. Rogers, who established the NAO as a distinct mode of variability through detailed analyses of winter temperature seesaws and associated atmospheric effects, highlighting its separation from other hemispheric patterns.
The terminology evolved in the 1980s, shifting from earlier descriptors like "North Atlantic seesaw"popularized in van Loon and Rogers' studiesto the standardized "North Atlantic Oscillation," reinforced by empirical orthogonal function analyses that identified it as the leading mode of Northern Hemisphere variability. John M. Wallace and David S. Gutzler, in 1981, formalized this through their identification of key teleconnection patterns, cementing the NAO's prominence in modern climatology.

Atmospheric Dynamics

Key Pressure Systems

The Azores High, also known as the subtropical high, is a semi-permanent anticyclone centered over the subtropical North Atlantic Ocean near the Azores archipelago, typically spanning latitudes from 25 to 35N. This high-pressure system arises from the descending branch of the Hadley cell circulation and plays a crucial role in the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) by influencing the position and intensity of mid-latitude westerlies.
In the positive phase of the NAO, the Azores High intensifies, leading to higher-than-average sea-level pressures that strengthen the northeasterly trade winds across the subtropical Atlantic and reinforce the subtropical jet stream, thereby promoting a more zonal (west-to-east) atmospheric flow.
The Icelandic Low, a semi-permanent subpolar cyclone, is centered near Iceland and extends toward Greenland, generally between 60 and 70N, driven by the ascending branch of the Ferrel cell and enhanced by cold polar air outbreaks. This low-pressure feature contributes to cyclogenesis and storm track development in the North Atlantic.
During the positive NAO phase, the Icelandic Low deepens with lower sea-level pressures, which amplifies baroclinic instability and increases storminess over the northern North Atlantic, steering mid-latitude cyclones along a more northerly path.
The core mechanics of the NAO revolve around the meridional pressure gradient between these two systems, often quantified as the difference in normalized sea-level pressure (SLP) between Lisbon, Portugal (representing the Azores High influence) and Stykkisholmur, Iceland (representing the Icelandic Low). This station-based index, developed by Hurrell, captures the oscillation's variability, with typical wintertime gradients averaging around 20 hPa, though fluctuations can range from 10 to 30 hPa depending on the phase strength.
The NAO's pressure gradient is most pronounced during the winter months (November to March), when enhanced thermal contrasts between the warm subtropical Atlantic and cold Arctic air masses drive greater SLP variability and more intense interactions between the Azores High and Icelandic Low.

Teleconnection Patterns

The North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) functions as a large-scale dipole in sea-level pressure anomalies, exciting planetary Rossby waves that propagate eastward and influence remote atmospheric circulation patterns through energy transfer and wave dispersion. These teleconnections arise from the interaction between the NAO's pressure centersthe deepened Icelandic Low and strengthened Azores High during the positive phaseand the midlatitude waveguide provided by the subtropical jet, allowing Rossby waves to alter the amplitude and trajectory of the polar front jet stream across the North Atlantic sector and beyond. This propagation mechanism links regional pressure variations to hemispheric-scale adjustments in geopotential height fields, as evidenced by downstream extensions of NAO signals into the Eurasian continent via guided Rossby wave trains.
In the positive NAO phase, the enhanced meridional pressure gradient intensifies and shifts the North Atlantic jet stream eastward toward the Mediterranean and poleward into higher latitudes, favoring a predominantly zonal flow regime that minimizes persistent high-pressure blocking anticyclones over Greenland and Scandinavia. This configuration reduces wave breaking and promotes efficient momentum transport equatorward, stabilizing the jet's path. During the negative phase, the weakened gradient allows the jet to meander southward, amplifying meridional flow components that foster high-latitude blocking patterns, such as the Greenland Blocking Index highs, and facilitate the advection of cold Arctic air masses into lower latitudes through amplified Rossby wave troughs. These phase-dependent shifts in jet position and orientation directly modulate the frequency and persistence of blocking events, with negative phases substantially increasing their occurrence (approximately 67% more blocking days) in winter.
The NAO shares partial spatial overlap with the Pacific-North American (PNA) teleconnection pattern, particularly in their mutual modulation of midlatitude jet variability and blocking across the Northern Hemisphere, where negative NAO and positive PNA phases often co-occur to enhance trans-Pacific wave trains influencing North American weather. This linkage reflects a common Rossby wave source in the midlatitudes, with temporal correlations between the indices reaching 0.3-0.5 during winter, though the NAO remains more Atlantic-centric. For diagnosing Rossby wave propagation in NAO events, the Takaya-Nakamura formulation of wave-activity flux offers a phase-independent diagnostic, capturing the three-dimensional energy flux of quasigeostrophic eddies relative to a zonally asymmetric basic state; it visualizes wave packets as "plumes" of activity diverging from the NAO dipole, without dependence on instantaneous wave phase, thereby highlighting upstream sources and downstream dispersion paths.
Vertically, the NAO's influence penetrates from the troposphere into the stratosphere, where its negative phase correlates with weakened westerly stratospheric winds and disruptions to the polar night jet, often preceding sudden stratospheric warmings (SSWs) by 10-30 days. SSWs involve the rapid breakdown of the stratospheric polar vortex due to enhanced upward-propagating planetary waves from the tropospheric NAO dipole, leading to temperature rises of 40-50 K at 10 hPa and vortex displacements or splits. This stratospheric extension underscores the two-way coupling between NAO variability and upper-level dynamics, with tropospheric precursors amplifying wave flux into the stratosphere during negative phases.

Indices and Variability

NAO Index Calculation

The North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) index quantifies the strength and phase of the oscillation through two primary methods: a station-based approach and an empirical orthogonal function (EOF) or principal component (PC) analysis. The station-based index relies on observed sea level pressure (SLP) differences between representative stations for the subtropical high and subpolar low pressure centers. It is calculated as the difference between normalized SLP at a southern station near the Azores or Gibraltar (approximately 37N, 8W) and a northern station in Iceland (approximately 65N, 18W), using the formula
NAO=SLPsouthSLPnorth,
\text{NAO} = \text{SLP}_{\text{south}} - \text{SLP}_{\text{north}},
NAO=SLPsouthSLPnorth,
where SLP values are anomalies normalized by their long-term standard deviations before subtraction to facilitate comparison across periods. This method produces daily, monthly, or seasonal indices, with the winter (DecemberMarch) average commonly used to capture the oscillation's peak variability.
The EOF or principal component method provides a more spatially comprehensive index by performing EOF analysis on SLP anomaly fields over the North Atlantic sector, typically the domain from 20N to 70N and 90W to 40E. The leading EOF mode captures the dipole pattern of the NAO, and its associated principal component time series (PC1) serves as the index, explaining approximately 4050% of the total SLP variance in winter months over this region. This approach weights contributions from multiple grid points, making it less sensitive to individual station data quality than the station-based method.
Both methods draw from reanalysis datasets for consistent, gridded SLP fields; the NCEP/NCAR Reanalysis provides monthly data from 1948 onward, while the HadSLP2 dataset extends historical coverage back to 1854 at 5 5 resolution using station and marine observations. Indices are standardized by dividing by the climatological standard deviation (often over 19512000 or the full record) to remove secular trends and express variability in units of standard deviations. The NAO phase is classified as positive when the index exceeds +1 standard deviation and negative when below -1 standard deviation, with neutral conditions in between.

Temporal Variability

The North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) exhibits a pronounced seasonal cycle, with its variability peaking during the winter months and diminishing in summer. The NAO pattern is strongest from January to March, when the pressure dipole between the Icelandic Low and the Azores High reaches maximum intensity, driving significant atmospheric teleconnections across the North Atlantic. In contrast, summer NAO phases are weaker, contributing over 20% of the variance in seasonal mean sea-level pressure over the Euro-Atlantic sector but with reduced amplitude and shifted nodal positions westward and northward into the central North Atlantic. This seasonal modulation arises from changes in storm track activity and baroclinic instability, which are more vigorous in winter.
On interannual timescales, the NAO displays substantial fluctuations, with a standard deviation of approximately 10 hPa in the winter sea-level pressure difference defining its typical variability. These variations manifest as prolonged positive or negative phases lasting several months, influencing mid-latitude weather patterns. The El Nio-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) modulates this interannual behavior, as negative NAO phases are more frequent during El Nio winters due to teleconnections that alter the North Atlantic pressure dipole through tropospheric pathways. For instance, El Nio events often promote a weakened subtropical high and intensified subpolar low, favoring negative NAO conditions in late winter.
Decadal and multidecadal NAO trends reflect longer-term oscillations, including a positive phase shift from the 1960s to the early 1990s, followed by a decline through the 2010s. This upward trend in the late 20th century, marked by stronger westerlies and milder European winters, aligns with the positive phase of the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO), a sea surface temperature variability cycle spanning 6080 years that influences North Atlantic atmospheric circulation. The subsequent downturn correlates with the AMO's transition toward neutrality, contributing to shifts in precipitation and temperature anomalies across the basin. Earlier records indicate a weaker positive trend from the 1890s onward, underscoring the oscillatory nature of these low-frequency changes.
In the 2020s, NAO variability has shown signs of increased volatility, with wider distributions in the summer index and more frequent extreme phases, potentially linked to Arctic amplification enhancing stratospheric-tropospheric interactions. However, no sustained long-term strengthening of the NAO has been observed, as multidecadal trends remain subdued amid ongoing AMO neutrality. From 2010 to 2024, the index exhibited mostly positive values but with heightened interannual swings, consistent with post-2000 elevations in sliding standard deviations. These patterns highlight the NAO's sensitivity to Arctic warming without clear directional trends in overall amplitude.

Regional Impacts

Weather Effects on Europe and North America

The North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) exerts profound influences on winter weather patterns across Europe and North America by modulating atmospheric circulation, leading to distinct temperature, precipitation, and storminess anomalies tied to its positive and negative phases. These effects arise primarily from variations in the strength and position of mid-latitude westerlies, which transport heat and moisture across the Atlantic basin. During strong phases, anomalies can deviate markedly from climatological norms in affected regions over the course of the season.
In the positive NAO phase, strengthened storm tracks across the Atlantic deliver mild, wet winters to northern Europe, including increased precipitation and above-average temperatures over Scandinavia and the British Isles due to enhanced southerly flow and frequent cyclones. Conversely, this phase brings cold, stormy conditions to Greenland and eastern Canada, where deepened lows and northerly winds amplify below-normal temperatures and heightened precipitation, often as snow. In North America, positive NAO conditions favor warmer, drier weather in the U.S. Northeast, reducing cold air outbreaks and storm frequency through higher surface pressures that suppress incursions from polar regions. Typical sea level pressure (SLP) maps for this phase show a deepened Icelandic Low and intensified Azores High, while 500 hPa height anomalies reveal a northward-shifted ridge over the North Atlantic.
The negative NAO phase reverses these patterns, producing cold, dry winters in northern Europe as blocking highs over Scandinavia divert storms southward, limiting moisture advection and fostering persistent easterly flows that cool the region. Southern Europe experiences milder, wetter conditions with increased rainfall from redirected cyclone paths, while the eastern U.S. sees enhanced precipitation and above-normal temperatures in its southern reaches. For North America more broadly, negative phases amplify cold air outbreaks from Canada into the U.S. Northeast and Midwest, leading to below-normal temperatures and greater storminess as lower pressures facilitate Arctic air advection. SLP anomaly maps depict a weakened pressure dipole with high anomalies over Greenland, and 500 hPa patterns exhibit a southward-displaced trough and amplified blocking. These phases also correspond to shifts in the North Atlantic jet stream, with positive conditions strengthening and displacing it northward.

Oceanic and Sea Level Effects

The North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) exerts a profound influence on oceanic circulation and heat transport within the North Atlantic basin, primarily through its modulation of wind patterns and associated atmospheric forcing. In the positive phase, strengthened westerly winds enhance the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) by facilitating greater heat extraction from the subpolar gyre, which promotes intensified deep convection and increases the horizontal density gradients driving the gyre. This strengthening of the North Atlantic Current results in increased northward heat transport, leading to warming of the subpolar gyre and broader redistribution of heat toward higher latitudes. Conversely, during the negative phase, weakened winds reduce deep-water formation in the Labrador Sea, diminishing the AMOC strength and causing cooling in that region due to diminished heat advection.
Associated with these circulation changes, the NAO induces distinctive sea surface temperature (SST) anomalies across the North Atlantic, manifesting as a tripole pattern. The positive phase features cooling in the subpolar North Atlantic south of Greenland, warming along the path of the Gulf Stream extension toward the Nordic Seas, and additional warming in the subtropical and eastern mid-latitude basins. These anomalies develop through wind-induced alterations in surface heat fluxes, Ekman transport, and oceanic advection, with the eastern basin and subtropics experiencing net warming due to reduced cooling by northerly winds. In the negative phase, the pattern reverses, promoting warming in the subpolar region and cooling elsewhere, which can persist through reemergence mechanisms in the subsurface ocean.
The NAO also impacts coastal sea levels via wind-driven dynamics, inverse barometer responses to pressure anomalies, and adjustments in basin-wide circulation. Along the U.S. East Coast, particularly north of Cape Hatteras, the positive phase correlates with lowered sea levels owing to intensified westerly winds that induce offshore Ekman transport and coastal upwelling. This effect has strengthened since the late 1980s, enhancing the negative correlation between the NAO index and regional sea level anomalies. In northern Europe, the positive phase often results in reduced mean sea levels due to the inverse barometer effect from higher subtropical pressures, though storm surges can vary locally. The negative phase reverses these patterns, elevating U.S. East Coast sea levels through diminished wind stress and onshore transport, as evidenced by the ~10 cm rise during the extreme 20092010 event.
On longer timescales, NAO variability contributes to multidecadal fluctuations in North Atlantic sea level, driven by persistent AMOC adjustments and steric changes. Such variability underscores the NAO's role in modulating coastal hazards and the predictability of sea level changes up to a decade in advance.

Broader Consequences

Influence on Atlantic Hurricanes

The North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) modulates tropical cyclone activity in the North Atlantic basin primarily through its influence on atmospheric circulation and sea surface temperatures (SSTs), with the spring phase exerting a persistent effect into the hurricane season. A negative spring NAO weakens the trade winds across the subtropical North Atlantic, reducing vertical wind shear in the main development region (MDR, spanning 1020N, 2080W) and promoting warmer SSTs that favor cyclone genesis and intensification. Conversely, a positive spring NAO strengthens the trade winds, enhancing wind shear and cooling SSTs in the MDR, which suppresses formation and steers developing storms northward into cooler subtropical waters where intensification is limited.
During the negative NAO phase, the resulting weaker deep-tropospheric wind shear (200850 hPa) across the MDR and Caribbean Sea creates more favorable conditions for hurricane development, leading to 30%40% higher track density in the western North Atlantic, Gulf of Mexico, and Caribbean compared to the positive phase. Higher SSTs in the MDR, often exceeding 28C, provide the thermal energy necessary for rapid intensification, while reduced shear allows storms to maintain vertical alignment and draw energy from the ocean surface. This phase also shifts storm tracks westward, increasing the likelihood of landfalls in the Caribbean and U.S. Gulf Coast. In contrast, the positive phase amplifies shear levels above 10 m/s in the MDR, disrupting storm organization, and promotes anticyclonic circulation that recurve storms northeastward, reducing their duration over warm waters and overall basin activity.
The NAO's influence is most pronounced during the early (JuneJuly) and late (OctoberNovember) portions of the hurricane season, when shear and SST anomalies from spring persist, correlating with higher accumulated cyclone energy (ACE) in years following negative phases. Negative spring NAO conditions often precede active seasons by exciting a positive Atlantic Meridional Mode that sustains low shear and high moisture through summer. For instance, the record-active 2005 season, with 28 named storms, 15 hurricanes, and an ACE of 285.4 10^4 ktthe highest on recordfollowed a strongly negative spring NAO (MarchMay index -1.5), which contributed to anomalously low shear and warm MDR SSTs around 29C. In comparison, the inactive 2013 season, with only 14 named storms, 2 hurricanes, and an ACE of 31.1 10^4 ktthe lowest since 1994occurred after a predominantly positive spring NAO, enhancing shear and cooling the MDR.

Ecological and Environmental Effects

The North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) significantly influences marine ecosystems, particularly through its modulation of ocean circulation and nutrient dynamics. In its positive phase, the NAO strengthens westerly winds and promotes the inflow of nutrient-rich Atlantic water into the North Sea, enhancing vertical mixing and upwelling that support increased phytoplankton biomass and earlier plankton blooms. For instance, during high positive NAO winters, such as in 1989, phytoplankton levels in the North Sea reached nearly three standard deviations above the long-term mean, fostering exceptional blooms of species like Thalassiothrix longissima. Conversely, the negative NAO phase reduces this inflow, leading to diminished nutrient availability and suppressed primary production across the region. In the Barents Sea, the negative phase correlates with cooler sea temperatures and reduced advection of warm Atlantic water, resulting in lower cod (Gadus morhua) recruitment and potential stock declines due to decreased larval survival and food scarcity. The NAO explains up to 53% of the interannual variability in Northeast Arctic cod recruitment, with stronger year classes observed during positive phases when warmer conditions expand suitable habitat and boost zooplankton prey like Calanus finmarchicus.
On land, NAO-driven climate variability alters terrestrial ecosystems, notably affecting avian migration and forest dynamics in Europe. Positive NAO winters bring milder temperatures and increased precipitation to western and central Europe, advancing the spring arrival of short-distance migratory birds by facilitating earlier breeding conditions and reducing migration risks. For example, analyses of 109 years of phenological data from the Czech Republic show that species wintering in Europe or North Africa, such as the skylark (Alauda arvensis) and starling (Sturnus vulgaris), arrive significantly earlier following positive NAO indices, with migration distance explaining 6872% of the observed NAO-arrival correlation. In Scandinavian forests, particularly for Scots pine (Pinus sylvestris), positive early-winter NAO phases correlate with enhanced radial growth (up to r = 0.30 in eastern subregions) through wetter conditions that promote soil moisture and reduce drought stress, while negative spring NAO phases delay snowmelt and limit growth by constraining early-season photosynthesis. These relationships have weakened since the 1930s, possibly due to shifting precipitation patterns influenced by broader climate trends.
Biodiversity in European ecosystems exhibits marked sensitivity to NAO phases, with implications for pest dynamics and fishery productivity. Negative NAO conditions, characterized by milder and wetter winters in southern Europe, enhance the survival of forest pests like the pine processionary moth (Thaumetopoea pityocampa), leading to increased defoliation outbreaks in Mediterranean pine forests up to three years later. In Spain, for instance, negative NAO indices from 19922009 were associated with higher defoliation rates in species such as Aleppo pine (Pinus halepensis), underscoring the NAO's predictive value for pest management. Fishery yields in the North Atlantic display substantial fluctuations tied to NAO variability, with cod recruitment varying by factors of up to 15-fold between strong and weak years, largely attributable to phase-dependent changes in temperature and prey abundance. Similar oscillations affect other species, such as herring (Clupea harengus) and sardines (Sardina pilchardus), where low NAO phases can double yields in certain regions through altered ocean conditions.
Broader environmental effects of the NAO include amplified soil moisture extremes that impact agriculture and accelerate Arctic ice dynamics. Positive winter NAO phases elevate summer potential soil moisture deficits by about 18% in parts of Europe, such as the UK, reducing herbage growth rates by nearly half and accounting for 22% of interannual agricultural variability through hotter, drier conditions. In the Arctic, negative summer NAO phases promote anticyclonic circulation, raising tropospheric temperatures and downwelling longwave radiation, which has driven accelerated sea ice melt rates of 11.3% per decade from the 1990s to 2012. This thermodynamic forcing enhances ice-albedo feedback, contributing to regional warming and ecosystem shifts beyond direct weather anomalies.

Notable Events

Winter 200910 in Europe

The winter of 200910 marked one of the most extreme negative phases of the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) on record, with the seasonal NAO index averaging -1.7, the lowest value since 1950, and reaching -2.38 standard deviations below the mean for December 2009 through February 2010. This persistent negative state was driven by a blocking high-pressure system over Greenland, which weakened the typical westerly winds across the North Atlantic and redirected cold Arctic air southward into Europe. The event's intensity was further amplified by a major sudden stratospheric warming (SSW) in late December 2009, which split the polar vortex and propagated downward influences to the troposphere, sustaining the negative NAO pattern through January.
Weather anomalies across Europe were severe, featuring prolonged cold snaps and heavy snowfall that deviated sharply from the long-term warming trend. Temperatures plummeted to -20C or lower in parts of the United Kingdom and France during early January 2010, with the UK recording its coldest winter since 197879 at a mean of 1.5C. Heavy snow accumulations, exceeding 30 cm in many regions, caused widespread transport disruptions, including the closure of major airports, railways, and highways from the Iberian Peninsula to Scandinavia. In contrast, while the negative NAO typically favors milder conditions along the U.S. East Coast, this event's extremity led to relatively cooler but less disruptive weather there compared to Europe's extremes.
Societal impacts were substantial, with heightened energy demands straining power grids and increasing natural gas consumption by up to 50% in some countries. Travel disruptions alone contributed to economic losses estimated at around 5 billion across Europe, encompassing costs from delayed shipments, school closures, and agricultural damage. Health effects included hundreds of cold-related deaths, with over 100 reported in December 2009 alone and a total exceeding 120 in Poland by mid-winter, primarily among the elderly and vulnerable populations.

Winter 201516 in Europe

The winter of 201516 was characterized by a predominantly positive phase of the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO), with the DecemberFebruary (DJF) index reaching +2.33, one of the strongest positive values in recent decades. This phase strengthened the westerly winds across the North Atlantic, directing mild, moist air masses toward northwestern Europe, resulting in above-average temperatures and enhanced storm activity. Across Europe, the season ranked as the warmest winter on record at the time, with surface air temperatures approximately 2.0C above the 19812010 climatological average. In the United Kingdom, the mean winter temperature was 5.5C, the third mildest since records began, while Ireland recorded 7.0C, its third warmest winter since 1901.
Precipitation was markedly elevated in northern and western regions due to the positive NAO's influence on cyclonic activity, contrasting with drier conditions farther south. The UK experienced 519 mm of rainfall, 159% of the long-term average and the second-wettest winter on record, while Ireland saw 602 mm, 189% of normal and the wettest since 1850. December 2015 stood out as exceptionally wet and mild, with UK-wide rainfall at 182% of averagethe wettest calendar month ever recordedtriggering widespread flooding from storms like Desmond and Eva. These extratropical cyclones, fueled by the strong zonal flow, caused record 24-hour rainfall of 341.4 mm in parts of northern England and affected over 16,000 properties with flooding, alongside 1.3 billion in insurance claims. Northern Europe broadly shared this wet pattern, while central and southern areas saw precipitation deficits.
Toward late winter, a sudden stratospheric warming event in late January disrupted the positive NAO, leading to a brief shift toward neutral or negative conditions in February and cooler, drier weather in northwestern Europe. This transition brought northwesterly flows, reducing rainfall below average in the UK and causing occasional cold outbreaks in northern Europe, though overall seasonal mildness persisted. The positive NAO early in the season, amplified by a strong El Nio, enhanced predictability of these wet and stormy conditions, with seasonal forecasts capturing the intensified cyclonic activity. Impacts included transport disruptions, power outages, and infrastructure damage from winds gusting up to 50 m/s during storms like Gertrude in January.
