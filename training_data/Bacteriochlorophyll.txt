Bacteriochlorophyll
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Bacteriochlorophylls are a class of magnesium-containing tetrapyrrole pigments essential for anoxygenic photosynthesis in various bacteria, enabling light energy capture without oxygen production. Unlike chlorophylls in plants, which feature a chlorin ring, bacteriochlorophylls possess a tetrapyrrole ring that is either a bacteriochlorin (with two reduced pyrrole rings) in variants a, b, and g or a chlorin (with one reduced pyrrole ring) in variants c, d, e, and f, allowing absorption of light in the near-infrared spectrum (typically 7001000 nm). This structural adaptation supports efficient photosynthesis in anaerobic or low-light environments where oxygenic phototrophs cannot thrive.
The most common variants include bacteriochlorophyll a (BChl a), predominant in purple bacteria such as Rhodobacter sphaeroides, which absorbs maximally around 800870 nm and serves as the core pigment in reaction centers; bacteriochlorophyll b (BChl b), found in species like Blastochloris viridis, extending absorption up to 1020 nm for broader spectral utilization; and bacteriochlorophylls c, d, and e, which form self-assembling chlorosomes in green sulfur bacteria like Chlorobium tepidum for high-efficiency light harvesting. Less common types include BChl f, found in some green sulfur bacteria, and BChl g, which occurs in heliobacteria, with variations arising from differences in substituents like acetyl, ethyl, or formyl groups on the macrocycle. Biosynthesis of bacteriochlorophylls, such as a and b, branches from chlorophyllide a, involving enzymes like chlorophyllide a oxidoreductase (COR) to reduce specific double bonds and form the bacteriochlorin ring, culminating in phytyl esterification for membrane integration. Variants c, d, e, and f follow a distinct pathway retaining a chlorin ring.
In bacterial photosystems, bacteriochlorophylls function in antenna complexes for energy transfer and in reaction centers for charge separation, driving cyclic electron flow to generate ATP without NADPH production. Their near-infrared absorption minimizes competition with chlorophyll-based oxygenic photosynthesis, allowing coexistence in aquatic and soil ecosystems. Beyond photosynthesis, bacteriochlorophyll derivatives show promise in photodynamic therapy due to their tumor-localizing properties and extended wavelength excitation.

Introduction and History

Definition and Characteristics

Bacteriochlorophylls are a class of photosynthetic pigments consisting of modified tetrapyrrole macrocycles, specifically bacteriochlorins or chlorins, that serve as the primary light-harvesting molecules in anoxygenic phototrophic bacteria. These pigments are structurally analogous to chlorophylls but adapted for anaerobic environments, enabling the capture of light energy without the production of oxygen. Found predominantly in bacteria such as purple sulfur and nonsulfur bacteria, as well as green sulfur bacteria, bacteriochlorophylls facilitate energy transduction in photosynthetic reaction centers and antenna complexes.
Key characteristics of bacteriochlorophylls include their central magnesium ion, which coordinates the tetrapyrrole ring and is essential for light absorption and energy transfer. A hydrophobic tail, typically phytol or farnesyl, anchors the pigment within bacterial membranes, promoting integration into lipid bilayers for efficient photochemical function. Notably, these pigments exhibit absorption maxima in the near-infrared region, ranging from 700 to 1000 nm, allowing phototrophs to utilize infrared light that penetrates deeper into aquatic or sediment environments compared to visible wavelengths. This spectral property distinguishes them from chlorophylls, which primarily absorb in the visible range.
Bacteriochlorophylls represent an evolutionary precursor to chlorophylls, sharing a common biosynthetic pathway but featuring reduced pyrrole rings that shift their absorption toward longer wavelengths for adaptation to low-oxygen niches. In ecological contexts, they enable anoxygenic photosynthesis, where bacteria use alternative electron donors such as hydrogen sulfide (HS) or organic compounds to generate reducing power, supporting carbon fixation and energy flow in anaerobic ecosystems like microbial mats and stratified water columns without contributing to atmospheric oxygen levels. This process underscores their role in diverse microbial communities, from freshwater sediments to marine interfaces.

Discovery and Development

In 19311932, microbiologist Cornelis B. van Niel conducted pioneering studies on purple sulfur bacteria, such as Chromatium species, revealing that these organisms conduct anoxygenic photosynthesis, utilizing hydrogen sulfide (HS) as an electron donor instead of water, thereby producing sulfur rather than oxygen. The pigment bacteriochlorophyll was first isolated in 1935 by Hans Fischer and Johann Hasenkamp. This observation distinguished bacterial photosynthesis from the oxygenic form in plants and algae, laying the foundation for recognizing bacteriochlorophyll as the key pigment enabling light absorption in anaerobic environments.
In the 1940s, researchers began characterizing bacteriochlorophyll's optical properties, with American scientist Charles Stacy French demonstrating its distinct absorption spectra in photosynthetic bacteria. French's analyses of photosynthina pigment-protein complexshowed characteristic peaks around 590 nm, 790 nm, and in the near-infrared (e.g., 840875 nm), varying by bacterial species and differing markedly from chlorophyll's visible-range absorptions. These findings, complemented by European efforts, confirmed bacteriochlorophyll's role in harvesting longer-wavelength light, essential for low-light aquatic habitats. By the 1950s and 1960s, spectroscopic methods, including UV-visible and infrared spectroscopy, facilitated structural elucidation. Building on Hans Fischer's degradative studies in the early 1940s, which proposed a bacteriochlorin macrocycle with reduced rings, researchers like R.P. Linstead refined stereochemistry through partial syntheses and chromatographic separations, establishing the core framework of bacteriochlorophyll a by the late 1950s.
Recent advancements have expanded the known diversity of bacteriochlorophyll variants. In 2012, bacteriochlorophyll f was discovered in the filamentous Chloroflexi bacterium Oscillochloris trichoides, marking the first natural occurrence of this "forbidden" pigment, previously predicted based on biosynthetic analogies but unobserved for decades. This variant, with a formyl group at C-7, enables absorption at even longer wavelengths (~700 nm), enhancing light harvesting in certain microbial mats. Post-2020 research has emphasized synthetic analogs, with bioengineers modifying bacteriochlorophyll pathways in model bacteria like Rhodobacter sphaeroides to incorporate non-native substituents, aiming to optimize energy transfer for biotechnological applications such as improved biofuel production.
The discovery and progressive characterization of bacteriochlorophyll fundamentally altered perceptions of photosynthesis, revealing it as an ancient, versatile process predating oxygenic variants and widespread across microbial domains. This paradigm shift, originating from van Niel's insights, has influenced fields like microbial ecology by underscoring the role of anoxygenic phototrophs in global carbon and sulfur cycles.

Chemical Properties

Molecular Structure

Bacteriochlorophylls are characterized by a core macrocyclic tetrapyrrole structure, consisting of four pyrrole rings connected by methine bridges to form a planar, aromatic porphyrin-like scaffold, with a central magnesium ion (Mg) chelated by the four nitrogen atoms of the pyrrole rings. This magnesium coordination is essential for the pigment's stability and function in photosynthetic systems. The macrocycle includes an annulated isocyclic ring (ring E) fused to ring C at positions C13 and C15, featuring a -keto ester moiety that contributes to the overall rigidity and electronic properties of the molecule.
Bacteriochlorophylls are classified based on the reduction state of their macrocyclic rings: those designated as bacteriochlorins, such as bacteriochlorophylls a, b, and g, feature reductions at both ring B (between C7 and C8) and ring D (between C17 and C18), resulting in a 7,8,17,18-tetrahydro porphyrin core. In contrast, bacteriochlorophylls c, d, e, and f are chlorins, with reduction limited to ring D (C17-C18 single bond), maintaining a dihydroporphyrin structure. These ring reductions disrupt the fully conjugated -system of the parent porphyrin, altering the electronic distribution across the macrocycle. For example, the molecular formula of bacteriochlorophyll a, a prototypical bacteriochlorin, is CHMgNO, reflecting the core tetrapyrrole (CHMgN) plus substituents and the ester tail.
Key substituents on the macrocycle distinguish individual bacteriochlorophyll variants and influence their chemical behavior. In bacteriochlorophyll a, representative of the bacteriochlorin class, an acetyl group (-COCH) is attached at C3 on ring A, while an ethyl group (-CHCH) is present at C8 on the reduced ring B; additional methyl groups occur at C2, C7, C12, C13, C18, and a propionic acid-derived ester at C17. The C17 substituent is esterified with a long-chain alcohol tail, typically phytol (3,7,11,15-tetramethylhexadec-2-en-1-ol, CHO) in bacteriochlorophylls a and b, or farnesol (CHO) in bacteriochlorophylls c, d, e, and f, forming a hydrophobic phytyl or farnesyl ester that aids in membrane integration. These peripheral groups modulate solubility and protein interactions without altering the core chelation.
The stereochemistry of bacteriochlorophylls is defined by chiral centers at C7, C8, C13, C17, and C18, typically exhibiting the natural configurations derived from biosynthetic pathways. Tautomerism occurs primarily in the isocyclic ring E, where the C9 carbonyl and C13 methylene can interconvert between keto and enol forms, stabilized by intramolecular hydrogen bonding between the C13 carbomethoxy group and the pyrrole NH; this equilibrium affects the macrocycle's planarity and electronic conjugation. The ring reductions in bacteriochlorins, particularly at C7-C8, break the extended conjugation of the -positions, localizing -electrons and enabling the molecule's adaptation for low-energy light capture in anoxic environments.

Spectroscopic and Physical Properties

Bacteriochlorophyll a (BChl a) displays characteristic absorption in the near-infrared region, with the Qy band peaking between 750 and 850 nm in organic solvents such as diethyl ether or methanol, for instance at approximately 770 nm in ethanol. In photosynthetic light-harvesting complexes like LH1, this band shifts to longer wavelengths, around 870 nm, enabling efficient capture of far-red light. The Soret band appears at about 380 nm, contributing to ultraviolet absorption. In comparison, chlorophyll a exhibits its Qy band near 680 nm, highlighting the bathochromic shift in bacteriochlorophyll that adapts it to low-light anaerobic environments.
The fluorescence quantum yield of BChl a is low, ranging from 2% to 6% in vivo, primarily due to efficient excited-state energy transfer within antenna complexes that outcompetes radiative decay. Excited-state lifetimes are correspondingly short, typically 0.15 to 0.3 ns under low-intensity conditions, though intrinsic lifetimes in isolated molecules extend to several nanoseconds.
BChl a is highly lipophilic, rendering it insoluble in water but readily soluble in organic solvents like methanol, ethanol, and diethyl ether, which facilitates extraction and spectroscopic studies. Its thermal stability is lower than that of chlorophyll a, with degradation in protein complexes accelerating above 60C, as observed in mesophilic bacterial systems. Sensitivity to pH is pronounced at acidic conditions, where protonation of the macrocycle below pH 7 promotes demetallation and conversion to bacteriopheophytin, altering spectral properties.
Redox properties of BChl a support its role in electron transfer, with one-electron oxidation potentials around +0.82 V vs. SCE in dichloromethane solutions. In bacterial reaction centers, the reduction potential of the active bacteriopheophytin a to its anion radical is approximately -0.6 V vs. SHE, facilitating rapid primary charge separation while preventing back-reaction.

Biological Role

Function in Photosynthesis

Bacteriochlorophyll plays a central role in anoxygenic photosynthesis, a process carried out by certain bacteria that generates ATP without producing oxygen. In this system, light energy absorbed by bacteriochlorophyll initiates charge separation at the reaction center. Anoxygenic phototrophs possess a single photosystemeither type I (analogous to photosystem I) or type II (analogous to photosystem II)but lack the water-splitting capability, relying instead on alternative electron donors such as hydrogen sulfide or organic compounds.
In bacteria with type II reaction centers, such as purple bacteria, a special pair of bacteriochlorophyll a molecules, known as P870, serves as the primary electron donor. Upon excitation, the P870 dimer undergoes rapid charge separation, transferring an electron to an acceptor pheophytin within picoseconds, which establishes a charge-separated state that drives electron transport. In type I centers, as in green sulfur bacteria, the special pair (P840, also BChl a) transfers the electron to an initial chlorophyll a acceptor.
The electron flow in anoxygenic photosynthesis is primarily cyclic, where electrons from the reaction center return via the cytochrome bc1 complex and quinones, creating a proton gradient across the membrane that powers ATP synthase without net oxygen evolution. This cyclic mechanism, facilitated by the bacteriochlorophyll special pair, allows efficient energy conservation in anaerobic environments, producing ATP at rates sufficient for bacterial growth and metabolism. Unlike oxygenic photosynthesis, this pathway avoids the high redox potential required for water oxidation, enabling adaptation to low-oxygen niches.
Bacteriochlorophyll molecules are integral to the light-harvesting complexes LH1 and LH2, which surround the reaction centers in purple bacteria and efficiently capture photons for transfer to P870. LH1 forms a core ring of approximately 16 bacteriochlorophyll a dimers (32 BChl a molecules) that directly encircles the reaction center, while peripheral LH2 complexes are typically arranged as nonameric or octameric rings with 89 pairs, each binding 1 BChl a monomer in the peripheral B800 ring and 2 BChl a molecules in the B850 ring (totaling 2427 BChl a molecules per complex). These antenna systems absorb light in the near-infrared region and funnel excitation energy to the reaction center through a series of Frster resonance energy transfers.
In green sulfur bacteria, bacteriochlorophylls c, d, or e in chlorosome antenna complexes harvest light and transfer energy to a type I reaction center (P840), enabling linear electron flow for both ATP and reductant production using sulfide as donor. Heliobacteria use BChl g in a simple antenna for type I centers adapted to soil environments.
Exciton delocalization across the bacteriochlorophyll arrays in LH1 and LH2 enhances transfer efficiency, with excitons coherently spreading over 4-10 molecules in the B850 band of LH2, minimizing energy loss from disorder. This delocalization, supported by strong electronic couplings of 200-300 cm between pigments, results in near-unity quantum yields for energy transfer, approaching 95% or higher, as measured by time-resolved spectroscopy. The process ensures rapid downhill energy migration from LH2 to LH1 and then to P870, occurring on timescales of 1-10 picoseconds.
The near-infrared absorption of bacteriochlorophyll, peaking around 800-870 nm, represents a key adaptation for exploiting low-energy light that penetrates deeper into aquatic environments shaded by chlorophyll-bearing organisms. This spectral niche allows bacteriochlorophyll-dependent bacteria to thrive in stratified water columns where visible light is attenuated, complementing chlorophyll-based systems in mixed microbial communities by utilizing otherwise inaccessible wavelengths.

Occurrence and Distribution

Bacteriochlorophylls are primarily synthesized by anoxygenic phototrophic bacteria across multiple phyla, serving as key pigments in their light-harvesting systems. In the Proteobacteria phylum, purple sulfur and nonsulfur bacteria such as genera in the Rhodobacterales and Chromatiales produce bacteriochlorophylls a and b, enabling anoxygenic photosynthesis in diverse aquatic and soil habitats. Green sulfur bacteria of the Chlorobi phylum synthesize bacteriochlorophylls c, d, and e, which are aggregated in chlorosomes for efficient light capture under low-light conditions. Heliobacteria in the Firmicutes phylum utilize bacteriochlorophyll g, a unique variant adapted to soil and sediment environments. Members of the Chloroflexi phylum, including filamentous green nonsulfur bacteria like Chloroflexus, produce bacteriochlorophyll c, contributing to mat communities in thermal and hypersaline settings.
These organisms occupy specialized environmental niches that favor anoxygenic photosynthesis, often in anaerobic or microaerobic zones. Purple bacteria thrive in stratified lakes, wastewater ponds, and coastal sediments where sulfide or organic compounds serve as electron donors. Green sulfur bacteria predominate in anoxic, sulfide-rich layers of meromictic lakes, marine basins, and hydrothermal vents, where they form dense populations at the oxic-anoxic interface. Heliobacteria are commonly found in anaerobic agricultural soils and freshwater sediments, tolerating brief oxygen exposure. Chloroflexi species form layered microbial mats in hot springs and hypersaline lagoons, gliding through biofilms to access varying light and chemical gradients.
Bacteriochlorophyll diversity encompasses at least seven main types (a through g) with numerous homologs differing in side-chain modifications, distributed across five major bacterial phyla: Proteobacteria, Chlorobi, Chloroflexi, Firmicutes, and Acidobacteria. This structural variation, exceeding 20 distinct homologs in some cases, allows adaptation to specific light wavelengths and ecological pressures. In rare instances, bacteriochlorophyll-containing bacteria form symbiotic associations with marine invertebrates, such as epiphytic green sulfur-like bacteria on the flatworm Convoluta roscoffensis, where chlorosome-bearing symbionts enhance host nutrition in intertidal zones.
The evolutionary origins of bacteriochlorophyll trace back over 3 billion years, predating oxygenic photosynthesis and reflecting an ancient adaptation to Earth's early anoxic conditions. Their distribution is closely tied to global sulfur biogeochemical cycles, as many producer bacteria, particularly green and purple sulfur species, oxidize sulfide or elemental sulfur, influencing sulfur flux in aquatic and sedimentary systems.

Variants

Bacteriochlorophyll a and b

Bacteriochlorophyll a (BChl a) is the predominant variant among bacteriochlorophylls, widely distributed in purple bacteria where it functions as the core pigment in anoxygenic photosynthesis. Its structure is based on a bacteriochlorin macrocycle featuring an acetyl group at the C3 position and an ethyl group (-CHCH) at the C8 position, esterified with a phytyl chain at C17 and bearing a carbomethoxy group at C13. The molecular formula is CHMgNO. In its monomeric form, typically observed in organic solvents, BChl a exhibits a Q_y absorption maximum around 800 nm, but in vivo within light-harvesting complexes like LH1 and LH2, this shifts to approximately 870 nm due to strong excitonic interactions among aggregated molecules. This red shift enhances energy transfer efficiency to the reaction center. BChl a is essential in the reaction centers of most purple bacteria, forming the special pair (P870) that drives primary charge separation upon photon absorption.
Bacteriochlorophyll b (BChl b) occurs in select purple bacteria, notably Blastochloris viridis (previously classified as Rhodopseudomonas viridis), and shares the core bacteriochlorin scaffold with BChl a, including the C3-acetyl group, but features a distinctive ethylidene substituent (=CH-CH) at the C8 position. This unsaturation extends the -conjugation across ring B, resulting in significantly red-shifted absorption compared to BChl a. In vivo, BChl b displays Q_y maxima between 850 and 1020 nm, with the special pair in reaction centers absorbing near 960 nm and light-harvesting complexes extending to over 1000 nm in some cases. The molecular formula mirrors that of BChl a at CHMgNO, reflecting the double bond in the C8 side chain. Unlike BChl a, which dominates in well-lit environments, BChl b equips its host bacteria for exploiting far-red light that penetrates deeper into shaded or aquatic habitats, broadening the usable solar spectrum beyond that competed for by oxygenic phototrophs.
Functional distinctions between BChl a and b arise primarily from their spectral properties and structural nuances, with BChl a serving as the standard in reaction centers across most purple bacteria for efficient near-infrared absorption around 870 nm. In contrast, BChl b's extended conjugation facilitates adaptation to low-light niches by capturing far-red wavelengths (up to 1020 nm), enhancing photosynthetic yield in environments where shorter wavelengths are attenuated. Homologs of these pigments vary subtly in the C8 side chainethyl for typical BChl a versus ethylidene for BChl bwith the degree of saturation influencing complex stability; for instance, the ethylidene group in BChl b promotes tighter aggregation in antennas, stabilizing excitonic states but requiring precise protein interactions to avoid aggregation-induced instability. These variations underscore evolutionary tuning for ecological niches without altering the fundamental photochemical role.

Bacteriochlorophyll c, d, e, f, and g

Bacteriochlorophylls c, d, and e are chlorin-based pigments primarily found in the light-harvesting chlorosomes of green sulfur bacteria (phylum Chlorobi) and some green non-sulfur bacteria (phylum Chloroflexi). These variants feature a farnesyl ester tail at the C-17 propionate and exhibit structural diversity through homologs differing in alkyl substituents at the C-8 and C-12 positions, such as ethyl or methyl groups. For instance, a common homolog of BChl c is the 8-ethyl-12-methyl form, which enables self-assembly into rod-like aggregates within chlorosomes. In their aggregated state, these pigments display red-shifted absorption maxima in the 650-750 nm range, with BChl c peaking around 736 nm, BChl d at approximately 720 nm, and BChl e near 721 nm, facilitating efficient capture of far-red light in low-oxygen aquatic environments.
BChl f, a rare variant structurally analogous to BChl e but lacking the methyl group at the C-20 position, was first identified in 2012 through genetic engineering of the green sulfur bacterium Chlorobaculum limnaeum. Like BChl e, it possesses a 7-formyl group and variable C-8 alkyl chains (often ethyl), paired with a farnesyl tail, but its absence of the C-20 methyl alters aggregation properties. In chlorosomes, BChl f aggregates absorb at about 705 nm, a blue shift from BChl e that partially overlaps with chlorophyll a absorption but supports phototrophic growth under very low light intensities (3-10 nEsm), albeit with reduced energy transfer efficiency to the reaction center.
BChl g, unique to heliobacteria (Firmicutes phylum), features an isobacteriochlorin core with reduced rings B and D, a vinyl group at C-3, and a farnesyl ester tail. This fully reduced macrocycle enables dual absorption bands in vivo at approximately 670 nm (for the core reaction center) and 790 nm (for antenna complexes), optimizing light harvesting in soil habitats. Notably, exposure to light and oxygen converts BChl g to chlorophyll a (Chl a), shifting its spectrum to match Chl a and potentially aiding survival during oxic stress.
A distinctive feature of BChls c, d, e, and f in green sulfur bacteria is their protein-independent self-assembly into large chlorosomes containing up to 250,000 pigment molecules, where mixtures of homologs fine-tune the absorption spectrum for environmental adaptation. This supramolecular organization enhances quantum efficiency in dim light, contrasting with the more rigid structures in other photosynthetic systems.

Biosynthesis

Pathway Overview

The biosynthesis of bacteriochlorophyll (BChl) shares its initial steps with that of chlorophyll, beginning with the formation of 5-aminolevulinic acid (ALA) from glutamate via the C5 pathway in most bacteria, followed by the assembly of porphobilinogen and subsequent macrocyclization to uroporphyrinogen III, hydroxylation and decarboxylation to coproporphyrinogen III, and oxidation to protoporphyrin IX. Magnesium insertion into protoporphyrin IX by magnesium chelatase yields Mg-protoporphyrin IX, which undergoes methylation at C13, oxidation of the C6 propionate, and cyclization of the E-ring to form 3,8-divinylprotochlorophyllide a (DV PChlide a). Reduction of the C17-C18 double bond in DV PChlide a by dark-operative protochlorophyllide oxidoreductase (DPOR) produces 3,8-divinylchlorophyllide a (DV Chlide a), and further reduction of the C8-vinyl group by divinyl reductase yields chlorophyllide a, the key branch point intermediate for all BChls.
From chlorophyllide a, the pathway diverges based on the reduction state of ring B, leading to bacteriochlorin-based BChls a, b, and g in purple bacteria and heliobacteria. Chlorophyllide a oxidoreductase (COR) catalyzes the stereospecific reduction of the C7-C8 double bond, transforming the chlorin macrocycle into a bacteriochlorin ring system and yielding 3-vinylbacteriochlorophyllide a (or bacteriochlorophyllide g in the case of an 8-vinyl precursor). Subsequent modifications include hydration of the C3-vinyl group to a hydroxyethyl intermediate and dehydrogenation to an acetyl group for BChl a and b, while BChl g retains the C3-vinyl and features an ethylidene group (=CH-CH) at C8 formed during the COR reaction on the 8-vinyl substrate. The bacteriochlorophyllide is then esterified with a phytyl (C20) or farnesyl (C15) diphosphate tail, followed by reduction of the C13 ester to an alcohol using NADPH.
In contrast, the pathway for BChl c, d, e, f in green sulfur bacteria and filamentous anoxygenic phototrophs diverges earlier, after magnesium chelation of protoporphyrin IX, without isocyclic ring formation by BchE, yielding chlorin macrocycles lacking the fifth ring. Key modifications involve methylation at C20 (for c and e), hydration and oxidation of the C8-vinyl to an ethyl or acetyl group, and additional substitutions such as a formyl group at C7 for BChl e and f via radical SAM-dependent mechanisms. BChl f, a variant lacking the C20 methyl group compared to BChl e, has been produced in mutants of Chlorobaculum limnaeum but not confirmed in wild-type natural populations as of 2025. These variants are esterified with farnesyl diphosphate rather than phytyl, distinguishing them from chlorophyll and other BChls in tail length and saturation.
The reduction steps, including those by DPOR and COR, require reduced ferredoxin and ATP, while the C13 ester reduction consumes NADPH, highlighting the high energy investment in these late-stage transformations to achieve the specific macrocycle reductions essential for near-infrared absorption.

Enzymes and Genetic Regulation

The biosynthesis of bacteriochlorophyll (BChl) involves several key enzymes that catalyze critical modifications to the tetrapyrrole macrocycle, particularly the reduction steps leading to the bacteriochlorin ring system unique to BChls. Chlorophyllide a oxidoreductase (COR), composed of the subunits BchX, BchY, and BchZ, performs the essential reduction of the C7=C8 double bond in the B ring of chlorophyllide a, forming the bacteriochlorin intermediate 7,8-dihydrochlorophyllide a (also known as bacteriochlorophyllide a). This nitrogenase-like enzyme complex requires ATP and reduced ferredoxin as cofactors and is specific to anoxygenic phototrophs, distinguishing BChl pathways from chlorophyll synthesis in oxygenic organisms. In organisms producing BChl g, such as heliobacteria, the geranylgeranyl reductase BchP plays a role in reducing the esterifying alcohol tail attached during the final phytylation or farnesylation step, ensuring proper hydrophobicity for pigment integration into photosynthetic complexes. For BChl c in chlorosomes of green sulfur bacteria (Chlorobi), post-synthetic modifications including farnesylation and methylation are catalyzed by enzymes such as BchK (BChl synthase homolog for esterification) and BchU (methyltransferase for C-8^1 and C-12^1 positions), which tune the pigment's aggregation and near-infrared absorption properties.
Genes encoding these enzymes are organized into clusters that vary across bacterial phyla, reflecting evolutionary adaptations to diverse photosynthetic lifestyles. In Proteobacteria, such as Rhodobacter species, the bch genes are part of a large photosynthesis gene cluster (PGC) comprising over 40 genes, including at least 15 dedicated to BChl and carotenoid biosynthesis (e.g., bchC, bchD, bchH, bchM), often arranged in multiple operons for coordinated expression under anaerobic conditions. This PGC integrates pigment synthesis with assembly of reaction centers and light-harvesting complexes, enabling rapid responses to environmental shifts. In contrast, Chlorobi exhibit more dispersed genetic organization, with separate clusters for chlorin-type BChls (c, d, e, f); for instance, genes like bchU and bchK for BChl c modifications are located in distinct loci from the core tetrapyrrole pathway genes, allowing independent regulation of chlorosome biogenesis. These architectural differences highlight the modular evolution of BChl pathways, with horizontal gene transfer contributing to cluster rearrangements in Proteobacteria.
Regulation of BChl synthesis is tightly controlled by environmental cues, primarily oxygen levels, to prevent wasteful production under aerobic conditions. In facultative anaerobes like Rhodobacter capsulatus, the two-component system RegB/RegA senses redox status via a cysteine-based sensor in RegB, leading to phosphorylation and activation of RegA, which upregulates bch operon promoters under low oxygen tension. This oxygen-sensitive mechanism represses photosynthesis genes aerobically, with RegA binding to enhancer-like sequences upstream of bch and pigment-binding protein genes. Light intensity provides additional feedback, where high light downregulates bch expression through the repressor PpsR (or AppA homologs), which senses singlet oxygen and blue light to inhibit tetrapyrrole accumulation and avoid photooxidative damage. Nutrient availability modulates synthesis via alternative sigma factors, such as RpoN (54) in some Proteobacteria, which integrates nitrogen status with bch promoter activity, ensuring balanced pigment production during growth limitation.
Variations in enzymatic and genetic machinery underscore the diversity of BChl biosynthesis across phyla. Heliobacteria, strict anaerobes, lack a complete bch operon equivalent to Proteobacteria and instead rely on an alternative light-dependent protochlorophyllide reductase (LPOR, encoded by crd genes) for early reduction steps, bypassing the dark-operative DPOR (BchLNB) while retaining COR (BchB/C/H homologs) for B-ring reduction in BChl g synthesis; this streamlined pathway reflects their primitive photosynthetic apparatus.
