 The ampere, symbol A, is the base unit of electric current in the International System of Units (SI). It is defined by fixing the numerical value of the elementary charge e to exactly 1.602 176 634 10-19 coulombs, such that one ampere represents the flow of exactly 6 241 509 074 362 548 482 elementary charges per second through a given cross-section. This redefinition, adopted in 2019, replaced the prior mechanical definition based on the force between two parallel current-carrying conductors, ensuring the unit's stability and alignment with fundamental physical constants.
Named after the French physicist Andr-Marie Ampre (17751836), who pioneered the study of electromagnetism shortly after Hans Christian rsted's 1820 discovery linking electricity and magnetism, the ampere quantifies the rate of charge flow in circuits and is essential for defining other electrical units like the volt, ohm, and coulomb via relationships such as Ohm's law. In practical terms, one ampere corresponds to the current produced by approximately 6 quintillion (6 1018) electrons passing a point each second, powering everyday devices from household wiring to electronic components.
The unit's formal adoption occurred in 1881 at the International Electrical Congress, where it was standardized as part of early efforts to create coherent international electrical units, evolving through refinements by bodies like the International Committee for Weights and Measures (CIPM) to support precision metrology in fields ranging from power distribution to quantum technologies.

Fundamentals

Definition

The ampere, symbol A, is the SI unit of electric current. It is defined by taking the fixed numerical value of the elementary charge $ e $ to be exactly $ 1.602176634 \times 10^{-19} $ when expressed in the unit C s1^{-1}1, where the second is defined in terms of the caesium hyperfine transition frequency $ \Delta \nu_{\text{Cs}} $. This definition, adopted in the 2019 revision of the International System of Units (SI), anchors the ampere directly to a fundamental physical constant, ensuring its stability and universality independent of experimental artifacts.
Conceptually, electric current represents the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor, expressed as $ I = \frac{dQ}{dt} $, where $ I $ is the current in amperes, $ Q $ is the electric charge in coulombs, and $ t $ is time in seconds. Under the modern definition, one ampere corresponds exactly to a current produced by the flow of $ \frac{1}{e} = 6.241509074 \times 10^{18} $ elementary charges per second. The elementary charge $ e $ serves as the basis, quantifying the charge of a single proton or electron, with the ampere thereby linking macroscopic electrical measurements to microscopic quantum phenomena.
As one of the seven base SI unitsalongside the kilogram, metre, second, kelvin, mole, and candelathe ampere underpins the measurement of all electrical quantities in the SI system since the 2019 revision. This role emphasizes its foundational status, where 1 A is equivalently defined as 1 coulomb per second (1 A = 1 C/s), providing a precise scale for current in circuits, devices, and natural processes.

Physical Basis

Electric current is defined as the net flow of electric charge carriers through a conductor, where the carriers are typically electrons in metallic conductors, moving under the influence of an electric field. This directed motion results in a measurable transfer of charge, quantified as the amount of charge passing a point per unit time. In conventional notation, the direction of current is taken as the flow of positive charge, opposite to the actual electron movement in most solids.
The physical basis of the ampere ties directly to the electromagnetic force between currents, as described by Ampre's force law, which quantifies the interaction between two parallel current-carrying wires. Historical Development

Naming and Early Concepts

The unit of electric current, the ampere, is named in honor of the French physicist and mathematician Andr-Marie Ampre (17751836), who is regarded as the founder of the science of electrodynamics, now known as electromagnetism. Ampre's groundbreaking work began in 1820, shortly after Hans Christian rsted's discovery that electric currents could deflect a magnetic compass needle, demonstrating a link between electricity and magnetism. Building on this, Ampre conducted extensive experiments showing that parallel wires carrying currents attract or repel each other depending on the direction of flow, and he developed a mathematical framework to describe these interactions.
Ampre's most significant contribution was the formulation of what is now called Ampre's circuital law, which relates the magnetic field around a closed loop to the electric current passing through the loop. In its integral form, the law is expressed as:
Bdl=0I
\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I
Bdl=0I
where B\mathbf{B}B is the magnetic field, dld\mathbf{l}dl is an infinitesimal element of the closed path, 0\mu_00 is the permeability of free space, and III is the total current enclosed by the path. This equation, derived from Ampre's experimental observations and theoretical insights published in his 18261827 memoirs, provided a foundational principle for understanding how steady electric currents generate magnetic fields.
Early conceptualizations of electric current in the 19th century distinguished it from static electricity, which involved transient accumulations of charge, such as those produced by friction on materials like amber (from which the term "electricity" derives). In contrast, "galvanic current" referred to the continuous flow of electricity generated by chemical reactions in devices like the voltaic pile, invented by Alessandro Volta in 1800. Volta's pile, a stack of alternating zinc and copper discs separated by electrolyte-soaked cardboard, produced a steady current that could be measured through electrochemical effects, such as the decomposition of water into hydrogen and oxygen during electrolysis, as later quantified by Michael Faraday in the 1830s. These initial measurements relied on observing gas evolution or metal deposition at electrodes, establishing current as a flow of charge rather than isolated sparks.
The ampere was first proposed as a unit of current intensity at the International Electrical Congress in Paris in 1881, where delegates honored Ampre's contributions by adopting his name for the practical unit equivalent to one tenth of the electromagnetic unit (emu) of current. This naming convention aimed to standardize electrical measurements amid growing industrial applications. The unit was later formalized and defined more precisely by the 9th General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM) in 1948, which adopted the ampere as one of the base units in the emerging International System of Units (SI), based on a recommendation from the International Committee for Weights and Measures (CIPM) in 1946.

Pre-2019 Definitions

The ampere was formally defined by the 9th General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM) in 1948 as the constant current that, if maintained in two straight parallel conductors of infinite length, of negligible circular cross-section, and placed one metre apart in vacuum, would produce between these conductors a force equal to $ 2 \times 10^{-7} $ newton per metre of length. This definition fixed the value of the magnetic constant $ \mu_0 $ at exactly $ 4\pi \times 10^{-7} $ H/m, establishing the ampere as a base unit in the International System of Units (SI).
Prior to 2019, the absolute realization of this definition relied on current balances, electromechanical devices that measured the force between current-carrying coils to calibrate currents directly against the specified force law. These balances compared the electromagnetic force to a known mechanical force, often derived from mass standards and gravitational acceleration, achieving relative uncertainties on the order of $ 10^{-7} $. For higher precision in practical metrology, the ampere was typically realized indirectly through the relation $ I = V / R $, where voltage $ V $ standards were maintained using the Josephson effect and resistance $ R $ standards via the quantum Hall effect. The Josephson effect provided voltage quantization in terms of the Josephson constant $ K_J = 2e / h $, while the quantum Hall effect yielded resistance plateaus at $ R_H = h / (e^2 i) $, linking current measurements to fundamental constants with uncertainties below $ 10^{-9} $ in combined systems.
This dual approachabsolute via mechanical balances and practical via quantum electrical standardshighlighted the distinction between the theoretical force-based definition and operational realizations, as no physical artifact prototype existed for the ampere, unlike for mass. However, the dependence on mechanical apparatus and artifact-based units like the kilogram introduced limitations, including drift in standards and challenges in achieving the idealized conditions of infinite conductors, resulting in overall measurement uncertainties around $ 10^{-7} $ for direct realizations. These constraints motivated the 2019 redefinition to a quantum-based standard fixing the elementary charge.

2019 Redefinition

In 2018, the 26th General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM) approved a comprehensive revision of the International System of Units (SI), which included redefining the ampere as part of fixing the numerical values of several fundamental constants: the speed of light ccc, the hyperfine transition frequency of caesium Cs\Delta \nu_{\rm Cs}Cs, the Planck constant hhh, the elementary charge eee, the Boltzmann constant kkk, the Avogadro constant NAN_{\rm A}NA, and the luminous efficacy KcdK_{\rm cd}Kcd. This redefinition took effect on 20 May 2019, establishing the ampere, symbol A, as the SI unit of electric current defined by setting the elementary charge to the exact value of 1.60217663410191.602176634 \times 10^{-19}1.6021766341019 C, where 1 C = 1 A s.
The primary rationale for this revision was to achieve a more invariant and universal system of units by basing the ampere on unchanging fundamental constants rather than on physical artifacts or experimental setups that could drift or vary. Previously, the ampere relied on an operational definition involving the force between two infinitely long parallel conductors carrying current, specified as exactly 21072 \times 10^{-7}2107 N/m, which depended on reproducible but imperfect mechanical measurements. The new definition eliminates this artifact dependence, ensuring stability independent of time, place, or measurement technology, while maintaining the same numerical value for the ampere through the fixed value of eee. It leverages quantum electrical effects, such as single-electron tunneling, where individual electrons are precisely transported and counted in nanoscale devices, enabling realizations with uncertainties approaching parts in 101010^{10}1010.
This shift has significant implications for electrical metrology, allowing for higher precision in calibrating current standards and instruments without reliance on macroscopic artifacts. By aligning the ampere with quantum technologies, including single-electron pumps and the quantum metrology trianglewhich interconnects current, voltage (via the Josephson effect), and resistance (via the quantum Hall effect)the redefinition facilitates advancements in fields like nanotechnology and quantum computing, where exact charge quantization is essential. Overall, it future-proofs the SI for emerging scientific and industrial needs while preserving continuity in practical measurements.

Related Units

Derived Electrical Units

The coulomb (C) is the SI derived unit of electric charge, defined as the quantity of electric charge transported by a current of one ampere in one second. Its expression in base units is 1 C = 1 A s, directly linking charge to the base unit of current and time. This unit plays a fundamental role in quantifying charge in electrostatics and electrodynamics, such as in the measurement of battery capacity or the flow of electrons in circuits.
The volt (V) is the SI derived unit of electric potential difference, representing the difference in electric potential between two points on a conducting wire carrying a constant current of one ampere when the power dissipated is one watt. Expressed as 1 V = 1 W / A, or in base units as kg m s A (since 1 W = kg m s), it derives from the ampere through the relationship between power and current. The volt is essential for describing energy per unit charge in electrical systems, enabling the specification of voltage in power supplies and sensors.
The ohm () is the SI derived unit of electrical resistance, defined as the resistance between two points of a conductor when a constant potential difference of one volt applied between them produces a current of one ampere. It is given by 1 = 1 V / A, or in base units as kg m s A, incorporating the ampere inversely to reflect opposition to current flow. This unit is critical for characterizing the behavior of resistors and conductors in circuits, influencing design in electronics and power distribution.
The watt (W) is the SI derived unit of power, defined as the power that gives rise to one joule of energy per second. In electrical contexts, it relates to current via 1 W = 1 V A, with base unit expression kg m s, deriving from mechanical units but applied electrically through the ampere. The watt quantifies the rate of energy transfer in electrical devices, such as motors and lighting, providing a measure of efficiency and consumption.
The farad (F) is the SI derived unit of electric capacitance, defined as the capacitance of a capacitor in which one coulomb produces a potential difference of one volt. Expressed as 1 F = 1 C / V, or equivalently in base units as A s kg m (substituting C = A s and V = kg m s A), it builds on the ampere through charge and potential. The farad is key in describing energy storage in capacitors, vital for applications in filtering and timing circuits.
The siemens (S) is the SI derived unit of electric conductance, defined as the conductance of a conductor between two points when a constant potential difference of one volt applied between them produces a current of one ampere. It is the reciprocal of the ohm, given by 1 S = 1 A / V, or in base units as A s kg m. This unit measures the ease of current flow, essential for analyzing conductive materials and semiconductor devices.

Associated Magnetic Units

The ampere serves as a foundational unit in defining several magnetic quantities within the International System of Units (SI), particularly those involving magnetic flux, flux density, and inductance, where current directly influences electromagnetic phenomena.
The weber (symbol: Wb) is the SI derived unit of magnetic flux, representing the amount of magnetic flux that, when linked with a single turn of a circuit, induces an electromotive force of one volt as the flux is uniformly reduced to zero in one second. This unit arises from Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction, expressed as =ddt\varepsilon = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}=dtd, where \varepsilon is the induced electromotive force in volts and \Phi is the magnetic flux in webers. Dimensionally, one weber equals one volt-second (1 Wb = 1 V s), but in base SI units, it is defined as 1 Wb = 1 kg m s A, highlighting the inverse dependence on the ampere as a base unit.
The tesla (symbol: T) is the SI derived unit of magnetic flux density, equivalent to one weber per square meter (1 T = 1 Wb/m). It quantifies the strength of a magnetic field passing through a unit area perpendicular to the field lines, with a dimensional expression of 1 T = 1 kg s A, again underscoring the role of the ampere in scaling magnetic intensity relative to mechanical units.
The henry (symbol: H) is the SI derived unit of inductance, defined as the inductance of a closed circuit in which an electromotive force of one volt is produced when the current varies uniformly at a rate of one ampere per second. Expressed as 1 H = 1 Wb/A or 1 V s/A, its base unit form is 1 H = 1 kg m s A, reflecting the quadratic dependence on the ampere due to the interplay between current and induced flux in inductive circuits.
In magnetostatics, the ampere also directly defines magnetic field strength, with the SI unit ampere per meter (A/m), which measures the magnetizing force along a path. Magnetomotive force, the equivalent of electromotive force in magnetic circuits, has the unit ampere (A).
These units connect directly to Ampre's law, which relates the magnetic field to the current producing it; for an ideal long solenoid with nnn turns per unit length carrying current III, the magnetic flux density inside is given by B=0nIB = \mu_0 n IB=0nI, where 0\mu_00 is the permeability of free space, demonstrating how the ampere quantifies the current's contribution to the generated field.

Scaling and Notation

SI Prefixes

SI prefixes are used to form decimal multiples and submultiples of the ampere (A), the SI base unit of electric current, allowing for the expression of currents across a wide range of magnitudes. These prefixes, defined by the International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM), follow powers of 10 from 10^{30} to 10^{-30}, enabling concise notation for both very large and very small currents.
In standard notation, a prefix symbol is attached directly to the unit symbol A without any space, forming a single inseparable symbol; for example, 1 kA denotes 1 kiloampere, equivalent to 1000 A. Similarly, the prefix name combines with the unit name to form a single word, such as "milliampere" for mA. This rule applies uniformly to all SI units, including derived electrical units like the volt (V) or ohm (), where prefixes such as kV or m are formed in the same manner.
The use of SI prefixes facilitates the description of electric currents from atomic and subatomic scales, where submultiples like pico (pA, 10^{-12} A) or femto (fA, 10^{-15} A) are relevant, to macroscopic applications in power grids, where multiples like kilo (kA, 10^3 A) or mega (MA, 10^6 A) are common. In electronics, prefixes such as milli (mA, 10^{-3} A), micro (A, 10^{-6} A), nano (nA, 10^{-9} A), and pico (pA, 10^{-12} A) are particularly prevalent for handling currents in circuits and devices.

Practical Applications

In electrical engineering, the ampere is fundamental to household wiring and power distribution. Standard residential outlets in the United States are typically rated at 15 to 20 amperes, allowing safe operation of common appliances like lights, computers, and small motors without exceeding circuit capacity. Power transmission lines, which deliver electricity over long distances, often carry currents in the range of hundreds to several thousand amperes per phase under normal and peak load conditions to minimize losses while handling high voltages.
In electronics, currents measured in microamperes (A) are prevalent in integrated circuits, where low-power logic gates, such as the 7408 quad AND gate, consume input currents around 40 A and deliver output currents up to 0.4 mA to drive subsequent stages efficiently. Semiconductor devices, including diodes and transistors, exhibit leakage currents on the order of nanoamperes (nA), which represent unintended conduction through insulating regions and must be minimized to prevent power dissipation and signal degradation in high-density chips.
Scientific applications leverage the ampere across vast scales for precise measurements. In electrophysiology, the patch-clamp technique records ionic currents through single ion channels at picoampere (pA) levelstypically a few pAenabling researchers to study cellular signaling in neurons and muscle cells without external interference. Particle accelerators, such as the Large Hadron Collider (LHC), operate with beam currents on the ampere scale; for instance, proton beams with currents around 0.6 A, facilitating high-energy collisions for fundamental physics experiments.
Safety standards rely on ampere ratings to protect circuits from overloads. Electrical fuses are specified by their ampere rating, which determines the maximum continuous current before the device melts due to IR heatingthe thermal effect where current squared times resistance generates heat that severs the fuse element during faults. This principle ensures that fuses interrupt excessive currents, such as those exceeding 10 to 30 A in household applications, preventing fires and equipment damage as per standards like UL 248.
