 Grundfos is a Danish multinational corporation and the world's largest manufacturer of circulator pumps, specializing in advanced, energy-efficient pumps and water solutions for applications including water supply, wastewater treatment, irrigation, heating, ventilation, air conditioning, and industrial processes. Founded in 1945 by Poul Due Jensen in Bjerringbro, Denmark, the company is headquartered there and operates through over 100 subsidiaries in more than 60 countries, employing approximately 20,800 people worldwide. With an annual production exceeding 15 million pump units, Grundfos holds a 50% global market share in circulator pumps and reported revenue of 4.46 billion in 2024.
The company is majority-owned by the Poul Due Jensen Foundation, which holds 87.5% of shares and supports its mission to pioneer solutions addressing global water and climate challenges while improving quality of life. Grundfos emphasizes sustainability, committing to halve its CO emissions by 2025 compared to 2008 levels, adhering to the UN Global Compact, and implementing initiatives like the Water2Life program since 2010 to provide clean water access in underserved communities. Its product portfolio includes digitalized systems for intelligent water management, serving sectors from residential buildings to large-scale utilities and agriculture, with certifications such as ISO 9001, 14001, and 45001 underscoring its quality and environmental standards.
Grundfos has grown from a small workshop into a global leader, driven by innovation in energy-efficient technologies that reduce resource consumption and climate impact. The company's core valuessustainability, openness, trustworthiness, people focus, independence, partnership, and relentless ambitionguide its operations, fostering long-term collaborations and investments in research to tackle pressing issues like water scarcity and energy waste.

Overview

Company Profile

Grundfos was founded in 1945 by Poul Due Jensen in Bjerringbro, Denmark, initially as a foundry that produced its first pump the following year. The company has since evolved from a local operation into a global leader in pump and water solutions.
As of 2025, Grundfos is recognized as the world's largest pump manufacturer, employing 20,818 people across more than 60 countries and generating 4.46 billion in revenue in 2024. With annual production exceeding 15 million pump units, Grundfos holds approximately 50% of the global market share in circulator pumps. Its global headquarters in Bjerringbro serves as the central hub for strategic operations, research, and development, overseeing a network of subsidiaries and production facilities worldwide. The company is organized into four main divisions: Commercial Building Services, Domestic Building Services, Industry, and Water Utility, each focusing on tailored solutions for specific sectors.
The United States represents Grundfos's largest single market, contributing approximately 25% of its global revenue as of 2025.

Mission and Strategic Focus

Grundfos's core purpose is to pioneer solutions to the world's water and climate challenges and improve quality of life for people. This mission centers on developing energy-efficient technologies for water movement, aiming to enhance accessibility, reliability, and sustainability in water systems worldwide. The company's motto, "Respect, protect, and advance the flow of water," underscores this commitment to responsible stewardship of vital resources.
As of 2024-2025, Grundfos's strategic drivers encompass sewer security, sustainable aquifer management, energy transition, and water access, forming the foundation of its broader transformation efforts. These priorities guide the Ignite'27 strategy for 2025-2027, which seeks to accelerate growth, foster innovation in intelligent water solutions, and position the company as a global leader in addressing climate imperatives. Through these drivers, Grundfos targets systemic improvements, such as reducing overflows in sewer networks via IoT-enabled monitoring and optimizing groundwater extraction to prevent depletion.
Grundfos demonstrates a strong commitment to innovation in water technology, evolving from its origins as a pump manufacturer into a comprehensive provider of integrated water and climate solutions. This shift emphasizes digital tools, efficient pumping systems, and holistic services to tackle global challenges like resource scarcity and environmental degradation.
Guiding these initiatives are Grundfos's core values: sustainable practices, openness and trustworthiness, a focus on people, independence, partnership, and relentless ambition. These principles, deeply rooted in the company's ownership by the Poul Due Jensen Foundation (also known as the Grundfos Foundation), ensure that innovation and responsibility remain integral to its operations and long-term strategy.

History

Founding and Early Development

Grundfos was founded in 1945 by engineer Poul Due Jensen in Bjerringbro, Denmark, initially under the name Bjerringbro Foundry and Machine Factory, during the post-World War II reconstruction era. Jensen established the venture to produce essential machinery, capitalizing on his technical expertise amid Denmark's economic recovery.
The company's early focus shifted to pump production following an order from a local farmer for an automated water supply system, as no high-quality electric pumps were available in the postwar market. In response, Jensen developed the first Grundfos pump in 1945, a shallow groundwater piston pump known as Foss 1 or "Grisen" (the pig) due to its robust, unconventional design, which emphasized durability for agricultural use. This innovation marked the transition from general foundry work to specialized pump manufacturing, targeting water extraction needs in farming communities.
Postwar challenges, including severe material shortages and limited access to raw materials, constrained operations, yet Jensen prioritized efficient designs for agricultural pumps to meet local demand. The company started with a small team in a modest workshop and experienced steady initial growth, expanding its workforce to support increasing production through the late 1940s. By the early 1950s, further innovations like the 1952 centrifugal pump (CP) addressed the limitations of heavier piston models, enhancing efficiency and scalability.

Mid-Century Growth and Milestones

In the 1960s, Grundfos underwent significant product innovation that solidified its position in the pump industry. The company formally adopted the name Grundfos in 1967, evolving from its earlier designation as Bjerringbro Foundry and Machine Factory, which had been used since its founding. This name change reflected the growing prominence of its Grundfoss pump line and marked a shift toward a unified global identity. Concurrently, Grundfos entered the building services market with the launch of the VP 35 circulator pump in 1965, featuring hydraulic adjustment for heating systems, which addressed rising demand for efficient domestic and commercial water circulation. The decade also saw the establishment of the company's first foreign subsidiary in Germany in 1960, focusing on sales and production in Wahlstedt, laying the groundwork for international expansion.
The 1970s and 1980s were characterized by accelerated growth, product diversification, and strategic ownership changes amid global economic challenges like the oil crises of 1973 and 1979. Grundfos expanded its wastewater pump offerings, culminating in the 1984 launch of the KP series, which enhanced capabilities for sewage and drainage applications and supported infrastructure development in Europe. International presence strengthened with subsidiaries in France in 1972 and the United States in 1973, enabling localized production and market penetration. In response to energy shortages during the oil crises, the company emphasized energy-efficient designs, such as its circulator pumps, which reduced consumption in heating systems and contributed to sustained demand. Ownership stability was secured in 1975 through the establishment of the Poul Due Jensen Foundation, which acquired majority shares to ensure long-term independence and reinvestment in growth. By the late 1980s, annual revenue exceeded 1 billion DKK, reflecting robust expansion in industrial and municipal sectors.
Entering the 1990s and early 2000s, Grundfos achieved key technological and market milestones that positioned it as a global leader. The SQ series of submersible pumps was introduced in 1999, offering compact, high-efficiency solutions for groundwater supply with built-in electronics for easy installation and operation, revolutionizing residential and agricultural applications. Revenue growth accelerated, surpassing 10 billion DKK by 2001, driven by diversified product lines and exports comprising over 90% of sales. By the early 2000s, Grundfos emerged as the world's largest pump manufacturer, with annual production exceeding 10 million units and a workforce that made it Denmark's biggest industrial employer, supporting around 3,600 jobs domestically by 2002. These developments underscored the company's focus on innovation and scalability during a period of rapid globalization.

Ownership and Governance

Family and Foundation Ownership

Grundfos is fully owned by the Poul Due Jensen Foundation, its employees, and the descendants of founder Poul Due Jensen, ensuring private control without any public stock listing. The founder's descendants maintain a significant stake, holding 8.8% of the shares in Grundfos Holding A/S as of 2025, which allows for ongoing family influence in the company's direction. This structure traces back to the original establishment by Poul Due Jensen in 1945, with subsequent generations, including his grandson Poul Due Jensen serving as CEO since 2020, actively participating in leadership to preserve the company's foundational values.
The Poul Due Jensen Foundation was established in 1975 by the founder himself to secure the long-term stability of Grundfos and promote philanthropic initiatives aligned with its mission. As a self-governing institution, the foundation was designed to prioritize sustainable growth and social responsibility over short-term financial gains, channeling profits into innovation, research, and global causes such as water access and climate resilience. By 2025, marking the foundation's 50th anniversary, it had committed substantial resources to these efforts, including DKK 295 million in grants for philanthropy in the prior year.
Under this ownership model, the foundation holds 87.9% of the shares in Grundfos Holding A/S, with employees owning the remaining 3.3%, reinforcing a 100% privately held status that shields the company from external market pressures. This arrangement enables a focus on long-term innovation, such as sustainable pump technologies, rather than quarterly profits, while family members contribute to governance through board roles and strategic oversight. The model's emphasis on philanthropy has supported initiatives in education, social inclusion, and environmental projects worldwide, aligning ownership with broader societal impact.

Organizational Structure

Grundfos operates through a divisional structure comprising four global business areas designed to enhance customer focus and operational agility: Water Utility, which specializes in large-scale water supply solutions; Industry, encompassing water treatment and purification technologies; Domestic Building Services, featuring circulator pumps for heating and cooling systems; and Commercial Building Services, providing HVAC and related building solutions.
The company's leadership is headed by Group President and CEO Poul Due Jensen, who has held the position since 2020 and continues in the role as of 2025, overseeing the executive team that includes the CFO, COO, CHRO, and CEOs of the four divisions, all reporting to the Board of Directors. The Board, chaired by Claus Aagaard, maintains oversight influenced by the Poul Due Jensen Foundation's majority ownership of 87.9%, ensuring alignment with long-term strategic goals.
Grundfos employs a decentralized organizational model, with regional management teams handling operations across more than 100 subsidiaries in over 60 countries to adapt to local markets, while central functions such as the majority of global R&D activities are concentrated in Bjerringbro, Denmark.
Employee governance is integrated through partial ownership stakes totaling 3.3% and the election of employee representatives to the Board, such as gnes Eszter Pauer and John Bjerregaard Jacobsen, fostering participation in decision-making processes.

Products and Markets

Core Product Lines

Grundfos's core product lines primarily consist of centrifugal pumps, submersible pumps, circulator pumps, and wastewater pumps, designed for reliable fluid handling across various pressures and flows. The CR series represents a flagship line of vertical multistage centrifugal pumps, featuring suction and discharge ports at the same level for efficient pressure boosting in water supply and industrial processes; these pumps are constructed with cast iron heads and bases, alongside stainless steel wetted parts for durability against non-corrosive liquids. Complementing this, submersible pumps like the SQ and SP series are optimized for groundwater extraction, with the compact SQ model suited for 3-inch or larger boreholes using multistage centrifugal design and built-in electronics for constant pressure operation, while the SP series offers all-stainless-steel construction in three material grades for boreholes starting from 4 inches.
Circulator pumps form another essential category, tailored for heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC) systems, where models like the ALPHA series deliver best-in-class efficiency through electronically commutated motors (ECM) that reduce energy consumption compared to traditional circulators. Wastewater pumps, including submersible sewage variants, operate as sealed units integrating pump and motor for submerged deployment, ensuring non-clogging performance in handling solids-laden fluids within municipal and industrial settings. These lines emphasize modular construction, allowing customization for specific hydraulic requirements while prioritizing corrosion resistance and long-term reliability.
Key technologies integrated across these product lines include variable speed drives (VSDs) for adaptive performance, intelligent pump controls for real-time optimization, and digital integration via the Grundfos iSOLUTIONS platform, which combines cloud connectivity, specialized software, and edge devices to enable predictive maintenance and system-wide monitoring. Evolution toward energy-efficient models has focused on high-efficiency motors and VSDs, achieving up to 50% reductions in energy use in applications like industrial cooling through optimized controls.
This extensive range reflects a strategic shift from isolated pumps to integrated water-moving solutions, where iSOLUTIONS facilitates holistic system intelligence for enhanced operational efficiency and sustainability.
Grundfos provides specialized digital solutions for water distribution networks, including Grundfos Utility Analytics and Demand Driven Distribution (DDD). Grundfos Utility Analytics offers zone-based water balance analysis, including for District Metered Areas (DMAs), real-time monitoring, non-revenue water (NRW) reduction, leak detection, and predictive insights through modules supporting monitoring, diagnosis, prediction, and planning functions. Demand Driven Distribution (DDD) employs intelligent pumps, sensors, and algorithms for dynamic pressure management in zoned networks, reducing leaks, energy consumption, and pipe bursts while maintaining stable supply. These offerings build on the Grundfos iSOLUTIONS platform to deliver targeted optimization for water utilities.

Industries and Applications

Grundfos serves diverse sectors through its advanced pump and water management solutions, with primary focus on water utilities for distribution and treatment, building services for heating, ventilation, air conditioning (HVAC), and hot water systems in commercial and residential settings, industrial manufacturing for process water handling, agriculture for irrigation systems, and wastewater management for treatment and transport. These industries represent the core of Grundfos's operations, organized into four main divisions: Commercial Building Services, Domestic Building Services, Industry, and Water Utility, which includes advanced digital tools for optimizing water distribution such as Grundfos Utility Analytics for District Metered Area (DMA) analytics, zone-based monitoring, leak detection, and non-revenue water (NRW) reduction, and Demand Driven Distribution (DDD) for dynamic pressure management to reduce leaks, energy use, and pipe bursts (see Core Product Lines for details).
In terms of market distribution as of 2024, building servicescombining commercial and domestic segmentsaccount for approximately 45% of Grundfos's revenue, underscoring its prominence in urban and residential infrastructure, while water utilities contribute around 15%, and the industry segment about 24%. In the first half of 2025, the company reported overall sales growth of 5.1%, reaching DKK 17.2 billion, with a full-year target of 4-7% growth amid strong performance in key markets. Agriculture and wastewater applications are integrated within water utility and industry divisions, supporting sustainable resource management amid global water scarcity.
Real-world applications highlight Grundfos's versatility, such as deploying high-efficiency pumps in desalination plants to facilitate large-scale water production in arid regions, integrating digital controls in smart building systems for optimized HVAC performance and energy savings, and providing circulation pumps for industrial cooling in data centers and manufacturing facilities to maintain precise temperature control. In agriculture, Grundfos irrigation pumps enable precise water delivery for crop optimization, reducing waste in variable climate conditions. By 2025, the company emphasizes climate-resilient solutions, including resilient pumping systems for wastewater handling during extreme weather and industrial processes vulnerable to water stress.
Grundfos customizes its offerings for specialized sectors, developing hygienic, corrosion-resistant pumps for food and beverage production to meet sanitary standards and precision dosing systems for pharmaceuticals to ensure contaminant-free processing. These tailored solutions incorporate intelligent monitoring to enhance reliability and efficiency across applications.

Global Operations

International Expansion

Grundfos initiated its international expansion in 1960 with the establishment of its first foreign subsidiary in Wahlstedt, Germany, focusing on sales and production to serve the European market. This was followed by further entries into Europe, including a subsidiary in Austria in 1971 and France in 1972, marking the company's growing presence beyond Denmark. In 1973, Grundfos founded its first subsidiary in the United States in Fresno, California, with official operations commencing in 1976 to tap into the North American demand for pumping solutions.
During the 1980s and 1990s, Grundfos accelerated its global outreach, entering Asia with the setup of operations in Singapore in 1984 and establishing Grundfos Pumps (Suzhou) Ltd. in China in 1995 for local production of sanitary and utility pumps. Expansion into Latin America began in the late 1980s through sales networks and subsidiaries, supporting regional water infrastructure needs. By 2000, the company had operations in more than 40 countries, bolstered by a network of over 70 subsidiaries worldwide.
In the 2010s and up to 2025, Grundfos deepened its footprint in emerging markets, with India serving as a key growth hub since its entry in 1998 through manufacturing and sales facilities that achieved significant localization by the mid-2020s. Penetration into Africa advanced notably in 2016 with new facilities in Nairobi, Kenya, to enhance East African activities, followed by partnerships addressing water access in rural areas across the continent. The United States emerged as a pivotal market, with sales hubs and production expansions, including a new headquarters in Brookshire, Texas, in 2022 and increased capacity in 2025 to meet domestic demand.
Central to Grundfos's international strategy has been the development of regional manufacturing hubs to minimize logistics costs and supply chain disruptions, as outlined in its global operations framework. Additionally, the company adapts its pump technologies to address local water challenges, such as customizing solutions for India's industrial needs and Africa's variable water scarcity through energy-efficient, context-specific designs. Key acquisitions, such as those in the water treatment sector, have further supported this expansion by integrating complementary technologies into new regions.

Manufacturing and Facilities

Grundfos maintains a global production network spanning more than 60 countries, with over 100 subsidiaries and sales companies supporting its operations. The company's headquarters and primary manufacturing facility are located in Bjerringbro, Denmark, where it originated and continues to centralize key production activities. Additional major production sites include Fresno, California, and the recently expanded facility in Brookshire, Texas, in the United States; Changshu in Jiangsu Province, China; Szkesfehrvr in Hungary; and Inija in Serbia, which serves as the third-largest production site globally, outputting over 4 million pump units annually. These sites reflect Grundfos's strategic international expansion, enabling localized manufacturing to meet regional demands.
The company operates more than 15 manufacturing facilities worldwide, collectively producing over 15 million pump units each year, encompassing circulator, submersible, and centrifugal pumps. Facilities emphasize advanced automation systems and lean manufacturing principles to enhance efficiency and sustainability, as demonstrated in initiatives like the Lighthouse project, which transforms operations into optimized, managed environments. For instance, the 2025 expansion in Brookshire, Texas, incorporates modern automation to double U.S. production capacity and reduce lead times, creating logistical efficiencies across North America. In 2025, the acquisition of Newterra added four production sites in the US and Canada, enhancing water treatment capabilities.
Grundfos's supply chain strategy prioritizes sustainability and risk management, with the majority of pump components sourced from global suppliers and approximately 12% procured locally to support regional operations. The company invests in supplier compliance, achieving a 91% CSR audit rate in 2024 and onboarding 87 suppliers to the EcoVadis platform for performance monitoring. In 2025, post-acquisition developments include the integration of Newterra's four production sites in the U.S. and Canada, enhancing water treatment capabilities and expanding the overall facility network for advanced water solutions.

Business Development

Key Acquisitions

Grundfos has undertaken a total of seven acquisitions as of September 2025, with a strong emphasis on bolstering its expertise in water treatment technologies and solutions. These moves have primarily targeted companies specializing in purification, desalination, filtration, and wastewater management, enabling Grundfos to develop a more comprehensive portfolio in sustainable water handling.
A notable early acquisition occurred in December 2008, when Grundfos purchased Yeomans Chicago Corporation (YCC), a U.S.-based manufacturer of wastewater pumps and related equipment located in Aurora, Illinois. This deal expanded Grundfos' offerings in municipal and industrial wastewater applications, integrating YCC's specialized dry-pit and submersible pumps into its broader pump ecosystem and enhancing market penetration in North American infrastructure projects. The acquisition contributed to portfolio diversification by combining YCC's engineering strengths with Grundfos' global distribution network, supporting long-term growth in environmental services.
Since 2020, Grundfos has pursued a deliberate strategy to construct an integrated water treatment business, focusing on acquisitions that add technical depth in purification processes and sustainable practices. In June 2020, Grundfos acquired Eurowater (formally Silhorko-Eurowater A/S), a Danish firm headquartered in Snderborg specializing in customized water treatment systems, including ion exchange, filtration, and softening technologies for industrial and municipal use. This move marked Grundfos' entry into advanced water conditioning, primarily serving European markets and aligning with sustainability goals by improving water reuse efficiency. The integration allowed Grundfos to offer end-to-end solutions, from pumping to treatment, reducing operational footprints for clients in sectors like food and beverage.
Building on this foundation, Grundfos acquired Mechanical Equipment Company, Inc. (MECO) in November 2021, a U.S.-based leader in desalination and ultrapure water systems headquartered in Pasadena, Texas. MECO's expertise in thermal and membrane-based desalination technologies complemented Grundfos' pump solutions, enabling comprehensive offerings for high-purity water needs in pharmaceuticals, power generation, and semiconductor industries. The acquisition added over 200 employees and expanded Grundfos' capabilities in energy-efficient water recovery, supporting global efforts to address water scarcity.
In early 2023, Grundfos further strengthened its filtration and purification portfolio through the acquisition of Water Works by its subsidiary MECO, targeting California-based operations focused on ultrapure water treatment for critical applications. This enhanced Grundfos' North American presence in advanced filtration systems, integrating specialized reverse osmosis and deionization processes to meet stringent regulatory standards. The deal underscored the ongoing strategy of layering complementary technologies to create scalable, sustainable water solutions.
In July 2023, Grundfos acquired Metasphere, a UK-based provider of telemetry and analytics solutions for water and wastewater networks, from XPV Water Partners. Metasphere's sensor technology and data platforms enable real-time monitoring and predictive insights for leak detection and asset management, enhancing Grundfos' digital water management capabilities. This acquisition added expertise in smart water infrastructure, supporting utilities in reducing non-revenue water losses and improving operational efficiency across global markets.
In September 2024, Grundfos completed the acquisition of Culligan's Commercial & Industrial (C&I) business in Italy, France, and the UK, expanding its water treatment offerings in softening, filtration, and reverse osmosis systems for commercial and industrial clients. This move bolstered Grundfos' European presence in the sector, integrating Culligan C&I's established customer base and service network to deliver tailored solutions for sectors like hospitality, manufacturing, and healthcare, while advancing sustainability through efficient water purification technologies.
The most recent acquisition, completed on August 20, 2025, involved Newterra, a Pittsburgh-based U.S. firm providing modular water and wastewater treatment systems with operations extending into Canada. Newterra's portable and containerized solutions for remote and industrial sites bolstered Grundfos' platform by adding expertise in decentralized treatment, including biological and advanced oxidation processes. Acquired from private equity firm Frontenac for an undisclosed amount, this move integrates approximately 280 employees and reinforces Grundfos' North American footprint, particularly in mining, oil and gas, and municipal sectors, while advancing sustainability through reduced chemical usage and energy optimization.
Overall, these acquisitions since 2020 have systematically built Grundfos' water treatment ecosystem, incorporating innovations in desalination, filtration, and wastewater handling to address global challenges like resource conservation and climate resilience. By prioritizing companies with proven sustainability impacts, Grundfos has enhanced its ability to deliver integrated systems that minimize environmental footprints.

Strategic Partnerships

Grundfos has pursued strategic partnerships with utilities and global water NGOs to enhance water access and sustainability initiatives worldwide. These collaborations often focus on deploying energy-efficient pumping solutions in underserved regions, leveraging Grundfos's expertise alongside partners' local networks. For instance, in October 2025, Grundfos established a partnership with UNICEF in Serbia to mitigate climate change impacts on children and youth through improved water and sanitation infrastructure. Similarly, through the Grundfos Foundation, the company works with UN agencies and NGOs such as Water Mission to implement solar-powered groundwater access projects, as seen in Tanzania where such efforts have transformed communities by providing reliable clean water sources.
In addition to NGO alliances, Grundfos collaborates with tech firms to integrate IoT capabilities into its pumps, enabling remote monitoring and predictive maintenance. A notable example is the partnership with Myriota, which developed satellite-connected solar pumps for off-grid water management, reducing operational costs and manual interventions in remote areas. Another key alliance is with Telenor Connexion, where IoT technology supports advanced water flow management and data-driven optimization for utilities and industries. These tech integrations facilitate real-time system performance insights without requiring full ownership of the enabling technologies.
While specific joint ventures in Asia for local R&D are limited in public records, Grundfos maintains collaborative ties with regional entities to adapt solutions for markets like China, including through its established R&D center that supports co-development with local stakeholders. Broader examples include partnerships with organizations like Save the Children for a three-year WASH program starting in 2024, aimed at delivering safe water and sanitation to vulnerable populations globally, and with the International Committee of the Red Cross for training engineers in efficient water pumping systems. These efforts align with UN Sustainable Development Goal 6 by combining Grundfos's hardware with partners' humanitarian reach.
As of 2025, Grundfos's partnerships emphasize the energy transition, particularly alliances with renewable energy companies to integrate pump systems into solar farms and decarbonization projects. In July 2025, the company joined the ALLICE Alliance to promote the water-energy nexus, accelerating industrial decarbonization through collaborative innovations. Similarly, the partnership with InPipe Energy targets municipalities and industries with intelligent renewable energy solutions for water treatment, while collaborations like the one with Outokumpu focus on greening supply chains with low-carbon materials for pump production. These alliances have enabled shared technology development, such as optimized pump efficiency in renewable setups, and expanded market access in emerging green sectors without direct ownership stakes.

Innovation and Sustainability

Research and Development

Grundfos allocates approximately 5% of its annual revenue to research and development, supporting a workforce of more than 2,000 dedicated employees focused on advancing pump and water technologies. In 2024, the company invested DKK 1,941 million in R&D, equivalent to 5.8% of net revenue, marking an increase from 5.3% the previous year. This investment underscores Grundfos's commitment to innovation, with the majority of activities centralized at its headquarters in Bjerringbro, Denmark, where 2,060 staff members contribute to core R&D functions as of year-end 2024.
To address regional needs, Grundfos maintains global R&D labs, including a key facility in Singapore that enhances capabilities for the Asia-Pacific market through closer collaboration with local industries and partners. These distributed efforts enable targeted advancements, such as the integration of artificial intelligence in smart pumps for predictive maintenance. For instance, the Grundfos Machine Health solution uses AI-driven sensors to monitor equipment in real-time, detecting anomalies and optimizing uptime while reducing maintenance costs. Additionally, Grundfos has pioneered low-energy impeller designs, exemplified by the S-TUBE impeller, which features a vaneless closed-channel configuration to minimize energy loss and prevent clogging in demanding applications.
In 2025, notable milestones include the launch of collaborative R&D projects for carbon-neutral water systems, such as the US$9.4 million Grundfos Foundation-funded initiative with Nanyang Technological University and Danish institutions to pioneer sustainable cooling technologies for megacities. In June 2025, Grundfos acquired Newterra, a Pittsburgh-based water and wastewater treatment company, to enhance its sustainable water solutions portfolio. Grundfos's intellectual property portfolio supports these efforts, with over 1,900 patents granted since 2009 and more than 1,000 active worldwide, including recent filings in digital water management like the hydrogeological management system for aquifer water content control. In 2024 alone, the company filed 45 new patents and secured 108 grants, reflecting sustained innovation in efficient water solutions.

Environmental and Social Initiatives

Grundfos has established ambitious environmental goals aligned with global climate objectives, including a commitment to achieve net-zero greenhouse gas emissions across Scopes 1, 2, and 3 by 2050, with interim targets to reduce absolute Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 50% and Scope 3 emissions by 25% by 2030, using a 2020 baseline year. These targets, validated by the Science Based Targets initiative, emphasize decarbonization of operations and supply chains. Grundfos is on track to reach its 2030 Scope 1 and 2 reduction goal five years ahead of schedule in 2025 through partnerships enhancing energy efficiency and renewable sourcing.
Key initiatives include efforts to minimize water and energy consumption in products and operations, with the company enabling end-users to save 1.5 billion cubic meters of water in 2024 alone through efficient pumping solutions. The Grundfos Foundation plays a central role in global sustainability, funding projects to provide clean water access in developing countries; for instance, a partnership with UNICEF has delivered safe drinking water to nearly 200,000 people across 31 rural communities in western Tanzania. Additional collaborations, such as with Save the Children, focus on improving water, sanitation, and hygiene for vulnerable populations in regions like sub-Saharan Africa.
On the social front, Grundfos promotes diversity and inclusion, with a target of 27% women in leadership positions by 2025; as of 2024, this stood at 25%, reflecting steady progress through employee resource groups like Women@Grundfos. The company also achieved its goal of 33% female representation on the Board of Directors in 2024. Community investments via the Grundfos Foundation support education and inclusion initiatives, including partnerships to enhance access to schooling in underserved areas, such as a 2025 collaboration with UNICEF and the LEGO Foundation in Ethiopia.
In 2025, Grundfos advanced aquifer protection by joining the Water Resilience Coalition and signing its pledge to deliver measurable water security impacts by 2030, emphasizing sustainable groundwater management to prevent depletion. For circular economy practices in manufacturing, the company exceeded its 2025 target by reducing waste to landfill by 73% since 2018 and collected over 89,000 kg of pumps for recycling in 2024; a notable update includes the adoption of low-emission Circle Green steel from Outokumpu for production starting early 2025.
