 10,000 (ten thousand) is the natural number following 9,999 and preceding 10,001.
In the decimal numeral system, it is the smallest five-digit number, written as 1 followed by four zeros, representing one group of ten thousands. It is also known in words as "ten thousand."
Mathematically, 10,000 is an even composite number with prime factorization 2 5, making it the product of two distinct primes raised to the fourth power. It is a perfect square, equal to 100, and simultaneously a power of ten, specifically 10. This dual property positions it uniquely in number theory, as it marks the threshold for five-digit integers while embodying exponential and quadratic forms. Additionally, 10,000 is an abundant number, where the sum of its proper divisors (14,211) exceeds the number itself, and it has exactly 25 positive divisors.
In historical numeral systems, 10,000 is denoted in Roman numerals as X (X with an overbar), indicating multiplication by 1,000. Its Euler's totient function value is 4,000, and the sum of its digits is 1, yielding a digital root of 1. These properties highlight 10,000's role in arithmetic, place value education, and computational contexts, such as scaling in scientific notation where it equals 1 10.

A key climatic event preceding this shift was the abrupt end of the Younger Dryas, a period of sudden cooling that lasted from about 12,900 to 11,700 years before present (approximately 10,900 to 9600 BCE), after which global temperatures rose rapidly, stabilizing into the Holocene's warmer conditions. Geologically, the 10,000-year span represents a brief interval within the broader context of Quaternary ice age cycles, during which post-glacial melting contributed to a sea level rise of roughly 120 meters from the Last Glacial Maximum, reshaping coastlines and enabling human expansion into previously glaciated regions.

Albrecht Drer's woodcut series The Martyrdom of the Ten Thousand (15081510), inspired by the medieval legend of the martyrdom of 10,000 Christian soldiers by the Persian king Saporat on Mount Ararat, depicts chaotic scenes of persecution in the Northern Renaissance style, using the number to symbolize collective Christian sacrifice and resilience. In modern installations, Song Dong's Waste Not (2005) arranges over 10,000 everyday objects hoarded by his mother during China's Cultural Revolution, transforming personal detritus into a monumental sculpture that critiques consumerism and familial memory.
The concept of the "ten thousand things" permeates Chinese art catalogs and catalogs, rooted in Taoist philosophy, where it signifies modular production and the infinite variety of the universe; Lothar Ledderose's seminal study Ten Thousand Things: Module and Mass Production in Chinese Art (2000) examines how ancient artisans replicated motifs across bronzes, jades, and ceramics to achieve this multiplicity. In Byzantine mosaics, the profusion of tesseraeoften numbering in the tens of thousands per panelsymbolizes divine infinity and eternal light, as seen in the gold-backed compositions of Hagia Sophia (6th century), where shimmering tiles evoke the boundless glory of heaven.

In film, television, and games

In film, the number 10,000 often evokes prehistoric or ancient settings, as seen in the 2008 epic 10,000 BC, directed by Roland Emmerich and starring Steven Strait as D'Leh, a young mammoth hunter who leads his tribe on a perilous journey across uncharted lands to rescue a loved one and confront an advanced civilization enslaving their people. The film blends action-adventure with speculative depictions of early human societies around 10,000 BCE, emphasizing themes of survival, prophecy, and migration, though it takes significant creative liberties with historical accuracy.
Television series frequently use 10,000 as a marker for vast temporal scales in science fiction narratives. In Stargate SG-1 and its spin-off Stargate Atlantis, the Ancientsan advanced human precursor raceabandoned their city of Atlantis approximately 10,000 years ago after a devastating war with the Wraith in the Pegasus Galaxy, fleeing through a wormhole to Earth and seeding human evolution there. This timeline is central to episodes like "Before I Sleep" (Stargate Atlantis, Season 1, Episode 15), where the Atlantis expedition witnesses the city's evacuation and the Ancients' desperate stand against the Wraith. Similarly, Doctor Who employs 10,000-year spans to highlight futuristic decay and human resilience; in the episode "The End of the World" (Series 1, Episode 2), the Tenth Doctor transports Rose Tyler to Platform One in the year 5 billion, but references a nearer future of 10,000 years ahead as the dawn of the New Roman Empire, underscoring the show's theme of time's inexorable march.
In video games, 10,000 BC inspires survival and strategy titles rooted in prehistoric eras. Primitive Era: 10000 BC, a mobile casual strategy game developed by Tap4Fun, places players as tribal leaders building civilizations from the Stone Age, managing resources, alliances, and conflicts among ancient groups like the Gendaya and Mesopotamians to thrive in a harsh, mammoth-filled world. The 2008 film 10,000 BC also spawned a promotional browser game, "Master Hunter," where players simulate mammoth hunts using spear-throwing mechanics to immerse in the movie's tribal warfare and exploration themes. These titles prioritize resource gathering and survival mechanics over historical fidelity, often featuring exaggerated elements like dinosaur encounters despite the era's actual timeline.

Miscellaneous uses

In sports and recreation

In athletics, the 10,000-meter run, often shortened to the 10K in road racing, emerged as a standard distance during the global running boom of the 1970s, when events like the Peachtree Road Race in Atlanta (launched in 1970) and the New York Mini 10K (introduced in 1972) helped popularize it among recreational and competitive runners alike. This distance balances endurance and speed, attracting participants from elite athletes to amateurs, with major races such as the Chicago 10K drawing thousands annually. The men's world record stands at 26 minutes and 11.00 seconds, achieved by Ugandan runner Joshua Cheptegei on October 7, 2020, during a track event in Valencia, Spain, shattering the previous mark by 6.53 seconds.
In cricket, accumulating 10,000 runs in Test matches represents a pinnacle of batting longevity and skill, with only a select group of players reaching this threshold over careers spanning decades. Indian legend Sachin Tendulkar became the fifth cricketer to accomplish this feat on March 16, 2005, during a match against Pakistan at Eden Gardens in Kolkata, reaching the milestone with a score of 27 in his innings after 195 matches.
Recreational activities often incorporate 10,000 as a motivational benchmark, notably in pedometer-based fitness goals. The 10,000-steps-a-day target originated in Japan in the 1960s as a marketing strategy for the Yamasa "manpo-kei" pedometer, released ahead of the 1964 Tokyo Olympics to promote walking as healthy exercise equivalent to about five miles for an average adult. This figure has since become a global standard in wearable tech and health apps, though research indicates benefits plateau around 7,000-8,000 steps for many individuals. In hiking, trails demanding 10,000 feet of elevation gain test endurance and acclimatization, exemplified by California's Cactus to Clouds route, which ascends from Palm Springs (under 500 feet above sea level) to San Jacinto Peak (10,834 feet) over 23 miles, often requiring 10-12 hours for fit hikers.
Modern Olympic Games routinely feature approximately 10,000 athletes competing across dozens of sports, as seen in the Sydney 2000 edition with over 10,600 participants from 200 nations and the Paris 2024 Games with 10,500 athletes.

In economics and finance

In the realm of currency, the United States issued a $10,000 Federal Reserve Note from 1918 to 1945, featuring the portrait of Salmon P. Chase, who served as Secretary of the Treasury under President Abraham Lincoln. This high-denomination bill was designed primarily for large-scale transactions between banks and was discontinued from circulation by the U.S. Treasury in 1969 as part of efforts to combat organized crime and simplify monetary policy, though existing notes remain legal tender. Similarly, Japan's 10,000 yen banknote, introduced in 1958 as part of the country's third series of currency releases, represents the highest circulating denomination and has undergone multiple redesigns for security, with the latest series featuring industrialist Eiichi Shibusawa on the obverse and advanced anti-counterfeiting elements like holograms.
In financial markets, the number 10,000 marks significant milestones, such as the Dow Jones Industrial Average first closing above 10,000 on March 29, 1999, amid a period of strong economic growth and investor optimism during the late 1990s bull market. This threshold symbolized a psychological barrier for investors, reflecting broader market maturity after the index's inception in 1896. Additionally, in stock trading, 10,000 shares often serves as a benchmark for block trades, defined by major exchanges as transactions exceeding this volume or a notional value over $200,000, which are typically executed off-exchange to minimize market impact. The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission's Regulation SHO also uses 10,000 shares as a threshold for identifying securities with persistent settlement failures, triggering additional reporting requirements to promote efficient trading.
Economic indicators frequently reference 10,000 as a developmental benchmark, particularly in emerging markets where GDP per capita approaching this level signals transition to middle-income status; for instance, countries like Malaysia reached approximately $10,000 in the early 2010s, highlighting improved living standards and industrialization. In investment performance analysis, "growth of 10K" is a standard metric that illustrates the hypothetical appreciation of a $10,000 principal over time, often used to evaluate mutual funds or indices against benchmarks like the S&P 500.
Historically, under the 19th-century gold standard, 10,000 ounces of gold held substantial value, equating to roughly $206,700 at the fixed U.S. price of $20.67 per ounce established by the Coinage Act of 1834 and maintained through much of the era, representing a considerable fortune equivalent to the annual income of thousands of workers or funding for major industrial ventures. This valuation underscored gold's role in stabilizing international trade and currency convertibility during the period.

In politics and demographics

In the United States, the U.S. Census Bureau, based on 2020 Census criteria, defines urban areas as densely settled territories with a core of at least 2,000 housing units or 5,000 persons, without distinguishing between urbanized areas and urban clusters as in prior censuses. This positions communities of around 10,000 people as qualifying urban areas that often serve as small cities or emerging urban centers for local governance and service provision. Globally, United Nations estimates indicate that approximately 15,000 babies are born every hour, highlighting the scale of demographic growth and the challenges in health and resource allocation for rapidly expanding populations.
In politics, 10,000 has emerged as a common benchmark for citizen participation thresholds, such as the number of signatures required to qualify ballot initiatives or referendums in various U.S. states and localities; for instance, Shasta County, California, mandates 10,000 verified signatures for certain county-level referendums to appear on the ballot. Historically, the Achaemenid Empire maintained an elite infantry unit known as the Persian Immortals, fixed at precisely 10,000 soldiers to ensure perpetual readiness, as described by ancient historian Herodotus and corroborated in scholarly analyses of Persian military organization.
Elections at the local level often reflect modest scales of engagement, with voter turnouts reaching around 10,000 in mid-sized U.S. communities during city council or municipal races, underscoring the intimate nature of grassroots democracy in such settings. United Nations documents, including reports submitted to the General Assembly, frequently reference cohorts of 10,000 refugees in discussions of humanitarian aid and resettlement, such as efforts to integrate or relocate specific groups from conflict zones.
The "10,000 hours rule," introduced by Malcolm Gladwell in his 2008 book Outliers: The Story of Success, asserts that mastery in complex skills typically demands about 10,000 hours of deliberate practice, a notion drawn from psychologist Anders Ericsson's research on expertise; this idea has shaped policy debates in education and workforce development, advocating for structured training programs to foster proficiency rather than relying solely on talent. However, Ericsson later clarified that the figure is an average for certain domains and not a universal threshold, stressing the importance of the quality of deliberate practice.
