 The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) of the United Nations is a specialized intergovernmental agency founded on October 16, 1945, through a constitution signed by 42 nations at its inaugural conference in Quebec, Canada, with the primary mandate to combat hunger, enhance nutrition, promote sustainable agricultural development, and improve rural livelihoods worldwide. Headquartered in Rome, Italy, since 1951 after initial operations in Washington, D.C., FAO serves 194 member nations and two associate members, operating as a neutral forum for policy dialogue, technical assistance, and data dissemination on food security and agrifood systems.
FAO's core activities encompass four main pillars: sustainable food and agriculture, nutrition and food systems, resilient livelihoods, and inclusive rural transformation, including the development of international standards like the Codex Alimentarius to ensure food safety and fair trade practices. Among its notable achievements, FAO contributed to the global eradication of the livestock disease rinderpest in 2011, the first animal disease eliminated through coordinated international efforts, and has supported increases in global food production while advancing standards for fisheries management and plant genetic resources conservation. Under Director-General Qu Dongyu, who assumed office in 2019 and was re-elected in 2023, the organization has emphasized agrifood systems transformation to align with Sustainable Development Goals, particularly ending hunger and poverty, with a biennial budget exceeding $2.6 billion to fund programs across 130 countries.
Despite these efforts, FAO has faced persistent criticism for bureaucratic bloat, operational inefficiencies, and politicization, with reports highlighting mediocrity in staff performance and a tendency toward ideological influences that undermine practical outcomes in hunger reduction. Previous leadership, such as under long-term Director-General Jacques Diouf, drew rebukes for excessive bureaucracy that hampered responsiveness, while recent proposals under Qu Dongyu to expand authority and budgets have raised concerns about further entrenching centralized control amid ongoing global food security challenges. These issues underscore tensions between FAO's aspirational goals and the practical constraints of multilateral governance, where member state politics often prioritize agendas over evidence-based agricultural advancements.

Historical Development

Founding and Initial Mandate (1943-1950s)

The origins of the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) trace to the United Nations Conference on Food and Agriculture, convened from May 18 to June 3, 1943, in Hot Springs, Virginia, United States, at the invitation of President Franklin D. Roosevelt amid World War II concerns over global food shortages. This gathering of representatives from 44 governments established an Interim Commission on Food and Agriculture to prepare for a permanent body, emphasizing post-war food security, nutrition improvement, and agricultural production enhancement. 
The Interim Commission drafted the FAO Constitution and coordinated preparatory work, culminating in the organization's formal establishment at the First Session of the FAO Conference, held from October 16 to November 1, 1945, in Quebec City, Canada. On October 16, 1945, 42 nations signed the Constitution, with 34 signatures sufficient to bring it into force, designating FAO as the first specialized agency of the United Nations focused on food and agriculture. Initial headquarters were set in Washington, D.C., reflecting the United States' role in hosting early operations.
The FAO Constitution outlined the initial mandate to achieve four primary aims: raising levels of nutrition and standards of living; securing improvements in the efficiency of food production and distribution; bettering conditions of rural populations; and contributing to the expansion, stability, and prosperity of the world economy through agriculture, forestry, fisheries, and nutrition advancements. Core functions included collecting, analyzing, interpreting, and disseminating information on nutrition, food, and agriculture; providing technical assistance and policy advice; promoting research and education; and facilitating international cooperation on commodity arrangements and rural welfare. This mandate prioritized empirical data gathering and practical interventions over ideological prescriptions, addressing immediate post-war reconstruction needs such as food aid distribution and agricultural rehabilitation in war-torn regions.
In the late 1940s and 1950s, FAO implemented its mandate through early programs like the establishment of the Forestry Division in 1946 and fisheries initiatives, alongside technical missions to member countries for soil conservation, crop improvement, and livestock development. Under the first Director-General, John Boyd Orr (19451948), emphasis was placed on global nutritional surveys and advocacy for equitable food distribution, though internal debates arose over the balance between production increases and consumption guarantees. Successor Norris E. Dodd (19481956) shifted toward technical assistance, aligning with emerging U.S.-led development aid frameworks, while FAO compiled foundational statistical databases on world agricultural output to inform policy. By the mid-1950s, membership had grown to over 60 nations, with activities expanding to include the 1952 International Rice Commission and early work on arid zone farming, reflecting adaptation to diverse global agricultural challenges without altering the core constitutional objectives.

Expansion Amid Decolonization and Green Revolution (1960s-1980s)

The Food and Agriculture Organization experienced substantial growth in membership and operational scope during the 1960s and 1970s, coinciding with widespread decolonization across Africa and Asia. Newly independent states, including over 30 African nations between 1960 and 1970, rapidly acceded to FAO, increasing its total membership from approximately 77 in 1960 to 141 by 1980. This expansion shifted FAO's priorities toward technical assistance for resource-poor countries, including land and water inventories, agricultural censuses, and capacity-building in fisheries and forestry sectors, often in direct response to post-colonial governance challenges.
In 1960, under Director-General B.R. Sen, FAO initiated the Freedom from Hunger Campaign (FFHC), a decade-long effort to mobilize public awareness, voluntary donations, and technical aid against global malnutrition, raising over $300 million by 1970 for projects in developing regions. The campaign facilitated field operations in decolonized areas, such as soil surveys in sub-Saharan Africa and extension services in South Asia, emphasizing practical interventions over ideological frameworks. Complementing this, FAO established the Fertilizer Programme in 1961 to enhance crop yields through efficient input use, directly supporting the adoption of high-yielding crop varieties in Asia and Latin America during the Green Revolution's peak.
FAO's contributions to the Green Revolution involved promoting hybrid seeds, irrigation expansion, and fertilizer application, which collectively boosted cereal production by an estimated 2-3% annually in adopting regions from the mid-1960s to the 1980s, averting famines in countries like India and Pakistan. However, outcomes varied; while yields rosewheat production in Mexico, for instance, tripled between 1960 and 1970implementation challenges in Africa, including inadequate infrastructure and soil variability, limited spillover effects, highlighting the technology's dependence on complementary investments. FAO emphasized integrated packages of modern inputs and farmer training, but critiques noted uneven benefits favoring larger farms and potential environmental costs from chemical overuse.
The 1974 World Food Conference, convened amid grain shortages and influenced by FAO's data on production shortfalls, affirmed every individual's right to adequate food as a common goal and spurred the establishment of the International Fund for Agricultural Development (IFAD) in 1977, with initial pledges of $1 billion for rural poor in developing nations. Under Director-General Edouard Saouma from 1976 to 1991, FAO intensified rural development programs, including agroforestry initiatives and fishery management in Pacific island states, though internal shifts toward politicization began to dilute purely technical foci by the late 1970s. By the 1980s, FAO's field presence had grown to over 100 countries, with budget allocations for technical cooperation rising to 40% of expenditures, reflecting adaptation to a multipolar world of sovereign developing states.

Post-Cold War Reorientation and Crises (1990s-2000s)

Following the end of the Cold War, the FAO shifted its focus toward supporting structural adjustment programs in developing countries, emphasizing market liberalization, reduced government subsidies, and export-oriented agricultural growth to address fiscal imbalances and promote efficiency. These programs, often implemented under the guidance of international financial institutions, aimed to integrate agriculture into global markets but yielded mixed results, with some analyses indicating reduced public investment in rural infrastructure and increased vulnerability for smallholder farmers. Under Director-General Jacques Diouf, elected in 1994, the organization pursued internal reforms to enhance decentralization and results-based management, adapting to a multipolar world by prioritizing poverty alleviation and sustainable resource use amid declining official development assistance.
The 1996 World Food Summit, hosted by the FAO in Rome, marked a pivotal reorientation, where 186 countries adopted the Rome Declaration on World Food Security and committed to halving the number of undernourished people by 2015from an estimated 824 million in the base period of 1990-1992 to no more than 412 million. The accompanying Plan of Action outlined 20 commitments, including boosting agricultural productivity, ensuring access to food, and fostering international trade, though implementation relied on national policies and voluntary funding, leading to critiques of insufficient binding mechanisms.
Persistent challenges in the 1990s included stagnant progress on hunger reduction, exacerbated by conflicts, economic liberalization's uneven impacts, and bureaucratic inefficiencies within the FAO that hindered effective aid delivery. The 2000s brought acute crises, notably the 2007-2008 global food price surge, during which the FAO's real food price index rose 27 percent in 2007 alone, driven by factors such as biofuel demand, high energy costs, adverse weather, and export restrictions by major producers. In response, the FAO convened a High-Level Conference on World Food Security in 2008, advocating for increased investment in agriculture, removal of distortive subsidies, and enhanced safety nets, while warning against protectionist measures that worsened shortages. Despite these efforts, the crisis highlighted longstanding underinvestment in agriculture, with FAO data showing that between 1990 and 2000, food aid volumes had declined significantly, contributing to vulnerabilities in low-income regions.

Contemporary Challenges and Reports (2010s-2025)

In the 2010s and early 2020s, the FAO confronted persistent stagnation in global hunger reduction, with undernourishment levels remaining high despite earlier declines. The organization's annual State of Food Security and Nutrition in the World (SOFI) reports indicated that approximately 925 million people were undernourished in 2010, a figure that fluctuated but hovered between 700 and 800 million by the late 2010s before spiking due to multiple shocks. By 2023, around 733 million people faced hunger, equivalent to one in eleven globally and one in five in Africa, with only modest declines to an estimated 673 million (8.2% prevalence) in 2024. These trends underscored the failure to meet Sustainable Development Goal 2 targets for ending hunger by 2030, as progress stalled post-2015 amid economic slowdowns and policy shortfalls.
Key reports highlighted intersecting challenges exacerbating food insecurity. The 2017 The Future of Food and Agriculture: Trends and Challenges emphasized demographic pressures, resource degradation, and climate variability as threats requiring paradigm shifts in agricultural paradigms, yet implementation lagged. SOFI editions from 2020 onward documented acute impacts from the COVID-19 pandemic, which disrupted supply chains and inflated food prices, alongside the 2022 Russia-Ukraine war that curtailed exports of wheat, maize, and oilseeds from major producers, affecting global markets. By 2025, SOFI reported that persistent inflation eroded purchasing power, particularly in low-income countries, with around 295 million people facing high acute food insecurity in analyzed hotspots. Climate change intensified these issues, with FAO analyses showing accelerated warming during 20112020 undermining agrifood systems through extreme weather, reduced yields, and heightened disaster frequency impacting 2.5 billion rural livelihoods. 
The FAO responded with emergency initiatives, data tools for market monitoring, and policy advisories, including assessments of war-induced risks and support for resilient supply chains in regions like Europe and Central Asia. However, the organization faced criticisms for bureaucratic inefficiencies and mediocre operational effectiveness, which some analysts argued rendered it less agile in addressing root causes of hunger compared to targeted national or private-sector interventions. Reforms under Director-General Jos Graziano da Silva (20112019) aimed to streamline operations and enhance field impact, yet persistent high hunger levels prompted calls for deeper structural changes to prioritize productivity gains over expansive administrative frameworks. 
FAO's Food Price Index illustrates volatility, with sharp rises during the 2022 Ukraine conflict and post-COVID recovery, contributing to affordability crises documented in SOFI reports.

Organizational Structure

Governance Bodies and Leadership

The supreme governing body of the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) is the Conference, which convenes biennially in Rome and consists of delegates from all 194 member nations, two associate members (the Faroe Islands and Tokelau), and one member organization (the European Union). The Conference sets FAO's policies, approves the programme of work and budget, elects members of the Council, and selects the Director-General every four years (with one possible renewal). It also addresses global food and agriculture issues through plenary sessions, committees, and resolutions, with decisions typically requiring a two-thirds majority of members present and voting.
The Council serves as the executive organ, comprising 49 member nations elected by the Conference for staggered three-year terms to ensure regional representation across seven FAO regions (Africa, Asia, Europe, Latin America and the Caribbean, Near East, North America, Southwest Pacific). Meeting approximately five times between Conference sessions, the Council reviews operations, prepares Conference agendas, supervises programmes, and oversees financial matters through its standing committees on finance, programme, and constitutional/legal issues. Independent Chairpersons, elected by the Conference, preside over the Council and its committees to facilitate impartial proceedings.
FAO's leadership is headed by the Director-General, the chief administrative officer responsible for implementing Conference and Council decisions, managing day-to-day operations, and representing the organization internationally. The position is elected by secret ballot in the Conference for a renewable four-year term. Dr. QU Dongyu of China has held the office since 1 August 2019, following his election on 23 June 2019 with 108 votes in the first round. Under the Director-General, core leadership includes deputy directors-general, office heads for strategy and resources, and specialized units, supported by technical committees (e.g., on agriculture, commodities, forestry, and fisheries) that provide expert advice on domain-specific policies. Regional conferences, held periodically for each of FAO's seven regions, further decentralize governance by addressing localized priorities and feeding recommendations upward.

Headquarters, Regional, and Field Operations

The headquarters of the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) is located at Viale delle Terme di Caracalla, 00153 Rome, Italy. This central position in Rome, near the Circo Massimo and Colosseum, has served as the primary base since the early 1950s, following a temporary establishment in Washington, D.C., after the organization's founding in 1945. The facility supports core administrative, policy, and technical functions, including governance meetings and global coordination.
FAO maintains five regional offices to oversee priority activities across continents, focusing on identification, planning, and implementation tailored to regional needs. These include the Regional Office for Africa in Accra, Ghana; the Regional Office for Asia and the Pacific in Bangkok, Thailand; the Regional Office for Europe and Central Asia in Budapest, Hungary; the Regional Office for Latin America and the Caribbean in Bridgetown, Barbados; and the Regional Office for the Near East and North Africa in Cairo, Egypt. Complementing these are 11 subregional offices, such as the Subregional Office for North Africa in Tunis, Tunisia, established in 1996 to support Maghreb countries including Algeria, Libya, Morocco, Mauritania, and Tunisia. 
Field operations are conducted through an extensive decentralized network comprising approximately 74 country representations, two multidisciplinary teams, and various liaison offices, excluding those hosted by other UN agencies. This structure, strengthened since 2012, enhances FAO's effectiveness at national and subnational levels by providing direct policy assistance, program development, and project support to governments. Country offices, led by FAO Representatives, prioritize long-term presence and field programming to address local agricultural challenges and emergencies. The decentralization strategy integrates operational guidance from Rome-based units like the Office of Support to Decentralized Offices, ensuring alignment with global mandates while adapting to regional contexts.

Budget, Financing, and Resource Allocation

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) finances its operations through a combination of assessed contributions from member states and voluntary contributions from governments, international organizations, private entities, and other donors. Assessed contributions, which form the core regular budget, are determined biennially by the FAO Conference based on a scale similar to the United Nations' assessment formula, reflecting members' capacity to pay. In the 2022-23 biennium, these accounted for approximately 24 percent of the integrated budget, with progressive erosion noted due to stagnant nominal increases amid rising costs.
Voluntary contributions constitute the majority of FAO's funding, comprising about 74-76 percent of recent budgets, but they are often earmarked for specific projects, reducing organizational flexibility in allocation. These funds are mobilized through targeted strategies emphasizing partnerships and appeals, with the United States as the largest single contributor, providing roughly USD 317 million in 2024. FAO's integrated funding model, adopted under its Strategic Framework, pools these sources to support the Programme of Work and Budget (PWB), approved every two years by the Conference following Finance Committee review. The 2024-25 PWB totals USD 4 billion, integrating regular programme activities with extrabudgetary resources for technical cooperation and emergency responses.
Resource allocation prioritizes four strategic "betters"better production, nutrition, environment, and lifewhile emphasizing normative functions like standards-setting, which receive dedicated regular budget support to ensure independence from donor influence. Adjustments to the PWB, such as those approved in December 2023, incorporate programmatic shifts based on performance evaluations and emerging needs, with extrabudgetary funds directed toward field operations and innovation. FAO's resource mobilization efforts focus on securing unearmarked or lightly earmarked voluntary funds to address underfunded areas, though reliance on earmarked contributions has led to critiques of donor-driven priorities over global needs. Ongoing challenges include funding shortfalls for core functions and volatility in voluntary pledges, prompting calls for reforms to bolster assessed contributions.

Mandate and Strategic Objectives

Core Goals: Ending Hunger and Promoting Sustainability

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) defines its core goals as defeating hunger through universal food securityensuring regular access to sufficient, high-quality food for active, healthy livesand advancing sustainability via resilient agrifood systems that preserve natural resources. These objectives align with Sustainable Development Goal 2 (Zero Hunger), emphasizing the eradication of malnutrition and poverty as interdependent with environmental stewardship. The FAO's approach prioritizes evidence-based interventions, recognizing that hunger affects approximately 783 million people globally as of recent estimates, necessitating integrated strategies beyond mere production increases.
Central to these goals is the Strategic Framework 20222031, endorsed by the FAO Conference on June 18, 2021, which structures efforts around four strategic objectives termed the "Four Betters." The Better Nutrition objective directly targets ending hunger and malnutrition by promoting nutritious food access, healthy diets for all, and reductions in food loss and waste, which account for about one-third of global food production. This includes supporting policies for diverse, nutrient-rich agrifood systems to address undernourishment, overweight, and micronutrient deficiencies, with a focus on vulnerable populations in low-income countries.
Sustainability is embedded across objectives, particularly through Better Production, which seeks efficient, inclusive agrifood systems via innovations like digital agriculture and blue transformation in fisheries, while avoiding resource depletion; and Better Environment, which aims to protect ecosystems, mitigate climate change impacts (projected to reduce yields by up to 21% by 2050 in some regions), and promote biodiversity conservation. The Better Life objective complements these by fostering equitable rural development and gender equality to ensure hunger reduction benefits reach marginalized groups, aligning with Sustainable Development Goals 1 (No Poverty) and 10 (Reduced Inequalities). Overall, the framework advocates a systems-oriented transformation of agrifood systems to be more efficient, resilient, and sustainable, leveraging cross-cutting tools such as data analytics and governance reforms.

Priority Domains: Agriculture, Forestry, Fisheries, and Nutrition

FAO identifies agriculture as a core domain, focusing on sustainable intensification to boost productivity, enhance resilience to climate variability, and minimize environmental degradation. Through initiatives like conservation agriculture, which involves minimal soil disturbance, crop rotation, and cover cropping, FAO supports smallholder farmers in adopting practices that maintain soil fertility and reduce erosion, as evidenced by global adoption covering over 180 million hectares by 2020. The organization also promotes integrated systems such as agroforestry and crop-livestock integration to optimize resource use and biodiversity, aligning with the Strategic Framework 2022-31's emphasis on efficient, inclusive agrifood systems. These efforts target yield gaps in developing regions, where low-input farming predominates, by providing technical guidance on water-efficient irrigation and pest-resistant varieties without endorsing unproven biotechnologies absent rigorous field validation.
In forestry, FAO prioritizes sustainable management to halt deforestation, which claims 420 million hectares globally since 1990, while ensuring forests contribute to livelihoods for 1.6 billion people dependent on them. The Forestry Programme delivers policy advice, capacity-building workshops, and tools for national forest inventories, fostering practices like selective logging and reforestation that balance timber production with carbon sequestration and habitat preservation. Under the UN Strategic Plan for Forests, FAO coordinates international efforts to reverse forest loss through afforestation targets, integrating forestry into broader agrifood transformations to support biodiversity and rural economies, though implementation varies by governance quality in member states.
Fisheries and aquaculture form another priority, with FAO advocating an ecosystem-based approach to manage stocks sustainably amid overexploitation affecting 35% of assessed fisheries. The biennial State of World Fisheries and Aquaculture reports track production, noting aquaculture surpassing capture fisheries at 94.4 million tonnes in 2022, driven by farmed species like carp and salmon, while calling for reforms to curb illegal, unreported, and unregulated fishing that undermines stocks. Initiatives such as the Blue Growth strategy promote responsible aquaculture expansion, including biosecurity measures and feed efficiency to reduce environmental impacts like mangrove loss, alongside technical aid for small-scale fishers in coastal communities to improve post-harvest handling and market access.
Nutrition integrates across domains, with FAO strategies aiming to transform agrifood systems for nutrient-dense outputs, addressing the 783 million undernourished people reported in 2023 via nutrition-sensitive agriculture. Programs emphasize diversifying crops toward biofortified varieties, such as iron-rich beans reaching 20 million farmers by 2022, and integrating livestock-fisheries for micronutrient access, while the annual State of Food Security and Nutrition reports quantify affordability barriers, revealing healthy diets cost 60% of income in low-income countries. FAO's framework prioritizes ending all malnutrition forms by linking production to consumption patterns, critiquing overreliance on staples like rice and maize that exacerbate hidden hunger, and advocating evidence-based fortification over unsubstantiated dietary shifts.

Normative Frameworks and International Standards

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) engages in normative work by developing voluntary international standards, guidelines, codes of conduct, and binding conventions to address risks in food safety, plant and animal health, pesticide use, and sustainable resource management. These instruments, often negotiated through intergovernmental bodies, aim to harmonize practices across countries, facilitate trade, and mitigate threats like pests and contaminants, though their implementation relies on national adoption and lacks enforcement mechanisms. FAO's efforts emphasize science-based approaches, with outputs referenced in agreements like the World Trade Organization's Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures.
A cornerstone is the Codex Alimentarius Commission, established in 1963 jointly with the World Health Organization, which has produced over 200 food standards, more than 100 codes of practice, and limits for over 3,700 pesticide and veterinary drug residues as of recent assessments. These cover contaminants, additives, and hygiene practices to protect consumer health and ensure fair trade, serving as benchmarks in global disputes. With 189 member countries representing over 99% of the world population, Codex standards influence national regulations but remain voluntary, with adoption varying by jurisdiction.
In plant health, the International Plant Protection Convention (IPPC), adopted in 1951 and administered by FAO, sets phytosanitary standards through International Standards for Phytosanitary Measures (ISPMs), ratified by 185 contracting parties. It focuses on pest risk analysis, surveillance, and safe trade in plants and products to safeguard agriculture, biodiversity, and ecosystems from invasive species, with over 400 ISPMs developed to date. The convention promotes equivalence in measures among members, reducing trade barriers while prioritizing evidence-based quarantine protocols.
FAO also issues codes addressing chemical inputs, such as the International Code of Conduct on Pesticide Management, revised in 2014 with WHO input, which outlines guidelines for registration, distribution, use, and disposal to minimize health and environmental risks, particularly in regions with weak regulatory frameworks. Similarly, the 1995 Code of Conduct for Responsible Fisheries provides voluntary principles for sustainable capture, processing, and aquaculture, influencing policies to prevent overexploitation and illegal activities. These frameworks, while non-binding, support broader goals like reducing antimicrobial resistance in agriculture through integrated pest management recommendations.
Additional guidelines cover areas like responsible agricultural supply chains, developed with the OECD in 2016, emphasizing due diligence on labor, environment, and land rights, and biodiversity utilization for nutrition programs. FAO's normative outputs are critiqued for potential overemphasis on harmonization at the expense of local contexts, yet empirical adoption correlates with improved trade volumes and reduced outbreaks in aligned sectors.

Programs and Technical Contributions

Food Security and Emergency Response Initiatives

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) coordinates global efforts to monitor and address food insecurity through flagship reports such as the State of Food Security and Nutrition in the World (SOFI), which in its 2025 edition analyzed persistent high food price inflation's impact on hunger, estimating 673 million people8.2% of the global populationexperienced hunger in 2024, a slight decline from 8.5% in 2023 but with reversals in Africa and Western Asia. FAO co-leads the annual Global Report on Food Crises (GRFC), with the 2025 report highlighting how conflict, economic shocks, climate extremes, and displacement drove acute food insecurity affecting millions, emphasizing integrated analysis across 59 countries. These tools inform policy by measuring the four pillars of food securityavailability, access, utilization, and stabilityvia methodologies like the Food Insecurity Experience Scale (FIES) and prevalence of undernourishment indicators.
In emergency contexts, FAO deploys rapid interventions to protect agrifood systems, including seed distribution, livestock vaccination, and crop protection, as seen in its support for Sudan's emergency seeds program amid conflict-driven displacement since 2023. The Emergency Management Centre (EMC), established to deliver assessments, remote guidance, and financial aid during crises affecting agriculture, fisheries, and forestry, coordinates with governments for on-ground responses. Funding via the Special Fund for Emergency and Resilience Activities (SFERA), launched as a flexible mechanism, enables quick allocation to humanitarian needs, with FAO requesting USD 102 million in 2025 to sustain agro-pastoral livelihoods ahead of lean seasons in vulnerable regions.
FAO's resilience-building initiatives integrate emergency aid with long-term strategies, such as home garden programs in crisis-hit areas to restore household food production and reduce vulnerability to shocks like those from the COVID-19 pandemic and the 2022 Ukraine conflict, which disrupted global grain supplies. The Data in Emergencies (DIEM) hub, operational since June 2020, provides real-time monitoring of agrifood system indicators in 58 countries, aiding targeted responses by tracking crop yields, livestock conditions, and market disruptions. Under the 2022-2031 Strategic Framework, these efforts align with broader objectives to transform agrifood systems for sustainability, prioritizing anticipation of crises through risk assessments and partnerships with entities like the World Food Programme.

Agricultural Productivity and Resource Management Programs

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) implements programs focused on enhancing agricultural productivity through sustainable intensification, emphasizing practices that increase yields while conserving resources. These initiatives target crop, livestock, and aquaculture systems, integrating technologies such as improved seed varieties, precision farming, and integrated pest management to counteract losses from pests and diseases, which FAO estimates reduce global crop yields by 20 to 40 percent annually. For instance, FAO's Climate-Smart Agriculture (CSA) approach promotes resilient practices like diversified cropping and agroforestry, adopted in over 100 countries since its formalization in 2010, aiming to boost output amid climate variability without expanding land use.
In resource management, FAO's Sustainable Land Management (SLM) programs deploy tools such as farmer field schools and conservation agriculture, which minimize soil erosion and enhance fertility through no-till methods and cover crops, implemented across Africa and Asia via partnerships like TerrAfrica. Complementary efforts in soil health include capacity-building for mapping and analysis in developing nations, as seen in projects launched in July 2025 to integrate soil data into national policies for long-term productivity gains. Water resource programs provide technical support for efficient irrigation systems, including drip technologies that can save up to 50 percent of water in arid regions, alongside global databases like AQUASTAT for monitoring usage trends since 1994.
Livestock productivity initiatives emphasize genetic improvements and health management to elevate output, with FAO research indicating that targeted productivity increases could halve projected antibiotic use in animal systems by 2030 through better feed efficiency and disease prevention. Examples include breeding programs that have raised lambing rates from 0.7 to 0.9 lambs per ewe in select rural projects, enhancing incomes while reducing environmental footprints via manure management and rotational grazing. Agroecology frameworks further support these by promoting diversified systems that build ecosystem services, such as natural pest control, to sustain yields without heavy chemical inputs. These programs collectively aim to close yield gaps, with FAO data showing potential for 50-100 percent increases in low-input regions through evidence-based scaling.

Data Collection, Statistics, and Analytical Tools

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) collects agricultural data primarily through standardized annual questionnaires distributed to national statistical offices in its 194 member countries and territories, focusing on domains such as crop and livestock production, trade, forestry, fisheries, and natural resources. These questionnaires incorporate methodologies derived from international standards, including censuses, sample surveys, and administrative records, with data undergoing validation, imputation for gaps, and harmonization to ensure comparability across countries. The process emphasizes empirical inputs from primary sources, though challenges in data quality persist due to varying national capacities, as evidenced by identified inconsistencies in historical FAOSTAT entries requiring cross-verification.
FAOSTAT serves as FAO's flagship statistical database, offering free access to time-series data spanning over 245 countries and territories since 1961, encompassing more than 300,000 records on food production, consumption, nutrition indicators, and environmental metrics. Updated periodically, with major releases such as agricultural production statistics for 20102023 disseminated on December 20, 2024, FAOSTAT supports tracking of Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) related to hunger (SDG 2) and sustainable resource use. Complementary thematic databases include AQUASTAT, which compiles over 180 variables on water resources and irrigation for agricultural management across 200+ countries, and specialized compilations on fisheries catches and forestry resources. 
FAO's analytical tools integrate these datasets for policy-relevant insights, notably the FAO Food Price Index (FFPI), a monthly composite index tracking international price changes for a basket of cereals, dairy, meats, vegetable oils, and sugar since 1990, with real and nominal variants adjusted for inflation. Produced under the Global Information and Early Warning System (GIEWS), the FFPI aids in monitoring market volatility, as seen in its peak values during the 20222023 crises driven by supply disruptions. Additionally, FAO contributes to the inter-agency Agricultural Market Information System (AMIS), providing crop forecasts, stock estimates, and market monitors to enhance transparency and inform responses to food security risks. The FAO Data Lab employs advanced techniques, including machine learning for price anomaly detection and food loss estimation, to derive causal insights from raw data. These tools prioritize verifiable, granular metrics over aggregated narratives, enabling users to query and visualize data via interactive platforms.

Specialized Treaties and Conventions

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) acts as depositary for several multilateral treaties that establish binding obligations for member states in areas such as plant health, genetic resources, and pesticide management, facilitating international cooperation to mitigate risks to agricultural productivity and food security. These instruments, often developed through FAO-led negotiations, emphasize prevention of transboundary threats and sustainable resource use, with the FAO Director-General serving as the custodian for ratifications, amendments, and certified true copies. As of 2023, the FAO Treaties Database records over 50 such agreements, predominantly focused on phytosanitary measures, fisheries governance, and commodity-specific protocols.
A cornerstone treaty is the International Plant Protection Convention (IPPC), adopted by the FAO Conference on December 6, 1951, via Resolution 85/51, and entering into force on April 3, 1952. The IPPC mandates contracting parties to apply phytosanitary measures to prevent the introduction and spread of pests affecting plants and plant products in international trade, while promoting technical cooperation and harmonized standards through International Standards for Phytosanitary Measures (ISPMs). It currently has 187 contracting parties, enabling coordinated surveillance, quarantine protocols, and capacity-building to protect global crop yields from invasive species, which cause annual losses estimated in billions of dollars. The convention's secretariat operates under FAO oversight, ensuring alignment with World Trade Organization sanitary and phytosanitary rules.
The International Treaty on Plant Genetic Resources for Food and Agriculture (ITPGRFA), negotiated under FAO auspices and adopted on November 3, 2001, in Madrid, entered into force on June 29, 2004, following ratification by 40 countries. Its objectives include the conservation and sustainable use of plant genetic resources for food and agriculture (PGRFA), alongside the fair and equitable sharing of benefits from their utilization, particularly via the Multilateral System covering 64 key crops and forages that underpin 80% of human caloric intake. The treaty has 149 contracting parties as of 2024, with FAO administering the Governing Body and facilitating access to over 5.5 million accessions in genebanks, though benefit-sharing mechanisms have generated modest fundingapproximately $200 million since inceptionfor conservation efforts. Critics note implementation challenges, including limited compliance in developing countries due to intellectual property tensions, but empirical data show it has enhanced ex situ collections critical for breeding resilient varieties against climate variability.
FAO co-implements the Rotterdam Convention on the Prior Informed Consent Procedure for Certain Hazardous Chemicals and Pesticides in International Trade, adopted on September 10, 1998, in Rotterdam, and effective from February 24, 2004. Jointly with the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), FAO supports the convention's aim to promote transparency and informed decision-making in the export of over 50 listed chemicals and pesticides, protecting agricultural workers and ecosystems from unintended exposures that contribute to health issues like pesticide poisoning, which affects millions annually in low-income regions. With 169 parties, the treaty requires exporters to obtain consent from importing states, and FAO provides technical assistance on safe pesticide use, though enforcement gaps persist in informal trade sectors.
In fisheries, FAO deposits instruments like the Agreement on Port State Measures to Prevent, Deter and Eliminate Illegal, Unreported and Unregulated Fishing, adopted on June 22, 2009, and entering into force on May 5, 2016. This treaty empowers port states to inspect and deny access to vessels engaged in IUU fishing, which depletes stocks and undermines sustainable management; it has 102 parties and has led to verifiable reductions in flagged IUU incidents through data-sharing platforms. Complementary regional treaties, such as the 1948 Agreement for the Establishment of the Asia-Pacific Fishery Commission, further FAO's normative role in sector-specific governance.

Empirical Impacts and Evaluations

Measurable Outcomes in Global Food Production and Trade

Global cereal production, a key indicator of food output, expanded from 877 million metric tons in 1961 to 2,836 million metric tons in 2023-24, more than tripling over the period according to FAO-compiled data. This growth exceeded population expansion by a factor of approximately 1.5, with average annual increases driven largely by yield improvements rather than expanded cropland. Cereal yields rose from 1.4 tonnes per hectare in the early 1960s to about 4 tonnes per hectare by the 2020s, reflecting technological shifts including hybrid seeds, synthetic fertilizers, and expanded irrigation during the Green Revolution era. 
FAO's technical contributions, such as extension services and pest management guidelines, have targeted reductions in global crop losses estimated at 20-40% annually from diseases and pests, potentially bolstering yields in vulnerable regions. However, evaluations attribute most yield gains to research from institutions like the International Maize and Wheat Improvement Center (CIMMYT) and national programs, with FAO's role primarily supportive through knowledge transfer rather than direct innovation. Overall agricultural output grew nearly fourfold from 1961 to 2020, with total factor productivity rising due to mechanization and input efficiencies, though growth rates slowed post-2000 amid resource constraints.
Agricultural trade volumes and values have paralleled production surges, with global exports of food and agricultural products increasing in line with economic output since 1961. Nominal export values quadrupled from 2000 to 2020, reaching levels supported by FAO's FAOSTAT trade database tracking commodities from 1961 onward, while the sector's share in total merchandise trade stabilized at 7-8% by 2022. FAO-facilitated standards via the Codex Alimentarius Commission have harmonized quality and safety norms, reducing non-tariff barriers and enabling smoother international flows, though trade growth reflects broader liberalization and supply chain efficiencies more than organizational interventions. Independent reviews note limited direct attribution of trade expansions to FAO, emphasizing data provision and policy advice as key but secondary functions.

Assessments of Hunger Reduction Efforts and SDG Progress

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO), in collaboration with other UN agencies, annually publishes the State of Food Security and Nutrition in the World (SOFI) report to monitor progress toward Sustainable Development Goal 2 (SDG 2), which aims to end hunger, achieve food security, improve nutrition, and promote sustainable agriculture by 2030. These assessments track indicators such as the prevalence of undernourishment (PoU), severe food insecurity, and malnutrition forms like stunting and wasting, using data from household surveys and national statistics. FAO's efforts include normative guidance, technical assistance to member states, and advocacy for investments in agriculture and social protection to reduce hunger.
Global hunger levels, as measured by PoU, have stagnated or reversed in recent years, with approximately 733 million peopleabout 9.1% of the world populationfacing undernourishment in 2023, marking the third consecutive year of high figures after a decline from 795 million in 2019. This represents a setback from pre-2020 trends, where undernourishment had decreased by roughly 167 million people over the prior decade, partly attributable to economic growth in Asia and agricultural productivity gains. FAO attributes the recent plateau to compounding factors including conflicts (e.g., in Ukraine and the Middle East), climate extremes, and economic disruptions from the COVID-19 pandemic, which exacerbated food price volatility and supply chain interruptions. Regionally, Africa bears the heaviest burden, with one in five people undernourished, compared to global averages.
Progress toward SDG 2 targets remains off-track, with projections indicating that 582 million peopleover 6% of the global populationwill still face chronic hunger by 2030 without accelerated interventions, failing the zero-hunger ambition. While child stunting rates have declined to 22.3% globally (149.2 million children under five in 2023), and exclusive breastfeeding rates reached 47.9%, these gains fall short of 2030 benchmarks, such as reducing stunting to 10% or less. FAO evaluations highlight partial successes in areas like genetic resource management and data dissemination but emphasize the need for transformational policy changes, including annual investments of $3950 billion to address undernourishment for 840909 million people. 
Independent analyses, including those from the High-Level Political Forum, confirm SDG 2's stalled trajectory, with 783 million people experiencing chronic hunger in 2022 and 30% lacking adequate food access, underscoring systemic challenges beyond FAO's direct control such as geopolitical instability. Historical FAO-linked targets, like halving hunger under the Millennium Development Goals (achieved modestly from 23.3% PoU in 19901992 to 12.9% projected for 2015 in developing regions), contrast with SDG 2's more ambitious scope, where recent "polycrises" have eroded prior gains. FAO's methodological reliance on modeled estimates for PoU has drawn scrutiny for potential under- or overestimation in data-scarce areas, though it remains the primary global benchmark. Overall, while FAO's monitoring and advocacy have informed national strategies, empirical outcomes reveal insufficient causal impact on hunger eradication amid persistent structural barriers.

Independent Critiques of Effectiveness and Attribution

Independent evaluations have highlighted significant challenges in attributing measurable impacts to FAO programs, primarily due to methodological limitations and confounding factors such as economic growth, technological innovations like hybrid seeds, and market liberalization, which have driven much of the observed declines in undernourishment since the 1960s. Rigorous impact evaluations, including randomized controlled trials, are often absent or underutilized in assessing FAO interventions, making it difficult to isolate causal effects from broader trends; for instance, a review of humanitarian assistance critiques notes that without such methods, claims of effectiveness rely on correlational data prone to overattribution. Economists and policy analysts argue that FAO's normative frameworks and technical assistance contribute marginally at best, as global calorie availability per capita rose 24% from 1961 to 2013 largely through private-sector agricultural productivity gains rather than multilateral coordination.
Critiques from think tanks and academic sources emphasize FAO's tendency to claim undue credit for hunger metrics improvements, such as the drop in Prevalence of Undernourishment from 23% in 1990-1992 to 9% in 2019, while underplaying flaws in its measurement tools that may inflate perceived progress. The FAO's PoU indicator, central to State of Food Security reports, has been faulted for underestimating hunger by excluding moderate malnutrition and relying on outdated household surveys, potentially masking persistent issues and complicating attribution of any reductions to FAO-led initiatives over national policies or trade. Independent assessments, including those by multilateral evaluation bodies, rate FAO's organizational effectiveness as moderate, with persistent gaps in evidence-based impact tracking; for example, a 2024 evaluation of FAO's internal evaluation function identified insufficient functional independence and credibility in verifying program outcomes.
Bilateral donors and journals have long criticized FAO for bureaucratic inertia that dilutes program efficacy, arguing that its expansive mandate across 194 member states leads to diffused efforts with limited scalable impacts, as evidenced by stalled progress on SDG 2 amid rising undernourishment in regions like sub-Saharan Africa since 2015. Attribution problems are exacerbated by FAO's partnerships with entities like governments and NGOs, where co-implementation obscures responsibility; rigorous studies underscore the need for more counterfactual analyses to disentangle FAO's role from parallel drivers like fertilizer subsidies or export growth in Asia. These critiques, often from sources skeptical of UN agencies' value-for-money, suggest that while FAO compiles valuable data, its operational contributions to productivity or resilience-building yield uncertain returns relative to investments exceeding $1.5 billion annually in regular and extrabudgetary funds as of 2022-2023.

Criticisms and Controversies

Bureaucratic Inefficiencies and Operational Failures

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) has been critiqued for its centralized and bureaucratic procedures, which have impeded efficient service delivery to member states. An internal FAO assessment acknowledged that the organization's structure was inadequately prepared for cost-effective operations due to these rigid processes. A 2013 management review further highlighted over-heavy administration, limited emphasis on measurable results, and suboptimal staffing policies that contributed to operational drag.
Recent evaluations reveal ongoing human resources challenges, including vacancy rates surpassing 20 percent as of the latest review period, with 295 positions unfilled for more than two years. Recruitment timelines averaged 178 days from job posting to candidate selection, exacerbating staffing gaps. Performance management systems were deemed ineffective by 62 percent of staff, who reported inadequate recognition of high performers, undermining motivation and accountability.
Operational delivery metrics underscore persistent failures in project execution. Between 2018 and 2021, only 43 to 44 percent of capital expenditure initiatives were completed on time, within budget, and meeting quality criteria. Risk management frameworks remain underdeveloped, with insufficient monitoring and evaluation capacity to systematically track outcomes or mitigate threats. Budget transparency is limited, as the Programme of Work and Budget lacks breakdowns by expenditure categories or detailed staffing allocations, complicating oversight of administrative burdens amid extrabudgetary funding comprising 80 percent of resources in 2022.
Historical cases illustrate how bureaucratic and political frictions have led to tangible failures. In 1984, internal disputes under Director-General Edouard Saouma delayed approval of emergency food aid for the Ethiopian famine by 20 days, reportedly contributing to avoidable deaths. The 1971 Nepal cotton project ballooned costs over a decade without initial feasibility analysis, yielding negligible adoption as farmers shifted to more viable alternatives. Such inefficiencies, compounded by unaccounted expenditures estimated at $100 million in one audit, reflect systemic waste in field operations. Internal audit coverage has declined, with expenditures falling from $7.1 million in 2010-2011 to $5.3 million in 2022-2023 despite revenue growth, limiting fraud detection and process improvements.

Political Influences and Ideological Agendas

The FAO's governance structure, featuring a one-nation-one-vote system in its Conferencethe organization's supreme bodygrants disproportionate influence to developing countries, which constitute the majority of its 194 member states. This dynamic has steered the agency's priorities toward policies favoring smallholder agriculture, international aid redistribution, and critiques of free-market agricultural liberalization, often at the expense of innovation-driven approaches advocated by wealthier nations.
Critics from conservative think tanks contend that this voting imbalance has politicized the FAO, embedding collectivist ideologies promoted by leftist-leaning member states into its programs, such as emphasis on state intervention over private sector-led growth. For instance, the agency's historical resistance to market-oriented reforms during the 1980s and beyond reflected the dominance of radical developing nations in UN proceedings, leading to inefficiencies and a focus on ideological goals like food sovereignty rather than empirical productivity enhancements.
The 2019 election of China's Qu Dongyu as Director-General, supported by a coalition of developing countries over Western-backed candidates, has amplified concerns about authoritarian influence on the FAO's agenda. Under Qu's leadership, the organization has endorsed China's Belt and Road Initiative as a pathway for agricultural development in poorer nations, raising alarms in the US and EU about potential prioritization of geopolitical interests, including state-controlled models of sustainability and technology transfer that align with Beijing's global ambitions. The EU has since criticized the FAO for delaying independent management reviews, attributing this to opaque governance under Chinese stewardship.

Allegations of Industry Capture and Report Manipulation

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) has faced accusations from civil society organizations and former officials of undergoing corporate capture, whereby agribusiness, pesticide manufacturers, and livestock industries exert undue influence over its policies and governance structures. A 2022 report by Pesticide Action Network and allies documented FAO's deepening partnerships with corporate entities, including multi-stakeholder platforms that prioritize private sector input, potentially sidelining small-scale producers and member states in decision-making processes. Critics, including FIAN International, argue this represents a "silent revolution" enhancing corporate sway, as evidenced by FAO's engagement with industry associations like CropLife, which represents major pesticide firms such as Bayer and Syngenta, until the partnership was terminated in June 2024 amid public pressure.
Allegations of report manipulation center on claims of censorship and data distortion to downplay environmental impacts of industrial agriculture. Former FAO officials, including Henning Steinfeld, who led the Livestock Information, Sector Analysis and Policy Branch until 2014, reported being "censored, sabotaged, undermined, and victimised" for over a decade after producing assessments linking livestock methane emissions to climate change, with internal resistance attributed to livestock industry lobbying. In 2023, an October FAO report presented at COP28 was accused by scientists co-authoring underlying studies of distorting their findings to minimize the climate benefits of reducing livestock numbers, concluding instead that such reductions would have only marginal effects on global emissions. Over 100 organizations and experts, in a July 2024 open letter, demanded retraction of the report for "serious errors" in underestimating dietary shifts' role in emissions mitigation, citing FAO's reliance on flawed modeling that ignored peer-reviewed evidence.
Additional concerns involve bias toward biotechnology interests. In 2010, a steering committee member resigned from FAO's International Technical Conference on Agricultural Biotechnology, protesting that preparatory documents were "hopelessly biased in favour of biotechnology" and designed to promote industry adoption over balanced assessment of risks. A senior FAO official, Samuel Jutzi, warned in 2010 that lobbying by "powerful" food corporations was obstructing reforms on health and environmental standards, including suppression of evidence on pesticide hazards. These claims, often raised by advocacy groups with environmentalist leanings, contrast with FAO's defense of multi-stakeholder engagement as essential for innovation, though independent analyses like those from IPES-Food highlight how such dynamics normalize corporate input in UN food governance.

Persistent Global Hunger Despite Decades of Intervention

Despite the Food and Agriculture Organization's establishment in 1945 to address global food shortages through technical assistance, policy coordination, and agricultural development initiatives, undernourishment affected 733 million people worldwide in 2023, equivalent to 9.1% of the global population. This absolute figure, while lower than the approximately 1 billion undernourished individuals in the early 1990s, reflects only modest per capita progress amid a world population that quadrupled from 2.5 billion in 1950 to over 8 billion today, with recent trends showing stagnation or reversal due to conflicts, economic shocks, and climate variability. Global calorie availability exceeds requirements at around 2,900 kcal per capita per day, indicating that aggregate production has kept pace, yet localized scarcities and access barriers persist in regions like sub-Saharan Africa, where undernourishment prevalence rose from 23% in 2000 to 29% by 2023.
FAO-led efforts, including support for the Green Revolution in the 1960s1970s that boosted yields in Asia and Latin America, contributed to prevalence declines from 23% in 19901992 to 8.9% by 2019, but absolute numbers rebounded to 720811 million by 2020 amid the COVID-19 pandemic, supply chain disruptions, and soaring food prices that peaked 30% above pre-2020 levels. In Africa and Western Asia, hunger increased over the same period, with 20% of the population undernourished by 2023, underscoring regional failures in adopting productivity-enhancing technologies despite FAO programs like the Technical Cooperation Programme. Critics attribute persistence to root causes such as inadequate property rights, market distortions from subsidies, and governance failures in high-burden countries, where corrupt or inefficient policies hinder local production and distribution more than global supply shortfalls.

Empirical analyses suggest that interventions emphasizing state planning over private incentives have limited impact, as evidenced by slower hunger reductions in centrally planned economies compared to market-oriented reformers, where agricultural output per capita grew 23 times faster post-1990s liberalization. Membership Dynamics and Reforms

Composition of Member States and Decision-Making

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) consists of 194 member nations, one member organization (the European Union), and two associate members (the Faroe Islands and Tokelau). Membership is open to sovereign states or regional economic integration organizations, subject to approval by a two-thirds majority vote in the FAO Conference. Associate membership applies to non-independent territories or groups, allowing participation in discussions but without voting rights in the Conference. As of 2023, the European Union exercises full membership rights, including voting, equivalent to those of sovereign states.
The FAO's supreme governing body is the Conference, which convenes biennially and includes one delegate per member with a single vote, regardless of population or economic size. Decisions in the Conference are typically made by consensus, but formal votes occur by show of hands, standing, or roll call when required, with a simple majority sufficient except for amendments to the Constitution or budget approval, which need a two-thirds majority of members present and voting. The Conference elects the 49-member Council from nominated candidates, representing seven geographic regions to ensure balanced regional input, and selects the Director-General for a non-renewable four-year term via secret ballot if necessary.
Between Conference sessions, the Council serves as the executive organ, meeting three times annually to review programmes, approve budgets, and prepare Conference agendas. Council decisions follow similar voting procedures, emphasizing consensus to facilitate broad agreement among diverse member interests. This structure aims for equitable representation but can lead to protracted deliberations due to the equal voting weight given to all members, potentially amplifying the influence of smaller or less agriculturally dominant states in global policy directions.

Partnerships, Alliances, and External Collaborations

The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) maintains extensive partnerships with United Nations agencies, civil society organizations, private sector entities, governments, and research institutions to advance its mandate on food security and sustainable agriculture. These collaborations emphasize leveraging complementary expertise, resource mobilization, and multi-stakeholder approaches aligned with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), particularly SDG 2 on zero hunger. As of 2023, FAO reported over 830 collaborative arrangements across technical departments, though evaluations indicate varying degrees of formalization and impact.
Within the UN system, FAO collaborates with more than 40 agencies, funds, and programs, contributing to the UN Development System (UNDS) reform and integrating efforts through UN Country Teams under the UN Sustainable Development Cooperation Framework. It co-leads initiatives with Rome-based agencies, including the World Food Programme (WFP) and International Fund for Agricultural Development (IFAD), to address root causes of hunger via food assistance, agricultural modernization, and nutrition programs. A longstanding alliance exists with the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), established in 1964, focusing on nuclear techniques for crop improvement, pest control, and food irradiation safety. Additionally, FAO participates in the Quadripartite alliance with the World Health Organization (WHO), United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), and World Organisation for Animal Health (WOAH) through the One Health Joint Plan of Action (20222026), targeting zoonotic diseases and antimicrobial resistance via integrated human-animal-plant-ecosystem approaches across six action tracks.
FAO engages hundreds of civil society organizations (CSOs), including NGOs, community-based groups, and networks, for technical cooperation, emergency response, policy advocacy, and capacity building, with a dedicated strategy to enhance dialogue and equity in decision-making. By December 2023, this included a network of 100 active non-state actor partnerships at global, regional, and country levels, facilitated by mechanisms like the CSO Multi-Stakeholder Partnership Mechanism and Advisory Group to amplify marginalized voices and improve project legitimacy. In the private sector, FAO pursues strategic engagements under its 20212025 framework to scale SDG-aligned efforts, such as mobilizing investments for smallholder farmers; examples include a partnership with the International Fertilizer Industry Association (IFIF) formalized in 2005 for sustainable fertilizer use and soil health, and collaborations with agribusiness firms in countries like Ghana since 2018 to link private investments with government policies. These aim to eradicate hunger but require adherence to FAO's guidelines on conflict-of-interest safeguards and transparency in partner selection.

Internal Renewal Efforts and Future Directions

The Food and Agriculture Organization has undertaken internal renewal efforts under Director-General Qu Dongyu, who assumed office on 1 August 2019 and was re-elected on 2 July 2023 for a second term. Qu has prioritized overhauling the Organization's business model through reforms emphasizing efficiency, best practices, and decentralization to address longstanding bureaucratic challenges identified in prior evaluations. These initiatives include streamlining administrative processes and fostering a more agile structure, resulting in a reported transition to a modernized entity over the subsequent six years.
Building on earlier reform agendas, such as the Immediate Plan of Action for FAO Renewal adopted in response to a 2008 external evaluation highlighting institutional inefficiencies, recent efforts have focused on performance improvements in budgeting, staff management, and program delivery. A 2024 Multilateral Organisation Performance Assessment Network (MOPAN) review noted progress in these areas, including enhanced internal collaboration and addressing prior shortcomings in organizational effectiveness. In late 2024, during the 176th session of the FAO Council, Qu proposed additional concrete measures for reform to accelerate adaptation to global crises, emphasizing operational agility amid persistent agrifood system vulnerabilities.
Looking to future directions, the FAO Strategic Framework 2022-2031 guides long-term priorities, aiming to transform global agrifood systems into ones that are more efficient, inclusive, resilient, and sustainableacronymized as "MORE"in alignment with the UN 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development. Developed amid challenges like the COVID-19 pandemic, climate variability, and geopolitical disruptions, the framework incorporates new implementation mechanisms to promote cross-divisional staff collaboration and policy coherence. Complementary strategies include the FAO Strategy on Climate Change 2022-2031, which supports member states in building climate-resilient agrifood practices through adaptation and low-emission mitigation efforts, and strategic priorities for food safety up to 2031, focusing on scientific guidance, capacity building, and risk management at national levels. These directions underscore a commitment to evidence-based interventions, though their success depends on member state implementation and resource mobilization amid competing global demands.
