 Interleukins (ILs) are a group of cytokines, which are small signaling proteins, that mediate communication between leukocytes and other cells to regulate immune responses. Initially identified as factors produced exclusively by white blood cells, interleukins are now known to be secreted by a wide variety of cells throughout the body, including macrophages, endothelial cells, and fibroblasts. There are over 40 distinct interleukins, each designated by a numerical suffix (e.g., IL-1, IL-2), and they exhibit pleiotropic effects, meaning they can influence multiple cell types and functions simultaneously.
The term "interleukin," meaning "between leukocytes," was coined in the late 1970s to standardize the nomenclature for these multifunctional cytokines, which were previously described under various names like lymphokines or monokines based on their cellular origins. This naming convention arose from early discoveries, such as IL-1 identified in 1977 as a lymphocyte-activating factor from macrophages, and IL-2 in 1976 as a T-cell growth factor.30715-1/fulltext) Over time, advances in molecular biology, including gene cloning in the 1980s, revealed that interleukins form families with structural similarities, such as the IL-1 family with 11 members sharing a beta-barrel structure. By the early 21st century, the interleukin family had expanded to include 33 members, reflecting ongoing research into their diverse roles in immunity.
Interleukins primarily function to activate, differentiate, proliferate, and migrate immune cells during inflammatory and immune responses, often acting in paracrine, autocrine, or endocrine manners. They can promote pro-inflammatory effects, such as IL-1 inducing fever and acute-phase responses or IL-6 driving B-cell differentiation, while others like IL-10 exert anti-inflammatory actions by suppressing cytokine production. For instance, IL-2 supports T-cell expansion essential for adaptive immunity, and IL-4 promotes Th2 responses involved in humoral immunity and allergy. These cytokines bind to specific high-affinity receptors on target cells, triggering signaling cascades like JAK-STAT pathways that amplify or resolve immune reactions.
In clinical contexts, interleukins are implicated in numerous diseases due to their central role in inflammation and immunity, with dysregulation contributing to conditions like autoimmune disorders, infections, and cancers. For example, excessive IL-1 activity is linked to rheumatoid arthritis and gout, while IL-2 deficiencies can lead to severe combined immunodeficiency. Therapeutically, recombinant interleukins such as IL-2 have been FDA-approved for treating melanoma and renal cell carcinoma by enhancing anti-tumor immunity. Ongoing research targets interleukin pathways for novel treatments, including inhibitors for chronic inflammatory diseases.

Introduction

Definition and Characteristics

Interleukins (ILs) are a subset of cytokines, which are small signaling proteins that mediate communication between cells, primarily modulating immune responses by promoting inflammation, cell proliferation, differentiation, and survival. Originally identified as factors secreted by leukocytes to regulate interactions between white blood cellshence the name "inter-leukins"they are now recognized as being produced by a diverse array of cell types beyond the immune system, including epithelial cells, endothelial cells, and fibroblasts. As of 2025, there are 38 recognized interleukins, designated IL-1 through IL-38, based on their sequential discovery and functional characterization.30715-1/fulltext)
Structurally, interleukins are predominantly small polypeptides with molecular weights ranging from 15 to 30 kDa, often adopting a compact four -helical bundle fold that serves as a common scaffold for ligand-receptor interactions, though some exhibit -barrel or other configurations. Many interleukins feature post-translational modifications such as N- and O-linked glycosylation, which enhance stability, solubility, and bioavailability by shielding hydrophobic regions and influencing folding. Disulfide bonds, typically involving conserved cysteine residues, are prevalent across several interleukin families, stabilizing the tertiary structure and facilitating proper secretion and receptor binding.
Interleukins are synthesized and secreted by various immune cells, including T cells, B cells, macrophages, and dendritic cells, as well as non-immune cells such as fibroblasts and endothelial cells, in response to stimuli like pathogens or tissue damage. Secretion typically occurs via the classical endoplasmic reticulum-Golgi pathway for glycosylated interleukins, involving signal peptides for translocation; however, some, particularly leaderless ones, employ unconventional mechanisms such as direct translocation across the plasma membrane or exosome release to bypass the secretory apparatus.30715-1/fulltext)
In terms of receptor interactions, interleukins generally bind to multi-subunit transmembrane receptors composed of ligand-specific chains and shared or chains, inducing receptor dimerization or oligomerization that activates intracellular signaling cascades, most commonly the Janus kinase-signal transducer and activator of transcription (JAK-STAT) pathway. General Biological Roles

Interleukins exhibit pleiotropy, meaning a single interleukin can exert multiple biological effects on diverse cell types, and redundancy, where overlapping functions among different interleukins ensure robust immune modulation even if one is absent. This arises from shared receptor subunits, such as the common gamma chain (c) utilized by IL-2, IL-4, IL-7, IL-9, IL-15, and IL-21, or gp130 shared by IL-6, IL-11, leukemia inhibitory factor (LIF), oncostatin M (OSM), ciliary neurotrophic factor (CNTF), cardiotrophin-1 (CT-1), and neuropoietin (NP-1). These properties allow interleukins to influence a broad spectrum of immune cells, including T cells, B cells, natural killer (NK) cells, and myeloid cells, promoting coordinated responses while providing evolutionary resilience against disruptions.
Interleukins play pivotal roles in hematopoiesis by supporting the development and maintenance of blood cells, particularly in the bone marrow. They drive lymphocyte activation, differentiation, proliferation, and migration, enabling adaptive immune responses; for instance, they facilitate T and B cell maturation, expansion, and trafficking to sites of infection. Additionally, interleukins regulate both acute and chronic inflammation by modulating pro-inflammatory cascades and counter-regulatory mechanisms, balancing pathogen clearance with tissue protection.
Upon binding to their receptors, interleukins activate intracellular signaling pathways that dictate pro- or anti-inflammatory outcomes, including the Janus kinase-signal transducer and activator of transcription (JAK-STAT) pathway, nuclear factor kappa B (NF-B), mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK), and phosphoinositide 3-kinase-Akt (PI3K-Akt). Interleukins interact synergistically with other cytokines to orchestrate immune responses; for example, IL-12 and IL-18 cooperate with interferons like IFN- to enhance Th1 differentiation and NK cell activity, while IL-1 induces TNF- production to amplify inflammation. They also collaborate with chemokines, such as IL-8 (CXCL8), to direct leukocyte migration and integrate innate and adaptive immunity.
Interleukins are evolutionarily conserved across vertebrates, with homologs like IL-7 and IL-17 present in jawed vertebrates, underscoring their ancient origins in host defense. They contribute to pathogen resistance by activating inflammatory responses and antimicrobial peptide production, while also maintaining tissue homeostasis through regulation of mucosal immunity and immune cell balance.

History and Nomenclature

Early Discoveries

The early investigations into interleukins began in the 1970s with the identification of soluble factors mediating immune cell interactions, initially focusing on activities produced by macrophages and lymphocytes. In 1972, Igal Gery and Byron H. Waksman described lymphocyte-activating factor (LAF), a soluble mediator released by adherent peritoneal exudate cells (primarily macrophages) in mice and humans that enhanced the proliferative response of thymocytes to suboptimal concentrations of mitogens such as phytohemagglutinin (PHA). This factor, later recognized as interleukin-1 (IL-1), was characterized through co-culture experiments demonstrating its role in potentiating T-cell responses without direct mitogenic activity on its own. Subsequent studies in the late 1970s linked LAF to leukocytic pyrogen, a fever-inducing protein from monocytes, further establishing IL-1's multifaceted role in inflammation and lymphocyte activation.
The purification and molecular characterization of IL-1 marked a pivotal advancement in the 1980s. In 1984, Charles A. Dinarello and colleagues purified human IL-1 from monocyte cultures to homogeneity, revealing two distinct forms (IL-1 and IL-1) with molecular weights around 17 kDa and isoelectric points near 5, using techniques like gel filtration and ion-exchange chromatography.86155-8/pdf) That same year, complementary DNA (cDNA) cloning efforts succeeded: Paul E. Auron et al. cloned the IL-1 precursor from human monocytes, while Peter T. Lomedico et al. cloned IL-1, encoding 271- and 269-amino-acid precursors, respectively, enabling recombinant production and confirmation of their identity with LAF activity. Early bioassays for IL-1 relied on thymocyte proliferation, where murine or human thymocytes were co-stimulated with suboptimal PHA and serial dilutions of test samples, measuring tritiated thymidine incorporation to quantify activity in units where 50% maximal response defined one unit.
Parallel discoveries highlighted the diversity of these factors. In 1976, Francis W. Ruscetti, Doris A. Morgan, and Robert C. Gallo identified T-cell growth factor (TCGF) in supernatants from PHA-stimulated human peripheral blood lymphocytes, enabling long-term culture and proliferation of antigen-specific T cells, which was crucial for dissecting T-cell activation pathways. This factor, later designated interleukin-2 (IL-2), was cloned in 1983 by Tadatsugu Taniguchi et al., revealing a 155-amino-acid precursor that supported T-cell expansion. Bioactivity of IL-2 was assessed using the CTLL-2 cell line, an IL-2-dependent cytotoxic T-lymphocyte clone, where proliferation was quantified by DNA synthesis assays, establishing units based on half-maximal growth.
The 1980s saw rapid expansion in identifying additional interleukins through functional assays and emerging recombinant DNA technologies. Interleukin-3 (IL-3), described in 1981 by James N. Ihle et al. as multi-colony-stimulating factor (multi-CSF), supported proliferation of multiple hematopoietic lineages including mast cells and granulocytes-macrophage progenitors in murine bone marrow cultures. In 1982, Maureen Howard and William E. Paul identified B-cell stimulatory factor-1 (BSF-1), now IL-4, which induced IgG1 and IgE class switching in murine B cells. Interleukin-5 (IL-5) was characterized in 1986 by Colin J. Sanderson et al. as a factor promoting eosinophil differentiation from bone marrow precursors. That year, Tadamitsu Kishimoto's group cloned BSF-2 (IL-6), which drove B-cell differentiation into plasma cells and acute-phase protein synthesis in hepatocytes. Interleukin-7 (IL-7), identified in 1988 by Arthur E. Namen et al. from murine bone marrow stroma, supported pre-B-cell growth. IL-8, discovered in 1987 by Kouji Matsushima et al. as a neutrophil chemoattractant from stimulated monocytes, was pivotal for understanding acute inflammation.
These milestones were facilitated by recombinant DNA techniques in the 1980s, which allowed expression of cloned genes in Escherichia coli and verification of biological activities, shifting from crude supernatants to pure proteins. By the 1990s, research revealed that interleukins were not exclusive to leukocytes but produced by diverse cells like endothelial and epithelial tissues, broadening their perceived roles beyond initial immune-centric views.

Naming and Numbering System

The term "interleukin" was coined in 1979 during the Second International Lymphokine Workshop in Ermatingen, Switzerland, to describe soluble factors mediating communication between leukocytes, initially focusing on products from white blood cells that regulate immune responses. This nomenclature replaced earlier terms like "lymphokines" and emphasized the regulatory role of these proteins in intercellular signaling. Over time, the definition expanded beyond leukocyte-derived factors to include cytokines produced by various cell types, such as endothelial cells and fibroblasts, reflecting their broader physiological involvement.
Interleukins are assigned numbers sequentially based on their discovery, molecular cloning, and validation of immune-related functions by expert committees, including the WHO-IUIS Nomenclature Subcommittee on Interleukin Designation. For instance, IL-1 was designated in the early 1980s following its cloning and characterization as a key inflammatory mediator, while subsequent numbers followed similar processes of sequence determination and functional confirmation.30715-1/fulltext) This system ensures systematic naming but has led to irregularities; numbers like IL-14 were skipped initially because the proposed B-cell growth factor lacked a cloned gene and verifiable sequence, leaving a gap that was never filled. Similarly, IL-8 retains its interleukin designation despite reclassification as the chemokine CXCL8 due to its chemoattractant properties, illustrating how historical naming persists alongside updated chemokine nomenclature.
As of 2025, 38 interleukins (IL-1 through IL-38) are officially recognized, with IL-38 added in 2013 and confirmed in subsequent reviews for its role in modulating inflammation.30715-1/fulltext) Inclusion requires demonstration of sequence homology to known interleukins, shared receptor usage, and primary functions in immune regulation, as overseen by bodies like the International Union of Immunological Societies (IUIS). However, controversies persist regarding certain members, such as IL-34, which exhibits significant non-immune roles in tissues like the brain (e.g., microglia homeostasis) and bone (e.g., osteoclast differentiation), prompting debates on whether it fully aligns with the traditional leukocyte-focused criteria for interleukins.

Classification into Families

IL-1 Superfamily

The IL-1 superfamily comprises a group of cytokines characterized by their shared -trefoil structure, consisting of a 12-stranded -barrel fold that forms a compact, globular domain with a hydrophobic core, distinguishing them from other interleukins that typically adopt -helical bundles. This architecture enables direct binding to cell-surface receptors without requiring a classical signal peptide for secretion; instead, pro-forms of members like IL-1 and IL-1 are processed intracellularly, often by caspase-1 within the inflammasome complex, to generate mature, bioactive proteins. The superfamily includes 11 members: the pro-inflammatory agonists IL-1, IL-1, IL-18, IL-33, and IL-36//; the anti-inflammatory IL-37; the regulatory IL-38; and antagonists such as IL-1 receptor antagonist (IL-1Ra) and IL-36Ra. These cytokines primarily drive innate immune responses, amplifying inflammation in response to infection, injury, or sterile damage.
Key members exhibit specialized pro- or anti-inflammatory functions through distinct yet overlapping mechanisms. IL-1 and IL-1, the founding members, promote fever, acute-phase responses, and endothelial activation by binding IL-1 receptor type 1 (IL-1R1), leading to leukocyte recruitment and cytokine production. IL-18 induces interferon- (IFN-) secretion from natural killer (NK) cells and T cells, enhancing Th1 polarization and antiviral immunity, particularly in synergy with IL-12. IL-33 acts as an alarmin released from damaged epithelial and endothelial cells, stimulating type 2 immune responses via ST2 receptor, including eosinophil activation and goblet cell hyperplasia in allergic contexts. The IL-36 subfamily (IL-36, IL-36, IL-36) drives skin and mucosal inflammation by activating keratinocytes and dendritic cells through IL-36R, contributing to conditions like psoriasis. In contrast, IL-37 suppresses inflammation by reducing pro-inflammatory cytokine expression and promoting regulatory T cell activity, while IL-38 antagonizes IL-36 signaling and exhibits broad anti-inflammatory effects, including inhibition of Th17 responses; recent studies highlight IL-38's protective role in autoimmunity, such as limiting joint inflammation in rheumatoid arthritis models.
Receptors in the IL-1 receptor (IL-1R) family share a conserved Toll/IL-1 receptor (TIR) domain for signaling, with most ligands requiring a primary receptor and accessory protein (IL-1RAcP) to form a ternary complex that recruits the adaptor MyD88. This initiates downstream cascades via IRAK kinases and TRAF6, culminating in NF-B and MAPK activation to transcribe inflammatory genes. The signaling equation can be represented as:

The IL-1 superfamily's discovery unfolded over decades, beginning with IL-1 in the 1970s as a lymphocyte-activating factor, with IL-1 and IL-1 distinguished in 1984; IL-18 was identified in 1989 as an IFN--inducing factor. Later additions include the IL-36 family in 2000, IL-37 also in 2000, IL-33 in 2005, and IL-38 in 2001, expanding the family through genomic cloning based on sequence homology to IL-1. Regulation occurs via natural antagonists like IL-1Ra, which competitively binds IL-1R1 without signaling, and IL-36Ra, preventing excessive inflammation; these are induced by the same stimuli as agonists, maintaining homeostasis. Evolutionarily, the superfamily traces to ancient innate immunity modules predating jawed vertebrates, with proto-IL-1 and IL-1R genes emerging around 500 million years ago in early deuterostomes, underscoring their role as foundational sensors of damage and infection across metazoans.

IL-2 (Common Gamma Chain) Family

The IL-2 family, also known as the common gamma chain (c) family, comprises cytokines that adopt a characteristic four--helix bundle structure, enabling their binding to heterodimeric receptors sharing the common c subunit (CD132). This structural motif facilitates ligand-induced dimerization and activation of downstream signaling cascades critical for adaptive immunity.30145-1.pdf)
The family includes six key members: interleukin-2 (IL-2), IL-4, IL-7, IL-9, IL-15, and IL-21, each with distinct yet overlapping roles in regulating lymphoid cell development, proliferation, and differentiation. IL-2, the prototype, promotes T-cell proliferation and survival while supporting regulatory T cell (Treg) maintenance and function to balance immune responses. IL-4 drives Th2 cell differentiation, B-cell class switching to IgE, and alternative macrophage activation, contributing to humoral immunity and allergic responses. IL-7 is essential for early lymphopoiesis, sustaining nave and memory T-cell homeostasis as well as B-cell development in the bone marrow. IL-9 supports Th9 cell differentiation and enhances mast cell and eosinophil function, influencing mucosal immunity and allergic inflammation. IL-15 drives natural killer (NK) cell and memory CD8+ T-cell expansion and survival, promoting cytotoxic responses without direct proliferation in most cases.30145-1.pdf) IL-21 modulates B-cell differentiation, plasma cell formation, and follicular helper T (Tfh) cell activity, while also enhancing cytotoxic lymphocyte function.
Receptors for these cytokines are typically heterodimeric, consisting of a cytokine-specific or chain paired with the shared c subunit, though IL-2 and IL-15 can form high-affinity complexes involving additional components like IL-2R. Upon ligand binding, receptor oligomerization recruits Janus kinases (JAK1 associated with c and JAK3 with private chains), leading to phosphorylation and activation of signal transducer and activator of transcription 5 (STAT5), which translocates to the nucleus to induce gene transcription. The core signaling pathway can be represented as:

\text{IL-2} + \text{IL-2R}\alpha/\beta/\gamma_c \to \text{JAK1/JAK3 activation} \to \text{STAT5 phosphorylation} \to \text{Target gene transcription (e.g., } c\text{-}myc, } bcl\text{-}2\text{)}

Similar mechanisms apply to other family members, with variations in STAT usage (e.g., STAT6 for IL-4) and additional pathways like PI3K-Akt for survival signals.
Discovery of these cytokines unfolded over decades, beginning with IL-2 identified in 1976 as a T-cell growth factor from activated lymphocyte supernatants. IL-4 was characterized in 1982 for its B-cell stimulatory and T-cell growth-promoting activities. IL-7 and IL-9 emerged in 1988, with IL-7 as a pre-B-cell growth factor and IL-9 as a T-cell and mast cell product. IL-15 was cloned in 1994 for its IL-2-like effects on NK cells via shared receptor components. IL-21, the most recent, was discovered in 2000 through expression cloning from activated CD4+ T cells. As of 2025, IL-21 has gained renewed attention in cancer immunotherapy, with engineered mimics demonstrating enhanced antitumor efficacy by prolonging NK and T-cell signaling in preclinical models.
Regulation of family signaling involves soluble receptor forms, such as soluble IL-2R (sIL-2R), which sequesters IL-2 to limit excessive activation, and feedback inhibition via SOCS proteins that suppress JAK-STAT pathways. These mechanisms prevent overstimulation, maintaining immune homeostasis while allowing context-dependent responses in adaptive immunity.

IL-6 Family

The IL-6 family of cytokines is characterized by their shared use of the glycoprotein 130 (gp130) as a common signal-transducing receptor subunit, which enables pleiotropic functions in inflammation, hematopoiesis, and immune regulation. These cytokines adopt a long-chain four-alpha-helix bundle structure, a motif typical of type I cytokines, where the helices are connected by loops and stabilized by disulfide bonds. Unlike many other interleukins, IL-6 family members often require association with specific soluble receptors to effectively present the ligand to the membrane-bound gp130, facilitating signal initiation on target cells.
Key members of this family include IL-6, IL-11, and IL-31, each contributing distinct yet overlapping roles in physiological processes. IL-6 primarily drives the acute-phase response in the liver, promotes Th17 cell differentiation, and supports B-cell maturation and antibody production. IL-11 is essential for megakaryopoiesis and platelet production, while also influencing bone remodeling and mucosal protection. IL-31 is implicated in pruritus and chronic skin inflammation, particularly in atopic dermatitis.
Receptor complexes for IL-6 family cytokines involve gp130 either as a homodimer (for IL-6 and IL-11) or heterodimer (with specific partners like OSMR for IL-31), leading to activation of downstream pathways such as JAK-STAT3 and MAPK. Signaling is initiated when the cytokine binds its cognate receptor, recruiting gp130 and triggering tyrosine phosphorylation. For IL-6, this process exemplifies the pathway, where IL-6 complexes with soluble IL-6 receptor (sIL-6R) to engage gp130, resulting in STAT3 activation and subsequent expression of acute-phase proteins like C-reactive protein (CRP) and hepcidin:

Recent studies as of 2025 have further elucidated IL-27's dual pro- and anti-inflammatory roles, such as promoting IFN- in acute infections while suppressing excessive Th17 responses in chronic metabolic disorders like obesity.
Regulation of IL-6 family signaling occurs through two distinct modes: classic signaling, mediated by membrane-bound specific receptors on limited cell types like hepatocytes and immune cells, which often yields regenerative or anti-inflammatory effects; and trans-signaling, involving soluble receptors that extend signaling to a broader range of cells expressing only gp130, predominantly driving pro-inflammatory responses. This dichotomy allows precise control over systemic inflammation and acute-phase reactions.

IL-10 Family

The IL-10 family comprises a group of class II cytokines characterized by their primarily immunosuppressive functions, which play critical roles in maintaining immune homeostasis, particularly at mucosal and epithelial barriers. These cytokines include IL-10, IL-19, IL-20, IL-22, IL-24, and IL-26, all of which share structural similarities and utilize receptor complexes involving the common -chain IL-10R2 paired with specific -chains. This family emphasizes protection against excessive inflammation by modulating epithelial integrity and antimicrobial defenses, thereby preventing tissue damage during infections or immune challenges.
Structurally, members of the IL-10 family adopt a short-chain four--helix bundle configuration typical of class II cytokines, with six or seven -helices (AF) forming a compact left-handed bundle primarily from helices A, C, D, and F. While most family members exist as monomers in solution, IL-10 and its viral homologs form non-covalent dimers essential for receptor binding and bioactivity. Key functions vary among members: IL-10 primarily deactivates macrophages and promotes immune tolerance by suppressing pro-inflammatory cytokine production; IL-19, IL-20, and IL-24 support epithelial barrier function and are implicated in skin disorders like psoriasis through keratinocyte proliferation and STAT3 signaling; IL-22 drives mucosal defense by inducing antimicrobial peptides and tight junction proteins in epithelial cells without directly affecting immune cells; and IL-26 exhibits antimicrobial properties by forming complexes with bacterial DNA to activate innate immunity.
Receptor engagement and signaling are conserved across the family, with ligand binding to heterodimeric receptors initiating JAK-STAT pathways. For IL-10, the dimeric ligand binds IL-10R1 and IL-10R2, recruiting JAK1 and TYK2 kinases to phosphorylate STAT3, which translocates to the nucleus to upregulate anti-inflammatory genes including SOCS3 for negative feedback regulation:

IL-10 was discovered in 1989 as cytokine synthesis inhibitory factor (CSIF) from Th2 cells, while IL-19 was identified in 2000 through homology searches, and IL-20, IL-22, IL-24, and IL-26 were cloned in the early 2000s via genomic approaches revealing their clustered genes on chromosome 1. Recent studies as of 2025 highlight IL-22's role in modulating gut microbiome composition through aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) interactions, enhancing microbial diversity and host defense.
Regulation of IL-10 family cytokines is highly context-dependent, often exhibiting anti-inflammatory effects in steady-state conditions but pro-inflammatory roles in chronic settings or specific tissues, such as IL-22 promoting Th17-driven pathology in autoimmunity. This duality arises from differential expression by immune cells (e.g., Tregs for IL-10, Th17/ILC3s for IL-22) and interactions with the microenvironment, ensuring balanced responses to pathogens while averting immunopathology.

IL-12 Family

The IL-12 family comprises a group of heterodimeric cytokines that are pivotal in shaping adaptive immune responses, primarily by promoting the differentiation of T helper (Th) cells into Th1 and Th17 subsets, thereby influencing cell-mediated immunity against infections and contributing to inflammatory pathologies. These cytokines share structural and functional similarities, including the use of common subunits and receptor components, which allow for coordinated regulation of immune polarization.30128-1) Unlike other interleukin families, the IL-12 members are secreted as disulfide-linked heterodimers, enabling precise control of their bioactivity in response to microbial stimuli.
Structurally, IL-12 family cytokines are composed of an -chain (such as p19, p28, or p35) and a -chain (p40 or EBI3), forming heterodimers that adopt a four--helix bundle conformation characteristic of the IL-6 cytokine superfamily. The -chains exhibit an up-up-down-down topology in their helix bundles, while the -chains, derived from the IL-6 family, feature a distinct fibronectin type-III domain arrangement that stabilizes the complex. This architecture facilitates receptor binding and signaling specificity, distinguishing the family from monomeric interleukins.
The family includes four key members with distinct functions in T cell differentiation. IL-12, formed by the p35 and p40 subunits, drives Th1 polarization by inducing interferon- (IFN-) production from natural killer and T cells, enhancing cytotoxic responses. IL-23, comprising p19 and p40, sustains Th17 cells and promotes IL-17 secretion, critical for defense against extracellular pathogens but implicated in autoimmunity.00031-6) IL-27, a p28/EBI3 heterodimer, supports early Th1 commitment and IFN- expression while also exerting anti-inflammatory effects in later stages.00324-4) In contrast, IL-35 (p35/EBI3) acts suppressively, expanding regulatory T cells to dampen excessive inflammation and maintain immune tolerance.
Receptor engagement and signaling pathways are conserved across the family, involving shared -chains like IL-12R1 and IL-12R2, which recruit Janus kinases TYK2 and JAK2 upon ligand binding. This approach exploits subunit commonality for broad yet selective immunomodulation.

IL-17 Family

The IL-17 family comprises six structurally related cytokines, IL-17A through IL-17F, that play pivotal pro-inflammatory roles at epithelial barriers such as the skin, lung, and gut, primarily by promoting neutrophil recruitment and amplifying immune responses against extracellular pathogens. These cytokines are characterized by a conserved cystine-knot fold, a structural motif formed by three disulfide bonds that stabilizes a homodimeric or heterodimeric assembly, enabling receptor binding; for instance, IL-17A can form homodimers or heterodimers with IL-17F to enhance signaling potency. This unique architecture distinguishes the IL-17 family from other interleukins and supports their localization at mucosal surfaces where they orchestrate host defense and tissue repair.
Among the members, IL-17A is the most potent inducer of inflammation, driving neutrophil infiltration, chemokine production (e.g., CXCL8), and antimicrobial peptide expression, with elevated levels implicated in autoimmune conditions like psoriasis. In contrast, IL-17B, IL-17C, IL-17D, and IL-17E exhibit milder pro-inflammatory effects and tissue-specific functions, such as IL-17B promoting lung epithelial repair or IL-17C enhancing gut barrier integrity against bacterial invasion. IL-17F shares the closest homology with IL-17A (50% sequence identity) and contributes to neutrophil-mediated responses in the airways, though with reduced potency; recent investigations as of 2025 have highlighted its role in sustaining chronic neutrophilic inflammation in asthma models.
The family signals through heterodimeric receptors, predominantly IL-17RA paired with IL-17RC for IL-17A and IL-17F, which lack intrinsic kinase activity but recruit the adaptor protein Act1 (CIKS) via a SEFIR domain interaction. This initiates a cascade involving TRAF6 ubiquitination, leading to activation of NF-B and AP-1 transcription factors, which in turn induce expression of pro-inflammatory genes such as those encoding cytokines and matrix metalloproteinases.

This pathway underscores the family's contribution to autoimmunity, where dysregulated signaling exacerbates barrier dysfunction.
IL-17A was first cloned in 1993 from a rodent T-cell hybridoma, initially termed CTLA-8, marking the discovery of this cytokine class. Subsequent homology screens identified IL-17B in 1998, IL-17C and IL-17E in 2000, IL-17D in 2002, and IL-17F in 2001, expanding the family and revealing their shared evolutionary origins.
Expression of IL-17 family members is predominantly derived from Th17 cells, with production amplified in inflammatory contexts to sustain barrier immunity.

Other Interleukins

Interleukins that do not align with the primary structural families, such as the IL-1 superfamily or IL-6 family, exhibit diverse functions in immune regulation, often grouped by shared receptor components or biological roles despite lacking common structural motifs. These "other" interleukins include key players in hematopoiesis, chemotaxis, Th2 responses, and innate immunity, with discovery spanning from the early 1980s to the 2000s; their classification remains challenging due to ongoing reassignments, such as IL-8's integration into the chemokine family.30715-1/fulltext)
The IL-3 and IL-5 subgroup shares a common -chain receptor (c) subunit, enabling cooperative signaling in hematopoietic processes, though they differ in cellular targets. IL-3, identified in 1981 as a multilineage colony-stimulating factor (multi-CSF), promotes the proliferation and differentiation of early hematopoietic progenitors, including mast cells, basophils, and eosinophils, by binding the IL-3 receptor -chain paired with c. IL-5, discovered in 1986, specifically drives eosinophil maturation, activation, and survival, contributing to allergic and parasitic responses through its dedicated -chain receptor linked to the same c subunit. This shared receptor architecture underscores their synergistic roles in type 2 immunity, despite structural divergence from other families.30715-1/fulltext)
IL-8, originally designated as an interleukin in 1987, functions as a potent neutrophil chemoattractant and activator, now reclassified as the chemokine CXCL8 due to its ELR motif and G-protein-coupled receptor signaling via CXCR1 and CXCR2. This reassignment highlights classification gaps, as IL-8's small size and CXC structure align more closely with chemokines than traditional interleukins, yet it retains historical IL nomenclature.30715-1/fulltext)
IL-13, cloned in 1990, shares functional overlap with IL-4 in promoting Th2 responses but signals through a distinct heterodimeric receptor (IL-13R1/IL-4R), inducing IgE class switching, mucus hypersecretion, and alternative macrophage activation in allergic diseases. Unlike IL-4, IL-13's unique receptor complex allows independent regulation of tissue remodeling and anti-helminth immunity.30715-1/fulltext)
Among less characterized members, IL-14, described in 1990, acts as a B-cell growth factor, enhancing proliferation and immunoglobulin secretion without clear family ties, though its receptor remains elusive. IL-16, identified in the early 1990s, serves as a CD4+ T-cell and monocyte chemoattractant, binding CD4 independently of MHC and modulating inflammatory migration via a unique tetrameric structure.30715-1/fulltext)
Later-discovered interleukins further diversify this category. IL-25, reported in 2000, promotes innate Th2-like responses by activating non-T/non-B cells to produce IL-4, IL-5, and IL-13, exacerbating allergic inflammation through IL-17RB signaling. IL-34, identified in 2008, supports monocyte and macrophage differentiation as a ligand for CSF-1R, paralleling M-CSF but with distinct tissue-specific expression in the brain and skin. IL-28 and IL-29, classified as type III interferons (IFN-) since their 2003 discovery, provide antiviral protection via IFNLR1/IL-10RB receptors, inducing ISGs in epithelial cells with fewer systemic effects than type I IFNs. IL-30, the -subunit of IL-27 isolated in the mid-2000s, exhibits independent proinflammatory activity by binding gp130 and modulating T-cell responses, separate from the IL-27 heterodimer.
IL-32, first characterized in 2005 as a TNF inducer, promotes inflammation across epithelial, endothelial, and immune cells through multiple isoforms and NLRP3 inflammasome activation, without a defined receptor family.00380-2) Recent studies as of 2025 highlight IL-32's role in enhancing caspase-1 expression during early SARS-CoV-2 infection, driving pyroptosis and viral clearance, while also contributing to tuberculosis resistance via NF-B-TNF pathways in macrophages.
These interleukins illustrate the evolving nomenclature and functional diversity of the IL system, with ongoing research addressing their orphan receptors and potential reclassifications to better reflect structural and signaling heterogeneities.

Roles in Human Physiology and Pathology

Regulation of Immune Responses

Interleukins play a pivotal role in regulating innate immune responses by activating key effector cells and coordinating inflammatory cascades. IL-1, a prototypical pro-inflammatory cytokine, drives the activation of macrophages and natural killer (NK) cells, promoting the release of secondary mediators that amplify innate defenses. Similarly, IL-6 enhances macrophage polarization toward pro-inflammatory phenotypes and supports the acute phase response, thereby bolstering humoral components of innate immunity. IL-8 functions primarily as a chemokine, recruiting neutrophils to sites of infection or injury, where they contribute to pathogen clearance through phagocytosis and degranulation. These actions collectively ensure rapid containment of threats while preventing unchecked inflammation.
In adaptive immunity, interleukins guide the differentiation and expansion of T and B lymphocytes, shaping effector responses tailored to specific antigens. IL-2 is essential for T cell proliferation and promotes the differentiation of regulatory T cells (Tregs) via STAT5 signaling, maintaining immune homeostasis. IL-4 drives Th2 cell differentiation, fostering B cell class switching to IgE and IgG1, which supports humoral immunity against extracellular pathogens. IL-12 induces Th1 polarization through STAT4 activation, enhancing IFN- production and cytotoxic T cell responses. IL-17, produced by Th17 cells, recruits neutrophils and promotes barrier integrity, though its differentiation is supported by IL-6 and TGF- in humans.
Interleukins exhibit intricate cross-regulation to balance pro- and anti-inflammatory signals, preventing immunopathology. Pro-inflammatory cytokines like IL-1 can indirectly induce IL-10 production in macrophages, establishing a feedback loop that dampens excessive responses. IL-10, in turn, upregulates suppressors of cytokine signaling (SOCS) proteins such as SOCS1 and SOCS3, which inhibit JAK/STAT pathways downstream of pro-inflammatory interleukins like IL-6 and IL-1. This negative feedback mechanism ensures transient inflammation resolves appropriately, with SOCS proteins providing rapid attenuation of signaling to maintain equilibrium.
Tissue-specific regulation by interleukins adapts immune responses to local microenvironments. In mucosal tissues, IL-22 promotes epithelial cell production of antimicrobial peptides and mucus, reinforcing barrier function against luminal threats. IL-25 activates type 2 innate lymphoid cells, driving eosinophil recruitment and tissue repair in the gut and airways. In the skin, IL-17 and IL-36 stimulate keratinocyte expression of defensins and chemokines, enhancing antimicrobial defenses at this barrier site. Within lymphoid organs, IL-7 supports nave T cell homeostasis and survival, while IL-15 sustains memory CD8+ T cells and NK cell persistence, ensuring long-term adaptive surveillance.
In humans, genetic variations in interleukin genes modulate immune regulation, influencing susceptibility to dysregulation. Polymorphisms in the IL-10 promoter, such as -592 A/C, alter cytokine expression levels, with low-producer alleles associated with heightened pro-inflammatory responses and impaired tolerance. Recent studies highlight IL-38's emerging role in promoting immune tolerance by inhibiting mTOR signaling and preventing trained immunity in monocytes, thereby suppressing chronic inflammation. These variations underscore the personalized nature of interleukin-mediated regulation in human physiology.

Involvement in Inflammatory Diseases

Interleukins play a pivotal role in acute inflammatory responses, particularly in conditions like sepsis where dysregulated production leads to a cytokine storm. In sepsis, elevated levels of IL-1, IL-6, and IL-8 are observed, driving excessive immune activation and correlating with disease severity; for instance, IL-6 levels are markedly increased in severe cases, produced by monocytes and macrophages, and serve as a prognostic marker for outcomes such as septic shock. IL-1 facilitates neutrophil and T-cell recruitment, amplifying the inflammatory cascade, while IL-8 promotes chemotaxis of neutrophils to infection sites, contributing to systemic inflammation. These interleukins' overproduction results in endothelial damage and multi-organ failure, highlighting their central involvement in acute pathology.
In chronic inflammatory diseases, interleukins sustain persistent immune activation leading to tissue damage. IL-17 and IL-23 are key drivers in psoriasis and inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), where IL-23 promotes Th17 cell differentiation and IL-17 production, exacerbating keratinocyte hyperproliferation in psoriasis and mucosal inflammation in IBD. In rheumatoid arthritis (RA), IL-6 signals through the JAK-STAT3 pathway to induce synovial inflammation, promoting B-cell differentiation and osteoclast activation that underlies joint destruction. Mechanisms of pathogenesis involve prolonged signaling; for example, in gout, monosodium urate crystals activate the NLRP3 inflammasome in macrophages, leading to IL-1 maturation and release, which recruits neutrophils and perpetuates acute flares with chronic joint erosion. This dysregulated axis fosters fibroblast-like synoviocyte proliferation and extracellular matrix degradation across these conditions.
Human genetic and clinical studies underscore interleukins' contributions to inflammatory susceptibility. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified IL-23R variants, such as rs11209026, as protective against Crohn's disease, with risk alleles enhancing Th17 responses and ileal inflammation. A 2023 meta-analysis confirmed elevated serum IL-33 levels in asthma patients, particularly in moderate-to-severe cases, linking it to eosinophilic airway inflammation and exacerbations. Conversely, interleukins like IL-10 and IL-37 exert protective effects by counterbalancing inflammation; IL-10 inhibits pro-inflammatory cytokine production from macrophages and T cells, preventing excessive responses in colitis models, while IL-37 suppresses innate immune activation via IL-1R8, reducing tissue damage in endotoxin shock and chronic neuroinflammation. Balanced expression of these anti-inflammatory interleukins is crucial for resolving inflammation without compromising host defense.

Associations with Autoimmunity and Cancer

Interleukins play pivotal roles in the pathogenesis of autoimmune diseases by promoting pro-inflammatory responses and disrupting immune tolerance. IL-17, produced primarily by Th17 cells, drives inflammation and demyelination in multiple sclerosis (MS) through recruitment of neutrophils and disruption of the blood-brain barrier. Similarly, IL-21 exacerbates systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) by enhancing B-cell differentiation and autoantibody production, while contributing to beta-cell destruction in type 1 diabetes (T1D) via Th17 cell activation. IL-23 sustains Th17 differentiation and amplifies IL-17 production, correlating with disease activity in SLE, including lupus nephritis, and promoting insulitis in T1D models. In contrast, diminished IL-2 signaling impairs regulatory T cell (Treg) function, leading to unchecked autoreactivity in autoimmune conditions such as MS and T1D, where genetic variants in IL2RA reduce IL-2 responsiveness on Tregs.
In cancer, certain interleukins exhibit pro-tumorigenic effects by fostering an immunosuppressive microenvironment, while others bolster anti-tumor immunity. IL-6, IL-10, and IL-22 activate STAT3 signaling in tumor cells and stromal components, promoting proliferation, survival, and angiogenesis in various malignancies, including colorectal and gastric cancers. Conversely, IL-2, IL-12, and IL-15 enhance cytotoxic T lymphocyte and natural killer cell activity, forming the basis of immunotherapies that induce tumor regression in melanoma and lymphomas. For instance, IL-12 synergizes with IL-15 to amplify CD8+ T cell responses, leading to durable anti-tumor effects in preclinical models.
Mechanistically, IL-17 within the tumor microenvironment (TME) sustains chronic inflammation that recruits myeloid-derived suppressor cells and endothelial cells, thereby facilitating tumor invasion and metastasis in cancers such as skin and ovarian tumors. The IL-10 paradox highlights its dual influence: while it suppresses anti-tumor effector responses to enable immune evasion in solid tumors, it also inhibits angiogenesis, potentially curbing progression in certain contexts like cervical cancer. These context-dependent actions underscore interleukins' influence on TME dynamics, where pro- and anti-inflammatory signals interplay to modulate tumor growth.
Human genetic and clinical evidence reinforces these associations. The common G allele of the IL-23R polymorphism rs11209026 is associated with susceptibility to ankylosing spondylitis by enhancing Th17 responses and spinal inflammation, whereas the minor A allele is protective. In cancer immunotherapy, preclinical studies have demonstrated that IL-21-engineered CAR-NK cells exhibit enhanced persistence and tumor killing against relapsed lymphoma. Dual roles are exemplified by IL-27, which exerts anti-tumor effects early via Th1 and CTL induction but shifts to pro-tumor promotion in advanced stages through PD-L1 upregulation and Treg expansion in the TME.

Therapeutic Applications

Endogenous Modulators and Inhibitors

Endogenous modulators of interleukin activity include soluble receptors that act as decoys or facilitators in signaling pathways. The soluble interleukin-6 receptor (sIL-6R), generated by proteolytic shedding of the membrane-bound IL-6R, enables trans-signaling by forming a complex with IL-6 that activates gp130 on cells lacking membrane IL-6R, thereby extending IL-6's pro-inflammatory effects beyond classical signaling cells. In contrast, the soluble form of interleukin-1 receptor type II (sIL-1RII) functions primarily as a decoy receptor, binding IL-1 with high affinity to prevent its interaction with signaling-competent IL-1 receptors, thus dampening acute inflammatory responses. These soluble receptors contribute to fine-tuning interleukin bioavailability and signaling specificity in vivo.
Antagonistic interleukins provide direct competitive inhibition of pro-inflammatory interleukins. Interleukin-1 receptor antagonist (IL-1Ra), a member of the IL-1 family, binds to IL-1 receptor type I (IL-1RI) without triggering downstream signaling, effectively neutralizing IL-1 and IL-1 activities and serving as a key endogenous brake on IL-1-driven inflammation. Similarly, IL-37 exerts broad anti-inflammatory effects by suppressing innate and adaptive immune responses, including inhibition of pro-inflammatory cytokine production via interactions with IL-18R and Smad3 signaling pathways. IL-38, another IL-1 family member, acts as an antagonist to IL-36 receptor signaling, blocking IL-36-induced inflammation and promoting resolution in epithelial and immune contexts. IL-10, while primarily known for its immunosuppressive role, inhibits downstream effects of multiple interleukins such as IL-1 and IL-6 by downregulating antigen presentation and cytokine synthesis in macrophages and dendritic cells.
Feedback mechanisms at the transcriptional and post-transcriptional levels further regulate interleukin expression and activity. Suppressors of cytokine signaling (SOCS) proteins, such as SOCS1 and SOCS3, are induced by STAT3 activation following IL-6 family signaling and form negative feedback loops by binding JAK kinases or cytokine receptors like gp130, thereby inhibiting further JAK/STAT pathway activation and preventing excessive inflammation. MicroRNAs also contribute to these loops; for instance, miR-146a, upregulated by NF-B in response to IL-1 or IL-6 stimulation, targets TRAF6 and IRAK1 to attenuate NF-B activity, reducing IL-6 production and establishing a post-transcriptional brake on inflammatory cascades.
Genetic variations in humans influence the efficacy of these modulators. Polymorphisms in the IL-1Ra gene, particularly the variable number tandem repeat (VNTR) in intron 2, are associated with altered IL-1Ra production levels; the allele with two repeats (A2) correlates with lower IL-1Ra expression and increased susceptibility to severe rheumatoid arthritis due to unchecked IL-1 activity.
Recent insights highlight IL-35's role in regulatory T cell (Treg)-mediated suppression, where this heterodimeric cytokine (composed of IL-12 and IL-27 subunits) is secreted by Tregs to inhibit effector T cell proliferation and pro-inflammatory cytokine release, thereby maintaining immune homeostasis and limiting autoimmunity.

Recombinant Analogues and Derivatives

Recombinant human interleukin-2 (rhIL-2), marketed as aldesleukin, is a non-glycosylated analog produced via recombinant DNA technology in Escherichia coli to stimulate T-cell proliferation and enhance adaptive immune responses in preclinical models of cancer and infectious diseases. Similarly, recombinant human interleukin-11 (rhIL-11), known as oprelvekin, is engineered to promote megakaryocyte differentiation and hematopoietic stem cell proliferation, addressing thrombocytopenia in preclinical hematopoiesis studies.
Derivatives of interleukins have been developed to improve pharmacokinetic properties, such as pegylated IL-2 variants that attach polyethylene glycol (PEG) chains to extend serum half-life and reduce clearance rates in preclinical pharmacokinetic analyses. Fusion proteins combining IL-2 and IL-15 signaling domains, like Neoleukin-2/15 constructs, enhance selective activation of CD8+ T cells and natural killer cells while minimizing toxicity in tumor-bearing mouse models.
Monoclonal antibodies serve as key inhibitors targeting interleukins; for instance, tocilizumab is a humanized IgG1 antibody that binds both soluble and membrane-bound IL-6 receptors, blocking downstream signaling pathways in preclinical inflammation assays. Secukinumab, a fully human anti-IL-17A IgG1 antibody, neutralizes IL-17A to inhibit Th17-mediated inflammatory cascades in experimental autoimmune models. Bispecific antibodies targeting IL-23, such as those simultaneously binding IL-23p19 and TNF- or IL-36R, demonstrate synergistic blockade of pro-inflammatory pathways in preclinical colitis and psoriasis xenografts.
Production of these recombinant analogues typically employs E. coli for non-glycosylated interleukins like rhIL-2 to achieve high yields of unglycosylated proteins with retained bioactivity, while Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells are used for glycosylated variants to mimic native post-translational modifications. Glycosylation engineering in CHO systems, including glycoengineered cell lines, enhances protein stability and half-life by optimizing sialylation and branching patterns, as shown in fed-batch cultures yielding bioactive IL-11 derivatives.
In preclinical studies as of 2025, engineered mimics of IL-38, including recombinant variants overexpressed in murine models, exhibit potent anti-inflammatory effects by suppressing IL-6 and TNF- production, attenuating arthritis severity through modulation of innate immune responses.

Clinical Uses and International Nonproprietary Names

Several interleukin (IL)-based therapies have received regulatory approval for clinical use, primarily targeting cancers and autoimmune or inflammatory conditions through recombinant analogues, receptor antagonists, or fusion proteins. Aldesleukin, the recombinant form of IL-2 with the international nonproprietary name (INN) aldesleukin, is approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of metastatic renal cell carcinoma and melanoma, where high-dose regimens have demonstrated objective response rates of 20-30% in melanoma patients, including durable complete responses in a subset. However, its use is associated with significant toxicities, notably capillary leak syndrome (CLS) occurring in up to 70% of patients, characterized by hypotension, edema, and potential organ dysfunction due to increased vascular permeability.
Oprelvekin, the recombinant human IL-11 with INN oprelvekin, was FDA-approved in 1997 for preventing severe thrombocytopenia and reducing the need for platelet transfusions in patients undergoing chemotherapy for non-myeloid malignancies. Clinical trials showed it reduced platelet transfusion requirements by approximately 40% in responsive patients, though common side effects include fluid retention and cardiac arrhythmias; however, it was discontinued in the US market in 2010 and is no longer available.
Anakinra, an IL-1 receptor antagonist (IL-1Ra) analogue with INN anakinra, is approved for rheumatoid arthritis (RA) in patients who have failed disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs, demonstrating American College of Rheumatology 20% improvement criteria (ACR20) response rates of 40-50% in pivotal trials, with a favorable safety profile dominated by injection-site reactions rather than systemic immunosuppression. For psoriasis, ustekinumab, a monoclonal antibody targeting the p40 subunit shared by IL-12 and IL-23 (INN ustekinumab), is FDA-approved and achieves Psoriasis Area and Severity Index (PASI) 75 response rates of 60-70% at week 12, with low rates of serious infections.
Among fusion protein analogues, denileukin diftitox (INN denileukin diftitox), a diphtheria toxin-IL-2 conjugate branded as LYMPHIR (denileukin diftitox-cxdl), was historically approved for persistent or recurrent cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (CTCL) expressing CD25, yielding overall response rates of 30-36% in phase III trials, though it was discontinued in 2014 due to manufacturing issues; an improved purity version received FDA approval on August 7, 2024, for relapsed or refractory Stage I-III CTCL after at least one prior systemic therapy, with phase II data showing 38% overall response rates and manageable hypersensitivity as the primary side effect.
Investigational IL-targeted therapies continue to advance, particularly in respiratory and autoimmune diseases. Anti-IL-33 monoclonal antibodies, such as itepekimab and tozorakimab, are in phase III trials for chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), with 2025 data from AERIFY-1 and AERIFY-2 studies indicating reductions in exacerbations of 30-34% over placebo, alongside forced expiratory volume in 1 second (FEV1) improvements, and a safety profile similar to other biologics. As of November 2025, no IL-38 inhibitors have achieved approval for lupus, but preclinical studies suggest potential in modulating Th17 responses in systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE). As of November 2025, no novel interleukin-based therapies received FDA approval beyond prior years, with focus on ongoing trials for IL-33 inhibitors in COPD.
Looking ahead, personalized medicine approaches leveraging IL gene polymorphisms are emerging to optimize therapy selection and dosing. For instance, polymorphisms in the IL-6 promoter (-174 G/C) have been associated with variable responses to IL-6 inhibitors in RA and cancer, enabling genotype-based stratification to predict efficacy and toxicity risks, while IL-16 variants correlate with survival outcomes in solid tumors treated with IL-2 pathways. These strategies aim to enhance response rates in heterogeneous populations, particularly in autoimmunity and oncology, by integrating pharmacogenomic testing into clinical decision-making.
