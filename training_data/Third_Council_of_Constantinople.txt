 The Third Council of Constantinople, recognized as the sixth ecumenical council by Eastern Orthodox and Catholic traditions, was convened by Byzantine Emperor Constantine IV in the imperial palace of Constantinople from November 7, 680, to September 16, 681, to definitively resolve the Monothelite controversy that had divided the Church for decades. The assembly, comprising approximately 174 bishops predominantly from Eastern sees along with legates dispatched by Pope Agatho, examined patristic testimonies and scriptural evidence to affirm the orthodox Christology of the Council of Chalcedon, proclaiming that Christ possesses two natural willsone divine and one humanand two corresponding natural energies or principles of operation, united without confusion, change, division, or separation in his single person. This doctrinal clarification rejected Monothelitism's assertion of a single will in Christ as incompatible with the integrity of his dual natures, thereby safeguarding the reality of his full humanity and divinity essential for human salvation.
In its eighteenth and final session, the council issued a synodal definition explicitly stating: "We proclaim equally two natural volitions or wills in him and two natural principles of action which undergo no division, no change, no partition, no confusion, in accord with the teaching of the holy fathers we confess that a natural volition of his divine and incorruptible divinity properly had fellowship with his human will which followed and willed in the same way." Key figures such as Patriarchs Sergius I of Constantinople, Pyrrhus, Paul II, and Macarius of Antioch, along with theologians like Theodore of Pharan, were posthumously or presently anathematized for promoting the heresy, with the council also condemning the late Pope Honorius I for having followed their views and thereby scandalized the flock. This condemnation of a Roman pontiff, ratified by Pope Leo II who succeeded Agatho, underscored the council's independence in doctrinal judgment while highlighting tensions between imperial initiative and papal authority in the Byzantine era. The proceedings reaffirmed the first five ecumenical councils and aimed to restore ecclesiastical unity amid external pressures like Arab invasions, though Monothelitism persisted in some regions, contributing to further schisms such as among the Maronites.

Theological Background

Origins and Development of Monothelitism

Monothelitism arose in the context of Emperor Heraclius's (r. 610641) military and political efforts to reunify the Byzantine Empire amid territorial losses to Persian and later Arab forces, particularly in regions dominated by Monophysite Christians who rejected the Council of Chalcedon's (451) affirmation of two natures in Christ. Seeking doctrinal compromise to restore ecclesiastical unity and imperial loyalty, Heraclius consulted Patriarch Sergius I of Constantinople (610638), who advocated avoiding explicit references to "two energies" (dyoenergism) in Christ to appease Monophysites while nominally upholding Chalcedon. This approach evolved from earlier monoenergist ideas, positing a single divine-human energy in Christ, but shifted emphasis to a single will (thelma) as a subtler formulation.
Sergius formalized the doctrine through correspondence, including a letter to Pope Honorius I (r. 625638) around 634, in which he proposed that Christ possessed "one will" to sidestep divisive terminology, claiming patristic precedent from figures like Athanasius of Alexandria without endorsing full Monophysitism. Honorius's ambiguous reply, suggesting avoidance of "two energies" talk, encouraged Sergius, who collaborated with Heraclius to draft the Ecthesis (Greek for "statement"), promulgated on October 4, 638. The Ecthesis decreed one will in Christ, forbidding discussion of energies or wills, and was endorsed by a synod in Constantinople shortly before Sergius's death on December 9, 638; it positioned monothelitism as imperial orthodoxy, circulated with Heraclius's endorsement, and briefly gained traction in the East. 
Following Heraclius's death in 641 and amid growing opposition from figures like Sophronius of Jerusalem, who rejected the doctrine at the 636 Synod of Jerusalem, his grandson Constans II (r. 641668) initially upheld the Ecthesis but faced escalating resistance, including from Pope Martin I's Lateran Synod of 649, which condemned it. To quell unrest without fully retracting the compromise, Constans II issued the Typos (Greek for "decree") in September 648, prohibiting all debate on one or two wills and energies alike, effectively suspending the Ecthesis while maintaining doctrinal ambiguity to preserve unity. Enforced harshly, including the exile of opponents like Maximus the Confessor in 653, the Typos prolonged monothelitism's influence until Constans's assassination in 668, delaying open resolution until the Third Council of Constantinople. 

Key Proponents and Opponents Prior to the Council

The doctrine of Monothelitism, positing that Christ possessed only one will despite his two natures, was initially advanced by Emperor Heraclius (r. 610641) as a compromise to unify the Byzantine Empire's divided Christological factions amid Persian and Arab invasions that weakened eastern provinces with significant Monophysite populations. Heraclius, seeking ecclesiastical reconciliation after military setbacks, collaborated closely with Ecumenical Patriarch Sergius I of Constantinople (r. 610638), who formulated the key theological expressions denying distinct human and divine operations in Christ. Sergius authored the Ecthesis edict issued by Heraclius toward the end of 638, which ordered confession of one will in Christ while prohibiting the expressions "one operation" and "two operations," suppressing public debate on the issue under imperial decree.
Sergius's successors perpetuated the doctrine: Pyrrhus I (patriarch 638641 and briefly 654) actively promoted it during debates, including a public disputation with Maximus the Confessor in 645 where he initially defended but later wavered under argumentation. Paul II of Constantinople (r. 641654) reaffirmed Monothelite tenets in correspondence with Pope Theodore I, leading to his excommunication by Rome in 649, while Macarius I of Antioch (r. 659681) staunchly upheld the single-will position in eastern synods and writings prior to the council, viewing dyothelitism (two wills) as Nestorian.
Opposition crystallized early among Chalcedonian stalwarts. Patriarch Sophronius of Jerusalem (r. 634638) vehemently protested the Ecthesis upon its promulgation, composing treatises and synodal letters in 633634 that insisted on two natural wills and operations in Christ to preserve the integrity of his humanity against any apparent Monophysite concessions. The monk Maximus the Confessor (c. 580662), active from the 640s, orchestrated resistance across regions, convincing North African councils in 646 to anathematize Monothelitism and engaging in epistolary campaigns that emphasized scriptural and patristic evidence for Christ's human will as essential to his full incarnation.
In the West, Pope Martin I (r. 649655) escalated opposition by convening the Lateran Synod from 5 October to 31 October 649, attended by 105 bishops, which issued 20 anathemas condemning Monothelite formulations like "one operation" and key proponents including Sergius, Pyrrhus, and Paul, while affirming two wills through appeals to councils such as Chalcedon (451). This synod's acts, dispatched to eastern patriarchs, provoked imperial backlash under Constans II, who issued the Typos in 648649 to forbid further discussion, yet solidified dyothelite orthodoxy among Latin and African sees.

Historical Context

Imperial Efforts at Doctrinal Compromise

Following the doctrinal divisions exacerbated by the Council of Chalcedon in 451, which affirmed two natures in Christ but alienated many Monophysites in the eastern provinces, Emperor Heraclius (r. 610641) sought to restore ecclesiastical unity within the Byzantine Empire amid existential threats from Persian and later Arab invasions. In 638, Heraclius promulgated the Ecthesis, a formula of faith drafted primarily by Patriarch Sergius I of Constantinople, which endorsed Monothelitismthe doctrine that Christ possessed two natures but only one divine-human will and operation (thelema and energeia). This was explicitly intended as a compromise to reconcile Chalcedonian orthodoxy with Monophysite sensibilities by conceding a unified will while upholding the Chalcedonian dyophysitism, thereby aiming to consolidate imperial loyalty in regions like Egypt and Syria where Monophysitism predominated.
The Ecthesis was endorsed by a synod in Constantinople shortly after its issuance and imposed as imperial orthodoxy, prohibiting teachings on two energies while mandating acceptance of one will as consonant with patristic tradition, particularly interpretations of writings by figures like Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite. However, it encountered immediate resistance in the West; Pope Severinus (r. 640) rejected it, leading to a papal interdict against Sergius until Heraclius' death in 641, after which the formula's enforcement waned under the regency for the young Constans II. Despite initial papal opposition, some Eastern patriarchs and theologians provisionally accepted it as a via media, though it failed to achieve widespread Monophysite reconciliation and instead deepened divisions by appearing to dilute Chalcedonian precision on Christ's full humanity.
Heraclius' successor, Constans II (r. 641668), inherited persistent schisms and escalating Arab conquests that further underscored the need for internal cohesion, prompting renewed imperial intervention in 648 with the Typos (or Type), an edict that superseded the Ecthesis by forbidding all public or private discussion, teaching, or writing on the number of wills or energies in Christ under penalty of exile or confiscation. Unlike the Ecthesis, the Typos avoided explicitly affirming Monothelitism, instead imposing doctrinal silence as a pragmatic compromise to halt polemics and enforce unity without alienating either dyothelite (two wills) proponents or lingering Monothelites, reflecting Constans' prioritization of political stability over theological resolution amid territorial losses.
Enforcement of the Typos involved suppressing dissent, including the exile of Pope Martin I after the Lateran Synod of 649 condemned both the edict and Monothelitism, and the trial and mutilation of Maximus the Confessor in 655 for refusing compliance; these measures highlighted the edict's coercive nature rather than genuine compromise, as it equated opposition with sedition threatening imperial security. The Typos achieved temporary quiescence in some Eastern sees but intensified Western alienation, with Rome viewing it as caesaropapism infringing on doctrinal authority, ultimately proving unsustainable as Arab advances continued unabated.

Ecclesiastical Divisions and Previous Synods

The Monothelite controversy, emerging in the early seventh century, fractured the Christian Church along doctrinal and jurisdictional lines, pitting advocates of a single will (thelema) in Christintended as a compromise with non-Chalcedonian Monophysitesagainst defenders of two wills corresponding to Christ's two natures as defined at the Council of Chalcedon in 451. Eastern patriarchs, under pressure from emperors Heraclius and Constans II, largely endorsed the doctrine via the Ecthesis of 638 and the Typos of 648649, which respectively affirmed one will and banned further debate, fostering compliance in Constantinople, Antioch, and Alexandria while alienating Rome and monastic centers like those in Palestine and Africa. Western bishops, emphasizing the integrity of Chalcedonian dyophysitism, rejected these imperial edicts as encroachments on orthodox Christology, resulting in schismatic tensions that saw key figures such as Sophronius of Jerusalem and Maximus the Confessor excommunicated or persecuted for upholding dyothelitism.
These divisions were compounded by ambiguous papal responses, notably Honorius I's 634 letter to Sergius I of Constantinople, which avoided explicit condemnation and was later interpreted by Monothelites as tacit approval, though subsequent popes like Severinus (640642) and John IV (640642) anathematized the Ecthesis. The rift extended to imperial-papal relations, as Constans II's Typos provoked defiance in the West, leading to arrests and trials; Pope Martin I's resistance exemplified this, framing Monothelitism not merely as theological error but as a threat to ecclesiastical autonomy against caesaropapist interference. By the 660s, the Eastern Church remained predominantly Monothelite under patriarchs like Peter of Alexandria, while Western synods and African bishops upheld dyothelite orthodoxy, creating a de facto schism that undermined ecumenical unity and military cohesion amid Arab conquests.
Prior synods both propagated and contested these positions. In the East, a Constantinopolitan synod in 638 under Sergius ratified the Ecthesis, endorsing one will as compatible with Chalcedon, while subsequent gatherings under Pyrrhus I (638641, 654) reaffirmed imperial formulas despite brief recantations. The pivotal opposing synod occurred in Rome: the Lateran Synod of 649, convoked by Pope Martin I and attended by 105 bishops, issued 20 canons condemning monoenergism, Monothelitism, the Ecthesis, and the Typos, anathematizing Sergius, Pyrrhus, Paul II of Constantinople, and Honorius for promoting doctrinal innovation. This assembly, drawing on patristic testimonies from figures like Athanasius and Cyril of Alexandria, asserted that Christ's two natures necessitated two natural wills and operations, without division or confusion, thereby galvanizing Western resistance and prompting Martin's deposition and exile to Cherson in 653655.
Such synods underscored the impasse, as Eastern endorsements prioritized imperial reconciliation with Monophysites over strict Chalcedonian fidelity, while the Lateran Council's canonscirculated widely despite persecutionpreserved dyothelite teaching in exile communities, awaiting resolution through broader conciliar authority. The failure of interim efforts, like Pyrrhus' 645 debate with Maximus in Carthage (where he temporarily conceded two wills before reverting), highlighted the controversy's intractability, paving the way for Emperor Constantine IV's convocation of an ecumenical council to heal the breach.

Convocation and Preparations

Emperor Constantine IV's Call to Council

Following the death of his father, Constans II, in 668, Emperor Constantine IV inherited a Byzantine Empire beset by the unresolved Monothelite controversy, which had originated under Heraclius and persisted through imperial edicts like the Ecthesis of 638 and the Typos of 648, fostering doctrinal division and schism with the Latin West. Recognizing the need for a definitive settlement to restore ecclesiastical unity amid external threats from Arab invasions, Constantine initiated consultations with Pope Agatho I, who had convened a synod in Rome to affirm dyothelitism. This dialogue reflected Constantine's intent to transcend prior compromises by assembling an ecumenical body under imperial auspices, prioritizing orthodox Christology over political expediency.
On September 10, 680, Constantine issued a formal sacraan imperial rescriptto Patriarch George I of Constantinople, explicitly ordering the convocation of a council of bishops drawn from across the empire, including representatives from the Eastern patriarchates of Alexandria, Antioch, and Jerusalem, as well as invitations to the Western Church to ensure universality. The edict mandated examination of the Monothelite doctrines, particularly the assertions of one will and one energy in Christ promoted by figures like Patriarch Sergius I and Maximus the Confessor's opponents, with the emperor emphasizing impartial inquiry grounded in the definitions of prior ecumenical councils such as Chalcedon. This directive marked a departure from Constans II's suppression of debate, signaling Constantine's commitment to open adjudication rather than enforced silence.
The call prioritized logistical feasibility, scheduling the assembly in the imperial palace at Constantinople for accessibility to Eastern delegates while accommodating travel for papal legates, who arrived bearing Agatho's doctrinal letter. Approximately 170 bishops ultimately attended the opening on November 7, 680, under Constantine's personal oversight in initial sessions, underscoring his role not merely as convener but as guarantor of the council's authority against potential Monothelite resurgence in imperial circles. This initiative effectively reversed the doctrinal policies of preceding emperors, aligning Byzantine orthodoxy with Western dyothelite consensus and paving the way for the council's condemnation of Monothelitism.

Papal Involvement and Delegation from Rome

In response to Emperor Constantine IV's overtures for ecclesiastical reconciliation, Pope Agatho organized regional synods across the West to gather opinions on Christological doctrine before convening a central synod in Rome around Easter 680, attended by 125 bishops who affirmed the existence of two natural wills and operations in Christ, in line with Chalcedonian orthodoxy, and rejected Monothelitism. This synod produced a dogmatic profession of faith that served as formal instruction for the papal legates dispatched to Constantinople.
Agatho dispatched a delegation of legates, including presbyters Theodore and George, deacon John, and subdeacon Constantine, carrying two key documents: a personal letter to the emperor outlining Rome's condemnation of Monothelitism and adherence to patristic tradition, and the synodal epistle from the 125 bishops elaborating the orthodox position. The legates arrived in Constantinople by September 680, prior to the council's opening on November 7.
During the council's fourth session on November 15, 680, the legates presented Agatho's letter to the emperor, which was read aloud and received with acclaim by most bishops as a faithful exposition of apostolic teaching from the successor of Peter; it emphasized that the Roman Church had preserved unaltered doctrine against Eastern innovations. The document's influence proved decisive, as the council in its eighth session on March 7, 681, explicitly adopted Agatho's doctrinal stance, condemning Monothelite proponents and affirming dyothelitism and dyenergism.
Though Agatho died on January 10, 681, before the council concluded, the legates continued to represent Rome, ensuring alignment with the papal position; the council's subsequent definition integrated the Roman profession, and its acts were later confirmed by Agatho's successor, Pope Leo II. This involvement underscored Rome's role in safeguarding orthodoxy amid imperial efforts at compromise.

Proceedings of the Council

Initial Sessions and Reception of Agatho's Letter

The Third Council of Constantinople convened its first session on November 7, 680, in the Trullus chamber of the imperial palace, under the presidency of Emperor Constantine IV. The assembly, initially comprising around 43 bishops, declared itself ecumenical and proceeded to recite the Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed, the creed of the 150 fathers of Constantinople I, and the definition of Chalcedon to underscore adherence to established Christological doctrine. The emperor addressed the gathering, expressing intent to resolve the Monothelite controversy through scriptural and patristic examination, while the papal legatesrepresenting Pope Agathodelivered speeches affirming Rome's unwavering orthodoxy.
Subsequent early sessions focused on procedural matters and the presentation of key documents. Pope Agatho's lettersone addressed to the emperor and another to the council, endorsed by a Roman synod of 125 bishopshad arrived in Constantinople by early September 680 via the papal delegation. These epistles articulated the dyothelite position, rejecting a single will in Christ and invoking Petrine primacy in preserving apostolic faith.
In the fourth session, held on November 15, 680, Patriarch George I of Constantinople requested the reading of Agatho's communications. The council heard the texts aloud, after which the bishops acclaimed them with enthusiasm, hailing the Roman profession as luminous and consonant with the fathers of Chalcedon. This reception positioned Agatho's doctrine as a foundational guide for deliberations, influencing the condemnation of Monothelitism in later sessions.

Debates, Examinations, and Condemnations

Following the reading of Pope Agatho's letter in the fourth session on February 9, 681, which affirmed the doctrine of two natural wills and operations in Christ, the council proceeded to intensive debates led primarily by the papal legates. These discussions scrutinized Monothelite interpretations of Christology, contrasting them with patristic evidence from figures such as Athanasius, Cyril of Alexandria, and Gregory of Nazianzus, whose writings were compiled into florilegia to demonstrate support for dyothelitism and dyenergism. Monothelite advocates, including Patriarch Macarius of Antioch, countered with their own selective quotations, arguing for a single divine-human will manifested in a "theandric" operation, but these were systematically refuted as misrepresentations or forgeries during sessions eight through twelve.
In the eighth session on March 7, 681, the bishops debated and adopted Agatho's exposition, explicitly condemning Monothelitism as incompatible with Chalcedonian Christology. Examinations intensified in subsequent sessions, particularly targeting Macarius and his disciple Polychronius of Bradara, whose writings and defenses were interrogated. Macarius, accused of promoting monoenergism since the time of Emperor Heraclius, presented texts purporting to show patristic endorsement of one operation but refused to anathematize key Monothelite figures like Sergius of Constantinople when pressed by the assembly. In the twelfth and thirteenth sessions, spanning late March 681, Macarius and Polychronius were formally tried; their persistent adherence to a single will led to their deposition and anathematization, with the council declaring their doctrines heretical after exhaustive review of over 200 patristic excerpts. Other bishops, such as Theophasius of Jerusalem, initially wavered but ultimately subscribed to the orthodox position under examination.
Condemnations culminated in the thirteenth session on March 28, 681, where anathemas were pronounced against principal Monothelite proponents, including Patriarchs Sergius, Pyrrhus, and Paul of Constantinople; Cyrus of Alexandria; Theodore of Pharan; Peter of Laodicea; and, posthumously, Pope Honorius I for his letters endorsing expressions like "one will" in Christ, which the council deemed supportive of the heresy. These anathemas were reiterated publicly in the sixteenth session and incorporated into the final dogmatic definition on September 16, 681, signed by 174 bishops, the papal legates, and Emperor Constantine IV. The process emphasized scriptural and conciliar precedents from Ephesus and Chalcedon, ensuring condemnations rested on doctrinal inconsistency rather than personal authority alone.

Final Sessions and Promulgation of the Definition

The council's seventeenth session culminated in the drafting and internal approval of the horos, or doctrinal definition, which synthesized the orthodox Christological teaching affirmed throughout the proceedings. This definition explicitly rejected monothelitism and monoenergism, declaring that Christ possesses two natural willsthe divine and the humanand two corresponding natural energies, indivisibly united in his single person without opposition, confusion, or diminution, in harmony with the teachings of previous ecumenical councils and the letter of Pope Agatho. The formulation drew directly from scriptural, patristic, and conciliar sources examined in earlier sessions, ensuring continuity with the dyophysite doctrine of Chalcedon while addressing the specific errors of imperial compromises under Heraclius and Constans II.
In the eighteenth and final session, convened on September 16, 681, the horos was publicly promulgated in the presence of Emperor Constantine IV, his court, and the assembled bishops within the imperial palace at Constantinople. The emperor, who had actively participated in key sessions to guide proceedings toward doctrinal resolution rather than political expediency, verbally endorsed the definition, affirming its alignment with apostolic tradition and its role in restoring ecclesiastical unity amid ongoing threats from Monophysite and Islamic expansions. All 174 participating bishops, including the papal legates representing Rome, affixed their signatures to the acts, symbolizing consensus across Eastern patriarchates and the West.
The promulgated definition was subsequently transmitted to Pope Leo II, who succeeded Agatho in July 681 and provided formal Roman confirmation, translating it into Latin for Western dissemination and emphasizing its vindication of Maximus the Confessor's witness against imperial heresy. This closure marked the council's success in enforcing dyothelitism as binding dogma, with the emperor issuing edicts for its imperial ratification and suppression of dissenting texts, though enforcement faced resistance from lingering Monothelite sympathizers in monastic and patriarchal circles.

Doctrinal Definitions

Affirmation of Dyothelitism and Dyenergism

The Third Council of Constantinople affirmed dyothelitism, the doctrine that Christ possesses two natural willsa divine will shared consubstantially with the Father and Holy Spirit, and a human will assumed in the Incarnationunited in his single person without division, change, partition, or confusion. This was codified in the council's horos (dogmatic definition), promulgated during its eighteenth and final session on September 16, 681, following eighteen sessions spanning November 7, 680, to that date. The definition explicitly stated: "We proclaim equally two natural volitions or wills in him and two natural principles of action which undergo no division, no change, no partition, no confusion." The human will was described as subordinate and concurring with the divine, as evidenced in Christ's prayer in Gethsemane ("not my will, but thine, be done"Luke 22:42), ensuring the volitional distinction preserves the reality of his full humanity while upholding hypostatic union.
Complementing dyothelitism, the council upheld dyenergism, asserting two natural operations or energies in Christone divine and one humanproceeding indivisibly and inconfusedly, with each nature performing actions proper to it in communion with the other. The horos declared: "We glorify two natural operations, without division, without change, without partition, without confusiona divine operation and a human operationaccording to the word of the holy fathers." This framework rejected any notion of a single "theandric" (divine-human) energy, which Monothelites like Patriarch Sergius I of Constantinople had promoted to reconcile Chalcedonian dyophysitism with Miaphysite concerns, but which the council deemed a novelty compromising Christ's distinct divine and human activities. Patristic support included citations from Athanasius of Alexandria, who distinguished Christ's human willing from divine, and Cyril of Alexandria, who affirmed natural operations proper to each nature without implying personal duality.
The affirmations were presented as a direct extension of the Fifth Ecumenical Council (553) and Pope Agatho's letter, read and acclaimed in the eighth session on March 7, 681, which confessed "two natural wills and two natural operations" in Christ for human salvation. By anathematizing Monothelite formulations as reviving errors akin to Apollinarianism and Eutychianism, the council emphasized causal integrity: the divine energy effects supernatural works (e.g., miracles), while the human energy enacts volitional acts (e.g., suffering and obedience), harmonized in the Logos without absorption or opposition. This doctrinal precision, endorsed by 174 bishops under Emperor Constantine IV's auspices, aimed to restore ecclesiastical unity fractured by imperial compromises like the Ecthesis (638) and Typos (648).

Integration with Prior Christological Councils

The dogmatic definition promulgated by the Third Council of Constantinople on March 28, 681, explicitly positioned its teachings as a continuation of the prior five ecumenical councils, stating that it followed "without deviation the traditions handed down to the Church of God from the very great and most pious fathers, all with one mind teaching the same doctrine, namely the five holy and universal synods." This integration preserved the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed from the First (325) and Second (381) Councils of Constantinople, which established the orthodox formulation of Christ's divinity and consubstantiality with the Father, while extending their implications to counter Monothelitism's threat to the integrity of Christ's person.
Building directly on the Council of Ephesus (431), which condemned Nestorianism and affirmed Christ's single personhood through the hypostatic union as articulated in Cyril of Alexandria's letters, the Sixth Council rejected any division of wills that might imply separate persons, insisting instead on the inseparable unity of the divine and human in one hypostasis. It further aligned with the Council of Chalcedon (451) by endorsing the Tome of Leo, which defined Christ as possessing two naturesdivine and humanunconfusedly, unchangeably, indivisibly, and inseparably; the new definition specified that these natures entailed "two natural wills and two natural operations," with the human will submitting harmoniously to the divine without opposition or absorption, thus completing Chalcedon's framework against Eutychian monophysitism and its variants. This dyothelitist affirmation ensured the full humanity of Christ, including volitional and energetic faculties proper to human nature, as implicit in Chalcedon's rejection of confusion or change in the natures.
The council also integrated the Second Council of Constantinople (553), which had condemned the "Three Chapters" associated with Nestorian tendencies while reaffirming Cyrilline Christology, by prohibiting any innovations that diluted the personal union; it declared that denying two wills risked reviving aphthartodocetism or other errors already anathematized in prior synods. Overall, the definition maintained the patristic consensus from 318 fathers at Nicaea to 630 at Chalcedon, prohibiting novel creeds and mandating adherence under penalty of deposition, thereby synthesizing Trinitarian orthodoxy with a robust dyophysite Christology that preserved causal distinctions between divine and human operations without compromising unity.

Specific Condemnations

Monothelite Patriarchs and Theologians

The Third Council of Constantinople anathematized several patriarchs of Constantinople for their role in originating and disseminating Monothelitism, a doctrine positing one will (thelema) and one energy (energeia) in Christ despite his two natures. Sergius I, patriarch from 610 to 638, initiated the heresy through his 633 letter to Pope Honorius I, proposing a single will to bridge Chalcedonian and Monophysite divides, and his Ecthesis under Emperor Heraclius in 638 formalized it imperial policy. His immediate successorsPyrrhus (patriarch 638641 and briefly 654), Paul II (641653), and Peter (654666)endorsed and enforced this teaching via synodal letters and defenses against dyothelite critics like Maximus the Confessor, persisting even after imperial shifts. These four were collectively condemned in the council's 13th session on March 28, 681, for subverting Chalcedon's dyophysitism by implying a divine absorption of Christ's human will.
Beyond Constantinople, patriarchal proponents included Cyrus of Alexandria (patriarch 630642), whose 633 synodical letter to Sergius equated one energy with Monophysite compromises, and Macarius I of Antioch (659681), who at the council's 11th and 12th sessions (March 15 and 28, 681) defended Monothelite patristic interpretations while refusing to sign Pope Agatho's dyothelite letter, leading to his on-site deposition and anathema for falsifying patristic texts. Macarius's disciple Stephen and hieromonk Polychronius were similarly expelled after examinations revealed their alignment with his truncated citations of figures like Athanasius and Gregory of Nazianzus to support unitary operations in Christ.
Key theologians condemned included Theodore of Pharan, a Palestinian monk and bishop active circa 630642, whose Exposition and letters explicitly denied distinct human operations in Christ, linking them to Severus of Antioch's Monophysitism; his writings were scrutinized in sessions 1013 and deemed heretical for contradicting Ephesus and Chalcedon. The council's horos in session 18 (September 16, 681) reinforced these anathemas, binding them to prior ecumenical affirmations of two wills, and Emperor Constantine IV ratified them via edict, enforcing exclusion from orthodox communion.

Posthumous Anathema of Pope Honorius I

In the thirteenth session of the Third Council of Constantinople, held on March 28, 681, the bishops examined two letters written by Pope Honorius I (r. 625638) to Patriarch Sergius I of Constantinople, dated circa 634635. These documents, read aloud during the proceedings, contained phrases such as "one will in the dispensation of the Incarnation of our Lord Jesus Christ," which the council interpreted as endorsing the Monothelite heresy by implying a single divine-human will in Christ rather than distinct divine and human wills. Following the reading, the council ordered the letters burned in the assembly hall as heretical, and the bishops exclaimed, "Anathema to the heretic Honorius!"
The condemnation was formalized in the council's horos (definition of faith), promulgated in the eighteenth and final session on September 16, 681. This decree explicitly anathematized Honorius posthumouslyover four decades after his death on October 12, 638alongside other Monothelite leaders like Sergius, Cyrus of Alexandria, Pyrrhus of Constantinople, and Paul of Constantinople. The text stated: "And with these we define that there shall be expelled from the holy Church of God and anathematized Honorius who was some time Pope of Old Rome, because we find in his letter to Sergius that in all respects he followed his view and confirmed his impious doctrines." The papal legates from Rome, representing Pope Agatho, initially voiced reservations but ultimately subscribed to the acts, including the anathema against their predecessor.
This action marked a rare instance of an ecumenical council condemning a former pope by name for doctrinal error, attributing to Honorius responsibility for aiding the spread of Monothelitism through negligent or affirmative support rather than explicit opposition to the emerging heresy. The council's report to Emperor Constantine IV described Honorius as "the confirmer of heresy who contradicted himself," emphasizing his failure to uphold orthodox dyothelitism (two wills) as defined in prior Christological councils. Subsequent papal confirmation came from Pope Leo II (r. 682683), who, in ratifying the council's decrees, anathematized Honorius for "following and confirming the impious doctrine," though later interpretations debated whether this constituted formal teaching of heresy or mere personal error. The anathema was reiterated in later councils, including the Quinisext Synod (692) and the Second Council of Nicaea (787), embedding the condemnation in Eastern liturgical diptychs excluding Honorius from commemorations.

Canons and Practical Decrees

Disciplinary Canons Issued

The Third Council of Constantinople, convened from November 7, 680, to September 16, 681, focused primarily on resolving the Christological controversy of Monothelitism through doctrinal definition and condemnations, without promulgating disciplinary canons. Its acts centered on eighteen sessions debating theological texts, examining witnesses, and affirming dyothelitism (two wills in Christ), culminating in the Horos (definition of faith) and anathemas against key proponents like Patriarchs Sergius I of Constantinople and Pyrrhus of Constantinople. No provisions were made for clerical discipline, simony, liturgical practices, or ecclesiastical governance, which were typical of other ecumenical councils' canonical output.
This omission reflected the council's urgent doctrinal mandate under Emperor Constantine IV and Pope Agatho I, prioritizing heresy refutation over administrative reforms. Subsequent councils addressed the gap: the Quinisext Council (also known as the Council in Trullo) of 692 issued 102 disciplinary canons to supplement the Fifth (553) and Sixth (680681) Ecumenical Councils, which had neglected such matters. These later canons covered topics like clerical continence, fasting rules, and prohibitions on simony, thereby completing the legislative framework absent from the Third Council of Constantinople.

Enforcement Mechanisms

Following the council's conclusion on September 16, 681, Emperor Constantine IV issued an imperial edict promulgating its doctrinal definitions and condemnations across the Byzantine Empire, mandating adherence to the affirmation of two wills and two energies in Christ while rejecting Monothelitism. This edict served as the primary enforcement tool, requiring all ecclesiastical and civil authorities to suppress Monothelite teachings and texts, with non-compliance penalized based on the offender's status: deposition from office for bishops and clerics, confiscation of property and removal from position for officials, and corporal punishment for private individuals. The edict's dissemination ensured rapid implementation in eastern provinces, where imperial oversight facilitated the removal of Monothelite sympathizers from key sees, such as the deposition of lingering adherents in Constantinople and Alexandria.
Ecclesiastical enforcement complemented imperial measures through the council's anathemas, which pronounced eternal condemnation on Monothelite leaders like Patriarchs Sergius, Pyrrhus, Paul, and Peter of Constantinople, as well as Pope Honorius I, thereby justifying their posthumous exclusion from liturgical commemoration and barring followers from sacraments. These anathemas, integrated into the council's horos (definition), empowered orthodox bishops to excommunicate dissenters, fostering self-policing within the church hierarchy; for instance, subsequent synods under Patriarch George I of Constantinople in 682 applied them to purge Monothelite remnants. In practice, this dual mechanismcivil coercion and spiritual sanctiondeterred public advocacy of condemned doctrines, though underground persistence occurred in regions like Syria until further imperial campaigns under Constantine's successors.
In the West, enforcement relied less on imperial reach and more on papal ratification; Pope Leo II, succeeding Agatho in 682, translated and confirmed the decrees by 683, incorporating them into Roman liturgy and anathematizing Honorius with heightened emphasis on his negligence, which extended the council's condemnations to Latin churches via episcopal subscriptions and synodal affirmations. This papal mechanism ensured doctrinal uniformity without direct Byzantine enforcement, though delays arose from communication lags and local resistance, highlighting the limits of centralized imperial authority beyond eastern territories. Overall, these intertwined imperial and ecclesiastical tools marked a shift from prior tolerance under Heraclius' Ecthesis and Constans II's Typos, prioritizing doctrinal rigor over political compromise.

Immediate Aftermath

Imperial Ratification and Eastern Acceptance

The Third Council of Constantinople concluded its eighteenth and final session on September 16, 681, after affirming dyothelitism and issuing anathemas against Monothelite proponents. Emperor Constantine IV, who had convened the council to resolve the Christological controversy amid imperial shifts away from his predecessor Constans II's support for Monothelitism, promptly ratified its doctrinal definitions and canons through an imperial edict. This edict ordered the promulgation of the decrees across the Byzantine Empire, enforcing adherence and marking official state endorsement of the council's rejection of single-will and single-energy doctrines in Christ.
The ratification facilitated rapid acceptance in Eastern ecclesiastical circles, as the emperor's authority compelled compliance among Byzantine bishops and patriarchs. Patriarch George I of Constantinople, who had participated and subscribed to the acts, integrated the council's teachings into local synodal practices, while deposed Monothelites like Patriarch Macarius of Antioch were replaced with dyothelite successors, such as Theophanes, ensuring doctrinal uniformity in key sees. Resistance from lingering Monothelite sympathizers in regions like Armenia and Syria waned under imperial pressure, solidifying the council's authority without significant organized opposition in the core Eastern patriarchates.
By the late seventh century, the Eastern Orthodox tradition fully recognized the council as the sixth ecumenical assembly, embedding its dyenergist Christology into liturgical and theological standards, as evidenced in subsequent Byzantine synods and patristic commentaries that upheld its definitions against imperial expediency. This acceptance contrasted with prior hesitations under emperors favoring compromise formulas like the Ecthesis and Typus, highlighting Constantine IV's causal role in prioritizing empirical orthodoxy over political unity with Monophysite fringes.

Western Reception and Papal Confirmation

Pope Agatho actively supported the council by convening a synod in Rome in 680, which produced a dogmatic letter outlining the Western rejection of Monothelitism and affirming dyothelitism, sent with papal legates to Constantinople. The legates arrived in late 680 and participated in the proceedings, insisting on adherence to Agatho's doctrine, which the council adopted in its eighth session on March 7, 681, leading to the condemnation of Monothelite leaders. 
Following Agatho's death in January 681, his successor Pope Leo II (r. 682683) received the council's acts, had them translated into Latin, and formally confirmed them in 682, anathematizing the Monothelites and expressly including Pope Honorius I for his negligence in failing to denounce the heresy promptly rather than for actively teaching it. In letters to Emperor Constantine IV and King Ervig of the Visigoths, Leo II praised the council's orthodoxy and emphasized Rome's role in preserving apostolic faith, ensuring its integration into Western liturgy and doctrine.
The Western Church, including African and Spanish bishops, accepted the council's definitions without significant opposition, as they aligned with longstanding Latin Christological traditions against imperial-backed compromises in the East. Leo II's confirmation mitigated potential scandal over Honorius' posthumous anathema by framing it as a failure of vigilance, preserving papal prestige while endorsing the council's dyothelite affirmations. This reception solidified the council's ecumenical status in the West, influencing subsequent Roman synods and reinforcing ecclesiastical autonomy against Byzantine caesaropapism.

Long-Term Legacy

Theological Influence on Orthodoxy

The Third Council of Constantinople (680681) decisively shaped Eastern Orthodox Christology by affirming dyothelitism, the doctrine that Christ possesses two natural willsone divine and one humancorresponding to his two natures, united without confusion, change, division, or separation. This pronouncement rejected monothelitism, which posited a single will in Christ, thereby completing the Chalcedonian framework (451) by specifying the volitional and operational integrity of the Incarnation. The council's definition stated: "in these two natures there is one Christ, Son, Lord, only-begotten... operating through two natural operations, without division, without change, without partition, without separation," ensuring the human will's free submission to the divine without diminishment of either.
This theological achievement profoundly influenced Orthodox soteriology, emphasizing that Christ's full humanity, including a distinct human will, enables genuine obedience and redemption of human nature. Orthodox tradition views the council's dyothelite orthodoxy as essential for theosis, wherein believers participate in divine life through union with Christ's deified humanity, preserved intact by two wills acting in harmony. The affirmation countered imperial compromises under Heraclius and Constans II, reinforcing scriptural exegesissuch as Christ's prayer in Gethsemane ("not my will, but yours be done")as evidence of distinct yet cooperative wills.
In subsequent Orthodox dogma, the council's canons integrated into liturgical confessions and patristic synthesis, notably influencing Maximus the Confessor's legacy, whose writings defended dyothelitism against monoenergism. Eastern Orthodoxy commemorates the council's 174 fathers on January 23 (Old Style), underscoring its enduring role in safeguarding Trinitarian and Christological precision against post-Chalcedonian heresies. The doctrine remains a non-negotiable boundary in ecumenical dialogues, distinguishing Orthodox fidelity to conciliar definitions from Western developments like scholastic refinements.

Role in Resisting Political Expediency

The emperors Heraclius (r. 610641) and Constans II (r. 641668) advanced Monothelitism through edicts such as the Ekthesis of 638 and the Typos of 648, respectively, as a pragmatic concession to Monophysite dissidents in Egypt, Syria, and Armenia, aiming to consolidate imperial loyalty during defeats against Persian and subsequent Arab invasions that cost the empire vast territories by 661. These measures prioritized short-term political cohesion over precise Christological orthodoxy, enforcing a single divine-human will in Christ to bridge Chalcedonian and non-Chalcedonian factions, but they provoked schism and persecution of dyothelite opponents, including the torture and exile of figures like Pope Martin I in 654 and St. Maximus the Confessor, who in 658 was tried in Constantinople for rejecting the Typos and upholding two wills as essential to Christ's full humanity and divinity.
The Third Council of Constantinople, convened by Constantine IV (r. 668685) on November 7, 680, marked a decisive ecclesiastical repudiation of this expediency, as 174 bishops, guided by Pope Agatho's doctrinal letter from a 680 Roman synod, condemned Monothelitism in its eighth session on March 7, 681, affirming Christ's two natural willsdivine and humanwithout confusion or division, in line with the Council of Chalcedon (451). Despite the emperor's initial presidency over eleven sessions, the council operated with autonomy, rejecting imperial precedents like the Ekthesis and Typos by anathematizing promoters such as Patriarchs Sergius I and Pyrrhus, and theologians Theodore of Pharan and Macarius of Antioch, thereby vindicating prior resisters like Maximus (d. 662) and establishing that doctrinal truth, not state-engineered compromise, undergirded authentic unity.
This stance underscored the council's prioritization of metaphysical accuracyrooted in scriptural and patristic exegesis of Christ's temptations, prayers, and obedienceover geopolitical imperatives, as the final definition on September 16, 681, prohibited further innovations and was ratified empire-wide only after papal confirmation by Leo II in 682, signaling the limits of caesaro-papism in resolving theological disputes. The outcome halted the cycle of compromise-driven heresies, reinforcing that ecclesiastical councils derive authority from apostolic tradition rather than imperial fiat, even as Constantine IV's support facilitated the gathering amid ongoing Arab threats.

Controversies and Scholarly Debates

Interpretation of Honorius' Condemnation

The Third Council of Constantinople, in its thirteenth session on March 28, 681, issued an anathema against Pope Honorius I (r. 625638), declaring: "We define that there shall be expelled from the holy Church of God and anathematized Honorius who was some time Pope of Old Rome, because of having in writing followed and confirmed the impious doctrine of the heretics." This condemnation grouped Honorius with Eastern Monothelite leaders like Patriarch Sergius I of Constantinople (r. 610638), without accusing him of originating the doctrine but of abetting it through correspondence.
Honorius's relevant letters, dated to 634 and 635, responded to Sergius's query on Christ's wills amid rising Monothelite controversy; he advised avoiding speculation on "one or two wills or operations" to prevent scandal, while endorsing patristic language of "one will of our Lord Jesus Christ" and urging unity without explicit condemnation of the heresy. These writings, while affirming orthodox elements like Christ's full humanity, employed phrasing that Monothelites later exploited to imply a single divine-human will, contributing to doctrinal confusion in the East for decades.
Catholic interpretations, drawing on Vatican I's 1870 definition of papal infallibility (limited to ex cathedra pronouncements on faith or morals intended as binding on the universal Church), maintain that Honorius's private epistles fell outside this scope, representing personal theological error or pastoral negligence rather than definitive teaching. Pope Leo II's 682 confirmation of the council emphasized Honorius's failure to "extinguish the flame of heresy" but "fomented" it by negligence, aligning the anathema with complicity over formal heresy. This view holds that the council's action, ratified by Leo, preserved ecclesiastical authority to correct papal shortcomings without contradicting later infallibility doctrines, as Honorius neither defined Monothelitism nor bound the Church to it.
Eastern Orthodox scholars and critics of Roman primacy interpret the condemnation as evidence that popes, like other bishops, remain fallible and subject to conciliar judgment, undermining claims of universal jurisdictional supremacy or personal impeccability in doctrine. They argue the council's explicit pairing of Honorius with heresiarchs demonstrates his effective promotion of error, regardless of intent, and note that subsequent popes like Leo II upheld the anathema without qualification in Eastern translations.
Among historians, debate persists on Honorius's culpability: some, analyzing his correspondence against patristic precedents, view him as inadvertently aiding heresy through ambiguous irenicism amid Byzantine imperial pressures, while others contend the council overstated his role to legitimize its dyothelite (two-wills) affirmation against prior papal inaction. Empirical review of the acts reveals no evidence Honorius taught Monothelitism as dogma, but his silence enabled its entrenchment until Agatho's 680 letter decisively rejected it, influencing the council's two-natures, two-wills decree. Modern ecumenical dialogues often frame the episode as a caution against hierarchical overreach, emphasizing shared conciliar mechanisms over individual authority.

Imperial Authority versus Ecclesiastical Autonomy

The Third Council of Constantinople (680681), convened by Emperor Constantine IV on November 7, 680, exemplified the Byzantine practice of imperial involvement in ecclesiastical affairs, as the emperor personally summoned bishops from across the empire and presided over the first eleven of its eighteen sessions held in the imperial palace. Constantine IV initiated the assembly following correspondence with Pope Agatho, seeking to resolve the Monothelite controversy that had strained relations with the Western Church, and he set the agenda while promising restraint from exerting authority over matters of faith during negotiations with Rome. This active role underscored the emperor's position as temporal head of the Church, a dynamic often characterized in historical analyses as caesaropapism, whereby the sovereign enforced doctrinal unity and provided logistical support, including the issuance of an edict on September 10, 680, to Patriarch George of Constantinople ordering the convocation.
Despite this imperial oversight, the council's proceedings revealed boundaries to monarchical control, as doctrinal deliberations in later sessions were conducted by the 174 participating bishops under the influence of papal legates, who presented Agatho's orthodox confession of faithaffirming two wills in Christwhich the assembly adopted verbatim in its eighth session as the basis for rejecting Monothelitism. The condemnation of this heresy, previously promoted through imperial edicts like the Ecthesis of Heraclius (638) and the Typos of Constans II (648) to foster compromise with non-Chalcedonians, demonstrated ecclesiastical autonomy in theological definition, overriding prior state-backed compromises for political expediency. Constantine IV's subscription to the council's synodal definition on September 16, 681, and his subsequent promulgation of its decrees via imperial edict across the empire, reflected deference to the bishops' consensus rather than dictation of its content, though enforcement remained a state prerogative.
Scholarly assessments highlight this interplay as a pivotal tension in Byzantine ecclesiology: while the emperor's convocation and presidency affirmed his role as guarantor of orthodoxy, the council's alignment with Roman primacyrequiring Pope Leo II's approval for full acceptanceand its anathematization of figures like former Patriarch Sergius I for doctrinal error illustrated the Church's capacity to correct imperial precedents without direct coercion. This dynamic did not eliminate caesaropapism but illustrated its limits, as Constantine IV recanted any residual alignment with Monothelite views to prioritize religious unity, enabling the council's decisions to bind the faithful independently of monarchical whim. Subsequent enforcement, including suppression of Monothelite texts, blended state power with conciliar authority, yet the event is viewed by historians as a triumph of episcopal consensus over unchecked political theology.

Modern Assessments and Ecumenical Implications

Modern historians and theologians assess the Third Council of Constantinople (680681) as the definitive ecclesiastical response to Monothelitism, a doctrine that posited a single will in Christ despite affirming two natures, thereby resolving a seventh-century Christological crisis that threatened to undermine the full humanity of the incarnate Word. By canonically defining dyothelitismtwo natural wills and energies operating in harmony without conflictthe council preserved the Chalcedonian framework (451) against theological dilutions that risked portraying Christ's human will as subsumed or inactive. This resolution is credited with halting imperial-backed compromises, as Emperor Constantine IV, initially tolerant of Monothelite formulas for unity with non-Chalcedonians, ultimately endorsed the council's orthodox decrees on September 9, 681, marking a pivot from political expediency to doctrinal fidelity.
Scholarly historiography emphasizes the council's methodological rigor, including its extensive review of patristic texts and prior conciliar acts across eighteen sessions, which fortified its authority against charges of novelty. Recent studies, including those in analytic theology, have highlighted overlooked Trinitarian implications in the council's florilegia and definitions, such as clarifications on the distinct yet cooperative operations of divine and human principles in Christ, extending its relevance beyond seventh-century polemics to broader pneumatological and soteriological questions. These assessments counter earlier views that marginalized the council as a mere epilogue to Chalcedon, instead portraying it as a bulwark against recurring temptations to harmonize wills at the expense of ontological precision.
Ecumenically, the council's universal reception by Eastern Orthodox and Roman Catholic traditionsratified by Pope Leo II in 682underscores a shared commitment to dyothelite Christology as non-negotiable, informing post-Vatican II dialogues and Joint International Commission efforts since 1979 to affirm common patristic foundations. Its rejection of Monothelitism as a unity ploy serves as a cautionary model against modern ecumenical overtures that prioritize institutional convergence over precise doctrinal congruence, particularly in contexts involving Oriental Orthodox or Protestant traditions wary of conciliar absolutism. The council's acts, preserved in Greek and Latin, continue to feature in Orthodox liturgical commemorations on January 15 and Catholic references to conciliar infallibility, fostering incremental alignment on the inseparability of Christ's wills as essential to salvation history, though interpretive divergences persist regarding the role of papal primacy in its proceedings.
