American Football Conference
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The American Football Conference (AFC) is one of the two conferences in the National Football League (NFL), the highest level of professional American football in the United States, comprising 16 teams organized into four geographic divisions: East, North, South, and West. The conference was formed in 1970 through the merger of the NFL and the rival American Football League (AFL), which had been competing since 1960 and agreed to combine operations in 1966 to create a unified 26-team league under the NFL banner, with the AFC absorbing all ten AFL franchises plus three established NFL teams (Baltimore Colts, Cleveland Browns, and Pittsburgh Steelers) to balance the structure against the National Football Conference (NFC).
The AFC's current teams include the Buffalo Bills, Miami Dolphins, New England Patriots, and New York Jets in the East Division; the Baltimore Ravens, Cincinnati Bengals, Cleveland Browns, and Pittsburgh Steelers in the North; the Houston Texans, Indianapolis Colts, Jacksonville Jaguars, and Tennessee Titans in the South; and the Denver Broncos, Kansas City Chiefs, Las Vegas Raiders, and Los Angeles Chargers in the West. During the regular season, each AFC team plays 17 games, primarily against divisional rivals and other conference opponents, with scheduling designed to promote competitive balance and regional matchups. Seven teams qualify for the AFC playoffs: the four division winners and three wild-card teams with the best records. The team with the best record (#1 seed) receives a first-round bye in the wild-card round, while the other six teams compete in that round, culminating in the AFC Championship Game; the victor advances to the Super Bowl to face the NFC champion for the NFL title.
Since its inception, the AFC has been a powerhouse in professional football, with its teams securing 28 Super Bowl victories as of the 2024 season, highlighted by dynasties like the New England Patriots under Tom Brady and Bill Belichick, who won six AFC titles and six Super Bowls between 2001 and 2018. The conference's rivalries, such as the Steelers-Ravens in the North or Chiefs-Raiders in the West, have defined eras of the sport, while expansions like the addition of the Houston Texans in 2002 have grown the AFC to its current 16-team format.

History

Formation and AFL-NFL Merger

The American Football League (AFL) was founded on August 22, 1959, by a group of investors led by Lamar Hunt, who sought to create a rival professional football league to the established National Football League (NFL) after being denied an expansion franchise in the older circuit. The league's inaugural meeting occurred on August 14, 1959, in Chicago, where initial franchises were awarded to representatives from Dallas (Lamar Hunt), Denver (Bob Howsam), Houston (K.S. "Bud" Adams), and Los Angeles (Barron Hilton). Additional teams were added shortly thereafter, including Minneapolis (Max Winter and Bill Boyer, later relocating to Oakland as the Raiders), New York City (Harry Wismer), Buffalo (Ralph Wilson on October 28, 1959), and Boston (William H. Sullivan on November 22, 1959), forming the original eight-team league that began play in 1960. These charter franchisesthe Boston Patriots, Buffalo Bills, Dallas Texans (later Kansas City Chiefs), Denver Broncos, Houston Oilers (later Titans), Los Angeles Chargers, New York Titans (later Jets), and Oakland Raidersemphasized innovative strategies, such as signing college stars and securing a television contract with ABC to build national visibility.
The AFL-NFL merger was first announced on June 8, 1966, by NFL Commissioner Pete Rozelle and AFL representatives, establishing a unified league structure effective for the 1970 season, though formal integration occurred after congressional approval of an antitrust exemption on January 7, 1970. Under the agreement, the ten AFL teams from 1969 formed the core of the new American Football Conference (AFC), joined by three NFL teamsthe Baltimore Colts, Cleveland Browns, and Pittsburgh Steelersto create a balanced 13-team conference, while the remaining NFL teams constituted the National Football Conference (NFC). This realignment ensured competitive parity, with the Colts, Browns, and Steelers selected due to their geographic proximity to AFL markets and to avoid divisional imbalances. The merger process included transitional measures, such as the unification of player drafts beginning in 1967, which ended bidding wars and stabilized talent acquisition across both leagues.
Key transition events marked the merger's implementation, including the first fully unified regular season in 1970 and the inaugural true AFC-NFC matchup in Super Bowl V on January 17, 1971, where the AFC's Baltimore Colts defeated the NFC's Dallas Cowboys 16-13 on a last-second field goal by Jim O'Brien. The rationale for the merger stemmed from intense competition between the leagues, which had driven up player salaries through rival bidding and fragmented television audiences, with the AFL securing a deal with ABC and the NFL with CBS, limiting shared revenue opportunities. Additionally, the NFL faced potential antitrust lawsuits from the AFL over territorial and broadcasting disputes, prompting the merger as a means to consolidate operations, pool TV revenues, and avert legal challenges under U.S. antitrust laws. This unification transformed professional football into a more cohesive entity, enhancing its commercial viability without ongoing rivalry.

Expansion, Realignments, and Key Events

Following the AFL-NFL merger, the league expanded in 1976 by adding the Tampa Bay Buccaneers and Seattle Seahawks, increasing the total number of teams to 28 and aiming to balance competition across conferences. The Buccaneers were initially placed in the NFC West, while the Seahawks joined the AFC West, with both teams participating in an expansion draft that allowed them to select players from existing rosters to build their inaugural squads. This addition helped distribute talent more evenly, though both expansion teams struggled early, combining for two wins in their debut season.
In the mid-1990s, the NFL further grew by awarding expansion franchises to the Carolina Panthers and Jacksonville Jaguars in 1995, bringing the league to 30 teams and prompting a realignment to accommodate the new entrants. The Jaguars were assigned to the AFC Central Division, while the Panthers joined the NFC West, with both teams selecting players in a special expansion draft to stock their rosters. This expansion enhanced geographic balance in the AFC, particularly in the South, and set the stage for competitive parity as the Jaguars quickly emerged as contenders, reaching the AFC Championship Game in 1999.
Key relocations reshaped the AFC landscape during this period. In 1997, the Houston Oilers, an original AFL franchise, relocated to Tennessee after owner Bud Adams sought a new stadium, playing temporarily as the Tennessee Oilers in Memphis before moving to Nashville and rebranding as the Titans in 1999. Similarly, the Cleveland Browns faced deactivation in 1996 when owner Art Modell moved the team to Baltimore, where it became the Ravens; however, the NFL reactivated the Browns as an expansion team in 1999, preserving the franchise's history and name in Cleveland while awarding the Ravens their own identity. These moves temporarily increased the AFC to a 16-team structure, which was balanced in 2002 through the addition of the Texans and the Seahawks' shift to the NFC, while highlighting the league's efforts to retain fan loyalty through reactivation and rebranding.
The turn of the century brought further realignments to optimize divisional balance. In 2002, following the addition of the Houston Texanswhich expanded the AFC South to four teamsthe Seattle Seahawks shifted from the AFC West to the NFC West, creating eight divisions of four teams each across both conferences for improved scheduling and regional rivalries. This adjustment, unanimously approved by NFL owners, addressed geographic imbalances, such as the Cardinals' isolation in the NFC East, and fostered more intraconference competition.
Later relocations continued to influence AFC alignments. In 2017, the Chargers relocated from San Diego to Los Angeles, where they shared SoFi Stadium with the Rams starting in 2020. In 2020, the Raiders moved from Oakland to Las Vegas, playing home games at Allegiant Stadium.
Significant milestones also influenced AFC dynamics. The introduction of the NFL salary cap in 1994, set at $34.6 million per team, promoted parity by limiting spending on player salaries after costs had reached 67% of league revenues in 1993, with the cap equivalent to 64% of designated gross revenues. In 2021, the regular season expanded from 16 to 17 games, approved by owners to boost revenue and global appeal, with each AFC team playing nine home games in even-numbered years to balance the odd total. This change increased physical demands but aligned with the conference's growth, as teams adapted through enhanced player protections in the collective bargaining agreement.
External developments indirectly affected AFC balance. The Los Angeles Rams' return to the NFC in 2016, after 21 years in St. Louis, revitalized the West Coast market and strengthened the NFC's overall competitiveness, prompting the AFC to focus on internal parity amid league-wide revenue sharing. As of the mid-2020s, discussions around potential NFL expansions to 34 teams have gained traction, with cities like San Antonio and Portland cited as viable candidates due to market size and infrastructure, though no concrete AFC additions have been approved.

Organizational Structure

Divisions

The American Football Conference (AFC) is structured into four divisionsEast, North, South, and Westeach comprising four teams, a format established during the NFL's 2002 realignment to accommodate the league's expansion to 32 teams with the addition of the Houston Texans. This setup promotes competitive balance by ensuring intra-division rivals play each other twice per season, fostering longstanding matchups while distributing travel demands more evenly across the conference.
The divisions are primarily organized on a geographic basis to minimize travel and enhance regional rivalries, with the East Division centered in the Northeast, the North Division spanning the Great Lakes and Midwest regions, the South Division covering the Southeast and Gulf Coast areas, and the West Division encompassing the Rocky Mountains and Pacific states. This alignment draws historical ties to the original American Football League (AFL) territories, as the AFC predominantly consists of former AFL franchises merged into the NFL in 1970, with a few original NFL teams reassigned for numerical balance between conferences.
Key operational traits include a rotating schedule of inter-division games, where teams face opponents from the other three AFC divisions on a three-year cycle to ensure comprehensive conference matchups without overemphasizing any single group. Division winners automatically secure the top four seeds in the AFC playoffs, granting them byes in the wild-card round and home-field advantage based on regular-season records, which incentivizes strong divisional performance.
Since the 2002 realignment, the AFC's divisional structure has remained unchanged, though discussions around potential future adjustments have surfaced in connection with league expansion plans, such as adding international or domestic franchises to reach 34 or more teams.

Season Format

Regular Season

The regular season of the American Football Conference (AFC) forms the primary phase of competition within the National Football League (NFL), spanning 18 weeks from early September to early January, during which each of the 16 AFC teams plays 17 games. This expanded format, adopted in 2021, increased the number of games per team from 16 to 17 to enhance competitive balance and global outreach, while maintaining the season's traditional timing to align with fall and winter weather conditions optimal for outdoor play.
AFC teams follow the NFL's standardized scheduling formula to determine their 17 opponents each year, ensuring a mix of intra-conference, inter-conference, and divisional matchups. Each team plays six games against its three division rivalshome and awayfostering intense regional rivalries. Additionally, teams face all four opponents from one rotating intra-conference division (e.g., an AFC East team might play the AFC West in a given year, on a three-year cycle), and all four from one inter-conference division (e.g., NFC South, on a four-year rotation). The remaining three games consist of two intra-conference games against teams from the other two AFC divisions (one from each, based on the previous season's division standings) and one additional inter-conference game against a similarly ranked team from an NFC division not scheduled in the rotation, promoting parity by matching clubs with comparable prior performance.
To allow for rest and recovery, each AFC team receives one bye week during the regular season, scheduled between Weeks 5 and 14 to avoid clustering at the start or end. This single off-week provides strategic preparation time without disrupting the overall 18-week structure, with bye assignments balanced across conferences to minimize scheduling conflicts.
Standings within the AFC are determined by each team's win-loss-tie record, with ties resolved through a series of tiebreaker criteria to establish seeding and divisional champions. For two teams tied in the same division, the primary tiebreaker is head-to-head record; if unresolved, it proceeds to division record, conference record, strength of victory, strength of schedule, point differential in division games, and further metrics like points scored or playoff points differential. For non-divisional ties relevant to wild-card positioning, the process adjusts to prioritize conference records after head-to-head, ensuring the highest-ranked teams advance based on merit across the conference.

Playoffs and Postseason

The American Football Conference (AFC) postseason consists of a single-elimination tournament involving seven teams selected from the conference's 16 clubs, a format established following the league's expansion in 2020. Qualification is determined by regular-season performance, with the four division winners (from the AFC East, North, South, and West) automatically advancing, regardless of their overall records. The remaining three spots are awarded to wild-card teams, which are the non-division-winning clubs with the best records in the conference. These seven teams are then seeded from 1 to 7 based on their regular-season winning percentages, with the top seed going to the division winner with the best record, followed by the other division winners in descending order of performance, and then the wild-card teams ranked by their records. The No. 1 seed receives a first-round bye, while seeds 2 through 7 compete in the Wild Card round.
The playoff bracket is not fully fixed and features re-seeding after the Wild Card round, structured as a single-elimination tournament with home-field advantage granted to the higher-seeded team in each matchup. In the Wild Card round, three games are played: the No. 2 seed hosts the No. 7 seed, the No. 3 seed hosts the No. 6 seed, and the No. 4 seed hosts the No. 5 seed. The winners advance to the Divisional round, where the bracket is re-seeded so the highest remaining seed hosts: the No. 1 seed hosts the lowest-remaining seed from the Wild Card winners, and the other two Wild Card winners play each other with the higher remaining seed hosting. The Divisional round victors then face off in the AFC Championship Game, hosted by the higher remaining seed, to determine the conference's Super Bowl representative. This format, implemented since the 2020 expansion, ensures that only six total games are needed within the conference to crown a champion, emphasizing merit-based progression and competitive balance.
The AFC playoffs typically commence in mid-January, immediately following the 18-week regular season, with the Wild Card round spanning a weekend and subsequent rounds spaced one week apart. For the 2025 season, this includes Wild Card games on January 10-12, 2026, Divisional playoffs on January 17-18, 2026, and the AFC Championship on January 25, 2026. The ultimate prize, the Super Bowl, pits the AFC champion against the NFC champion at a predetermined neutral-site stadium in early FebruarySuper Bowl LX is scheduled for February 8, 2026highlighting the independent nature of each conference's playoffs while culminating in a unified league championship. While the AFC and NFC conduct their postseason tournaments separately, the shared Super Bowl underscores the NFL's structure as two rival conferences competing for supremacy.

Championships and Legacy

AFC Championship Game

The AFC Championship Game originated as the American Football League (AFL) Championship Game, first played on January 1, 1961, when the Houston Oilers defeated the Los Angeles Chargers 2416 at Jeppesen Stadium in Houston, Texas. This annual postseason contest determined the AFL's representative for the league's title game until the AFL-NFL merger took effect in 1970, at which point it was rebranded as the AFC Championship Game. The inaugural AFC version occurred on January 3, 1971, following the 1970 season, with the Baltimore Colts defeating the Oakland Raiders 2717 at Memorial Stadium in Baltimore to advance to Super Bowl V. Since then, the game has served as the AFC's culminating playoff event each January, pitting the winners of the conference's divisional playoffs against each other to decide the Super Bowl participant.
The game's format has evolved significantly since its inception. Early AFL championships and the initial post-merger AFC games were held at neutral sites, often selected in advance without regard to regular-season performance. This changed in 1975, when the NFL introduced a seeded playoff system that awarded home-field advantage to the higher-seeded team throughout the postseason, including the conference championship, based on regular-season records. Broadcasters have maintained a tradition of extended pregame coverage for the event, typically featuring a two-hour show to analyze matchups and build anticipation, as seen in CBS's format for the 2023 season game.
Venues for the AFC Championship Game rotate based on the host team's home stadium, with the higher seed's field hosting since 1975, leading to contests in prominent NFL facilities across the conference. Recent examples include the 2023 season game at M&T Bank Stadium in Baltimore, where the Kansas City Chiefs defeated the top-seeded Ravens 1710 on January 28, 2024, and the 2024 season matchup at GEHA Field at Arrowhead Stadium in Kansas City, where the Chiefs edged the Buffalo Bills 3229 on January 26, 2025. The Pittsburgh Steelers hold the record for most appearances with 16, underscoring the game's competitive history among perennial contenders.
Often dubbed the "road to the Super Bowl," the AFC Championship Game carries immense cultural significance as a high-stakes gateway to the NFL's ultimate prize, frequently producing dramatic moments that captivate fans. Notable upsets include the 2005 season game on January 22, 2006, when the sixth-seeded Pittsburgh Steelers routed the top-seeded Denver Broncos 3417 at Invesco Field at Mile High, marking the first time a wild-card team advanced to the Super Bowl. Such outcomes highlight the game's unpredictability and its role in shaping NFL narratives.

Super Bowl Success

The American Football Conference has enjoyed considerable success in the Super Bowl since the championship game's inception, with its teams capturing a total of 29 victories as of 2025, compared to the National Football Conference's 30, including the pre-merger American Football League's two wins. This tally reflects the competitive balance between the conferences, with the AFC establishing dominance in certain eras through powerhouse franchises. The first Super Bowl in 1967 saw the NFL's Green Bay Packers defeat the AFL's Kansas City Chiefs 35-10, marking an early setback for the AFC predecessor but setting the stage for future triumphs.
AFC dynasties have defined much of the conference's Super Bowl legacy, particularly the Pittsburgh Steelers' six championships, four of which came during their 1970s dynasty under head coach Chuck Noll, featuring stars like quarterback Terry Bradshaw and running back Franco Harris, who helped secure back-to-back wins in Super Bowls IX and X, followed by victories in XIII and XIV. The New England Patriots matched the Steelers' total with six Super Bowl wins, forming one of the NFL's most dominant dynasties in the 2000s and 2010s led by quarterback Tom Brady and coach Bill Belichick, including three titles in four years from 2001 to 2004 and additional successes in 2014, 2016, and 2018. More recently, the Kansas City Chiefs have emerged as a modern dynasty with four victoriesSuper Bowl IV in the pre-merger era and three in the 2020s (LIV, LVII, and LVIII)driven by quarterback Patrick Mahomes and coach Andy Reid, emphasizing explosive offense and resilience in high-stakes matchups.
The AFC boasts the most Super Bowl appearances by any conference franchise with the Patriots' 11 trips to the big game, underscoring their sustained excellence despite a 6-5 record, while the Steelers follow with eight appearances and a 6-2 mark. Notable near-misses include the Buffalo Bills' four consecutive Super Bowl losses from 1991 to 1994 (XXV to XXVIII), a unique streak that highlights the conference's depth and heartbreak in pivotal moments.
In terms of legacy, AFC players have earned a substantial share of Super Bowl MVP honors, contributing to the conference's 29 total championships and reinforcing its reputation for producing elite performers on the biggest stage; standout examples include Tom Brady's record-tying five MVP awards (all with AFC teams) and Patrick Mahomes' three, which exemplify the individual brilliance that has propelled AFC squads to victory. These achievements, from dynastic runs to iconic individual efforts, cement the AFC's enduring impact on the Super Bowl's history.

Branding and Media

Logo and Visual Identity

The American Football Conference (AFC) logo was introduced in 1970 as part of the AFL-NFL merger, establishing a distinct visual identity for the newly formed conference. The original design consists of a bold red capital letter "A" with six red stars arranged in a semicircle above it, drawing inspiration from the American Football League's emblem with six stars to honor its heritage. The red hue represented energy, passion, and the competitive spirit of professional football.
The "A" stands for both "American" and "AFC," evoking national pride and the conference's foundational role in American sports culture. The stars, symmetrically placed, convey balance, unity, and the collective strength of the conference's teams. This logo served as the primary emblem from 1970 to 2009, appearing consistently on official documents, event graphics, and league materials without major alterations during that period.
In 2010, the AFC logo underwent a significant update to align with the conference's 2002 realignment into four divisions, reducing the number of stars from six to four white ones positioned to the right of the red "A." The letter itself was widened with beveled serifs for a more contemporary and stable appearance, enhancing its adaptability for digital and print media while preserving the original's simplicity and recognizability. The four stars now directly represent the AFC East, North, South, and West divisions, underscoring the conference's streamlined organizational evolution.
The updated logo continues to embody the AFC's core symbolism of American heritage and athletic excellence, with its clean lines and patriotic color scheme distinguishing it from the NFC's blue counterpart. It is widely applied across AFC branding, including player helmets, apparel, merchandise, and the Lamar Hunt Trophy awarded to conference champions, ensuring a unified visual presence that reinforces league identity.
While the primary AFC logo maintains strict consistency league-wide, variations appear in division-specific patches worn on team jerseys, such as shield-shaped emblems denoting the AFC East in blue to highlight regional affiliations without altering the overarching conference design.

Television and Broadcasting Coverage

The broadcasting of American Football Conference (AFC) games has been a cornerstone of the NFL's media strategy since the league's inception, with CBS serving as the primary network for AFC regular-season games since 1956. This longstanding partnership was extended in a comprehensive 11-year agreement valued at $110 billion across multiple networks, securing CBS's rights to the AFC Sunday afternoon package through the 2033 season. As of 2025, NBC holds exclusive rights to Sunday Night Football, featuring one primetime AFC or NFC matchup each week, while ESPN and ABC broadcast Monday Night Football, which often includes AFC contests. Amazon Prime Video covers Thursday Night Football, with a significant portion of these games featuring AFC teams due to the conference's scheduling rotations.
Historically, the AFL's games were first nationally televised by ABC from 1960 to 1964, marking the league's entry into broadcast prominence before shifting to NBC for the 1965-1969 seasons. Following the 1970 NFL-AFL merger, CBS assumed dominance over AFC coverage, televising the majority of regular-season and playoff games for decades. A pivotal shift occurred in the 1990s when Fox acquired NFC rights from CBS in 1994, leading to a temporary realignment where NBC briefly held AFC packages until CBS regained them in 1998, solidifying the current conference-network alignment that has persisted into 2025.
In the postseason, broadcasting rights to the AFC Championship Game are held by CBS, with NBC occasionally airing select postseason games under the media agreements; CBS aired the 2024 season's matchup between the Buffalo Bills and Kansas City Chiefs, drawing 57.4 million viewers. For the 2025 season's playoffs, CBS is scheduled to broadcast the AFC Championship. Super Bowl coverage rotates among networks with rights deals; Super Bowl LIX, concluding the 2024 season on February 9, 2025, was aired by Fox, while Super Bowl LX for the 2025 season in 2026 will be on NBC.
Internationally, AFC games are accessible via NFL Game Pass on DAZN, which streams all regular-season, playoff, and Super Bowl content to viewers outside the United States, Canada, and China, enhancing global reach since its exclusive DAZN partnership began in 2023.
These television agreements generate the bulk of the NFL's revenue, accounting for over 90% of the league's approximately $20 billion annual income, with AFC broadcasts contributing significantly through high viewership. For instance, Kansas City Chiefs games following Patrick Mahomes' arrival have consistently driven elevated ratings, such as the 2025 Bills-Chiefs matchup averaging 30.8 million viewers on CBS, the second-highest audience of the season and up 25% from the prior year's equivalent game.
