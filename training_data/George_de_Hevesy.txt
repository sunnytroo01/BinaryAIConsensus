George de Hevesy
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 George Charles de Hevesy (Hungarian: Hevesy Gyrgy Kroly; 1 August 1885 5 July 1966) was a Hungarian-born chemist who became a Swedish citizen and received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1943 for his development of the use of isotopes as tracers in the study of chemical processes. Born in Budapest to a prosperous Jewish family, de Hevesy studied chemistry in Budapest, Berlin, and Freiburg, earning his doctorate from the University of Freiburg in 1908. He conducted pioneering research on radioactive tracers, initially attempting to separate isotopes of radium from lead in 19111913, which led to the realization that isotopes could serve as markers for tracing elements in chemical reactions and biological systems. In 1923, while working in Copenhagen under Niels Bohr, he co-discovered the element hafnium with Dirk Coster by identifying it in Norwegian zircon ore.
De Hevesy's career spanned multiple institutions, including early work with Ernest Rutherford in Manchester and later professorships in Freiburg and Stockholm, from which he fled Nazi persecution in 1934 due to his Jewish heritage, relocating first to Denmark and then to Sweden during World War II. His isotope tracer techniques revolutionized fields such as biochemistry, enabling precise tracking of metabolic processes and laying foundational work for nuclear medicine. De Hevesy authored numerous papers and books on radiochemistry, contributing to activation analysis and the separation of stable isotopes, and continued research until his death in Freiburg in 1966.

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Childhood

George de Hevesy, born Gyrgy Bischitz on August 1, 1885, in Budapest, Hungary, came from a prosperous, ennobled family of Hungarian-Jewish origin. He was the fifth of eight children to Louis (Lajos) de Hevesy, a court councillor who served as general manager of a major mining and metallurgical company in northern Hungary, and Eugnie, ne Baroness Schosberger, whose family wealth derived from banking interests. The family's elevated social status, including noble titles granted in recognition of industrial contributions, provided financial security and connections within Hungary's elite circles during the Austro-Hungarian Empire.
Raised in Budapest amid this privileged environment, de Hevesy experienced a childhood shaped by his family's assimilated Jewish heritage, which included conversion to Roman Catholicism. His early education consisted of instruction from private tutors, supplemented by attendance at a Catholic gymnasium, reflecting the era's emphasis on classical and religious schooling for upper-class youth. Biographical accounts note no specific childhood pursuits beyond the general intellectual stimulation afforded by his surroundings, though his later scientific aptitude suggests an environment conducive to curiosity about natural phenomena.

Academic Training and Early Influences

De Hevesy completed his secondary education at the Piarist Gymnasium in Budapest, graduating in 1903 with a focus on mathematics and physics in his final year. He initially studied chemistry at the University of Budapest for one year before transferring to the Technical University of Berlin, where he encountered influential chemists such as Ludwig Gattermann. In 1906, he began his doctoral research under Georg Franz Julius Meyer at the University of Freiburg, earning his Ph.D. in physics in 1908; his thesis examined the solubility of sodium in molten sodium hydroxide, identifying it as the cause of low yields in sodium electrolysis processes.
Following his doctorate, de Hevesy served as a research assistant in Zurich from 1908 to 1910, engaging with advancements in physical chemistry amid the intellectual environment that included figures like Albert Einstein. He then moved to Karlsruhe to work under Fritz Haber at the Technische Hochschule, where he honed skills in chemical separations and high-pressure techniques, though he later reflected on Haber's emphasis on practical applications as somewhat diverging from his own theoretical inclinations. These experiences grounded de Hevesy in rigorous experimental chemistry, emphasizing precise measurement and interdisciplinary approaches.
A pivotal influence came in 1911 when de Hevesy joined Ernest Rutherford's laboratory at the University of Manchester, tasked with separating lead and radium isotopesa challenge that introduced him to radioactivity beyond his chemical training. Rutherford's mentorship, focusing on alpha-ray measurements and atomic structure, redirected de Hevesy's interests toward nuclear phenomena, fostering his eventual innovations in isotopic tracers despite initial frustrations with separation difficulties. This period marked a shift from pure physical chemistry to radiochemistry, influenced by Rutherford's empirical rigor and the era's discoveries in atomic disintegration.

Scientific Career and Discoveries

Collaboration with Rutherford and Early Radioactivity Work

In 1910, George de Hevesy traveled to Manchester, England, to work under Ernest Rutherford, where he sought to familiarize himself with radioactivity measurements and practical radiochemistry. Rutherford's laboratory at the University of Manchester was a hub for pioneering atomic research, including the recent development of the nuclear model of the atom in 1911. Hevesy's efforts during this period focused on the chemical behavior of radium and lead isotopes, bridging chemistry and physics in the study of radioactive decay products.
A key assignment from Rutherford in January 1911 involved attempting to separate radium Da radioactive isotope of lead (Pb), known as a decay product of radiumfrom ordinary stable lead, which proved chemically inseparable due to their identical properties. Despite intensive efforts over more than a year, Hevesy and collaborators, including later work with Friedrich Paneth, failed to achieve separation, confirming the isotopic identity of radium D with lead. This challenge highlighted the limitations of classical chemical methods for distinguishing isotopes and shifted Hevesy's approach toward exploiting radioactivity as a detectable label.
The unsuccessful separation led Hevesy to pioneer the use of radium D as a radioactive indicator, or tracer, for tracking lead in chemical processes, marking an early application of isotopic labeling. In initial experiments, he applied this method to investigate the solubility of sparingly soluble lead compounds, such as lead sulfide and lead chromate, demonstrating precise quantification of minute quantities of lead ions through radiation detection. These studies, building directly on Manchester's radioactivity techniques, yielded results showing solubilities on the order of 10 to 10 moles per liter, far beyond conventional analytical capabilities, and were published in 1913. Hevesy's work under Rutherford thus laid foundational principles for tracer methodology, emphasizing empirical detection over physical isolation.

Discovery of Hafnium

In the early 1920s, Niels Bohr's atomic model predicted that element 72, previously sought among the rare earths, should instead exhibit chemical properties homologous to zirconium due to similar electron configurations, prompting a targeted search in zirconium-rich minerals. George de Hevesy, then at the University of Copenhagen, collaborated with Dutch physicist Dirk Coster to investigate this hypothesis using X-ray spectroscopy, a method Hevesy had refined for elemental identification. They analyzed samples of zircona zirconium silicatefrom a Norwegian locality, expecting the new element to co-occur as an impurity chemically akin to zirconium.
On January 17, 1923, Coster and Hevesy detected distinct X-ray emission lines in the zircon samples that precisely matched the expected spectrum for atomic number 72, confirming the element's presence at concentrations of about 1-5% in the mineral. This identification resolved prior discrepancies, such as Georges Urbain's 1911 claim of discovering element 72 (named celtium) in rare-earth fractions, which proved to be contaminated lutetium rather than the true homolog of zirconium. The discovery validated Bohr's theoretical framework over empirical rare-earth assumptions, as the element's ionic radius and tetravalency mirrored zirconium's, enabling its separation via fractional crystallization of double fluorides.
The element was formally announced in a letter to Nature on January 20, 1923, with the name hafnium (Hf) proposed, derived from Hafnia, the Latin term for Copenhagen, honoring the site of the breakthrough. Hevesy and Coster subsequently isolated hafnium oxide (HfO) in pure form by 1924, determining its atomic weight as 178.4 through chemical analysis, and fully characterized its refractory properties akin to those of zirconium dioxide. This work not only filled a gap in the periodic table but also advanced X-ray spectroscopic techniques for trace element detection, influencing subsequent geochemical and metallurgical applications.

Development of Isotope Tracer Methods

In 1911, while working at the University of Manchester under Ernest Rutherford, George de Hevesy attempted to separate radium D (Pb), an isotope derived from pitchblende, from ordinary lead but failed due to their identical chemical properties. This inseparability led him to conceive of using the radioactive isotope as a detectable marker for lead, forming the foundational idea of isotopic tracers, where isotopes behave chemically like their non-radioactive counterparts but allow tracking via radiation measurement.
By 1912, de Hevesy collaborated with Friedrich Adolf Paneth at the Vienna Radium Institute to apply this concept practically, labeling lead chromate with thorium B (Pb) to study the solubility of lead compounds, marking one of the earliest experimental uses of a radioactive isotope as a tracer. In 1923, he published the first dedicated studies employing isotopes as tracers, including measurements of lead uptake and ion exchange in plants via radiation detection, demonstrating the method's potential for following elemental paths in biological systems. During the 1920s, further refinements enabled self-diffusion studies in solid lead using thorium B and radium D, quantifying atomic mobility in metals.
The technique expanded in the 1930s with the advent of artificial radioisotopes produced by cyclotrons, such as P following the Joliot-Curies' discoveries in 1934, allowing de Hevesy to investigate phosphorus metabolism in biological processes. He also pioneered stable isotope tracers, including deuterium and N, for non-radioactive applications in chemical and biochemical research, broadening the method's versatility beyond radiation detection to mass spectrometry and other analytical tools. These developments established isotope tracers as a precise tool for elucidating chemical exchange rates, diffusion, and metabolic pathways, underpinning advancements in chemistry and biology.

Applications and Recognition

Biological and Medical Uses of Tracers

George de Hevesy pioneered the application of radioactive isotopes as tracers to investigate elemental dynamics in biological systems, allowing precise measurement of absorption, distribution, and exchange without disrupting physiological processes. In 1923, he utilized naturally occurring radioactive lead-210 (then known as thorium-B or radium-D) to track lead uptake in fava bean plants (Vicia faba), demonstrating that roots absorbed the isotope from nutrient solutions and translocated it to leaves and seeds at rates dependent on plant age and environmental factors. This work quantified lead's mobility, revealing accumulation levels up to 10% of the plant's dry weight in older specimens, and established isotopes as inert labels indistinguishable from stable counterparts in metabolic pathways.
Hevesy's tracers extended to animal physiology, where he employed short-lived isotopes like bismuth-214 to measure ion exchange rates in blood and tissues, confirming rapid turnover of heavy metals in mammals. The advent of artificial radioisotopes via cyclotron bombardment enabled more versatile studies; in 1935, Hevesy and Ole Chievitz produced phosphorus-32 by deuteron irradiation of sulfur, using it to elucidate phosphorus metabolism in rats. Their experiments showed that injected P-32 rapidly equilibrated with body phosphates, with skeletal deposition accounting for approximately 70% of the dose within days, and facilitated quantification of bone formation rates through serial measurements of isotope retention.
In medical contexts, Hevesy and Chievitz administered P-32 intravenously to human patients with leukemia in 1935, marking the first therapeutic and diagnostic use of an artificial radioisotope in vivo. This revealed a phosphate turnover half-time of about 90 minutes in plasma, far exceeding normal values, and highlighted accelerated metabolism in malignant cells, providing early insights into disease-specific kinetics. Subsequent applications included tracking sodium-24 in electrolyte balance and iodine-131 in thyroid function, though Hevesy's foundational phosphorus studies underscored tracers' utility for non-invasive diagnostics, influencing treatments like targeted radiotherapy for polycythemia vera. These methods relied on the beta emissions of P-32 (half-life 14.3 days, maximum energy 1.71 MeV) for detection via Geiger counters, ensuring sensitivity without excessive radiation exposure in tracer quantities.

Nobel Prize in Chemistry

The Nobel Prize in Chemistry for 1943 was awarded to George de Hevesy "for his work on the use of isotopes as tracers in the study of chemical processes," recognizing his pioneering development of isotopic tracer techniques that revolutionized the investigation of chemical, biological, and physiological processes. This method, first demonstrated in 1913 when de Hevesy used the naturally radioactive isotope radium D (lead-210) to trace lead's solubility and absorption in biological systems, allowed precise tracking of elements without altering their chemical behavior. His early experiments, conducted in collaboration with others like Friedrich Adolf Paneth, involved marking lead compounds to measure their solubility and uptake in plants, such as determining lead absorption in beans.
Due to World War II disruptions, the prize was formally presented on December 12, 1944, in Stockholm, where de Hevesy had fled after hiding Nobel medals in Denmark. In his Nobel lecture titled "Some Applications of Isotopic Indicators," de Hevesy detailed applications including the use of radioactive phosphorus to study phosphate metabolism in animals and humans, demonstrating rapid bone formation rates and phosphorus turnover in blood. He highlighted how stable isotopes like deuterium and heavy nitrogen enabled non-radioactive tracing, expanding the technique's utility before artificial radioisotopes from cyclotrons became available in the 1930s.
The Nobel Committee emphasized de Hevesy's quantitative precision in measuring minute quantities, such as lead in the human body, and his extension of tracers to biological research, including self-absorption studies of elements like bismuth and thallium. This work laid foundational principles for nuclear medicine and radiochemistry, with tracers proving essential for mapping metabolic pathways and element distributions in living organisms. De Hevesy's contributions earned him the accolade as the father of isotopic tracers, influencing fields from agriculture to medicine long after the award.

World War II and Displacement

Research in Occupied Denmark

Following the German invasion of Denmark on April 9, 1940, George de Hevesy persisted with his investigations into isotopic tracers at the Niels Bohr Institute in Copenhagen, where access to the institute's cyclotron enabled production of short-lived radioisotopes despite wartime shortages. His research emphasized applications in biochemistry, building on pre-war tracer methods to quantify dynamic processes in vivo.
In collaboration with colleagues such as Jacob Ottesen, de Hevesy examined phosphorus metabolism, including the incorporation of radioactive phosphorus-32 into nucleic acids. A key 1943 study detailed the rate of nucleic acid synthesis in rat organs, revealing turnover times on the order of days to weeks and demonstrating tracer specificity in tracking metabolic pathways under controlled dietary conditions. These experiments underscored the precision of isotopic labeling for distinguishing endogenous from exogenous nutrient fluxes, even as occupation imposed logistical hurdles like material rationing and surveillance.
De Hevesy's output during this interval reinforced the foundational principles of radiotracer methodology, contributing directly to his 1943 Nobel Prize recognition for isotope applications in chemical process studies. Operations at the institute faced increasing interference by 1943, prompting his relocation to Sweden later that year, yet the period yielded enduring insights into biological kinetics amid geopolitical duress.

Hiding Nobel Medals and Escape to Sweden

In April 1940, shortly after the German invasion of Denmark on April 9, George de Hevesy collaborated with Niels Bohr at the Institute for Theoretical Physics in Copenhagen to safeguard Nobel Prize medals entrusted to Bohr for safekeeping. The medals belonged to German physicists Max von Laue (awarded 1914 for X-ray diffraction) and James Franck (awarded 1925 for the Franck-Hertz experiment), both of whom had opposed the Nazi regime and faced risks of confiscation under Nazi policies targeting Jewish or anti-Nazi laureates' assets. De Hevesy dissolved the 23-carat gold medals, each weighing approximately 200 grams, in aqua regia (a mixture of nitric and hydrochloric acids), creating a solution of chloroauric acid that rendered the precious metal unrecognizable and irretrievable without specialized recovery. The resulting beaker was stored inconspicuously on a laboratory shelf amid other chemical solutions, evading detection during Nazi inspections of the institute.
The solution remained undisturbed through the occupation, as the Nazis did not suspect its contents despite searching for valuables. In 1945, after Denmark's liberation, de Hevesy returned to the laboratory, identified the intact beaker, and chemically precipitated the gold from the solution. He forwarded the recovered gold to the Nobel Foundation in Sweden, which recast identical replacement medals presented to von Laue and Franck, preserving the originals' historical integrity without loss of material.
By 1943, escalating Nazi control in Denmark, including the deportation of Jews and crackdown on intellectuals following Niels Bohr's escape to Sweden in October, prompted de Hevesyhimself of Jewish ancestryto flee as well. He escaped to neutral Sweden that year, securing a position at the University of Stockholm's Institute of Organic Chemistry, where he resumed research on isotopic tracers amid wartime displacement. This relocation allowed de Hevesy to continue his scientific work uninterrupted by the intensifying occupation, contributing to advancements in radiochemistry until after the war.

Later Career and Personal Life

Post-War Contributions and Retirement

Following the end of World War II in 1945, de Hevesy continued his research on isotopic tracers at Stockholm University, where he had been an associate of the Institute of Research in Organic Chemistry since 1943. His post-war studies focused on the effects of X-rays on nucleic acid formation in tumors and normal organs, as well as iron transport mechanisms in healthy and cancerous organisms, with funding from the Swedish State Research Council and the Wallenberg Foundation. In 1949, he served as Franqui Professor at the University of Ghent, delivering lectures on radiochemical applications. He became a Swedish citizen in 1951 while maintaining his affiliation with Stockholm.
De Hevesy retired from his formal professorship at Stockholm University College in 1961 at age 76 but remained an active scientific associate, contributing to ongoing radiochemistry and nuclear medicine research. During this period, he received the Atoms for Peace Award in 1959 for advancing the peaceful uses of nuclear energy, the Niels Bohr Medal in 1961, and the Rosenberger Medal in 1961.
De Hevesy died on July 5, 1966, at age 80, at the University of Freiburg Medical Center in West Germany. 

Family and Death

De Hevesy married Pia Riis, a Danish woman, in 1924. The couple had one son, Georg, and three daughters: Jenny, Ingrid, and Pia. After World War II, the family settled in Stockholm, Sweden, where De Hevesy continued his research.
De Hevesy died on July 5, 1966, at the age of 80 in Freiburg im Breisgau, West Germany, following several years of suffering from lung cancer. He was treated at Freiburg University's Medical Center prior to his death. His wife Pia survived him, passing away in 1979.

Legacy and Impact

Influence on Nuclear Medicine and Radiochemistry

De Hevesy's pioneering application of radioactive isotopes as tracers fundamentally transformed radiochemistry and laid the groundwork for nuclear medicine. Beginning in 1913, while attempting to separate radium D (210Pb) from lead, he recognized that isotopic indistinguishability precluded chemical separation but enabled tracking via radioactivity, marking an early conceptual use of tracers to monitor lead's behavior in processes. By 1923, collaborating with Dirk Coster, Hevesy conducted the first systematic study using naturally radioactive 212Pb to trace lead absorption and translocation in plants, such as fava beans, revealing root retention and limited mobilitydemonstrating tracers' capacity for non-destructive analysis of dynamic systems. These experiments established radiotracers as precise tools for following elemental pathways, shifting radiochemistry from static separations toward kinetic studies of reactions and ion mobilities.
The advent of artificial radioactivity in 1934 by the Joliot-Curies amplified Hevesy's impact, allowing production of suitable isotopes for biological applications. In 1935, at Niels Bohr's Institute in Copenhagen, Hevesy and Hilde Levi used neutron-irradiated phosphorus-32 to quantify phosphate metabolism in rats, measuring uptake rates and turnover in organs like the liver and kidneyspioneering in vivo metabolic tracing without invasive dissection. This extended to studies of water distribution via heavy water and ion exchanges, introducing isotope dilution methods that quantified unknown concentrations by mixing known radioactive standards, enhancing analytical accuracy in complex matrices. Such techniques influenced nuclear medicine's emergence as a diagnostic and therapeutic discipline, enabling radiopharmaceuticals for imaging organ function and targeting diseases, with tracers now central to procedures like thyroid scans using iodine-131, introduced shortly after his foundational work.
Hevesy's innovations in activation analysis, where neutron bombardment induces measurable radioactivity in elements for trace detection, further advanced radiochemistry's analytical power, achieving sensitivities down to parts per billion and supporting material science applications beyond biology. His emphasis on empirical tracing of causal pathways in living systemsprioritizing detectable signals over assumptionsfostered causal realism in interpreting metabolic data, countering prior reliance on indirect chemical assays prone to artifacts. Collectively, these contributions, recognized in his 1943 Nobel Prize, established isotope-based methods as enduring pillars, influencing modern PET/SPECT imaging and radionuclide therapies while underscoring the primacy of verifiable isotopic signatures in scientific inference.

Enduring Scientific Recognition

De Hevesy's pioneering use of isotopic tracers garnered him election as a fellow to the Royal Society of London in 1939, the Swedish Academy of Sciences, the Gothenburg Academy, and eleven other scientific academies worldwide. He was also recognized as an honorary member of chemical societies in England, France, Germany, Hungary, India, and the United States. These affiliations underscored the broad international esteem for his foundational advancements in radiochemistry and their applications across disciplines.
Post-Nobel accolades further affirmed his stature, including the Copley Medal from the Royal Society in 1949 for investigations into radioactive elements and isotopic tracers. In 1959, he received the Ford Foundation's Atoms for Peace Award Medal, followed in 1961 by the Niels Bohr Medal and the Rosenberger Medal from the University of Chicago.
His enduring influence is evident in eponymous honors, such as the George de Hevesy Award, conferred annually by the Society of Nuclear Medicine and Molecular Imaging (SNMMI) since 1965 to recognize exceptional achievements in nuclear medicine research and development. Similarly, the George Hevesy Medal Award, established as the foremost international prize for excellence in radioanalytical and nuclear chemistry, perpetuates his legacy by honoring ongoing innovations in tracer methodologies and isotopic applications.
