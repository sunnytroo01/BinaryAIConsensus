Iroquoian languages
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Iroquoian languages form a distinct family of indigenous North American languages, primarily spoken historically across the northeastern United States, southeastern Canada, and parts of the southeastern United States, with a division into Northern and Southern branches. The Northern branch includes languages associated with the Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) ConfederacyM Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, and Senecaalong with Tuscarora and the Huronian languages like Wyandot, while the Southern branch is represented solely by Cherokee, which is geographically and somewhat linguistically isolated from its northern relatives. These languages are notable for their polysynthetic structure, allowing complex ideas to be expressed in single words through extensive affixation; a phonological inventory lacking labial consonants; and intricate systems of pronominal prefixes and accentual patterns. Contemporary speaker numbers remain low, with Cherokee boasting the largest population at approximately 11,000 to 22,000 fluent or proficient users, followed by Mohawk with around 3,500, and most other varieties endangered or extinct due to historical assimilation pressures and limited intergenerational transmission. Efforts in language revitalization, including documentation and educational programs, continue among Iroquoian communities to preserve these tongues, which underpin cultural identities tied to longstanding confederacies and traditional knowledge systems.

Classification

Internal Subgroups

The Iroquoian languages comprise two primary branches: Northern Iroquoian and Southern Iroquoian, with the divergence between them estimated at 3,500 to 4,000 years ago based on glottochronological analysis.
Southern Iroquoian consists exclusively of Cherokee, a language with approximately 10,000 speakers as of recent surveys, primarily in eastern Oklahoma and western North Carolina, exhibiting distinct phonological features such as the retention of certain Proto-Iroquoian vowels not preserved in Northern varieties.
Northern Iroquoian includes seven historically attested languages, five of which form the core of the Iroquois (Haudenosaunee) linguistic cluster: Mohawk (about 2,500 speakers), Oneida (fewer than 100 fluent speakers), Onondaga (around 50 speakers), Cayuga (under 100 speakers), and Seneca (fewer than 100 fluent speakers). Tuscarora, with about 100 speakers, and the now-extinct Huron-Wyandot (last fluent speakers in the mid-20th century) complete the branch, with Huron-Wyandot showing closer lexical and morphological ties to the Iroquois cluster than to Tuscarora.
Subdivisions within Northern Iroquoian reflect varying degrees of mutual intelligibility and shared innovations. The Mohawk-Oneida languages form a tight subgroup, sharing vocabulary overlaps exceeding 80% and similar verb prefix paradigms. Cayuga and Seneca constitute another close pair, with innovations like merged pronominal prefixes distinguishing them from Onondaga, which stands somewhat apart but aligns more closely with the Iroquoian proper languages than with Tuscarora or Huron-Wyandot. Tuscarora represents a peripheral subgroup, with evidence of historical contact but distinct phonological shifts, such as the development of fricatives from stops.

Extinct Varieties

Several extinct varieties of Iroquoian languages belonged to the Northern branch, including Laurentian, spoken along the northern shore of the St. Lawrence River in present-day Quebec until its disappearance in the late 16th century due to warfare with Mohawk groups. Documentation of Laurentian is sparse, consisting primarily of a few vocabulary items recorded by French explorers and missionaries in the 1530s1580s, such as words for body parts and numerals, alongside Iroquoian-derived toponyms like Hochelaga.
Susquehannock, spoken in eastern Pennsylvania and adjacent areas, became extinct by the mid-18th century following tribal dispersal amid colonial conflicts and Iroquois raids, with the last speakers assimilated into other groups around 1763. It was closely related to Onondaga and Oneida, as evidenced by a 100-word vocabulary compiled by Swedish missionary Johan Campanius in the 1640s, which shares phonological and lexical features like aspirated stops and kinship terms with those languages.
The Erie language, used by groups south of Lake Erie in Ohio and Pennsylvania, went extinct in the mid-17th century after defeat by the Iroquois Confederacy around 1655, leaving no direct textual records but inferred Iroquoian affiliation from historical accounts of linguistic similarity to Wyandot. Neutral (Attiwandaronk), spoken in southern Ontario, similarly vanished by 1651 due to Iroquois invasions, with limited evidence from Jesuit relations indicating an Iroquoian structure including rhotic sounds retained from Proto-Northern Iroquoian, unlike some modern varieties. Wenro, associated with groups in western New York, became extinct by the early 1630s after absorption by Hurons and Neutrals, known mainly through brief mentions in French missionary documents without surviving vocabulary lists.
In the Southern branch, Nottoway (Cheroenhaka) ceased to be spoken natively by the early 19th century among Virginia tribes, though a vocabulary of over 200 words was recorded in 1820 by John Wood from speaker Edie Turner, confirming its close relation to Tuscarora via shared morphemes for verbs and numerals. These extinctions highlight the vulnerability of small linguistic communities to demographic collapse, with surviving records enabling partial reconstructions of phonological inventories featuring glottalized consonants and polysynthetic morphology typical of the family.

Historical Development

Proto-Iroquoian Origins

Proto-Iroquoian refers to the hypothetical ancestor language reconstructed through comparative methods from the attested Iroquoian languages, including Northern branches like Mohawk and Southern ones like Cherokee. Efforts to reconstruct its phonology, lexicon, and morphology began in the mid-20th century, with early work by Floyd Lounsbury focusing on shared innovations and glottochronological dating. A more comprehensive reconstruction, including systematic sound correspondences and lexical items, was advanced in Charles Julian's 2010 dissertation, which proposed forms for core vocabulary such as numerals and body parts based on regular phonological changes across daughter languages.
Estimates of the time depth for Proto-Iroquoian vary due to methodological differences, but glottochronology applied to lexical retention rates suggests a divergence between Northern and Southern Iroquoian around 3,500 to 3,800 years before present, placing the proto-language emergence circa 15001800 BCE. More recent quantitative analyses of lexical phylogenetics, incorporating borrowing filters and tree-building algorithms, date the proto-language to approximately 2624 BCE, with confidence intervals reflecting uncertainties in cognate identification and mutation rates. These estimates align with archaeological transitions from Late Archaic foraging economies to Early Woodland semi-sedentary patterns, where linguistic divergence likely accompanied subsistence shifts like increased nut mast exploitation in upland forests. 
The proposed homeland for Proto-Iroquoian speakers centers on the Appalachian highlands, particularly the Finger Lakes region of west-central New York, supported by linguistic divergence models that minimize migration distances and correlate with genetic data indicating deep continuity in regional populations. Archaeological evidence from sites like the Laurentian tradition shows material culture consistent with early Iroquoian traits, such as broad-bladed tools, though direct links remain inferential due to the perishable nature of Iroquoian longhouse architecture and maize horticulture, which postdate the proto-stage. Cultural reconstructions from lexical data reveal a proto-vocabulary reflecting forested environments with terms for deciduous trees, game animals, and seasonal activities, underscoring ecological adaptation over time rather than external impositions. Discrepancies in homeland models arise from assumptions about borrowing versus inheritance, with conservative reconstructions privileging shared retentions over potential Algonquian loans that could inflate perceived antiquity.

Divergence and Prehistoric Migrations

The divergence of Proto-Iroquoian into its primary branches is estimated through glottochronological methods and lexical comparisons to have occurred between approximately 3800 and 3500 years before present (BP), corresponding to roughly 18001500 BCE, during the transition from the Late Archaic to Early Woodland periods in the Appalachian highlands. This split separated the Southern branch, primarily represented by Cherokee, from the Northern branch encompassing languages such as Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, and Tuscarora, with some analyses placing the Northern-Southern divide closer to 2500 years ago based on shared vocabulary retention rates. The divergence likely coincided with subsistence shifts from reliance on upland mast forests (acorns, hickory nuts) and hunting-gathering to early adoption of horticulture, including maize cultivation, which supported population expansion and linguistic differentiation across diverse ecological zones.
Within the Northern Iroquoian subgroup, further splits occurred later, with Proto-Onondaga diverging from Proto-Seneca-Cayuga around 822 238 years ago (circa AD 11001400), followed by rapid diversification of the Five Nations languages over a roughly 200-year span amid village clustering and intergroup interactions. Huronian languages, another Northern clade, saw Wendat and Wyandot separate approximately 647 188 years ago (AD 11651541), aligning with archaeological evidence of dispersed settlements in the Great Lakes region. These internal divergences reflect not gradual in-place evolution but punctuated changes tied to demographic pressures, resource competition, and technological adaptations like intensified maize agriculture, which glottochronology correlates with accelerated lexical drift.
Prehistoric migrations played a central role in Iroquoian dispersal, with linguistic reconstructions supporting an origin in the southern Appalachian highlands or Allegheny Plateau, from which Northern Iroquoian speakers moved northward into present-day New York, Ontario, and Quebec between 1000 and 1500 years ago, encountering Algonquian and Siouan groups en route. This migration model challenges in situ development theories by positing small, homogeneous Proto-Northern-Iroquoian groups expanding via kinship networks and conflict-driven relocations, introducing matrilocality and horticultural practices that transformed local subsistence economies. Cherokee populations, conversely, maintained a more southerly trajectory in the Southeast, with archaeological sites indicating continuity from 4000 BP but linguistic isolation accelerating divergence through geographic barriers like the Appalachian Mountains. These movements, inferred from cognate distributions and site patterns, underscore causal links between environmental carrying capacity, agricultural intensification, and language family branching, though precise routes remain debated due to limited pre-contact genetic data.

Linguistic Structure

Phonological Inventory

The phonological systems of Iroquoian languages are characterized by small consonant inventories, typically 610 phonemes, with a near-total absence of labial obstruents such as /p/ or /b/, and nondistinctive voicing contrasts among obstruents. Proto-Iroquoian is reconstructed with voiceless stops *t and *k, affricate *ts, fricatives *s and *h, nasal *n, liquid *r (developing into /l/ or // in daughter languages), approximants *w and *y, and glottal stop *, yielding a total of nine consonants; labial nasals like *m are unattested natively and appear only marginally in loanwords across the family. Consonant clusters are permitted word-finally and intervocalically, often involving sequences like /tsn/ or /hkw/, but initial clusters are rare.
Vowel systems in Proto-Iroquoian comprised five oral vowels*i, *e, *a, *o, * (a central unrounded schwa)with corresponding nasal counterparts *, *, *, *, *, though nasalization was phonemic only for *e and *o in the proto-language, expanding in descendants. Northern Iroquoian languages (e.g., Mohawk, Seneca) retain this five-vowel oral system plus three to five nasals (commonly //, //, //, with // and // marginal or allophonic), and exhibit vowel length contrasts in some environments, but lack tone. Southern Iroquoian, represented by Cherokee, innovates a pitch-accent system with high, low, rising, and falling tones on vowels, deriving from lost consonants, while maintaining a similar oral inventory (/i, e, a, o, /) but with fewer distinct nasals (/, /) and no phonemic length distinction.

Morphology and Syntax

Iroquoian languages are characterized by polysynthetic morphology, in which verbs typically incorporate pronominal affixes, lexical roots, and derivational elements to encode entire propositions, often rendering independent nouns and pronouns optional or absent in clauses. Verbs form the morphological core, featuring prefixes that mark agent and patient arguments with distinctions for person, number, gender, and inclusivity; for instance, Northern Iroquoian pronominal systems include up to 14 distinct agent prefixes and 10 patient prefixes, reflecting hierarchical access to agency. Suffixes convey aspect (e.g., punctual, habitual, stative), tense (future, remote past in some varieties), and modality, with verb roots often specifying semantic roles like handling, motion, or instrument use, allowing for classificatory verbs in Cherokee that differentiate object shapes or consistencies.
Noun morphology is simpler, primarily involving possessive prefixes and, in some cases, suffixes for location or state; kinship terms and inalienably possessed nouns (e.g., body parts) integrate directly into verbs via noun incorporation, a process where noun roots precede the verb stem to form compact expressions like "house-build" for "he is building a house," prevalent in Northern branches but less systematic in Cherokee. Particles function as adverbs, conjunctions, or discourse markers without inflection, while multicategorization allows certain forms to shift between noun and verb roles based on context, as in Cayuga where "road" can nominalize a motion verb root.
Syntactically, Iroquoian languages exhibit head-marking dependency, with verbs governing argument agreement rather than case marking on dependents, and clauses show flexible word order driven by pragmatics rather than rigid templates; no dominant subject-verb-object sequence exists, though verb-initial orders (e.g., VSO) predominate in discourse, and reconstruction suggests a proto-shift from SOV to SVO under areal influences. Subordination occurs via verb suffixes or incorporated complementizers, minimizing embedded clauses, while coordination relies on juxtaposed verbs or particles influenced by contact with European languages in modern varieties. This structure yields concise sentences, often comprising a single complex verb equivalent to an English multi-word clause.

Documentation and Writing

Early European Records

The earliest European documentation of an Iroquoian language consists of word lists compiled by French explorer Jacques Cartier during his voyages to the Gulf of St. Lawrence in 1534 and 1535, recording approximately 200 terms from the speech of the St. Lawrence Iroquoians (also known as Laurentian) at sites including Stadacona (near modern Quebec City) and Hochelaga (near Montreal). These records, derived partly from interactions with local inhabitants and captives brought to France, represent the first attested North American indigenous vocabulary in a European source, though the orthography reflects French approximations and lacks grammatical analysis. The St. Lawrence Iroquoian language, now extinct following the dispersal of its speakers in the late 16th century, shows phonological and lexical affinities to other Northern Iroquoian tongues, aiding later reconstructions despite transcription inconsistencies.
Systematic linguistic documentation expanded in the 17th century through Jesuit missionaries in New France, who targeted the Wendat (Huron) confederacy. Initial efforts by Rcollet friars from 1615 provided rudimentary instruction, but Jesuits such as Jean de Brbeuf arrived in 1626 and began compiling vocabularies, grammars, and dictionaries of Wendat by the 1630s, including a foundational French-Wendat dictionary attributed to Brbeuf and associates that incorporated hymns, prayers, and basic morphology. These works, preserved in the Jesuit Relations (16101791), detailed Wendat's polysynthetic structure and verb complexities, though limited by the missionaries' focus on evangelization and occasional reliance on interpreters, which introduced potential inaccuracies in tone and affixation. By 1636, Brbeuf noted the language's agglutinative traits and absence of labials, providing early comparative data against French.
For the Mohawk language, among the core tongues of the Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy, the first written records emerged in the 1630s from Dutch colonial interactions in the Hudson Valley. Explorer Harmen Meyndertsz van den Bogaert documented a Mohawk vocabulary during his 16341635 journey, marking the earliest philological treatment of the language, followed by contributions from Nicolaes van Wassenaer and Johannes Megapolensis in the 1640s, who recorded terms for missionary and trade purposes. French Jesuits extended this in the 1660s, producing catechisms and texts that captured Mohawk's intricate noun incorporation and aspectual verb systems, though early orthographies varied due to non-native scribes and lacked standardization until later centuries. These records, while fragmentary, reveal dialectal variations among Mohawk communities and inform reconstructions of pre-contact phonology, such as consistent glottalization and fricative contrasts absent in European languages. Overall, early European efforts prioritized utility for communication and conversion over exhaustive description, yielding invaluable but orthographically inconsistent data that linguists later refined through comparative methods.

Modern Orthographies

Modern orthographies for Northern Iroquoian languages, such as Mohawk, Seneca, and Oneida, typically employ practical Latin-based scripts adapted for phonetic accuracy, featuring a limited set of consonants and vowels with diacritics to denote length, nasality, or stress. These systems prioritize ease of use in education and documentation, often using 10-12 Roman letters supplemented by marks like acute accents (e.g., for stress in Seneca) or dedicated symbols for nasal vowels (e.g., "en" and "on" in Mohawk). Standardization efforts began in the mid-20th century; for Mohawk, a unified orthography was established in 1972 by educators, translators, and elders to facilitate school instruction, drawing on earlier missionary adaptations but refining them for consistency across dialects. Similar practical orthographies were developed for Seneca through bilingual education projects, such as the one in Salamanca, New York, emphasizing lowercase usage and phonetic spelling to reflect glottalized and aspirated sounds without complex digraphs.
In contrast, Cherokee, the sole surviving Southern Iroquoian language, employs a syllabary of 85 characters invented by Sequoyah in 1821, which represents syllable nuclei rather than individual phonemes and remains the primary writing system today. This syllabary enables high literacy rates historically and continues in modern applications, including digital fonts, educational materials, and publications, with refinements for typesetting but no fundamental changes to the core symbols. Recent technological adaptations, such as Unicode support and mobile keyboards implemented by the Cherokee Nation Language Department since the 2010s, have integrated the syllabary into contemporary devices while preserving its phonetic fidelity.
Across the family, orthographic choices reflect causal priorities of revitalization: Northern languages favor Roman scripts for accessibility in bilingual contexts, while Cherokee's syllabary endures due to its cultural entrenchment and efficiency for native speakers, though both face challenges from dialectal variation and incomplete standardization in lesser-documented varieties like Cayuga or Tuscarora. No pan-Iroquoian orthography exists, as developments are language-specific and driven by community needs rather than linguistic unity.

Contemporary Status

Speaker Populations

The Iroquoian language family features severely limited speaker populations, with all member languages classified as endangered or moribund by linguistic assessments, reflecting historical disruptions from colonization, assimilation policies, and intergenerational language shift. As of 2021 Canadian census data, approximately 2,055 individuals reported conversational proficiency in an Iroquoian language, concentrated in Ontario and Quebec reserves. In the United States, speakers are primarily affiliated with Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) nations in New York, Cherokee communities in Oklahoma and North Carolina, and scattered Tuscarora groups, though precise national aggregates are fragmented due to varying definitions of fluency.
Fluency levels distinguish between first-language (L1) native speakersoften elderlyand second-language (L2) learners, with L1 cohorts dwindling rapidly; for instance, Cherokee Nation reported losing about 60 L1 speakers in 2024 alone. Mohawk (Kanien'kha) maintains the largest base, with roughly 3,0003,500 speakers across Canada (1,600 conversational proficiency) and the U.S., supported by immersion programs in Quebec, Ontario, and New York. Cherokee (Tsalagi) follows, with 1,5002,000 L1 speakers and additional proficient L2 users, mainly in Oklahoma.

Revitalization Initiatives

Revitalization efforts for Iroquoian languages address their endangered status, with fluent speakers numbering in the dozens for many varieties as of the early 2020s. Community-led immersion programs, academic partnerships, and technological applications form the core strategies, often funded by tribal governments and supported by linguistic documentation from historical records.
Mohawk (Kanyen'kha) revitalization includes the Onkwawenna Kentyohkwa adult immersion program, launched in 1999, which aims to produce fluent speakers through full-time instruction. The Saint Regis Mohawk Tribe's :se Tsi Tew:ton two-year adult program emphasizes conversational proficiency, requiring placement assessments for participants. Queen's University offers a Mohawk Language and Culture Certificate, comprising four courses over two years to build foundational skills. The Six Nations Language Commission supports Mohawk alongside Cayuga and Onondaga through community projects and resources.
Cherokee initiatives, backed by the Cherokee Nation's Durbin Feeling Language Preservation Act, have allocated over $68 million since its passage to expand immersion and apprenticeship models. Programs include preschool immersion, a master-apprentice pairing for adultsset to quadruple in scale by 2025and university partnerships for teacher certification. The Cherokee Language Consortium develops new terminology and technology aids, while collaborations with the U.S. Department of the Interior extend resources to other tribes. 
Seneca preservation leverages the Seneca Nation Language Department's community outreach, including online classes and professional development for teachers. A 2018 Rochester Institute of Technology project applied deep learning to transcribe historical Jesuit texts, aiding speech recognition for fewer than 50 remaining fluent speakers documented in 2022. Immersion occurs at sites like the Faithkeepers School.
For Tuscarora, efforts focus on decolonizing approaches post-fluency loss, integrating community mobilization with documentation. Huron-Wendat (Wendat) revival, dormant since the 17th century, involves the Communaut-Diffusion-Fdration des Mtis Huron-Wendat creating teaching materials and training speakers from reconstructed sources. Linguist John Steckley's 20th-century reconstructions underpin ongoing spoken revival.
Broader frameworks, such as the American Indian Higher Education Consortium's 2021 three-year plan, build institutional capacity across Iroquoian and other Native languages through strategic funding and training. These initiatives prioritize oral transmission and cultural integration, though success metrics vary, with persistent challenges from intergenerational transmission gaps.

External Affiliations

Proposed Macro-Siouan Links

The Macro-Siouan hypothesis posits a distant genetic affiliation uniting the Siouan, Iroquoian, and Caddoan language families under a common proto-language, with divergence estimated at 3,000 to 4,000 years ago based on glottochronological approximations. This proposal emerged from comparative work by linguist Wallace L. Chafe, who in the 1960s and 1970s systematically compared reconstructed proto-forms across these families, emphasizing resemblances in core vocabulary such as body parts, numerals, pronouns, and natural phenomena. Chafe's analysis highlighted potential regular sound changes, including shifts in initial consonants and vowel correspondences, to argue against mere areal diffusion or coincidence as explanations for the observed parallels.
Key lexical evidence includes correspondences like Proto-Siouan *si- or *i- 'foot' aligning with Iroquoian *a-tsi- 'foot' (as in Mohawk a-si), and Proto-Siouan *sk 'hand' with Iroquoian *a-k: 'hand'. Similar patterns appear in terms for 'two' (Proto-Siouan *nw vs. Iroquoian *te-y-kwa) and 'water' (Proto-Siouan *mini vs. Iroquoian *o-ni-). Chafe extended these to Caddoan, proposing links such as Proto-Caddoan *wts 'light (not heavy)' with Siouan-Iroquoian forms for 'light' or 'bright', suggesting shared inheritance rather than borrowing. These comparisons drew from over 100 proposed cognates, selected for semantic stability and phonological plausibility.
Morphological parallels further underpin the hypothesis, including agglutinative verb structures with pronominal prefixes and suffixing for tense-aspect, as well as noun incorporation patterns common to all three families. For instance, instrumental prefixes and locative suffixes show analogous formations, such as Siouan *wa- 'by mouth' paralleling Iroquoian equivalents in verbal derivations. Recent scholarship has revisited Iroquoian-Caddoan ties specifically, analyzing inflectional morphemes like alienable possession markers and derivational suffixes to identify deeper structural affinities consistent with a Macro-Siouan framework.
Proponents argue that the geographic proximity of ancestral speech communities in the eastern Woodlands and Great Plains supports the plausibility of such relatedness, with archaeological correlations suggesting population movements that could align with linguistic divergence timelines. While Chafe's work forms the foundation, subsequent quantitative assessments of cognate sets have aimed to test the hypothesis through statistical measures of lexical similarity beyond chance levels.

Empirical Challenges to Broader Hypotheses

The Macro-Siouan hypothesis, linking Iroquoian languages with Siouan-Catawban and Caddoan, has been challenged primarily by the absence of systematic sound correspondences between reconstructed proto-forms, a cornerstone of the comparative method in historical linguistics. Proposed cognates, such as those assembled by Wallace Chafe in 1976 for over 100 lexical items, often fail to exhibit regular phonological patterns across the families; for instance, vowel correspondences vary irregularly, and consonant matches appear sporadic without predictable shifts. This methodological shortfall contrasts with established families like Algonquian, where sound laws (e.g., Proto-Algonquian * > Mohawk ts) hold consistently.
Lexical resemblances cited in support, including basic vocabulary like numerals or body parts, are frequently attributable to chance convergence or areal diffusion rather than inheritance from a common ancestor. Lyle Campbell's analysis in his 1997 survey assigns the hypothesis only a 20% probability of validity, emphasizing that many etymologies dissolve under scrutiny for homophony or borrowing, as Iroquoian and Siouan groups maintained prolonged geographic proximity in the Eastern Woodlands, facilitating loanwords. Shared grammatical traits, such as polysynthesis or agent-patient pronominal prefixes, reflect typological parallels common in North American languages but lack specific innovations uniting the families exclusively; for example, Iroquoian noun incorporation differs structurally from Siouan patterns without demonstrable homology.
Quantitative assessments reinforce skepticism: computational phylogenetic studies of North American languages, incorporating Swadesh lists, yield low cognate densities (under 10%) between Iroquoian and Siouan proto-vocabularies, below thresholds for reliable distant relatedness (typically 15-20% for proto-languages separated by 5,000-6,000 years). Divergence timelines further complicate claims; glottochronological estimates place Proto-Iroquoian around 3,500-4,000 years ago, with Siouan branching similarly early, yet no post-proto shared innovationssuch as unique morphological mergersemerge to support a macro-family node. Critics like Campbell note that without such evidence, proposals risk conflating contact-induced similarities with genetic descent, a pitfall amplified by reliance on mass comparison over rigorous reconstruction. These challenges have led most historical linguists to treat Macro-Siouan as unproven, prioritizing Iroquoian as an isolate family pending stronger data.
