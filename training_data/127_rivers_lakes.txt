Rivers are the arteries of the land. They carry water from the mountains to the sea, carving valleys, building deltas, depositing fertile soil, and providing the freshwater that every terrestrial ecosystem and every human civilization depends upon. Every great city in history was built on a river: Rome on the Tiber, London on the Thames, Paris on the Seine, Cairo on the Nile, Baghdad on the Tigris, New York on the Hudson. Rivers are not just geographical features. They are the reason civilization exists where it does.

A river begins its life as a trickle of water emerging from a spring, a melting glacier, or a marsh in the highlands. As it flows downhill, it gathers tributaries, streams that join the main channel and add their water to the flow. The area of land that drains into a river system is called a watershed or catchment. The Amazon River has the largest watershed on Earth, covering approximately two point seven million square miles, roughly the size of the continental United States. Every drop of rain that falls within this enormous area eventually finds its way into the Amazon.

The Nile, at approximately four thousand one hundred and thirty miles, is generally considered the longest river on Earth, though the Amazon, at approximately four thousand miles, is a close rival and carries far more water. The Amazon discharges approximately fifty-five million gallons of water per second into the Atlantic Ocean, more than the next seven largest rivers combined. During the wet season, sections of the Amazon can be thirty miles wide, and the river system contains approximately twenty percent of all the fresh water flowing into the world's oceans.

Rivers shape the landscape through erosion and deposition. In their upper courses, where the gradient is steep and the water flows fast, rivers cut downward into the bedrock, carving V-shaped valleys, waterfalls, and gorges. The Grand Canyon in Arizona was carved by the Colorado River over approximately five to six million years, exposing nearly two billion years of geological history in its layered walls. In their middle courses, where the gradient decreases, rivers meander in broad curves across floodplains, eroding the outer bank of each curve and depositing sediment on the inner bank. In their lower courses, where the river approaches the sea and the gradient is nearly flat, rivers deposit the sediment they have been carrying, building deltas, which are among the most fertile and most densely populated landscapes on Earth.

Lakes are bodies of standing water that occupy depressions in the Earth's surface. They are formed by a variety of geological processes. Glacial lakes, the most common type, were carved by glaciers during the ice ages. The Great Lakes of North America, which contain approximately twenty-one percent of the world's surface freshwater, were formed by glacial erosion and deposition. Tectonic lakes form in basins created by the movement of tectonic plates. Lake Baikal in Siberia, the deepest and oldest lake on Earth, occupies a rift valley created by the divergence of tectonic plates. It is approximately twenty-five million years old, over a mile deep, and contains approximately twenty percent of the world's unfrozen freshwater.

Crater lakes form in the calderas of extinct volcanoes. Crater Lake in Oregon, one of the deepest lakes in North America at nearly two thousand feet, fills the caldera of Mount Mazama, which erupted catastrophically approximately seven thousand seven hundred years ago. Oxbow lakes form when a meandering river cuts off one of its own curves, leaving a crescent-shaped body of water separated from the main channel.

Freshwater is one of the most precious and most threatened resources on Earth. Although water covers seventy-one percent of the planet's surface, ninety-seven percent of it is salt water in the oceans. Of the remaining three percent that is fresh, approximately sixty-nine percent is locked in glaciers and ice caps, approximately thirty percent is groundwater, and less than one percent is in surface lakes, rivers, and swamps. This means that all the rivers and lakes on Earth, every stream, every pond, every reservoir, contain less than one hundredth of one percent of all the water on the planet.

Water scarcity already affects approximately two billion people worldwide, and the problem is expected to worsen as population grows, climate change alters precipitation patterns, and demand for water from agriculture, industry, and households increases. The Aral Sea in Central Asia, once the fourth largest lake in the world, has shrunk to less than ten percent of its original size since the 1960s because the rivers that fed it were diverted for cotton irrigation, in what has been called one of the worst environmental disasters in history.

Rivers and lakes are not just physical features. They are ecosystems that support extraordinary biodiversity. Freshwater habitats cover less than one percent of the Earth's surface but support approximately ten percent of all known species, including approximately forty percent of all fish species. The Amazon River basin alone contains over three thousand species of freshwater fish, more than are found in the entire Atlantic Ocean.

The relationship between human civilization and rivers is so fundamental that the very word "rival" comes from the Latin "rivalis," meaning someone who shares the same river. Rivers have been sources of water, food, transportation, energy, and inspiration for as long as humans have existed. They have also been sources of conflict, as communities upstream and downstream compete for the same finite resource. The management of shared river systems is one of the great challenges of international cooperation, and the equitable distribution of freshwater will be one of the defining issues of the twenty-first century.

Every river tells the story of the land it flows through. The color of its water reveals the soil it has eroded. The speed of its current reveals the steepness of the terrain. The width of its floodplain reveals the history of its floods. The life in its waters reveals the health of the ecosystem it supports. A river is a biography of a landscape, written in water, and it is being written continuously, one drop at a time, from the mountain spring to the sea.
