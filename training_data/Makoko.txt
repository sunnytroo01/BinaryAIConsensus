Makoko
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Makoko is an informal waterfront settlement in Lagos State, Nigeria, consisting of stilted wooden structures over the Lagos Lagoon and adjacent land-based housing, originally established as a fishing village in the 18th century. Its growth stems from rural-urban migration driven by economic opportunities in Lagos, resulting in a densely populated area where residents primarily rely on fishing, small-scale trade, and canoe transport for livelihoods. Living conditions are characterized by substandard housing, inadequate sanitation, and vulnerability to flooding, with over 70% of households classified as poor or socially disadvantaged based on income and access metrics.
The settlement has endured repeated government interventions aimed at eviction and demolition, justified by officials as necessary for environmental sanitation, public health, and urban infrastructure development, including a 2012 operation that displaced hundreds after a brief notice period. Earlier forced evictions in 2005 similarly targeted structures deemed illegal, highlighting a pattern of conflict between resident claims to customary land use and state priorities for lagoon reclamation. Community resistance, including legal challenges and proposals for sustainable regeneration, has occasionally delayed demolitions, as seen in initiatives that prompted policy reevaluations, though underlying issues of infrastructure deficits and pollution persist due to unchecked expansion and limited state provision of alternatives.

Geography and Location

Physical Setting and Layout

Makoko is positioned along the Lagos Lagoon in Lagos State, Nigeria, directly adjacent to the mainland areas of Yaba and Ebute Metta, with coordinates approximately at 629'46"N latitude. The settlement extends into the lagoon, where structures are erected on wooden stilts driven into the shallow waters, forming a waterborne community that interfaces with swampy coastal land. This dual terrestrial-aquatic setting results from the lagoon's tidal influences and silting, creating an environment dominated by brackish water and mangrove fringes.
The layout comprises a dense, irregular array of approximately one-third of the community built over water, with the remainder on unstable, flood-prone swampy ground. Water sections feature clustered shacks connected by narrow, often precarious plank bridges and walkways, while primary navigation occurs via canoes through a maze of canals averaging 2-5 meters wide. Land portions exhibit haphazard clustering of shanties without formalized roads, reflecting organic expansion constrained by the watery terrain. Structures typically rise 2-3 meters above water level on stilts up to 4 meters long, utilizing scavenged timber and corrugated metal for walls and roofs.
This configuration exposes the community to environmental vulnerabilities, including tidal flooding and erosion, as the lagoon's depth varies from 1-3 meters in inhabited zones, with no engineered barriers. Historical maps from 1962 depict Makoko as a nascent fishing outpost along the lagoon edge, contrasting with its current expansive, improvised sprawl driven by unconstrained settlement growth.

Relation to Lagos Urban Area

Makoko occupies a strategic position on the Lagos Lagoon, immediately adjacent to Lagos Mainland, integrating it into the broader Lagos metropolitan region as one of the city's oldest informal waterfront settlements. Spanning approximately 1 square kilometer with coordinates around 630'9"N, 323'31"E, it borders developed areas like Oworonshoki to the east and Yaba to the north, positioning it within 5-10 kilometers of central commercial districts such as Lagos Island. This proximity facilitates daily commutes for residents to urban employment hubs, including the nearby oil industry and port facilities, underscoring Makoko's role as a peripheral yet functionally linked extension of Lagos's urban economy.
The Third Mainland Bridge, Africa's second-longest at nearly 12 km, arches directly over Makoko, connecting the settlement's landward sections to mainland thoroughfares and Lagos Island via exits to areas like Herbert Macaulay Way in Yaba. While this infrastructure enhances accessibilityenabling bus and vehicle links from shore pointsMakoko's waterborne portions depend on canoes and unregulated ferries for internal movement and external ties, highlighting a partial integration hampered by the absence of formal roads, sewers, or utilities extending from the mainland grid. Recent expansions of stilt housing have encroached closer to the bridge's supports, raising concerns over encroachment on vital urban transport corridors amid Lagos's unchecked sprawl.
Despite its embeddedness in the urban continuumevident in land-based markets interfacing with mainland trade flowsMakoko exemplifies the dual city dynamic of Lagos, where informal lagoon communities contrast with adjacent formalized zones, yet contribute to the megalopolis's labor pool and density, with over 80,000 residents sustaining ties to city-wide economic circuits. Government eviction threats since 2012 have tested this relational fabric, prompting community mapping initiatives to assert spatial claims within the urban planning discourse.

Historical Development

Origins as Fishing Community

Makoko originated as a fishing settlement established by migrants of the Egun ethnic group, who constructed homes on stilts over the Lagos Lagoon. These fishermen primarily came from Badagry, a coastal town west of Lagos, and neighboring regions in the Republic of Benin, seeking the lagoon's abundant fish stocks for their livelihood.
The community's founding is commonly dated to the late 19th century, when Egun immigrants adapted to the marshy, waterlogged terrain by building elevated wooden structures directly above the water, facilitating immediate access to fishing grounds via canoes. This stilt-based architecture, integral to the Egun fishing tradition, allowed year-round exploitation of lagoon resources despite seasonal flooding and tidal influences.
Some accounts trace the initial settlement to the 18th century, attributing it to immigrants from Benin and Togo who reclaimed lagoon areas for fishing camps. Regardless of the precise timeline, the core activity from inception was subsistence and small-scale commercial fishing, with residents relying on species like tilapia and catfish abundant in the lagoon ecosystem. Early inhabitants organized around family-based fishing units, with women often processing catches through smoking and drying for local markets.

Expansion Through Migration and Informal Settlement

Makoko's expansion beyond its foundational fishing village was driven primarily by waves of migration from rural Nigeria and neighboring West African nations, attracted by employment prospects in Lagos's burgeoning urban economy during the mid-20th century. Rural-urban migrants, often from impoverished inland regions, supplemented the original Egun settlers with diverse ethnic groups including those from Togo and Ghana, who integrated into the community's fishing and informal trade networks. This influx accelerated after Nigeria's independence in 1960, as Lagos's population surged from approximately 300,000 in 1950 to over 1 million by 1970, channeling low-skilled laborers into peripheral settlements like Makoko.
The scarcity of affordable land prompted informal settlement patterns, with residents erecting stilt-supported wooden structures over the lagoon to accommodate growing households, a practice that evolved organically without municipal zoning or infrastructure support. By the 1960s, maps documented Makoko's delineation as a distinct community adjacent to Lagos Mainland, evidencing this spatial proliferation amid unchecked urbanization. Economic pull factors, such as demand for unskilled labor in Lagos's ports and markets, sustained migration flows, transforming the area into a dense, unregulated expanse of over 85,000 residents by the early 21st century, half residing on water.
Satellite-based assessments quantify this informal built-up growth, revealing a 27.6% increase in Makoko's constructed area from 2000 to 2010, followed by 7.7% expansion between 2010 and 2020, attributable to persistent in-migration and family-based subdivision of plots. These developments occurred amid broader Lagos megacity dynamics, where rapid population doubling every 15 years outpaced formal housing supply, entrenching Makoko as a quintessential informal settlement reliant on communal self-provisioning rather than state intervention. Despite eviction threats, such as the 2012-2013 government demolition notices affecting thousands, migration continued, underscoring the community's resilience to policy disruptions.

Demographics and Social Dynamics

Population Estimates and Composition

Estimates of Makoko's population vary significantly due to its status as an informal settlement lacking official census data; figures range from approximately 85,000 to 250,000 residents as of recent assessments. A 2024 community mapping initiative pegged the number at around 250,000, predominantly in stilted waterfront structures, while earlier reports from educational and urban studies sources cite lower bounds near 85,000 to 100,000, reflecting challenges in enumeration amid ongoing migration and unregistered births. Higher claims, such as 400,000 from local leaders or up to 1 million in anecdotal accounts, lack verification from systematic surveys and may inflate for advocacy purposes.
The community comprises six subcommunities, with four main ethnic groups dominating: primarily the Egun (also known as Egun), who originated as fishing migrants from the Benin Republic in the late 19th century, alongside Ilaje, Yoruba, and smaller Igbo populations. This multi-ethnic makeup reflects broader patterns of rural-urban migration and economic pull factors in Lagos, with Egun and Ilaje forming the core due to historical ties to lagoon fishing, while Yoruba and Igbo integrate through trade and informal labor. Religious diversity includes Christianity, Islam, and traditional practices, coexisting across five spoken languages, though no precise breakdowns exist owing to the absence of demographic registries. Indigenous ethnic majorities distinguish Makoko from typical migrant-driven slums, underscoring its evolution from a specialized fishing enclave rather than generalized rural exodus.

Community Organization and Social Issues

Makoko's governance relies on traditional leadership structures dominated by Baales, or local chiefs, who collaborate with religious leaders to make key decisions on community matters. Notable figures include Emmanuel Shemede, Baale of Adogbo Village since 2005, and Chief Raymond Adekunle Olaiya, recognized as head of the broader Makoko community. The settlement is segmented into distinct villages, such as Oko Agbon, Adogbo, Migbewhe, and Yanshiwhe, enabling localized administration amid the absence of formal municipal oversight. Youth leaders, like Mr. Whewumi Raymond, also contribute to mobilization efforts, particularly in resisting external threats such as government evictions.
Pervasive poverty and unemployment underpin many social challenges, with residents confronting high rates of crime linked to economic desperation, including theft and sporadic violence. Health issues are acute, featuring endemic malaria, respiratory illnesses, and malnutrition, compounded by open defecation, contaminated lagoon water, and lack of public hospitals or sanitation infrastructure. Education remains severely constrained, with only limited facilities like a single French-medium primary school established in 1989 serving around 120 pupils at N50 daily fees, while poverty and abuse disproportionately bar girls from schooling.
Gender dynamics exacerbate vulnerabilities, as womenoften primary breadwinners in fish processing and tradeface barriers to literacy, credit access, and protection from domestic violence and sexual abuse, perpetuating cycles of marginalization. Community-led responses, including solar-powered floating schools and participatory mapping projects initiated around 2020, seek to bolster social inclusion and service delivery, though implementation remains hampered by funding shortages and recurrent flooding.

Economy and Livelihoods

Traditional Fishing and Informal Trade

Traditional fishing in Makoko centers on small-scale operations in the Lagos Lagoon, where residents primarily use wooden canoes for navigation and deploy traps to capture fish species such as tilapia and catfish. Men, often from the Egun ethnic group originating from coastal Benin, conduct most fishing expeditions, employing both longstanding basket traps and innovated variants designed for higher efficiency in shallow waters. These methods sustain daily catches that form the backbone of household protein and income, with fishermen typically departing at dawn and returning by midday to avoid peak heat and tidal shifts.
Fish processing follows capture, involving sun-drying or smoking over open fires on stilt platforms to preserve hauls against spoilage in the humid climate. Women play a central role here, gutting, scaling, and roasting fish using rudimentary smokers fueled by local wood, a labor-intensive process that extends product shelf life for trade. This gendered division reflects cultural norms, though some women also fish directly, facing constraints like limited access to gear and capital compared to men.
Informal trade revolves around vending smoked fish, fresh catches, and byproducts through canoe-based markets or shoreline stalls, often bartering with mainland buyers for staples like garri or kerosene. Transactions occur without formal regulation, yielding average daily earnings equivalent to $1.25 per person amid broader informal sector activities including sand dredging for construction and salt production from lagoon brine. These exchanges link Makoko's economy to Lagos' urban demand, though yields fluctuate with fish availability and weather, underscoring reliance on unregulated networks for survival.

Economic Constraints and Informal Sector Realities

The economy of Makoko is predominantly informal, with the majority of residents engaged in subsistence activities such as fishing, small-scale trading, sand dredging, and salt production, lacking formal contracts, regulatory oversight, or access to credit markets. These livelihoods support an estimated population of over 80,000, but generate minimal income, often as low as $1.25 per day per household, insufficient to meet basic needs amid rising living costs in Lagos. The informal nature exposes workers to volatility, including fluctuating fish yields due to lagoon pollution and overfishing, which have reduced catches and forced diversification into precarious trades like net mending and petty commerce.
Key constraints include limited education and skills training, perpetuating low productivity and barring entry into Lagos's formal sectors like manufacturing or services, where over 60% of the city's jobs remain inaccessible to slum dwellers. Government policies exacerbate vulnerabilities through eviction threats and inadequate infrastructure, disrupting trade networks and capital accumulation without providing alternatives, as seen in stalled regeneration efforts that prioritize urban renewal over informal sector integration. Women, often primary breadwinners in fishing households, face compounded barriers, including childcare responsibilities and exclusion from cooperative funding, leading to higher poverty rates and reliance on unregulated markets.
Broader structural realities tie Makoko's informal economy to Lagos's rapid urbanization, where rural-urban migration outpaces job creation, trapping residents in a cycle of marginalization without legal land tenure or public investments in vocational programs. Economic data from urban slum studies indicate that such communities contribute informally to Nigeria's GDP through unrecorded labor but receive negligible returns, with vulnerability indices highlighting exposure to shocks like flooding that destroy boats and stocks annually. Absent formalization pathways, these realities sustain high unemploymentestimated above 40% among youthand intergenerational poverty, underscoring causal links between policy neglect and persistent underdevelopment.

Infrastructure and Daily Life

Housing Types and Adaptations

The predominant form of housing in Makoko consists of simple wooden structures elevated on stilts above the lagoon's surface. These dwellings are typically constructed from timber planks for walls and roofs, with stilts made from bamboo or wooden poles hammered into the soft lagoon bed to provide elevation. This basic timber framing allows for rapid, low-cost assembly using locally sourced materials, often by residents themselves in an incremental manner as families expand.
Stilt elevation, commonly 2 to 4 meters above water level, represents a primary adaptation to the area's tidal fluctuations, seasonal flooding, and high water table, preventing submersion during high tides or heavy rains. Structures are clustered densely along waterways, with narrow walkways or canoes serving as access routes, reflecting the community's aquatic orientation but also exposing homes to rot, termite damage, and structural instability over time due to untreated wood exposure.
In response to escalating flood risks from urbanization and climate factors, prototypes for fully floating housing have emerged as adaptive alternatives to fixed stilts. The Makoko Floating System, developed by NL architects since 2012, employs prefabricated, modular A-frame units from sustainable timber and recycled plastic drums for buoyancy, enabling hand-assembly and relocation as water levels change. Similar innovations, such as the 2013 Makoko Floating Schoola three-story buoyant structure on triangular pontoonsdemonstrate potential for scalable, flood-resilient designs using local labor and materials, though widespread adoption remains limited by cost and government policy constraints.

Access to Utilities and Basic Services

Residents of Makoko lack formal connections to municipal utilities, relying instead on informal and often inadequate alternatives. Electricity access is absent from the national grid, with households using diesel-powered generators that operate intermittently due to high fuel costsestimated at over 1,000 naira per liter in recent yearsand supply disruptions, resulting in average daily power availability of only a few hours. This dependence exacerbates economic burdens and contributes to environmental degradation through emissions and noise pollution.
Potable water supply is critically deficient, as the surrounding lagoon water is heavily contaminated by waste and industrial runoff, unsuitable for consumption without treatment. Residents procure water via canoe-delivered vendors, rainwater collection during the wet season (typically June to October), or community boreholes funded by NGOs, but these sources often fail to meet demand for the estimated 80,000-100,000 inhabitants, leading to reliance on untreated lagoon water for cooking and bathing in many cases. A 2022 study highlighted that household-level access to safe water remains below 20% in similar Lagos informal settlements, correlating with elevated incidences of cholera and diarrhea.
Sanitation infrastructure is minimal, dominated by shared pit latrines or bucket latrines that overflow during floods, discharging untreated sewage directly into the lagoon and fostering bacterial contamination levels exceeding WHO safety thresholds by factors of 10-100 times in sampled waters. Open defecation persists in peripheral areas, amplifying health risks amid overcrowding. NGO initiatives, such as the deployment of SANI AMANZI modular purification units by SCISAN, have supplied treated water to small clusters since around 2020, filtering up to 5,000 liters daily per unit and reducing reliance on contaminated sources in targeted zones, though scalability is hindered by funding and logistical challenges in the waterborne environment. Government provision remains negligible, with state reports from 2023-2025 indicating no comprehensive utility extension due to the area's informal status and flood vulnerability.

Environmental Challenges

Water Pollution and Sanitation Deficiencies

Makoko's lagoon waters are severely polluted by untreated sewage, household waste, and industrial effluents discharged directly into the surrounding aquatic environment, exacerbating contamination from the community's stilt-based settlements. Residents predominantly lack access to formal sanitation systems, with open defecation and rudimentary pit latrines common practices that contribute to fecal matter entering the water via tidal flows and runoff.
Groundwater sources, including shallow hand-dug wells relied upon by many households, exhibit high contamination levels from sewage infiltration, with studies detecting elevated pathogens, nitrates, and heavy metals that render the water unfit for human consumption. A 2024 assessment of eight water samples from Makoko's wells and boreholes confirmed widespread microbial and chemical pollution, primarily attributable to proximate sanitation deficiencies and lagoon seepage. Similarly, analysis of 20 surface and well samples collected on December 12, 2020, revealed bacterial contaminants and toxins linked to intestinal disorders.
These deficiencies drive endemic health risks, including waterborne diseases such as cholera, typhoid, diarrhea, and gastrointestinal infections, compounded by the consumption of contaminated fish from the polluted lagoon. Heavy metal bioaccumulation in local seafood species further amplifies noncarcinogenic and carcinogenic hazards for consumers, as documented in bioaccumulation studies of lagoon fisheries. Saltwater intrusion and open drains intensify groundwater salinization, limiting potable supplies and perpetuating reliance on unsafe alternatives amid rising sea levels.

Flooding Risks and Long-Term Sustainability

Makoko's location on the Lagos Lagoon exposes it to recurrent flooding, primarily driven by seasonal heavy rainfall, inadequate drainage systems, and coastal dynamics such as storm surges and tidal influences. Flood events occur annually during the rainy season from May to October, with infrastructural deficits like nonexistent or clogged waterways amplifying inundation of stilt and floating dwellings. In 2011, a major flood in Lagos displaced over 5,000 residents, including those in Makoko, due to overwhelmed lagoon capacities and urban runoff. By 2019, over 34% of Lagos territory, including Makoko, was classified as flood-vulnerable, a sharp rise from 1% at the century's start, attributable to subsidence, unchecked urbanization, and lagoon sedimentation.
Flooding inflicts direct economic and health burdens, eroding livelihoods reliant on fishing and informal trade while facilitating waterborne diseases. Structures suffer structural failure, as evidenced by the 2016 collapse of the Makoko Floating School amid storms, which highlighted vulnerabilities in even purpose-built adaptive designs. Impacts include loss of equipment for small-scale operators, such as barbers, and heightened disease transmission, with floods promoting cholera, typhoid, and leptospirosis in dense, sanitation-poor settings; studies link these events to exacerbated poverty through disrupted income and increased medical costs. Men in Makoko report higher flood-related losses than women, tied to outdoor occupations, though overall community resilience relies on informal coping like elevated platforms, which prove insufficient against intensified events.
Long-term sustainability faces existential threats from climate-driven sea-level rise and ecosystem degradation, rendering Makoko's adaptive floating architecture increasingly precarious without systemic upgrades. Nigeria's sea levels are rising at 3.7 mm annually, with Lagos projections estimating up to 90 cm by 2100 under moderate warming scenarios exceeding 2C, intensifying erosion and surge penetration into the lagoon. Outdated construction materials and maintenance gaps compound risks, as wooden stilts degrade from constant submersion and pollution, while socio-economic constraints limit retrofitting; peer-reviewed analyses deem current setups unsustainable amid frequent inundations that outpace natural buoyancy adaptations. Causal factors like lagoon siltation from upstream waste and groundwater extraction-induced subsidence further erode viability, suggesting relocation or engineered barriers as potential necessities, though community dependence on aquatic access resists such shifts.

Government Interventions and Policy Debates

Eviction Campaigns and Demolition Actions

In April 2005, the Lagos State Government initiated a forced eviction in Makoko, providing residents with only 48 hours' notice without obtaining a required court order; over three days, bulldozers demolished homes, churches, schools, and a medical center, displacing approximately 3,000 people and injuring some during the operation, with no alternative housing offered. The action was justified by officials as addressing illegal structures on government land, though evictees reported inadequate compensation and immediate hardships including loss of livelihoods.
A larger-scale campaign followed in July 2012, when the Lagos State Government served a 72-hour eviction notice to Makoko residents, citing security threats, environmental degradation, and the need to prevent slum expansion in a nearly 200-year-old waterfront settlement; demolitions targeted stilt houses and other informal structures, displacing thousands and prompting residents to seek court injunctions, though partial enforcement proceeded amid protests. Government statements emphasized reclaiming lagoon areas for urban planning, but human rights groups documented the lack of relocation support, exacerbating vulnerability for fishing-dependent families.
Subsequent threats persisted under Governor Akinwunmi Ambode's administration, with a October 2016 directive ordering the demolition of all creek-side shanties within seven days, including in Makoko, as part of broader waterfront clearances; while full-scale action in Makoko was limited by community resistance and legal appeals, related evictions in adjacent areas like Otodo-Gbame in March 2017 left over 30,000 homeless across Lagos waterfronts, with some Makoko families resorting to living in canoes due to spillover effects. These campaigns reflect a pattern of rapid enforcement prioritizing infrastructure goals over resident consultation, contributing to cycles of displacement without verified long-term urban benefits for affected populations.
In late 2025 to January 2026, the Lagos State Government under Governor Babajide Sanwo-Olu demolished illegal structures in Makoko built too close to high-tension power lines, enforcing a 100-meter safety buffer despite community negotiations that reduced it to 30 meters and later 50 meters, as insisted upon by Permanent Secretary Gbolahan Oki in alignment with state and federal regulations. This action, displacing over 10,000 residents with some resorting to sleeping in boats, involved tear gas deployment affecting at least three individuals and provided no resettlement plans, reviving eviction efforts initially planned in 2012 under Governor Babatunde Raji Fashola. No alternative housing was reported, raising concerns from NGOs about humanitarian impacts on shelter, food security, and livelihoods for fishing-dependent families.

Regeneration Plans and Alternative Proposals

In 2021, the Lagos State Government signed an agreement with FBT Coral Estate Limited for a 54.58-hectare mixed-use development at the Makoko waterfront, allocating shares at a 75%-25% ratio respectively, with sand filling and land reclamation activities commencing thereafter to enable facilities including a sports centre, business hotel, yacht club, convention centre, mall, offices, schools, and hospital. The project, ongoing as of February 2025, seeks to integrate commercial, residential, and civic infrastructure but has encountered legal challenges, including a community injunction against evictions and demolitions, amid criticisms from groups like Rethinking Cities regarding potential displacement of residents, ecosystem disruption, and loss of cultural heritage without adequate resettlement plans.
The Makoko/Iwaya Waterfront Regeneration Plan, developed from 2013 onward by FABULOUS URBAN in collaboration with local communities, elders, women, youth, NGOs, and academic institutions, proposes a community-led approach emphasizing preservation of lagoon-front culture through low-cost housing, sustainable aquaculture, tourism-driven economic activities, disaster-resilient infrastructure, and primary healthcare centers to serve over 40,000 residents while serving as a model for other informal settlements.
Complementing this, the Makoko Sustainable Regeneration Plan employs deliberative methods such as surveys, focus groups, and public hearings to address deficiencies in sanitation, healthcare, housing, energy, and education, introducing a neighborhood management model inspired by UK practices to foster local governance and participation; it successfully halted prior demolition threats and facilitated the construction of a solar-powered floating school completed in August 2015, which received the Aga Khan Award for Architecture in 2016.
Alternative proposals prioritize water-based adaptations over land reclamation, as exemplified by the Makoko Water Cities initiative, a pre-development project partnering with UNDP, UN-Habitat, UNCDF, and the Lagos State Government to create climate-resilient urban systems integrating architecture, infrastructure, and ecological regeneration for over 100,000 beneficiaries, scalable across African coastal cities facing flooding. Similarly, NL's Makoko Floating School serves as a prototype for ecological, floating building systems that promote urban water cultures resilient to climate change and urbanization, aiming to generate sustainable alternatives for coastal populations without displacing water-dependent livelihoods.

Competing Perspectives on Urban Development

The Lagos State Government has pursued urban development in Makoko primarily through eviction and demolition campaigns, viewing the settlement as an environmental hazard, security threat, and obstacle to waterfront modernization efforts aimed at transforming Lagos into a "world-class" megacity. In 2012, officials issued an eviction notice citing risks to nearby infrastructure like the Third Mainland Bridge and power lines, leading to the demolition of structures housing over 3,000 residents without adequate resettlement. These actions align with broader policies since 2005 to upgrade Lagos waterfronts, prioritizing formal urban expansion over informal settlements that lack legal recognition.
In contrast, Makoko residents and community advocates emphasize in-situ upgrading over relocation, arguing that evictions exacerbate poverty and disrupt livelihoods tied to lagoon fishing and informal trade, which sustain over 100,000 inhabitants. Protests in 2012 and ongoing resistance highlight demands for participatory planning, with residents rejecting displacement plans that ignore their adaptive floating architecture and economic dependencies. Local groups, such as those behind the Makoko Sustainable Regeneration Plan initiated around 2012, propose community-led interventions to address sanitation, housing, and education gaps while preserving the site's functionality, critiquing top-down approaches for failing to integrate bottom-up needs.
Academic analyses underscore tensions between these views, noting that government-led slum clearance often results in gentrification pressures without resolving underlying urbanization drivers like housing shortages and migration, as seen in Makoko's persistent regrowth post-demolitions. Slum upgrading initiatives, such as limited infrastructure projects fitting Lagos's global city ambitions, have improved select aspects like waste management but face criticism for inadequate scale and exclusion of informal economies. Courts in Lagos have occasionally ruled against forced evictions lacking due process or alternatives, signaling legal pushback, though enforcement remains inconsistent. These debates reflect causal realities: rapid population growth outpaces formal housing, rendering relocation inefficient without massive investment, while upgrading risks subsidizing unsustainable lagoon settlements vulnerable to flooding.

Cultural Adaptations and Significance

Architectural Innovations in Floating Structures

The traditional architecture of Makoko's floating community predominantly features stilt houses constructed from locally sourced timber and bamboo, elevated above the lagoon waters to mitigate periodic flooding and tidal fluctuations. These structures, often built by residents using rudimentary hand tools, employ A-frame designs for stability, with bamboo poles driven into the lagoon bed as supports, allowing homes to rise 2-3 meters above water level during high tides. This vernacular approach represents an adaptive innovation rooted in necessity, enabling over 100,000 inhabitants to inhabit a high-density aquatic environment without formal infrastructure, though it relies on ongoing maintenance to prevent decay from constant moisture exposure.
A notable modern innovation emerged in 2013 with the Makoko Floating School, a prototype designed by Dutch-Nigerian architect Kunl Adeyemi of NL, intended as a scalable model for flood-resilient education and housing in water-based settlements. Unlike fixed stilts, this triangular A-frame structure floats on modular pontoons assembled from recycled plastic barrels and timber, enabling vertical and horizontal mobility with rising water levels up to 2 meters, thus reducing vulnerability to storm surges and sea-level rise projected for Lagos Lagoon. The design incorporates passive ventilation through its open pyramid form, photovoltaic panels on the roof generating up to 2.5 kW of electricity, and a rainwater harvesting system filtering lagoon water for non-potable use, all fabricated locally to minimize costs at approximately $7,000 for the 10x10 meter prototype.
The Makoko Floating System (MFS), an extension of this prototype developed by NL, emphasizes prefabrication for rapid assembly, using interlocking timber modules that can be produced by hand in community workshops and scaled for homes, markets, or sanitation facilities. This system addresses limitations of stilt construction by decoupling buildings from the unstable seabed, potentially supporting denser urban expansion in similar lagoon communities across West Africa, though implementation has been limited by funding and the 2016 partial collapse of the original school due to structural fatigue from unaddressed leaks. Empirical assessments highlight its potential for carbon-neutral construction via renewable timber and waste materials, contrasting with the high environmental footprint of land reclamation alternatives debated in Lagos policy circles.

Resilience Narratives vs. Structural Dependencies

Resilience narratives frequently depict Makoko's residents as exemplars of adaptive ingenuity, constructing stilt and floating dwellings from timber and navigating the lagoon via canoes for fishing and trade, thereby sustaining a community of approximately 85,000 to 250,000 people amid urban pressures. These accounts underscore social capitalsuch as kinship ties and reciprocal support networksas bolstering short-term coping with hazards like flooding and resource scarcity, enabling higher resilience compared to other Lagos slums. However, such portrayals derive from observable survival tactics rather than evidence of long-term viability, often sourced from media emphasizing "ingenuity" while sidelining empirical indicators of fragility, including vulnerability to environmental degradation and economic shocks.
In reality, these adaptations mask profound structural dependencies on an informal economy centered on lagoon fishing, which supplies livelihoods for most households but faces depletion from pollution hotspots and overfishing, with fish yields diminished by criminal activities, cheap imports, and climate variability. Over 50% of residents subsist below $1.25 daily, compounded by 98.4% occupancy of substandard wooden structures prone to collapse and 99% absence of formal infrastructure, rendering the community reliant on depleting natural resources without diversified income or land tenure security. Women's roles in fish processing and trading exemplify this, as limited literacy and credit access perpetuate funding shortages and intergenerational poverty cycles.
Government disengagement amplifies these dependencies, with sporadic aid like the Makoko Floating School project highlighting external interventions' limited scale against chronic exclusion from urban planning and services. Residents' widespread opposition to regeneration92% fearing eviction-induced homelessness (89.82% projected)reflects entrapment in a tolerated but unsupported status quo, where "resilience" equates to endurance of outdated building technologies and frequent inundation rather than proactive autonomy. Proposals for sustainable floating development goals prioritize livelihood diversification to mitigate such vulnerabilities, yet implementation lags, underscoring how narratives of self-reliance obscure causal realities of unplanned migration, policy neglect, and ecological limits.
