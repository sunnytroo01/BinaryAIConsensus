Spirulida
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Spirulida is an order of coleoid cephalopods within the subclass Decapodiformes, characterized by small, deep-sea species possessing a unique, spirally coiled, internal calcareous shell divided into numerous gas-filled chambers that provide buoyancy control. The order encompasses a single extant species, Spirula spirula (the ram's horn squid), alongside several extinct taxa known from the fossil record, with the lineage tracing back to at least the early Miocene for the modern genus, though broader coleoid relatives extend to the Mesozoic. These cephalopods are adapted to mesopelagic and bathypelagic environments, featuring bioluminescent organs, vertical migration behaviors, and a pelagic lifestyle that distinguishes them from more benthic relatives like cuttlefish. In situ observations, first captured in 2020 off Australia and again in April 2025 in the tropical Atlantic at depths of 837860 m by the Schmidt Ocean Institute, confirm its mesopelagic lifestyle and head-down orientation.
Taxonomically, Spirulida is classified under the family Spirulidae (Owen, 1836), with Spirula Lamarck, 1799 as the sole living genus; synonyms such as Lituus Gray, 1847 are now considered junior to Spirula. Phylogenetic analyses place Spirulida as a distinct lineage within Decapodiformes, potentially sister to the oceanic squids (Oegopsida) or more closely allied with sepioids, supporting convergent evolution of traits like autogenic photophores in association with pelagic habitats. The internal shell, unlike the flat cuttlebone of Sepiida, consists of 2537 chambers and is fully enclosed within the mantle, contributing to the organism's characteristic "head-down" orientation during swimming. Adults typically reach a mantle length of 3045 mm (females up to 60 mm), with reddish-brown coloration, eight arms, two tentacles armed with suckers, and small, flap-like fins positioned dorsolaterally.
Spirula spirula inhabits tropical and subtropical oceanic waters worldwide, excluding polar regions, at depths ranging from 1001,000 m, with diel vertical migrationsdeeper (600700 m) by day and shallower (<300 m) at nightwhere water temperatures at 400 m exceed 10C. As nocturnal predators, individuals feed on small crustaceans and fish using their tentacles, while employing bioluminescence from a large photophore at the mantle's posterior for camouflage or communication in the deep sea. Reproduction is oviparous with internal fertilization, occurring year-round upon reaching sexual maturity at ~30 mm mantle length after 1215 months; the lifespan is approximately 1820 months, with eggs likely deposited on substrates near continental slopes and no parental care provided. Ecologically, Spirulida plays a role in midwater food webs as prey for larger predators like swordfish and whales, though it holds no commercial value and is occasionally taken as bycatch in trawl fisheries. The order's evolutionary history highlights adaptations to deep-sea niches, with molecular studies underscoring its basal position among decapodiforms and the independent evolution of buoyancy mechanisms across cephalopod lineages.

Description

Living Species

Spirula spirula, the sole extant species in the order Spirulida, is a small, deep-water cephalopod mollusk resembling a squid in overall form. Known commonly as the ram's horn squid or little post horn squid, it measures 35 to 45 mm in mantle length and possesses eight arms along with two longer tentacles, all lined with suckers for capturing prey; these appendages are fully retractable into the mantle for protection.
A defining feature of S. spirula is its unique internal shell, a planispiral calcareous structure coiled in a tight, ram's horn-like spiral that functions in buoyancy regulation through gas- or liquid-filled chambers. This shell consists of 2537 chambers. Additionally, the species bears a single large photophore located between the posterior fins on the mantle, which emits a pale yellowish-green light believed to facilitate counter-illumination in the dim mesopelagic environment.
The species has long been known primarily from beached specimens and washed-up shells, with its elusive nature limiting direct observations. The first in situ video footage of a living individual was captured in 2020 at depths near the northern Great Barrier Reef, revealing previously unobserved behaviors such as vertical orientation with the head upward.

Morphological Characteristics

Spirulida is characterized by a distinctive planispiral, chambered internal shell known as a phragmocone, which serves as the order's defining skeletal feature and enables gas and fluid regulation for buoyancy. This endoskeleton consists of a series of chambers separated by concave septa, with a siphuncle running through a ventral tube connecting the chambers; the shell wall features an outer fibrous aragonite layer, an inner prismatic aragonite layer, and a middle organic periostracum, while the septa exhibit a lamello-fibrillar microstructure rather than nacreous layering. Unlike the external shells of ammonites, the spirulid phragmocone is fully internalized within the mantle cavity, providing protection and support without external exposure.
The body plan of Spirulida follows a decapodiform structure typical of many coleoid cephalopods, featuring a mantle, head, and ten appendageseight shorter arms and two longer tentacles equipped with suckers for prey capture. This configuration resembles that of squids but differs from cuttlefish (Sepiida) in the complete internalization and coiling of the shell, which lacks the broad, calcified cuttlebone. The living species Spirula spirula exhibits a small, squid-like body measuring 3545 mm in mantle length, with smooth reddish-brown skin, large eyes lacking corneas, and tentacular clubs on the tentacles for grasping.
In living spirulids, the radula is absent or highly vestigial, contrasting with its presence in most other cephalopods, while prey capture relies primarily on the suckered tentacles and a strong beak. Spirula spirula possesses a single large photophore, a bioluminescent organ located at the posterior end of the mantle between the ear-shaped fins, capable of emitting pale yellowish-green light for hours; this structure is surrounded by smaller accessory organs and aids in counterillumination or predator deterrence in deep-water environments.

Classification

Taxonomic History

The order Spirulida was established by Stolley in 1919 as part of the Decapodiformes, based on fossil phragmocones exhibiting a tightly coiled internal shell morphology akin to that of the extant genus Spirula. Early 20th-century classifications frequently positioned Spirulida within the broader Sepioidea due to superficial shell resemblances with cuttlefish, a view explored by Chun (1914) and elaborated by Naef (19211923) through comparative anatomy of Recent and fossil forms.
Throughout the 20th century, significant debates arose concerning Spirulida's phylogenetic ties to belemnites and aulacocerids, with hypotheses suggesting derivation from a belemnoid or diplobelid ancestor based on shared phragmocone and proostracum features in Cretaceous fossils like Groenlandibelus and Cyrtobelus. Proposed Carboniferous origins were particularly contentious, centered on Shimanskya postremus (originally described as Bactrites postremus by Miller in 1930), which Doguzhaeva et al. (1999) reinterpreted as the earliest spirulid based on its prismatic, non-nacreous shell wall structure, though subsequent analyses have questioned this assignment due to ambiguous ultrastructural evidence.
Mid-20th-century revisions, notably by Jeletzky (1966), integrated inferences from preserved soft-part impressions in fossilssuch as mantle attachments and caecato differentiate Spirulida from belemnites and refine its distinction from Sepioidea, ultimately elevating it to an independent order within Decapodiformes.
21st-century molecular and phylogenetic analyses, exemplified by Fuchs et al. (2012), have bolstered the monophyly of Spirulida through cladistic evaluations of shell and soft-tissue characters in Late Cretaceous taxa, while ongoing debates persist over subordinal divisions such as the placement of early forms like Naefia. A pivotal milestone is the estimated divergence of Spirulida from Sepiida approximately 150 million years ago, derived from molecular clock calibrations using hemocyanin sequences.

Modern Taxonomy

Spirulida is classified in the kingdom Animalia, phylum Mollusca, class Cephalopoda, subclass Coleoidea, and superorder Decapodiformes. The order encompasses a diverse fossil record alongside a single extant lineage, reflecting its evolutionary history from the Carboniferous onward.
The order is divided into three suborders: two extinct and one that includes living forms. The extinct suborder Groenlandibelida (Khromov, 1990) comprises the families Groenlandibelidae (Jeletzky, 1966) and Adygeyidae (Doguzhaeva, 1996), known from Late Cretaceous deposits. The suborder Belopterina (Engeser, 1998) includes the families Belemnoseidae (Wiltshire, 1869) and Belopteridae (Owen, 1856), with genera such as Beloptera representing Cenozoic spirulirostrids characterized by sheath-formed pro-ostraca.
The suborder Spirulina (Pompeckj, 1912) contains both extinct and extant taxa, including the families Spirulirostridae (Naef, 1921), Spirulirostrinidae (Naef, 1921), and the living Spirulidae (Rafinesque, 1815). The family Spirulidae is monotypic, represented solely by the genus Spirula Lamarck, 1799, and its single species S. spirula (Linnaeus, 1758), the ram's horn squid. Despite this limited modern diversity, the order's fossil record features numerous extinct genera, such as the late Carboniferous Shimanskyaa contested early spirulid with a longiconic shell lacking a nacreous layerand others within Belopterina.

Distribution and Habitat

Geographic Range

Spirulida, represented solely by the living species Spirula spirula, exhibits a circumglobal but patchy distribution confined to tropical and subtropical oceanic waters, spanning latitudes approximately 40N to 40S. This range includes the tropical Atlantic Ocean, where specimens have been recorded near the Canary Islands and off the coasts of South Africa, as well as the Indo-Pacific region, with confirmed occurrences in the Arabian Sea and along the Great Barrier Reef. The species is notably absent from polar regions, reflecting its preference for warmer, mid-latitude seas.
Fossil records of extinct Spirulida taxa reveal a more restricted paleogeographic distribution, primarily within the ancient Tethyan realms during the Jurassic and Cretaceous periods. For instance, early representatives such as Kostromateuthis memeri are known from Upper Jurassic deposits in central Russia, part of the peri-Tethyan province. Later Cretaceous fossils, including unambiguous spirulids, also originate from Tethyan-influenced areas, marking the order's diversification in warm, epicontinental seas. In the Cenozoic, post-Cretaceous records expand slightly, with Miocene occurrences documented in New Zealand and various European localities.
The apparent cosmopolitan nature of S. spirula's modern distribution is partly facilitated by the buoyancy of its empty internal shells, which frequently wash ashore on beaches far beyond the species' core habitat. These lightweight, chambered shells have been reported on Pacific islands, Mediterranean coastlines, and even temperate shores like those in New Zealand and Texas, potentially exaggerating the perceived range through post-mortem dispersal by ocean currents.
Vertical migration patterns further influence the observed geographic range of living Spirulida, with individuals descending to depths of around 1,000 m during the day and ascending to 100300 m at night, allowing access to broader oceanic expanses while remaining within tropical-subtropical zones.

Environmental Preferences

Spirulida, represented solely by the living species Spirula spirula, primarily inhabits the mesopelagic zone of tropical and subtropical oceanic waters, typically at depths of 5001,000 m during the day, where it avoids surface waters except in rare instances. This depth preference aligns with the species' occurrence in mid-water open ocean environments, often associated with warmer oceanic currents near islands and continental shelves. The optimal temperature range for S. spirula centers around 10C, with recorded values spanning 4.711.6C (mean 7.1C) in these habitats, reflecting adaptation to stable, cool deep-sea conditions.
Fossil records of Spirulida, spanning the Jurassic to Miocene, are preserved in offshore marine sedimentary deposits, indicating historical preferences for deep-water settings similar to those of modern S. spirula. For instance, phragmocones attributed to Spirula have been recovered from Lower Miocene strata in New Zealand, consistent with depositional environments of ancient open marine basins. Isotopic analyses of fossil and recent shells further support ontogenetic shifts paralleling deep-water origins, with early life stages inferred in cool, profundal conditions.
Due to their deep-sea distribution, Spirulida face minimal direct human impacts such as fishing or pollution. However, ocean acidification poses a potential threat by undermining the aragonitic integrity of the internal shell, as observed in related coleoid cephalopods where elevated pCO reduces calcification efficiency.

Biology

Anatomy

Spirula spirula, the sole living species in Spirulida, possesses a centralized nervous system typical of advanced cephalopods, with a brain that is notably large relative to its body size, enabling complex sensory processing in the deep-sea environment. The optic lobes are particularly well-developed, featuring intricate layering that supports enhanced vision in low-light conditions, as evidenced by comparative neuroanatomical studies across cephalopod taxa.
The digestive system includes a chitinous beak adapted for crushing prey, paired with a short, efficient gut that facilitates rapid processing of ingested material. Lacking a radula, as in some other deep-water cephalopods, S. spirula relies on this beak to handle its diet, primarily consisting of small crustaceans, fish, and other zooplankton. Nutrient absorption occurs mainly in the caecum and associated digestive gland, optimizing energy extraction in nutrient-scarce mesopelagic depths.
Circulation in S. spirula follows the closed system common to cephalopods, employing three heartstwo auricular hearts for gill oxygenation and a single systemic heart for body distributionalong with haemocyanin-based oxygen transport. The single pair of gills exhibits countercurrent exchange adapted for efficient oxygen uptake in low-oxygen deep waters, supporting sustained activity at depths exceeding 200 meters.
An ink sac is present, functioning as a muscular reservoir of melanin that ejects defensive clouds through the anus via funnel propulsion, integrated with the digestive tract. Photophores, embedded in the mantle skin, enable bioluminescent emission, with their activity under direct nervous control from the central brain, allowing coordinated light patterns for camouflage or communication in the dark ocean.
Fossil evidence for Spirulida soft anatomy is limited, as internal organs are rarely preserved, but the presence of rostra and guards in extinct taxa like those in related belemnoid lineages suggests analogous internal configurations, with chambered shells implying similar circulatory and digestive accommodations around the phragmocone.

Behavior and Physiology

Spirula spirula exhibits diel vertical migration, descending to depths of 5501000 m during the day and ascending to 100300 m at night to facilitate feeding in shallower waters while avoiding predators in deeper zones. This behavior is supported by its unique internal chambered shell, which enables precise buoyancy control through the exchange of gas and fluid between chambers via a siphuncular connection, allowing the squid to adjust its density without active swimming for prolonged periods. In situ observations reveal that the squid maintains near-neutral buoyancy, often remaining motionless with head upward and using fin beats at 2.53 times per second for stability during these migrations.
The species employs bioluminescence via large photophores on the mantle, emitting pale yellowish-green light that likely serves for counter-illumination to match the downwelling light from the surface, thereby reducing its silhouette visibility to predators below. This adaptation is particularly effective in the dim mesopelagic environment, where the light emission can vary in hue with temperature changes at different depths.
As an ambush predator, Spirula spirula primarily feeds on small crustaceans, fish, and other zooplankton, using its tentacles to capture prey while hovering motionless in the water column. Its diet reflects a low trophic position of approximately 2.5, indicating reliance on primary consumers such as small crustaceans and fish rather than higher-level predation. Evidence of occasional surface vulnerability comes from its presence in the stomachs of seabirds such as grey-headed albatrosses, suggesting that upward migrations may expose it to avian predators.
Sensory adaptations include large, protruding eyes suited for detecting faint light in the deep sea, enabling effective navigation and prey detection during low-light conditions.
Physiologically, Spirula spirula demonstrates osmoregulation suited to the stable but varying salinities of mesopelagic waters, maintaining internal fluid balance as an osmoconformer typical of marine cephalopods. Its slow metabolism, adapted to the cold temperatures of deep waters, supports energy conservation during extended periods of inactivity and migration, with inferred low oxygen consumption rates consistent with its torpid lifestyle.

Fossil Record

Geological Timeline

The fossil record of Spirulida begins with a contested earliest occurrence in the Late Carboniferous Gzhelian stage, approximately 300 million years ago, represented by the genus Shimanskya from deposits in the southern Mid-Continent of the United States. This tentative assignment stems from similarities in shell wall microstructure to later spirulids, though its placement within the order remains debated due to the primitive nature of the specimen.
Uncontested records of Spirulida originate in the Late Jurassic, around 160 million years ago, with the first unambiguous fossils described from Upper Jurassic sediments in central Russia, including a rare coleoid assigned to the order based on shell morphology and ultrastructure. Diversification accelerated during the Cretaceous, particularly in the Late Cretaceous, as evidenced by multiple genera preserved in marine deposits from Greenland and Canada, marking the expansion of spirulids within decapodiform cephalopods.
Peak diversity occurred from the Eocene to Miocene epochs, with widespread stratigraphic occurrences across the globe, including Europe, New Zealand, and the Czech Republic. Notable among these is the oldest known spirulid phragmocone from Eocene sediments in southern Moravia, Czech Republic, representing an early form with a markedly endogastrically coiled internal shell. Additional records from the Lower Miocene of New Zealand further illustrate this period of abundance, with phragmocones closely resembling those of the modern genus.
Post-Miocene, spirulid fossils become exceedingly rare, reflecting a sharp decline in diversity that culminated in the order's current monotypic status, represented solely by the extant Spirula spirula. Preservation in the geological record is dominated by phragmocones and calcified shells embedded in marine sedimentary rocks, with no known documentation of soft body parts.

Extinct Taxa

The order Spirulida encompasses a diverse array of extinct genera, with over a dozen recognized across its fossil record, in stark contrast to the single extant species Spirula spirula. These extinct forms span from the Carboniferous to the Miocene, exhibiting a range of phragmocone morphologies from straight (orthoconic) to weakly coiled, often with specialized sheaths or rostra that distinguish them from modern spirulids.
Early representatives include Shimanskya postremus from the late Carboniferous of the southern Mid-Continent, USA, recognized as the oldest known spirulid. This taxon features a longiconic shell with a prismatic wall structure lacking a nacreous layer, a marginal siphuncle, and chamber proportions indicative of an internal shell secreted by coleoid cephalopods, forming the basis of the family Shimanskyidae.
The suborder Groenlandibelina, comprising families such as Groenlandibelidae, is characterized by elongated phragmocones with thin investment-like sheaths and rod-like proostraca. Key genera include Groenlandibelus rosenkrantzi from the upper Maastrichtian of Greenland, which possesses a longiconic phragmocone (apical angle 1415), chamber length-to-diameter ratios of 0.370.47, and an ovoid protoconch approximately 1.5 mm in diameter. Another notable form is Cyrtobelus birkelundae from the upper Maastrichtian of Greenland and C. hornbyense from the upper Campanian of Vancouver Island, Canada, both exhibiting cyrtoconic phragmocones (apical angle 1720), low chamber ratios (0.270.33), and small protoconchs (0.40.5 mm), with nacre-less conothecae and siphuncles less than 10% of chamber diameter.
The suborder Belopterina includes genera allied to Belopteridae, featuring straight or slightly curved guards and phragmocones adapted for deeper-water habitats. Representative taxa such as Beloptera broilii and Belopterina neumaieri from the Paleocene (Thanetian) of Austria display weakly curved phragmocones with low chamber ratios (~0.25), dorsal attachment scars, and sheaths bearing weak ventral ridges, preserved in inner shelf deposits at depths of 50150 m. These forms extend into the Eocene with genera like Belosepia, marked by less inclined septa and prong-like spines. Earlier Jurassic-Cretaceous belopterin-like structures are evident in related decabrachians, highlighting a transition toward more enrolled shells in later spirulids.
Fossils attributable to the genus Spirula appear in the Miocene, demonstrating morphological continuity with the modern species. A notable specimen is a six-chambered phragmocone segment from lower Miocene rocks at Kaipara Harbour, northern New Zealand, representing the first confirmed fossil record of the genus and indicating persistence of the tightly coiled, chambered internal shell characteristic of extant S. spirula.

Evolutionary Relationships

Phylogenetic Position

Spirulida is positioned within the subclass Coleoidea of Cephalopoda, specifically as part of the superorder Decapodiformes, where it represents a distinct order characterized by its unique internal chambered shell. The monophyly of Spirulida is supported by morphological synapomorphies, including the planispiral phragmoconea coiled, chambered structure reminiscent of ancestral cephalopod shellsand the decapod arm arrangement typical of Decapodiformes, featuring eight arms and two longer tentacles equipped with suckers. These features distinguish Spirulida from other decapodiform lineages while aligning it with the broader group that diverged from Octopodiformes, the sister clade encompassing octopuses and vampire squids.
Older molecular clock analyses suggested that Spirulida forms a sister group to Sepiida (cuttlefishes) within Decapodiformes, with their divergence estimated at approximately 150 30 million years ago (Late Jurassic to Early Cretaceous) based on hemocyanin sequences. However, recent phylogenomic studies have challenged this exact sister relationship, recovering Spirulida as more closely allied to Oegopsida (oceanic squids), underscoring ongoing debates in decapodiform evolution. This placement highlights Spirulida as a basal coleoid lineage within Decapodiformes, retaining primitive traits that position it closer to extinct belemnites than to modern squids (Teuthida), particularly evident in the shell microstructure, which preserves a narrow marginal siphuncle and globular protoconch indicative of early coleoid morphology. A 2017 mitochondrial genome analysis further supports its basal position among decapodiforms. Bayesian phylogenetic reconstructions of early cephalopod fossils (as of 2022) reinforce origins from belemnoid-like ancestors in the Mesozoic.
Phylogenetic challenges arise from the incomplete preservation of soft parts in fossil Spirulida, limiting morphological comparisons, while molecular data derived from the single extant species, Spirula spirula, confirm its deep-sea adaptations through hemocyanin sequences and mitochondrial genomes, revealing adaptations like reduced metabolic rates suited to mesopelagic environments. In cladograms from combined morphological and molecular analyses, Spirulida typically branches after the Octopodiformes divergence but before Idiosepida in some reconstructions, positioning it as an early-diverging decapodiform lineage that bridges benthic and pelagic forms. These analyses emphasize the need for additional genomic sampling to resolve conflicts between morphological retention of ancestral traits and molecular signals of rapid diversification.

Relation to Other Cephalopods

Spirulida shares an internal chambered shell with the extinct order Belemnitida (belemnites), but differs in coiling and structure; while belemnites possessed a straight or slightly curved phragmocone often guarded by a robust external rostrum for protection and buoyancy, the modern spirulid Spirula spirula features a fully internal, planispiral-coiled shell that is more compact and lacks any rostrum. This coiling allows for enhanced maneuverability in deep-sea environments, contrasting with the belemnites' more rigid, bullet-like form adapted for faster swimming in shallower Mesozoic seas. Similarly, Spirulida exhibits morphological affinities to the extinct Aulacocerida, another group of early coleoids with internal phragmocones, though aulacocerids had less pronounced coiling and aragonitic rostra, suggesting Spirulida as a potential descendant or close relative through transitional forms like diplobelids.
In comparison to Sepiida (cuttlefish), Spirulida displays a similar decapodiform body plan with ten arms and an internal calcareous shell for buoyancy control, but the spirulid shell is a tightly coiled, chambered spiral fully embedded within the mantle, whereas the sepiid cuttlebone is a broad, porous, flattened structure with stacked chambers that provides both support and gas regulation. This difference reflects divergent adaptations: cuttlefish favor near-shore, benthic lifestyles where the cuttlebone aids in precise depth adjustment, while spirulids are obligate deep-water dwellers relying on the spiral shell's gas-filled chambers for neutral buoyancy at mesopelagic depths.
Relative to Teuthida (squids), both orders occupy deep-sea niches, but Teuthida lack any chambered shell, instead possessing a flexible, chitinous gladius remnant for muscle attachment, highlighting a key divergence in skeletal reduction. Bioluminescence appears convergently in both, with S. spirula emitting pale green light from a posterior photophore for counter-illumination or signaling, akin to the autogenic photophores in many oegopsid squids, though this trait likely evolved independently to counter predation in low-light environments.
Phylogenetically, Spirulida likely descends from aulacocerid-like ancestors within the broader Belemnoidea, forming a clade with belemnites that branches basal to modern Decapodiformes, with no direct relation to Octopodiformes (including octopuses). The planispiral coiling of the spirulid shell represents a unique trait among extant decapodiforms, absent in the linear gladii of squids or the lamellar cuttlebones of cuttlefish, underscoring Spirulida's relictual status as a "living fossil" bridging extinct and modern coleoid forms.
