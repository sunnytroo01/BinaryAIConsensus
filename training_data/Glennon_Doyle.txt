 Glennon Doyle (born March 20, 1976) is an American author, speaker, and activist whose writings center on personal recovery from eating disorders and substance abuse, family life, and advocacy for self-determination.
Her breakthrough memoir Carry On, Warrior (2013) recounts her battles with bulimia starting at age ten and alcoholism in adolescence, which led to psychiatric hospitalization during high school; she achieved sobriety at 26 and later became a teacher before launching the Momastery blog.
Doyle married Craig Melton in 2002, with whom she had three children, and documented their reconciliation after his infidelity in the Oprah Book Club selection Love Warrior (2016); the couple separated later that year, after which she entered a relationship with and married soccer player Abby Wambach in 2017.
Her 2020 book Untamed, emphasizing rejection of conventional roles for individual authenticity, became a #1 New York Times bestseller with over three million copies sold, establishing her as a prominent voice in women's self-empowerment literature.
Doyle co-hosts the podcast We Can Do Hard Things with Wambach and her sisters, and founded Together Rising, a nonprofit aiding women and children in crisis.
Notable controversies include critiques of inconsistency between Love Warrior's marital repair narrative and her subsequent divorce and same-sex marriage, prompting questions about the sincerity of her earlier commitments, as well as perceptions of her advice as reflective of privilege rather than universal applicability.

Early Life and Education

Childhood and Family Background

Glennon Doyle was born on March 20, 1976, in Burke, Virginia, an affluent suburb in Fairfax County.
She grew up in a moderately observant Catholic household with her parents, Richard "Bubba" Doyle, an Army veteran who worked as a middle school principal and high school football coach, and Patricia Doyle, a high school guidance counselor noted for her nurturing demeanor.
Her parents emphasized a structured, idyllic family life, including annual vacations aboard a 29-foot sailboat and regular attendance at her father's Friday night football games, fostering a sense of community and tradition.
Doyle has one younger sister, Amanda Doyle, with whom she maintains a close relationship, often referring to her as "Sister" in public discussions.

Personal Struggles with Addiction and Eating Disorders

Glennon Doyle developed bulimia at age 10, prompted by cultural pressures on girls' bodies, which she later described as an early response to societal expectations. By her senior year of high school, her struggles had expanded to include addiction to alcohol and drugs, marking a progression from eating disorder behaviors to substance use as coping mechanisms.
In college, Doyle's substance abuse intensified; she reported regularly drinking to the point of blacking out and using cocaine, behaviors that continued post-graduation amid repeated failed attempts at self-control. These patterns, intertwined with her ongoing bulimia, formed a cycle of self-destructive habits she attributed to underlying emotional pain rather than isolated vices. Doyle has characterized overeating, drinking, and drugging not as primary problems but as flawed solutions to deeper issues like clinical depression and anxiety.
After approximately two decades of recovery from bulimia, Doyle experienced a relapse during the 2021 holiday season, which she publicly disclosed on her podcast in May 2022, emphasizing the difficulty of maintaining long-term abstinence amid life stressors. In January 2023, she revealed a formal diagnosis of anorexia nervosa, shifting her self-understanding from a history of bulimia to a more restrictive eating disorder, with symptoms overlapping prior behaviors but requiring adjusted recovery approaches. This diagnosis highlighted diagnostic complexities in eating disorders, where patients often exhibit mixed symptoms not fitting neat categories. Doyle has maintained that body and food-related issues persisted throughout her life, influencing her advocacy on mental health.

Recovery and Sobriety

Doyle achieved sobriety from alcohol addiction in 2002, at age 26, shortly after the birth of her first child, Chase. She has attributed the decision to her sister's intervention, who accompanied her to her first recovery meeting on the sixth day of sobriety, where Doyle began confronting her dependencies through structured support. This marked the end of her active substance use, which she later described as intertwined with broader patterns of avoidance and emotional numbing.
Concurrently, sobriety coincided with cessation of bulimic behaviorsbinging and purgingthat had persisted from age 10 through her mid-20s, totaling over 16 years of severe episodes. Doyle has framed this dual recovery as a foundational pivot, enabled by participation in 12-step programs emphasizing accountability and community, though she has emphasized personal agency over any singular methodology. For approximately two decades following 2002, she maintained abstinence from alcohol and reported stability in eating patterns, integrating these experiences into her writing on resilience and self-awareness.
Challenges resurfaced in late 2021, when Doyle experienced a relapse into disordered eating over the holiday period, involving restriction and purging after 20 years of relative remission. In seeking renewed treatment for what she identified as bulimia in mid-2022, clinicians diagnosed her with anorexia nervosa, highlighting underlying restrictive tendencies rather than solely binge-purge cycles. She entered recovery for this condition, publicly disclosing the diagnosis in early 2023 via her podcast We Can Do Hard Things, underscoring the diagnostic evolution and ongoing management required despite prior milestones. No evidence indicates relapse in alcohol sobriety post-2002; Doyle continues to advocate total abstinence as essential for her sustained functionality.

Formal Education

Glennon Doyle attended James Madison University in Harrisonburg, Virginia, where she was a member of the Delta Rho chapter of Sigma Kappa sorority. She earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in English from the university in 1999. There is no record of her pursuing postgraduate education.

Career Development

Emergence as a Blogger

Glennon Doyle, then writing under the name Glennon Doyle Melton, began her blogging career in 2009 with the launch of Momastery, a personal platform where she documented her experiences as a mother of three, her Christian faith, and her recovery from bulimia and alcohol addiction. Prompted by a friend's suggestion during a period of introspection, Doyle followed an online tutorial to set up the site and committed to early-morning writing sessions starting at 4:30 a.m., before her children awoke, to process and share raw accounts of her "brutiful" life a term she coined blending "brutal" and "beautiful." Her posts focused on themes of imperfection, grace, and redemption, rejecting polished narratives in favor of confessional essays about marital strains, parenting failures, and spiritual doubts, which contrasted with prevailing ideals of domestic perfection in evangelical circles.
The blog's appeal stemmed from Doyle's unvarnished style, which fostered a sense of communal vulnerability among readers, primarily women navigating similar tensions between personal flaws and cultural demands for flawlessness. By offering empathetic, faith-infused reflections without prescriptive moralizing, Momastery built a dedicated following that viewed Doyle as an authentic voice amid the often performative genre of mommy blogging. This organic growth positioned her as a key figure in the early 2010s Christian online community, where her emphasis on honest storytelling over idealized piety differentiated her from contemporaries and laid the groundwork for broader influence, though some critics later noted the platform's evolution reflected selective self-presentation rather than exhaustive transparency.

Transition to Authorship

Doyle's blog, Momastery, launched in 2009, featured raw essays on her experiences with motherhood, faith, addiction recovery, and relational dynamics, attracting a dedicated readership through its vulnerable, confessional style. The site's viral growth, driven by relatable content on imperfect living, prompted interest from publishers and culminated in a book contract with Scribner, an imprint of Simon & Schuster.
Her debut publication, Carry On, Warrior: Thoughts on Life Unarmed, appeared in hardcover on March 19, 2013. The memoir compiles revised selections from Momastery posts alongside new essays, exploring themes of self-acceptance, family resilience, and spiritual authenticity amid personal flaws. This shift from digital self-publishing to commercial authorship marked Doyle's entry into mainstream literary circles, leveraging her online platform's authenticity to appeal to audiences seeking unfiltered narratives on womanhood and recovery. The book's immediate commercial traction, including New York Times bestseller status, validated the transition by demonstrating demand for her voice beyond the blog format.

Major Publications and Themes

Glennon Doyle's major publications consist primarily of memoir-style books that compile and expand upon her earlier blog essays from Momastery, blending personal narrative with reflective advice on resilience, relationships, and self-acceptance. Her debut book, Carry On, Warrior: Thoughts on Life Unarmed, published in 2013 by Scribner, draws from her experiences with addiction recovery, motherhood, and faith, emphasizing vulnerability as a source of strength amid life's "brutiful" (brutal yet beautiful) challenges. The collection portrays recovery and honest living as ongoing battles, where authenticity and humor help navigate imperfections without pretense of perfection.
In 2016, Doyle released Love Warrior: A Memoir, selected for Oprah's Book Club, which chronicles her struggles with bulimia, alcoholism, and marital infidelity, framing healing through truth-telling and redefined intimacy rather than superficial fixes. Themes center on redemption via vulnerability, the deceptiveness of external appearances in relationships, and personal agency in confronting betrayal, culminating in a shift toward self-trust over relational dependence.
Untamed, published in March 2020 by The Dial Press, became her most commercially prominent work, selling over three million copies and topping the New York Times bestseller list. It explores Doyle's divorce from her husband, entry into a same-sex relationship with Abby Wambach, and broader critique of societal conditioning for women, advocating "wildness" through self-reliance, rejection of conformity, and prioritization of internal truth over external expectations. Recurring motifs include dismantling "cages" of performative roles in family and femininity, with essays urging readers to embrace individuality despite cultural pressures.
Accompanying Untamed is Get Untamed: The Journal (2021), a workbook for applying its principles through prompts on personal reflection. Doyle's oeuvre consistently themes empowerment via raw disclosure of flaws, evolving from Christian-inflected recovery narratives to secular calls for autonomy, though critics note the inspirational tone often prioritizes subjective experience over empirical universality.

The talk emphasized vulnerability over performative strength, positioning everyday authenticity as a form of courage rather than superheroic feats.
Doyle co-hosts the podcast We Can Do Hard Things with wife Abby Wambach and sister Amanda Doyle, launched on May 11, 2021, which has surpassed 500 million downloads by addressing personal and societal challenges through candid discussions. The program, produced under her Treat Media company, features episodes on topics like anxiety, exclusion, and activism, often with celebrity guests, and has positioned Doyle among The Hollywood Reporter's most powerful podcast influencers.

Philanthropy and Activism

Establishment of Together Rising

Together Rising, a nonprofit organization dedicated to grassroots philanthropy, was established in 2012 by author and activist Glennon Doyle alongside her sister Amanda Doyle. The initiative originated organically from Doyle's online blog community at Momastery.com, where readers collectively sponsored a family in need during a holiday season fundraiser, sparking broader mobilization efforts that evolved into a structured entity focused on aiding women, families, and children in crisis. 
The organization's founding principle centered on bridging donors motivated by shared heartbreak with recipients requiring urgent support, emphasizing collective action over individual charity. As an all-women-led venture, Together Rising pioneered time-limited crowdfunding campaigns termed "Love Flash Mobs" to address immediate community needs, such as family reunification and crisis intervention, thereby institutionalizing ad hoc blog-driven philanthropy into a formalized 501(c)(3) nonprofit. 
Doyle served as founder and president, guiding the volunteer board in its early operations without a dedicated executive director until 2023, which allowed for rapid, community-responsive scaling from informal origins to distributing millions in targeted aid. 

Fundraising Outcomes and Programs

Together Rising, founded by Glennon Doyle in 2012, raised a total of $55,118,582.16 over its 12-year operation, concluding with its sunset in 2024. This funding supported 11,713 direct grants to individuals and families in crisis, addressing immediate needs such as rent, utilities, medical bills, tuition, and school supplies. Additionally, the organization allocated resources to 298 partner organizations for broader initiatives, including refugee aid, disaster relief, and youth shelters. In its final year, Together Rising distributed $362,457.30 in grants directly to families.
The nonprofit's programs emphasized rapid, targeted philanthropy through two main channels: ongoing monthly giving for sustained urgent support and "Love Flash Mobs," time-bound campaigns mobilizing donors for specific crises. Monthly contributions funded everyday interventions, such as providing heat for foster families in Texas, breast cancer treatment for single mothers, or rent assistance for college students facing eviction.
Domestic programs focused on U.S.-based vulnerabilities, including family reunification for separated migrant children and opioid recovery. Notable Love Flash Mobs included $2 million raised in two days in June 2019 for the child detention crisis; $1.5 million (with $1 million in nine hours) in May 2018 for border-separated children; $350,000 in December 2017 for recovery homes for pregnant teens affected by opioids; and $560,000 in May 2016 for homeless youth.
Internationally, initiatives addressed refugee crises, natural disasters, and maternal health. Examples from Love Flash Mobs encompass $653,354.92 in January 2020 for Australian bushfire victims; $594,844 in 48 hours in April 2018 for Syrian aid (food, medical teams, hygiene kits); $1,168,311 in December 2016 for Syrian refugees (ambulances, mobile and pediatric hospitals); $1 million in 31 hours in December 2015 for European refugees; and $475,141 in 24 hours in October 2015 for a maternity wing in Haiti plus support for refugee mothers. In early 2024, over $1 million was directed to partners aiding Gaza amid conflict.
These efforts prioritized women and children, with 100% of tax-deductible donations allocated to programs after operational costs, reflecting Doyle's model of grassroots, women-led response to heartbreak-driven action. The organization's closure in 2024 followed a board decision to end operations after achieving substantial scale, having transformed donor contributionsmost frequently $25into direct crisis intervention.

Personal Life

First Marriage and Family Formation

Glennon Doyle met Craig Melton, a semi-professional soccer player and former model, in her mid-20s shortly after achieving sobriety from alcohol and drug addiction. Their initial relationship involved resuming substance use, as Doyle later recounted in her memoir Carry On, Warrior, but she recommitted to sobriety prior to marriage. The couple wed in 2002 and settled into family life, with Doyle embracing motherhood and Christian homemaking roles, including teaching Sunday school.
Doyle and Melton had three children together: a son named Chase, followed by daughters Tish (born 2006) and Amma (born 2008). Early family formation centered on Doyle's transition from personal struggles to domestic stability, which she chronicled through blogging on Momastery, sharing raw accounts of parenting, faith, and marital dynamics to connect with a growing online audience of mothers. This period marked Doyle's public persona as a relatable "mommy blogger," emphasizing vulnerability and redemption within a traditional family structure.

Divorce and Sexual Orientation Revelation

Glennon Doyle and Craig Melton, whom she married after meeting in 2001 and having their first child shortly thereafter, ended their 14-year marriage in 2016. The couple, parents to three childrendaughters Tish and Amma, and son Chasefaced challenges stemming from Melton's infidelity, which Doyle detailed in her 2016 memoir Love Warrior, framing it initially as a narrative of marital reconciliation through therapy and commitment.
Doyle announced their separation on her Momastery blog in August 2016, citing a disclosure from Melton during therapy as the catalyst, though she emphasized the marriage's transformative value rather than failure. This came months before Love Warrior's September release, shifting public perception of the book's themes from endurance to prelude for dissolution. Melton later described the divorce as a shock, attributing it partly to Doyle's subsequent self-realization regarding her attractions.
On November 13, 2016, Doyle publicly revealed her romantic relationship with former U.S. soccer player Abby Wambach via social media, stating, "I'm going to share with you my new love. Her name is Abby," accompanied by a photo of them holding hands. This disclosure, occurring shortly after both women's prior marriages ended, marked Doyle's first explicit acknowledgment of same-sex attraction, which she later characterized in memoirs like Untamed (2020) as an awakening to her authentic orientation after years of unexamined heterosexual assumptions. The rapid timelineseparation announcement in summer 2016 followed by the relationship reveal two months laterdrew scrutiny for its implications on familial stability, though Doyle maintained it reflected internal clarity rather than external impulse.

Second Marriage and Current Family Dynamics

Glennon Doyle married Abby Wambach, a retired U.S. women's national soccer team star and two-time Olympic gold medalist, on May 14, 2017, in a ceremony attended by family and close associates. The union followed Doyle's public announcement of their relationship in November 2016 and engagement in February 2017, marking a significant personal transition after her divorce.
The couple's family comprises Doyle's three children from her first marriagesons Chase and Henry, and daughter Tishwith Wambach acting as stepparent, often described by the pair as a "bonus parent." They maintain an amicable co-parenting arrangement with Doyle's ex-husband, Craig Melton, including collaborative efforts like jointly coaching one child's soccer team, which they have cited as a deliberate choice to prioritize the children's stability amid the family reconfiguration.
Doyle and Wambach have relocated their household multiple times since the marriage, including to Hermosa Beach, California, in 2021, as part of adapting to professional demands and family needs; Doyle has noted seven such moves in public discussions. In podcasts and interviews from 2023 to 2025, they portray their dynamics as centered on breaking intergenerational patterns, fostering emotional resilience in the children, and integrating Wambach's influence without displacing Melton's role, while both partners navigate sobriety and high-profile careers. As of 2025, the family remains intact, with Doyle and Wambach actively sharing insights on parenting challenges, such as managing adolescents' independence and community integration, through their joint podcast We Can Do Hard Things.

Critiques of Ideological Framing

Critics, particularly from Christian theological perspectives, have argued that Doyle's ideological framework in works like Untamed (2020) elevates subjective personal experience as the ultimate arbiter of truth, effectively rejecting objective moral standards derived from religious doctrine. For instance, reviewer Melissa Dougherty contends that Doyle's emphasis on following one's "knowing" or inner voice promotes a form of relativism where individual desires supersede biblical teachings on sin, self-denial, and communal ethics, framing traditional Christianity as a restrictive "cage" rather than a source of transcendent reality. Similarly, The Gospel Coalition's review describes this as a "counterfeit freedom" that liberates readers from concepts of right and wrong, positioning Doyle's philosophy as a secular gospel that undermines scriptural authority in favor of self-defined authenticity.
This framing has been critiqued for fostering extreme individualism, where personal liberation trumps familial and societal obligations. The Christian Research Institute highlights Doyle's contrast between "untamed" desires and Christian selflessness, arguing that her narrative encourages overturning religious, familial, and cultural orders to prioritize self-expression, potentially eroding structures that emphasize sacrifice and interdependence. Canadian Bible Guy echoes this, portraying Doyle's moral code as self-pleasing above all, which critics say inverts traditional ethical hierarchies by deeming conformity to external norms as oppression rather than a pathway to virtue.
Secular observers have similarly faulted Doyle's ideology for an insular individualism that neglects broader systemic contexts. In America magazine, Anna Keating notes that Doyle's portrayal of the nuclear or chosen family as a self-contained "island" reflects a "New Puritanism," isolating individuals from wider communal responsibilities and framing external critiques or traditions as threats to personal sovereignty, rather than opportunities for collective growth. Some leftist critiques further contend that this approach embodies "white feminism," prioritizing privileged women's self-actualization without sufficiently integrating racial or class-based inequities, thus reinforcing rather than dismantling hierarchical power dynamics.

Criticisms and Controversies

Challenges to Personal Authenticity

Doyle's 2016 memoir Love Warrior, selected for Oprah's Book Club and detailing her husband's 2013 infidelity confession alongside their subsequent reconciliation efforts, has faced scrutiny for misrepresenting the viability of her first marriage. Critics contend that the book's optimistic framing of marital perseverance was undermined when Doyle announced her separation from Craig Melton on March 24, 2017less than seven months after publicationand entered a relationship with Abby Wambach, whom she met at a December 2016 event. This timeline prompted accusations that Doyle knowingly promoted a narrative of heterosexual marital redemption for commercial gain, only to abandon it upon pursuing same-sex attraction, thereby eroding trust in her self-reported personal truths.
In her 2020 book Untamed, Doyle reflects on this period, describing her realization of attraction to women as emerging amid the marital strain, yet critics like author Cindy Robinson interpret her retrospective account as an admission that Love Warrior's reconciliation arc was "a total sham," suggesting she withheld her evolving sexual orientation during the earlier memoir's creation to sustain her image as a devoted wife and mother. Doyle has maintained that her feelings for Wambach crystallized post-separation, with no physical infidelity occurring while married, but skeptics highlight the proximity of eventsengagement to Wambach announced February 2017, marriage in May 2017as evidence of premeditation that conflicts with Love Warrior's emphasis on spousal loyalty and self-examination within the marriage. This perceived inconsistency has fueled broader doubts about the authenticity of her personal evolution narrative, particularly given her prior career as a Christian mommy blogger advocating traditional family values through platforms like Monkee See and Momastery.
Further challenges arise from Doyle's frequent invocation of "authenticity" and "truth-telling" in her writing and public persona, which some observers, including Substack writer Susannah PK, view as performative rather than substantive, especially when juxtaposed against the rapid pivot from evangelical roots to progressive queer advocacy. For instance, Untamedwhich sold over 4 million copies by 2023positions her divorce and remarriage as a triumphant embrace of inner truth, yet detractors argue this reframing conveniently aligns with market demands for inspirational stories of late-life sexual awakening, potentially at the expense of candor about earlier suppressions or ambiguities in her attractions during her 14-year marriage and childbearing years (20022016). Such critiques, often voiced in online forums and personal essays rather than mainstream outlets, underscore questions about whether Doyle's disclosures prioritize narrative coherence and empowerment messaging over unvarnished causal accounts of her relational history.

Societal and Familial Consequences Promoted

Doyle advocates for redefining family structures through divorce when individuals pursue personal authenticity, asserting that maintaining traditional marital forms to prevent "brokenness" is a flawed societal dictate. In Untamed (2020), she describes her own divorce from husband Craig Meltonafter 14 years and three childrenas a necessary evolution rather than dissolution, stating she "burned the memo insisting that the way a family avoids brokenness is to keep its structure by any means necessary." This perspective frames separation as liberating, prioritizing parental self-actualization and modeling "bravery" for children over preserving intact households, even amid her prior public commitments to marital reconciliation via therapy.
Critics contend that such messaging promotes familial instability by elevating individual desires above spousal vows and children's needs for consistent two-parent environments. For instance, commentators argue Doyle's narrative recasts breakups as inherent opportunities, implying "the pain of disappointing the people you love instead of yourself is always worth it," which may encourage readers to dissolve marriages preemptively upon encountering dissatisfaction or new attractions. With Untamed selling over three million copies, this influence is said to amplify a cultural shift toward self-prioritization, potentially normalizing divorce initiationdisproportionately by women in heterosexual unionsfor subjective "truths," at the expense of co-parenting partners left to navigate emotional and logistical fallout.
Empirical data underscores the familial risks Doyle's approach is criticized for endorsing: children of divorced parents face elevated odds of mental health disorders, including anxiety, depression, and suicidal ideation, alongside higher poverty rates (28% vs. 19% in intact families) and intergenerational instability. Critics from Christian and traditionalist viewpoints further argue this fosters a "self-centered gospel" antithetical to sacrificial relational commitments, contributing to broader societal erosion of marriage as a stabilizing institution amid declining union rates and rising single-parent households. Such promotion, they claim, overlooks causal links between parental disruption and offspring outcomes, favoring anecdotal empowerment over evidence-based family preservation.

Responses to Detractors and Platform Exits

Glennon Doyle has characterized criticism as an inevitable consequence of public visibility, particularly for women, stating in a 2020 social media post that "backlash will be swift and fierce and painful" for those who "dare to show up in the world." She advocates dismissing non-constructive detractors by categorizing their feedback as "junk" intended to silence rather than improve, as outlined in a 2022 Oprah Daily article where she described early encounters with online vitriol labeling her as "too flighty, too crazy, too ugly." In response to queries about inconsistencies between her pre- and post-"Untamed" writingswhere critics alleged prior works were dishonest given her evolving personal disclosuresDoyle emphasized her commitment to ongoing honesty, arguing that personal growth invalidates neither past nor present truths.
Following her 2017 public support for same-sex marriage alongside figures like Jen Hatmaker, Doyle reported being "shunned and mocked" by elements of the evangelical "Christian machine," framing such exclusion as a rejection of inclusive positions rather than a substantive critique of her views. She has not directly rebutted broader detractor claims of self-victimization or privilege in her narratives, such as those portraying her family transitions as uniquely virtuous amid ordinary challenges, instead redirecting focus to broader themes of authenticity and quitting unfulfilling roles.
In early 2025, Doyle exited mainstream social media platforms, including Instagram, citing profound mental health benefits comparable to her prior sobriety journey, which reduced anxiety over public reactions and enabled freer artistic expression. She briefly migrated to Substack in April 2025 to engage subscribers directly, but departed within weeks amid escalated criticism from existing users who viewed her arrivaland rapid subscriber influxas disruptive "gentrification" of the platform's culture, amplifying prior hate directed at her self-help framing and personal story. This exit, described in May 2025 commentary as potentially influenced by bullying, aligned with her pattern of withdrawing from environments where detractor volume overwhelmed constructive dialogue.
