Soil moisture
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Soil moisture, also known as soil water content, refers to the amount of water contained within the unsaturated zone of soil above the groundwater table, typically quantified as the volumetric water contentthe ratio of the volume of water to the total volume of soil (including solids, water, and air), often expressed as a percentage. Surface soil moisture pertains to the top 510 cm of soil, where it directly influences evaporation and plant uptake, while root-zone soil moisture extends deeper (up to 12 m) to support vegetation growth.
This parameter is fundamental to numerous Earth system processes, serving as a key integrator of precipitation, evaporation, and transpiration. In agriculture, soil moisture governs crop water availability, nutrient transport, and overall productivity, with deficits leading to reduced yields and excesses causing nutrient leaching or root damage. Hydrologically, it regulates the partitioning of rainfall into infiltration versus surface runoff, modulates groundwater recharge, and affects flood and drought dynamics. In the climate system, soil moisture drives land-atmosphere feedbacks by controlling surface energy fluxes, evapotranspiration rates, and regional temperature extremes, making it essential for weather forecasting and climate modeling. Its variability, influenced by soil texture, vegetation cover, topography, and climate, underscores its role in ecosystem resilience and resource management worldwide.

Fundamentals

Definition and Importance

Soil moisture refers to the water content held within the pores of soil, which can be quantified in two primary ways: volumetrically, as the ratio of the volume of water to the total volume of soil (denoted as , in units of m water per m soil), or gravimetrically, as the mass of water per unit mass of dry soil, often expressed as a percentage. This water exists in various forms, from tightly bound to more mobile states, influencing the soil's physical and chemical properties.
Soil moisture plays a critical role in soil formation by facilitating chemical weathering and physical breakdown processes that contribute to soil development over time. It enables nutrient transport through the soil matrix, allowing essential elements to dissolve and move to plant roots, while also supporting plant growth by providing the water necessary for physiological processes such as photosynthesis and transpiration. In agriculture, soil moisture levels guide irrigation decisions to optimize crop yields and prevent water stress, whereas in hydrology, it determines rates of infiltration, runoff, and evaporation, thereby regulating the water cycle. Furthermore, as an Essential Climate Variable recognized by the World Meteorological Organization, soil moisture mediates land-atmosphere interactions, influencing weather patterns, drought propagation, and regional climate dynamics.
The significance of soil moisture was first systematically explored in 19th-century soil science, notably by Eugene W. Hilgard, who in the 1890s linked variations in soil water content to crop yields and irrigation efficiency in arid regions. On a global scale, inadequate soil moisture contributes to land degradation, which affects up to 40% of the world's land, exacerbating droughts and posing severe threats to food security by reducing agricultural productivity. This ties directly to United Nations Sustainable Development Goals, particularly Goal 6 on clean water and sanitation through sustainable water management, and Goal 15 on life on land by addressing soil degradation and ecosystem resilience.

Key Moisture Concepts

Field capacity represents the maximum amount of water that soil can retain against the force of gravity after excess water has drained away, typically measured at a soil water suction of -0.33 bar (-33 kPa). This state occurs 1-2 days after saturation, when drainage has largely ceased, and the water is held primarily in smaller pores by capillary forces. Volumetric water content at field capacity varies by soil texture; for example, loams typically hold 0.25-0.35 m/m, while sands hold less and clays more due to differences in pore size distribution.
The permanent wilting point is the minimum soil moisture level at which plants can no longer extract sufficient water to prevent wilting, even when the soil is shaded and temperatures are favorable for recovery; this occurs at approximately -15 bar suction. Below this threshold, water is held too tightly by soil particles for most plant roots to access it. Volumetric contents at the permanent wilting point range from 0.05-0.10 m/m in sands to 0.15-0.20 m/m in clays, reflecting greater retention in finer-textured soils.
Available water capacity, often abbreviated as AWC, is the portion of soil water that plants can readily use, calculated as the difference between field capacity and the permanent wilting point. In medium-textured soils like loams, AWC typically ranges from 0.10-0.20 m/m, providing a buffer for plant growth during dry periods. Water below the permanent wilting point is considered unavailable to plants, as it remains bound to soil particles despite its presence. These thresholds are crucial for determining water availability, directly influencing plant physiological processes such as transpiration and nutrient uptake.
Soil saturation occurs when all pores are completely filled with water, corresponding to a suction of 0 bar, allowing free drainage under gravity. At this point, air is displaced from the soil matrix, and if saturation persists, it can lead to anaerobic conditions that inhibit root respiration and promote denitrification.
Soil moisture deficit refers to the shortfall between current soil water content and field capacity, while surplus indicates excess water beyond the soil's storage capacity, often leading to runoff or percolation. These concepts are integral to crop models, such as the Thornthwaite-Mather soil-water-balance approach, where they help simulate water supply-demand dynamics to predict yields and irrigation needs.

Measurement and Monitoring

In Situ Techniques

In situ techniques for measuring soil moisture involve direct, ground-based methods that provide localized data at specific depths and sites, offering high accuracy for point-scale assessments but requiring physical access to the soil. These approaches contrast with remote sensing by enabling precise, on-site quantification of water content or potential, essential for calibration of broader monitoring systems and detailed hydrological studies. Common methods include sampling-based and probe-based sensors, each suited to different soil conditions and measurement needs, though they often demand site-specific calibration to account for soil texture, density, and salinity.
The gravimetric method serves as the standard reference technique for determining soil water content by direct sampling. It involves collecting undisturbed soil cores or subsamples from desired depths, weighing them to obtain the wet mass, oven-drying at approximately 105C for 24 hours to remove water, and reweighing to find the dry mass. The gravimetric water content is then calculated as the ratio of water mass loss to dry soil mass, expressed as a percentage, which can be converted to volumetric water content using the soil's bulk density for comparability with other methods. This destructive approach is labor-intensive, time-consuming due to drying periods, and unsuitable for frequent monitoring, yet it remains the benchmark for validating sensor-based techniques owing to its absolute accuracy without calibration needs.
Tensiometers provide a direct measure of soil water matric potential, which indicates the energy status of water in the soil and guides irrigation decisions. These devices consist of a sealed tube filled with water, connected to a porous ceramic cup inserted into the soil at a specific depth; as soil dries, water moves out of the cup into the surrounding matrix, creating a partial vacuum that is measured via a manometer or pressure transducer. Standard tensiometers operate effectively in the wet to moderately moist range, from 0 to -80 centibars (approximately -0.1 to -0.8 bar), beyond which cavitation limits readings unless using specialized vacuum-regulating designs. They are widely used for real-time irrigation scheduling in agriculture, as lower potentials signal increasing plant stress, though they require periodic maintenance to refill and de-air the system.
Neutron moisture meters estimate volumetric soil water content through neutron thermalization, a non-destructive probe method suitable for deeper profiles. The device emits fast neutrons from a radioactive source (typically americium-beryllium) into the soil via an access tube; these neutrons slow down upon colliding with hydrogen atoms in water molecules, and the returning thermal neutrons are counted to infer water volume. Measurements can reach depths of 1-2 meters with access tubes, providing integrated values over a spherical volume of about 30 cm radius, but accuracy depends on soil-specific calibration to correct for variations in clay content, organic matter, and bulk density, typically achieving 0.02 m/m precision. This technique is valuable for long-term field monitoring in research and agronomy, though its use of radioactive materials necessitates licensing and safety protocols.
Time-domain reflectometry (TDR) measures soil dielectric permittivity by sending electromagnetic pulses along parallel metal rods inserted into the soil, offering rapid, non-destructive volumetric water content estimates. The travel time of the reflected pulse correlates with the soil's dielectric constant, which is dominated by water's high permittivity (around 80) compared to soil solids (3-5) and air (1); empirical models, such as Topp's equation, convert this to water content without soil-specific calibration in many cases. TDR sensors achieve high accuracy of 0.02 m/m across a wide range (0-0.6 m/m), support real-time data logging, and are robust in various soils, though performance can degrade in highly saline or air-gapped conditions. Developed in the 1980s, this method has become a staple for precision agriculture and vadose zone studies due to its minimal soil disturbance and automation potential.
The heat pulse method infers soil water content from transient thermal properties using needle-like sensors embedded in the soil. A heater probe emits a short heat pulse (typically 1-30 seconds), while adjacent thermistors monitor temperature changes; the heat dissipation rate, influenced by water's high specific heat capacity, allows calculation of thermal conductivity and diffusivity, from which volumetric water content is derived via models like those based on de Vries theory. This approach excels in lysimeters and profile arrays for simultaneous measurement of moisture, temperature, and even water flux, with accuracies around 0.01-0.03 m/m after calibration, and is particularly useful in heterogeneous soils or for studying water movement dynamics. Recent refinements, including dual-probe configurations, enhance resolution for near-surface applications.
Advances in wireless sensor networks since the 2010s have enabled continuous, automated in situ monitoring of soil moisture across fields using IoT-integrated devices. These networks deploy low-power nodes equipped with TDR, capacitance, or heat pulse sensors, communicating data via radio frequencies (e.g., ZigBee or LoRa) to a central gateway for real-time analysis, reducing manual labor and enabling spatial mapping over hectares. Early implementations focused on battery-efficient protocols and solar powering, achieving deployment densities of 1-10 nodes per acre with data refresh rates of minutes to hours; challenges like signal interference in wet soils have been addressed through mesh topologies and edge computing. Such systems support scalable precision irrigation and environmental research, with studies demonstrating up to 30% water savings in crop management.

Remote Sensing Methods

Remote sensing methods provide indirect estimates of soil moisture over large spatial scales using satellite- and aircraft-based sensors, leveraging electromagnetic interactions with the soil surface to infer moisture content without direct contact. These techniques are essential for global monitoring, offering coverage that in situ methods cannot achieve, and play a key role in climate monitoring by tracking soil moisture as an Essential Climate Variable. Microwave approaches dominate due to their penetration through clouds and sensitivity to soil dielectric properties, which vary significantly with water content, while optical and thermal methods complement them in clear-sky conditions.
Passive microwave remote sensing measures natural thermal emissions from the soil at L-band frequencies (around 1.4 GHz), where the brightness temperature inversely correlates with soil moisture because wetter soils emit less microwave radiation. The European Space Agency's Soil Moisture and Ocean Salinity (SMOS) mission, launched on November 2, 2009, exemplifies this approach, providing global soil moisture maps at a spatial resolution of about 35-50 km with a revisit time of 1-3 days. SMOS retrieves moisture in the top 0-5 cm of soil with an accuracy of approximately 0.04 m/m, though performance degrades in areas with dense vegetation or saline soils. These sensors are particularly effective for bare or sparsely vegetated surfaces, as higher frequencies increase atmospheric interference but reduce vegetation opacity.
Active microwave remote sensing, such as synthetic aperture radar (SAR), transmits pulses and measures backscatter from the soil surface, where soil moisture increases the dielectric constant and thus enhances radar return signals. The Copernicus Sentinel-1 mission, with Sentinel-1A launched on April 3, 2014, operates at C-band (5.4 GHz) and delivers high-resolution (down to 5 m) soil moisture estimates by inverting backscatter models that account for soil texture and incidence angle. Sentinel-1 achieves accuracies around 0.05 m/m for the top 5-10 cm layer, benefiting from its all-weather capability and frequent revisits (6-12 days), but requires corrections for topographic effects in rugged terrains. Combining active and passive data, as in SMOS-Sentinel fusions, improves resolution to 1-3 km while maintaining reasonable precision.
Optical and thermal remote sensing infers soil moisture indirectly through proxies like vegetation indices or land surface temperature, as drier soils lead to higher thermal emissions and stressed vegetation with lower normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI) values. NASA's Landsat-8, operational since 2013, and MODIS on Terra/Aqua satellites provide multispectral data at resolutions of 30 m and 250-1000 m, respectively, enabling correlations between shortwave infrared reflectance and surface moisture under clear skies. These methods are limited to the top 1-2 cm and are heavily affected by cloud cover, atmospheric aerosols, and canopy occlusion, achieving accuracies of 0.05-0.08 m/m only in sparse vegetation areas. They serve as valuable supplements to microwave data for high-resolution mapping in arid regions.
Data assimilation models integrate remote sensing observations with land surface models to enhance soil moisture estimates, propagating surface measurements into root-zone profiles and reducing uncertainties from sparse observations. NASA's Soil Moisture Active Passive (SMAP) mission, launched in January 2015, combines L-band passive radiometry (36 km resolution) with active radar (discontinued in 2015 but fused with Sentinel-1) to produce global maps every 2-3 days at 9-36 km resolution, with an overall accuracy of 0.04 m/m validated against in situ networks. SMAP's ensemble Kalman filter assimilates brightness temperature data into models like the Catchment Land Surface Model, improving forecasts for hydrological applications. Both SMOS and SMAP datasets are designated as Essential Climate Variables, supporting long-term climate trend analysis.
Key challenges in remote sensing include interference from vegetation cover, which attenuates microwave signals and masks soil emissions, and surface roughness, which scatters radar waves unpredictably, leading to retrieval errors up to 0.1 m/m in heterogeneous landscapes. Validation relies on ground-based networks like the International Soil Moisture Network, but discrepancies arise from scale mismatches between satellite footprints and point measurements. Soil texture variations and topography further complicate inversions, necessitating ancillary data like land cover maps for corrections.
Recent developments since 2020 have leveraged artificial intelligence to enhance retrievals, particularly in heterogeneous terrains, by training machine learning models on multi-sensor data to disentangle vegetation and roughness effects. For instance, convolutional neural networks fusing SMAP, Sentinel-1, and optical data achieve sub-1 km resolutions with accuracies improved by 20-30% over traditional physics-based methods in complex environments. The NASA-ISRO NISAR mission, launched on July 30, 2025, employs L- and S-band synthetic aperture radar to generate global soil moisture products at 200 m spatial resolution with a 6-12 day revisit cycle, significantly improving near-surface moisture estimates in vegetated and complex terrains. These AI approaches, as in the Consistent Artificial Intelligence-based Soil Moisture dataset, enable consistent long-term global monitoring, addressing gaps in legacy missions and advancing applications in precision agriculture and drought prediction.

Soil-Water Dynamics

Water Retention

Soil water retention refers to the capacity of soil to hold water within its pores against gravitational drainage, primarily through physical forces acting at the soil-water interface. Adhesion between water molecules and soil particle surfaces forms thin films around particles, while cohesion among water molecules maintains continuity within pores. In smaller pores, capillary actionresulting from surface tension and the combined effects of adhesion and cohesioncreates meniscus forces that elevate water levels and resist drainage, thereby enhancing retention in unsaturated conditions.
Soil texture profoundly influences retention capacity, as finer particles provide greater surface area for adhesion and smaller pores for capillary forces. Clay soils, with high clay content, exhibit stronger retention due to their extensive microporosity, whereas sandy soils drain rapidly owing to larger pores and lower surface area. For example, field capacitythe water content after gravity drainagein clay soils typically reaches about 0.4 m/m, compared to roughly 0.1 m/m in sands. This difference arises because clays hold water at higher suctions via capillary retention, while sands release it more readily at low suctions.

Water Flow

Water flow in soil refers to the movement of water through the interconnected pore spaces of the soil matrix, driven primarily by gradients in water potential. This transport is governed by physical laws that account for both saturated conditions, where pores are fully filled with water, and unsaturated conditions, where air and water coexist in the pores. In saturated flow, water moves freely under the influence of gravity and pressure gradients, whereas in unsaturated flow, the process is more restricted due to the tortuous paths through water-filled films and capillaries, resulting in slower velocities compared to saturated conditions.
The foundational description of soil water flow is provided by Darcy's law, originally formulated for saturated flow through porous media. Darcy's law states that the volumetric flux $ q $ (volume of water per unit area per unit time) is proportional to the hydraulic gradient:

q = -K \frac{dh}{dz}
q=Kdzdh
where $ K $ is the saturated hydraulic conductivity, $ h $ is the hydraulic head, and $ z $ is the vertical distance. For unsaturated soils, the law is extended as the Buckingham-Darcy law, incorporating the soil water pressure head $ \psi $:
q=K()ddz(+z)
q = -K(\psi) \frac{d}{dz} (\psi + z)
q=K()dzd(+z)
Here, $ K(\psi) $ decreases with increasing soil dryness (more negative $ \psi $), reflecting the reduced connectivity of water pathways in unsaturated conditions. For downward vertical flow, this simplifies to $ q = -K(\psi) \left( \frac{d\psi}{dz} + 1 \right) $. This extension highlights that unsaturated flow rates are significantly lower than saturated rates due to the dependence on matric potential gradients.
Soil water flow manifests in distinct types based on driving forces and soil saturation. Gravity-driven drainage occurs in saturated soils, where excess water percolates downward under gravitational pull, often following rainfall or irrigation events that exceed soil storage capacity. Capillary rise represents upward unsaturated flow, where water is drawn from a water table or saturated zone into drier overlying soil layers through adhesive and cohesive forces in fine pores, counteracting gravity over distances of several centimeters to meters depending on soil texture. Infiltration describes the initial entry of water at the soil surface, transitioning from high initial rates to equilibrium; the initial infiltration rate can be approximated by Horton's empirical equation:

where $ f(t) $ is the infiltration rate at time $ t $, $ f_0 $ is the initial rate, $ f_c $ is the constant final rate, and $ k $ is a decay constant. This process is crucial for surface water entry but slows as surface layers approach saturation.
Soil properties profoundly influence water flow rates and pathways. Texture determines saturated hydraulic conductivity $ K $, with coarse-textured sands exhibiting high values around $ 10^{-4} $ m/s due to large pores that facilitate rapid drainage, while fine-textured clays have low $ K $ on the order of $ 10^{-9} $ m/s because of small, tortuous pores that impede flow. Macropores, such as earthworm channels or root voids, create preferential flow paths that bypass the soil matrix, allowing rapid transport of water and solutes at rates approaching saturated conductivity and enhancing overall infiltration in structured soils.
Osmotic and pneumatic flows play minor roles in most soil water dynamics. Osmotic flow arises from solute concentration gradients in saline soils, inducing water movement toward regions of lower salt content across semi-permeable barriers, but its contribution is typically negligible compared to gravitational or matric forces unless salinity levels are extreme. Pneumatic effects, driven by air pressure differences in soil pores, can influence water displacement in aerated or compacted zones, yet they remain secondary to dominant potential gradients in natural settings.

Plant-Soil-Water Relationships

Water Uptake by Plants

Plants absorb water from the soil primarily through their root systems via osmosis, where water moves from the soil solution across semi-permeable root membranes into the root cells due to a water potential gradient, and to a lesser extent by diffusion. Root hairs, which are tubular extensions of root epidermal cells, dramatically increase the surface area available for absorption, enhancing contact between roots and soil particles. This adaptation allows plants to extract water more efficiently from the soil matrix.
The movement of water into and through the plant is largely driven by transpiration pull, explained by the cohesion-tension theory. As water evaporates from leaf mesophyll cells through stomata, it creates negative pressure (tension) in the xylem vessels, pulling a continuous column of water from the roots upward; this tension can reach up to -20 bars in the xylem under high transpiration rates. Root system architecture plays a key role in accessing available soil water, with extensive fibrous systems in crops like rye exhibiting total root lengths of approximately 622 km per plant, maximizing exploration of the soil volume. In contrast, deep-rooted species such as alfalfa develop taproots extending up to 79 meters or more into the soil, enabling access to stored water in subsoil layers during surface drying.
Many plants form symbiotic associations with arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi, whose hyphae extend far beyond the root depletion zone into the soil, effectively increasing the absorptive surface area and facilitating water transport directly to the host plant. This symbiosis is particularly beneficial in dry soils, where mycorrhizal plants often maintain higher water status and uptake compared to non-mycorrhizal counterparts by bridging gaps in water availability.
Water uptake is limited when soil moisture drops below the permanent wilting point, at which the soil water potential is too low (typically around -1.5 MPa) to sustain osmotic flow into roots, leading to plant wilting and halted absorption. Additionally, uptake follows diurnal patterns influenced by transpiration, with rates peaking at midday when solar radiation and evaporative demand are highest, before declining in the afternoon as leaf water potential decreases.

Consumptive Use and Water Use Efficiency

Consumptive use refers to the total amount of water consumed by crops through evapotranspiration, encompassing transpiration from plant surfaces and evaporation from the soil and intercepted water within the crop canopy. This process directly depletes soil moisture, as water is drawn from the soil profile to sustain plant growth and is not returned to the system, making it a critical factor in agricultural water balance. Under optimal conditions, consumptive use aligns with crop evapotranspiration (ETc), calculated as ETc = Kc ETo, where Kc is the crop-specific coefficient and ETo is the reference evapotranspiration derived from the FAO Penman-Monteith equation. However, when soil moisture falls below the readily available water threshold, a water stress coefficient (Ks) reduces ETc, limiting consumptive use to conserve soil reserves and prevent plant wilting.
Soil moisture availability profoundly influences consumptive use by regulating the rate of water extraction from the root zone, with drier soils promoting stomatal closure to minimize transpiration losses. In irrigated systems, management practices such as deficit irrigation can lower consumptive use by 10-20% in crops like wheat while maintaining yields, as reduced soil evaporation redirects water toward productive transpiration. For instance, in arid regions, consumptive use for major crops like alfalfa can reach 1,200-1,600 mm per season, heavily dependent on maintaining soil moisture above 50% of field capacity to avoid stress-induced reductions.
Water use efficiency (WUE) quantifies the productivity of consumptive use, defined as the ratio of biomass or grain yield produced per unit of water transpired or evapotranspired, typically expressed in kg m or g kg. At the leaf level, intrinsic WUE is measured as the net photosynthetic rate (A_n) divided by stomatal conductance (g_s), or A_n / transpiration rate (E), highlighting how soil moisture sustains open stomata for efficient carbon assimilation without excessive water loss. Adequate soil moisture enhances WUE by maximizing transpiration efficiency (TE = biomass / transpired water), with studies showing improvements in C3 crops like rice under controlled moisture regimes that avoid drought stress. Conversely, prolonged low soil moisture triggers partial stomatal closure, reducing photosynthesis and dropping WUE in field conditions, as observed in maize across varying soil types.
Improving WUE involves optimizing soil moisture through practices like mulching, which reduces non-productive evaporation by 20-40%, thereby increasing the proportion of water allocated to transpiration and biomass production. At the crop scale, WUE can be modeled as yield = transpiration TE harvest index, where maintaining soil moisture near field capacity boosts TE via enhanced root water uptake. Seminal assessments, such as those using FAO-56 frameworks, demonstrate that integrating soil moisture monitoring with precision irrigation elevates WUE in diverse agroecosystems, from 1.0-1.5 kg m in dryland wheat to over 2.0 kg m in irrigated sorghum.
