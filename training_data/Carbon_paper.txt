Carbon paper
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Carbon paper consists of thin sheets coated on one side with a waxy or oil-based ink containing pigments such as carbon black or aniline dyes. When placed between an original sheet and one or more copy sheets, the pressure from handwriting or typewriter keys transfers the coating to produce simultaneous duplicates of the document.
The earliest documented development of carbon paper occurred around 1801, when Italian inventor Pellegrino Turri created it to provide ink for a custom typing machine designed to enable his blind lover, Countess Carolina Fantoni, to write legible letters independently. In 1806, English inventor Ralph Wedgwood patented a similar apparatus known as the "manifold stylographic writer," which incorporated carbon paper to facilitate writing for the blind or in low light without traditional ink. This device earned Wedgwood significant profits and marked the first formal patent for the technology.
Carbon paper revolutionized office duplication by allowing multiple copies to be made efficiently alongside the original, gaining widespread adoption following the invention of the typewriter in 1868 and remaining a staple for business, legal, and administrative tasks until photocopying technologies and computers rendered it obsolete in the mid-20th century.

History

Invention and Early Development

In 1801, Italian inventor Pellegrino Turri created the first known form of carbon paper, consisting of a thin sheet coated with a mixture of wax, ink, and carbon to serve as an inking medium for his mechanical typing machine. This device, developed to facilitate private correspondence for Countess Carolina Fantoni da Fivizzano, who had become blind, allowed the production of readable duplicates by pressing the coated paper between the original writing surface and a copy sheet, transferring carbon-based impressions without quill or fluid ink. Turri's innovation addressed the need for tactile, ink-free writing methods suitable for the visually impaired, marking the initial practical application of carbon transfer for duplication.
Five years later, in 1806, English inventor Ralph Wedgwood secured British Patent No. 2972 for his "apparatus for producing duplicates of writings," commercialized as the stylographic manifold writer. Wedgwood's system utilized thin oiled tissue paper coated with moistened carbon black pigment, layered between an original document and copies, with a blunt stylus applying pressure to transfer the image via the carbon layer. Like Turri's earlier work, Wedgwood's patent emphasized utility for the blind, enabling self-communication without visible ink, though it extended to general letter copying for efficiency in administrative tasks. The patent formalized the core mechanism of carbon paper as a reusable intermediary for manifold duplication, distinguishing it from prior ad hoc methods by specifying materials like lampblack (carbon black) mixed with oil for adhesion and transfer.
Early refinements in the subsequent decades focused on improving coating durability and transfer clarity, with experimenters exploring variations in binders such as oils and waxes to prevent smudging on the oiled base sheets. By the 1820s, producers like Cyrus P. Dakin began manufacturing coated papers using oil-carbon black mixtures, enhancing portability and reducing the need for on-site preparation of the duplicating surfaces. These adjustments addressed initial limitations in Wedgwood's design, such as the paper's tendency to dry out or produce faint impressions, laying groundwork for broader adoption without altering the fundamental pressure-based transfer principle.

Commercialization and Peak Usage

Following the initial patent by Ralph Wedgwood in 1806, carbon paper entered commercial production in the early 19th century, with American inventor Cyrus Dakin manufacturing and selling it to the Associated Press by 1823, marking an early step toward broader market availability. Industrialization in the mid-19th century facilitated mass production techniques, transitioning carbon paper from artisanal creation to scalable output suitable for growing administrative demands in Europe and North America.
Its integration with the typewriter, commercialized from the 1860s onward, drove widespread adoption starting in the 1870s, as improved carbon formulations enabled reliable multi-copy production for typewritten documents. Peak usage spanned the 1880s to the 1950s, particularly in bookkeeping, legal documentation, and bureaucratic record-keeping, where it served as the primary method for simultaneous original-and-copy generation in offices lacking alternative duplication technologies. This era saw carbon paper become integral to business correspondence worldwide, underpinning efficient replication in pre-photocopy administrative workflows.
The technology's proliferation extended globally through expanding commercial networks and administrative standardization, including in colonial bureaucracies reliant on duplicated records for governance and trade documentation, as well as wartime applications such as field reporting during World War II, where portable duplication supported operational efficiency amid logistical constraints. Prior to the dominance of xerographic methods, carbon paper's low-cost, immediate duplication capability played a causal role in scaling paper-based record systems, handling the surge in documentation volume from industrialized economies and international administration.

Composition and Mechanism

Materials and Production Process

Traditional carbon paper for duplication consists of a thin tissue paper substrate coated on one side with a pigmented waxy formulation designed for pressure-induced transfer. The core components include carbon black or lampblack as the primary pigment for opacity and marking, typically comprising 15% of the coating mix; waxes such as paraffin (around 33%) and carnauba (6-32%) to provide lubricity and adhesion; binders including mineral oil (15-26%) and oleic acid (3-15%) for cohesion; and fillers like kaolin clay (10-12%) or talc (up to 39%) to improve transfer efficiency, smoothness, and density without excessive smearing.
Variations in formulation exist for specific uses, such as reusable types incorporating lard oil (16%) and dyes like victoria blue base (7%) for indelible copies, while one-time sheets prioritize economical disposable coatings with higher filler content. The carbon black pigment is produced via thermal decomposition of hydrocarbons in furnaces at 1,100-1,700C, yielding fine amorphous particles.
Manufacturing begins with dispersing the pigment and fillers into a vehicle of waxes, oils, and solvents to form a uniform ink. In the traditional Mayer hot-melt process, the tissue paper unwinds over a dope roll submerged in the heated ink pan (maintained at 76-82C), transferring the coating via viscous adhesion; an equalizer rod then smooths the layer for evenness, followed by rapid cooling on a chill roll to solidify the wax matrix before rewinding.
Alternative solvent-based or flexographic coating methods meter the ink for precise thickness control, applying it via print rolls onto the paper substrate under impression, with subsequent drying or chilling to set the coating. The finished rolls are slit, cut into sheets, and optionally perforated for interleaving in forms, ensuring the sub-micron pigment particles (typically 20-100 nm in diameter) enable shear-activated release under writing or typing pressure for sharp, non-bleeding impressions.

Operational Principles for Duplication

Carbon paper enables duplication through a mechanical transfer process where localized pressure disrupts a thin coating of wax-embedded pigment on the coated side of the sheet. The coating, typically comprising paraffin wax or microcrystalline wax mixed with finely dispersed carbon black particles, exhibits brittleness under impact, causing micro-fractures or shear in the wax matrix. This releases loose pigment particles, which then adhere to the facing surface of an underlying sheet via frictional displacement and intermolecular forces, such as van der Waals attractions between the pigment and the paper fibers.
The efficiency of pigment transfer depends on several physical factors. Uniformity in the coating thickness and pigment distribution ensures consistent release across the impacted area, as irregularities can lead to patchy or faint impressions. Applied pressure must exceed a threshold to initiate matrix disruptiontypically achieved through writing or typing impactswhile excessive force risks embedding wax residues, promoting smudging from volatile components that migrate over time.
In multi-sheet interleaving, transfer quality diminishes progressively for deeper copies due to pressure dissipation across the stack, limiting reliable duplication to approximately eight copies with manual typewriters and up to twelve with powered mechanisms, beyond which insufficient force reaches lower sheets. The process is inherently unidirectional, with pigment transferring only from the coated side to the adjacent uncoated surface, contributing to inefficiencies in bidirectional or high-volume setups and observed variability in copy legibility from mechanical inconsistencies.

Traditional Applications and Impact

Document Copying and Administrative Uses

Carbon paper served as a staple tool for administrative document duplication in offices from the early 1900s to the 1960s, enabling the simultaneous creation of original and copy documents via manual typewriters or handwriting on multi-sheet assemblies. Sheets were inserted between layers of plain paper, with the carbon-coated side facing the underlying sheet, transferring ink under pressure to produce up to several legible duplicates for routine records like invoices, contracts, and receipts.
In accounting practices, it supported the maintenance of duplicate ledgers by allowing clerks to generate parallel entries in one pass, minimizing transcription errors compared to hand-rewriting each version. Railway operations relied on it for issuing tickets and copying train orders dictated over telegraph, where operators used layered forms to record and distribute instructions efficiently across stations. Government bureaucracies adopted it extensively for form-based record-keeping; for instance, the U.S. Post Office Department procured over 1.66 million sheets in a single mid-20th-century bidding cycle to fill duplicate postal forms.
This approach yielded practical efficiency by obviating separate copying steps, with inexpensive sheetsoften costing fractions of a cent eachoffering immediacy superior to predecessors like hectographs, which required chemical processing and drying time. However, limitations included smudging from the waxy coating and misalignment risks in multi-sheet stacks, necessitating precise alignment and occasional retyping of flawed copies.

Variations and Specialized Forms

Carbon paper exists in one-sided and two-sided variants, with the coating applied to a single surface for standard unidirectional transfer or to both surfaces for bidirectional duplication in interleaved stacks. Two-sided carbon paper enables multiple copies without reorienting sheets, though it incurs about 25% higher production costs compared to one-sided types.
Colored iterations incorporate pigments or aniline dyes, such as red, blue, or purple, to produce coded duplicates for tasks like error marking or form differentiation, enhancing legibility in multi-part documents.
Specialized manifold books feature pre-attached, interleaved carbon and plain sheets in bound formats, optimized for generating simultaneous originals and copies in applications like billing and receipt issuance, streamlining administrative workflows.
Erasable formulations employ microencapsulated or low-adhesion pigments that permit correction by rubbing without smearing underlying text, improving accuracy in typing and form preparation.

Decline and Residual Uses

Factors Leading to Obsolescence

The introduction of plain-paper photocopiers, exemplified by the Xerox 914 launched in 1959, marked a pivotal shift by enabling automatic, high-fidelity duplication of documents without manual interleaving of carbon sheets, thereby reducing labor-intensive processes prone to misalignment and smudging. This technology slashed duplication time and error rates compared to carbon paper methods, which required precise sheet alignment and often yielded inconsistent copies due to pressure variations.
In the 1970s, the proliferation of dot-matrix impact printers further eroded carbon paper's dominance by automating multi-part form printing through high-impact print heads that struck interleaved carbon or carbonless sheets, eliminating the need for typewriter-based manual duplication for invoices and records. These printers handled continuous-feed multi-copy paper efficiently, lowering per-copy operational costs and minimizing human error in repetitive administrative tasks.
The 1980s digital revolution, driven by widespread adoption of personal computers and word processors, accelerated obsolescence by enabling electronic document creation and printing, which bypassed physical duplication entirely for most office workflows. U.S. business paper usage surged overall from 850 billion to 1.4 trillion pages between 1981 and 1984 due to expanded printing capabilities, but this masked a causal decline in manual carbon methods as digital tools rendered interleaving redundant and costlier in time and materials.
Carbon paper's inherent drawbackssuch as ink smearing, staining on hands and clothing, and generation of waste from discarded sheetsexacerbated its replacement, as photocopiers and printers offered cleaner, scalable alternatives with lower hygiene risks and no ancillary material disposal. By the late 1990s, the market for traditional carbon paper had contracted sharply, supplanted by these efficiencies despite persistent overall paper consumption trends.

Contemporary Non-Industrial Applications

In artistic and craft practices, carbon paper remains employed for precise pattern transfer onto fabrics and other surfaces where digital printing may lack the required accuracy or tactile feedback. Crafters use it to trace embroidery designs onto dark-colored materials by placing the pattern over carbon paper and the target fabric, then applying pressure with a stylus or needle to deposit the carbon residue as a guideline. This method, detailed in instructional resources as of 2022, allows for customizable adjustments during transfer, preserving the analog control valued in handcrafting traditions.
Within tattooing, specialized hectograph carbon transfer papercoated with a blend of carbon black, alcohol-soluble dye, and waxy bindersserves to outline stencil designs onto skin prior to inking. Artists apply the paper beneath a stencil sheet, trace the outline firmly, and achieve a semi-permanent purple or black guideline that withstands initial skin preparation without smudging excessively. As of 2024, this manual technique persists in professional studios for its reliability in freehand customization, outperforming thermal transfers in scenarios demanding immediate, non-digital replication.
Archival contexts occasionally retain carbon paper for generating physical duplicates of sensitive documents, leveraging its inherent anti-forgery properties through the irreversible carbon imprint that resists chemical alteration. Suppliers note its role in creating verifiable backups for preservation, particularly where electronic systems are unavailable or distrusted, though such applications constitute a negligible fraction of modern duplication methods due to the dominance of scanning and carbonless alternatives.

Carbon Paper in Fuel Cells

Material Properties and Role in Fuel Cell Design

In proton exchange membrane (PEM) fuel cells, carbon paper serves as the primary substrate for the gas diffusion layer (GDL), consisting of non-woven carbon fibers typically derived from polyacrylonitrile (PAN) precursors to form a highly porous, electrically conductive matrix. These fibers are assembled into a macroporous backing, often augmented with a microporous layer (MPL) of carbon black and polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) to enhance hydrophobicity and contact with the catalyst layer. The structure exhibits porosity levels of 70-90%, enabling efficient gas permeation while maintaining mechanical integrity under operational compression.
As the GDL, carbon paper facilitates the transport of reactant gaseshydrogen and oxygento the catalyst sites, concurrently managing water removal to prevent electrode flooding and ensuring proton conduction across the membrane. It also provides electronic conductivity pathways, with in-plane electrical resistivity typically below 0.1 cm to minimize ohmic losses during current collection. Thickness ranges from 100-400 m, balancing permeability and structural support without impeding stack assembly. Unlike traditional duplicating carbon paper, which relies on wax-pigment coatings for inert mechanical transfer, fuel cell variants prioritize electrochemical functionality, including corrosion resistance under acidic, humid conditions at potentials up to 1.0 V. This distinction underscores its role in enabling high power densities, often exceeding 1 W/cm in PEMFC designs.

Manufacturing and Performance Characteristics

The manufacturing of carbon paper for use as gas diffusion layers (GDLs) in proton exchange membrane fuel cells (PEMFCs) begins with the dispersion of chopped carbon fibers, typically polyacrylonitrile (PAN)- or pitch-based, in an aqueous medium to form a uniform slurry via wet-laid papermaking techniques. This fiber mat is then impregnated with a thermosetting resin, dried, and subjected to pyrolysis (carbonization at temperatures around 10001500C) followed by graphitization at higher temperatures (up to 25003000C) to enhance electrical conductivity and structural integrity. Subsequently, a microporous layer (MPL) comprising carbon black and polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) is applied via coating or spraying, imparting hydrophobicity to mitigate water flooding while maintaining gas permeability; PTFE content is typically optimized at 530 wt% to balance wettability.
Major suppliers such as Toray Industries and SGL Carbon dominate production, offering standardized products like Toray's TGP-H series and SGL's SIGRACET papers, which feature thicknesses of 60370 m, porosity of 7080%, and in-plane electrical conductivity exceeding 100 S/cm to minimize ohmic losses. These materials undergo quality control for metrics including tensile strength (>10 MPa) and gas permeability (e.g., >1 10^{-11} m for air flow).
In performance, carbon paper GDLs contribute to membrane electrode assembly (MEA) durability exceeding 5000 hours under accelerated stress tests with cycling, as per U.S. Department of Energy targets for automotive applications, by providing mechanical support and corrosion resistance during operation at 6080C. However, stack compression (12 MPa) reduces through-plane gas permeability by 2050%, quantified via Darcy's law coefficients dropping from ~10^{-11} m uncompressed to lower values, which can induce localized flooding and mass transport losses if hydrophobicity is insufficient. Empirical tests show optimal performance at compression ratios of 2030%, balancing contact resistance (<0.05 cm) against pore collapse.

Advancements and Challenges

Since the early 2000s, advancements in carbon paper gas diffusion layers (GDLs) for proton exchange membrane fuel cells (PEMFCs) have focused on enhancing porosity, reducing thickness, and improving durability to mitigate mass transport limitations and carbon corrosion. For instance, Toray Industries developed thinner, more durable carbon paper GDLs in April 2024, enabling better mechanical stability and potentially smaller fuel cell stacks by optimizing gas permeability and water management. Laboratory modifications, such as incorporating carbon nanotubes or nanofibers into carbon paper substrates, have demonstrated power density increases of 15-25% under high-current conditions by improving electron conductivity and flood resistance, as shown in electrospinning-based studies from 2025. 
Despite these innovations, persistent challenges hinder widespread adoption. Carbon paper GDLs suffer from high production costs, often exceeding $50 per square meter due to intricate wet-laid papermaking and graphitization processes, which limit scalability for mass-market applications compared to emerging alternatives like metallic foams that offer greater mechanical robustness. Mechanical fragility under stack compressiontypically 1-2 MPacauses pore collapse and increased contact resistance, reducing long-term performance by up to 20% over 5,000 hours of operation, as evidenced by finite element analyses of deformation behavior. Scalability is further constrained by variability in fiber alignment and treatment uniformity, exacerbating durability issues like oxidative degradation at potentials above 0.6 V.
These material properties have supported incremental commercialization, such as in the Toyota Mirai's PEMFC stack, where carbon paper GDLs facilitate efficient reactant diffusion contributing to 114 kW output. However, broader PEMFC viability remains undermined by upstream hydrogen production emissionsprimarily from steam methane reforming without carbon capture and storage (CCS), which generates 9-12 kg CO2 per kg H2offsetting localized zero-emission benefits unless paired with electrolytic hydrogen from renewables. Empirical data from durability tests indicate that while GDL innovations extend operational life to 5,000-10,000 hours in lab settings, field reliability lags due to unaddressed synergies with catalyst and membrane degradation.
