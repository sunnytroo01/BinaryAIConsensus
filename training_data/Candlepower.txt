Candlepower
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Candlepower is a historical and now obsolete unit of luminous intensity, measuring the amount of visible light emitted by a source in a specific direction per unit solid angle. It originated in the 19th century as the luminous intensity produced by a standard spermaceti candle, defined precisely as a candle weighing one-sixth of a pound (approximately 75.5 grams) with a uniform cross-section, burning at a steady rate of 120 grains (7.776 grams) per hour and emitting light horizontally from the flame.
The concept of candlepower emerged from early efforts to standardize light measurement using flame-based references, with initial definitions varying by nation; for instance, the British standard was established under the Metropolitan Gas Act of 1860, tying it to the output of a spermaceti candle of specific dimensions. By the late 19th century, inconsistencies in national unitssuch as the French Violle candle or the German Hefner candleprompted international collaboration. In 1909, representatives from the United States, Britain, and France adopted the "international candle" at a conference in Washington, D.C., standardizing it as the luminous intensity of the spermaceti candle described above, which served as a common reference for photometry in scientific and industrial applications.
The limitations of flame standards, including variability due to material quality and burning conditions, led to the development of more precise incandescent-based references by the early 20th century. In 1933, the International Commission on Illumination (CIE) and the International Committee for Weights and Measures (CIPM) began work on a unified unit, culminating in 1946 when the CIPM defined the "new candle" as one-sixtieth of the luminous intensity per square centimeter of a blackbody radiator at the temperature of freezing platinum (approximately 2042 K) under standard atmospheric pressure. This was ratified by the 9th General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM) in 1948, renaming the unit the candela (symbol: cd) to replace the international candle and eliminate national variations in candlepower. The 10th CGPM in 1954 formally established the candela as one of the base units of the International System of Units (SI), and it was further refined in 1967.
The current SI definition of the candela, adopted by the 16th CGPM in 1979 and reaffirmed in the 2019 revision, is the luminous intensity, in a given direction, of a source that emits monochromatic radiation at a frequency of 540 10 hertz (green light) and has a radiant intensity in that direction of 1/683 watt per steradian; this ties the unit to human visual perception via the luminous efficiency function while grounding it in radiometric standards. One international candle corresponds closely to one candela, with one historical candlepower equaling approximately 1.019 candela. Although candlepower is no longer used in formal scientific contexts, the term persists informally in some engineering and consumer lighting discussions, often synonymously with candela, highlighting its enduring legacy in the evolution of photometric measurement.

Definition and Fundamentals

Core Definition

Candlepower (cp) is a historical unit of luminous intensity, quantifying the amount of visible light emitted by a source in a particular direction as perceived by the human eye. It represents the directional brightness of a light source, originally calibrated against the flame of a standardized candle.
The unit derives its conceptual foundation from the luminous intensity produced by a pure spermaceti wax candle, weighing approximately one-sixth of a pound (about 75 grams) and burning at a uniform rate of 120 grains (roughly 7.8 grams) per hour; such a candle was established to emit a luminous intensity of 1 candlepower.
Unlike luminous flux, which measures the total quantity of light emitted by a source in all directions (in lumens), or illuminance, which assesses the light falling on a surface (in lux), candlepower specifically focuses on the concentrated intensity along a given line of sight.

Relation to Luminous Intensity

Candlepower serves as a unit for luminous intensity within the field of photometry, quantifying the amount of visible light emitted by a source in a specific direction per unit solid angle. It is expressed in candelas (cd), the SI base unit, where 1 cd equals 1 lumen per steradian (lm/sr), representing the luminous flux density in that direction. This measurement weights the radiant power by the human eye's spectral sensitivity, specifically the CIE photopic luminosity function V(), which peaks at approximately 555 nm and describes the eye's response under well-lit conditions.
Luminous intensity, and thus candlepower, focuses on the perceptual brightness observed in a particular direction rather than the total light output of the source. For instance, a narrow-beam spotlight can exhibit high candlepower along its axis due to concentrated flux, appearing much brighter in that direction than a diffuse bulb with the same total luminous flux but spread over a wider area.

Historical Development

Origins and Early Concepts

In the 17th and 18th centuries, candles served as the primary artificial light source for everyday illumination, forming the basis for informal comparisons of light intensity across various settings. In households, particularly in colonial America and Europe, tallow candles were commonly used to light rooms, with their glow providing a benchmark for assessing the adequacy of light from other sources like oil lamps or fireplaces. In theaters, such as those in England during the early 17th century, candles mounted in chandeliers or footlights illuminated both the stage and auditorium, allowing performers and audiences to gauge visibility and dramatic effect relative to the candle's output; reflectors were often employed to enhance this light, as pioneered by Inigo Jones. Early lighthouses, including the Eddystone Lighthouse in England (built 1698 and rebuilt 1759), relied on clusters of tallow or wax candles within enclosed lanterns to signal ships at sea, where their collective brightness was compared to natural beacons like coastal fires for navigational reliability.
The perceived intensity of candlelight varied significantly depending on the material, contributing to inconsistencies in these early comparisons and highlighting the need for more uniform standards. Tallow candles, made from rendered animal fat, produced a dimmer, smokier flame that required frequent trimming and offered lower luminous output, making them the most accessible but least reliable for precise assessment. Beeswax candles burned cleaner and brighter with a higher melting point, allowing longer unattended use, though they were costlier and reserved for wealthier households or venues. Spermaceti candles, derived from whale oil, emerged as a superior option in the late 18th century, emitting a steadier and more intense white light that became an informal reference for brightness in signaling and household applications, yet regional availability and manufacturing differences still led to variability in output.
Early scientific curiosity in the late 18th and early 19th centuries began to formalize these observations, shifting from practical utility toward conceptual quantification of light. In navigation and signaling, such as in lighthouses, candles functioned as rudimentary "units" where the number and type determined visibility rangefor instance, 24 candles in Smeaton's 1759 Eddystone design were visible up to 12 miles with aid, serving as a de facto measure for maritime safety. Figures like William Hyde Wollaston contributed to this evolution around 1802 through prismatic studies of refractive and dispersive properties, which laid groundwork for later intensity measurements without yet establishing standardized scales. These efforts underscored the candle's role as a relatable, accessible reference, bridging everyday experience with emerging photometric ideas.

Standardization Efforts

The first formal legal definition of candlepower emerged with the London Metropolitan Gas Act of 1860, which established the "standard candle" as a benchmark for evaluating gas lighting illuminating power in the United Kingdom. This standard was specified as the light produced by a pure spermaceti candle weighing one-sixth of a pound (76 grams) with a steady cylindrical flame, burning at a uniform rate of 120 grains per hour (7.78 grams per hour) in a stable atmosphere. The act required gas suppliers to achieve an output equivalent to at least 12 such candles from a standard burner consuming five cubic feet of gas per hour, marking the initial effort to create a reproducible photometric unit for commercial regulation.
By the late 19th century, inconsistencies between national candle standardssuch as the English spermaceti candle, the French bougie dcimale (approximately one-tenth of a Carcel lamp), the Violle candle (based on a blackbody radiator), and emerging German variantshindered international trade and scientific collaboration in lighting technology. These discrepancies, often varying by 10-20% due to differences in materials and burn conditions, were partially addressed in the 1890s through the introduction of the Hefner candle by German engineer Friedrich von Hefner-Alteneck in 1893. Defined as the horizontal luminous intensity from a flame of a lamp burning amyl acetate with a wick diameter of 8 mm and flame height of 40 mm, the Hefner candle produced about 0.9 times the intensity of the English standard and became the official unit in Germany, Austria, and Scandinavia until 1942.
A pivotal advancement occurred in 1909 when representatives from the United States, United Kingdom, and France reached an agreement under the International Committee on Weights and Measures to define the International Candle as a unified unit of luminous intensity. This standard was based on the British spermaceti candle of the specified dimensions and burn rate from the 1860 act, calibrated to produce a luminous intensity of approximately 0.98 candela in modern terms, ensuring equivalence across the three nations' previous standards (one International Candle equaling one British candle, one American candle after a 1.6% adjustment, and one French bougie dcimale). The U.S. National Bureau of Standards (now NIST) was instrumental in this process, conducting inter-laboratory comparisons and providing calibration services to adopt and propagate the unit domestically and internationally.
Efforts to enhance reproducibility continued into the 1930s, when the International Candle was refined by shifting primary realization from spermaceti candlesprone to variations in wax qualityto pentane burner standards for greater consistency. The Harcourt pentane lamp, burning purified pentane at a controlled rate to produce 10 candlepower, was adopted as a working standard after initial trials in the late 19th century, with 1930s improvements by bodies like the International Commission on Illumination reducing output variability to under 2% under specified atmospheric conditions (76 cm Hg pressure and defined humidity). This burner-based approach remained in use until 1948, bridging the gap between flame artifacts and emerging electrical standards.

Measurement Techniques

Visual Comparison Methods

Visual comparison methods for assessing candlepower relied on the subjective judgment of trained observers to equate the brightness of a test light source with that of a standard candle, forming the basis of early photometry before instrumental precision became feasible. These techniques emerged in the mid-19th century as lighting technologies like gas lamps proliferated, demanding quantitative evaluation of luminous output. By directly comparing perceived illumination, practitioners could estimate relative intensities without complex apparatus, though results depended heavily on the observer's experience and environmental controls.
The Bunsen grease-spot photometer, invented by Robert Bunsen in 1844, exemplified this approach and became one of the most enduring designs for visual photometry. The device featured a thin screen of white paper treated with a translucent grease spot in its center, mounted on an adjustable carriage between the standard candle and the test source, often along a darkened bench. The observer, viewing the screen from behind, slid the carriage until the grease spot vanished in contrastappearing uniformly bright when the illuminance from both sources matched on opposite sides of the paper. This point of equality enabled relative candlepower determination through geometric ratios of distances.
To quantify the comparison, these methods invoked the inverse square law of illumination, which states that illuminance decreases with the square of the distance from a point source, expressed as $ E \propto \frac{I}{d^2} $, where $ E $ is illuminance, $ I $ is luminous intensity, and $ d $ is distance. At the balance point where $ E_1 = E_2 $, the ratio of intensities follows $ \frac{I_\text{test}}{I_\text{standard}} = \left( \frac{d_\text{test}}{d_\text{standard}} \right)^2 $, allowing candlepower calculation from measured distances alone. This application simplified assessments in resource-limited settings, such as workshops evaluating gas lamp efficiencies.
Despite their practicality, visual comparison techniques suffered from inherent limitations tied to human physiology, introducing variability that could exceed 5-10% in replicate measurements. The eye's sensitivity fluctuated with factors like adaptation time, fatigue, and spectral composition, with errors most pronounced under low illuminance where scotopic vision dominated and color perception falteredcommon in testing dim gas flames during the 1800s. Observers often required dark adaptation and multiple trials to mitigate bias, yet interpersonal differences persisted.
In historical contexts, these methods proved vital for advancing lighting applications beyond laboratories, including theater productions where gas jets needed balancing for even stage illumination and early electric incandescent bulbs, tested against standardized spermaceti candles to rate outputs like 16 candlepower at specific voltages. Such evaluations supported the rapid commercialization of illumination in the late 19th century, bridging artisanal judgment with emerging industrial standards.

Instrument-Based Calibration

Instrument-based calibration methods emerged in the late 19th and early 20th centuries to provide more objective and reproducible measurements of candlepower, reducing reliance on subjective visual judgments while still incorporating human perception in some designs. These techniques utilized mechanical devices and early photodetectors to compare luminous intensities between a standard source and the test light, often employing principles like the inverse square law for distance-based adjustments.
The Lummer-Brodhun photometer, invented in 1889 by Otto Lummer and Eugen Brodhun at the Physikalisch-Technische Reichsanstalt in Berlin, represented a significant advancement in precision photometry. This divided-field instrument employed prisms and mirrors to superimpose images of the standard and test light sources onto a screen, creating two adjacent visual fields separated by a boundary. A convex prism with a ground-glass flat enhanced contrast reduction at the interface, allowing an observer to adjust the distance of one source until the boundary became indistinguishable due to equal illumination. With experienced operators, it achieved high measurement precision, typically better than 1%, making it a preferred tool for laboratory standardization of luminous intensity.
Flicker photometry, developed in 1893 by Ogden N. Rood, offered another approach by exploiting the human eye's heightened sensitivity to temporal fluctuations in light. In this method, the standard and test sources were alternated rapidly using rotating discs or sectors, typically at rates around 10-20 Hz, and their intensities adjusted until the perceived flicker was minimized or the lights fused into a steady field. This technique proved particularly useful for comparing sources of different spectral compositions, where steady-state visual matching might fail due to color differences, though its accuracy was generally around 5% owing to variations in flicker perception. By the early 1900s, refinements by researchers like Frederick E. Ives improved its reliability for heterochromatic measurements, including candlepower assessments of colored lamps.
In the early 1900s, non-visual detectors began supplementing these visual instruments, enabling fully objective calibrations independent of observer variability. Selenium cells, first demonstrated for light detection in 1873 and incorporated into a practical photometer by Siemens and Halske in 1882, measured luminous intensity through changes in electrical resistance or current proportional to incident light. These barrier-layer cells, consisting of selenium coated on a metal plate, were used in comparative setups to quantify candlepower, such as in 1907 determinations of the moon's light equivalent to terrestrial sources, though their sensitivity to red light and instability over time posed challenges. Similarly, photographic plates served as early detectors by recording light exposure as silver halide density on emulsion-coated glass; calibration involved exposing plates to known standards and measuring blackening rates to infer relative intensities, akin to test exposures in primitive actinometry.

Transition to Modern Units

Adoption of the Candela

The adoption of the candela marked a pivotal shift in photometric standards during the mid-20th century, driven by the inherent limitations of candle-based units. Candlepower, reliant on physical candles such as those made from spermaceti wax, suffered from significant variability due to inconsistencies in materials, wick size, and combustion conditions, which undermined reproducibility across laboratories and nations. Additionally, the scarcity of spermaceti, derived from sperm whales, had long posed supply challenges, exacerbating the need for a stable, physics-based alternative that could be realized independently of biological resources. This transition aimed to establish an absolute, reproducible standard grounded in thermal radiation principles, ensuring consistency in international measurements.
In 1946, the International Commission on Illumination (CIE), in collaboration with the International Committee for Weights and Measures (CIPM), proposed a new unit defined as one-sixtieth of the luminous intensity per square centimeter of a blackbody radiator at the freezing point of platinum (approximately 2042 K), effectively decoupling the standard from actual candles. This definition was promulgated by the CIPM and ratified by the 9th General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM) in 1948, which officially named the unit the "candela" (symbol: cd) to replace the "new candle." The choice of platinum solidification provided a practical, high-temperature reference that approximated blackbody radiation, offering greater precision than prior flame or filament standards while maintaining approximate equivalence to the international candle for continuity in existing measurementsspecifically, 1 cd 1 cp (with 1 cp 0.981 cd).
The candela's integration into the International System of Units (SI) was formalized in 1954 at the 10th CGPM, where it was established as one of the six base units (alongside the metre, kilogram, second, ampere, and kelvin), reflecting its fundamental role in photometry. This inclusion solidified the candela's status as the global standard for luminous intensity, promoting uniformity in scientific and industrial applications worldwide.
Further refinement occurred in 1979, when the 16th CGPM redefined the candela to address practical challenges in realizing the platinum-based blackbody standard, such as the difficulties in maintaining precise high-temperature conditions. The new definition tied the candela to the luminous intensity of a source emitting monochromatic radiation at a frequency of 540 terahertz (green light) with a specified radiant intensity of 1/683 watt per steradian, enabling more accurate realization using lasers and advancing radiometric techniques. This change enhanced reproducibility and aligned photometry more closely with absolute physical measurements.

Conversion Factors and Equivalences

The international candlepower (cp), established as a standard unit in 1909, was defined such that 1 cp 1 candela (cd) following the 1948 redefinition of the candela for the purpose of maintaining continuity in photometric measurements (specifically, 1 cp 0.981 cd).
Prior to the international standard, various national units of luminous intensity existed, each calibrated against specific light sources and differing slightly from the modern candela. The English candle, based on a spermaceti wax standard, was approximately 0.95 cd. The German Hefner candle (or Hefnerkerze), defined using a flame from a specific wick and rape oil, equated to about 0.9 cd. In contrast, the French carcel lamp, an oil-burning device standardized in the early 19th century, produced a luminous intensity of roughly 9.6 cd per unit. Contemporary Applications

Use in Lighting Specifications

In lighting specifications, candlepower, now standardized as candela (cd), plays a key role in ANSI and IES standards for characterizing directional light intensity in beam patterns. The Illuminating Engineering Society (IES) defines center-beam candlepower (CBCP) as the luminous intensity along the centerline of a directional fixture, often assumed to represent the maximum intensity, and it is widely used in beam intensity charts for luminaires such as downlights and spotlights. For instance, ANSI/IES LM-79-24, the approved method for photometric measurements of solid-state lighting products, requires reporting candela values to assess intensity distribution, enabling designers to evaluate how light is focused rather than total output. This approach is essential in architectural applications where precise beam control prevents glare and ensures targeted illumination.
Candlepower specifications are particularly valuable in automotive and stage lighting, where directional intensity outweighs overall luminous flux. In automotive headlamps, high-beam ratings typically range from 20,000 to 75,000 cd to meet visibility requirements while complying with regulatory limits on peak intensity. For stage lighting, spotlights are rated in candela to quantify beam intensity, allowing lighting designers to achieve focused illumination on performers without excessive spill, as candela directly measures the light's directional strength in a specific solid angle.
Modern goniophotometry employs automated scanning to generate candlepower distribution curves and isocandela diagrams, which plot intensity contours in candela across angular coordinates for comprehensive fixture evaluation. These diagrams, derived from far-field measurements, visualize how intensity varies by direction, aiding in the design of uniform lighting in architectural and theatrical settings. According to the IES Practical Guide to Photometry, isocandela plots are constructed from such curves to represent equal-intensity loci, providing a visual tool for predicting beam spread.
As of 2025, IES guidelines continue to incorporate candela in LED specifications, such as through the 2024 update to LM-79 testing protocols, despite the broader SI emphasis on lumens for total output; this persistence ensures compatibility with legacy directional metrics in high-performance applications like floodlights and projectors.

Legacy in Non-Scientific Contexts

Despite the official transition to the candela in scientific photometry, candlepower endures as an informal unit in consumer marketing for portable lights, where it conveys beam intensity and perceived distance rather than total output. Flashlight and lantern advertisements frequently highlight "peak beam candlepower" to appeal to users seeking long-range illumination, such as spotlights rated at 1 million candlepower for automotive or outdoor use, providing a rough indicator of throw distance in practical scenarios. These ratings are often approximate, prioritizing marketing impact over precise measurement.
In automotive and firearm accessories, candlepower specifications remain common in 2020s product catalogs and enthusiast discussions to denote visibility range for handheld devices. For example, tactical weapon lights are promoted with candlepower values to emphasize focused beams for target acquisition, as noted in shooting industry guides recommending at least 2,000 candela (equivalent to candlepower) for effective performance. Such usage persists in hobbyist forums and retailer listings, where it aids quick comparisons of light throw without delving into lumens.
The term candlepower also lingers in cultural references, particularly in science fiction literature, where it describes intense artificial lights in speculative settings, such as the "million-candlepower beams" illuminating spaceports in Michael McCollum's Antares Dawn. In everyday hobbies, it appears in camping gear specifications, like kerosene lanterns rated at 500 candlepower for ambient outdoor lighting, evoking traditional candle-based illumination. Beekeeping equipment similarly draws on this legacy through beeswax candles used in apiary tasks, though the unit itself plays a minor, historical role.
Misconceptions often stem from conflating candlepower with lumens, resulting in exaggerated claims in consumer products where high candlepower ratings imply superior overall brightness, despite measuring only directional intensity. This confusion enables overstated marketing, such as multi-million candlepower spotlights that underperform in total light delivery, with no regulatory oversight in non-professional goods.
