 Jews (Hebrew: , romanized: Yehudim) are an ancient ethnoreligious group and nation originating from the Israelites (Hebrew: , romanized: Bnei Yisra'el) and Hebrews (Hebrew: , romanized: Ivrim) of the Iron Age Levant kingdoms. They share descent from biblical patriarchs, adherence to Judaisma monotheistic Abrahamic faith centered on the Torahand genetic markers of primary Middle Eastern ancestry with diaspora admixtures.
As of 2025, their global population is about 15.8 million (0.2% of humanity), concentrated in Israel (7.2 million) and the United States (67 million). Despite this small share, Jews claim 22% of Nobel Prizes since 1901, with outsized impact in physics, chemistry, medicine, economics, and literature. Their history features ancient sovereignty in Israel and Judah, repeated exiles and expulsions over two millennia, and genocides like the Holocaust, yet resilience fostered a diaspora culture stressing education, solidarity, and ethical monotheism.

Terminology and Etymology

Origins of the Term

The Hebrew term Yehudi (), from which "Jew" derives, originally denoted a member of the tribe of Judahone of Jacob's twelve tribesor an inhabitant of the Kingdom of Judah, established after the united Israelite monarchy divided around 930 BCE. After the Assyrian conquest of the Northern Kingdom of Israel in 722 BCE and the Babylonian destruction of Judah in 586 BCE, followed by exile and return, Yehudi expanded in the 6th century BCE to include all surviving Israelites, with Judahites at the core of the restored community in Yehud Medinata (the Province of Judah), an Achaemenid Persian province.
In contrast, "Hebrew" (Ivri) predates Yehudi as an ethnic or linguistic term, likely from the second millennium BCE, applied to Abraham's descendants or Hebrew speakers without tribal or territorial ties. "Israelite," from "Israel" (Jacob's name in Genesis 32:28), served mainly as a pre-exilic national label for the twelve tribes under united or divided kingdoms, focusing on covenantal descent rather than post-exilic geography.
The term reached Greek as Ioudaios () via Aramaic yehudhai, used in the Septuagint (3rd2nd centuries BCE) to translate Yehudi, referring to both Judean residents and their practices. Latin Iudaeus or Judaeus evolved into Old French giu and Middle English Gyw or Jew by the 12th century CE, fixing Western usage for the people once called Israelites.

Modern Usage and Variants

In contemporary English, "Jews" is the standard noun for the ethnic and religious group. Some prefer "Jewish people" or "Jewish individuals" to avoid historical antisemitic connotations, but many Jewish writers and organizations view "Jew" as neutral and appropriate. This preference emerged in the 20th century to counter derogatory associations, though surveys show broad acceptance of "Jews" in formal and academic contexts, with avoidance often driven by external sensitivities.
"Jew" is capitalized in English as a proper noun for adherents of Judaism or the Jewish ethnos, matching styles for "Christian" or "Muslim," despite style guide debates over uncapitalized terms like "gentile."
External powers imposed "Palestinian" as a geographic exonym on Jews to emphasize their connection to the territory, often regardless of self-identification. Following the Bar Kokhba Revolt's suppression in 135 CE, Rome renamed Judea Syria Palaestina to sever Jewish national ties, a designation persisting in Western administrative and scholarly thought. During the Enlightenment, Immanuel Kant described European Jews as "the Palestinians living among us" to highlight their perceived foreign status. This reached its legal peak under the British Mandate (19201948), issuing "Palestinian" citizenship and passports to Jewish residents, with institutions like the Palestine Post adopting the label for recognition.
Modern variants include ethnic subgroup identifiers from post-medieval dispersions: "Ashkenazi" for Jews tracing descent from Rhineland and later Central/Eastern European communities, now the global majority; "Sephardi" for those from Iberia before the 1492 expulsion, resettling in North Africa, the Ottoman Empire, and the Americas; "Mizrahi" for Jews indigenous to the Middle East and North Africa, with ancient Near Eastern roots predating Arab conquests but distinguished in modern terms from Sephardi liturgical traditions.
Israel's Law of Return (enacted July 5, 1950, amended 1970) defines a Jew as born to a Jewish mother or converted to Judaism, excluding members of other religions, granting aliyah and citizenship. It follows Orthodox halakha's matrilineal standard, extending eligibility to grandchildren, while diverging from patrilineal recognitions in Reform circles, prioritizing verifiable maternal lineage or conversion to prevent fraud amid post-Holocaust goals.

Identity

Ethnic and Genetic Dimensions

Jewish populations show genetic continuity from ancient Levantine origins, evidenced by shared Y-chromosome haplogroups J1 and J2 that dominate paternal lineages across Ashkenazi, Sephardi, Mizrahi, and other subgroups. These haplogroups, prevalent among Middle Eastern non-Jews like Palestinians and Jordanians, reflect a common inland Levantine ancestral pool, with Jewish samples clustering near Syrians, Lebanese, and Jordanians. Genome-wide analyses position Jewish groups closer to northern Fertile Crescent populations (e.g., Kurds, Armenians) than neighboring Arabs, confirming Bronze Age Levantine paternal descent.
Mitochondrial DNA traces reveal subgroup-specific maternal admixture differing from paternal patterns. Ashkenazi Jews derive about 40% of mtDNA variation from four founders with prehistoric European ancestry, mainly Southern European including Italian, via haplogroups K and N1b. Recent 2025 mtDNA studies align Ashkenazi maternal lines with Southern and Western European sources, indicating early medieval gene flow from Italian-like populations rather than Near Eastern continuity. Sephardi and Mizrahi maternal lineages retain more Levantine and regional Middle Eastern/North African elements with less European input; North African Jewish groups cluster between European Jews and local Berbers.
Autosomal DNA quantifies admixture on a Middle Eastern core: Ashkenazi Jews average 50-80% Levantine ancestry plus 20-50% Southern European, rejecting major Eastern European or Caucasian inputs. Sephardi Jews include up to 20-30% Iberian and North African elements; Mizrahim show mostly undiluted Middle Eastern profiles; Ethiopian Jews blend Levantine with East African signals. Traces of African ancestry (3-5%) appear across groups, dating to ~2,000 years ago via ancient trade. Medieval Ashkenazi remains (14th-century Erfurt) support this, with ~33% early Eastern European admixture evolving through endogamy to modern levels.
Genetic evidence refutes the Khazar hypothesis of Turkic-Caucasian Ashkenazi origins. Principal component analyses and linkage disequilibrium mapping place Ashkenazim near Italians and Southern Europeans, distant from Khazar-proximate groups like Armenians or Georgians, with no detectable Khazar ancestry. This aligns with diaspora admixture models preserving Bronze/Iron Age Jewish signatures amid host intermixing; founder effects and endogamy sustain subgroup distinctions despite 30-60% non-Levantine admixture.

Religious and Cultural Definitions

In halakha, traditional Jewish law defines Jewish identity through matrilineal descenta child of a Jewish mother is Jewish regardless of the father's statusor formal conversion involving acceptance of the mitzvot, ritual immersion, and male circumcision. This Talmudic principle, as in Kiddushin 68b, prioritizes maternal lineage, with conversion as the main alternative. Orthodox and Conservative Judaism uphold this for communal recognition and rituals like marriage or aliyah under Israel's Law of Return.
Reform Judaism recognizes patrilineal descent if the child is raised Jewish via education, home practice, and public identification, per the Central Conference of American Rabbis' 1983 resolution. Aimed at interfaith families in the diaspora, this approach faces rejection outside Reform and fuels denominational debates on authenticity.
Beyond halakha, cultural Jewish identity encompasses mitzvot observancesuch as Shabbat from Friday sunset to Saturday night, kosher laws, and bar/bat mitzvaheven without orthodox belief. Traditionally 613 in number (248 positive, 365 negative), these practices promote cohesion and continuity, enduring secularly as heritage symbols. Yet they clash with secular claims of Jewishness via ancestry or culture alone, bypassing halakha's ritual focus.
Unlike proselytizing faiths such as Christianity or Islam, Judaism maintains low historical conversion rates, with active recruitment discouraged by halakhic stringencyrequiring sincere commitment and often a rabbinic court reviewand external factors like medieval bans on Jewish proselytism under Christian and Muslim rule. Conversions, while possible and documented in antiquity (e.g., during the Hellenistic period), comprised a negligible fraction of Jewish population growth, estimated at under 10% in pre-modern eras, prioritizing endogenous transmission over expansion.

Debates on Inclusion and Conversion

Orthodox Judaism upholds the traditional halakhic matrilineal descent for Jewish identity, rejecting patrilineal descent. Reform Judaism, via its 1983 resolution, recognizes a child as Jewish if either parent is Jewish and the child receives Jewish upbringing, adapting to intermarriage trends. Conservative Judaism retains matrilineal descent but accepts some patrilineal cases with rigorous Jewish education. These differences drive disputes: Orthodox authorities view patrilineal acceptance as eroding halakhic integrity and promoting assimilation, diverging from Talmudic maternal lineage standards.
Conversion heightens tensions. Orthodox criteria include intensive study, male circumcision, mikveh immersion, and commitment to all mitzvot before a beit din; non-Orthodox processes are deemed inadequate. In Israel, Chief Rabbinate courts apply these for marriage and burial, often nullifying Reform or Conservative conversions, even as the Law of Return offers citizenship based on ancestry alone. Progressive critics argue this excludes committed individuals; Orthodox defenders, referencing past insincere conversions that caused discord, emphasize verifiable adherence for communal stability.
High U.S. intermarriage rates58% for Jews married between 2005 and 2013 per Pew data, up from 43% in 1990, with non-Orthodox at 71%have intensified debates, as intermarried households show lower synagogue affiliation and ritual observance among children. While about two-thirds of such couples raise children with some Jewish identity, only 33% ensure formal education, compared to higher retention in endogamous families. Advocates of stricter criteria argue that inclusive policies yield numerical growth but substantive loss of distinctiveness, underscoring boundary maintenance for minority preservation.
Forced conversions, such as during the Spanish Inquisition from 1478, compelled tens of thousands of Jews to nominally adopt Christianity, fostering crypto-Jewish practices amid suspicion and fragmentation, which culminated in Spain's 1492 expulsion of up to 200,000. Unlike these coerced cases, contemporary voluntary inclusivity debates question whether lax standards risk similar erosion by admitting those without full commitment; Orthodox insist on rigorous vetting to safeguard against historical patterns undermining internal vitality.

Origins

Biblical Accounts

The Book of Genesis narrates Israelite origins through Abraham, called by God from Ur of the Chaldeans to Canaan with an unconditional covenant of land, protection, and descendants as numerous as stars. Renewed with son Isaac and grandson Jacob (renamed Israel), it establishes identity as a chosen people tied to monotheistic fidelity.
Exodus recounts Jacob's descendants multiplying in Egypt, then enslaved until God raises Moses for deliverance via ten plagues and Red Sea parting, dated traditionally to 1446 BCE by biblical timeline. At Sinai, God reveals the Torah and Ten Commandments, mandating sole worship of Yahweh (, Paleo-Hebrew: ) and ethical duties bound to the land.
Joshua details Canaan conquest under his command, with divine acts like Jericho's walls falling and city-state battles, fulfilling Abraham's promise via judgments on Canaanite idolatry. The judges era follows, cycling through apostasy and rescue to stress obedience's rewards and disobedience's penalties.
Samuel and Kings describe a united monarchy around 1000 BCE, starting with Saul, then David conquering Jerusalem and Philistines, and Solomon erecting the First Temple for monotheistic rites. Prophets Isaiah and Jeremiah uphold Yahweh's sovereignty, Israel's ethical mission, and covenant endurance against idolatry-driven exile.

Archaeological Evidence

The earliest extrabiblical reference to Israel is on the Merneptah Stele, an Egyptian victory inscription from c. 1208 BCE describing a Canaan campaign: "Israel is laid waste; his seed is not," depicting Israel as seminomadic rather than a state. Housed in Cairo's Egyptian Museum, it attests Israel in the southern Levant among defeated groups like Ashkelon and Gezer.
Archaeological surveys show Israelite identity emerging gradually in Canaan's central hill country during the late 13th12th centuries BCE, with over 250 new settlements featuring four-room houses, collar-rim jars, and absent pig bonestraits distinguishing them from coastal Philistine sites. This ethnogenesis arose indigenously from local Canaanites, evident in continuous pottery and settlement patterns, without signs of massive invasion or widespread destruction layers from an Israelite incursion. Mainstream consensus finds no material trace of a large-scale exodus from Egypt, as such an event would produce detectable disruptions in Egyptian records or Sinai sites, which are lacking. 
By the 9th century BCE, inscriptions confirm Israelite polities. The Tel Dan Stele, an Aramaic victory monument from c. 840 BCE erected by an Aramean king (likely Hazael), boasts defeats of the "king of Israel" and "king of the House of David," providing extrabiblical evidence for a Davidic dynasty in Judah. Destruction layers at Lachish and Jerusalem, dated to 586 BCE via pottery, seals, and Babylonian arrowheads, corroborate the Neo-Babylonian conquest under Nebuchadnezzar II, which razed the First Temple and much of the cityas shown by burned structures and ash deposits verified through magnetic and stratigraphic methods. Roman forces under Titus destroyed the Second Temple in 70 CE, attested by Jerusalem debris layers with melted artifacts and weapons; the Arch of Titus in Rome depicts Temple spoils, including the menorah, paraded in triumph.

Genetic Studies

Genetic studies using autosomal DNA analysis since the early 2000s reveal a shared ancestral component in Jewish populations tracing to the ancient Levant, combined with admixture from host populations during diaspora. These methods, relying on genome-wide markers and principal component analysis, position Jewish groups intermediately between Levantine and regional Eurasian or African populations. A 2010 study of diverse cohorts identified substantial Middle Eastern signatures across Ashkenazi, Sephardic, and Mizrahi samples, indicating continuity from Bronze Age Levantine sources with post-exilic gene flow. Recent analyses, such as a 2020 high-resolution study of Jewish structure in Europe, the Middle East, and North Africa, confirm this pattern, dating admixture to medieval periods rather than population replacements.
Ashkenazi Jews show about 50% Levantine-derived autosomal ancestry, plus 30-40% Southern European (mainly Italian-like) input from post-2010 admixture models. This reflects endogamous growth from a bottlenecked founder population of roughly 350 individuals around 600-800 CE, marked by high runs of homozygosity and linkage disequilibrium patterns. The bottleneck, supported by Ashkenazi genome sequencing, contributes to elevated recessive disorders like Tay-Sachs and Gaucher disease, with carrier rates up to 1 in 25 for some alleles. Maternal mtDNA indicates ~40% European ancestry from four prehistoric founders, while paternal Y-chromosomes align more with Near Eastern profiles, pointing to sex-biased admixture in early medieval migrations.
Sephardic and Mizrahi Jews show closer genetic affinity to Middle Eastern and North African populations, with autosomal profiles indicating 70-90% regional continuity and minimal European admixture beyond Iberian influences after 1492. A 2012 analysis of North African Jews revealed distinct gene pools sharing Levantine-Maghreb components, separate from non-Jewish Berber groups, which points to limited conversion-driven origins. Mizrahi groups from Iraq to Yemen cluster nearest to ancient Levantine proxies in global PCA plots.
Autosomal and uniparental marker data refute hypotheses of Ashkenazi origins from mass European conversions or Khazars; Khazar-specific Turkic or Caucasian signals are absent, and principal components align Ashkenazim closer to other Jews than to proposed source groups. Although Elhaik's 2013 model suggested Khazar admixture, subsequent critiques and larger datasetsincluding 2013 Y-chromosome analyses of Levitesaffirm Levantine paternal continuity over alternative narratives. Endogamy preserved core ancestry amid dispersals, as shown by low Fst distances among Jewish subgroups relative to outgroups.

History

Ancient Kingdoms and Exile

The united monarchy under Kings David and Solomon (c. 1020930 BCE) fragmented after Solomon's death into the northern Kingdom of Israel (ten tribes, capital Samaria) and southern Kingdom of Judah (tribes of Judah and Benjamin, centered on Jerusalem). Heavy taxation and forced labor under Rehoboam sparked the schism, fostering rivalry and divergent paths.
Assyria's incursions began in 732 BCE under Tiglath-Pileser III, deporting annexed populations, and culminated in Shalmaneser V and Sargon II's conquest of Samaria in 722 BCE. Annals report ~27,000 deportees replaced by foreigners, aiding Israelite assimilation and the "Ten Lost Tribes" legend. Destroyed sites like Hazor and Megiddo confirm devastation archaeologically, though deportation scale is debated due to potentially exaggerated Assyrian inscriptions.
Judah endured as an Assyrian vassal, paying tribute (e.g., Jehu depicted on the Black Obelisk c. 841 BCE), but faced Babylonian threats after Assyria's fall. Nebuchadnezzar II's 597 BCE siege deported King Jehoiachin and ~10,000 elites, including artisans and priests; the 586 BCE conquest razed Jerusalem, destroyed the First Temple, and exiled ~832 more, targeting urban skilled classes while sparing rural peasants. Babylonian chronicles and finds like ash layers and arrowheads on Mount Zion attest to the siege's intensity.
The Babylonian Exile (597539 BCE) entailed phased deportations to Mesopotamia. Exiles sustained communal structures and religious practices, promoting textual preservation, prophetic literature, and cultural adaptation. Cyrus the Great's 539 BCE conquest of Babylon yielded his 538 BCE edict allowing Jewish return and Temple rebuilding; the Cyrus Cylinder evidences his repatriation policy for displaced peoples and cult restorations, with Jewish details from later sources. Persian rule saw returnees under Zerubbabel complete the Second Temple by 516 BCE, regaining limited Yehud province autonomyyet many stayed in Babylon, broadening Jewish communities.
Alexander the Great's 332 BCE conquest incorporated Judea into Hellenistic realms with minimal resistance, as local leaders yielded en route from Tyre to Egypt. Ptolemaic and Seleucid alternation followed until Antiochus IV Epiphanes' 167 BCE bans on Jewish rites, Temple desecration via Zeus altar, and Hellenization mandates provoked the Maccabean Revolt under Judah Maccabee. Guerrilla successes rededicated the Temple in 164 BCE and forged Hasmonean independence by 140 BCE, a fleeting Jewish sovereignty against Hellenistic tides.

Early Diaspora

The First Jewish-Roman War (6670 CE) arose from Roman taxation and governance disputes in Judaea, ending with Titus's destruction of Jerusalem and the Second Temple. Flavius Josephus estimated over 1.1 million deaths and 97,000 Jews enslaved and dispersed across the empire. Temple loss ended sacrifices and central authority, shifting Judaism toward synagogue practices and textual study.
The Bar Kokhba Revolt (132135 CE), led by Simon bar Kokhba against Hadrian's Aelia Capitolina founding and circumcision ban, marked the final Judean independence push. Roman forces under Julius Severus killed an estimated 580,000 combatants (per Cassius Dio), with mass enslavement and exile depopulating Judaea and expanding the diaspora. Hadrian's renaming to Syria Palaestina sought to sever Jewish land links, cementing dispersion.
These crises fostered rabbinic Judaism as a portable continuity system. Yochanan ben Zakkai founded a Yavneh academy ca. 70 CE, preserving Pharisaic oral law traditions. It standardized prayers replacing sacrifices and initiated Mishnah compilation, enabling survival without Temple or sovereignty. Diaspora communities in Alexandria and Antioch expanded westward to Italy, southern Gaul, and Hispania, while Parthian Mesopotamia sheltered eastern groups from Roman rule.
Organized Jewish life in northern Europe emerged by the 4th century CE, as evidenced by Constantine's 321 CE decree requiring representatives in Cologne's curia, though 1st-century claims lack archaeological support. In North Africa, communities in Cyrenaica and Carthagepredating the revolts but bolstered by post-revolt migrationsintegrated into Roman provincial society while upholding distinct practices. These networks enabled trade and cultural exchange, preserving Jewish identity within host societies until medieval changes.

Medieval Developments

During the medieval period from approximately 500 to 1500 CE, Jewish communities evolved distinctly in Christian Europe and Islamic lands, shaped by varying degrees of tolerance, economic restrictions, and intellectual pursuits. In Europe, Ashkenazi Jews established Rhineland communities in western Germany around the 10th century, forming a distinct cultural group within the Holy Roman Empire. These often formed voluntary quarters for religious convenience, self-defense, and communal autonomy (e.g., Judengasse in German cities), but faced increasing segregation as the Lateran Councils of 1179 and 1215 promoted separation from Christiansparticularly the Third Lateran Council's canon prohibiting cohabitationfostering assigned quarters that anticipated later enclosed ghettos.
Excluded from guilds and land ownership, Jews turned to moneylendingfilling a niche barred to Christians by church usury bans but allowed under Jewish law for non-Jewswhich aided survival but bred resentment. This fueled accusations, including the first blood libel in 1144 Norwich, falsely charging Jews with ritually murdering boy William. Spreading libels incited violence and expulsions, such as Edward I's Edict of 18 July 1290 banishing ~3,000 English Jews and seizing property for wars and debts, and Philip IV's 1306 expulsion of ~100,000 French Jews to fund fiscal crises.
In contrast, Sephardi Jews in Islamic territories, especially Al-Andalus under Umayyad rule from the 8th century, enjoyed relative prosperity as dhimmisprotected non-Muslims paying the jizya tax for religious and communal autonomy. This allowed intellectual growth, with Baghdad's Talmudic academies under the Geonim serving as global hubs until the 11th century, issuing responsa and upholding Babylonian Talmud traditions. In Cordoba, Hasdai ibn Shaprut founded a key academy in the 10th century, importing Baghdad libraries and promoting Talmud, philosophy, and medicine during the 10th12th-century "golden age" of Jewish-Arab synthesis.
Scholars like Moses Maimonides (11381204), born in Cordoba, embodied this era: he codified law in the Mishneh Torah, wrote Guide for the Perplexed to align Aristotelian reason with Torah, and fled Almohad persecution to Egypt in 1168. Yet tolerance varied; the 1147 Almohad invasion curtailed Spain's golden age, compelling conversions or exile, and dhimmi status shielded neither from recurrent zealotry nor exploitation. These patterns revealed tensions: Europe's economic roles spurred backlash amid dependence, while Islamic integration hinged on rulers' pragmatism, not equality.

Early Modern Period

The Early Modern Period opened with intensified medieval anti-Jewish measures in Western Europe. Spain's Alhambra Decree, issued March 31, 1492, by Ferdinand II and Isabella I, mandated conversion or departure by July 31, displacing about 200,000 Jews, many of whom died en route. Portugal followed in 1496 under King Manuel I with an expulsion order, but shifted to mass forced conversions in 1497, fostering crypto-Judaism and later inquisitorial flights. These expulsions scattered Sephardic Jews to the Mediterranean, North Africa, and the Ottoman Empire. Sultan Bayezid II welcomed them, sending ships for evacuation and granting settlement, seeing economic benefits despite opposition.
In Italy, expulsions occurred in areas like the Papal States and Naples, but Venice created the first ghetto in 1516, segregating Jews on Cannaregio island per Senate decree. Restrictions curbed residence, commerce, and movement, yet allowed moneylending and trade for republican gain. This segregation model spread across Italian cities, favoring controlled coexistence over expulsion due to economic value. Meanwhile, Eastern Europe offered refuge: the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth's tolerant policies from the 14th century drew Ashkenazi and Sephardic Jews, growing their numbers to over 450,000 by 1600. Jews led in leaseholding, trade, and crafts during a "golden age" of autonomy via the Council of Four Lands.
This prosperity shattered during the Khmelnytsky Uprising of 1648, led by Cossack hetman Bohdan Khmelnytsky against Polish rule in Ukraine, resulting in massacres targeting Jewish estate managers and communities, with contemporary accounts estimating up to 100,000 Jewish deaths from violence, starvation, and disease across over 300 settlements, decimating the population and prompting theological crises. Religious ferment followed, exemplified by the Sabbatean movement, when Ottoman Jew Sabbatai Zevi proclaimed himself Messiah in 1665, gaining fervent followers across Europe and the Levant through kabbalistic prophecies disseminated by Nathan of Gaza, only for Zevi's 1666 conversion to Islam under threat to fracture the movement into antinomian sects challenging rabbinic orthodoxy. These upheavals underscored the period's volatility, blending refuge with recurrent peril and internal schisms.

Emancipation and Nationalism

The emancipation of Jews began with the French Revolution, when the National Assembly granted full citizenship to Jews on September 27, 1791, marking the first time a European state extended equal rights to its Jewish population without requiring religious conversion. This decree applied the principles of the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen to approximately 40,000 Jews in France, abolishing special taxes and occupational restrictions, though implementation varied regionally until fully realized by 1799. The French model influenced gradual emancipation elsewhere in Western Europe, with the Netherlands following in 1796, Prussia partially in 1812, and full equality achieved in the unified German Empire in 1871, enabling Jews to enter professions, universities, and civil service previously barred to them. 
Intellectual movements accompanied these legal changes, notably the Haskalah, or Jewish Enlightenment, which emerged in the mid-18th century in German-speaking lands under figures like Moses Mendelssohn, who advocated rationalism, secular education, and cultural integration while upholding Jewish observance. This fostered secularization and adaptation, giving rise to Reform Judaism in early 19th-century Germany, where rabbis like Abraham Geiger modified ritualssuch as introducing vernacular sermons and organ musicto align with modern sensibilities and promote ethical universalism over strict traditionalism. Emancipation thus spurred both assimilation and internal reform, with Jews achieving prominence in finance, science, and arts in Western Europe, yet often facing social exclusion that reinforced communal ties.
Persistent antisemitism, however, undermined the promise of integration, as evidenced by the Dreyfus Affair of 1894, in which French army captain Alfred Dreyfus, convicted on fabricated treason charges amid widespread anti-Jewish sentiment, exposed how emancipation failed to eradicate prejudice even among the assimilated elite. In Eastern Europe, where emancipation lagged, tsarist Russia's restrictions fueled pogroms, including waves in 18811882 and the Kishinev massacre of April 1903, where mobs killed 49 Jews, wounded hundreds, and destroyed property, incited by blood libel rumors and official inaction. These events prompted political responses, notably Theodor Herzl's 1896 pamphlet Der Judenstaat, which argued that assimilation was illusory and proposed a sovereign Jewish state in Palestine as the only safeguard against recurrent violence. 
As an alternative to Zionism, mass emigration surged, with approximately two million Jews fleeing Eastern Europe's pogroms and economic restrictions for the United States between 1881 and 1914, reshaping global Jewish demographics and fostering new communities centered on labor activism and cultural preservation. This period's tensionsbetween emancipation's opportunities and nationalism's imperativeshighlighted causal realities: legal equality did not neutralize deep-seated ethnic animosities, driving Jews toward either territorial self-determination or geographic escape.

20th Century Crises

After the 1917 Bolshevik Revolution, Jewscomprising 4-5% of party membersheld disproportionate leadership roles, including Leon Trotsky as People's Commissar for Foreign Affairs and War, and Yakov Sverdlov as the first head of state. Amid the civil war and Red Terror's 50,000-200,000 executions, this fueled the antisemitic "Judeo-Bolshevism" theory portraying communism as a Jewish plot, amplified by White Russian migrs and Nazi ideologues despite no evidence of coordinated conspiracy.
In Mandatory Palestine, the Fifth Aliyah (1929-1939) brought heightened Jewish immigration amid European antisemitism, raising tensions with Arabs and sparking the 1929 riots over Western Wall access in Jerusalem. These killed 133 Jews and wounded 339, with massacres in Hebron (67 dead) and Safed (18-20 dead), prompting British troop deployments and Jewish self-defense via the Haganah. The ensuing 1936-1939 Arab Revolt featured strikes, bombings, and assaults claiming over 500 Jewish and 5,000 Arab lives (many from intra-Arab or British clashes); Jews responded with special night squads led by Orde Wingate to guard settlements.
Nazi Germany's rise on January 30, 1933, began systematic Jewish persecution, including April 1 business boycotts and the September 15, 1935, Nuremberg Laws stripping citizenship and banning intermarriage. Kristallnacht (November 9-10, 1938) killed 91 Jews, arrested 30,000, and destroyed over 1,000 synagogues, spurring emigration by about 300,000 German and Austrian Jews by 1939hindered by quotas and the vian Conference's July 1938 failure, where 32 nations mostly declined higher intakes.
World War II escalated these crises into the Holocaust. The Nazi invasion of Poland on September 1, 1939, confined Jews to ghettos such as Warsaw (holding 400,000 by 1941), while Einsatzgruppen mobile killings murdered over 1 million by late 1941. The Wannsee Conference on January 20, 1942, coordinated the "Final Solution" for systematic extermination, establishing death camps like Auschwitz-Birkenau, where 1.1 million perished mainly via gas chambers. By May 1945, approximately 6 million Jews had been murdered through shootings, starvation, and gassing.

Post-1948 Developments

The State of Israel was established on May 14, 1948, after the United Nations Partition Plan and the British Mandate's end, fulfilling Zionist goals for a Jewish homeland amid post-Holocaust displacement and regional conflicts. Israel then absorbed about 3.2 million Jewish immigrants by 2017from Arab countries in the 1950s, Ethiopia in the 1980s1990s, and the former Soviet Unionexpanding its Jewish population from roughly 650,000 in 1948 to over 7 million by the 2020s. Known as aliyah, this influx stemmed from persecution, economic prospects, and ideology, yet strained resources, demanded ethnic integration, and coincided with persistent security threats.
Israel confronted existential threats in the Six-Day War (June 510, 1967), preemptively striking Egypt, Jordan, and Syria amid escalations like Egyptian Sinai deployments and the Straits of Tiran blockade; Israel seized the Sinai Peninsula, Golan Heights, West Bank, and Gaza Strip. The Yom Kippur War opened on October 6, 1973, with Egypt and Syria launching a surprise attack on the Jewish holy day, breaching defenses before Israeli counteroffensives; casualties exceeded 2,500 Israeli dead, and cease-fires enabled later accords like the 1979 Camp David treaty. These wars highlighted Israel's vulnerabilities, dependence on swift mobilization, and internal debates on security and territorial policies.
In the diaspora, the United States held the largest Jewish population, at 7.7 million in 2024 (2.3% of the total), concentrated in New York and California. Soviet Jewish emigration rose in the 1970s, with 165,000 reaching Israel by 1988 through refusenik campaigns and global pressure; over 1 million followed from the former Soviet Union in the 1990s after its dissolution, enhancing Israel's high-tech economy while posing integration hurdles. 
The October 7, 2023, Hamas-led attack on southern Israel killed about 1,200 peoplemostly civiliansand resulted in 251 hostages taken to Gaza, prompting Israel's military operation to dismantle Hamas infrastructure amid international scrutiny over civilian casualties in densely populated areas. In the U.S., antisemitic incidents spiked afterward, with the Anti-Defamation League documenting surges in campus harassmentover 25% of Jewish students reported faculty promoting anti-Jewish hostilityand 507 tracked incidents in the 20252026 academic year, often tied to anti-Israel protests blurring into antisemitism despite distinctions claimed between criticism and bigotry. Advocacy and congressional reports link this rise to radicalized activism fostering prejudice, while critiquing university administrations for weak responses amid ideological influences.

Religion

Core Tenets and Practices

Judaism affirms strict monotheism: one incorporeal, eternal, and omnipotent God as the uncaused cause of creation, who formed an eternal covenant with the Jewish people through Abraham and at Sinai. This doctrine rejects anthropomorphism, plurality, and idolatry, requiring exclusive worship of this deity, as in the Shema from Deuteronomy 6:4: "Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one." Maimonides' 12th-century Thirteen Principles of Faith outline core beliefs, including God's omniscience, the Torah's immutability as Moses' divine revelation, Moses' unique prophetic status, reward and punishment by deeds, the Messiah's arrival for peace and ingathering of exiles, and bodily resurrection.
The 613 mitzvot248 positive and 365 negative commandmentsfrom the Five Books of Moses form Judaism's practical foundation, binding Jews through ethical, ritual, and civil laws as a perpetual covenant. Key practices include Shabbat, a weekly halt to creative work from Friday sunset to Saturday nightfall, emulating divine rest (Exodus 20:8-11); kashrut, barring pork, shellfish, blood, and mixing meat with dairy (Leviticus 11; Deuteronomy 14); and brit milah, circumcising male infants on the eighth day (Genesis 17:10-14), which overrides Shabbat in halakha. Daily prayers, ideally three times in a minyan of ten adult males at synagogue, replace Temple sacrifices and include the communal Amidah to affirm submission to God.
The High Holidays emphasize repentance. Rosh Hashanah marks the civil new year in early Tishrei with shofar blasts symbolizing awakening and God's kingship, initiating the Ten Days of Awe that culminate in Yom Kippura 25-hour fast centered on confession, prayer, and atonement rituals for divine forgiveness. Judaism teaches that mitzvot enable tikkun olam by elevating kabbalistic divine sparks scattered in matter, thus rectifying creation through covenantal duties rather than universal ethics alone. Modern secular adaptations repurpose tikkun olam for social justice activism, diverging from its traditional halakhic emphasis on Torah observance as the path to cosmic repair.

Denominations and Schisms

Divisions within Judaism deepened after the Haskalah, the Jewish Enlightenment of the late 18th and early 19th centuries, which introduced rationalist critiques and demands for cultural adaptation, eroding traditional rabbinic authority. This period spawned non-Orthodox denominations seeking alignment with modernity, while Orthodox Judaism upheld halakhalaw from Torah and Talmudas immutable, opposing changes to rituals.
Reform Judaism began in Germany around 1810, stressing personal autonomy, ethical priorities over rituals, and reforms such as vernacular liturgy and gender equality to match Enlightenment principles. Conservative Judaism emerged in the United States by the 1880s as a moderate alternative, maintaining halakha's validity but allowing evolution through historical study, including egalitarian clergy and adjusted dietary practices. Reconstructionist Judaism, launched by Rabbi Mordecai Kaplan in the 1920s, treats Judaism as an evolving civilization guided by communal democracy, reducing emphasis on the supernatural and highlighting cultural aspects.
Intra-Orthodox divisions arose before the Enlightenment's full effects, especially the 18th-century split between Hasidisminitiated by Israel Baal Shem Tov around 1730, promoting ecstatic mysticism, devotion, and rebbes as guidesand the Mitnagdim under the Vilna Gaon, who prioritized Talmudic discipline and dismissed Hasidic fervor as superstitious. After mutual excommunications, the groups partially reconciled by the 19th century under Orthodoxy, though Hasidic courts remain prominent.
Haredi (ultra-Orthodox) Judaism, including Hasidic and non-Hasidic adherents, has grown rapidly to 2.1 million globally (14% of world Jewry) by 2022, with 3.5-4% annual increases from fertility rates of 6-7 children per woman. In Israel, Haredim reached 1.28 million (13.5% of the population) in 2023 and are projected at 16% by 2030, heightening tensions with secular Jews over military exemptions, state-funded yeshivas, and workforce participation. Secular Israelis, emphasizing national over religious identity, see Haredi insularity as economically draining and politically blockinga divide ranked among Israel's top issues by 73% of Jews in polls.

Philosophical and Ethical Contributions

Biblical prophets like Amos (c. 760 BCE) and Isaiah emphasized social justice over insincere rituals, condemning exploitation of the vulnerable and demanding righteousness in worship. Amos proclaimed God requires justice to "roll down like waters," urging hatred of evil and love of good for societal equity. Isaiah linked true fasting to freeing the oppressed and feeding the hungry, framing ethics as covenantal duty beyond mere observance. These teachings stress moral causality in societal harms and subordinate ritual to interpersonal justice, shaping later Jewish ethics.
Rabbinic Judaism refined ethical reasoning through Talmudic dialecticsrigorous debate dissecting dilemmas via thesis, antithesis, and resolutioncompiled between the 3rd and 6th centuries CE. The Talmud curbs discourse with bans on slander, balances individual rights and communal duties (e.g., in joint ownership and hypotheticals), and favors logical scrutiny over dogma, upholding ethical integrity in private and public life.
Early modern thinkers Baruch Spinoza and Moses Mendelssohn highlighted tensions between faith and reason. Excommunicated on July 27, 1656, for heresy, Spinoza advanced pantheismequating God with naturerejecting anthropomorphism and favoring reason-derived, deterministic ethics over revelation. Mendelssohn, conversely, reconciled Judaism with Enlightenment values; in Jerusalem (1783), he portrayed Jewish law as rational and divinely ordained, aligning with civic tolerance, natural religion, and ethical universalism while retaining ritual.
Judaism's ethical frameworkmonotheistic accountability and precepts like care for the strangershaped Christianity and Islam by transmitting moral law and Sabbath rest into their traditions. The Mosaic covenant's chosenness elects Jews for moral responsibility to exemplify ethical monotheism, not superiority; though critiqued for separatism amid universal duties, Emmanuel Levinas reframes it as ethical priority for the other, emphasizing causal realism in relations.
Twentieth-century Orthodox philosopher Joseph B. Soloveitchik, in The Lonely Man of Faith (1965), portrayed humanity's dual naturemajestic conqueror via reason and humble covenantal partner via faithas irreconcilable yet complementary, rejecting synthesis for existential tension that enriches ethical depth without contradiction. Abraham Joshua Heschel blended mysticism and activism, viewing faith as an active leap attuned to divine pathos in suffering, driving prophetic action against injustice, including civil rights. These ideas highlight Judaism's revelation-intellect dialectic, prioritizing empirical moral realism over abstract universalism.

Culture

Languages and Literature

Hebrew, a Northwest Semitic language attested from the late second millennium BCE, formed the core of ancient Jewish linguistic heritage as both vernacular and literary medium until the Babylonian Exile in the 6th century BCE. After the exile, Aramaica related Semitic languageemerged as the primary administrative and spoken tongue among Jews in the Persian Empire and beyond, shaping Hebrew syntax and vocabulary while Hebrew endured in scholarly and sacred roles. The Dead Sea Scrolls, found near Qumran between 1947 and 1956 and dating from the 3rd century BCE to 1st century CE, contain the oldest surviving Hebrew manuscripts, illustrating the shift from biblical to Mishnaic Hebrew and continuity in everyday and legal texts.
In the diaspora, Jews created hybrid vernaculars mixing Hebrew with local tongues. Yiddish, blending High German with Hebrew and Slavic elements, arose in the 9th10th centuries CE among Ashkenazi Jews in the Rhineland and spread eastward, peaking at 1113 million speakers by the 1930s before the Holocaust reduced it to about 600,000 today, mostly in ultra-Orthodox communities in the United States and Israel. Ladino (Judeo-Spanish), based on medieval Castilian with Hebrew and Ottoman Turkish influences, developed after the 1492 Spanish expulsion among Sephardic Jews; it once supported 100,000175,000 speakers but now has fewer than 100,000, mainly in Israel, Turkey, and the Balkans. These tongues enabled secular literature, folklore, and trade, separate from liturgical Hebrew.
Eliezer Ben-Yehuda drove the late 19th-century revival of Hebrew as a spoken language starting in the 1880s, converting it from a liturgical relic into Israel's official vernacular through neologisms rooted in biblical and Mishnaic sources. His work, including a comprehensive dictionary published posthumously in 1922, adapted Hebrew for Zionist immigrants' daily use, establishing it as a living language during the 1920s Mandate period.
Jewish literature in these languages includes profane genres from medieval secular poetry to modernist prose. Yehuda Halevi (c. 10751141), a Spanish-Jewish polymath, wrote Hebrew verses on exile, nature, and philosophy, blending Arabic poetic forms with Hebrew metrics in his Diwan and influencing Iberian-Jewish expression. In the modern era, Hayim Nachman Bialik (18721934) advanced revived Hebrew poetry, exploring diaspora alienation and national revival in collections like Sefer ha-Aggadah (19081911), which compiled folk narratives and elevated Hebrew prose. Franz Kafka (18831924), writing in German, incorporated Jewish existential motifs from Yiddish theater into novels like The Trial (1925), bridging Central European Jewish vernaculars to universal themes without Hebrew composition. Yiddish literature, such as Sholem Aleichem's late 19th-century tales of shtetl life, captured social realities before the language's decline.

Customs and Social Norms

Jewish lifecycle customs, derived from biblical commandments, mark key transitions through rituals. Male infants undergo brit milah (circumcision) on the eighth day, performed by a mohel and including naming, even on Shabbat. At age 13 for boys (or 12-13 for girls in non-Orthodox traditions), bar mitzvah or bat mitzvah denotes religious adulthood, with responsibility for mitzvot (commandments) and often public Torah reading. Marriage features a ketubah, a contract detailing the groom's duties to the bride, read publicly under the chuppah to affirm commitments.
Endogamy has reinforced community cohesion, though rates vary by region and denomination. As of 2023, global intermarriage is 26%, with Israel's 5% rate contrasting the Diaspora's 42% amid assimilation pressures. In the U.S., 72% of non-Orthodox Jews married non-Jews from 2010-2020 per Pew, often yielding less observant offspring. Orthodox communities sustain near-total endogamy via matchmaking (shidduch).
Kashrut, prohibiting pork, shellfish, and meat-dairy mixing, shapes daily meals and communal boundaries. U.S. observance varies: 95% of Orthodox maintain kosher homes, versus 24% Conservative and 5% Reform. In Israel, 46% keep strict kashrut and 23% partial, aided by rabbinic hechsher certifications. Many non-Orthodox selectively observeavoiding non-kosher meat but mixing dairyto adapt to modern life without full abandonment.
Gender roles vary across denominations, blending continuity and divergence. Orthodox traditions assign men public ritual leadership, such as leading prayers and forming minyanim, while exempting women from time-bound mitzvot to emphasize family; this Talmudic division prioritizes complementary roles over equality. In contrast, Reform and Conservative Judaism advance egalitarianism, ordaining women rabbis since 1972 in Reform, with mixed seating and shared obligations shaped by feminist influences and halakhic reinterpretations.
Universal literacy and education form a core norm, rooted in males' daily Torah study mandate, which achieved near-total male literacy by the medieval period amid European rates below 10%. Boys began memorizing Scripture in cheders from age five, a practice now including girls in Orthodox schools to transmit knowledge and build communal resilience.
Tzedakahas obligatory justice, not voluntary charityrequires aiding the needy, favoring fellow Jews and loans over handouts to promote interdependence; Talmudic rankings prioritize anonymous giving to uphold dignity. Family units stay central, with multigenerational support strengthening identity during dispersion.

Arts and Intellectual Traditions

Jewish intellectual traditions emphasize rigorous debate and analytical reasoning, especially through pilpul, a Talmudic study method of dialectical disputation to resolve textual contradictions. Emerging in the 15th16th centuries among Ashkenazi scholars, it honed logical distinctions and verbal precision in yeshivas via intense, adversarial arguments over rabbinic texts. Prioritizing agility over consensus, this approach extended forensic argumentation into wider philosophical discourse.
Jewish music includes klezmer, an Ashkenazi Eastern European folk tradition from the Yiddish klezmer (Hebrew kli zemer, "instrument of song"), performed by professionals at weddings. Featuring dance rhythms, modal scales blending Eastern European and Middle Eastern influences, and improvisational solos, it peaked in the 19thearly 20th centuries, declined post-Holocaust, and revived in the U.S. from the late 1970s. Complementing it, cantorial music (hazzanut) offers emotive synagogue chanting akin to classical vocals, with a "Golden Age" in the early 20th century; virtuosos like Yossele Rosenblatt preserved intense prayer renditions in recordings.
Jewish visual arts long followed aniconic biblical bans on images, restricting figurative work in religious settings until modernity. After 19th-century emancipation, secular artists joined mainstream styles; Marc Chagall (18871985) fused Jewish themesshtetl life, Hasidim, Yiddish symbolswith dreamlike, cubist elements. Holocaust responses included White Crucifixion (1938), portraying Jewish torment via Christian symbols for universal appeal, and Yellow Crucifixion (1943), evoking pogroms and exile in fiery, fragmented scenes.
In performing arts, Jewish comedians pioneered irreverent humor in early 20th-century American film, as seen in the Marx Brothers' vaudeville-derived mockery of authority in Duck Soup (1933). Woody Allen later extended neurotic, self-deprecating narratives in Annie Hall (1977), drawing on Borscht Belt traditions where humor defended against antisemitism by exaggerating stereotypes of anxiety. Critics argue that such self-deprecation, while resilient, risks reinforcing tropes and limiting broader comedic vitality.

Demographics

Population Estimates

The core global Jewish populationindividuals who self-identify as Jewish above all else and do not adhere to another religionstands at approximately 15.8 million as of 2025. Demographer Sergio DellaPergola's annual projections yield this figure by aggregating censuses, surveys, and vital statistics with consistent criteria to prevent double-counting or non-core inclusions. Enlarged estimates, including partial ancestry or affiliated non-Jews via marriage or descent, surpass 20 million but vary due to differing affiliation standards.
From 15 million in 2010, the population grew modestly by 6% over the decade, driven mainly by Israel's fertility rate (averaging 3 children per Jewish woman) and aliyah, especially post-2022 amid instability, though offset by sub-replacement diaspora birth rates (except among Orthodox), intermarriage, and assimilation reducing later self-identification. Israel holds 7.2 million Jewish residents as of 2024 per its Central Bureau of Statisticsnearly half the global totaland accounts for most net growth through births and immigration.
Diaspora estimates face challenges from outdated surveys and proxies like synagogue membership, which may distort active identification; DellaPergola prioritizes self-reports over ancestry for cross-country consistency. Annual growth is projected at 0.5-1%, hinging on curbed assimilation and Israel's demographic strength.

Ethnic Subgroups

Ashkenazi Jews, about 70-80% of the global Jewish population, originated in medieval Rhineland communities of Germany and northern France, migrating eastward to Poland, Lithuania, and Russia after 11th-century expulsions and pogroms. Their genetics reflect a 600-800-year-ago bottleneck, with 50-80% Levantine ancestry admixed with European elements and elevated endogamy-driven mutations like Tay-Sachs. Culturally, they developed Yiddisha Hebrew-German-Slavic fusionand liturgical traditions with unique prayer pronunciations and melodies.
Sephardi Jews primarily descend from Iberian Peninsula communities expelled by the 1492 Alhambra Decree for refusing conversion, dispersing to North Africa, the Ottoman Empire, and parts of Europe. Genetically, they blend Middle Eastern, Iberian, and North African ancestries, with less European admixture than Ashkenazim but shared Levantine markers confirming ancient Israelite roots. Their culture features Ladino (Judeo-Spanish), preserved in ballads and proverbs, and a prayer rite shaped by scholars like Maimonides, distinct from Ashkenazi in synagogue melodies and holiday practices.
Mizrahi Jews, distinct from Sephardim despite some adoption of Sephardic rites, comprise indigenous Middle Eastern and North African communities with ancient continuity, including in Iraq, Yemen, Syria, and Persia. Genetic studies show the highest unmixed Middle Eastern ancestry among subgroups, minimal European input, and local admixtures like Persian or Arab from long regional residence. They preserve dialects such as Judeo-Arabic and Judeo-Persian, plus customs like Yemenite henna ceremonies or Iraqi piyyutim (liturgical poems) predating Iberian Sephardic traditions.
Smaller subgroups include Beta Israel (Ethiopian Jews), whose traditions claim descent from the Tribe of Dan or ancient Egyptian migrants; they practiced pre-rabbinic Judaism without Talmudic influence until modern times, with genetics revealing Levantine paternal lineages amid African maternal lines. Mountain Jews (Juhuro), from Azerbaijan's and Dagestan's Caucasus, speak Juhuria Judeo-Tat language akin to Persiantrace to ancient Persian exiles, show Iranian Jewish genetic ties, and maintain highland martial traditions. Autosomal DNA studies indicate these groups cluster around an ancient Judean core but diverge via isolation and admixture, yielding distinct disease risks and practices.
Since Israel's 1948 founding, intermarriages among subgroups have risen, especially Ashkenazi-Sephardi/Mizrahi unions in Israelfrom negligible early rates to over 20% by the 1990sdue to urbanization and national identity, though ultra-Orthodox endogamy endures. This blending homogenizes some genetic and cultural traits without effacing core distinctions from millennia of divergence.

Geographic Distribution

Israel has the largest Jewish population, approximately 7.76 million as of September 2025, due to immigration and natural growth since 1948. The United States follows with around 7.7 million, mostly Ashkenazi and urban-based, forming the second-largest diaspora. France ranks third at about 440,000, mainly Sephardi and North African, but declining due to security-driven emigration.
Canada (398,000, concentrated in Toronto and Montreal) and the United Kingdom (312,000, mostly in London) each exceed 300,000. Argentina has about 170,000 in Buenos Aires, the largest in Latin America. Australia maintains roughly 120,000, primarily in Sydney and Melbourne from post-World War II migration.
Europe's Jewish population fell from nearly 9.5 million in 1939 to 1.3 million today, with communities in Germany (118,000) and Russia (under 150,000) affected by emigration to Israel and assimilation. Globally, Jews cluster in cities: New York City's metro area holds over 1.5 million, and Israel's Gush Dan region more than 3 million. These concentrations support communal institutions but increase vulnerability to disruptions.

Demographic Trends

The global Jewish population has grown slowly since the mid-20th century, driven by Israel's higher fertility rates, while diaspora communities stagnate or decline due to sub-replacement fertility and assimilation.
Israel's Jewish total fertility rate reached 3.06 children per woman in 2024, above replacement level and surpassing Muslim women's 2.86 for the first time. This rate has held steady near 3.0 for decades, fueled by cultural preferences for larger families among even secular Jews, though delayed marriages and rising non-marriage signal a gradual drop. Diaspora rates, by contrast, fall between 1.5 and 2.0, mirroring low-fertility patterns in developed countries and worsened by secularization and intermarriage, which reduce the number of children raised Jewish.
Assimilation drives diaspora losses, with U.S. intermarriage hitting 61% for marriages from 2010-2020 (up from 45% in 2000-2009); 42% of married U.S. Jews are intermarried overall, rising to 47% among non-Orthodox, often resulting in children who do not identify as Jewish. Declining religious observance further erodes identity transmission, yielding negative population dynamics despite immigration gains.
World War II halved the global Jewish population, from 16.6 million prewar to 11 million postwar, via the murder of 6 million, mostly in Europe (home to 9.5 million in 1933) and concentrated in the Easta gap yet to close.
Net migration favors Israel, with French aliyah averaging 3,000 yearly amid antisemitism and spiking over 500% in interest after October 7, 2023. The Ukraine war since 2022 has prompted relocations, but inflows offset under half of Israeli emigrants and fail to stem diaspora assimilation.

Achievements and Influence

Scientific and Intellectual Contributions

Jews have received about 22% of Nobel Prizes from 1901 to 2020, despite comprising less than 0.2% of the global population. In physics, chemistry, and physiology or medicine since 2000, they account for 26% of prizesroughly 110 times their population sharebased on laureates identified as Jewish by heritage or upbringing.
Notable physics contributions include Albert Einstein's 1921 Nobel for the photoelectric effect, extending his relativity theories that reshaped space-time and gravity. Niels Bohr's 1922 prize advanced atomic quantum models, and Richard Feynman shared the 1965 award for quantum electrodynamics in particle physics. In medicine, Ernst Chain's 1945 Nobel enabled penicillin's mass production, while Jonas Salk's 1955 polio vaccine reduced U.S. cases from 35,000 annually to near zero by 1961.
Economics Nobels follow suit, with Jews taking around 40% since 1969; Milton Friedman won in 1976 for consumption and monetary analysis, and Paul Samuelson in 1970 for mathematical economic models.
Studies estimate Ashkenazi average IQ at 107-1150.75-1 standard deviation above European normswith verbal IQ near 125, linking to abstract-field innovation. Cochran, Hardy, and Harpending attribute this to medieval selection for demanding roles like finance and scholarship, plus persecution-induced bottlenecks favoring intelligence traits. Causal factors remain debated but, with culture, explain outsized achievements.

Economic and Societal Roles

In medieval Europe, Christian laws and barriers restricted Jews from owning land, joining guilds, and many crafts, directing them to moneylendingprohibited for Christians by usury canon law. This enabled survival but sparked resentment over debts to Jewish lenders, fueling expulsions among nobility and clergy. Accumulated expertise later shaped modern banking among Jewish families.
The Rothschild dynasty illustrates this: Mayer Amschel Rothschild (17441812) started as a Frankfurt coin dealer and court factor, then built international finance via his five sons in key European cities by the early 1800s. Their network funded governments, wars, and projects like British Napoleonic subsidies, scaling historical roles into major banking houses.
Today, Jews (2% of U.S. population) hold outsized economic roles, comprising 2030% of American billionaires per 20242025 lists. Tech examples include Larry Ellison ($213.7 billion, software) and Mark Zuckerberg ($202.4 billion, social media).
Jewish immigrants founded major Hollywood studios in the early 1900sUniversal (Carl Laemmle, 1912), Paramount (Adolph Zukor, 1912), MGM (Louis B. Mayer, 1924), Warner Bros. (Warner brothers, 1923)using East Coast exclusion to develop the U.S. film hub. This influence endures in executive roles and entertainment.
Prominent Jewish philanthropists exert societal impact through targeted giving. George Soros has donated billions via the Open Society Foundations since 1979 to promote global democracy and human rights. Sheldon Adelson and Miriam Adelson contributed over $200 million annually to Jewish causes, including $25 million to Yad Vashem in 2011 and funding for Birthright Israel. 
In politics, AIPAC demonstrates lobbying power, spending $51.8 million on 2024 contributions and $3.3 million on lobbying for U.S.-Israel policies. This shaped congressional outcomes, such as defeating Israel critics via primary challenges exceeding $23 million against two incumbents in 2024. 

Explanatory Factors

Ashkenazi Jews have an average IQ of 107115, above the general mean of 100, with strengths in verbal intelligence. One hypothesis, from researchers like Cochran, Hardy, and Harpending, links this to medieval European selection pressures. Jews, restricted to demanding roles in moneylending, trade, and management, faced rewards for intelligence and heterozygote benefits from sphingolipid disease alleles, such as Tay-Sachs, in neural growth; the idea remains debated. Centuries of endogamy fostered genetic isolation and founder effects, curbing gene flow from outsiders.
From the second century CE, religious rules required male literacy for Torah study, moving Jews from farming to urban trades, finance, and commerce. This shift built diaspora networks and specialization. Yeshivas, stressing Talmudic analysis, sharpened reasoning, verbal skills, and discipline, sustaining high education; Jews average 13.4 years of schooling globally, surpassing other religious groups. Cultural norms thus amplified genetic factors via human capital transmission.
Group cohesion generated network benefits: trust and shared knowledge cut transaction costs and boosted mobility. Preindustrial European cities with Jews grew 30% faster than similar ones without. Critics cite nepotism for success, but evidence favors merit in intelligence-driven fieldsmirroring overseas Chinese, who use education, endogamy, and internal trade for outsized influence as minorities. These patterns emphasize adaptive group traits over singular cultural factors.

Controversies

Historical Persecutions and Responses

Medieval antisemitism shifted from theological anti-Judaismrooted in Christian doctrines of Jewish deicide and supersessionto violent scapegoating, including blood libels, well-poisoning accusations, and economic resentments, especially amid Crusades and Black Death crises. These drew on religious (deicide and ritual claims), economic (moneylending resentments), social (outsider perceptions from endogamy and practices), and political (ruler exploitation for gain) factors under Christian dominance. Historians debate continuity with modern racial antisemitism: some stress enduring tropes, others a secular-pseudoscientific rupture, while analyses of Rhineland massacres see them as bridging genocidal forms.
In 1096, during the First Crusade, Rhineland bands targeted Jews in Speyer, Worms, and Mainz, killing 2,0005,000 overall (Hebrew chronicles and estimates; historians often cite 2,0004,000) via massacres fueled by deicide charges and zeal. Hebrew sources like the Solomon bar Simson Chronicle and Mainz Anonymous portray martyrdom as redemptive heroism, while Latin accounts such as Albert of Aachen's link violence to crusading against Christendom's foes, often minimizing non-combatant harm amid biases. Mainz saw 8001,100 deaths (Hebrew figures, varying scholarly views; Speyer ~11, Worms ~800), with attackers enforcing conversions or slaughtering resisters in synagogues and homes. Crusading fervor amplified theological animosity toward Christendom's perceived enemies, though papal edicts condemned non-combatant violence.
The Black Death (13481351) triggered pogroms across Europe, with Jews accused of well-poisoning, destroying 200350 communitiesestimates range from 210 to over 350 incidentsmainly in the Holy Roman Empire, Switzerland, and Low Countries. Entire populations vanished in Strasbourg (2,000 burned alive) and Basel, while Antwerp and Brussels communities ended by 1350, leaving few Jews in Germany or the Low Countries. Building on blood libels and moneylending resentmentsenabled by Christian usury bans amid debtor crisestorture-extracted confessions drove the violence, despite 1348 papal bulls exonerating Jews and citing natural causes.
Medieval expulsions compounded these massacres, motivated by fiscal gain, religious pressures, and social exclusion. Edward I expelled England's ~3,000 Jews in 1290, confiscating property for wars after taxation reduced their utility. Philip IV expelled French Jews in 1306 to seize assets for debts (accounts claimed ~100,000 affected, though numbers were lower), repeating in 1322 and 1394. Comparable decrees targeted southern Italy (early 14th century) and German principalities (through 15th century), often over ritual murder claims or refusal to convert. Jews' finance niche, barred from landownership and guilds, bred envy and scapegoating in downturns, reinforced by deicide myths.
Jewish responses emphasized communal solidarity and defiance, with many choosing martyrdom (kiddush ha-Shem) over conversion during the 1096 massacresincluding extreme self-sacrifice like suicide and infanticide to evade capture, torture, or baptismas Jeremy Cohen analyzes, framing these acts as accelerating messianic redemption amid trauma that shaped Ashkenazi memory and expectations. Survivors rebuilt via cohesion, relocating to eastern Europe or Italy for protections, as persecutions inspired kabbalistic and messianic literature expecting divine aid. Mobility and endogamy sustained identity despite forced conversions; halakhic bans on intermarriage and idolatry blocked integration, amplifying otherness perceptions. These traits drove diaspora shifts eastward to Poland and Lithuania, aiding adaptation and endurance against recurrent threats.

Claims of Disproportionate Influence

Claims of disproportionate Jewish influence in various sectors have persisted for centuries, rooted in historical occupational restrictions that directed Jews into finance and trade. In medieval Europe, Christian prohibitions on usury excluded Christians from moneylending, while Jews faced barriers to guilds, land ownership, and other trades, funneling them into this niche. This focus built lasting financial expertise, leading to overrepresentation in banking and commerce after emancipationdespite Jews forming less than 1% of Europe's population. These patterns show no evidence of conspiracy and are often exploited for baseless allegations.
In the modern United States, Jews make up about 2.4% of the population (roughly 7.5 million people), yet they exceed this share in elite finance. Jewish founders established key Wall Street firms like Goldman Sachs and Lehman Brothers, and they hold prominent roles today, including CEOs of BlackRock (Laurence Fink) and Goldman Sachs (David Solomon). Jews represent 20-25% of top U.S. billionaires in 2025 estimates, underscoring influence in investment and business. In finance, Jews are overrepresented by 3-5 times their population share, due to cultural stress on education58% of Jewish adults have college degrees versus 29% of all U.S. adultsand urban professional concentrations. Comparable overrepresentation from education and migration affects other groups, such as Indian Americans (1.5% of the population) leading about 3% of Fortune 500 companies.
Similar patterns appear in media and entertainment, where Jews played foundational roles in establishing Hollywood studios such as Paramount, MGM, and Warner Bros., driven by exclusion from other industries and opportunities in emerging film production. Today, disproportionate Jewish involvement persists in executive and creative positions, though major conglomerates like Disney and Comcast own these entities, diluting direct control narratives. Claims of monolithic "media control" often invoke tropes like the fabricated Protocols of the Elders of Zion, a 1903 Russian forgery plagiarized from earlier satires and exposed as such in 1921 by The Times of London, yet patterns of overrepresentation fuel perceptions of coordinated influence absent evidence of conspiracy. These antisemitic narratives evolved into key propaganda elements, particularly in the Nazi era, where the Protocols were disseminated to justify persecution and contributed to the Holocaust despite their debunked origins. Such tropes have also fueled real-world violence, including the 2018 Pittsburgh synagogue shooting, the deadliest antisemitic attack in U.S. history, where 11 worshippers were killed by a perpetrator motivated by antisemitic hatred. Amid rising antisemitic incidents, with the Anti-Defamation League reporting a record 9,354 incidents in 2024surpassing all previous annual totals since tracking began in 1979these claims continue to pose harms. Scholarly analyses attribute observed overrepresentations to high educational attainment and cognitive factors rather than conspiratorial cabals.
Accusations of dual loyalty arise from observable diaspora affinities to Israel, including financial and political support, with surveys indicating around 51% of respondents in recent Western polls endorsing the view that Jews prioritize Israel over host nations. These claims, historically leveled since Roman times, highlight tensions between ethnic solidarity and national allegiance but lack substantiation of disloyalty in policy influence beyond standard lobbying, with similar perceptions applied to other diaspora groups such as Irish Americans regarding Ireland. Empirical overrepresentation in power centerssuch as Ivy League enrollment (historically 10-20 times population share)has seen declines post-2023, with Jewish student numbers dropping at institutions like Harvard, Columbia, and Cornell due to rising antisemitism on campus; this stems from high educational attainment and selective migration, not inherent cabals, though it invites scrutiny of causal networks like familial and communal ties. 

Ideological Entanglements

Jews exhibited notable overrepresentation in early Bolshevik leadership relative to their share of the Russian population, which stood at approximately 4-5% in the early 20th century. In the 1917 Central Committee, 6 of 21 members (~29%) were Jewish, though overall party membership reached only 5.21% Jewish by the 1922 census. For instance, Leon Trotsky (born Lev Bronstein), Grigory Zinoviev, and Lev Kamenev held key positions such as People's Commissar for Foreign Affairs and members of the first Politburo, while Yakov Sverdlov served as the initial head of the Soviet state. Many early Jewish Bolsheviks were non-practicing or rejected Judaism and perished in Stalin's purges. While Jewish individuals were overrepresented in early Bolshevik leadership, the majority of Russian Jews supported other parties such as the Bund or Zionists rather than the Bolsheviks in 1917 elections, reflecting broader socialist but not exclusively Bolshevik inclinations. This prominence stemmed from factors including high literacy rates among Jewsfostered by religious study and urban concentrationand systemic exclusion from tsarist professions, positioning them as outsiders receptive to ideologies promising equality and emancipation from pogroms and the Pale of Settlement restrictions. 
Such patterns extended to broader socialist movements in Europe and America during the late 19th and early 20th centuries, where Jews comprised a disproportionate share of activists in parties advocating class struggle and internationalism. Historians attribute this to causal dynamics of marginalization: repeated expulsions and discriminatory quotas channeled intellectual energies toward universalist revolutions that rejected ethnic hierarchies, contrasting with assimilationist paths blocked by host societies. Yet, this involvement drew critiques from contemporaries and later analysts, who argued it facilitated radical ideologies undermining traditional orders, with estimates for Jewish figures in early Cheka security roles varying; primary data show overall low representation (~3.7% in the Moscow apparatus, 9.1% provincial in 1920), though higher in specific investigator departments (~19-50% in some), but not exceeding 30% broadly; similar overrepresentations occurred among other small ethnic groups, such as Latvians who formed about 35% of central Cheka employees in 1918 despite comprising a tiny fraction of the population. 
In American liberalism, Jewish figures played foundational roles in organizations advancing civil rights for minorities, exemplified by Joel Elias Spingarn, who became chairman of the NAACP board from 1913 to 1939 and helped shape its early legal strategies against segregation. Pre-1945 efforts included funding and advocacy for anti-lynching legislation, driven by parallels drawn between Jewish persecution and African American oppression, though some observers contend this promotion of multiculturalism eroded cohesive national identities in host countries by prioritizing group rights over assimilation. Such views, articulated in evolutionary and historical analyses, posit that diaspora strategies favoring pluralism mitigated immediate threats but imposed long-term costs on majority cultures.
Conservative counterexamples illustrate variability: urban intellectualism, rooted in mercantile and scholarly traditions, did not always produce leftism, as seen among Orthodox Jews (about 60% conservative). Former Trotskyists Irving Kristol and Norman Podhoretz critiqued New Deal excesses and allied with William F. Buckley Jr. through National Review in the 1950s-1960s, shaping anti-communist fusionism and early neoconservatism. Their shift responded to radical overreach and Soviet antisemitism, demonstrating how outsider status could yield universalist radicalism or defensive traditionalism based on threats. Thus, 19th- and early 20th-century leftward tilts arose from exclusion and intellectual concentration, but exceptions show no monocausal determinism.

Contemporary Conflicts

Surge in Antisemitic Incidents Post-October 7

Following the Hamas terrorist attacks on Israel on October 7, 2023, which killed approximately 1,200 people and took over 250 hostages, antisemitic incidents in the United States surged to record levels. The Anti-Defamation League (ADL) documented 8,873 antisemitic incidents in 2023, a 140% increase from 2022, and 9,354 in 2024, a 5% increase from 2023. The FBI reported approximately 1,832 anti-Jewish hate crimes in 2023, accounting for 68-69% of religion-based hate crimes, rising to 1,938 in 2024, a slight 5.8% increase and the highest ever recorded. Trends persisted into 2025, with the ADL noting a dramatic rise in antisemitic incidents in its Year in Review and the American Jewish Committee (AJC) reporting in its State of Antisemitism in America 2025 that 31% of American Jews experienced an antisemitic incident, confirming the persistence of elevated levels. This resurgence included both far-right tropes, such as conspiracy theories about Jewish control, and far-left expressions framing Jews collectively as complicit in Israeli policies, with some anti-Zionist rhetoric criticized as crossing into antisemitism per definitions like the IHRA.
Comparatively, Europe experienced similar surges; in France, nearly 1,570 antisemitic acts were reported in 2024, following highs in 2023 that quadrupled post-October 7, with some linked to Islamist extremism.

Campus Dynamics and Protests

On U.S. college campuses, anti-Israel protests intensified post-October 7, with encampments, chants calling for Israel's elimination, and incidents targeting Jewish students, such as blocking access to events or harassment. ADL tracked around 1,694 such incidents in 2024, alongside reports of over 1,400 antisemitic incidents during the 2023-2024 academic year, many involving anti-Zionist rhetoric classified as antisemitic, including endorsements of Hamas or comparisons of Israel to Nazi Germany. While some protests focused on policy critiques and human rights groups like the ACLU raised concerns about suppression of demonstrations, defending certain chants and protests as political expression rather than inherently antisemitic, empirical data from Jewish student surveys indicated heightened fear, with 9 in 10 American Jews perceiving increased campus antisemitism. In response, universities formed task forces to address antisemitism following federal hearings in December 2023, which exposed leadership failures and led to resignations at institutions like Harvard and the University of Pennsylvania.

BDS Movement and Economic Claims

The Boycott, Divestment, and Sanctions (BDS) movement, a Palestinian-led nonviolent campaign launched in 2005 to pressure Israel economically and politically, gained traction amid these tensions, targeting companies and universities for divestment. Despite claims by BDS advocates of contributing to Israel's economic strain, a 2015 Israeli government analysis estimated annual losses at under $1.4 billion, or less than 0.5% of GDP, with negligible overall impact on trade or investment due to Israel's diversified economy. Supporters view it as accountability for occupation policies, while critics argue it delegitimizes Jewish statehood, echoing historical boycotts; adoption remains limited, rejected by major U.S. states and the European Union. In Jewish diaspora communities, BDS has fueled internal divisions.

IHRA Definition Debates

Debates over the International Holocaust Remembrance Alliance (IHRA) working definition of antisemitism, adopted by the U.S. State Department in 2010 and over 40 countries, as well as more than 1,334 entities worldwide by 2026, intensified, as it includes examples like denying Jewish self-determination as potentially antisemitic depending on context. Proponents cite it for clarifying boundaries amid rising incidents, while critics, including some human rights groups and institutions facing backlash such as Harvard's 2025 adoption perceived as chilling speech, argue it stifles legitimate Israel criticism and conflicts with academic freedom, though empirical adoption has correlated with better hate crime tracking without suppressing speech, per government reports. In Europe, similar disputes arose, with left-leaning governments resisting full endorsement amid accusations of protecting anti-Zionist extremism.

U.S. Political and Donor Shifts

In U.S. politics, Jewish donors exhibited splits, with traditional liberal leanings challenged by post-October 7 security concerns; pro-Israel PACs like AIPAC spent over $100 million in 2024 primaries targeting Democrats critical of Israel, such as Cori Bush (with over $8 million in her race) and Jamaal Bowman, contributing to their defeats, while some donors shifted rightward amid perceptions of Democratic tolerance for far-left antisemitism. Orthodox and younger conservative Jews increasingly aligned with Republicans on Israel and border security, contrasting with Reform Jews' support for progressive causes, highlighting diversity in Jewish political alignments.

Immigration Policy Tensions

Diaspora Jewish advocacy for open immigration policies reflected differing priorities from Israeli emphases on security vetting, informed by data linking unchecked migration to terrorism; in Europe, Jewish communities reported spikes in antisemitic attacks, multifaceted in origin including far-right elements, protests, and some tied to Islamist extremism potentially among migrants, with France seeing incidents from such sources. Israeli policy prioritizes empirical risks, as seen in strict border controls preventing infiltration, while U.S. diaspora groups, per Pew surveys, often prioritize humanitarianism over terrorism statistics.

Broader Implications

These developments highlight ongoing debates over antisemitism's boundaries, empirical rises in incidents, and tensions between free speech concerns and protections against hate, including critiques of Israel, without resolution favoring one perspective.
