St. Petersburg paradox

 The St. Petersburg paradox is a classic problem in probability theory and decision theory, in which a gamble offers an infinite expected monetary value but rational agents are willing to pay only a finite amount to participate, challenging the notion that decisions should be based solely on expected value. In the game's setup, a fair coin is flipped repeatedly until it first lands on heads; if heads appears after exactly n flips (with the first n-1 flips being tails), the player receives a payoff of 2n2^n2n units of currency, yielding probabilities of (12)n\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^n(21)n for each outcome n = 1, 2, 3, ..., and an expected value of n=1(12)n2n=n=11=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^n \cdot 2^n = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 1 = \inftyn=1(21)n2n=n=11=.
The paradox originated in 1713 when Swiss mathematician Nicolas Bernoulli posed the problem in correspondence with French mathematician Pierre Rmond de Montmort, highlighting inconsistencies in early probability theory regarding infinite expectations. It gained prominence through analysis by Daniel Bernoulli, Nicolas's cousin, in his 1738 paper "Exposition of a New Theory on the Measurement of Risk," presented to the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg, where he introduced the concept of moral expectationnow known as expected utilityto explain why individuals exhibit risk aversion due to diminishing marginal utility of wealth. This resolution posited that the utility of money decreases logarithmically, transforming the infinite monetary expectation into a finite utility value (a value of 2 using log base 2 utility on the payoffs, corresponding to a certainty equivalent of $4).
Over centuries, the paradox has influenced foundational developments in economics and decision theory, including Gabriel Cramer's 1728 suggestion of square-root utility and later formalizations in von Neumann-Morgenstern utility theory (1944), which incorporated risk aversion via concave utility functions. Empirical studies, such as those by Hayden and Platt (2009), confirm that participants bid medians of $1.50$1.75 to play despite the infinite expectation, supporting alternative explanations like the median heuristic, where decisions prioritize typical outcomes over rare extremes. Other proposed resolutions address practical constraints, such as finite resources limiting payoffs (Feller, 1957) or non-ergodicity in repeated plays (Peters, 2011), emphasizing that long-run averages diverge from single-instance expectations. The paradox remains a benchmark for exploring bounded rationality, infinite series in probability, and the limitations of expected value maximization in real-world decision-making.

The Game

Rules and Setup

The St. Petersburg game is a straightforward lottery designed as a thought experiment in probability theory. A player pays an upfront entry fee to participate, after which a fair coinassumed to have an equal probability of landing heads or tails on each flipis tossed repeatedly until the first heads appears. The payoff depends on the number of tosses required: if heads occurs on the first toss, the player receives 2 units of currency; if on the second toss (following one tails), the payoff doubles to 4 units; if on the third toss (following two tails), it doubles again to 8 units; and so on, with the payoff equaling 2k2^k2k units if heads appears on the kkk-th toss.
This setup relies on the coin's fairness, where each independent flip has a probability of exactly $ \frac{1}{2} $ for heads or tails, ensuring that the probability of heads first appearing on the kkk-th toss is $ \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^k $. The game's minimal equipment and rules highlight its elegance as a device for probing foundational issues in decision-making under uncertainty, without requiring complex mechanics or multiple participants.

The Paradox

Core Statement

The St. Petersburg paradox centers on a gambling game where a fair coin is flipped repeatedly until it lands heads, with the player receiving a payoff of 2k2^k2k dollars if heads appears on the kkkth flip; the expected monetary value of this game is infinite, yet most rational individuals would pay only a modest finite amount, typically a few dollars, to participate. This discrepancy highlights a fundamental tension: while mathematical calculation suggests unlimited value, human decision-making intuitively limits the entry fee far below infinity, often to around $2 or 2 based on experimental auctions.
Empirical evidence from controlled experiments reinforces this behavioral pattern. In a real-stakes study with university students using a Becker-DeGroot-Marschak auction mechanism, the median willingness-to-pay was $1.75, with bids clustering at $1 or $2 despite the game's infinite expectation. Similarly, classroom experiments across multiple truncations of the game (e.g., maximum payoffs of 250 to 10,000) yielded median bids of 1.50 to 2.00, showing no significant increase even as potential payouts grew, as participants effectively ignored low-probability high-reward outcomes. Hypothetical surveys produce comparable results, with medians around $1.50 and ranges extending to higher outliers but dominated by low values, confirming that actual or simulated choices rarely exceed $10.
This contradiction poses a profound challenge to expected value theory in decision-making, which posits that rational agents should maximize anticipated monetary gains by paying any finite amount to enter a game with infinite expectation. Instead, observed behavior reveals a gap between theoretical infinity and practical valuation, questioning whether expected monetary value alone suffices as a normative guide for choices under uncertainty. The core issue thus becomes reconciling this mathematical ideal with empirical intuition: why do people systematically undervalue the game, and what alternative principles better capture rational conduct?

Historical Context and Discovery

The St. Petersburg paradox originated in the early 18th century amid the Enlightenment's burgeoning interest in probability theory, which had been advanced through analyses of gambling and games of chance by mathematicians like Jacob Bernoulli, whose posthumous Ars Conjectandi (1713) established key principles of probabilistic reasoning. This intellectual environment, focused on quantifying risks in wagers and lotteries, provided the backdrop for the paradox's formulation. The problem was first articulated by Nicolas Bernoulli, a Swiss mathematician and nephew of Jacob Bernoulli, in a letter dated September 9, 1713, to the French probabilist Pierre Rmond de Montmort. In this correspondence, Nicolas described a game involving dice rolls with exponentially increasing payoffs, highlighting the counterintuitive infinite expected value and questioning its implications for rational decision-making.
The paradox gained wider attention through refinements and discussions in probabilistic circles. In 1728, the mathematician Gabriel Cramer proposed a simplified coin-flip variant in a letter to Nicolas Bernoulli, while suggesting that diminishing marginal utility might limit the game's perceived value to a finite amount, around 20 ducats. This idea influenced subsequent thought, including Nicolas's communications with his cousin Daniel Bernoulli. Daniel Bernoulli, also a prominent mathematician, popularized the paradox in a 1738 presentation to the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburghence its namepublished in the academy's proceedings (Commentarii Academiae Scientiarum Imperialis Petropolitanae). In this work, Daniel critiqued the strict application of expected monetary value as inadequate for human behavior, arguing it failed to account for individual risk aversion, though he credited Cramer's earlier insights on utility.
By the 19th century, the paradox continued to provoke analysis amid evolving views on probability's practical limits. In his 1843 treatise Exposition de la thorie des chances et des probabilits, French mathematician and philosopher Antoine Augustin Cournot examined the game's infinite expectation, questioning its real-world applicability by emphasizing that events with exceedingly small probabilitiessuch as prolonged sequences of tails in the coin gameare effectively impossible due to physical and temporal constraints. Cournot posited a "limit to the smallness of chance," suggesting that such extremes rarely, if ever, materialize in practice, thereby bounding the paradox's theoretical infinity.

Resolutions via Utility and Risk

Expected Utility Theory

Expected utility theory provides a framework for decision-making under risk by positing that rational agents maximize the expected value of a utility function over outcomes, rather than the expected monetary value. This approach was formalized by John von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern, who demonstrated that if preferences satisfy certain axiomscompleteness, transitivity, continuity, and independencethen they can be represented by a utility function such that the preference for a lottery is determined by the expected utility of its outcomes.
Daniel Bernoulli applied this concept to resolve the St. Petersburg paradox in 1738, arguing that the infinite expected monetary value fails to account for the diminishing marginal utility of wealth. He proposed a logarithmic utility function $ u(w) = \log(w) $, where $ w $ represents total wealth, reflecting the idea that additional wealth provides progressively less incremental satisfaction. Under this utility, the expected utility of the game, starting from initial wealth $ w_0 $, is given by

The diminishing marginal utility inherent in the logarithmic function explains why individuals exhibit low willingness to pay for the game despite its infinite expected value. For an initial wealth of $ w_0 = 1000 $, the fair entry pricethe amount $ p $ that equates the utility of not playing to the expected utility of playingis approximately $10 to $20, as solving $ \log(w_0) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{2^n} \log(w_0 - p + 2^n) $ yields a small finite $ p $ due to the concave shape of the utility function. This resolution highlights how utility theory aligns theoretical expectations with observed risk-averse behavior in lotteries with heavy-tailed payoffs.

The marginal utility of wealth diminishes as wealth increases, meaning that each additional unit of money provides less incremental satisfaction or utility. This concept is captured by a concave utility function u(w)u(w)u(w), where www denotes total wealth, ensuring that the utility derived from large windfalls grows more slowly than linearly. In the context of the St. Petersburg paradox, this concavity renders the expected utility of the game's infinite expected monetary value finite, as the outsized payoffs, though improbable, contribute disproportionately little to overall utility relative to smaller, more likely outcomes. Daniel Bernoulli first articulated this resolution in 1738, arguing that rational decisions hinge on expected utility rather than expected monetary value alone.

The diminishing marginal utility framework also explains broader behaviors, such as why risk-averse individuals purchase insurance despite its negative expected monetary value. A concave utility function implies that the disutility from a substantial loss (e.g., a catastrophic event) far exceeds the utility from equivalent small gains (e.g., returned premiums), prompting acceptance of a premium to avoid the risk. For instance, full insurance coverage equalizes marginal utilities across states, ensuring the expected utility with insurance surpasses that without, even after accounting for loading fees.
Criticisms of this approach highlight its reliance on specific utility forms, which may not consistently capture observed behavior; for example, it assumes global concavity that fails to explain inconsistencies in risk aversion across stakes, as evidenced by the Allais paradox where individuals violate independence axioms by preferring certain gains over probabilistic ones inconsistently. Additionally, calibration theorems show that mild risk aversion over small gambles implies implausibly extreme aversion over large ones, challenging the empirical fit of concave utilities derived from the paradox resolution. Bernoulli's logarithmic case exemplifies a particular form, but broader applications reveal limitations in predicting gambling or insurance choices under varying wealth levels.

Resolutions via Probability and Bounds

Probability Weighting

One resolution to the St. Petersburg paradox arises from the observation that individuals do not treat probabilities linearly, but instead apply a non-linear probability weighting function, as formalized in prospect theory. Developed by Kahneman and Tversky in 1979, prospect theory posits that decision-makers transform objective probabilities $ p $ into subjective weights $ \pi(p) $, where small probabilities are typically overweighted such that $ \pi(p) > p $ for low $ p $, while moderate to high probabilities are underweighted. The overall subjective value of a prospect is then given by $ \sum \pi(p_i) v(x_i) $, where $ v $ is a concave value function for gains that exhibits diminishing sensitivity to larger outcomes. This weighting, combined with the value function, can yield a finite expected value for prospects with infinite monetary expectation, addressing the paradox by reflecting how people psychologically discount or elevate certain probability ranges.
In the St. Petersburg game, the payoffs $ 2^n $ are associated with successively smaller probabilities $ p = 1/2^n $. For large $ n $, these extreme tail probabilities are very small, and although $ \pi(p) > p $ implies some overweighting, the weighting function $ \pi $ approaches zero rapidly enough in practiceparticularly when integrated with the sublinear growth of $ v(2^n) $to truncate the infinite sum, resulting in a finite subjective valuation. This mechanism explains empirical reluctance to pay high amounts for the game, as the perceived contribution of remote high-payoff events diminishes. Analyses within prospect theory frameworks confirm that appropriate parameter choices for $ \pi $ ensure convergence, resolving the paradox without invoking physical bounds.
Empirical evidence supporting non-linear probability weighting includes demonstrations of subadditivity in judgments of rare events, where the assessed probability of a disjunction falls short of the sum of individual probabilities, indicating that low-probability outcomes are not additively combined but distorted in perception. Such patterns, observed in laboratory tasks involving hypothetical risks, align with the overweighting of small probabilities and provide behavioral validation for the weighting function's role in truncating tail contributions.
A refinement appears in cumulative prospect theory, introduced by Tversky and Kahneman in 1992, which replaces separate weights on individual probabilities with ranking-dependent weights applied to cumulative distribution functions. For prospects with ordered outcomes, like the St. Petersburg game, decision weights become $ \pi(P(X \geq x_k)) - \pi(P(X > x_k)) $ for gains, allowing more nuanced handling of tail probabilities and ensuring finite values under realistic parameters, thus enhancing the theory's explanatory power for infinite-expectation lotteries.

Alternative Perspectives and Rejections

Ignoring Low-Probability Events

One informal approach to resolving the St. Petersburg paradox involves disregarding outcomes with extremely low probabilities, treating them as effectively impossible in practical decision-making. This heuristic reflects a form of practical rationality where decision-makers focus on likely scenarios rather than theoretically infinite tails of the probability distribution. Historically, Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon, proposed in 1777 that rational agents should ignore "morally impossible" events with probabilities below 1/10,000, such as the chance of a 56-year-old man dying within 24 hours (approximately 1/10,189), arguing that such low-probability high-payoff events do not influence real-world choices.
A common rule of thumb in this vein is to ignore events with probabilities less than 10^{-6} (or 1 in a million), beyond which contributions to expected value become negligible in everyday contexts. In the St. Petersburg game, each outcome at step n has probability 1/2^n and payoff 2^n, yielding an expected contribution of 1 per term; however, truncating the series beyond n=20where p 9.5 10^{-7}limits the expected value to approximately 20, as the probability of the game reaching that length is so remote that further terms can be dismissed without material impact on decisions. Similarly, Jean le Rond d'Alembert advocated neglecting cases with very small probabilities, assuming them to be zero to align theory with observed behavior.
This approach manifests in empirical betting prices, which stabilize around $2 when participants implicitly truncate the game at n=12, reflecting a de facto disregard for the infinite tail despite the theoretical divergence. For instance, early analyses showed that excluding outcomes beyond moderate lengths yields finite values consistent with willingness-to-pay observations in controlled settings. The philosophical basis lies in practical probability assessments, akin to everyday decisions where agents prioritize probable events over improbable extremes, as Buffon emphasized to bridge mathematical expectation with human intuition.
Criticisms of this method highlight its arbitrariness: thresholds like 10^{-6} or n=20 lack a principled foundation and vary by context, potentially leading to inconsistent decisions. Moreover, it fails to resolve the theoretical infinity of the expected value, merely sidestepping the paradox without altering the game's formal structure, unlike formalized bounded variants that explicitly cap payoffs.

Rejection of Expected Value Maximization

Critics of expected value maximization argue that it is unduly sensitive to extreme outcomes in the tails of the probability distribution, leading to counterintuitive recommendations in scenarios like the St. Petersburg paradox, where infinitesimal probabilities of enormous payoffs inflate the expected value to infinity. This sensitivity renders expected value an unreliable decision criterion, particularly when rare events dominate the calculation, as rational agents intuitively discount such tails due to practical constraints or behavioral factors.
Historical proponents of rejecting expected value maximization include John Maynard Keynes, who in his 1921 treatise critiqued its application under uncertainty, arguing that it fails to account for the "weight of the argument" or evidential reliability of probabilities, making it inappropriate for the paradox's unbounded setup. In modern robust decision theory, alternatives such as the median or minimax regret criteria have been proposed to prioritize central tendencies or worst-case avoidance over mean-based expectations. For instance, the median payoff in the St. Petersburg game is typically $2, providing a more intuitive finite valuation that aligns with observed willingness to pay.
A key application of rejectionist approaches is the maximin strategy, which focuses on guaranteeing the minimum payoff; in the St. Petersburg game, this yields a low assured return of $2 (the outcome on the first tails flip in standard variants), encouraging caution by limiting exposure to the entry fee beyond this threshold and reflecting real-world risk aversion. This shift has broader implications for decision-making, directing attention to alternative risk measures: notably, the variance of the game's payoffs is also infinite, underscoring the limitations of even second-moment assessments. Coherent risk measures, introduced as subadditive and translation-invariant functionals, offer a robust alternative by assigning finite values to heavy-tailed risks like the paradox, thereby resolving the infinite expectation without invoking utility transformations.

Modern Interpretations

Ergodicity and Repeated Play

In the context of the St. Petersburg paradox, ergodicity refers to the property of a stochastic process where the time average of a quantity over a single trajectory equals the ensemble average across many parallel trajectories. For ergodic processes, these averages coincide, allowing the use of ensemble expectations to predict long-term outcomes for an individual agent. However, the St. Petersburg game is non-ergodic because its multiplicative payout structure leads to diverging time and ensemble averages: the ensemble average payout is infinite, but the time average growth rate for repeated plays remains finite. This non-ergodicity arises from the game's potential for extreme outcomes that disproportionately affect individual wealth trajectories, preventing the interchange of averages.
A resolution to the paradox, proposed by Ole Peters, shifts focus from the infinite expected value of a single play to the expected logarithmic growth rate of wealth in repeated plays, which is finite. Specifically, the relevant quantity is the expectation $ E\left $, where $ w_t $ is wealth at time $ t $, capturing the geometric growth rate rather than arithmetic expectation. This measure evaluates the game's impact on long-term wealth sustainability, yielding a negative value for the St. Petersburg game that discourages participation.
In repeated plays, the infinite expected value of a single round masks severe risks, including potential bankruptcy, due to the non-ergodic nature of the process. Over multiple iterations, an agent faces trajectories where low-probability high-payout events are rare, often leading to wealth depletion before any windfall occurs; thus, the optimal strategy is to avoid the game entirely to preserve capital. Applications in Finance and Decision-Making

In quantitative finance, the St. Petersburg paradox informs models addressing fat-tailed risks, where asset returns exhibit heavy tails leading to potentially infinite expectations that traditional pricing theories like Black-Scholes fail to handle adequately. For instance, options on assets with Lvy-stable distributions can yield infinite variances, prompting alternative pricing frameworks that truncate extreme outcomes or incorporate risk-adjusted expectations to align with observed market behaviors. These models highlight why traders avoid St. Petersburg-like instrumentssuch as highly leveraged derivatives with theoretically unbounded payoffsdespite positive expected values, due to the dominance of volatility and ruin risks in practice.
In behavioral finance, the paradox underscores human resistance to bets with infinite expected value, linking to biases such as the underweighting of low-probability events in prospect theory, where decision-makers insufficiently account for rare extremes. A seminal illustration is Paul Samuelson's 1963 "lunch bet" challenge, where he proposed a fair coin-flip gamble (win $200 or lose $100 with equal probability) to colleagues; while many accepted a single bet, none would accept 100 such bets despite the positive expected value per trial, revealing inconsistencies in expected utility application and aversion to repeated risk exposure. This experiment demonstrates how behavioral factors, including loss aversion, lead decision-makers to reject theoretically favorable infinite-expectation opportunities.
William Feller's 1950s analysis in his probability treatise further elucidates infinite means in the paradox, arguing that while the St. Petersburg game's expectation diverges to infinity, practical bounds arise from finite resources and the law of large numbers' limitations for variables with infinite expectations. Feller emphasized that such infinite expectations do not imply unbounded real-world gains, influencing modern risk assessment in finance by cautioning against overreliance on ensemble averages for volatile investments.
In the 21st century, Ole Peters' ergodicity economics reframes the paradox through time-averaged growth rates rather than ensemble expectations, resolving it without invoking utility functions and applying it to portfolio theory. By focusing on non-ergodic dynamicswhere individual paths differ from population averagesPeters shows that optimal portfolio allocation prioritizes growth rate maximization, explaining why investors shun St. Petersburg-style bets in repeated play and advocating for regulations like margin limits to mitigate systemic risks from fat-tailed exposures.
Recent 2020s research extends these insights to AI decision-making under uncertainty. For example, a 2020 algorithmic study resolves the paradox at the computational level, showing how AI systems can replicate human-like aversion to infinite-expectation gambles through bounded computations or heuristics, without relying on utility functions. Such approaches are relevant to algorithmic trading, where models solely maximizing expected returns may amplify volatility, suggesting the integration of ergodicity principles with machine learning for more robust strategies.
