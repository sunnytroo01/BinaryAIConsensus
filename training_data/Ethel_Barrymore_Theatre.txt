Ethel Barrymore Theatre

1x
 The Ethel Barrymore Theatre is a historic Broadway venue located at 243 West 47th Street in New York City's Theater District, renowned for hosting landmark productions since its opening in 1928. Built by the Shubert brothersLee and J.J. Shubertas a dedicated space for affiliated performers, it is the only surviving theater from their pre-Depression era constructions and has remained under Shubert ownership throughout its history.
Designed by architect Herbert J. Krapp, the theater features a classically inspired Beaux-Arts facade with intricate terra-cotta ornamentation, including a Roman bath-inspired grille-work screen, while its interior showcases elegant Adamesque detailing with garlands, rosettes, and foliate bands for a comfortable yet opulent atmosphere. The venue seats approximately 1,040 patrons and was designated a New York City Landmark on November 4, 1987, recognizing its architectural and cultural significance. It opened on December 20, 1928, with the play The Kingdom of God, starring the theater's namesake, acclaimed actress Ethel Barrymore, a member of the illustrious Barrymore acting dynasty who had risen to fame under producer Charles Frohman in 1901.
Over the decades, the Ethel Barrymore Theatre has been a stage for transformative Broadway works, including the original productions of Pal Joey (1940, starring Gene Kelly), A Streetcar Named Desire (1947, with Marlon Brando and Jessica Tandy, running 855 performances), and A Raisin in the Sun (1959, featuring Sidney Poitier). Revivals and modern hits have also graced its boards, such as The Glass Menagerie (2005, with Jessica Lange), Death of a Salesman (2012, starring Philip Seymour Hoffman), The Band's Visit (2017), The Inheritance (2019), Othello (2025, starring Denzel Washington), and November (2025 revival, starring Nathan Lane). The longest-running show in its history was I Love My Wife (19771979), with 857 performances, underscoring the theater's enduring role in showcasing innovative and critically acclaimed drama, musicals, and plays.

Location and Site

Address and Neighborhood

The Ethel Barrymore Theatre is located at 243 West 47th Street, between Broadway and Eighth Avenue, in the Theater District of Midtown Manhattan, New York City.
This venue sits at the heart of the bustling Theater District, a vibrant entertainment hub immediately adjacent to Times Square, characterized by neon lights, high pedestrian foot traffic, and a concentration of Broadway theaters. It is in close proximity to other prominent venues, such as the Samuel J. Friedman Theatre at 261 West 47th Street and the St. James Theatre at 246 West 44th Street, contributing to the area's dense cluster of performance spaces that attract millions of visitors annually.
Accessibility to the theater is facilitated by extensive public transportation options, including multiple subway lines serving nearby stations: the N, Q, R, and W trains at 49th Street; the 1, 2, and 3 trains at Times Square-42nd Street; the 7 train at Times Square; and the A, C, and E trains at 50th Street or 42nd Street-Port Authority. These connections support the theater's integration into the area's intense pedestrian flow, where theatergoers and tourists navigate crowded sidewalks amid the district's commercial and cultural activity.
The site aligns with Midtown Manhattan's historic grid layout, established in the 19th century under the Commissioners' Plan of 1811, positioning the theater within a block bounded by landmark-designated structures that preserve the area's theatrical heritage. The Barrymore Theatre itself received New York City Landmark designation for its exterior in 1987 (Borough of Manhattan Tax Map Block 1019, Lot 12), with its first-floor interior also protected as an interior landmark that same year, reflecting the surrounding block's role in safeguarding early 20th-century Broadway architecture.

Historical Site Development

The site of the Ethel Barrymore Theatre, located at 243 West 47th Street in Manhattan's Theater District, was part of the area's northward expansion following World War I, driven by population growth, improved mass transit such as the 1904 opening of the IRT subway, and increasing demand for vaudeville and legitimate theater venues. Prior to 1928, the surrounding neighborhood, originally known as Long Acre Square, had been a hub for carriage and harness-making businesses during the late 19th and early 20th centuries, with land uses that were primarily commercial and sparsely occupied at night before the rise of entertainment districts.
In 1927, brothers Lee and J.J. Shubert, founders of the dominant Shubert Organization, acquired the site as part of their aggressive expansion of Broadway theaters, which by the mid-1920s included ownership or control of over 30 venues in New York City; they proposed naming the new house after actress Ethel Barrymore in exchange for her starring in its inaugural production. The Shuberts selected this central location amid the 1920s theater boom to capitalize on the district's growing prominence.
Site preparation involved clearing the lot for construction under the direction of architect Herbert J. Krapp, addressing the engineering demands of building a mid-block theater in a densely developing urban area, including foundation work to support the structure on the irregular plot; the project received necessary regulatory approvals typical of the era's zoning for commercial and entertainment uses in the Theater District. This development contributed to the post-war transformation of the area into a consolidated entertainment hub.

Architecture and Design

Exterior Facade

The Ethel Barrymore Theatre's exterior facade, designed by architect Herbert J. Krapp, draws inspiration from Roman public baths with classically-derived Beaux-Arts influences, marking it as one of his most elaborate commissions for the Shubert brothers. Constructed in 1928, the facade employs glazed white brick in a running bond pattern above a granite water table and rusticated terra-cotta base, creating a symmetrical composition that is wider than it is tall.
Rising three stories to match the site's footprint on West 47th Street, the design emphasizes horizontal emphasis through its broad proportions and decorative elements, including a prominent two-story terra-cotta grillwork screen framed by a large arch. Ornamental cornices feature shallow profiles with Greek key motifs and foliated talon moldings, crowning the upper level beneath a stepped parapet that includes a central arched element for visual balance. The original metal tracery in the grillwork has been replaced with black glazed tiles to preserve the pattern while adapting to maintenance needs.
The street-facing entrance is defined by two large arched openings that originally supported glass canopies, now modernized into a marquee with signage held by bronze brackets; these arches flank the main doors and house ticket booths on either side, with a smaller central doorway providing access. Signage has evolved from early 20th-century electric displays typical of Broadway openings to contemporary LED systems, enhancing visibility while complying with landmark guidelines.
Designated a New York City Landmark on November 4, 1987, the facade received further attention through the Shubert Organization's late-1980s restoration program, which addressed wear from decades of use and ensured structural integrity without altering core features. The facade underwent additional renovations as part of a $9 million theater-wide update completed by 2008. No major facade alterations have been documented after 2008 as of November 2025, maintaining its status as a preserved example of interwar Broadway architecture.

Auditorium Layout

The auditorium of the Ethel Barrymore Theatre is configured as a proscenium-style space with a roughly square plan, accommodating 1,058 seats distributed across the orchestra level (582 seats) and a single mezzanine level divided into front (196 seats) and rear (256 seats) sections, plus 24 box seats. The proscenium arch opening measures 39 feet 3 inches wide by 24 feet 10 inches high, framing the stage area which extends to a depth of 28 feet 3 inches to the proscenium line and 31 feet 3 inches to the front of the stage, providing a modest apron extension for performer-audience proximity.
The stage setup includes a counterweight fly system installed at the theatre's opening in 1928, supporting scenery and lighting rigging within a fly loft structure designed for efficient vertical movement. Architect Herbert J. Krapp incorporated acoustic enhancements through a sloped orchestra floor that rises gently toward the rear for optimal sightlines and sound projection, complemented by ornamental plaster wall panels that contribute to balanced auditory distribution across the space.
Following the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, the theatre implemented accessibility modifications in the ensuing decades, including the addition of wheelchair seating positions in the orchestra level (five spaces) and ramps providing step-free access from street level to this area in 2003, though the mezzanine remains accessible only by stairs.

Interior Spaces and Features

The Ethel Barrymore Theatre's seating configuration includes an orchestra level with 582 seats, a front mezzanine with 196 seats, a rear mezzanine with 256 seats, and 24 box seats, yielding a total capacity of 1,058. The orchestra level is fully accessible without steps, accommodating five wheelchair spaces and companion seating, while the front and rear mezzanine levels are reached via three flights of stairs totaling 30 steps, with handrails provided for safety.
Sightlines across all sections are excellent, ensuring unobstructed views of the stage from every seat due to the theater's intimate scale and strategic layout. Legroom varies by section, with the orchestra offering more generous space overall; in the mezzanine levels, it is more limited, though aisle seatsparticularly inside aislesprovide additional room for taller patrons.
The theater's lobbies and foyers reflect a blend of historical and practical design, featuring modern aluminum and glass doors within arched openings that lead patrons into the interior spaces. The overall interior decor incorporates Elizabethan, Mediterranean, and Adamsesque styles, evident in the ornate plasterwork and columned porticos surrounding the box seating areas.
As part of a $9 million renovation in 2004, interior elements were updated to enhance functionality while preserving historical features.
Backstage facilities support production needs with access from 47th Street, including areas for wardrobe and crew movement, though specific details on the number of dressing rooms remain consistent with standard Broadway configurations for mid-sized venues. The theater maintains compliance with current New York City building and fire safety codes, including provisions for accessibility in public and backstage areas.

History

Construction and Opening (1920s)

The Ethel Barrymore Theatre was commissioned by the Shubert Organization in 1927 as a venue dedicated to performers affiliated with the brothers, marking it as the last Broadway theater they developed before the Great Depression. Named in honor of the renowned actress Ethel Barrymore, the project came with the condition that she star in and select the opening production, a gesture reflecting her stature in the American theater world.
Construction began in 1927 under the design of architect Herbert J. Krapp, who incorporated a blend of Renaissance Revival and Roman bathhouse elements into the structure. The Shubert brothers oversaw the build on the site at 243 West 47th Street, with the project completed at a cost of $275,000 and a seating capacity of 1,096. The rapid timelinefrom planning to completion in under two yearshighlighted the Shuberts' efficiency in expanding their theater empire during the booming 1920s.
The theater held its inaugural performance on December 20, 1928, with the American premiere of The Kingdom of God by Gregorio Martnez Sierra, translated by Helen and Harley Granville-Barker. Starring Ethel Barrymore as Sister Gracia, the religious drama drew a full house and praise for the venue's impressive architecture and Barrymore's commanding presence, though some critics found the play's mystical themes uneven. The opening solidified the theater's role in legitimate Broadway productions, transitioning seamlessly from its debut into a steady schedule of dramatic works by 1929.

1930s and 1940s

The Great Depression profoundly affected the Ethel Barrymore Theatre's operations in the 1930s, leading to reduced bookings and a shift toward more affordable revues and shorter runs to attract audiences amid economic hardship. Productions like the musical revue Gay Divorce, which ran for 248 performances from 1932 to 1933, exemplified this adaptation, as theaters across Broadway struggled with declining attendance and financial viability. By the mid-1930s, the theater transitioned to a mix of musicals and dramas that reflected broader social concerns of the era, maintaining Shubert control without major disruptions. Ownership remained stable under the Shuberts amid industry-wide consolidations, with no significant structural alterations to the building.
During the 1940s, World War II brought further challenges, including adaptations to blackout regulations through adjusted performance schedules and lighting protocols common to Broadway houses, alongside an uptick in patriotic-themed productions. Dramas such as Tomorrow the World, a wartime play addressing fascism that ran for 499 performances in 1943, highlighted this focus on timely, morale-boosting content. Post-war revival began in 1946, marked by longer engagements that signaled economic recovery; for instance, A Streetcar Named Desire achieved 855 performances from 1947 to 1949, underscoring the theater's resilience and return to extended runs under continued Shubert stewardship.

1950s to 1970s

During the 1950s, the Ethel Barrymore Theatre experienced a boom in hosting innovative and experimental works that reflected post-war cultural shifts, including comedies, dramas, and social commentaries. Notable productions included the supernatural comedy Bell, Book and Candle (1950), which ran for 233 performances starring Rex Harrison and Lilli Palmer, and the domestic drama The Fourposter (1951) featuring Jessica Tandy and Hume Cronyn, which achieved 632 performances. The decade also saw longer runs with Robert Anderson's Tea and Sympathy (1953), a sensitive exploration of adolescence starring Deborah Kerr, lasting 712 performances, and Ketti Frings' adaptation Look Homeward, Angel (1957) with Anthony Perkins, which garnered 564 performances. Culminating the era was Lorraine Hansberry's groundbreaking A Raisin in the Sun (1959), a civil rights-themed family drama starring Sidney Poitier, Claudia McNeil, and Ruby Dee, that ran for 530 performances and addressed racial tensions in American society.
The 1960s brought turbulence to the theater, influenced by broader societal upheavals such as the civil rights movement and the Vietnam War, with Off-Off-Broadway's experimental ethos seeping into mainstream Broadway presentations at the Barrymore. Productions like Brendan Behan's The Hostage (1960), a provocative Irish drama, and Ira Levin's thriller Critic's Choice (1960) starring Henry Fonda, each ran for around 189 performances, showcasing edgier narratives. Frederick Knott's suspenseful Wait Until Dark (1966), featuring Lee Remick and Robert Duvall, achieved 374 performances, while a revival of Ben Hecht and Charles MacArthur's The Front Page (1969) with Robert Ryan lasted 64 performances, blending classic journalism satire with contemporary relevance. Throughout, management remained under the steady oversight of the Shubert Organization, with occasional leasing to independent producers for specific runs.
By the 1970s, the Ethel Barrymore Theatre navigated a period of decline driven by economic recession, suburban flight, and urban disinvestment in Times Square, which reduced overall Broadway attendance and led to the conversion or demolition of several venues. Despite these challenges, the theater persisted with a mix of experimental plays and lighter fare, such as Melvin Van Peebles' raw urban drama Aint Supposed to Die a Natural Death (1971), running 325 performances, and David Mamet's gritty American Buffalo (1977) starring Robert Duvall, which drew 122 performances for its portrayal of small-time crooks. Cy Coleman's musical I Love My Wife (1977) provided a commercial bright spot with 857 performances, while Bernard Slade's Romantic Comedy (1979) with Mia Farrow ran 396 performances. These years underscored the Barrymore's resilience amid Broadway's broader struggles.

1980s to 1990s

During the 1980s, the Ethel Barrymore Theatre benefited from the broader revitalization of Times Square, where city-led cleanup efforts transformed the area from a hub of crime and adult entertainment into a safer, more family-friendly destination, boosting attendance at surrounding Broadway venues. This resurgence aligned with a renaissance in Broadway theater, exemplified by the long-running production of Jane Wagner's The Search for Signs of Intelligent Life in the Universe, starring Lily Tomlin, which premiered at the Barrymore on September 26, 1985, and ran for 391 performances until October 5, 1986.
In 1987, the New York City Landmarks Preservation Commission designated both the theater's exterior facade and interior auditorium as official landmarks, recognizing their architectural significance amid a citywide push to protect historic Broadway houses. As part of the Shubert Organization's restoration initiatives for its portfolio of aging venues, the Barrymore underwent renovations in the late 1980s, including updates to preserve its original decorative elements while improving functionality.
Entering the 1990s, the theater adapted to new federal regulations under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), enacted in 1990 and fully effective by 1992, which prompted Broadway houses to install assistive listening devices, widen aisles, and add accessible seating to accommodate patrons with disabilities. In 1998, the original marquee was restored as part of ongoing preservation efforts tied to the theater's landmark status, enhancing its street-level presence in the revitalized district.
The decade also reflected broader industry shifts, with increased corporate sponsorships providing financial support for productions and marketing; for instance, Continental Airlines became Broadway's first full-year sponsor in 1997, funding initiatives across theaters like the Barrymore. In response to the ongoing AIDS crisis, the theater participated in benefit performances organized through the newly merged Broadway Cares/Equity Fights AIDS (formed in 1992), which raised millions for HIV/AIDS services via events and collections at Broadway venues. These efforts underscored the theater's role in the cultural and philanthropic landscape of late-20th-century Broadway.

2000s to Present

In the 2000s, the Ethel Barrymore Theatre continued to host a mix of revivals and new works that blended classic drama with contemporary wit, attracting critical acclaim and solid audiences. A revival of Tom Stoppard's The Real Thing opened in January 2000, earning two Tony Awards for its lead and featured performances, highlighting the venue's affinity for sharp, intellectual plays. This was followed by Charles Busch's comedy The Tale of the Allergist's Wife, which ran from November 2000 to September 2002, becoming one of the longest-running shows of the decade at the theatre with over 750 performances and appealing to audiences through its humorous take on urban neuroses. Later entries included Nora Ephron's Imaginary Friends in 2002, starring Swoosie Kurtz and Cherry Jones as rival authors Mary McCarthy and Lillian Hellman, and a 2005 revival of Tennessee Williams's The Glass Menagerie featuring Jessica Lange, which garnered Drama Desk Awards for its intimate exploration of family dynamics. The decade also saw musical ventures like the Johnny Cash tribute Ring of Fire in 2006 and David Mamet's politically charged November in 2008, starring Nathan Lane, reflecting a trend toward star-driven productions that balanced commercial viability with thematic depth.
The 2010s marked a period of innovation at the Barrymore, with immersive storytelling and diverse narratives drawing international attention and multiple accolades. Revivals like the 2012 Tony-winning production of Arthur Miller's Death of a Salesman, directed by Mike Nichols and starring Philip Seymour Hoffman, ran for over 200 performances and earned three Tonys, underscoring the theatre's role in revitalizing American classics. The musical Chaplin followed in 2012, offering a biographical spectacle that, despite a shorter run, showcased innovative staging techniques. Simon Stephens's adaptation of The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time premiered in October 2014 and became a landmark, winning five Tony Awards including Best Play for its multimedia approach to neurodiversity and running nearly two years. The decade closed with Matthew Lopez's The Inheritance in 2019, a two-part epic inspired by E.M. Forster's Howards End that addressed LGBTQ+ themes and secured two Tonys, including Best Play, while fostering global discussions on identity and legacy. These productions exemplified a shift toward inclusive narratives and technological integration, with many originating from London transfers that enhanced the theatre's international profile.
Entering the 2020s, the Barrymore adapted to post-pandemic challenges by prioritizing shorter, high-impact runs with diverse casts and modern relevance, often incorporating digital elements like virtual ticketing and hybrid promotions to broaden accessibility. Peter Morgan's Patriots, which opened in April 2024 and explored Russian political intrigue with Will Patton and Michael Stuhlbarg, ran for about three months and received Drama Desk nominations for its timely geopolitical commentary. A revival of Thornton Wilder's Our Town, directed by Kenny Leon and featuring a multicultural ensemble including Jim Parsons and Zoey Deutch, began previews in September 2024 and continued into early 2025, emphasizing communal resilience with inclusive casting that reflected contemporary America. The season culminated in a high-profile revival of Shakespeare's Othello in March 2025, starring Denzel Washington and Jake Gyllenhaal under Kenny Leon's direction, which incorporated diverse performers and digital tie-ins such as app-based accessibility features; it closed in June 2025 after earning Tony nominations for its bold interpretation of race and jealousy. As of November 2025, the theater is dark, with the next production being a revival of August Wilson's Joe Turner's Come and Gone beginning previews on March 30, 2026. 21st-century productions at the Barrymore have earned numerous Tony nominations and wins since 2000, signaling sustained excellence amid evolving trends like abbreviated engagementsaveraging 4-6 monthsand cross-cultural appeal that attracts global audiences through touring extensions and streaming partnerships.

Notable Productions

1920s to 1990s

The Ethel Barrymore Theatre opened on December 20, 1928, with the drama The Kingdom of God by G. K. Chesterton, starring the theatre's namesake Ethel Barrymore in her chosen vehicle, running for 92 performances and marking the venue's debut as a hub for serious dramatic works. In 1929, the theatre hosted Death Takes a Holiday, a romantic fantasy adapted from the Italian play by Walter Ferris, which ran for 180 performances and explored themes of mortality and love, contributing to the venue's early reputation for sophisticated storytelling.
During the 1930s and 1940s, the theatre became known for long-running hits that captured social and emotional complexities. Clare Boothe Luce's The Women (1936), an all-female cast satire on high society, achieved 657 performances and highlighted gender dynamics in pre-war America. The 1940 musical Pal Joey by Rodgers and Hart, starring Gene Kelly, ran for 374 performances and introduced the anti-hero protagonist to Broadway musicals, influencing the genre's shift toward edgier narratives. Tennessee Williams's A Streetcar Named Desire (1947), starring Marlon Brando and Jessica Tandy, set a benchmark with 855 performances and a Pulitzer Prize for Drama, delving into psychological realism and Southern decay.
The 1950s and 1960s saw a blend of intimate comedies, family dramas, and thrillers that underscored the theatre's versatility. Bell, Book and Candle (1950), a supernatural romantic comedy by John Van Druten, enjoyed 233 performances, while Ketti Frings's adaptation Look Homeward, Angel (1957) ran for 564 performances, adapting Thomas Wolfe's novel to explore youth and ambition. Lorraine Hansberry's A Raisin in the Sun (1959), the first Broadway play by a Black woman, ran for 489 performances and addressed racial discrimination and family aspirations, earning widespread acclaim for its cultural significance. In the 1960s, Frederick Knott's suspense thriller Wait Until Dark (1966) achieved 557 performances, cementing the theatre's role in staging taut, character-driven suspense.
From the 1970s to the 1980s, productions leaned toward ensemble-driven stories and revivals, reflecting broader theatrical experimentation. Barry England's Conduct Unbecoming (1970), a military drama, ran for 535 performances and won a Tony Award for Best Featured Actor (Paul Hecht). Melvin Van Peebles's Ain't Supposed to Die a Natural Death (1971), a groundbreaking musical revue on urban Black life, garnered 325 performances and secured two Tony Awards (Best Book and Best Featured Actress for Carolyn Michelle). The 1983 musical Baby, with music by David Shire and lyrics by Richard Maltby Jr., ran for 192 performances and received seven Tony nominations, including Best Musical. David Rabe's Hurlyburly (1984), a raw exploration of Hollywood masculinity, achieved 188 performances and earned Tony nominations for its ensemble.
In the 1990s, the theatre featured witty comedies and intimate dramas that bridged classic and contemporary voices. Peter Shaffer's Lettice and Lovage (1990), starring Maggie Smith, ran for 304 performances and won the Tony Award for Best Leading Actress in a Play. The venue hosted over 200 productions from the 1920s through the 1990s, with many exceeding 200 performances, such as The Women and A Streetcar Named Desire, demonstrating sustained audience engagement. At least five Tony Awards were bestowed on shows during this era, including those for Ain't Supposed to Die a Natural Death and Lettice and Lovage.
Thematically, the Barrymore's programming evolved from predominantly straight dramas and comedies in the 1920s1940s, emphasizing social satire and psychological depth, to incorporating more musicals in the mid-century, like Pal Joey, which ran nearly a year and signaled a genre maturation. By the 1950s1970s, works like A Raisin in the Sun and Conduct Unbecoming amplified diverse voices and historical reflections, with average runs for major hits often surpassing 400 performances, underscoring cultural resonance amid post-war optimism and social upheaval. This progression toward musicals and revivals in the 1980s1990s, exemplified by Baby and Lettice and Lovage, reflected Broadway's diversification, with the theatre's intimate 1,058-seat auditorium fostering impactful, character-focused storytelling that influenced American theatre's emphasis on ensemble dynamics and thematic innovation.

Operations and Records

Ownership and Management

The Ethel Barrymore Theatre has been owned by the Shubert Organization since its construction and opening in 1928, when it was developed by the Shubert brothers as the final Broadway venue they built prior to the Great Depression. Originally managed through the Shubert Theatre Corporation, ownership transitioned seamlessly to the broader Shubert Organization by 1973, with no subsequent changes in proprietorship.
Management of the theatre evolved from the direct involvement of Lee and J.J. Shubert, who oversaw operations until Lee's death in 1953 and J.J.'s in 1963, to a modern professional team within the Shubert Organization responsible for leasing arrangements, facility maintenance, and daily operations across their portfolio of venues. The organization leases the theatre to independent producers on a rental basis, allowing flexibility for mounting commercial productions while ensuring consistent upkeep and compliance with industry standards.
The theatre adheres to standard Broadway operational policies, including affiliations with key labor unions such as the International Alliance of Theatrical Stage Employees (IATSE) for backstage crew and Actors' Equity Association for performers and stage managers, which govern working conditions and contracts. Following 2020, the Shubert Organization introduced diversity, equity, and inclusion initiatives, notably The Artistic Circle program launched in 2021 to support BIPOC producers in developing commercial theatre projects through financial and institutional assistance.
As of 2025, the Ethel Barrymore Theatre remains fully operational under the Shubert Organization's ownership, with no alterations to its governance structure; the organization maintains an estimated annual operating budget in the hundreds of millions across its holdings, supported by a staff of approximately 673 professionals organization-wide, including dedicated teams for individual venues like the Barrymore.

Box Office Achievements

The Ethel Barrymore Theatre has marked several milestones in Broadway box office history, with records evolving alongside rising ticket prices and audience demand. One early peak came during the 2014-2016 run of The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time, which achieved weekly grosses approaching $1 million in peak periods and amassed a total of $68,321,435 over its engagement, reflecting strong critical acclaim and consistent sell-outs. In the late 2010s, The Band's Visit set a house benchmark for a nine-performance week, grossing $1.56 million in December 2017, the highest at the time for that schedule.
Modern productions have shattered these figures, driven by star power and premium seating. Waitress established a single-day record of $197,878 on September 3, 2021. The 2025 revival of Othello, starring Denzel Washington and Jake Gyllenhaal, eclipsed prior marks with a weekly gross of $2,818,297 during previews in March and ultimately set the all-time house record of $3,807,875 over eight performances in June 2025, surpassing previous highs by over 140%.
Revenue trends at the Barrymore have mirrored Broadway's broader growth, with average weekly grosses for major hits rising from around $600,000-$800,000 in the 2000s (as seen in productions like A Raisin in the Sun totaling $16 million) to over $1.5 million in the 2020s, fueled by dynamic pricing and tourism recovery post-pandemic. Among the Shubert Organization's 17 venues, the Barrymore ranked highly in the 2024-2025 season, achieving 95% capacity during Othello's run compared to the group's average of 85-90% across theaters like the Cort and Shubert. This positions it as a top performer, with sell-out rates exceeding 90% for star-driven plays amid overall Broadway attendance of 14.7 million for the season.
