Meaning-making
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Meaning-making is the psychological process through which individuals actively construct, interpret, and derive significance from their experiences, particularly in response to stressful or traumatic events, by integrating situational appraisals into their broader worldview. This involves cognitive and emotional efforts to resolve discrepancies between the meaning assigned to a specific eventsuch as its causes, implications, or purposeand one's global meaning system, which encompasses foundational beliefs about the world (e.g., benevolence or controllability), personal goals, and an overarching sense of life's purpose. Successful meaning-making restores coherence, reduces distress, and facilitates adjustment, though unsuccessful attempts can prolong psychological struggle.
Central to contemporary understandings is the Meaning Making Model, developed by Crystal L. Park, which distinguishes between global meaning as a stable orienting framework and situational meaning as event-specific interpretations that may violate it, prompting deliberate or automatic coping processes like reappraisal, rumination, or narrative reconstruction. These efforts aim to produce "meanings made," such as acceptance of the event, perceived benefits, or revised global beliefs, with empirical evidence indicating that the quality and resolution of meaning-makingrather than its mere occurrencepredict outcomes like lower PTSD symptoms or enhanced well-being following trauma. Research across longitudinal studies of bereavement, illness, and disasters supports this model, showing that meaning-making attempts are nearly universal after adversity, occurring most intensely in the early stages before diminishing over time.
Beyond trauma and stress, meaning-making extends to developmental and educational contexts, where it describes how learners actively organize knowledge, relationships, and self-concepts through evolving "orders of consciousness," progressing from socially embedded interpretations to self-authored understandings. Influenced by interdisciplinary perspectives from philosophy, sociology, and cognitive science, it underscores the human capacity for resilience and growth, with applications in therapy, education, and social movements to foster adaptive interpretations of challenging experiences.

Definition and Core Concepts

Definition and Process

Meaning-making is the active psychological process through which individuals construe, understand, and integrate life events, relationships, and experiences into their broader worldview, thereby creating a sense of coherence and purpose. This process involves interpreting stimuli in relation to one's existing beliefs, goals, and assumptions, often triggered by stressors or novel situations that challenge prior understandings. As a fundamental aspect of human cognition, it enables adaptation by transforming raw experiences into personally significant narratives that align with one's sense of self and reality.
The key mechanisms of meaning-making include cognitive appraisal, where individuals initially evaluate the significance and implications of an event; emotional processing, which involves experiencing and regulating feelings to manage distress; narrative construction, through which stories are built to connect events to one's life story; and revision of beliefs, aimed at resolving discrepancies between expectations and reality by adjusting core assumptions or goals. Cognitive appraisal draws on frameworks like those outlined by Lazarus and Folkman, assessing events for threat or opportunity relative to personal resources. Emotional processing facilitates integration by allowing confrontation with affective responses, while narrative construction provides a structured way to weave disparate elements into a unified account. Belief revision occurs when inconsistencies arise, prompting shifts in global meanings to restore equilibrium.
The process typically unfolds in stages: an initial disruption, where an event violates core assumptions and generates distress; a search for coherence, involving active exploration and reappraisal to bridge the gap between the event's appraised meaning and one's worldview; and eventual reconstruction, where new meanings are formed, leading to integration and potential growth. This sequential progression is not always linear but reflects a dynamic effort to restore psychological balance.
In everyday contexts, meaning-making manifests in responses to common challenges, such as interpreting a sudden job loss not merely as a setback but as an opportunity for personal or career growth, thereby reframing it within a narrative of resilience and redirection. This example illustrates how the process transforms potential despair into adaptive insight, contributing to overall psychological well-being.

Global and Situational Meaning

Global meaning refers to an individual's enduring and overarching beliefs, goals, and assumptions about the world, the self, and relationships with others, forming a stable orienting system that guides expectations and interpretations across life domains. These components include cognitive beliefs about order and justice (e.g., assumptions that "life is fair" or that the world operates predictably) and motivational goals representing valued purposes, such as personal growth or interpersonal harmony. Additionally, global meaning encompasses a subjective sense of purpose or meaningfulness that provides coherence to one's life narrative.
In contrast, situational meaning arises from the specific appraisal of an event or circumstance in relation to one's global meaning, representing the immediate interpretation of that event's significance, implications, and controllability. For instance, a personal failure might be appraised as a temporary setback if it aligns with a global belief in personal agency and resilience. Situational meaning is thus derived and filtered through the lens of global meaning, allowing individuals to evaluate events within their broader worldview without necessarily altering foundational beliefs.
The interplay between global and situational meaning is central to the meaning-making process, where discrepanciesviolations of global assumptions by an event's appraised implicationsserve as the primary trigger. Such violations generate psychological distress proportional to the magnitude of the mismatch, prompting efforts to reconcile the two by either reinterpreting the situational meaning to fit global beliefs (assimilation) or revising global meaning to accommodate the event (accommodation). This reconciliation restores coherence and reduces distress, facilitating adaptive functioning.
Conceptually, global meaning functions as a foundational framework that filters and shapes situational appraisals, as illustrated in models where global meaning precedes and influences event-specific interpretations, with feedback loops for adjustment when discrepancies arise. In this structure:

Global Meaning: Broad, stable beliefs and goals (e.g., "people are inherently good").

Situational Meaning: Event-derived interpretation (e.g., appraising an interpersonal conflict as a misunderstanding).

Discrepancy: Mismatch leading to distress.

Reconciliation: Efforts to align the two levels.

This diagram highlights the dynamic yet hierarchical relationship, with global meaning providing stability while situational meaning allows flexibility in response to specific contexts.

Theoretical Frameworks

Philosophical and Existential Foundations

The philosophical foundations of meaning-making trace back to existentialism, where thinkers grappled with the human condition in a seemingly absurd or indifferent universe, emphasizing individual agency in forging personal significance. Sren Kierkegaard, often regarded as the father of existentialism, posited that truth is fundamentally subjective, rooted in personal passion and commitment rather than objective universality. He argued that "subjectivity is truth," highlighting how individuals must appropriate ethical and religious truths through inwardness, as detached knowledge fails to engage the self authentically. This subjective appropriation culminates in the "leap of faith," a decisive personal act of trust beyond rational certainty, enabling one to create meaning amid existential despair by embracing the infinite alongside the finite aspects of existence.
Friedrich Nietzsche extended this inquiry by rejecting traditional moral frameworks as life-denying illusions, proposing instead the "will to power" as the fundamental drive animating all existence. For Nietzsche, this will manifests as an affirmative force for overcoming obstacles and enhancing one's vitality, serving as a basis for self-imposed values in a godless world stripped of inherent purpose. Complementing this, his doctrine of eternal recurrence challenges individuals to imagine their lives repeating infinitely, demanding a wholehearted affirmation of every moment to affirm existence despite its absurdity: "What if a demon were to creep after you one night... and say, 'This life as you now live it... you will have to live once more and innumerable times more'?" Through these concepts, Nietzsche framed meaning-making as an active, creative rebellion against nihilism, where one imposes significance by embracing life's flux.
Martin Heidegger deepened this existential perspective by analyzing human existence (Dasein) as inherently oriented toward its own finitude, introducing "being-towards-death" as the key to authenticity. Death, as "the possibility of the absolute impossibility of Dasein," individualizes the self, compelling confrontation with meaninglessness and freeing one from inauthentic conformity to the "they-self" of everyday norms. Authenticity arises from this anticipation, involving resoluteness in owning one's finite possibilities and taking responsibility for choices, thus transforming potential despair into a grounded mode of being.
Jean-Paul Sartre synthesized these ideas in his radical humanism, declaring that "existence precedes essence," meaning humans enter the world without predefined purpose and must construct their essence through free choices. This freedom entails total responsibility, as "man is condemned to be free," with no external excuses for one's life project, rendering meaning-making an ongoing, anguished act of self-definition. Sartre emphasized that choices not only shape personal identity but also imply values for humanity, underscoring the ethical weight of creating meaning in an absurd reality.
These existential foundations transitioned into early 20th-century thought through Otto Rank, who explored meaning-making via the tension between life denial and affirmation in creative acts. Rank viewed human existence as marked by a primal "trauma of birth," symbolizing separation from unity, which engenders a fear of life that can lead to denial through neurosis or rigid ideologies. In contrast, affirmation emerges through the creative will, where individuals transcend this fear by artistically shaping their lives, accepting mortality while embracing separation as a source of vitality and individuality. Rank's framework thus bridges philosophy and emerging psychology, portraying meaning as dynamically forged in the balance of denial and creative acceptance.

Psychological Models and Theories

Viktor Frankl's logotherapy posits that the primary human drive is the "will to meaning," which surpasses other motivations like pleasure or power, enabling individuals to discover purpose even amid profound suffering. Central to this approach is the concept of "freedom of attitude," asserting that while external circumstances may be uncontrollable, people retain the capacity to choose their response, thereby preserving inner liberty and fostering resilience. Logotherapy employs specific techniques to facilitate meaning-making, including dereflection, which redirects attention from self-absorption to external values or relationships, and paradoxical intention, where individuals intentionally confront their fears to diminish anxiety, such as by wishing for the exaggerated occurrence of a phobia. These principles emphasize that suffering, when unavoidable, can yield meaning through attitude adjustment or sacrificial purpose, as supported by Frankl's observations in concentration camps.
George Kelly's personal construct theory frames individuals as "scientists" who actively construe their reality through unique, bipolar personal constructsmental frameworks that interpret experiences along dimensions like "friendly-hostile" or "stable-changeable." Meaning-making occurs as a dynamic process of revising these constructs in light of new evidence, akin to scientific hypothesis testing, where individuals anticipate events and adapt their interpretations to better predict and navigate the world. This theory underscores that personal meaning is subjective and constructed, with psychological health arising from flexible, permeable constructs that allow for ongoing revision rather than rigid adherence. Empirical support derives from applications in psychotherapy, where techniques like repertory grid analysis help elicit and restructure constructs to resolve maladaptive meanings.
Crystal Park's meaning-making model (MMM) delineates a process by which individuals reconcile stressors with their existing worldview, distinguishing between global meaningenduring beliefs, goals, and purpose that form one's orienting systemand situational meaning, the interpretation of specific events. Appraised meaning refers to the initial evaluation of an event's significance, such as its threat level or controllability, which may create discrepant meaning when it conflicts with global meaning, thereby generating distress and motivating coping efforts. The coping process involves deliberate or automatic meaning-making strategies, including assimilation (adjusting the event's appraisal to fit global meaning) or accommodation (modifying global beliefs or goals), leading to "meanings made" like restored coherence, acceptance, or growth. The model incorporates feedback loops, where unresolved discrepancies perpetuate distress and further attempts at meaning-making, while successful resolutions enhance adjustment and may refine global meaning for future events; it is typically illustrated in a diagram (e.g., Figure 1 in Park, 2010) showing arrows between discrepancy detection, meaning-making processes, outcomes, and bidirectional influences on global and situational meaning. Empirical validation stems from studies on trauma and loss, demonstrating that higher initial discrepancies predict greater distress, but effective meaning-making correlates with improved psychological adjustment.
In positive psychology, Martin Seligman's PERMA model integrates meaning as one of five pillars essential for flourishing: positive emotion (hedonic well-being through joy and gratitude), engagement (flow states in absorbing activities), relationships (supportive social bonds), meaning (connection to something greater than oneself, such as community or ideals), and accomplishment (pursuit of mastery and achievement). Meaning contributes to flourishing by providing a sense of purpose that transcends personal gain, buffering against adversity and enhancing overall life satisfaction when aligned with prosocial contributions. This framework builds on empirical research showing that meaning-oriented interventions, like purpose reflection exercises, boost well-being metrics across diverse populations.
A more recent development is existential positive psychology (often termed PP 2.0), proposed by Paul T. P. Wong in 2020, which integrates existential concerns with positive psychology to address flourishing through suffering. This approach critiques traditional positive psychology for overlooking negative existential realities and emphasizes self-transcendence and the management of both positive and negative life forces. Building on Frankl's logotherapy and Park's meaning-making model, it incorporates elements of comprehension, purpose, and mattering while adding a focus on suffering as a pathway to growth. Associated with it is Integrative Meaning Therapy (IMT), a holistic therapeutic framework that treats individuals as bio-psycho-social-spiritual beings, blending clinical, existential, and positive perspectives to facilitate meaning reconstruction and resilience.

Historical Development

Early Influences in Philosophy and Early Psychology

The concept of meaning-making finds early philosophical precursors in ancient Greek thought, particularly in Aristotle's notion of eudaimonia, which refers to human flourishing achieved through rational activity aligned with virtue and purpose. In his Nicomachean Ethics, Aristotle posits that eudaimonia is not mere pleasure but the realization of one's potential via deliberate, purposeful actions within a structured ethical framework, laying groundwork for later ideas of individuals actively constructing meaning through goal-directed living.
Similarly, Stoic philosophy introduced the idea of logos as the rational, ordering principle permeating the universe, serving as a divine reason that humans could align with to derive meaning from cosmic harmony. Thinkers like Zeno of Citium and later Epictetus emphasized living in accordance with logosthe logical structure governing all eventsas a means to construct personal significance amid inevitable adversity, viewing human reason as a participatory extension of this universal order.
In the 19th century, Romanticism shifted emphasis toward subjective individual experience as the primary source of meaning, challenging Enlightenment rationalism by prioritizing emotion, intuition, and personal narrative over imposed universal structures. This movement, exemplified in the works of poets like William Wordsworth and Samuel Taylor Coleridge, promoted the idea that meaning emerges from authentic self-expression and communion with nature, influencing subsequent views that meaning is inherently constructed through personal interpretation rather than objective decree.
Arthur Schopenhauer's pessimistic philosophy further complicated these dynamics by portraying the world as driven by an irrational, insatiable will, rendering imposed cosmic meaning illusory and urging individuals to create their own through aesthetic contemplation or ascetic denial of desires. In The World as Will and Representation (1818), Schopenhauer argued that life's inherent suffering undermines any transcendent purpose, positioning created personal meaningvia art or renunciationas a fragile counter to existential void.
Transitioning to early psychology, William James's pragmatism in The Principles of Psychology (1890) framed consciousness as a functional, adaptive process for interpreting and organizing experience to navigate the environment effectively. James viewed mental life not as static content but as a dynamic "stream" that constructs meaning through practical utility, where beliefs and perceptions gain significance based on their role in promoting survival and adjustment.
In contrast, Sigmund Freud's early psychoanalytic theory largely sidelined explicit meaning-making in favor of unconscious instinctual drives, such as the libido, as the core motivators of behavior. Freud's focus in works like The Interpretation of Dreams (1900) emphasized resolving internal conflicts from repressed desires over conscious purpose-seeking, treating symptoms as manifestations of drive tensions rather than quests for significance.
Alfred Adler's individual psychology, outlined in The Practice and Theory of Individual Psychology (1927), countered this drive-centric view by centering human motivation on a striving for significance and social embeddedness. Adler conceptualized the "inferiority complex" as a spur for compensatory efforts toward superiority, framing meaning-making as an active, holistic pursuit of goals within communal contexts to overcome feelings of inadequacy.

Modern Developments in the 20th and 21st Centuries

In the aftermath of World War II, Viktor Frankl's Man's Search for Meaning (1946), based on his experiences as a Holocaust survivor in Nazi concentration camps, marked a pivotal post-war emergence of meaning-making in psychology by establishing logotherapy as a therapeutic approach centered on discovering purpose amid suffering. Frankl argued that the human drive toward meaning surpasses basic needs, influencing subsequent existential therapies.
During the 1960s and 1980s, existential psychology advanced through Rollo May's exploration of anxiety as an existential phenomenon in The Meaning of Anxiety (initially published 1950, revised 1977), which framed meaning-making as essential for confronting modern alienation. Complementing this, Irvin Yalom's Existential Psychotherapy (1980) identified four ultimate concernsdeath, freedom, existential isolation, and meaninglessnessas core to human experience, integrating meaning-making into clinical practice to address these inescapable realities.
From the 1990s onward, empirical models gained prominence, with Crystal L. Park's Meaning Making Model (MMM), first proposed in 1997 with Susan Folkman, distinguishing global and situational meanings to explain adjustment to stressors; this framework was refined in Park's 2010 integrative review, emphasizing how discrepancies between appraised and existing meanings drive coping processes. Parallel developments in trauma psychology included Ronnie Janoff-Bulman's shattered assumptions theory (1992), which posits that traumatic events disrupt foundational beliefs about benevolence, meaningfulness, and self-worth, necessitating reconstructive meaning-making for recovery, thus bridging existential and cognitive approaches.
In the 21st century, neuroscientific research illuminated the biological underpinnings of meaning-making, with fMRI studies in the 2010s revealing that narrative processing engages networks like the default mode and salience systems to construct personal significance, as seen in investigations of story comprehension linking brain connectivity to subjective meaning in life. Cultural dimensions expanded alongside, as Paul T. P. Wong's Positive Psychology 2.0 (2011) advocated a balanced model incorporating suffering and cultural contexts in meaning pursuit, promoting self-transcendence across diverse traditions. A key institutional milestone was the establishment of the International Network on Personal Meaning in 1998, which fostered global research and biennial conferences starting in 2000 to advance meaning-centered interventions.
More recent developments from 2020 onward have applied meaning-making frameworks to global crises, notably the COVID-19 pandemic. Longitudinal studies have shown that deliberate meaning-making efforts, such as acceptance and positive reframing, predict increased purpose in life and better adjustment during lockdowns, with research indicating nearly universal attempts at meaning reconstruction in response to collective trauma. These findings extend earlier models to contemporary contexts, including digital narratives and AI-influenced experiences, underscoring ongoing empirical and interdisciplinary growth in the field as of 2025.

Applications in Learning and Education

Constructivist Approaches

Constructivist approaches to meaning-making in education emphasize that learners actively build knowledge and understanding through personal and social interactions with their environment, rather than passively receiving information. This perspective posits that meaning emerges from the integration of new experiences into existing cognitive structures, fostering deeper comprehension and retention in learning settings. Rooted in psychological theories, these approaches highlight the dynamic processes by which students construct personal interpretations of concepts, enabling adaptive and contextualized knowledge formation.
Jean Piaget's cognitive constructivism frames meaning-making as an internal process driven by assimilation and accommodation. Assimilation involves incorporating new information into pre-existing mental schemas, allowing learners to interpret novel experiences in familiar terms, while accommodation entails modifying those schemas when new information cannot be easily integrated, leading to cognitive restructuring. Through these mechanisms, children actively construct meaning by achieving equilibrium between their current understanding and environmental demands, progressing through developmental stages where knowledge is not transmitted but built individually. This process underscores meaning-making as a self-regulating activity that promotes intellectual growth in educational contexts.
Lev Vygotsky's social constructivism extends meaning-making to collaborative interactions, introducing the zone of proximal development (ZPD) as the space between what a learner can accomplish independently and what they can achieve with guidance from more knowledgeable others. Within the ZPD, scaffoldingtemporary support provided by teachers or peersfacilitates meaning construction through dialogue, modeling, and shared problem-solving, transforming social experiences into internalized knowledge. This approach views meaning as co-constructed in cultural and social contexts, where educational interactions enable learners to negotiate interpretations and extend their cognitive capabilities beyond solitary efforts.
Jerome Bruner's contributions to constructivism highlight the narrative mode of thought, wherein learning involves crafting coherent stories from experiences to imbue them with personal significance. In this mode, students engage in storytelling to organize disparate events into meaningful narratives, contrasting with the logical-scientific mode by emphasizing human intentions, emotions, and cultural contexts. Bruner argued that such narrative construction allows learners to create subjective realities, enhancing engagement and retention by linking abstract concepts to lived stories in educational practices.
Empirical studies from the 1980s to 2020s support the efficacy of constructivist meaning-making, particularly through problem-based learning (PBL), where students actively construct knowledge via real-world problems. A 2022 meta-analysis of undergraduate medical education found PBL superior to lectures in both knowledge acquisition (effect size = 0.76) and long-term retention (ES = 0.88), attributing gains to active engagement and schema building. For instance, research on gifted high school students showed higher retention rates in PBL units compared to traditional methods. These findings affirm that constructivist methods enhance educational outcomes by promoting deeper, self-directed meaning construction.

Meaning-Making in Adult Learning

In adult learning, meaning-making refers to the process by which learners construct personal significance from new information by integrating it with prior life experiences, particularly in self-directed and non-formal contexts such as lifelong education and professional development. This approach emphasizes autonomy, relevance to real-world roles, and the use of accumulated knowledge to interpret and apply learning, distinguishing it from more structured, teacher-led models in formal education. This includes developmental progression through "orders of consciousness," as described by Robert Kegan, where adults evolve from socially embedded interpretations (order 3) to self-authored understandings (order 4), enabling more reflective and inclusive meaning-making in higher education and beyond.
A foundational framework for this is Malcolm Knowles' theory of andragogy, which posits that adults are self-directed learners who actively draw on their life experiences to make meaning from new knowledge, viewing education as a collaborative process oriented toward problem-solving in immediate contexts. Knowles outlined six key assumptions: adults need to know why they are learning, have self-concepts as responsible individuals, bring rich reservoirs of experience that serve as resources for learning, are readiness-oriented toward societal roles, are problem-centered in their approach to life, and are motivated internally by factors like increased job satisfaction. This theory underscores how meaning emerges when learning aligns with adults' orientations, fostering deeper engagement in settings like continuing professional education.
Building on similar experiential foundations, Jack Mezirow's transformative learning theory describes meaning-making as a process of critical reflection on assumptions, leading to perspective transformation in response to disorienting dilemmassuch as challenging life events that disrupt established worldviews. Mezirow (1991) defined transformative learning as the revision of meaning structures through discourse and self-examination, where adults reassess habits of mind to achieve more inclusive interpretations of experience. This iterative process enables learners to reconstruct personal narratives, enhancing adaptability in dynamic environments like career shifts or skill updates.
Complementing these, David Kolb's experiential learning theory (1984) frames meaning construction as an iterative cycle involving four stages: concrete experience (engaging directly with a situation), reflective observation (reviewing the experience), abstract conceptualization (forming theories), and active experimentation (testing implications in new scenarios). Kolb emphasized that learning occurs through this dialectical process, where adults build knowledge by resolving tensions between perception, feeling, thinking, and doing, making it particularly suited to self-directed contexts. This cycle supports ongoing meaning-making by allowing learners to personalize abstract concepts through practical application.
In practice, these frameworks apply to workplace training and continuing education, where adults integrate new skills with existing identities to navigate professional growth. For instance, studies on career transitions in the 2000s highlight how narrative identitypersonal stories that organize life experiencesfacilitates meaning-making during role changes, such as from employee to manager, by reconstructing coherent self-concepts that enhance adjustment and performance. Research from this period, including Ibarra and Barbulescu (2010), demonstrates that provisional identities in transitional narratives help adults experiment with new meanings, reducing uncertainty and improving outcomes in adult education programs focused on vocational shifts. Such applications, often aligned with constructivist principles of active knowledge building, have shown increased retention and motivation in informal learning settings like corporate workshops.

Applications in Adversity and Coping

In Bereavement and Grief

In bereavement, meaning-making involves the active process of reconstructing an individual's assumptive worldthe foundational beliefs about self, others, and the worldthat is shattered by loss. According to Robert A. Neimeyer's meaning reconstruction theory, grieving entails integrating the death into one's life narrative through sense-making (explaining why the loss occurred) and benefit-finding (identifying positive changes or growth from the experience). This process often progresses from initial rumination, characterized by repetitive, distressing thoughts about the circumstances of the death, to eventual benefit-finding, which fosters emotional resolution and adaptation. Longitudinal research indicates that successful meaning reconstruction correlates with lower levels of prolonged grief symptoms over time.
Within family contexts, meaning-making strengthens bonds through shared narratives and rituals that honor the deceased, enabling the maintenance of continuing bonds rather than severing ties. Bereaved family members co-construct stories about the loved one's life and legacy during conversations, memorial events, or family gatherings, which helps preserve emotional connections and reduces isolation in grief. These practices, such as creating family albums or annual commemorations, facilitate a collective reconfiguration of family identity post-loss, promoting resilience. Empirical studies show that such shared meaning-making activities are associated with decreased grief intensity and improved family functioning.
Spirituality and religiosity provide frameworks for deriving purpose from bereavement, often by reframing death as a transition or divine plan rather than an endpoint. Kenneth I. Pargament's model of religious coping highlights positive strategies, such as seeking spiritual support or viewing the loss through sacred lenses, which aid in finding ultimate meaning amid suffering. For instance, religious rituals like prayer or communal services help bereaved individuals attribute significance to the death, buffering against despair. Research on religious coping demonstrates that these approaches are linked to lower depression and greater post-loss growth in diverse populations.
Bereaved individuals may channel grief into philanthropic endeavors, transforming personal loss into altruistic actions that endow the deceased's memory with enduring legacy. Examples include founding support organizations or awareness campaigns in honor of the loved one, which create a sense of purpose and continuity. This form of meaning-making not only honors the deceased but also benefits others facing similar losses, as seen in case studies of parents establishing foundations after child bereavement. Such initiatives are mutually reinforcing, enhancing the founder's emotional recovery while extending impact.
A meta-analysis from the 2010s on interventions for complicated grief affirms that meaning reconstruction in bereavement significantly reduces risks of complicated grief, with longitudinal data showing sustained improvements in well-being when individuals progress from sense-making to benefit-finding over 12 years. These findings underscore the therapeutic value of fostering meaning-making, particularly through integrated spiritual and communal efforts.

In Illness and Trauma Recovery

Meaning-making plays a central role in the psychological adaptation to illness and trauma, enabling individuals to reconstruct their sense of self and purpose amid profound disruptions to health and identity. In the context of physical health crises, such as cancer diagnosis, patients often appraise the event as a catalyst for reevaluating life priorities, fostering post-traumatic growth (PTG) through processes like deliberate rumination, where individuals intentionally reflect on the trauma to derive positive insights. Deliberate rumination, distinct from intrusive thoughts, involves purposeful cognitive processing that helps integrate the experience into one's worldview, leading to perceived benefits such as greater appreciation for life and strengthened personal resilience. This form of meaning-making has been linked to improved emotional adjustment in cancer survivors, with studies showing that those who engage in it report higher levels of PTG compared to those relying on avoidance.
In chronic illness management, meaning-making facilitates identity reconstruction, shifting from a pre-illness self-concept (e.g., "healthy individual") to an adapted one (e.g., "survivor" or "thriver"). This process often manifests in PTG domains, including enhanced interpersonal relationshipswhere illness deepens connections with othersand the emergence of new possibilities, such as pursuing previously overlooked goals or advocacy work. Narrative approaches to meaning-making, where individuals reauthor their life stories to incorporate the illness, support this reconstruction by emphasizing continuity and agency despite ongoing symptoms. For instance, patients with conditions like inflammatory bowel disease describe how reframing their illness as a source of wisdom aids in maintaining vitality and social roles.
For non-death traumas, such as accidents or interpersonal violence, meaning-making aids recovery by helping survivors process the event's implications for safety, trust, and future outlook. This involves constructing narratives that resolve cognitive dissonance, such as why the trauma occurred and how it alters one's life trajectory, often leading to PTG through renewed purpose. The dual-process model highlights oscillation between loss-oriented coping (confronting emotional pain from the trauma) and restoration-oriented coping (addressing practical disruptions like rebuilding routines), which facilitates adaptive meaning-making without linear progression. Evidence from trauma literature indicates that successful meaning-making correlates with reduced PTSD symptoms and increased growth, particularly when supported by therapeutic guidance.
Clinical evidence underscores the efficacy of meaning-centered interventions in enhancing quality of life during illness and trauma recovery. Harvey Chochinov's dignity therapy, a brief psychotherapy for advanced cancer patients, focuses on affirming life legacies and purpose, with randomized trials demonstrating improvements in sense of meaning, reduced distress, and better end-of-life experiences compared to supportive counseling. Similarly, individual meaning-centered psychotherapy has shown in pilot trials to sustain peace and purpose in patients with metastatic cancer, leading to measurable gains in PTG domains like personal strength. These interventions highlight how structured meaning-making promotes adaptation, with meta-analyses confirming modest but significant effects on psychological well-being across trauma types.

Broader Implications for Well-Being

In Spirituality, Religion, and Philanthropy

In spirituality and religiosity, meaning-making often emerges through intrinsic religious orientations, where faith serves as an end in itself rather than a means to other goals, providing a stable framework for global meaning and purpose. Gordon Allport's seminal work distinguished this intrinsic approach as a mature form of religiosity, internalized as a guiding life principle that fosters coherence and existential fulfillment across diverse experiences. Spiritual practices such as meditation support meaning-making by promoting awareness and emotional processing, which can enhance a sense of connection to transcendent aspects of life. For instance, mindfulness-based meditation encourages reflective processing that can deepen overall meaning-making, often independent of doctrinal adherence.
Philanthropy and altruism contribute to meaning-making by channeling generativity, a psychosocial stage in Erik Erikson's developmental theory where individuals seek to nurture future generations through productive contributions. This drive manifests in altruistic behaviors like volunteering, which not only affirm personal legacy but also mitigate existential anxiety by instilling a sense of purpose beyond the self. Research from the 2010s, including longitudinal studies on older adults, demonstrates that sustained volunteer engagement correlates with reduced depressive symptoms and improved well-being indicators, as participants derive meaning from tangible impacts on others. Generativity thus bridges individual fulfillment with communal benefit, reinforcing altruism as a pathway to psychological resilience.
The interplay between spiritual frameworks and philanthropy is evident in religious doctrines that motivate altruistic action as a sacred duty. In Judaism, the concept of tikkun olamrepairing the worldurges believers to engage in social justice and charitable acts as a divine partnership to perfect creation, thereby deriving profound meaning from collective improvement. Similarly, in Buddhism, the principle of karma posits that compassionate deeds generate positive future outcomes, encouraging philanthropy through dana (generosity) as a means to cultivate ethical living and interconnectedness, which enhances personal and cosmic harmony. These traditions illustrate how spiritual beliefs transform giving into a meaningful pursuit of moral and existential balance.
Cultural variations shape spiritual meaning-making, with collectivist societies emphasizing communal harmony and interdependence in religious practices, while individualist cultures prioritize personal enlightenment and autonomy. In collectivist contexts, such as many East Asian traditions, spirituality often involves group rituals that derive meaning from shared narratives and ancestral continuity, fostering a sense of belonging over solitary reflection. Conversely, individualist approaches, prevalent in Western contexts, focus on self-directed spiritual quests that integrate personal growth with broader purpose, though both orientations ultimately support well-being through adaptive meaning construction.

In Therapy and Positive Psychology

Meaning-centered therapy represents an adaptation of Viktor Frankl's logotherapy within cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) frameworks, focusing on enhancing clients' sense of purpose and meaning as a primary mechanism for psychological change. This approach integrates logotherapy's emphasis on discovering personal significance through attitude, experience, and creative actions with CBT's structured techniques, such as cognitive restructuring to challenge meaning-disrupting beliefs. Key components include values clarification exercises, where clients rank and explore core values to align daily behaviors with deeper motivations, and purpose assessment tools like goal-setting worksheets that evaluate life domains for sources of fulfillment.
In positive psychology, interventions leveraging meaning-making promote flourishing by cultivating optimism and appreciation. The best possible self visualization exercise, introduced by King (2001), encourages individuals to vividly imagine and write about their ideal future self across personal, professional, and relational domains, thereby boosting subjective well-being and a perceived sense of purpose. Gratitude journaling, as demonstrated in experimental studies by Emmons and McCullough (2003), involves regularly noting positive events or relationships, which fosters a broader appreciation for life's meaning and reduces negative emotional states. Additionally, the VIA Inventory of Strengths survey identifies signature character strengthssuch as hope, spirituality, or kindnessthat individuals can apply to construct purpose-driven narratives, linking personal virtues to meaningful goals.
Empirical evidence supports the efficacy of meaning-making interventions in alleviating depression. A 2023 meta-analysis of 32 studies revealed that higher purpose in life is significantly associated with lower depression symptoms (r = -0.30), with stronger effects in clinical populations. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses of meaning-centered psychotherapies indicate moderate reductions in depressive symptoms and psychological distress (Hedges' g 0.5), comparable to or exceeding standard CBT outcomes in general populations; however, effects are smaller (d 0.2) in adaptations for advanced cancer patients. Similarly, 2020s meta-analyses on acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT), which incorporates values-based meaning-making, show significant improvements in depression severity (SMD = -0.68) and psychological flexibility, particularly in diverse mental health contexts.
Future directions in meaning-making therapy emphasize integration with mindfulness-based approaches and digital platforms to broaden accessibility and efficacy. Pilot studies have developed online mindfulness-based logotherapy programs that combine meaning reconstruction with present-moment awareness, yielding preliminary reductions in depressive symptoms among targeted groups like cyberbullied youth. Emerging research also highlights the potential of digital tools, such as app-based interventions and virtual reality simulations, to support ongoing meaning reconstruction by enabling personalized exercises outside sessions, though larger trials are needed to validate long-term impacts. As of 2025, recent pilots explore AI-assisted values clarification in logotherapy apps, showing promise for scalable access in post-pandemic mental health support.
