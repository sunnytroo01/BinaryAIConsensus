ELIZA
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 ELIZA is an early natural language processing computer program developed from 1964 to 1966 at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) by Joseph Weizenbaum, designed to demonstrate the superficiality of a machine's use of language by simulating conversations in English. Written in the MAD-SLIP programming language for the MAC time-sharing system on an IBM 7094 computer, ELIZA employed a pattern-matching and substitution methodology to decompose user inputs into keywords and generate responses, without any genuine understanding of meaning or context. Its most famous implementation used a script to mimic a Rogerian psychotherapist, rephrasing the user's statements as questions to encourage elaboration, such as transforming "I am unhappy" into "Why do you say you are unhappy?"
The program's interactions revealed the ELIZA effect, a phenomenon where users attributed human-like intelligence, empathy, and even emotional depth to the machine, despite its limited rule-based operations. Weizenbaum, initially intending ELIZA as a parody of human-computer communication, was alarmed by these responses, including his secretary's request for privacy during sessions and colleagues' ethical concerns about using it with vulnerable individuals. This led him to critique the over-enthusiasm for artificial intelligence in his 1976 book Computer Power and Human Reason, arguing against replacing human judgment with computational processes.
As a pioneering chatbot, ELIZA influenced subsequent developments in conversational AI, serving as a precursor to modern systems like Siri and ChatGPT, while highlighting ethical issues in human-AI interaction.

History and Development

Origins and Creation

ELIZA was created by Joseph Weizenbaum, a German-Jewish immigrant who fled Nazi persecution with his family in 1936 and later became a prominent computer scientist at MIT. Born in Berlin in 1923 to Jewish parents, Weizenbaum arrived in the United States as a teenager, eventually earning a degree in mathematics and pursuing a career in computing that led him to MIT in the early 1960s. As the sole developer, he conceived ELIZA as a personal project without collaborative input, driven by his interest in the boundaries of machine intelligence.
Development of ELIZA began in 1964 at MIT as an experiment in natural language processing aimed at demonstrating the potential for communication between humans and machines using rudimentary computational methods. Weizenbaum's initial motivation was to investigate whether simple rule-based systems could simulate human-like conversation, drawing inspiration from the non-directive techniques of Rogerian psychotherapy developed by psychologist Carl Rogers in the mid-20th century. Rogers' approach emphasized reflective listening and empathy, where the therapist mirrors the client's statements to foster self-exploration, a model Weizenbaum adapted to test how pattern recognition might evoke meaningful dialogue without deep semantic understanding.
A key milestone came in 1966 when Weizenbaum completed the first working version of ELIZA, implemented on an IBM 7094 computer within MIT's Compatible Time-Sharing System (CTSS) and programmed in MAD-SLIP, an extension of the MAD language for list processing. This version, running the DOCTOR script to simulate a psychotherapist, marked ELIZA's debut and was publicly demonstrated at MIT later that year, showcasing its ability to engage users in text-based interactions. The project unfolded amid the vibrant 1960s atmosphere of MIT's Artificial Intelligence Laboratory, a pioneering hub for computational research.

Development at MIT

ELIZA was developed at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology's Artificial Intelligence Laboratory, established in 1963 as part of Project MAC, which received a $2.2 million grant from the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) to advance computing and AI research during the era's AI boom. This funding supported the lab's exploration of natural language processing and time-sharing systems, providing the institutional framework for Joseph Weizenbaum's work on human-computer communication.
The program ran on the IBM 7094 mainframe computer within MIT's MAC time-sharing system, which offered only 32,000 words (approximately 144 kilobytes) of user memory and imposed strict limits on processing time and storage to support multiple simultaneous users via remote typewriters. These constraints necessitated efficient coding in MAD-SLIP, a list-processing language, with ELIZA's core logic fitting into minimal spaceusing a keyword dictionary of up to 128 entries and scripts limited to essential decomposition and reassembly rules. Weizenbaum designed the system to prioritize economical resource use, avoiding memory-intensive features like extensive context tracking.
Development occurred over several months in 1964 and 1965 through an iterative process of scripting, testing, and refinement, where Weizenbaum adjusted rules based on conversational outcomes to simulate natural dialogue. Testing involved colleagues and students interacting with early versions to evaluate response coherence, revealing how simple patterns could elicit extended exchanges despite the program's lack of true comprehension.
In January 1966, Weizenbaum published a detailed description of ELIZA in Communications of the ACM, including pseudocode and sample scripts, which facilitated its informal sharing and rapid adoption across academic institutions for research in language processing. This openness addressed a key challenge: achieving convincingly human-like interactions through basic pattern matching and keyword substitution, without relying on emerging techniques like machine learning, thereby demonstrating the potential of rule-based systems under hardware limitations.
In 2021, original ELIZA source code and an early DOCTOR script were rediscovered in Weizenbaum's MIT archives. As of January 2025, researchers successfully reanimated the program on an emulated IBM 7094 running CTSS, confirming its original functionality and providing new opportunities for study.

Technical Design

Core Architecture

ELIZA operates as a rule-based expert system designed as a script interpreter that relies on predefined patterns and transformations to simulate conversational responses. This architecture enables the program to process natural language inputs without employing statistical models or machine learning, instead drawing from a collection of scripted rules authored in a domain-specific language. The system's core functionality centers on interpreting user statements through a series of matching and substitution operations, allowing it to mimic dialogue in a structured yet flexible manner.
The main components of ELIZA's architecture include keyword recognition, decomposition rules, and reassembly templates. Keyword recognition scans the input for significant words assigned ranks and precedence levels, prioritizing those that trigger relevant response categories. Once a keyword is identified, decomposition rules parse the surrounding input text into structured segments, such as placeholders for subject and object phrases, to analyze context. Responses are then formed using reassembly templates, which substitute the parsed elements into predefined output patterns to generate coherent replies.
ELIZA's memory model features no persistent state or learning mechanisms, with each response generated independently based on the current input. A limited "MEMORY" feature employs a first-in-first-out stack to store and occasionally retrieve transformed prior inputs as fallback responses when no keywords are detected, but this does not enable adaptation or long-term context retention.
Input and output handling in ELIZA utilizes a text-based interface connected via teletype terminals within the MIT MAC time-sharing system, processing user statements line-by-line until terminated by a double carriage return. This sequential approach ensures real-time interaction, with the program echoing the user's input for confirmation before generating and displaying the response.
The architecture supports scalability through extensible script files, which define the patterns and templates as data rather than hardcoded program elements, facilitating the creation of different "personalities" such as variations in language or conversational style beyond the default psychotherapy simulation. These scripts can be edited using the system's ED editor, allowing researchers to customize and experiment with the program's behavior without altering the underlying interpreter.

Pattern Matching and Response Generation

ELIZA's pattern matching process begins by scanning the user's input sentence from left to right to identify keywords, which serve as triggers for subsequent analysis. Each keyword is associated with one or more decomposition rules, which are template patterns designed to break the input into structural components. For instance, a rule might take the form (0 YOU 0 ME), where 0 represents an indefinite number of words, allowing the system to isolate segments before and after the keywords "YOU" and "ME" to capture the sentence's context. Keywords such as "mother" are specifically targeted, often tagged for semantic categories like /(/page/Noun) (/page/Family) to enable flexible matching through techniques such as or-ing, where a single rule can apply to related terms like (/FATHER (/page/Mother)).
Once a keyword is found, the corresponding decomposition rule is applied to fragment the input, prioritizing the highest-ranked keyword based on a precedence system. Keywords are assigned numerical ranks, with higher values indicating greater importance; for example, "MY" has a rank of 5, ensuring it overrides lower-ranked matches in the processing stack if multiple keywords appear. This ranking mechanism guides the selection of the most relevant decomposition, building a "keystack" that holds the matched keywords and their decomposed parts for further use.
Response generation follows by applying transformation rules to the decomposed elements, which modify the input to create a coherent reply from pre-defined templates, known as reassembly rules. Transformation rules handle pronoun substitutions, such as replacing "MY" with "YOUR" or "ME" with "YOU", to shift the perspective from the user to the program. A reassembly rule might then insert these transformed parts into a template, for example, reconstructing "WHAT MAKES YOU THINK I 3 YOU" where 3 denotes the user's original decomposed segment, effectively reflecting the input back in a question form. To vary responses and avoid repetition, the system cycles through multiple reassembly rules associated with a keyword, selecting the next unused one in sequence.
For handling ambiguities in user input, ELIZA employs reflection techniques that rephrase statements as questions to probe deeper without requiring deep semantic understanding. An example is transforming a declarative input like "I feel sad" into "Why do you feel sad?", achieved by decomposing the sentence and applying rules that append interrogative elements to the reflected content. This approach relies on the substitution grammar to mirror the user's words, fostering an illusion of empathetic engagement through syntactic manipulation rather than true comprehension.
In cases of unrecognized input, error handling ensures conversational continuity via default mechanisms. If no keywords match, the system invokes the "NONE" keyword, triggering content-free responses such as "Please go on" or similar neutral prompts to encourage further input. Additionally, a "MEMORY" mechanism stores transformed prior inputs triggered by certain keywords (e.g., "MY") and retrieves them from a FIFO stack as fallback responses when no keywords are detected, providing a fallback that maintains the flow without breaking the interaction.

Pseudocode Representation

ELIZA's operational logic can be represented in simplified pseudocode to illustrate its core processes of input parsing, keyword identification, rule application, and response formulation, drawing from descriptions in its foundational implementation. This abstraction highlights the program's reliance on script-based rules rather than complex natural language understanding. The main loop continuously processes user input until termination, scanning for keywords and applying predefined transformations to generate responses.
The high-level pseudocode structure is as follows:
Initialize script (load KEYWORDS list, RULES array with patterns and response templates)
MEMORY = empty stack // For storing previous inputs if no match

While not end_of_conversation:
 INPUT = get_user_input() // Read and preprocess: remove (/page/Punctuation), apply substitutions (e.g., "I" -> "you")
 if INPUT contains quit_command:
 end_of_conversation = true
 else:
 KEYWORD = find_highest_rank_keyword(INPUT, KEYWORDS) // Scan left-to-right, select highest precedence
 if KEYWORD found:
 PATTERN = select_matching_pattern(INPUT, RULES) // Decompose INPUT into segments based on (/page/Pattern)
 RESPONSE = reassemble(PATTERN, response_template) // Substitute decomposed parts into template
 if memory_trigger_keyword(KEYWORD): // e.g., "MY"
 push(transformed_INPUT, MEMORY) // Store selectively for potential future use
 else:
 if MEMORY not empty:
 RESPONSE = pop_from_MEMORY() // Retrieve from FIFO stack
 else:
 RESPONSE = default_response() // Use generic reply like "Please go on."
 output(RESPONSE)
This outline captures the essential flow: preprocessing transforms the input for consistency (e.g., pronoun shifts), keyword search prioritizes significant terms, decomposition breaks the input into parts matching rule patterns, and reassembly inserts those parts into output templates to create coherent replies.
Key variables include the KEYWORDS list, a ranked dictionary of terms (e.g., "mother" ranked high due to its association with family topics), and the RULES array, which pairs decomposition patterns (e.g., (0 YOU 0 ME) where 0 denotes variable segments) with corresponding response templates containing placeholders (e.g., numbers for segment insertion). Temporary buffers, such as decomposition arrays, hold input segments during matching, while the MEMORY stack manages up to a fixed number of prior transformed inputs to enable fallback responses. These elements enable rule-based substitutions without maintaining conversation state beyond simple recall.
A concrete example pseudocode snippet for a specific rule demonstrates the decomposition and reassembly: for inputs like "I am sad", preliminary substitutions change it to "YOU ARE SAD", which matches a decomposition rule such as (0 YOU ARE 0 (-SAD UNHAPPY DEPRESSED SICK) 0), extracting the emotional term, and reassembles using a template like "I AM SORRY TO HEAR YOU ARE ".

 DECOMPOSED_EMOTION = extract_emotion_part(INPUT) // e.g., "SAD"
 RESPONSE = "I AM SORRY TO HEAR YOU ARE " + DECOMPOSED_EMOTION
 // Output: "I AM SORRY TO HEAR YOU ARE SAD"
This rule exemplifies ELIZA's technique of reflecting user statements back to simulate engagement.
For instance, processing the input "My mother is annoying" involves matching the keyword "mother" (from a family-related list), selecting a rule that decomposes around familial terms, and generating the response "Tell me more about your family" via a template encouraging elaboration on the topic. Such isolated applications showcase the program's modular rule system.
This pseudocode abstracts the original MAD-SL implementation, prioritizing readability over the exact syntax and low-level details like hashing or equivalence classes used in the 1966 system.

Operation and User Interaction

Simulation of Psychotherapy

ELIZA's DOCTOR script emulates the style of a Rogerian psychotherapist, a non-directive approach developed by Carl Rogers that prioritizes empathy, unconditional positive regard, and reflection of the client's feelings to facilitate self-exploration without providing advice or interpretations. This simulation relies on scripted responses designed to encourage users to delve deeper into their emotions and experiences, fostering an illusion of therapeutic dialogue through mirroring and probing questions.
The DOCTOR script consists of approximately 420 lines of rules, primarily focused on topics related to family dynamics, emotional states, and personal self-reflection. These rules use keyword recognition to decompose user inputs and reassemble them into responses that reflect back the user's statements, often transforming pronouns and rephrasing for emphasis, such as responding to a user's mention of unhappiness with a question like "DO YOU THINK COMING HERE WILL HELP YOU NOT TO BE UNHAPPY?" to provoke further elaboration. This interaction style avoids directive guidance, instead aiming to maintain a neutral, supportive persona that prompts the user to continue expressing themselves.
Sessions in the DOCTOR script follow an open-ended structure, beginning with an initial prompt such as "HOW DO YOU DO. PLEASE TELL ME YOUR PROBLEM," and continuing indefinitely based on user input until the user terminates the conversation, thereby simulating the flexible, client-led nature of Rogerian therapy. However, the persona has inherent limitations, as ELIZA possesses no genuine comprehension of context or emotions; it operates solely through surface-level pattern recognition and predefined transformations, discarding most input details after processing and unable to form conclusions or retain long-term insights.

Example Dialogues

One of the most illustrative examples of ELIZA's operation is a short exchange focusing on personal feelings, drawn from the original implementation's DOCTOR script. In this dialogue, the user expresses emotional distress, and ELIZA responds with empathy and reflection to encourage further disclosure:
User: He says I'm depressed much of the time.
ELIZA: I AM SORRY TO HEAR YOU ARE DEPRESSED.
This response is generated by decomposing the input to identify the keyword "depressed," which triggers a pre-defined reassembly rule expressing sympathy while mirroring the user's sentiment to simulate Rogerian psychotherapy. The rule substitutes "you" for "I" in the reflection, maintaining engagement by validating the user's experience without providing direct advice.
A longer sample dialogue demonstrates ELIZA's handling of family-related topics, showcasing keyword chaining and reflective questioning to probe relationships:
User: My mother takes care of me.
ELIZA: WHO ELSE IN YOUR FAMILY TAKES CARE OF YOU
User: My father.
ELIZA: YOUR FATHER
User: You are like my father in some ways.
ELIZA: WHAT RESEMBLANCE DO YOU SEE
User: You are not very aggressive.
ELIZA: WHAT MAKES YOU THINK I AM NOT VERY AGGRESSIVE
Here, the initial input matches the keyword "mother" (tagged as a family term), prompting a rule that inquires about other family members to expand the conversation. Subsequent responses reflect the user's words (e.g., repeating "YOUR FATHER" to acknowledge the statement) or apply transformation rules to keywords like "like" and "aggressive," substituting possessives and adding interrogative phrases to sustain dialogue flow. These mechanisms chain responses based on keyword precedence, ensuring the interaction feels attentive and exploratory.
ELIZA's flexibility allowed for alternative scripts beyond psychotherapy simulation, such as the SPACKS persona for poetry analysis, where it would parse user-submitted verses and generate interpretive commentary using similar pattern-matching rules adapted for literary critique.
Common pitfalls in ELIZA interactions often arose from inputs lacking recognizable keywords, leading to generic or repetitive responses. For instance, if a user's statement like "The weather is nice today" fails to match any decomposition rule, ELIZA defaults to "PLEASE GO ON" or a similar evasive prompt, which could frustrate users by halting meaningful progression and highlighting the program's reliance on scripted triggers. In extended sessions, repeated use of the same keywords might cycle through limited reassembly options, resulting in echoing reflections that feel stagnant.

Impact and Legacy

The ELIZA Effect

The ELIZA effect describes the human tendency to anthropomorphize computers by attributing qualities such as intelligence, empathy, or intentionality to them, particularly in conversational interactions, despite recognizing their mechanistic nature. This phenomenon emerged from users' reactions to ELIZA, the 1966 chatbot program created by Joseph Weizenbaum at MIT, which simulated a Rogerian psychotherapist through simple keyword recognition and response rephrasing. Weizenbaum observed that even brief exposures led individuals to project personal meanings onto the program's outputs, fostering an illusion of mutual understanding.
At MIT, Weizenbaum noted striking emotional engagements with ELIZA among students and staff, who treated the program as a confidant capable of therapeutic insight. A notable example involved Weizenbaum's secretary, who insisted on privacy during her sessions with ELIZA and reacted with distress upon learning that Weizenbaum could access the conversation logs, revealing how the therapy script's empathetic mirroring prompted users to disclose intimate details and form bonds with what was merely a rule-based system. These interactions underscored psychological factors like confirmation bias and the human inclination to infer agency from ambiguous responses, amplified by the script's non-directive style that encouraged self-reflection. Weizenbaum's concerns about this anthropomorphism grew from these observations, highlighting ethical risks in human-computer relations.
In response, Weizenbaum articulated his unease in his seminal 1966 paper, cautioning that programs like ELIZA could too easily manufacture the appearance of comprehension, potentially misleading users in critical contexts. He expanded on these themes in his 1976 book Computer Power and Human Reason: From Judgment to Calculation, where he critiqued the societal over-reliance on machines for empathetic or judgmental roles, arguing that such substitutions erode human rationality and ethical discernment. Weizenbaum viewed the ELIZA effect as a symptom of broader technological hubris, urging restraint in deploying AI for inherently human domains.
The ELIZA effect retains relevance today, as users frequently exhibit similar projections toward advanced chatbots, mistaking pattern-based replies for genuine sentience or care.

Influence on Artificial Intelligence

ELIZA's demonstration of pattern-matching and scripted responses in the mid-1960s exerted a profound immediate impact on the development of conversational AI, inspiring a wave of chatbots in the 1970s and 1980s. Notably, Kenneth Colby's PARRY program, released in 1972, used similar rule-based techniques to ELIZA to model paranoid schizophrenia, incorporating keyword-driven responses and a simulated emotional state to engage users in therapy-like dialogues. This progression highlighted ELIZA's role in pioneering rule-based natural language processing (NLP) techniques, which emphasized syntactic parsing over semantic understanding and laid groundwork for early frameworks in dialogue systems.
In academic circles, ELIZA's publication of its algorithm and pseudocode in Weizenbaum's 1966 paper spurred numerous implementations and adaptations, enabling researchers worldwide to experiment with conversational algorithms and extend its script-based model to diverse domains. Its interactions fueled pivotal discussions on the Turing Test, positioning ELIZA as a benchmark for evaluating whether machines could simulate human-like conversation convincingly, even without true comprehensionprompting debates on intelligence metrics that persist in AI evaluation today. ELIZA's legacy also permeated cultural narratives, contributing to broader depictions of sentient computers in media and in seminal AI history texts that trace the roots of human-computer interaction.
ELIZA established enduring foundations for rule-based systems. This contrasts sharply with contemporary machine learning-driven chatbots, such as those based on GPT models, which generate responses via probabilistic training on massive corpora rather than predefined rules, marking a shift from explicit scripting to data-driven inference. In January 2025, researchers resurrected the original ELIZA code on an emulated IBM 7094, allowing modern access to its historical implementation. Furthermore, ELIZA ignited ethical debates on AI's boundaries, particularly its inability to replicate genuine human insight, as Weizenbaum himself critiqued in his 1976 book Computer Power and Human Reason: From Judgment to Calculation, where he warned against overattributing intelligence to computational simulations.
