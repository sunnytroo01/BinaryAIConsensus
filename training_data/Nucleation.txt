Nucleation

 Nucleation is the initial process in phase transitions where small clusters of atoms, ions, or molecules aggregate to form stable nuclei capable of growing into a new thermodynamic phase, such as crystals from a solution or vapor, droplets in condensation, or bubbles in boiling. This phenomenon occurs in supersaturated, supercooled, or compressed systems and is governed by the balance between the bulk free energy gain driving phase separation and the surface free energy penalty of creating an interface. Nucleation marks the onset of spontaneous growth, distinguishing it from mere fluctuations, and is fundamental to processes in physics, chemistry, and materials science.
There are two primary types of nucleation: homogeneous, which arises uniformly within the bulk of a pure phase without external aids, requiring significant supersaturation to overcome a high free energy barrier; and heterogeneous, which is facilitated by impurities, surfaces, or container walls that lower the energy barrier, making it the dominant mechanism in most practical scenarios. In homogeneous nucleation, the rate depends exponentially on the supersaturation level and temperature, as clusters must reach a critical size beyond which growth is favored over dissolution. Heterogeneous nucleation, by contrast, reduces the effective interfacial energy, allowing initiation at lower driving forces and influencing the sites and orientation of the resulting phase.
The theoretical foundation of nucleation is provided by classical nucleation theory (CNT), which models the process as a thermally activated event where the free energy of cluster formation, G, combines a negative volume term proportional to the chemical potential difference () and a positive surface term involving interfacial tension (). Formulated in the 1930s by Becker and Dring based on earlier ideas from Gibbs, CNT predicts a maximum free energy barrier G* at the critical nucleus radius r*, given approximately by G* = (16)/(3()) for spherical nuclei in three dimensions, leading to a nucleation rate J exp(-G*/kT). While CNT captures qualitative trends, it often overestimates rates due to assumptions like the capillary approximation and has been refined by observations of multistep pathways involving intermediate dense phases.
Nucleation plays a pivotal role in diverse applications, from controlling crystal size and polymorphism in pharmaceutical manufacturing to influencing grain structure in metallurgy and ice formation in atmospheric clouds. In materials processing, manipulating nucleation via additives or shear can tailor microstructures for enhanced properties, while in geophysics and biology, it governs phenomena like mineral precipitation and biomineralization. Understanding and controlling nucleation remains a challenge, driving ongoing research into non-classical mechanisms and nanoscale effects.

Fundamentals

Definition and Importance

Nucleation is the process by which a new thermodynamic phase or structure forms within a parent phase through the aggregation of molecules or atoms into clusters, or embryos, that surpass a critical size and become stable nuclei capable of further growth. This phenomenon is particularly relevant to first-order phase transitions, where thermodynamic properties such as volume, entropy, and energy exhibit discontinuous changes at the transition point, in contrast to continuous, or second-order, phase transitions that involve gradual variations without latent heat or abrupt shifts./04:_Phase_Transitions/4.02:_Continuous_phase_transitions)
The foundational understanding of nucleation emerged from the work of J. Willard Gibbs, who in 18761878 developed the thermodynamic framework for heterogeneous equilibria, elucidating how new phases arise under conditions of supersaturation or supercooling in systems like fluids of varying density. Gibbs' analysis highlighted the energy barriers to phase formation, setting the stage for later models such as Classical Nucleation Theory.
Nucleation is pivotal across scientific and industrial domains, governing the initiation of crystallization in pharmaceutical manufacturing to control drug particle size, polymorphism, and bioavailability; influencing condensation and ice formation in atmospheric processes that drive weather patterns and cloud development; and directing solidification in metallurgy to refine grain structures that enhance material strength and ductility. These roles underscore nucleation's impact on everything from material properties to large-scale environmental phenomena.

Thermodynamic Principles

Nucleation is driven by the thermodynamic tendency of a system to minimize its free energy by transitioning from a metastable phase to a more stable one, with the driving force quantified by the difference in chemical potential between the parent and emerging phases. This chemical potential difference, denoted as , arises from supersaturation and is expressed as = kT \ln S, where k is the Boltzmann constant, T is the temperature, and S is the supersaturation ratio, typically defined as the ratio of the actual concentration (or activity) to the equilibrium saturation value. For S > 1, the system is supersaturated, providing the energetic impetus for phase separation, such as crystallization from solution or condensation from vapor.
The total Gibbs free energy change associated with forming a cluster of the new phase balances two competing contributions: a favorable bulk term and an unfavorable surface term. The bulk free energy gain per unit volume, G_v, is negative and proportional to the chemical potential difference, given by G_v = - / v_m, where v_m is the molecular volume in the new phase; this term scales with the cluster volume V as -|G_v| V. Opposing this is the positive interfacial energy penalty due to the creation of a new interface, A, where is the interfacial free energy per unit area and A is the surface area of the cluster. The net free energy change is thus G = -|G_v| V + A.
For a spherical cluster, this leads to the concept of a critical radius r^, below which clusters are unstable and dissolve, while those above r^ grow spontaneously. The critical radius is derived by maximizing G with respect to r, yielding r^* = 2 / |G_v|. At this size, the bulk driving force exactly balances the surface tension penalty, marking the saddle point in the free energy landscape.
The magnitude of G_v, and thus the driving force, is modulated by temperature and pressure, which influence the degree of supersaturation and the phase equilibrium. Lowering temperature typically increases supersaturation in solutions by reducing solubility, thereby enhancing |G_v|, while pressure can shift the chemical potential difference through changes in the volume term in the Gibbs free energy, as per the relation = V dP at constant T. These external parameters thus control the thermodynamic feasibility of nucleation across diverse systems, from atmospheric condensation to materials processing.

Types of Nucleation

Homogeneous Nucleation

Homogeneous nucleation refers to the formation of a new phase within a pure, supersaturated parent phase, occurring uniformly throughout the volume without the influence of any foreign substrates, impurities, or container walls. This process demands a high degree of supersaturation to surmount the full free energy barrier, denoted as G*, associated with creating the interface between the emerging nucleus and the surrounding medium. In the absence of heterogeneous catalysts, the nucleation event initiates spontaneously in the bulk fluid when thermal fluctuations generate a sufficiently stable cluster of the new phase.
The characteristics of homogeneous nucleation include its stochastic nature and the requirement for extreme thermodynamic conditions, such as deep supercooling in liquids or high supersaturation in vapors, to achieve appreciable rates. It is predominantly observed in meticulously cleaned systems where all potential nucleation sites have been eliminated, as the process is highly sensitive to even trace contaminants. In such ideal scenarios, the nucleation rate follows the classical expression derived from transition state theory, exponentially dependent on the barrier height G*. Due to this pronounced temperature and supersaturation dependence, homogeneous nucleation remains rare under typical laboratory or natural conditions.
A prominent example of homogeneous nucleation is the freezing of supercooled pure water droplets, which occurs around -40C to -42C, well below the equilibrium freezing point of 0C, in the absence of ice-nucleating particles. This threshold marks the point where the free energy barrier for ice embryo formation becomes surmountable purely through bulk fluctuations. Another instance involves bubble formation via cavitation in pure liquids subjected to negative pressures or tension, as seen in overstretched water where metastable states lead to spontaneous vapor bubble nucleation without gas pockets or surfaces. These examples highlight the process's occurrence in clean, impurity-free environments under extreme driving forces.
Homogeneous nucleation is less common in practice because the nucleation rate exhibits an exponential sensitivity to the free energy barrier G*, such that even modest reductions in this barrieroften provided inadvertently by impuritiesdramatically favor alternative pathways. This inherent rarity underscores why most real-world phase transitions proceed via lower-barrier mechanisms in impure systems.

Nucleation Mechanisms

Primary and Secondary Nucleation

Primary nucleation refers to the spontaneous formation of new crystal nuclei from a supersaturated solution in the absence of pre-existing crystals, serving as the initiation mechanism for batch crystallization processes. This process can occur through homogeneous nucleation, where nuclei form uniformly throughout the solution without foreign particles, or more commonly via heterogeneous nucleation on impurities or container surfaces, which lowers the energy barrier required. Primary nucleation is driven primarily by the degree of supersaturation, with rates increasing exponentially as supersaturation rises, though it typically requires higher supersaturation levels compared to subsequent nucleation events.
In contrast, secondary nucleation involves the generation of new nuclei from existing crystals in the system, often triggered by mechanical forces such as shear, attrition, or direct contact between crystals and equipment surfaces in stirred crystallizers. These mechanisms detach small crystal fragments or induce surface roughening that acts as nucleation sites, leading to a proliferation of new crystals at lower supersaturation levels than primary nucleation. Secondary nucleation rates are influenced by factors including crystal size, suspension density, and agitation intensity, with experimental studies showing rates that can exceed primary nucleation by up to six orders of magnitude under typical industrial conditions.
The key differences between primary and secondary nucleation lie in their dependencies: primary nucleation relies solely on supersaturation to overcome the free energy barrier for nucleus formation, independent of existing crystals, while secondary nucleation is contingent on the presence of seed crystals and external perturbations like agitation. For secondary processes, models highlight how hydrodynamic conditions, such as power input per unit mass, modulate nucleation behavior. This distinction is critical for process design, as secondary nucleation often dominates in dynamic environments, altering the overall kinetics from those predicted by primary mechanisms alone.
In industrial applications, secondary nucleation plays a pivotal role in controlling the particle size distribution (PSD) during crystallization, particularly in processes like sugar refining where excessive secondary nuclei can lead to fines that reduce product quality and recovery efficiency. Strategies such as controlled seeding and agitation optimization are employed to harness secondary nucleation for uniform crystal sizes while minimizing unwanted proliferation.

Spinodal Decomposition

Spinodal decomposition represents a barrierless mechanism of phase separation that occurs within the spinodal region of a binary mixture's phase diagram, where the system is thermodynamically unstable. This instability arises because the second derivative of the free energy density with respect to composition is negative, 2fc2<0\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial c^2} < 0c22f<0, rendering small composition fluctuations amplified spontaneously without requiring thermal activation over an energy barrier. The mechanism involves the diffusive amplification of infinitesimal composition fluctuations, leading to the formation of a modulated structure that evolves into coarser domains. These two curves converge at the critical point, where the distinction vanishes, and the order parameter susceptibility diverges. Unlike nucleation-dominated separation in the metastable region between the binodal and spinodal, which yields discrete, spherical droplets of the minority phase, spinodal decomposition produces an interconnected bicontinuous morphology due to the absence of a nucleation barrier and the diffusive nature of the instability. This results in a labyrinthine structure that coarsens via diffusion and hydrodynamics, fundamentally altering the kinetics and final microstructure compared to droplet-based growth.

Nucleation in Crystallization

Crystal Nucleation Processes

Crystal nucleation begins with the aggregation of molecules or ions into small, transient clusters that must overcome thermodynamic barriers to evolve into stable crystal lattices. These clusters form through diffusive attachment and detachment processes in a supersaturated solution or melt, where the initial assemblies lack long-range order but gradually develop positional and orientational correlations as they grow beyond the critical size. In anisotropic crystals, such as those with non-spherical building units, orientational order plays a crucial role by aligning molecular dipoles or facets to minimize interfacial energy, facilitating the transition from disordered clusters to ordered lattices with specific symmetry. This alignment is particularly evident in systems like liquid crystals, where pre-existing nematic ordering in the parent phase lowers the nucleation barrier compared to isotropic fluids. Primary nucleation serves as the foundational mechanism here, initiating spontaneous cluster formation without pre-existing crystals.
The extent of the metastable zone, where supersaturated solutions remain without detectable crystallization, is governed by the free energy barrier G\Delta G^*G to nucleation. Higher barriers extend the zone by slowing the kinetics of cluster formation to critical size, considering the time required for detectable nucleation to occur. Complementing this, the induction time tindt_{ind}tind, the lag between achieving supersaturation and observable crystallization, is inversely proportional to the nucleation rate JJJ, such that tind1/Jt_{ind} \propto 1/Jtind1/J. This relationship arises because JJJ dictates the frequency of critical nucleus formation, with shorter induction times signaling faster kinetics under higher supersaturation.
In polymorphic systems, metastable polymorphs often nucleate at higher rates than their stable counterparts due to lower solid-liquid interfacial energy \gamma, which reduces the free energy barrier G\Delta G^*G according to G3\Delta G^* \propto \gamma^3G3. This kinetic preference aligns with Ostwald's rule of stages, where the polymorph with the lowest nucleation barrier forms first, even if thermodynamically less stable, as seen in pharmaceuticals like paracetamol where metastable forms emerge rapidly under moderate supersaturation. The lower \gamma for metastable forms stems from better lattice matching with the solution phase, enabling faster attachment rates during early cluster stages.
Non-classical nucleation pathways, such as two-step processes, deviate from direct cluster-to-crystal transitions by involving a dense liquid intermediate that acts as a precursor state. In protein solutions like lysozyme, molecules first condense into metastable dense liquid droplets, which concentrate solute and lower the local barrier for subsequent crystalline ordering within these clusters. This mechanism enhances nucleation efficiency by decoupling density fluctuations from structural ordering, as observed in atomic force microscopy studies where lysozyme crystals emerge from liquid-like precursors rather than sparse aggregates. Such paths are prevalent in biomolecular systems, where the dense phase provides a scaffold for oriented assembly, reducing the overall activation energy compared to classical routes. Recent studies as of 2025 have observed similar multistage pathways in inorganic molten salts like LiF, mirroring biomolecular processes and supporting the generality of non-classical mechanisms.

Experimental Observations

Experimental observations of nucleation in crystallization have been pivotal in validating theoretical models and revealing the stochastic nature of the process. Key techniques include the use of droplet emulsions to isolate homogeneous nucleation by minimizing heterogeneous sites, allowing researchers to study pure solution behavior under controlled conditions. For heterogeneous nucleation, optical and atomic force microscopy techniques enable direct visualization of nucleation sites on surfaces, such as impurities or container walls, providing insights into site-specific activation energies. Small-volume statistics, often employing droplets on the order of 10^{-9} L (nanoliters), facilitate the measurement of induction time (t_ind) distributions by increasing the frequency of rare nucleation events through high-throughput sampling.
Nucleation times in these experiments typically exhibit a lognormal distribution, reflecting the multiplicative effects of varying supersaturation and local fluctuations in cluster formation during the induction period. Overall crystallization kinetics are commonly described by the Avrami equation, which captures the transformation fraction X(t) as a function of time:
X(t)=1exp(ktn)
X(t) = 1 - \exp(-k t^n)
X(t)=1exp(ktn)
where k incorporates nucleation and growth rates, and the exponent n indicates the dimensionality and mechanism (e.g., n 3-4 for sporadic nucleation with 3D growth). These distributions allow fitting of experimental data to nucleation rate expressions J, linking observed times to theoretical barriers.
In small volumes, nucleation follows Poisson statistics due to its rarity, enabling precise rate calculations from the fraction of nucleated droplets. For instance, experiments on ice nucleation in supercooled water droplets have shown that heterogeneous mechanisms dominate above approximately -35C due to impurities, while homogeneous nucleation occurs around -37C in pure systems.
Recent advances have employed high-speed imaging to capture cluster formation dynamics, revealing rapid attachment and reconfiguration of pre-critical aggregates in milliseconds during supersaturated solutions. Microfluidic devices further enhance control, generating uniform droplets under precise supersaturation gradients to quantify nucleation rates and polymorph selectivity in real-time.

Nucleation in Solids

Nucleation in solids primarily occurs during solid-state phase transformations, such as those in metals, alloys, and amorphous materials, where atomic mobility is limited compared to fluids, often leading to heterogeneous mechanisms at defects or interfaces. These processes are crucial for controlling microstructure and properties in materials like steels and glasses. In metallic systems, nucleation can be broadly classified into martensitic and diffusional types, each governed by distinct kinetics and driving forces.
Martensitic nucleation involves athermal shear transformations, typically in steels, where rapid, diffusionless shear displaces atoms collectively to form a body-centered tetragonal structure from face-centered cubic austenite. This process is heterogeneous, initiating at defects like dislocations or inclusions in the parent phase, with the driving force provided by undercooling below the martensite start temperature (M_s). Seminal work by Olson and Cohen established a dislocation-based mechanism involving glide of Shockley partials on close-packed planes, enabling the formation of nuclei with martensite crystallography without long-range diffusion. In contrast, diffusional nucleation in alloys proceeds via thermally activated atomic jumps, often forming coherent precipitates that maintain lattice continuity with the matrix to minimize interfacial energy. For instance, in aluminum alloys, Guinier-Preston zones nucleate coherently as solute-rich clusters, evolving into metastable '' precipitates before stable phases, as modeled by classical nucleation theory with Gibbs free energy barriers G* = (16)/(3g_v), where is the interfacial energy and g_v the volume driving force.
Recrystallization nucleation arises during annealing of deformed metals, where new strain-free grains form to reduce stored deformation energy. Nucleation sites are preferentially deformation heterogeneities, such as shear bands, transition bands, or grain boundaries, where high dislocation densities create local regions of high misorientation. The driving force is the stored energy from dislocations, expressed as G_v = E_stored, with as dislocation density and E_stored as the average energy per dislocation (approximately G b / 2, where G is the shear modulus and b the Burgers vector). Early studies by Bailey and Hirsch highlighted how these sites enable subgrain rotation or boundary bulging to form viable nuclei, with the process accelerating at higher stored energies from severe deformation.
In amorphous solids like glasses, nucleation drives devitrification, transforming the non-crystalline structure to crystalline phases, often starting above the glass transition temperature. Surface crystallization dominates due to enhanced molecular mobility at free surfaces, where reduced coordination lowers activation barriers for nucleus formation compared to the bulk. Experimental observations in silicate and organic glasses show crystals growing upward from surfaces at rates up to 1,000 times faster than in the interior, attributed to surface diffusion bypassing bulk viscosity constraints.
A key example is the eutectoid transformation in the iron-carbon system at 727C, where austenite decomposes into ferrite and cementite (pearlite). Nucleation occurs heterogeneously at prior austenite grain boundaries, which lower the energy barrier for ferrite allotriomorphs to form, followed by cooperative ledgewise growth of lamellae via carbon diffusion. This grain boundary preference arises from the high interfacial energy of boundaries (~0.5-1 J/m), facilitating the initial phase separation in hypoeutectoid steels. In certain alloys, spinodal decomposition may precede nucleation by creating composition modulations that serve as precursors for precipitate formation.

Ice Nucleation

Ice nucleation in pure water occurs via homogeneous mechanisms only at significant supercooling, typically around -39C, where the process is hindered by a high free energy barrier arising from the structural strain required to organize water molecules into the rigid hexagonal lattice of ice Ih. This temperature marks the point where the nucleation rate becomes appreciable in the absence of impurities, as determined from laboratory experiments with purified water droplets. The barrier reflects the energetic cost of forming an ordered cluster in the disordered liquid, with classical nucleation theory predicting rates that align with observations near this threshold.
In atmospheric and environmental contexts, ice formation predominantly proceeds through heterogeneous nucleation on foreign particles, which reduce the energy barrier by providing templates that mimic the ice lattice. Common nucleants include mineral dust such as kaolinite, a clay mineral whose layered structure facilitates epitaxial matching with ice, enabling nucleation at temperatures as warm as -15C to -20C depending on particle size and surface properties. Bacterial ice nucleating proteins (INPs), particularly from species like Pseudomonas syringae, further lower this barrier through repetitive protein domains that align water molecules, promoting ice formation near -2C and playing roles in frost damage to plants. Organic particles, such as soot or biological debris, also serve as sites, though their efficiency varies with surface chemistry. Heterogeneous processes operate in distinct modes: immersion freezing, where particles within supercooled droplets initiate crystallization, and deposition nucleation, where ice deposits directly from water vapor onto dry particles in subsaturated conditions.
Supercooled water remains metastable as a liquid down to approximately -40C, a stability limit tied to the homogeneous nucleation threshold, allowing persistent liquid phases in atmospheric clouds despite subfreezing temperatures. This persistence is crucial for mixed-phase clouds, where the Bergeron process drives precipitation: ice crystals grow preferentially by sublimation of vapor from surrounding supercooled droplets due to the lower saturation vapor pressure over ice compared to liquid water. Experimental droplet studies have observed these dynamics, revealing stochastic freezing events that underscore the role of particle concentration in cloud glaciation.
Biological systems leverage specialized proteins to modulate ice nucleation for survival or function, with INPs from bacteria exemplifying how molecular templates can drastically reduce the supercooling required, enabling ice formation at near-zero temperatures. These proteins lower the nucleation barrier by providing ordered hydration layers that bridge liquid water to the ice structure, a mechanism harnessed in cryopreservation to induce controlled extracellular ice formation and minimize cellular damage from intracellular freezing. Such applications highlight the potential of bio-inspired nucleants to improve freeze-thaw processes in biotechnology and food preservation.

Computational Studies

Simulation Models

Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations model nucleation processes at the atomic and molecular scales by integrating the equations of motion for interacting particles, enabling the direct observation of cluster formation and growth in model systems. In studies of homogeneous nucleation rates, MD has been applied to Lennard-Jones (LJ) fluids, where pairwise additive potentials mimic simple liquids, allowing computation of steady-state rates under supercooling or supersaturation conditions. To address the rarity of nucleation events, which occur on timescales far exceeding typical MD trajectories, forward flux sampling (FFS) is integrated with MD; this path-sampling method divides the transition from the metastable fluid to the nucleated phase into sequential interfaces, efficiently accumulating flux across them to yield rate constants.
Monte Carlo (MC) methods complement MD by stochastically sampling equilibrium configurations from the canonical or grand canonical ensembles, facilitating the exploration of phase space without dynamical constraints. The Gibbs ensemble MC technique simulates phase equilibria in closed systems by allowing particle, volume, and identity swaps between coexisting subvolumes, bypassing the need for explicit interfaces and enabling accurate determination of coexistence curves relevant to nucleation precursors. For quantifying the nucleation free energy barrier G(r) as a function of cluster radius r, umbrella sampling restrains simulations to windows along a reaction coordinate (e.g., cluster size), with subsequent reweighting to reconstruct the unbiased potential of mean force; this approach has been pivotal in resolving the size dependence of barriers in vapor-liquid transitions.
Classical density functional theory (DFT) provides a continuum description of nucleation by expressing the grand potential as a functional of the local density profile, which is variationally minimized to obtain equilibrium structures such as critical nuclei and interfacial tensions. In classical DFT applications, weighted density approximations or perturbation theories around a reference hard-sphere fluid yield density profiles across fluid-solid or vapor-liquid interfaces, capturing nonclassical effects like anisotropic growth. Phase-field models, closely related to classical DFT, employ a diffuse order parameter (e.g., density deviation) governed by a Cahn-Hilliard-type equation to simulate the spatiotemporal evolution of nucleating phases, particularly effective for modeling post-nucleation growth and morphological instabilities on mesoscales.
Simplified lattice-based models elucidate fundamental nucleation kinetics in reduced dimensions. The two-dimensional lattice gas model, where sites are occupied or vacant with nearest-neighbor attractions, simulates vapor condensation onto crystalline seeds, revealing scaling laws for critical nucleus shapes and finite-size effects on rates. The Ising model, equivalent to the lattice gas via a particle-hole transformation, models spinodal decomposition near the metastability limit through spin-flip dynamics, demonstrating how fluctuations amplify into domains without a distinct barrier, contrasting with nucleation-dominated regimes. These models are often benchmarked against classical nucleation theory for validation of barrier heights and prefactors.

Key Findings from Simulations

Computational simulations have revealed significant deviations from classical nucleation theory (CNT) in the mechanisms of phase transitions. In Lennard-Jones (LJ) fluids, molecular dynamics studies demonstrate a two-step nucleation process in the metastable regime, where the formation of a critical crystal nucleus is preceded by the assembly of a dense, liquid-like precursor cluster that reduces the free energy barrier. Near the spinodal limit, this process transitions to barrierless pathways, where density fluctuations spontaneously evolve into ordered structures without a distinct activation barrier, as observed in simulations of supercooled LJ liquids.
Heterogeneous nucleation dominates over homogeneous pathways in practical scenarios, with simulations confirming substantial reductions in the catalytic factor $ f(\theta) $, where $ \theta $ is the contact angle. For instance, in LJ systems on catalytic surfaces with low $ \theta $, the nucleation rate can increase by up to $ 10^6 $-fold compared to bulk homogeneous rates, highlighting the role of substrate wettability in lowering the energy barrier and promoting embryo attachment.
In the spinodal region, density functional theory (DFT) simulations uncover oscillatory density profiles around nascent clusters, reflecting competition between attractive and repulsive interactions that stabilize intermediate states. The crossover from nucleation-dominated to spinodal decomposition occurs at much deeper supersaturations than the binodal, where critical cluster sizes diverge and the transition becomes diffusion-limited rather than barrier-controlled, as evidenced in condensing LJ vapors.
Post-2010 advancements include machine learning (ML) potentials that enable long-timescale simulations of protein crystal nucleation, capturing complex two-step mechanisms involving dense liquid precursors in systems like lysozyme, with improved accuracy over classical force fields. Similarly, ab initio molecular dynamics (MD) augmented by ML models of water at around -40C reveal the formation of hexagonal ice embryos during homogeneous nucleation, showing stacked hexagonal rings as stable precursors that evolve into Ih ice without cubic stacking faults. Recent 2024-2025 studies using advanced deep neural networks have further refined these ab initio simulations, achieving higher accuracy in predicting nucleation rates and pathways in supercooled nano-droplets.
