Epimorphism
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 In category theory, an epimorphism is a morphism $ f: A \to B $ in a category C\mathcal{C}C such that for every object $ C $ in C\mathcal{C}C and every pair of morphisms $ g, h: B \to C $, the equality $ g \circ f = h \circ f $ implies $ g = h $. This right-cancellative property generalizes the concept of surjectivity from concrete categories like the category of sets, where epimorphisms coincide exactly with surjective functions, but diverges in more abstract settings.
Epimorphisms play a central role in understanding categorical structure, as they capture a form of "onto" behavior that is preserved under composition: the composite of two epimorphisms is itself an epimorphism, and if $ \alpha \circ \beta $ is an epimorphism, then so is $ \alpha $. In many familiar categories, such as groups or modules, epimorphisms align with surjective homomorphisms, but counterexamples abound in others; for instance, in the category of rings, the inclusion $ \mathbb{Z} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{Q} $ is an epimorphism despite not being surjective on underlying sets. Split epimorphisms, which admit a left inverse (a section $ s: B \to A $ with $ f \circ s = \mathrm{id}_B $), form a subclass that is preserved by any functor, highlighting their equational robustness.
Beyond basic properties, epimorphisms interact deeply with other categorical notions, such as appearing in exact sequences and pullback diagrams in abelian categories, where they ensure certain morphisms remain epimorphic under limits. They are dual to monomorphisms in the opposite category and are reflected by faithful functors, aiding in the study of equivalences and embeddings. However, not all products of epimorphisms are epimorphisms, as shown by counterexamples in categories of abelian groups or sheaves, underscoring the context-dependence of these morphisms in advanced constructions.

Definition

Formal Definition

In category theory, a morphism (also called an arrow) is a structure-preserving map between two objects in a category.
A morphism f:ABf: A \to Bf:AB in a category C\mathcal{C}C is an epimorphism if it is right-cancellative, meaning that for every object DDD in C\mathcal{C}C and every pair of parallel morphisms g,h:BDg, h: B \to Dg,h:BD, the equality gf=hfg \circ f = h \circ fgf=hf implies g=hg = hg=h.

Universal Property

In categories equipped with pullbacks, a morphism $ f: A \to B $ satisfies the universal property characterizing it as an epimorphism if it is the coequalizer of its kernel pair, the pair of parallel arrows induced by $ f $. This means that $ f $ coequalizes the kernel pair and is universal with respect to that property, ensuring right-cancellability in the categorical sense.

Formally, for parallel arrows $ d_0, d_1: E \to A $, the coequalizer coeq(d0,d1)\mathrm{coeq}(d_0, d_1)coeq(d0,d1) is the morphism $ f: A \to B $ if $ f \circ d_0 = f \circ d_1 $ and $ f $ is universal: for any morphism $ g: A \to C $ with $ g \circ d_0 = g \circ d_1 $, there exists a unique $ h: B \to C $ such that $ h \circ f = g $.
This coequalizer characterization holds in many categories, including the category of sets and various algebraic categories, providing a constructive method to verify epimorphisms by explicitly forming the kernel pair and confirming the coequalizer condition. It facilitates proofs of epimorphic status without relying solely on the cancellative definition, particularly in settings where pullbacks and coequalizers are readily available.

In Algebraic Structures

In the category of groups, denoted Grp, epimorphisms coincide with surjective group homomorphisms. This equivalence holds because the forgetful functor from Grp to Set preserves and reflects epimorphisms, and surjections in Set are epimorphisms. A representative example is the canonical projection :ZZ/nZ\pi: \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}:ZZ/nZ, which sends each integer kkk to its residue class modulo nnn; this map is surjective and hence an epimorphism.
In the category of abelian groups, Ab, epimorphisms are likewise the surjective homomorphisms. Since Ab is an abelian category, every epimorphism has zero cokernel, implying surjectivity on underlying abelian groups. This follows from the exactness properties of Ab, where kernels and cokernels are computed set-theoretically.
The situation diverges in the category of rings, Ring (unital rings and unital homomorphisms), where epimorphisms need not be surjective. A canonical counterexample is the inclusion i:ZQi: \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Q}i:ZQ, which embeds the integers into the rationals but misses most elements of Q\mathbb{Q}Q as a set map. Then \phi and \psi agree on Z\mathbb{Z}Z. In the category of rngs, Rng (rings without required identity, with additive and multiplicative homomorphisms), the inclusion ZQ\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Q}ZQ is also an epimorphism. Thus, if two rng-homomorphisms from Q\mathbb{Q}Q agree on Z\mathbb{Z}Z, they agree everywhere.
In categories of modules over a commutative ring RRR, such as R-Mod, epimorphisms are surjective RRR-linear maps, analogous to the abelian group case (where R=ZR = \mathbb{Z}R=Z). This holds because R-Mod is an abelian category, and epimorphisms correspond to surjections via cokernel computations. For instance, over R=kR = kR=k a field, epimorphisms in vector spaces are precisely the surjective linear transformations.
A trivial but illustrative case arises with the zero ring (the terminal object in Ring, with a single element where 0=10 = 10=1): its identity map is an epimorphism, as it is the unique homomorphism to itself and right-cancellative in the trivial sense, though this exemplifies the boundary behavior without providing new divergence from surjectivity.

In Other Categories

In the category of topological spaces, denoted Top, epimorphisms are precisely the surjective continuous maps. This contrasts with settings like Hausdorff spaces, where a weaker condition suffices. For instance, the universal covering map RS1\mathbb{R} \to S^1RS1, given by te2itt \mapsto e^{2\pi i t}te2it, is a surjective continuous map and thus an epimorphism, as it satisfies the right-cancellation property for continuous maps out of the circle.
In the subcategory of Hausdorff topological spaces, denoted Haus, epimorphisms are the continuous maps with dense image. A representative example is the inclusion of the rational numbers into the real numbers, QR\mathbb{Q} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}QR, which is an epimorphism because the image of Q\mathbb{Q}Q is dense in R\mathbb{R}R, ensuring that any two continuous maps from R\mathbb{R}R agreeing on Q\mathbb{Q}Q must coincide everywhere. This highlights a pathology present in Haus but absent in general Top, where non-surjective maps like dense inclusions fail to be epimorphisms.
In the category of monoids, Mon, the inclusion of the additive monoid of natural numbers into the additive group of integers, NZ\mathbb{N} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{Z}NZ, is a non-surjective epimorphism. This follows from the fact that any monoid homomorphism from Z\mathbb{Z}Z that factors through N\mathbb{N}N must respect the generated structure, making the inclusion right-cancellative despite missing negative elements.
In concrete categories more broadly, epimorphisms often correspond to maps whose images are "dense" in an appropriate sense, though the definition varies by structure; for example, in the category of finite sets, FinSet, epimorphisms coincide with surjective functions, mirroring the behavior in Set.
In the category of posets, Pos, with order-preserving maps as morphisms, epimorphisms are the surjective monotone functions, but exceptions arise in related settings like preorders, where the presence of equivalence relations can make non-surjective maps epimorphic if they are dense with respect to the preorder structure.

Properties

Basic Properties

Epimorphisms in a category C\mathcal{C}C are closed under composition. Specifically, if f:ABf: A \to Bf:AB and g:BCg: B \to Cg:BC are epimorphisms, then their composite gf:ACg \circ f: A \to Cgf:AC is also an epimorphism. Advanced Characterizations

In category theory, a regular epimorphism is an epimorphism that is the coequalizer of its kernel pair. This characterization ensures that regular epimorphisms capture a form of "surjectivity" in a categorical sense, particularly in regular categories where every morphism factors through a regular epimorphism followed by a monomorphism. In varieties of universal algebras, such as groups or rings, regular epimorphisms coincide precisely with the surjective homomorphisms, providing a concrete realization of this abstract property.
An extremal epimorphism refines this notion further: it is an epimorphism f:ABf: A \to Bf:AB such that for any factorization f=mef = m \circ ef=me where mmm is a monomorphism, the morphism eee must be an isomorphism. In regular categories, extremal epimorphisms are equivalent to regular epimorphisms. Strong epimorphisms provide an even stronger condition, being epimorphisms left orthogonal to monomorphisms (satisfying a diagonal fill-in property against monic arrows); in categories with pullbacks, extremal and strong epimorphisms coincide. This property highlights epimorphisms that cannot be "refined" non-trivially by inserting a monic factor, emphasizing their role in precise categorical decompositions.
Many categories admit a factorization system where every morphism factors as a regular epimorphism followed by a monomorphism; such categories are called regular, and in balanced categories (where monomorphisms and epimorphisms coincide with their regular counterparts), this factorization applies universally to all morphisms. For instance, in the category of sets, this corresponds to the standard image factorization, where the epimorphic part is surjective onto the image. Split epimorphisms, which are epimorphisms admitting a right inverse (a section), form a special subclass: they are precisely the retractions in categories with pullbacks, and their existence implies the object splits as a product.
In abelian categories, epimorphisms admit a particularly clean characterization as cokernels of morphisms, aligning with their role as quotients by subobjects. Specifically, for an epimorphism f:ABf: A \to Bf:AB, there exists a morphism k:KAk: K \to Ak:KA such that f=coker(k)f = \operatorname{coker}(k)f=coker(k), and this holds equivalently in any abelian category due to the exactness properties. This equivalence underscores the duality between epimorphisms and monomorphisms in such settings, where epis dualize to monics via contravariant functors.

Related Concepts

Monomorphisms

In category theory, a monomorphism, or mono, is a morphism $ g: B \to C $ such that for any morphisms $ h, k: A \to B $ in the category, if $ h \circ g = k \circ g $, then $ h = k $. This property means that $ g $ is left-cancellative: it can be cancelled on the left in equations of morphisms. Monomorphisms are the categorical dual of epimorphisms, obtained by reversing all arrows in the definition, and thus epimorphisms in the opposite category.
In the category of sets Set\mathbf{Set}Set, monomorphisms are precisely the injective functions. More generally, in concrete categoriesthose equipped with a faithful underlying-set functor to Set\mathbf{Set}Setmonomorphisms correspond to morphisms that are injective on underlying sets, ensuring left-cancellative behavior with respect to the forgetful functor. In algebraic categories, such as the category of groups Grp\mathbf{Grp}Grp or rings Ring\mathbf{Ring}Ring, monomorphisms are typically the injective homomorphisms, often called embeddings, which preserve the algebraic structure injectively.
Monomorphisms satisfy several basic properties, including stability under composition: if $ f: A \to B $ and $ g: B \to C $ are monomorphisms, then $ g \circ f: A \to C $ is also a monomorphism. This follows directly from the left-cancellative definition, as composing with an additional mono preserves the cancellation property.

Surjections and Other Morphisms

In the category of sets, a morphism is an epimorphism if and only if it is surjective. This equivalence holds more broadly in many concrete categories, such as the category of groups, where epimorphisms coincide with surjective homomorphisms, and similarly in the category of abelian groups and the category of topological spaces. However, this identification does not hold in general categories; for instance, in the category of rings, the inclusion map ZQ\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Q}ZQ is an epimorphism but not surjective as a function, since any ring homomorphism from Q\mathbb{Q}Q is uniquely determined by its values on Z\mathbb{Z}Z, making the map right-cancellative. In the category of commutative rings, epimorphisms include localizations and quotients but extend beyond mere surjections, with the tensor product condition SRSSS \otimes_R S \cong SSRSS characterizing them.
A bimorphism is a morphism that is both a monomorphism and an epimorphism. In balanced categoriesthose where every bimorphism is an isomorphismsuch morphisms are precisely the isomorphisms. Abelian categories are balanced, so bimorphisms therein are isomorphisms; this follows from the fact that monomorphisms are kernels and epimorphisms are cokernels, and a morphism that is both admits a kernel-cokernel pair implying invertibility. Examples of non-balanced categories include the category of rings, where bimorphisms need not be isomorphisms, such as certain embeddings that fail to split.
Epimorphic images arise as the codomains of epimorphisms and play a key role in factorization systems; in categories admitting (epi, mono)-factorizations, every morphism factors uniquely as an epimorphism followed by a monomorphism, with the codomain of the epimorphism serving as the epimorphic image, generalizing quotient constructions. For endomorphisms, those that are epimorphisms are right-cancellative self-maps; in settings with splitting idempotents, such as when an epic endomorphism admits a retraction, it corresponds to an idempotent e:AAe: A \to Ae:AA with e2=ee^2 = ee2=e via the splitting Aim(e)ker(e)A \cong \operatorname{im}(e) \oplus \ker(e)Aim(e)ker(e), as seen in abelian categories or modules. In the category of posets with order-preserving maps, epimorphisms are exactly the surjective monotone functions, ensuring right-cancellativity in the order structure.

History and Terminology

Historical Origins

The term "epimorphism" was first introduced in the mid-20th century in the context of abstract algebra and category theory. Early uses aligned it with surjective homomorphisms, particularly in modules over rings and group homomorphisms. This usage reflected a structuralist approach to algebra, emphasizing morphisms that preserve essential properties while ensuring onto mappings.
Prior to the full development of category theory, the notion of epimorphism had roots in module theory during the 1930s, where it described surjective (onto) linear maps between modules, reflecting the influence of earlier work in abstract algebra by figures like Emmy Noether and her school. The concept was soon extended to the broader framework of categories by Saunders Mac Lane in the late 1940s and 1950s, following the foundational paper on categories by Mac Lane and Samuel Eilenberg in 1945. Mac Lane's 1954 paper on cohomology groups and subsequent writings integrated epimorphisms as right-cancellative morphisms, distinguishing them from mere set-theoretic surjections and adapting them to abstract categorical settings.
By the 1960s, mathematicians recognized that epimorphisms do not always coincide with surjections in categories like rings, with the inclusion map from the integers to the rationals providing a prominent non-surjective example; this insight arose from studies in commutative algebra and ring theory during that decade. These developments were systematically clarified in the 1971 textbook Category Theory by Horst Herrlich and George E. Strecker, which offered precise characterizations of epimorphisms, including their factorization properties and behavior in concrete versus abstract categories. The 1970s further saw epimorphisms gain prominence through influences from homological algebrawhere they feature in exact sequences and derived functorsand topos theory, as developed by William Lawvere and Myles Tierney, emphasizing regular epimorphisms for descent and sheaf conditions.

Modern Usage and Variations

In modern category theory, the term "epimorphism" predominantly follows the categorical definition as a right-cancellative morphism, a standard established since the 1980s. This consensus is reflected in comprehensive references like Borceux's Handbook of Categorical Algebra, which defines epimorphisms via the cancellation property without restricting to surjectivity. Earlier debates, such as those between Bourbaki's strict usagewhere epimorphisms were limited to surjective homomorphisms in algebraic categoriesand Saunders Mac Lane's advocacy for the broader categorical sense, have largely resolved in favor of the latter. Mac Lane himself explored distinctions like "strict epimorphisms" for those surjective on underlying sets in concrete categories, as proposed in his 1971 text Categories for the Working Mathematician, but these terminological efforts did not gain traction.
Field-specific variations persist, adapting the concept to specialized structures. In topos theory, epimorphisms are precisely the effective descent morphisms, enabling the descent of objects and properties along such maps in a way that mirrors covering spaces in geometry. This equivalence holds because topoi are regular categories with stable reflexive coequalizers, ensuring every epimorphism is regular and thus effective for descent. In higher category theory, including (,1)-categories, the notion extends to weak epimorphisms, which are right-cancellative up to higher homotopy, accommodating the weakened equalities inherent to higher-dimensional structures.
Contemporary applications, particularly in the 2020s, emphasize epimorphisms within applied category theory, such as in modeling information flow. This usage addresses outdated confusions in pre-1980s texts that equated epimorphisms solely with surjections, clarifying their role in query optimization and related areas. Occasionally, outside pure mathematics, the term "epi" is loosely applied in computer science contexts to denote projections in product types, though these are not epimorphisms in the categorical sense unless the category satisfies specific conditions.
