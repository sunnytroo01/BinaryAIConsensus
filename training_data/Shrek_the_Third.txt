 Shrek the Third is a 2007 American computer-animated fantasy comedy film produced by DreamWorks Animation. It serves as the third installment in the Shrek franchise, following Shrek 2 (2004), and features returning characters including the ogre Shrek, his wife Princess Fiona, the talkative Donkey, and Puss in Boots. Directed by Chris Miller and co-directed by Raman Hui, the film was released in the United States on May 18, 2007, by Paramount Pictures. The story centers on Shrek, who, upon the death of his father-in-law King Harold, is thrust into the role of heir to the throne of Far Far Away but seeks out the young Arthur "Artie" Pendragon to assume the crown instead, while Fiona and other princesses defend the kingdom from an invasion led by Prince Charming.
The voice cast includes Mike Myers as Shrek, Eddie Murphy as Donkey, Cameron Diaz as Princess Fiona, Antonio Banderas as Puss in Boots, Julie Andrews as Queen Lillian, John Cleese as King Harold, Rupert Everett as Prince Charming, and Justin Timberlake as Artie, with additional voices by Eric Idle as Merlin and Larry King as Doris. Produced on a budget of $160 million, Shrek the Third achieved significant commercial success, grossing $322.7 million in North America and $808.3 million worldwide, making it the fourth highest-grossing film worldwide that year, the second highest-grossing film in North America, the highest-grossing animated film released in 2007, and the third highest-grossing animated film at the time. It opened at number one at the North American box office with $121.6 million in its first weekend, setting a record for the largest opening for an animated film until surpassed by later releases.
Critically, the film received mixed reviews, with a 41% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 210 reviews, where critics noted its humor and animation but criticized the plot as disjointed and less innovative than its predecessors. Roger Ebert awarded it 2.5 out of 4 stars, praising some sequences but lamenting a lack of the original's energy. Despite the reception, it won awards for its animation and music, such as at the Kids' Choice Awards and Visual Effects Society Awards, and contributed to the franchise's enduring popularity.

Plot and Characters

Plot

The film opens with Shrek and Fiona attending the funeral of King Harold, who has died from illness, leaving them as the next in line to rule Far Far Away. Reluctant to abandon his swamp life, Shrek learns from Harold's final wordsdelivered via a magical messagethat Arthur "Artie" Pendragon, Fiona's teenage cousin, is the rightful heir. Accompanied by his friends Donkey and Puss in Boots, Shrek sets out on a quest to Worcestershire Academy, a school for wayward youth, where he finds Artie being bullied and uninterested in kingship. During the journey, Fiona announces her pregnancy, sparking Shrek's anxiety about fatherhood and his fears of failing as a parent, themes that underscore his internal conflict with destiny and responsibility.
Meanwhile, in a parallel subplot, the disgraced Prince Charming, seeking revenge after his mother's death in the previous film, rallies an army of fairy tale villainsincluding Captain Hook, Rumpelstiltskin, the Witch, and othersat the Poison Apple tavern to stage a coup in Far Far Away. Charming's forces invade the kingdom, capturing Fiona and a group of fairy tale princesses: Cinderella, Snow White, Rapunzel, and Sleeping Beauty. Betrayed by the treacherous Rapunzel, who is infatuated with Charming, the princesses initially find themselves imprisoned but soon subvert traditional damsel-in-distress tropes by organizing their own defense, with Fiona emerging as a leader who embraces her pregnancy as a source of strength.
Shrek's group encounters the wizard Merlin on his secluded island, who agrees to transport them back to Far Far Away but accidentally swaps Donkey and Puss's bodies in the process due to a miscast spell, adding comic chaos to their return. Upon arriving, they learn of the takeover and infiltrate Charming's climactic performance at a theater, where he plans to publicly execute Shrek. Artie, inspired by Shrek's encouragement to forge his own path, steps up to confront the villains, delivering a speech about overlooked potential that sways them to his side and highlights themes of self-determination. In the ensuing battle, Shrek defeats Charming, who is crushed by a falling stage prop, while Dragon arrives to aid the fight. With the kingdom saved, Artie is crowned king, allowing Shrek to return home. Merlin reverses the body swap, and Shrek, having overcome his reluctance, welcomes the birth of their ogre triplets with Fiona.

Production

Development

The development of Shrek the Third stemmed from DreamWorks Animation's commitment to expand the franchise into a five-film series, a plan outlined by CEO Jeffrey Katzenberg following the success of the initial entries and publicly revealed in May 2004.
Originally titled Shrek 3, the project underwent a title change to Shrek the Third in March 2006, intended to underscore the film's emphasis on family dynamics and responsibilities. The directorial team shifted as well, with Chris Miller taking the helm as directorhis feature debut after contributing as a story artist on prior filmsand Raman Hui joining as co-director; John H. Williams, who had produced the first two installments, moved to an executive producer role.
Script development centered on Shrek's reluctance toward kingship and fatherhood, building on the character's established aversion to societal expectations, with the screenplay credited to Jeffrey Price and Peter S. Seaman, alongside contributions from Miller and producer Aron Warner. Hired in 2004 specifically for the project, Price and Seaman crafted the narrative to explore these themes while incorporating returning characters from the previous films.
The film's release was postponed from its initial November 2006 target to May 18, 2007, a decision by DreamWorks Animation to sidestep holiday season competition and permit further refinement of the production. With a total budget of $160 million, resources were directed toward broadening the fairy tale ensemble, introducing new antagonists and allies to enrich the story's scope.

Animation and Design

The animation for Shrek the Third utilized DreamWorks Animation's proprietary tools, including advanced simulation software for rendering fur and hair to achieve more realistic motion and interactions, particularly enhancing the detailed fur on characters like Donkey and Puss in Boots. This included a physics-based strand simulator developed specifically for the film to model hair dynamics, allowing for natural collisions and movements in complex scenes. The body swap sequence between Donkey and Puss required innovative rigging and animation approaches, where animators transferred the characters' distinct personalities and movements across mismatched bodies to heighten the comedic effect, involving paired animators for intricate scene handling.
Character designs were updated to reflect higher resolution models, with legacy figures like Shrek and Fiona receiving refined facial controls and details such as nose wrinkles for expressive subtlety. Artie was conceptualized as a contemporary, bullied teenager attending Worcestershire Academy, a setting parodying American high schools with elements like yearbooks and mascots, evoking a northern European autumn aesthetic to underscore his outsider status. The villains' lair was reimagined as a dilapidated theater inspired by medieval machinery, serving as a stage for Prince Charming's coup with elaborate, rundown props. The princess characters received expanded designs emphasizing empowerment, featuring individualized hairstyles, outfits, and group dynamics that highlighted their agency in action sequences.
Voice recording took place in 2006 at DreamWorks studios, where actors including Mike Myers, Eddie Murphy, and Antonio Banderas participated in collaborative sessions that encouraged improvisation to capture authentic comedic timing. For instance, Eddie Murphy ad-libbed lines as Donkey to infuse natural humor, contributing to the film's improvisational energy alongside input from other cast members.
The filmmaking process began with storyboarding completed by 2005, incorporating actor feedback to refine comic elements and character arcs. Test screenings prompted adjustments to Artie's development, strengthening his transformation from reluctant teen to confident leader. The final runtime was set at 93 minutes to balance pacing and humor.
In post-production, editing was led by Michael Andrews and Joyce Arrastia, focusing on tightening comedic beats and narrative flow. Visual effects, supervised by Philippe Gluckman, included custom medieval-style magic simulations for Merlin's spells and dynamic crowd animations for the theater battle, populating the scene with up to 2,500 unique background characters from a library of nearly 5,000 models.

Music

The original score for Shrek the Third was composed by Harry Gregson-Williams, marking his return to the franchise after contributing to the music for the first two films. His work consists of over 20 cues that blend orchestral fairy tale whimsy with modern electronic and rock-infused twists, emphasizing themes of adventure and emotional introspection. Notable examples include "The Royal Treatment," which accompanies regal sequences in Far Far Away with sweeping strings and brass, and "Fatherly Dreams," a poignant cue highlighting Shrek's reluctance toward impending fatherhood through melancholic woodwinds and piano. The score enhances comedic elements, such as the body swap sequence, by layering whimsical motifs with chaotic percussion to underscore the characters' disorientation.
The official score album, Shrek the Third: Original Motion Picture Score, was released on June 19, 2007, by Varse Sarabande Records and features 18 selected tracks spanning approximately 40 minutes. It opens with "The Royal Treatment" and progresses through cues like "Another Adventure" for quest-driven action and "Ogre Resistance" for climactic confrontations, maintaining continuity with Gregson-Williams's established "Shrek's Theme" from prior installments.
In parallel, the licensed songs were compiled on the Shrek the Third: Motion Picture Soundtrack album, released on May 15, 2007, by Geffen Records, comprising 12 tracks without any original performances by the voice cast. Standout inclusions are Eels' "Royal Pain" as the opening villain theme, Fergie's rock cover of Heart's "Barracuda," Led Zeppelin's "Immigrant Song," and the Ramones' "Do You Remember Rock 'N' Roll Radio?," selected to amplify the film's irreverent humor and energy.
These songs integrate seamlessly into key scenes to heighten action and comedy, such as Wings' "Live and Let Die" during Prince Charming's villain rally for dramatic flair and the traditional "Happy Birthday" in a humorous poison gag. The soundtrack album achieved modest commercial success, peaking at number 34 on the US Billboard 200 and number 2 on the Soundtrack Albums chart.

Release and Distribution

Theatrical Release

Shrek the Third had its world premiere at the Mann Village Theatre in Westwood, Los Angeles, on May 6, 2007. The film received a wide theatrical release in the United States on May 18, 2007, distributed by Paramount Pictures. This followed a delay from its originally planned November 2006 release date, which was shifted to better align with the summer movie season.
The film opened in over 4,000 theaters across North America, marking one of the widest domestic releases for an animated feature at the time. Internationally, the rollout was staggered, beginning in select markets in late May 2007, such as Russia on May 17 and several European countries including Austria, Italy, and Belgium in late May and early June. Further releases followed in other European territories like France on June 20, Germany and Spain on June 21 and 22, and the United Kingdom on June 29, while Asian markets saw openings in June and July, including Australia on June 7 and Japan on June 30. To accommodate global audiences, the film was dubbed into more than 20 languages, including Spanish, French, German, Italian, and Portuguese, among others.
Paramount Pictures handled the initial worldwide theatrical distribution in 2007 under a multi-year agreement with DreamWorks Animation. In 2012, DreamWorks signed a five-year distribution deal with 20th Century Fox, effective for films starting in 2013, which included rights to the existing Shrek catalog for subsequent releases and home media. Following Comcast's 2016 acquisition of DreamWorks Animation, distribution rights for the franchise transferred to Universal Pictures in 2019, encompassing ongoing management of the library.
The film's runtime is 93 minutes, and it received a PG rating from the Motion Picture Association of America for some crude humor, suggestive content, and swashbuckling action. Advance screenings were organized in approximately 1,000 theaters starting late on Thursday, May 17, 2007, providing early access ahead of the official Friday opening. While 3D conversions were later applied to the Shrek series for home media releases beginning in 2010, no theatrical 3D re-release was pursued for Shrek the Third at the time.

Marketing and Promotion

The marketing campaign for Shrek the Third was one of DreamWorks Animation's most extensive efforts, leveraging widespread commercial partnerships to promote the film's themes of family and adventure ahead of its May 18, 2007, theatrical release. The strategy emphasized cross-promotions with major brands, resulting in toys, apparel, books, and digital content that saturated media channels and retail spaces. TV spots highlighted key characters like Shrek, Donkey, and Puss in Boots embarking on their quest, while online microsites and games encouraged fan engagement.
A cornerstone of the campaign was McDonald's global promotion, described as the chain's largest movie tie-in to date, which ran through June 7, 2007, or until supplies lasted. It featured 10 talking Shrek toy premiums in Happy Meals, alongside collector's glasses priced at $1.99 with purchase and Shrek-themed Arch Cards up to $50 in value; to align with healthier eating initiatives, the promotion included apple dippers and milk options in kids' meals, supported by custom DreamWorks animations in commercials. Other food partnerships amplified reach, including PepsiCo's Sierra Mist campaign with ads showing Shrek and Donkey in "Rock, Paper, Scissors" contests for free drinks, and Burger King's kids' meals bundled with Shrek the Third toys. Mars contributed ogre-sized M&M's packages, Shrek-flavored Skittles, and Snickers bars with in-pack tattoos, tied to an online "Fairy Tale Ransom" game; Kellogg's offered themed packaging for cereals like Frosted Flakes and Pop-Tarts, plus mail-in premiums such as Shrek bowls and soccer balls; General Mills promoted via Yoplait Go-Gurt tubes featuring character designs; and Frito-Lay marketed Baked Cheetos that turned tongues green.
Merchandise extended beyond food tie-ins, with Activision developing and publishing the Shrek the Third video game, released on May 15, 2007three days before the filmfor platforms including PlayStation 2, Wii, Xbox 360, PlayStation Portable, Nintendo DS, Game Boy Advance, and PC; the action-adventure title allowed players to control Shrek and allies in missions mirroring the movie's plot. Scholastic supported literacy efforts with book adaptations, including the Level 3 Popcorn ELT Readera simplified storybook for young learnersand the junior novelization retelling the film's events. Additional partners like Energizer ran sweepstakes and games on a dedicated microsite, while HP hosted online photo contests.
Global promotional activities included localized trailers and high-profile premieres attended by cast members, such as voice actor Justin Timberlake (Arthur) at events in Los Angeles on April 28, 2007, Madrid on June 13, 2007, and Berlin on June 8, 2007, where he engaged fans and media to spotlight the film's royal heir quest. The campaign's scale drew attention, including unofficial satirical responses from Adult Swim's Tim and Eric duo, who aired parody ads in April 2007 mocking the promotional saturation through absurd, over-the-top spoofs.

Home Media

Shrek the Third was first released on DVD and HD DVD on November 13, 2007, by Paramount Home Entertainment as a two-disc edition. The set included bonus features such as audio commentary by director Chris Miller and co-director Raman Hui, deleted scenes, a featurette titled "Secrets of Shrek the Third," and an interactive yearbook on Worcestershire Academy. In its debut week, the DVD sold approximately 4.4 million units in the United States, generating $66 million in revenue. By early 2021, cumulative domestic DVD sales reached 12.1 million units, contributing to total home video revenue of $179 million.
The film received its initial Blu-ray release on September 23, 2008, also distributed by Paramount, featuring high-definition audio and video enhancements along with the DVD extras. Subsequent Blu-ray re-releases occurred in 2011 and 2018 by Universal Pictures Home Entertainment following the 2016 acquisition of DreamWorks Animation by NBCUniversal, often bundled in franchise collections like the "Shrek Ultimate Edition." Blu-ray sales totaled around 68,850 units domestically.
In 2023, Shrek the Third was remastered and released on 4K Ultra HD Blu-ray on September 12 by Universal Pictures Home Entertainment, supporting Dolby Vision for HDR and Dolby Atmos for audio. This edition included the Blu-ray disc and digital code, emphasizing improved color grading and detail in the animation.
Digital downloads became available in the 2010s through platforms like iTunes and Amazon Video, with a notable re-release on February 14, 2020. As of November 2025, the film streams on Peacock Premium, part of NBCUniversal's catalog, having left Netflix on November 1, 2025, with prior rotating availability on services like Netflix. The title has been included in special franchise box sets, such as the "Shrek Ultimate Collection" in the 2010s, enhancing accessibility for collectors.

Reception

Box Office

Shrek the Third premiered in theaters on May 18, 2007, during the Memorial Day weekend, opening to $121.6 million in the United States and Canada across 4,122 theaters, setting a record for the largest opening weekend for an animated film at the time. This figure marked the biggest debut for any film during the Memorial Day weekend for animated features, surpassing the previous record held by Finding Nemo in 2003, and it remained unbroken until Finding Dory in 2016. The film ultimately grossed $322.7 million domestically, accounting for 39.9% of its worldwide total.
Internationally, Shrek the Third earned $485.6 million from over 90 markets, with particularly strong performance in Europe, where it grossed more than $150 million, led by the United Kingdom ($78.8 million), France ($45.9 million), and Germany ($30.8 million). In Asia Pacific, the film collected over $100 million, including $28.7 million in Australia and $23.6 million in Russia and the Commonwealth of Independent States, while top Latin American markets contributed additional earnings, such as $24.8 million in Mexico and $19.7 million in Brazil.
The film's global box office total reached $808.3 million against a production budget of $160 million, delivering a profitable return of approximately five times its cost. This made it the fourth-highest-grossing film of 2007 worldwide, trailing Pirates of the Caribbean: At World's End ($961 million), Harry Potter and the Order of the Phoenix ($942 million), and Spider-Man 3 ($890 million). Compared to its predecessor Shrek 2, which earned $928 million globally, Shrek the Third underperformed but still exceeded financial expectations despite receiving mixed critical reception.

Critical Response

Shrek the Third received mixed reviews upon its release, with aggregate scores reflecting a general sense of disappointment compared to its predecessors. On Rotten Tomatoes, it holds a 41% approval rating based on 210 critic reviews, with an average rating of 6.1/10. The site's consensus states that the film "has pop culture potshots galore, but at the expense of the heart, charm, and wit that made the first two Shreks classics." Similarly, Metacritic assigns it a score of 58 out of 100, categorized as "mixed or average," derived from 35 critics.
Critics praised certain elements for their appeal to younger audiences, including the film's kid-friendly humor and visual gags, which provided lighthearted entertainment despite overall flaws. Voice performances were frequently highlighted as a strength, particularly Eddie Murphy's energetic portrayal of Donkey and Antonio Banderas's charismatic take on Puss in Boots, which added levity through improvisation and timing. Roger Ebert awarded it 2.5 out of 4 stars, noting its visual enchantment and family-oriented charm, though he lamented the reduced comic energy.
However, the film faced substantial criticism for its formulaic plot and perceived inferiority to earlier entries, with reviewers arguing it relied too heavily on franchise familiarity without innovation. New characters, such as Justin Timberlake's Arthur and the ensemble of princesses, were often described as underdeveloped and underutilized, contributing to a sense of narrative bloat. Variety's Todd McCarthy offered a mixed review, describing the film as amusing but not matching the energy of prior installments.
Thematically, the film's subversion of fairy tale tropessuch as reluctant kingship and gender roles among the princessesearned some acclaim for continuing the series' irreverent style, but was widely seen as repetitive and less impactful than before. Shrek's arc exploring impending fatherhood and paternal responsibility was noted in reviews as adding emotional layers, though some found it disconnected from the humor. In retrospective analyses from the 2010s, the film has been reevaluated by some as "underrated fun" amid broader franchise fatigue, appreciating its chaotic energy and select gags, and as of 2025, no significant shifts in critical consensus have emerged.

Awards and Nominations

At the 61st British Academy Film Awards in 2008, Shrek the Third was nominated for Best Animated Film but lost to Ratatouille.
The film earned several nominations at the 35th Annie Awards in 2008, including Best Animated Feature for directors Chris Miller and Raman Hui, Directing in an Animated Feature Production for Miller and Raman Hui, Character Animation in a Feature Production for Guillaume Aretos (for the Donkey and Puss in Boots body swap sequence), and Storyboarding in a Feature Production for Gary Trousdale, though it did not win in any category.
Shrek the Third received a nomination for Best Animated Feature at the 13th Critics' Choice Awards in 2008, where it was defeated by Ratatouille.
Unlike its predecessors, Shrek the Third received no nominations at the 65th Golden Globe Awards in 2008.
At the 2008 Nickelodeon Kids' Choice Awards, Eddie Murphy won Favorite Voice from an Animated Movie for his performance as Donkey, while the film itself was nominated for Favorite Animated Movie but lost to Ratatouille.
Shrek the Third won the People's Choice Award for Favorite Family Movie at the 34th People's Choice Awards in 2008.
The film won the Golden Reel Award at the 2008 Motion Picture Sound Editors Awards for Best Sound Editing - Sound Effects and Foley in a Feature Film (Animated).
The film garnered two nominations at the 6th Visual Effects Society Awards in 2008: Outstanding Effects in an Animated Motion Picture for Matt Baer, Greg Hart, Krzysztof Rost, and Anthony Field, and Outstanding Performance of a Character Animation in an Animated Motion Picture for John Cleese as King Harold (with Guillaume Aretos, Tim Cheung, and Sean Mahoney), but did not win either.

Legacy

Controversies

One notable controversy surrounding Shrek the Third involved voice actor Eric Idle, who provided the voice for Merlin and is a member of Monty Python. Idle threatened legal action against DreamWorks Animation after discovering that the film included a gag directly referencing the coconut horse-riding bit from Monty Python and the Holy Grail, where characters mimic horse sounds using coconut shells due to a lack of real horses in the budget. In the scene, Prince Charming and his henchmen replicate the joke almost verbatim while approaching the ogre's swamp. Idle, who attended the film's premiere on May 17, 2007, reportedly stormed out upon seeing the sequence, later stating in an interview that the studio had "patently stolen" the joke without permission or credit, dismissing any notion of it being an homage.
The dispute highlighted tensions over intellectual property in comedic references, especially given Idle's involvement in the production. No lawsuit was ultimately filed, and Idle later re-entered the premiere after calming down, though the outcome of any private negotiations remains undisclosed.
The film's marketing campaign also faced criticism for its perceived over-saturation, with aggressive advertisingsuch as ubiquitous trailers, tie-in merchandise, and slogans like "The Wait Is Ogre"bombarding audiences in the months leading to its May 2007 release. This led to public annoyance, exemplified by satirical parodies created by comedians Tim Heidecker and Eric Wareheim, who produced mock promotional videos exaggerating the relentless hype to underscore its excessiveness for a family-oriented film. While no formal complaints or regulatory actions resulted, media coverage noted the campaign's intensity as potentially alienating viewers.
Beyond these issues, Shrek the Third sparked minor debates regarding the maturity of its humor, particularly innuendos in the body-swap sequence where Donkey and Puss in Boots exchange forms after an encounter with Merlin. The scene includes jokes alluding to anatomical differences and discomfort, such as references to private parts, which some content reviewers flagged as edging toward adult themes unsuitable for younger audiences despite the film's PG rating. These elements did not escalate into broader public outcry or censorship demands but contributed to discussions on the franchise's balance of kid-friendly and mature content.

Sequels

The immediate sequel to Shrek the Third is Shrek Forever After, released on May 21, 2010, and directed by Mike Mitchell in his feature animation debut. The film continues the narrative shortly after the birth of Shrek and Fiona's triplets at the end of Shrek the Third, with Rumpelstiltskinfirst introduced in the prior installment as a minor antagonist aiding Prince Charmingemerging as the primary villain. It grossed $752.6 million worldwide.
The franchise expanded with the spin-off Puss in Boots in 2011, which builds on the character's development across the main series, including his role as a loyal companion in Shrek the Third. Released on October 28, 2011, and directed by Chris Miller, the film explores Puss's backstory while maintaining ties to the broader Shrek universe. This was followed by Puss in Boots: The Last Wish, a sequel to the 2011 film, released on December 21, 2022, and directed by Joel Crawford and Januel Mercado. The movie received widespread critical acclaim for its animation, storytelling, and emotional depth, grossing $485.3 million worldwide and revitalizing interest in the Shrek franchise.
In July 2024, DreamWorks Animation announced Shrek 5, with original voice actors Mike Myers, Eddie Murphy, and Cameron Diaz returning to their roles. In February 2025, Zendaya was cast to voice one of Shrek and Fiona's children, and a teaser trailer was released. Initially slated for July 1, 2026, the release was delayed to December 23, 2026, and then further to June 30, 2027, as of August 2025. Plot details remain under wraps, but it is expected to advance the family-oriented storyline established after Shrek the Third.
Shrek the Third establishes key elements carried forward in the sequels, such as Arthur Pendragon's ascension to the throne of Far Far Away and the arrival of Shrek's children, both of which are referenced in Shrek Forever After. There are no direct prequels to Shrek the Third within the core narrative, though Puss in Boots serves as a prequel to Puss's involvement in the main films.
The overall franchise arc fulfills an early vision outlined by DreamWorks co-founder Jeffrey Katzenberg, who in 2004 revealed that the Shrek storyline had been planned for five films from near its inception following the 2001 original. Distribution of the series transitioned to Universal Pictures after its 2016 acquisition of DreamWorks Animation.
