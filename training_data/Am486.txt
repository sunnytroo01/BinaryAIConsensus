Am486
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Am486 is a family of 32-bit x86 microprocessors developed by Advanced Micro Devices (AMD) as compatible second-source implementations of Intel's 80486 architecture, introduced in April 1993 to provide cost-effective alternatives for personal computers.
These processors marked AMD's final major cloning effort under its second-source agreement with Intel, amid ongoing legal disputes with Intel over microcode access starting in 1993, which led AMD to develop its own microcode for the Am486 and later variants. The Am486 lineup included models such as the Am486DX (2540 MHz), Am486DX2 (5080 MHz with 2x clock multiplier), and Am486SX (system-integrated variants without an on-chip floating-point unit), all featuring an integrated 8 KB unified L1 cache, pipelined integer unit, and on-chip memory management for virtual addressing up to 4 GB physical and 64 TB virtual memory. The Am486DX4, introduced in 1994 (75120 MHz with 3x multiplier), and enhanced versions released from 1995 onward, such as the Am5x86 (133 MHz with ~3.3x multiplier and 16 KB L1 cache), incorporated write-back caching, burst bus transfers up to 128 MB/s, and power-saving modes compliant with the Energy Star program, while maintaining pin-compatible operation with 486 motherboards.
Historically, the Am486 processors undercut Intel's pricingoften by 2030%while offering higher clock speeds in some models, such as the DX2-66 competing with early Pentiums, thereby sustaining AMD's market presence in the 486 era until the shift to fifth-generation architectures like AMD's K5 in 1996. Fabricated on a 0.81.0 micron CMOS process initially and later 0.5 micron for enhanced models, they supported binary compatibility with MS-DOS, Windows, OS/2, and Unix, executing frequent instructions in a single clock cycle and using the MESI cache coherency protocol. Available in 168-pin PGA or 208-pin SQFP packages with 3.3V core and 5V I/O tolerance, the family powered budget systems and upgrades, with power consumption ranging from 660 mA at 66 MHz to 1.2 A at 120 MHz under typical loads.

History and Development

Announcement and Launch

In February 1992, AMD announced the Am486 family of microprocessors as a fully compatible alternative to Intel's 80486, following a favorable arbitration ruling that secured AMD's rights to extend its x86 cloning efforts beyond the 386 generation.
Production of the Am486 began in 1992 at AMD's Submicron Development Center in Sunnyvale, California, utilizing a 0.7 m CMOS process initially limited to internal fabrication.
The official launch took place in April 1993, introducing the initial models such as the Am486DX-25 and Am486DX-33, which operated at 25 MHz and 33 MHz, respectively, and maintained pin-for-pin compatibility with Intel's 80486.
Positioned as a cost-effective alternative to Intel's offerings, the Am486 processors featured simplified pricing structures, with the 33 MHz model available at $306 per unit in quantities of 1,000, matching Intel's rates while enabling broader system integration.
In its debut year, the Am486 line generated $250 million in revenue for AMD and captured a 20% share of the 486-compatible CPU market.

Legal and Market Challenges

AMD faced substantial legal obstacles during the rollout of the Am486, primarily stemming from a protracted dispute with Intel over intellectual property rights related to x86 designs. In April 1993, Intel filed a lawsuit in federal court accusing AMD of copyright infringement on microcode used in the Am486 processor, a reverse-engineered clone of the Intel 80486. This action built on earlier microcode litigation dating back to 1991 and sought to halt AMD's production and sales, effectively delaying widespread shipments of the Am486 until legal resolution.
A pivotal breakthrough occurred in March 1994 when a federal jury in San Jose ruled in AMD's favor, clearing the company of infringing Intel's copyrights and allowing immediate production and distribution of the Am486 without further injunctions. The broader conflict culminated in a comprehensive settlement in January 1995, under which AMD paid Intel $58 million for past damages related to the 486 microcode. In exchange, AMD secured a perpetual, royalty-free license to Intel's 386 and 486 microcode, enabling continued production of compatible chips; however, the agreement imposed restrictions by explicitly excluding licenses for future architectures, such as Intel's forthcoming Pentium series. This limited AMD's ability to evolve beyond 486-era designs without additional negotiations or independent development.
These legal battles occurred against a backdrop of intense market competition, where Intel's original 80486 microprocessorlaunched in April 1989had established overwhelming dominance in the x86 ecosystem, capturing the vast majority of PC processor demand through superior branding and ecosystem integration. AMD countered by marketing the Am486 as a high-value alternative, emphasizing its compatibility and pricing strategy to appeal to cost-sensitive original equipment manufacturers (OEMs) and system integrators. For instance, AMD priced its 40 MHz Am486DX at levels comparable to or below Intel's 33 MHz 80486DX (around $306 versus $329 in volume for 1,000 units in 1993), delivering roughly 20% better performance at similar or lower costs, which translated to effective price advantages of 20-30% when factoring in speed-normalized value. This approach helped AMD penetrate the market despite the four-year head start Intel enjoyed.
Despite the litigation-induced delays, the Am486 played a crucial role in bolstering AMD's position in the x86 market, driving revenue growth and establishing a lasting foothold. By the mid-1990s, AMD's x86-compatible processors, led by the Am486 lineup, contributed to the company's ascent as the second-largest player in the microprocessor segment, with market share estimates reaching up to 25% in certain quarters amid heightened price competition. This success underscored the Am486's importance in diversifying the PC supply chain and challenging Intel's monopoly-like control.

Manufacturing Evolution

The Am486 family began production using a 0.7 m CMOS process technology at AMD's Submicron Development Center in Sunnyvale, California, starting with the initial DX models in 1993. This fabrication method, featuring three-layer metal interconnects, supported early clock speeds up to 50 MHz while maintaining compatibility with existing 80486 systems.
By late 1994, AMD transitioned the DX2 series to a 0.5 m CMOS process, known internally as CS-24, which improved manufacturing yields and power efficiency for clock-doubled variants operating at frequencies like 66 MHz and 80 MHz. This shrink reduced die size and thermal output, allowing for more reliable production without altering the core microarchitecture. The DX4 models, introduced in 1994, initially retained the 0.5 m process for 3.3 V operation, consuming approximately 2.6 W at 100 MHz.
Further evolution occurred with the Enhanced Am486 variants from 1995 to 1997, adopting a 0.35 m CMOS process (CS-34) that enabled tighter transistor densities and supported higher internal clock multipliers up to 5x for embedded and desktop applications. These advancements in process scaling facilitated incremental performance gains, such as reduced power draw to around 3 W at 120 MHz, by minimizing voltage requirements and enhancing on-chip integration without requiring full redesigns.
Packaging options evolved alongside fabrication, with the standard 168-pin Ceramic Pin Grid Array (CPGA) suited for desktop Socket 2 and Socket 3 motherboards, while the 208-pin Shrink Quad Flat Pack (SQFP) targeted embedded systems for better thermal management in compact designs. Voltage compatibility shifted from 5 V core operation in early 0.7 m models to 3.3 V (with 5 V-tolerant I/O) in 0.5 m and later processes starting around 1994, aligning with industry trends toward lower-power components and simplifying motherboard integration.
Production of the Am486 line concluded around 1997 as AMD redirected resources to its in-house K5 (launched 1996) and K6 (launched 1997) architectures, marking the end of 486-compatible manufacturing in favor of fifth-generation x86 designs. These process evolutions collectively allowed the Am486 to achieve clock speeds up to 133 MHz and improved efficiency, sustaining competitiveness in budget and embedded markets through the mid-1990s.

Architecture and Design

Core Microarchitecture

The Am486 processor implements a 32-bit x86 architecture, featuring a five-stage pipeline consisting of instruction fetch, decode, execute, memory access, and writeback stages, closely mirroring the design of Intel's i486 while incorporating AMD-specific optimizations in the integer execution unit to reduce average instruction cycle times through RISC-inspired techniques and enhanced addressing modes. This pipelined structure enables overlapping execution of instructions, improving throughput for integer operations common in general-purpose computing tasks. The core's design emphasizes efficient handling of the IA-32 instruction set, with the fetch stage supported by a 32-byte instruction queue capable of prefetching up to approximately 10 instructions.
Key integrated units within the Am486 core include an on-chip memory management unit (MMU) that supports both segmentation and paging mechanisms, featuring a 32-entry, four-way set-associative translation lookaside buffer (TLB) with pseudo-LRU replacement for virtual-to-physical address translation and two-level paging with 4-Kbyte page sizes. The bus interface unit (BIU) manages external interactions via a 32-bit data bus, supporting dynamic bus sizing for 8-, 16-, and 32-bit peripherals and addressing up to 4 GiB of physical memory space. In DX-series models, a fully integrated floating-point unit (FPU) handles IEEE 754-compliant operations, whereas SX variants omit the FPU to reduce cost and die area, requiring external coprocessors for floating-point tasks.
Initial Am486 implementations on a 0.7 m CMOS process featured approximately 1.2 million transistors and a die size of around 89 mm for DX models, with subsequent shrinks to 0.5 m and smaller nodes reducing the die area by up to 37% while maintaining architectural compatibility. These specifications reflect AMD's focus on high-volume production efficiency, enabling the Am486 to deliver performance competitive with contemporary Intel offerings at lower manufacturing costs.

Instruction Set Compatibility

The Am486 microprocessor family provides full adherence to the IA-32 instruction set architecture (ISA) as implemented in the Intel 80486, encompassing all standard 486 instructions, protected mode operations, virtual memory management via paging and segmentation, and support for real, virtual 8086, and system management modes. This compatibility excludes post-486 extensions such as conditional move (CMOV) instructions, which were introduced in later Intel architectures like the Pentium.
As a result, the Am486 achieves complete binary compatibility with software compiled for the Intel 80486, functioning as a direct drop-in replacement in Socket 1, Socket 2, and Socket 3 motherboards without necessitating recompilation or modifications for 486-era applications. This design choice enabled seamless interoperability with existing x86 ecosystems, allowing operating systems like DOS and Windows 3.x to run identically on Am486 systems as on their Intel counterparts.
AMD incorporated minor architectural tweaks in the Am486, such as enhancements to error handling and system management mode (SMM) for power-saving features, but deliberately avoided proprietary instructions to uphold broad ecosystem compatibility and prevent fragmentation in the x86 software base.
A key limitation of the Am486 lies in its omission of certain Intel-specific debugging capabilities, notably the in-circuit emulation (ICE) microcode, which was removed from production models following a 1994 legal settlement with Intel to resolve copyright disputes over microcode usage. This adjustment ensured legal independence while maintaining core functionality, though it impacted advanced hardware debugging tools reliant on ICE.
To guarantee this level of compatibility, AMD employed rigorous validation processes, including reverse-engineering of Intel 80486 designs through techniques like chip decapping and microscopic analysis, coupled with extensive testing against Intel binaries to confirm 100% software equivalence.

Key Features

Integrated Components and Cache

The Am486 microprocessor incorporates an on-chip Level 1 (L1) cache to enhance performance by reducing access times to frequently used instructions and data. In early models such as the Am486 DX and DX2, the L1 cache is a unified 8 KB structure that serves both instructions and data, organized as a 4-way set-associative array with a write-through policy. Later variants in the Enhanced Am486 family, including certain DX4 models, upgrade this to a 16 KB unified L1 cache, maintaining the 4-way set-associativity while introducing support for more advanced configurations.
Cache policies in the Am486 evolve across models to optimize throughput and memory consistency. Base implementations employ a write-through policy, where all writes update both the cache and external memory simultaneously to ensure data coherency in multi-processor or bus-snooping environments. Enhanced versions incorporate a write-back policy, configurable via the WB/WT pin or software, which delays writes to main memory until a cache line is replaced or explicitly flushed, thereby reducing bus traffic and improving performance for write-intensive workloads; this uses a modified MESI (Modified, Exclusive, Shared, Invalid) protocol for cache line coherency.
The Memory Management Unit (MMU) in the Am486 facilitates virtual memory addressing through segmentation and paging mechanisms. It supports standard 4 KB page sizes for efficient memory allocation and protection, with translations handled via page directories and tables. The integrated Translation Lookaside Buffer (TLB) consists of 32 entries in a 4-way set-associative configuration, caching virtual-to-physical address mappings to accelerate translations and minimize page table accesses; it employs a pseudo-LRU replacement algorithm for entry management.
In DX-series models, the Am486 integrates a fully compatible Floating-Point Unit (FPU) on-chip, eliminating the need for a separate coprocessor. The FPU supports 80-bit extended precision arithmetic, including a 64-bit significand and 15-bit exponent, alongside single (32-bit) and double (64-bit) precisions as defined by the ANSI/IEEE 754-1985 standard; it handles all x87 instruction set operations on eight 80-bit registers. Error reporting occurs via the FERR pin for unmasked exceptions such as overflow or invalid operations, with the IGNNE pin allowing selective ignoring of numeric errors to maintain execution flow.
Additional integrated components include hardware interfaces for clock multiplication in DX2 and DX4 models. These feature an on-chip phase-locked loop (PLL) that supports doubling (2x) or tripling (3x) of the external bus clock to generate the internal processor clock, selectable via the CLKMUL pin without altering bus timing. This multiplier hardware enables higher internal frequencies while maintaining compatibility with standard 486 bus protocols.

Clocking and Power Efficiency

The Am486 processors employed clock multiplication techniques to achieve higher internal operating frequencies without requiring corresponding increases in the external system bus speed, thereby enhancing performance while maintaining compatibility with existing 486-era motherboards. In the DX2 series, the core clock ran at twice the external bus frequency, effectively doubling the processor's speed relative to the bus. The DX4 series extended this approach with a 3x internal clock multiplier, allowing the core to operate at three times the bus speed for further performance gains. These multipliers were integrated via on-chip phase-locked loop (PLL) circuitry, which synchronized the internal clock to the external input while minimizing jitter and ensuring stable operation.
Early Am486 models operated at a 5V supply voltage, aligning with the standard for initial 486-compatible designs and supporting bus frequencies up to 40 MHz. Later iterations, particularly enhanced variants produced after AMD's process shrink around 1994-1995, transitioned to a 3.3V core voltage with 5V-tolerant I/O pins, significantly reducing power dissipationtypically to 2.6 watts at 100 MHz and 3.2 watts at 120 MHzwhile enabling compatibility with mixed-voltage systems. This dual-voltage capability allowed for lower thermal output and extended battery life in portable applications without necessitating full motherboard redesigns.
Power-saving modes in the Am486 family included stop-clock functionality, which halted the internal clock under controlled conditions during periods of inactivity, drastically lowering dynamic power consumption while preserving system state. Embedded variants further incorporated suspend modes and auto-halt power-down states, where the processor entered low-power idle by stopping the pipeline and granting bus control back to the system, often in response to signals like STPCLK#. These features, compliant with 486 bus protocols, enabled energy-efficient operation in laptops and industrial systems, with power draw dropping to near-static levels during suspension.
Thermal management for Am486 processors did not mandate integrated heatsinks for lower-speed models, relying instead on ambient airflow and passive cooling due to their modest power profiles. However, AMD provided guidelines for higher-frequency variants, such as those reaching 120 MHz, recommending enhanced airflow or optional heatsink attachments to maintain junction temperatures below 90C under load. The shift to 3.3V processes further aided thermal efficiency by reducing overall heat generation to 3-4 watts TDP in optimized designs.
The Am486 supported external bus speeds ranging from 25 MHz to 40 MHz, with configurable wait states to ensure timing compatibility across diverse memory and peripheral configurations. Zero-wait-state operation was possible for fast RAM, but additional wait statesinserted per 486 bus protocolaccommodated slower components, preventing data corruption while allowing seamless integration into legacy systems.

Models and Variants

DX and SX Series

The Am486DX series comprised AMD's initial full-featured 80486-compatible processors, equipped with an integrated floating-point unit (FPU) for handling complex mathematical computations on-chip. These models operated at clock speeds ranging from 25 MHz to 40 MHz, incorporating an 8 KB write-through L1 instruction and data cache to enhance performance in pipelined operations. Fabricated on a 0.7 m CMOS process and powered by a 5 V supply, the Am486DX processors utilized a 168-pin ceramic pin grid array (CPGA) package, ensuring compatibility with Socket 1 motherboards commonly found in desktop systems of the era.
Key variants included the Am486DX-25, Am486DX-33, and Am486DX-40, which served as flagship offerings targeted at general-purpose desktop computing, providing a cost-effective upgrade path from 386-based systems for tasks like office productivity and basic multimedia. Launched in April 1993, the DX series established AMD's presence in the high-performance PC segment, with production emphasizing quality for premium integrations by OEMs such as Compaq and Gateway.
In contrast, the Am486SX series offered a budget-oriented alternative by disabling the on-chip FPUnecessitating an external coprocessor like the Intel 487SX for floating-point taskswhile retaining the core integer processing capabilities. Clock speeds spanned 33 MHz to 40 MHz, paired with the same 8 KB L1 cache, 0.7 m process, 5 V operation, 168-pin CPGA packaging, and Socket 1 compatibility as the DX models. Clock-doubled variants like the Am486SX2-50 and SX2-66 were later introduced. This design choice reduced die size and cost without compromising compatibility with existing 486 software ecosystems.
Representative examples were the Am486SX-33 and Am486SX-40, aimed at entry-level desktops and embedded applications where FPU usage was optional or infrequent, such as text-based business software or industrial controls. Introduced in July 1993, the SX series bolstered AMD's market share through higher production volumes in 1993-1994, appealing to price-sensitive consumers and system builders seeking reliable 486 performance on a budget.

DX2 and DX4 Series

The Am486DX2 series introduced clock multiplication to AMD's 486 lineup, employing a 2x internal multiplier to double the core frequency relative to the external bus, thereby enhancing performance without requiring faster system buses. Fabricated initially on a 0.7 m process and later on 0.5 m, these processors operated at core speeds from 50 MHz to 100 MHz across bus speeds of 25 MHz to 50 MHz, with initial models using 5 V and later variants shifting to 3.3 V for reduced power draw. Cache configurations ranged from 8 KB to 16 KB on-chip unified L1, with enhanced models supporting write-back mode alongside the standard write-through for better efficiency in data handling.
Key models in the series included the Am486DX2-50 (25 MHz bus, 50 MHz core), Am486DX2-66 (33 MHz bus, 66 MHz core), Am486DX2-80 (40 MHz bus, 80 MHz core), and Am486DX2-100 (50 MHz bus, 100 MHz core), released starting in 1994 to target high-end 486 systems seeking affordable speed upgrades. Variants such as the DX2-66V8B featured specialized packaging for original equipment manufacturers (OEMs), often in 168-pin PGA format, while maintaining compatibility with existing 486 motherboards. The series' pipeline optimizations, including support for burst modes up to 128 MB/s at 40 MHz bus, enabled frequent instructions to execute in a single cycle, contributing to overall system responsiveness.
Building on the DX2 foundation, the Am486DX4 series in 1995 implemented a 3x clock multiplier, achieving core frequencies of 75 MHz to 120 MHz on bus speeds of 25 MHz to 40 MHz, all at 3.3 V to align with emerging power-efficient designs. These processors incorporated write-back cache as a standard enhancement, configurable to 8 KB or 16 KB L1, which reduced bus traffic and improved throughput compared to prior write-through implementations. Examples encompassed the Am486DX4-75 (25 MHz bus, 75 MHz core), Am486DX4-100 (33 MHz bus, 100 MHz core), and Am486DX4-120 (40 MHz bus, 120 MHz core), positioned as cost-effective alternatives to next-generation architectures.
The DX4 line featured refined pipeline enhancements for sustained high clocks, including better branch prediction and multiprocessor cache consistency, alongside native Socket 3 support via the 168-pin PGA package, which allowed seamless integration into upgraded 486 platforms without socket modifications. This combination enabled the top-end DX4-120 to outperform early Pentium processors, such as the 60 MHz and 66 MHz models, in integer-heavy tasks and certain application benchmarks, extending the 486 era's viability into 1996.

Embedded and Enhanced Variants

The Am486 family included several embedded-oriented models designed for low-power and industrial applications, such as the Am486DE2 series, which operated at clock speeds ranging from 25 MHz to 66 MHz and featured an 8 KB write-through cache to support efficient operation in appliances and control systems. These processors utilized a 3.3 V core voltage with 5 V tolerant I/O pins, enabling compatibility with legacy systems while reducing power consumption through features like Stop Clock mode, which drew as little as 0.6 mA in suspend states, and Auto Halt Power-Down for energy-efficient idle periods. Packaged in compact 208-lead SQFP or 168-pin PGA formats, the Am486DE2 supported dynamic bus sizing for 8-, 16-, or 32-bit interfaces, making it suitable for space-constrained embedded designs.
Enhanced variants, such as the Am486DX4-120SV8B, extended these capabilities with a 16 KB write-back cache and clock multiplication up to 3x (120 MHz internal from a 40 MHz bus), incorporating System Management Mode (SMM) via a two-pin SMI interface for advanced power management and suspend/resume functionality. This model, compatible with Socket 3, added error correction mechanisms in the cache for improved reliability in demanding environments and operated at 3.3 V to minimize heat output, achieving up to 40% power savings in compliant systems. Other low-voltage adaptations, like the Am486DXL series, emphasized further power reduction with 3.3 V operation and Stop Grant states drawing around 20 mA at 66 MHz, targeting battery-powered or thermally sensitive applications.
The Am486SE represented a single-chip embedded solution without an integrated floating-point unit, available at 25 MHz or 33 MHz in a 168-pin ceramic grid module package, integrating peripherals for simplified system-on-chip designs in cost-sensitive industrial setups. These variants supported extended temperature ranges, with industrial grades rated from -40C to +100C, enabling deployment in harsh conditions like factory automation. Production of Am486 embedded models continued into the late 1990s, powering applications in industrial controls and point-of-sale (POS) systems where reliability and legacy software compatibility were paramount.

Performance and Legacy

Comparisons to Intel 486

The AMD Am486 processors achieved performance parity with Intel's 80486 family on a clock-for-clock basis, but later models like the Am486 DX4-120 delivered higher effective speeds through internal clock multiplication, yielding roughly 54-61 SPECint92 in integer benchmarks comparable to the Intel 486 DX4-100. In SPECint92 integer benchmarks, the Am486 DX4 series often matched or slightly exceeded equivalent Intel 486 models at similar external bus speeds, with the DX4-120 providing up to 20% better real-world application performance in tasks like word processing and spreadsheets due to AMD's process optimizations and write-back cache. Notably, the Am486 outperformed early Intel Pentium 60 processors in select integer-heavy operations, such as database queries, though it lagged in floating-point computations.
Cost advantages were a key differentiator, with AMD pricing the Am486 20-40% below Intel equivalents; for instance, the Am486 DX4-100 retailed for around $150 in 1995, compared to Intel's 486 DX4-100 at over $200, making it ideal for budget-oriented PCs and upgrades in emerging markets. This pricing strategy, combined with drop-in socket compatibility (Socket 3), allowed the Am486 to capture significant market share among value-conscious consumers without requiring motherboard changes. Feature-wise, both families shared identical x86 instruction set architecture (ISA) support and integrated floating-point units, but later Am486 variants incorporated write-back L1 cache (8 KB for DX4 series) for improved efficiency over Intel's predominant write-through implementation (8 KB), reducing memory access latency in cache-intensive scenarios; the enhanced Am5x86 variant featured 16 KB.
Power consumption remained similar across both lines, typically 2.53.5 W for 3.3 V models at 100120 MHz, enabling comparable thermal envelopes in desktop systems. However, Am486 chips at elevated clocks like 120 MHz generated slightly higher heat without Intel's proprietary OverDrive optimizations, which included enhanced voltage regulation for stability under load. Overall, these attributes positioned the Am486 as a cost-effective alternative that extended the viability of 486-based platforms into the mid-1990s.

Applications and Market Impact

The Am486 processors found widespread adoption in budget-oriented clone PCs and entry-level systems during the mid-1990s, where their compatibility with existing 80486 motherboards, including Socket 1 variants, enabled cost-effective upgrades for desktop and laptop configurations. These chips powered early multimedia setups, supporting sound cards like the Sound Blaster and basic 3D acceleration, while offering full compatibility with Windows 95, which became a standard operating system for 486-based machines by 1995.
In the market, the Am486 helped AMD secure a substantial portion of the 80486 segment, reaching up to 40% share by 1995 and generating hundreds of millions in revenue, which critically funded the development of AMD's next-generation K5 architecture. Total shipments of Am486 variants are estimated in the millions, reflecting their role as a high-volume product that positioned AMD as the leading second-source supplier in the x86 ecosystem.
Am486 processors were used in legacy embedded applications, such as ATMs and medical devices, valued for their proven reliability in industrial environments where upgrades posed risks to certified operations. Production of the Am486 line was phased out by 1997, transitioning to enhanced variants like the Am5x86 and early Pentium-compatible clones, though its influence endured in promoting open competition within the x86 standard.
The Am486 symbolized AMD's pivotal challenge to Intel's dominance, serving as the last major Intel-licensed clone and encouraging broader adoption of second-source CPUs, which diversified the PC industry and pressured pricing in the microprocessor market.
