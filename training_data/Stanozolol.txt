 Stanozolol is a synthetic anabolic-androgenic steroid derived from dihydrotestosterone with both anabolic and androgenic properties. Developed in the early 1960s and first marketed in 1962 under the brand name Winstrol by Winthrop Laboratories, it is approved for medical use in treating hereditary angioedema by reducing the frequency and severity of attacks through its effects on C1-inhibitor deficient conditions. Despite its therapeutic applications, stanozolol is classified as a Schedule III controlled substance in the United States due to its potential for abuse and is prohibited in sports by organizations like the World Anti-Doping Agency.
The steroid gained notoriety through high-profile doping scandals, most famously the 1988 Olympic case of sprinter Ben Johnson, whose gold medal was stripped after testing positive for stanozolol, highlighting its role in enhancing explosive power and muscle growth. It remains one of the most frequently detected anabolic agents in anti-doping tests, implicated in over 1,200 violations as of 2023, particularly in track and field. Abuse is driven by its oral bioavailability and ability to promote lean muscle gains without significant water retention, though it carries substantial health risks including hepatotoxicity from its 17-alkylation, cardiovascular complications, and endocrine disruptions. These adverse effects underscore the empirical evidence of harm from non-medical use, often outweighing purported performance benefits in controlled studies.

Medical Applications

Treatment of Hereditary Angioedema

Stanozolol is approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration for the prophylactic treatment of hereditary angioedema (HAE), a rare genetic disorder caused by deficiency or dysfunction of C1 esterase inhibitor (C1-INH), leading to recurrent, potentially life-threatening episodes of swelling in subcutaneous tissues, gastrointestinal tract, and upper airways. The medication is administered orally in 2 mg tablets and serves as long-term therapy to prevent attacks rather than acute intervention.
As an attenuated androgen, stanozolol exerts its therapeutic effect by stimulating hepatic synthesis of C1-INH and complement component C4, thereby restoring functional levels of these proteins and attenuating the bradykinin-mediated pathway responsible for vascular permeability in HAE. This mechanism reduces the frequency and intensity of edema attacks, with clinical improvements often accompanied by measurable increases in plasma C1-INH antigenic levels and functional activity, as well as C4 concentrations. Unlike fibrinolytic agents, stanozolol's antifibrinolytic properties further contribute to stabilizing vascular integrity during prophylaxis.
Clinical evidence from prospective studies supports its efficacy at low doses; in one cohort of 27 patients with HAE type I, daily administration of 0.5 to 2 mg achieved complete symptom control in all participants, with higher initial doses (up to 4 mg) unnecessary for ongoing management and associated with excessive biochemical responses without added clinical benefit. Another long-term evaluation over a decade confirmed sustained attack prevention in responsive patients, with dose reduction to 1 mg daily possible after initial control at 2 mg for two months. Effectiveness persists in both HAE types I and II, though individual titration is required based on attack frequency and biochemical markers.
Therapy typically begins with an initial dose of 2 mg orally three times daily (total 6 mg per day), with downward adjustment to the minimal effective maintenance dose of 2 mg per day or less under monitoring of clinical response and liver function, as prolonged use demands regular assessment to balance benefits against potential androgenic effects. Studies indicate that 60% of female patients may experience menstrual irregularities at maintenance doses, underscoring the need for gender-specific considerations in prophylaxis. Overall, stanozolol remains a viable option for long-term HAE management when patients are closely supervised, though its use has declined with the availability of targeted C1-INH replacement therapies.

Beyond hereditary angioedema, other human therapeutic applications of stanozolol have included treatment of aplastic anemia via its erythropoietic stimulation, though its use has declined due to hepatotoxicity risks and availability of alternatives. Men may experience rapid-onset aggression, mood alterations, or priapism due to heightened androgen receptor activation, though empirical data on incidence remains limited to anecdotal reports and small cohorts. Historical Development

Discovery and Early Studies

Stanozolol was first synthesized in 1959 by R. O. Clinton and colleagues at Winthrop Laboratories, a division of Sterling Drug Inc., as part of efforts to develop heterocyclic derivatives of dihydrotestosterone with enhanced anabolic activity relative to androgenic effects. The compound, chemically 17-methyl-17-hydroxy-5-androstanopyrazole, featured a pyrazole ring fused to the A-ring of the steroid backbone, a modification intended to alter receptor binding and metabolic stability compared to unmodified androgens. This synthesis was detailed in a 1959 publication in the Journal of the American Chemical Society, marking it as one of the early examples of structure-activity optimization in anabolic-androgenic steroids (AAS).
Early pharmacological studies in the late 1950s and early 1960s primarily involved animal models to evaluate anabolic potency, nitrogen retention, and protein synthesis. Rat bioassays demonstrated stanozolol's ability to promote weight gain and muscle mass accrual at doses producing minimal virilization, attributed to its favorable 30:1 anabolic-to-androgenic ratio compared to testosterone. These preclinical investigations, conducted by Winthrop researchers, confirmed reduced hepatotoxicity and estrogenic activity relative to 17-alkylated steroids like methyltestosterone, paving the way for clinical translation.
Human trials commenced shortly after synthesis, with initial applications targeting conditions involving protein catabolism, such as anemia and growth retardation. A 1963 study examined stanozolol's effects on hemoglobin levels in patients, reporting elevations without excessive androgenic side effects at low oral doses. Concurrent evaluations in debilitated children showed accelerated linear growth and improved nitrogen balance, though skeletal maturation rates required monitoring to avoid premature epiphyseal closure. These studies established stanozolol's oral bioavailability and efficacy in promoting anabolism, leading to its commercial release as Winstrol in 1962 for therapeutic use in muscle-wasting disorders.

Commercialization and Widespread Adoption

Stanozolol entered commercialization in 1962 after receiving U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval for human medical use on January 9 of that year. It was initially marketed for therapeutic applications, particularly in managing hereditary angioedema by promoting the production of C1 esterase inhibitor to reduce swelling episodes. 
The drug rapidly found adoption in veterinary practice under the brand Winstrol-V, where it was administered to promote appetite stimulation, weight gain, and muscle strength in debilitated animals such as dogs and horses suffering from anemia or cachexia. By the mid-1960s, its oral and injectable formulations facilitated broader clinical and animal health applications, reflecting its favorable anabolic profile with reduced estrogenic side effects compared to earlier steroids.
Widespread non-medical adoption emerged in the 1970s among athletes and bodybuilders, drawn to stanozolol's capacity for enhancing lean muscle mass, strength, and vascularity while minimizing water retention and androgenic effects. This off-label use proliferated in competitive sports, including track and field and weightlifting, leading to its classification as a performance-enhancing agent and subsequent bans by international governing bodies. High-profile doping violations, such as those documented in over 1,000 cases across various disciplines, underscored its entrenched role in illicit athletic enhancement by the 1980s.

Critics argue that anti-doping policies, including the World Anti-Doping Agency's (WADA) prohibitions on anabolic-androgenic steroids like stanozolol, fail to eradicate doping despite escalating enforcement efforts. The 1988 Olympic scandal involving stanozolol use by sprinter Ben Johnson prompted stricter rules, yet subsequent analyses show no substantial decline in overall substance use, as athletes shift to unregulated or exempt compounds like therapeutic nandrolone. Prohibition is critiqued for exacerbating health risks rather than mitigating them, as bans drive doping into clandestine markets lacking medical oversight.
