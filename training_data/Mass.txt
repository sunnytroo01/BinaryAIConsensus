 In physics, mass is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that quantifies the amount of matter in an object and determines its resistance to changes in motion when subjected to a force. It is experimentally defined through inertial mass, which measures an object's inertia via Newton's second law of motion, $ F = ma $, where $ F $ is the net force, $ m $ is the mass, and $ a $ is the acceleration produced. The international standard unit of mass in the International System of Units (SI) is the kilogram (kg), defined since 2019 in terms of the Planck constant for precision independent of physical artifacts.
Mass also manifests in gravitational interactions, where gravitational mass determines both the strength of the gravitational force exerted by an object (active gravitational mass) and its response to external gravitational fields (passive gravitational mass). The equivalence of inertial mass and gravitational mass, a principle first empirically observed by Galileo and formalized by Newton, implies that all objects accelerate identically in a uniform gravitational field regardless of their composition, as demonstrated in vacuum drop experiments. This weak equivalence principle underpins Einstein's general theory of relativity, where gravitational effects arise from the curvature of spacetime rather than a traditional force, and experimental tests have confirmed the equality to within parts in $ 10^{15} $.
In the framework of special relativity, mass refers to the invariant rest mass $ m $, a Lorentz scalar that remains constant across reference frames and corresponds to the object's rest energy via $ E_0 = mc^2 $, where $ c $ is the speed of light. Unlike the outdated concept of relativistic mass, which varies with velocity, modern physics treats mass as frame-independent, with total energy incorporating kinetic contributions. Mass is distinct from weight, which is the gravitational force on an object and varies with location, whereas mass remains constant. In particle physics, the masses of elementary particles arise from interactions with the Higgs field, explaining why particles like photons are massless while others acquire mass through symmetry breaking.

Fundamental Concepts

Definition and Phenomena

Mass is a fundamental property of matter that serves as a quantitative measure of the amount of matter in an object, reflecting its resistance to changes in motion (inertia) and its capacity to interact gravitationally with other masses. In everyday observations, this property manifests in phenomena such as the conservation of mass during chemical reactions, where the total mass of reactants equals that of products, as established by Antoine Lavoisier's law in 1789. Mass also determines an object's density, defined as the mass per unit volume (=mV\rho = \frac{m}{V}=Vm), which influences behaviors like buoyancy in fluidsobjects with lower density than the surrounding medium experience an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the displaced fluid, allowing them to float.
Near Earth's surface, the gravitational force on an object of mass mmm is given by F=mgF = mgF=mg, where ggg is the local gravitational acceleration, approximately 9.8m/s29.8 \, \mathrm{m/s^2}9.8m/s2. This equation highlights mass's role in determining the force pulling objects downward, distinct from weight, which specifically refers to this gravitational effect. In practical examples, mass governs inertial responses, such as in collisions where a heavier vehicle requires greater force to alter its velocity compared to a lighter one, conserving overall momentum. Similarly, in sedimentation processes, particles with greater mass settle more rapidly under gravity due to their higher inertia and gravitational pull relative to drag forces in a fluid medium.

Inertial vs. Gravitational Mass

In physics, inertial mass $ m_i $ quantifies an object's resistance to changes in its motion when subjected to a force, as expressed in Newton's second law: $ \mathbf{F} = m_i \mathbf{a} $, where $ \mathbf{F} $ is the net force and $ \mathbf{a} $ is the resulting acceleration. This property determines how much acceleration a given force produces; larger inertial mass leads to smaller acceleration for the same force.
Gravitational mass $ m_g $, in contrast, measures the strength of an object's participation in gravitational interactions, appearing in Newton's law of universal gravitation: $ F = G \frac{m_{g1} m_{g2}}{r^2} $, where $ F $ is the attractive force between two objects, $ G $ is the gravitational constant, and $ r $ is the distance between their centers. Here, gravitational mass acts as the "charge" that sources and responds to gravitational fields, with the force proportional to the product of the two masses.
The equivalence principle posits that inertial mass and gravitational mass are identical, such that $ m_i = m_g $ for all objects, implying that the ratio $ m_i / m_g = 1 $ within experimental limits. This equivalence ensures the universality of free fall, where all bodies accelerate identically in a gravitational field regardless of composition.
Early confirmation came from torsion balance experiments by Lornd Etvs and collaborators between the 1880s and 1920s, which compared the gravitational and inertial responses of different materials and achieved a precision of about 3 10^{-9} for the mass ratio. Modern tests using torsion balances and atom interferometry with laser-cooled atoms have pushed this verification to levels of 10^{-13} or better, such as the Et-Wash group's measurements ruling out deviations to parts in 10^{13}. Satellite-based experiments like MICROSCOPE (20162018) further confirmed the equivalence to 1.3 10^{-15} using differential acceleration of test masses in orbit.
This equivalence underpins the empirical observation that objects of different masses fall at the same rate in vacuum, a principle first demonstrated by Galileo around 1590 through experiments with inclined planes, showing that the acceleration due to gravity is independent of mass in the absence of air resistance.

Mass vs. Weight

Mass is a fundamental property of matter representing the amount of matter in an object, remaining constant regardless of location. In contrast, weight is the gravitational force exerted on that mass by a celestial body, which varies depending on the local gravitational field. This force is quantified by the equation $ W = m g $, where $ W $ is weight, $ m $ is mass, and $ g $ is the acceleration due to gravity. For example, on Earth, $ g $ is approximately 9.8 m/s, while on the Moon it is about 1.62 m/s, meaning an object with the same mass weighs roughly one-sixth as much on the Moon as it does on Earth.
A common misconception arises from everyday language, where "weight" is often used interchangeably with mass, such as when "weighing" produce at a market actually involves measuring mass through a calibrated spring scale that converts the gravitational force reading to mass units. In reality, scales measure weightthe force of gravitywhile balances determine mass by comparing the unknown object to standard masses in a way that cancels out gravitational effects. This distinction highlights that mass is an intrinsic, invariant quantity, whereas weight is context-dependent and can change dramatically.
Weight can appear to vanish in free fall or orbital motion, as experienced by astronauts on the International Space Station, where continuous free fall around Earth creates a microgravity environment despite the persistent pull of gravity. Apparent weight also varies in accelerating frames, such as an elevator: during upward acceleration, the normal force increases, making a person feel heavier, while downward acceleration reduces it, simulating lightness. In the imperial system, this confusion led to the introduction of "pound-mass" (lbm) for mass and "pound-force" (lbf) for force, with one lbf defined as the weight of one lbm under standard gravity to clarify the difference. Gravitational mass, the property that determines this force in the equation, is experimentally equivalent to inertial mass but pertains here to the practical measurement of weight.

Historical Perspectives

Pre-Newtonian Concepts

In ancient Greek philosophy, particularly in the work of Aristotle around 350 BCE, concepts of heaviness and motion were tied to the four elementsearth, water, air, and fireeach possessing a natural place and motion. Heavy elements like earth and water naturally moved downward toward the center of the universe, which coincided with the Earth's position, while lighter elements like air and fire moved upward. This framework explained falling objects as seeking their natural resting place, with heaviness arising from the predominance of earthy composition in bodies, though without a distinct notion of mass as quantity separate from weight.
During the early medieval period, John Philoponus in the 6th century began challenging Aristotelian ideas on motion, proposing that weight could be understood in relation to the "quantity of matter" inherent in a body, suggesting a proportional link between heaviness and the amount of material substance. This view influenced later scholars who increasingly treated weight as a measure of quantity of matter, laying groundwork for distinguishing intrinsic properties from observed gravitational effects. By the 1340s, Jean Buridan developed the impetus theory, positing that a projectile's continued motion resulted from an impressed force proportional to its speed and quantity of matter, serving as an early precursor to inertia while equating heaviness with this material quantity.
In astronomical models from antiquity through the Renaissance, such as Ptolemy's geocentric system in the 2nd century CE and Copernicus's heliocentric reform in the 16th century, massive bodies like Earth or the Sun were implicitly central influencers of planetary orbits, maintained through uniform circular motion on spheres or epicycles. These kinematic frameworks accounted for observed paths without quantifying mass or specifying attractive forces, relying instead on geometric ideals and natural celestial tendencies.
In the early 17th century, Galileo's experiments on free fall, including the legendary drop from the Leaning Tower of Pisa and more precise inclined plane tests around 16031604, demonstrated that objects accelerate downward at the same rate regardless of their mass, when air resistance is minimizedchallenging Aristotelian views and highlighting a universal gravitational effect independent of heaviness. These findings marked a shift toward empirical quantification of motion, paving the way for later syntheses.

Newtonian Mass

In his Philosophi Naturalis Principia Mathematica (1687), Isaac Newton introduced mass, termed the "quantity of matter," as a fundamental property defined by the measure arising conjointly from a body's density and volume. This scalar quantity determines both the inertial resistance to acceleration and the gravitational influence of an object, with mass being proportional to the product of density and bulk. Newton explicitly stated in the work's definitions: "The quantity of matter is the measure of the same, arising from its density and bulk conjunctly. Thus air of a double density, in a double space, is quadruple in quantity; in a triple space sextuple in quantity." This formulation marked a shift toward a precise, mathematical characterization, distinguishing it from earlier qualitative notions.
To demonstrate the universal reach of gravitational attraction, Newton described a thought experiment involving a cannonball fired horizontally from atop a tall mountain. With increasing muzzle velocity, the projectile's trajectory evolves from a steep parabolic fall to a gentler curve, and at sufficient speed, it circles the Earth indefinitely, balancing tangential inertia against the planet's gravitational pull. This illustration underscores mass's dual role: the Earth's substantial mass generates the centripetal force required for orbital motion, while the cannonball's own mass contributes negligibly but exemplifies inertial tendencies.
Newton's law of universal gravitation posits that the attractive force $ F $ between two point masses $ m_1 $ and $ m_2 $ separated by distance $ r $ is directly proportional to the product of the masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance:
F=Gm1m2r2,
F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2},
F=Gr2m1m2,
where $ G $ is a universal constant. Here, mass serves as the source term dictating the strength of gravitational interaction, unifying terrestrial and celestial mechanics. Although Newton left $ G $ undetermined in the Principia, its value was experimentally established in 17971798 by Henry Cavendish, who used a torsion balance to measure the feeble attraction between lead spheres, yielding Earth's density and thus $ G \approx 6.74 \times 10^{-11} $ m kg s (close to the modern value).
Within the framework of Newtonian mechanics, conservation of mass is an implicit principle, as the quantity of matter in isolated systems remains unchanged during interactions, collisions, or motions, with no mechanisms for creation or annihilation. This constancy underpins the predictability of mechanical systems, where total mass balances before and after events like impacts.

Role in Motion and Inertia

In classical mechanics, mass plays a central role in defining inertia, which is the property of an object to resist changes to its state of motion. According to Newton's first law of motion, an object at rest remains at rest, and an object in uniform motion continues in a straight line at constant velocity unless acted upon by a net external force./Axioms,_or_Laws_of_Motion) Mass quantifies this inertial resistance; greater mass implies a stronger tendency to maintain the current velocity, requiring more force to accelerate or decelerate the object. This concept, often called the law of inertia, establishes mass as the measure of an object's reluctance to deviate from its motion path.
Newton's second law further elucidates mass's role in motion by relating force, mass, and acceleration: the net force $ \mathbf{F} $ acting on an object equals its mass $ m $ times its acceleration $ \mathbf{a} $, expressed as $ \mathbf{F} = m \mathbf{a} $. This equation derives from the rate of change of momentum, where linear momentum $ \mathbf{p} $ is defined as $ \mathbf{p} = m \mathbf{v} $ with $ \mathbf{v} $ as velocity, such that $ \mathbf{F} = \frac{d\mathbf{p}}{dt} $. For constant mass, this simplifies to $ F = ma $, showing that acceleration is inversely proportional to mass for a given force. Impulse, the integral of force over time $ \int \mathbf{F} , dt = \Delta \mathbf{p} $, illustrates how forces alter momentum, with mass determining the resulting velocity change.
The principle of conservation of momentum underscores mass's importance in interactions, stating that the total momentum of an isolated system remains constant if no external forces act. In elastic collisions, both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved, leading to $ m_1 \mathbf{v}{1i} + m_2 \mathbf{v}{2i} = m_1 \mathbf{v}{1f} + m_2 \mathbf{v}{2f} $, where subscripts denote initial and final velocities for masses $ m_1 $ and $ m_2 $. In inelastic collisions, only momentum is conserved, often resulting in the objects sticking together with total momentum $ (m_1 + m_2) \mathbf{v}f = m_1 \mathbf{v}{1i} + m_2 \mathbf{v}_{2i} $, highlighting how mass distributions influence post-collision velocities.
For systems of multiple particles, the center of mass simplifies analysis by treating the entire system as an equivalent point mass equal to the total mass $ M = \sum m_i $ located at the weighted average position $ \mathbf{r}{cm} = \frac{1}{M} \sum m_i \mathbf{r}i $./10%3A_Linear_Momentum_and_the_Center_of_Mass/10.03%3A_The_center_of_mass) Under no external forces, the center of mass moves with constant velocity, following Newton's first law, while internal motions may vary; external forces accelerate the center of mass as if all mass were concentrated there, per $ \mathbf{F}{ext} = M \mathbf{a}{cm} $.
A practical example is firearm recoil, where conservation of momentum governs the backward motion of the gun upon firing a bullet. If the bullet of mass $ m_b $ exits with velocity $ \mathbf{v}_b $, the gun of mass $ m_g $ recoils with velocity $ \mathbf{v}_g = -\frac{m_b}{m_g} \mathbf{v}_b $, demonstrating how the gun's larger mass results in smaller recoil speed. Similarly, in planetary systems, perturbations from passing bodies alter orbits via momentum exchange, with the center of mass of the system maintaining uniform motion absent external influences.

Mass in Modern Physics

Special Relativity

In special relativity, introduced by Albert Einstein in 1905, the concept of mass is reframed to account for the invariance of physical laws under transformations between inertial frames moving at constant relative velocities. The rest mass $ m_0 $, also known as invariant mass, is a scalar quantity that remains constant regardless of the observer's frame, serving as a fundamental property of a particle or system. This invariance contrasts with classical notions where mass was assumed unchanging but frame-independent in a more limited sense.
Historically, the idea of relativistic mass $ m = \gamma m_0 $, with $ \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} $ where $ v $ is the object's speed and $ c $ is the speed of light, was used to describe how inertial resistance appears to increase with velocity. However, in modern physics, this concept is disfavored because it can lead to misconceptions; instead, only the rest mass $ m_0 $ is employed as the measure of inertia, with relativistic effects manifested through modifications to momentum and energy. The relativistic momentum is given by $ \mathbf{p} = \gamma m_0 \mathbf{v} $, ensuring conservation laws hold across frames without implying a velocity-dependent mass. This approach clarifies that there is no true increase in a particle's intrinsic mass with speed; rather, the dynamics are governed by the Lorentz factor in the expressions for momentum and energy.
A cornerstone of special relativity is the mass-energy equivalence, where the rest energy of an object is $ E_0 = m_0 c^2 $. This relation emerges from the energy-momentum four-vector $ p^\mu = (E/c, \mathbf{p}) $, whose Minkowski norm is invariant and equal to $ m_0^2 c^2 $, linking energy $ E $, momentum $ \mathbf{p} $, and rest mass through $ E^2 = (p c)^2 + (m_0 c^2)^2 $. In particle accelerators, protons or electrons are driven to near-light speeds not by altering their mass but by imparting kinetic energy, which raises their total energy $ E = \gamma m_0 c^2 $ while their rest mass remains fixed, allowing exploration of high-energy regimes. Similarly, in nuclear processes, the binding energy deficit appears as a mass loss: the total mass of a bound nucleus is less than the sum of its unbound nucleons by $ \Delta m = E_b / c^2 $, where $ E_b $ is the binding energy. This mass defect was experimentally confirmed in the 1932 Cockcroft-Walton experiment, where protons bombarded lithium nuclei, producing alpha particles and releasing energy consistent with the predicted mass-energy conversion to within 0.5%.

General Relativity

In general relativity, formulated by Albert Einstein in 1915, the equivalence principle is extended beyond its initial formulation to establish the complete equivalence between inertial mass and gravitational mass, positing that the effects of gravity are indistinguishable from those of acceleration in a local frame. This principle implies that gravity arises not as a force acting on mass, but as the curvature of spacetime geometry induced by the presence of mass and energy. Consequently, the path of objects, including light, follows geodesics in this curved spacetime, reinterpreting gravitational attraction as inertial motion along these paths.

Quantum Field Theory

In quantum mechanics, the mass of a particle appears explicitly in the Schrdinger equation, which describes the non-relativistic dynamics of quantum systems. The time-independent form of the equation is

In quantum field theory (QFT), the origin of particle masses is explained through mechanisms that break electroweak symmetry. The Higgs mechanism, proposed in 1964, involves spontaneous symmetry breaking of the SU(2) U(1) gauge symmetry via a scalar Higgs field acquiring a nonzero vacuum expectation value. This generates masses for the weak gauge bosons $ W^\pm $ and $ Z^0 $ through their interactions with the Higgs field, while the photon remains massless as it is associated with the unbroken U(1) electromagnetic symmetry. Fermions, such as quarks and leptons, acquire mass via Yukawa couplings to the Higgs field, proportional to the coupling strength. The Higgs boson itself, the quantum excitation of this field, was discovered in 2012 by the ATLAS and CMS experiments at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC), confirming the mechanism with a particle mass of approximately 125 GeV/c.
Specific particle masses illustrate these principles within the Standard Model. The electron has a rest mass of 0.511 MeV/c, while the proton's mass is 938 MeV/c, predominantly arising from the binding energy of its constituent quarks and gluons rather than the quarks' bare masses. Neutrinos, long thought massless, possess tiny masses with an upper limit of less than 0.45 eV/c (as of 2025), evidenced by the discovery of neutrino oscillations in 1998 by the Super-Kamiokande experiment, which observed muon neutrino disappearance in atmospheric data, implying mixing between neutrino flavors and thus nonzero masses.
Beyond electroweak contributions, quark masses receive significant dynamical enhancement from quantum chromodynamics (QCD). In QCD, the chiral symmetry of massless quarks is spontaneously broken by the formation of a quark-antiquark condensate in the vacuum, generating effective constituent masses on the order of 300400 MeV for light quarks like the up and down quarks, far exceeding their current quark masses (around 25 MeV). This non-perturbative effect explains much of the nucleon mass.
In condensed matter systems, QFT concepts extend to effective masses for quasiparticles. In band theory of solids, electrons in a periodic lattice potential experience an effective mass $ m^* $, determined by the curvature of the energy band $ E(\mathbf{k}) $ via $ m^* = \hbar^2 / (\partial^2 E / \partial k^2) $, which can differ substantially from the free electron mass due to interactions with the lattice. For instance, in semiconductors like silicon, conduction band electrons have $ m^* \approx 0.26 m_e $ along certain directions, influencing charge transport and optical properties.

Measurement and Scales

Units of Mass

The kilogram (kg) is the base unit of mass in the International System of Units (SI), redefined on May 20, 2019, by fixing the numerical value of the Planck constant at exactly $ h = 6.62607015 \times 10^{-34} $ J s, thereby linking mass directly to fundamental physical constants rather than a physical artifact. This redefinition ensures the kilogram's stability and universality, allowing precise realization through experiments like the Kibble balance, which equates mechanical power to electrical power using quantum standards.
Historical units of mass date back to ancient civilizations, such as the Egyptian deben, used around 3000 BCE for weighing precious metals and goods, equivalent to approximately 91 grams. In ancient Rome, the libra served as a standard unit, roughly 329 grams, dividing into 12 unciae (ounces) and facilitating trade across the empire. By the medieval period in Europe, the pound emerged as a common unit, standardized in England as the avoirdupois pound at about 0.4536 kg, influencing local commerce and weights for commodities like wool and grain.
The imperial system, rooted in British standards, contrasts with the metric system: the avoirdupois pound is defined as exactly 0.45359237 kg, with the ounce at 1/16 pound (about 28.35 grams), while metric uses decimal multiples of the kilogram. These differences have historically affected international trade, requiring conversions that increase costs and risk errors in global supply chains, as seen in export industries adapting to metric-dominant markets.
Prior to 2019, the kilogram was realized using the International Prototype Kilogram (IPK), a platinum-iridium cylinder crafted in 1889 and stored at the International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM) in France, serving as the global mass standard despite gradual mass drift over time. Post-redefinition, the Kibble balance enables laboratories to realize the kilogram with high precision by measuring electromagnetic forces against gravitational ones, eliminating reliance on artifacts.
In everyday applications, mass units span vast scales within the SI framework; for instance, the microgram (1 g = $ 10^{-9} $ kg) is used in pharmaceuticals for precise drug dosing, such as in vitamin supplements or medications. At astronomical scales, the solar mass, approximately $ 1.989 \times 10^{30} $ kg, serves as a reference for stellar masses, equivalent to about 333,000 Earth masses. Practical measurements often relate mass to weight via local gravity, but units focus on invariant mass.

Atomic and Molecular Masses

In atomic and molecular physics, the atomic mass unit (u), also known as the dalton (Da), serves as the standard unit for expressing the masses of atoms and molecules on a relative scale. It is defined as exactly one-twelfth the mass of an unbound atom of carbon-12 in its ground state. This definition ensures a precise, isotope-specific reference, with the value of 1 u equivalent to 1.66053906892 \times 10^{-27} kg.
Atomic masses are distinguished between isotopic masses, which refer to specific isotopes, and average atomic masses, which account for the natural abundance of isotopes in a sample. For instance, the isotope carbon-12 has an exact mass of 12 u by definition, while the standard atomic weight of naturally occurring carbon, incorporating about 1.1% carbon-13, is 12.011 u. This averaging is crucial for practical calculations in chemistry, as it reflects the weighted mean based on isotopic abundances determined through precise measurements.
Molecular mass, or molecular weight, is calculated as the sum of the atomic masses of its constituent atoms, expressed in u. For water (H_2O), using standard atomic weights of hydrogen (1.008 u) and oxygen (15.999 u), the molecular mass is 18.015 u. This relative scale facilitates comparisons and computations without needing absolute masses in kilograms.
The connection between atomic-scale masses and macroscopic quantities is provided by Avogadro's constant, N_A, an exact SI-defined value of 6.02214076 \times 10^{23} \mol^{-1}. It relates the mass of a single atom or molecule, m (in u or kg), to the molar mass M (in g/mol) via M = m \times N_A, where the atomic mass in u directly equals the molar mass in g/mol for the same entity.
These concepts find essential applications in chemistry, particularly in stoichiometry, where atomic and molecular masses determine reactant and product ratios in chemical equations. In mass spectrometry, the atomic mass unit enables the identification of isotopic compositions by measuring mass-to-charge ratios, allowing precise determination of isotope ratios for elements like carbon in environmental or biological samples.

Exotic and Theoretical Aspects

Tachyonic and Imaginary Mass

In theoretical physics, tachyons are postulated as faster-than-light particles possessing an imaginary rest mass, conventionally denoted as $ m = i |m| $, where $ i = \sqrt{-1} $ and $ |m| $ is a real positive quantity. This imaginary mass formulation emerges from the special relativistic energy-momentum relation $ E^2 = p^2 c^2 + m^2 c^4 $, where for velocities $ v > c $, the Lorentz factor $ \gamma = (1 - v^2/c^2)^{-1/2} $ yields imaginary values if $ m $ is real; assigning $ m $ as imaginary restores reality to key observables like energy and momentum.
Tachyons lack a rest frame since $ v > c $ always, so they have no real rest energy; their energy-momentum relation can be rewritten as $ E^2 = p^2 c^2 - \mu^2 c^4 $ (with $ \mu $ real), ensuring real positive energies in any frame, with a dispersion relation that permits arbitrarily high speeds while energy decreases with increasing velocity, leading to instabilities. More critically, tachyons would exhibit inverted causality, where signals propagate backward in time in certain reference frames, potentially enabling paradoxes such as effects preceding their causes and undermining the chronological order of events in relativity.
Within quantum field theory, tachyonic fieldscharacterized by negative mass squared $ m^2 < 0 $signal vacuum instabilities that facilitate spontaneous symmetry breaking. For instance, in the Higgs mechanism, the scalar potential $ V(\phi) = -\mu^2 |\phi|^2 + \lambda |\phi|^4 $ initially features a tachyonic mass term $ m^2 = -2\mu^2 < 0 $, driving the field from an unstable symmetric state to a stable vacuum with nonzero expectation value, thereby generating masses for particles. This tachyonic instability resolves into a stable configuration without implying free superluminal particles, serving instead as a mathematical indicator of phase transitions.
The propagation of tachyon fields is described by an extension of the Klein-Gordon equation, $ (\square + m^2) \psi = 0 $, where $ \square = \partial^\mu \partial_\mu $ is the d'Alembertian and $ m^2 < 0 $ yields solutions with spacelike momenta and superluminal phase velocities. Such complex mass terms maintain Lorentz invariance in the field equations but introduce acausal features in particle interpretations.
Despite their theoretical intrigue, no experimental evidence for tachyons exists; searches in cosmic rays for anomalous Cerenkov radiation or superluminal signals, as well as accelerator experiments probing neutrino speeds and decay anomalies, have yielded negative results. Their hypothetical presence would violate causality principles central to standard relativistic quantum field theories, rendering them incompatible with observed physics without additional interpretive frameworks.

Mass in Particle Physics Beyond Standard Model

In extensions to the Standard Model of particle physics, mass scales play a pivotal role in addressing fundamental puzzles such as the origin of the electroweak scale relative to gravity and the identity of dark matter, often introducing new particles with masses spanning many orders of magnitude.
The hierarchy problem highlights the tension between the observed Higgs boson mass of 125 GeV and the Planck scale of about 101910^{19}1019 GeV, where radiative corrections from virtual particles would otherwise generate contributions pushing the Higgs mass up to the Planck value, necessitating an unnatural fine-tuning of parameters to at least 30 decimal places for stability. This issue underscores the perceived incompleteness of the Standard Model, as the required cancellations appear contrived without additional structure.
Supersymmetry offers a leading solution by positing superpartnersfermionic for bosons and bosonic for fermionswith masses around the TeV scale (10310^{3}103 GeV), whose loop contributions exactly cancel the quadratic divergences in the Higgs mass corrections, rendering the electroweak scale naturally stable against high-energy physics. As of late 2024, LHC experiments have imposed stringent lower bounds on superpartner masses, excluding gluinos up to 2.2 TeV and squarks up to 1.7 TeV in simplified models using data analyzed through 2024, with no evidence for supersymmetry observed to date and ongoing Run 3 analyses expected to increase sensitivity further.
Dark matter, comprising about 27% of the universe's energy density, motivates massive particle candidates in beyond-Standard-Model theories, such as weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs) with masses typically in the 101000 GeV range, which could arise as lightest supersymmetric partners or other neutral relics stable on cosmological timescales. Complementary candidates include axions, ultralight pseudoscalars solving the strong CP problem, with masses around 10510^{-5}105 eV that allow them to behave as cold dark matter through coherent oscillations in the early universe.
Grand unified theories seek to merge the three gauge interactions of the Standard Model into a single force at energies near 101610^{16}1016 GeV, predicting proton decay via the exchange of heavy leptoquarks or colored gauge bosons (such as X and Y in SU(5) models) with masses exceeding 101510^{15}1015 GeV to suppress the decay lifetime beyond current experimental limits of 103410^{34}1034 years. These high masses ensure consistency with non-observation of proton decay while enabling unification of coupling constants.
Neutrino oscillations imply tiny masses for the active neutrinos, around 0.010.1 eV, far below those of charged leptons; the type-I seesaw mechanism resolves this by adding sterile right-handed neutrinos with Majorana masses at the GUT scale (1015\sim 10^{15}1015 GeV), yielding light masses via $ m_\nu \approx \frac{m_D^2}{M_R} $, where $ m_D $ is the electroweak-scale Dirac mass term from Yukawa couplings. This extension naturally ties neutrino masses to high-scale physics without fine-tuning, though it introduces three heavy neutrinos per generation whose decays could explain the cosmic baryon asymmetry.
