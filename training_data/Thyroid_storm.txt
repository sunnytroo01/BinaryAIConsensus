Thyroid storm

 Thyroid storm, also known as thyrotoxic crisis, is a rare and acute life-threatening manifestation of thyrotoxicosis characterized by severe hyperthyroidism leading to multi-organ dysfunction and high mortality if untreated. It represents an extreme exacerbation of thyroid hormone excess, often precipitated by stressors in individuals with underlying hyperthyroid conditions such as Graves' disease. Despite advances in medical care, thyroid storm remains a medical emergency requiring immediate intensive intervention to prevent fatal complications like cardiac failure, shock, or disseminated intravascular coagulation.
The condition typically arises in patients with pre-existing hyperthyroidism when a precipitating event disrupts thyroid hormone homeostasis, such as infections (e.g., pneumonia), surgery, trauma, discontinuation of antithyroid medications, or exposure to iodinated contrast agents. Less commonly, it can occur in toxic multinodular goiter or thyroiditis. Epidemiologically, thyroid storm is uncommon, with recent estimates indicating an incidence of approximately 1 case per 100,000 persons per year in the United States (as of 2020) and 0.2 per 100,000 in Japan; it affects women more frequently than men in a ratio of approximately 3:1 and typically occurs in adults with an average age around 60 years. Pathophysiologically, it involves not only elevated free thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) but also heightened tissue sensitivity to these hormones, increased sympathetic nervous system activity, and cytokine-mediated responses that amplify organ stress.
Clinically, thyroid storm presents with exaggerated symptoms of thyrotoxicosis across multiple systems, including high fever (often exceeding 104F or 40C), tachycardia (heart rate >140 beats per minute), and central nervous system disturbances such as agitation, delirium, or coma. Cardiovascular manifestations like atrial fibrillation, congestive heart failure, or hypotension are common, alongside gastrointestinal symptoms including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and jaundice due to liver dysfunction. In elderly patients, presentations may be atypical, featuring apathy or subdued symptoms rather than the classic hyperactive state.
Diagnosis is primarily clinical, supported by laboratory confirmation of thyrotoxicosis (elevated free T4 and T3 with suppressed TSH), and guided by scoring systems such as the Burch-Wartofsky Point Scale (a score >45 indicates high likelihood) or the Japanese Thyroid Association criteria, which emphasize thermoregulatory, cardiovascular, gastrointestinal-hepatic, and central nervous system involvement. Identifying and addressing precipitating factors through history, physical exam, and tests like blood cultures or imaging is crucial.
Management involves rapid initiation of supportive care in an intensive care setting, including intravenous fluids, oxygen, and cooling measures, alongside targeted therapies to inhibit thyroid hormone synthesis (e.g., propylthiouracil or methimazole), block hormone release (e.g., potassium iodide), control adrenergic effects (e.g., beta-blockers like propranolol), and prevent relative adrenal insufficiency (e.g., glucocorticoids). Treatment of the underlying precipitant is essential, and plasmapheresis or thyroidectomy may be considered in refractory cases. Prognosis has improved with prompt intervention, but mortality remains 8% to 25%, higher in older patients or those with multi-organ failure.

Background

Definition and overview

Thyroid storm, also referred to as thyrotoxic crisis, is defined as a rare, life-threatening exacerbation of thyrotoxicosis characterized by severe multi-organ dysfunction and a hypermetabolic state. It represents the most extreme manifestation of hyperthyroidism, where excessive thyroid hormone levels trigger systemic decompensation, often necessitating intensive care unit admission. Unlike chronic hyperthyroidism, thyroid storm involves a rapid onset of symptoms that can lead to cardiovascular collapse, hepatic failure, and altered mental status if not promptly addressed.
The condition was first described in the medical literature in the 1920s, with Frank Howard Lahey reporting it in 1926 as "the crisis of exophthalmic goiter," linking it to severe cases of Graves' disease. Early accounts highlighted its association with surgical interventions or infections in patients with underlying thyroid disorders, underscoring its historical recognition as a perioperative emergency. Over the subsequent decades, advancements in thyroid management have reduced its frequency, but it remains a critical entity in endocrinology.
Core features of thyroid storm include an exaggerated hypermetabolic response manifesting as high fever, tachycardia, and central nervous system disturbances such as agitation or delirium, driven by unchecked thyroid hormone excess. These symptoms distinguish it from routine hyperthyroidism by their acuity and potential for rapid progression to organ failure, often requiring immediate therapeutic intervention. Thyroid storm reflects its rarity yet high mortality risk of 10-30% even with treatment.

Epidemiology

Thyroid storm is a rare but life-threatening endocrine emergency, with an estimated incidence of 0.91 to 1.03 cases per 100,000 person-years in the general population in the United States (as of 2020). Among hospitalized patients, the incidence rises to 4.8 to 5.6 cases per 100,000 admissions per year. The condition disproportionately affects women, with a female-to-male ratio of approximately 3:1. Age distribution shows a peak incidence between 40 and 60 years, though cases can occur across all age groups, with an average age at presentation of 42 to 43 years.
Geographic variations in thyroid storm occurrence are linked to the prevalence of underlying hyperthyroid conditions, with higher rates observed in iodine-sufficient regions where Graves' disease is more common. Graves' disease accounts for 60% to 90% of thyroid storm cases, while other etiologies include toxic multinodular goiter and toxic adenoma. Key risk factors include untreated or poorly controlled hyperthyroidism, often precipitated by triggers such as recent thyroid or nonthyroid surgery, infections, trauma, or abrupt discontinuation of antithyroid medications.
Historically, mortality rates for thyroid storm ranged from 20% to 50%, but with advances in critical care and targeted therapies, contemporary rates have declined to 10% to 30%. Recent studies through 2023 report even lower in-hospital mortality around 5% to 8% in specialized cohorts, though overall figures remain elevated compared to uncomplicated hyperthyroidism; recent data indicate an increasing incidence, with mortality continuing to decline in treated cases, as low as 5.5% in multicenter registries (as of 2024). Approximately 90% of patients require intensive care unit admission for hemodynamic monitoring and multiorgan support.

Clinical presentation

Signs and symptoms

Thyroid storm manifests as a life-threatening exaggeration of hyperthyroid symptoms, characterized by profound metabolic derangements leading to multiorgan dysfunction. Cardinal features include high fever exceeding 38.5C (often 4041C or higher), marked tachycardia with heart rates over 140 beats per minute, gastrointestinal disturbances such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, and neurological alterations ranging from agitation and delirium to seizures or coma.
In elderly patients, thyroid storm may present atypically with apathetic features, including lethargy, stupor, and minimal signs of thyrotoxicosis, rather than the classic hyperactive state.
Cardiovascular signs are prominent and may include arrhythmias (such as atrial fibrillation), congestive heart failure with pulmonary edema or peripheral edema, and progression to hypotension or cardiogenic shock, reflecting the excessive circulatory demands imposed by hypermetabolism. Thermoregulatory failure contributes to hyperpyrexia, driven by unchecked increases in basal metabolic rate that overwhelm the body's heat dissipation mechanisms.
The Burch-Wartofsky Point Scale (BWPS) is a clinical tool used to assess the likelihood of thyroid storm based on observable signs, assigning points across key domains to guide diagnosis when distinguishing storm from uncomplicated hyperthyroidism or intercurrent illness is challenging. Precipitating factors

Thyroid storm typically occurs in individuals with underlying hyperthyroidism, such as Graves' disease or toxic nodular goiter, when an acute stressor overwhelms the body's compensatory mechanisms, leading to a sudden decompensation of thyroid hormone regulation. These precipitating factors disrupt the fragile balance in untreated or poorly controlled hyperthyroid states, resulting in the rapid release or enhanced effects of thyroid hormones. While most cases (approximately 75-80%) are triggered by identifiable events, 20-25% occur without an obvious precipitant, underscoring the importance of vigilance in high-risk patients.
Infections represent the most common precipitating factor, accounting for a significant proportion of cases, with examples including pneumonia, upper respiratory tract infections, urinary tract infections, and sepsis. These conditions impose physiological stress that exacerbates hypermetabolic states, often in patients with pre-existing hyperthyroidism. For instance, bacterial or viral infections can trigger an inflammatory response that amplifies thyroid hormone action.
Surgical procedures, particularly thyroidectomy or other non-thyroid surgeries in inadequately prepared hyperthyroid patients, are frequent iatrogenic triggers due to the associated anesthesia, tissue manipulation, and postoperative stress. Radioiodine therapy and administration of iodinated contrast media, such as during radiographic procedures, can also precipitate storm by causing acute release of stored thyroid hormones or inhibiting hormone synthesis temporarily, leading to a rebound effect. Excess iodine exposure from medications like amiodarone similarly contributes by providing substrate for hormone production in susceptible individuals.
Non-compliance with antithyroid medications, including abrupt discontinuation, is a preventable precipitant that allows unchecked progression of hyperthyroidism, often culminating in storm during intercurrent illnesses. Trauma, including burns or major injuries, imposes catecholamine surges that interact with elevated thyroid hormones to provoke decompensation.
Other notable triggers include metabolic disturbances such as diabetic ketoacidosis or hypoglycemia, which compound the hypermetabolic burden; cerebrovascular events like stroke; and obstetric complications, particularly labor, delivery, or postpartum periods in pregnant women with hyperthyroidism. These factors highlight how diverse stressors can precipitate thyroid storm by straining physiological reserves in predisposed patients.

Pathophysiology

Mechanisms of thyroid hormone excess

Thyroid storm represents an acute exacerbation of thyrotoxicosis characterized by severe effects of thyroid hormones, primarily through elevated circulating levels of free thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3). This exacerbation typically stems from a sudden increase in thyroid gland secretion of T4 and T3, often triggered by stressors such as infection, surgery, or trauma that disrupt follicular integrity and promote hormone release. In parallel, peripheral tissues contribute to the hormone surplus via enhanced conversion of T4 to the more biologically active T3, amplifying the hypermetabolic state.
A key mechanism involves the upregulation of type 1 deiodinase (D1), an enzyme predominantly expressed in the liver, kidney, and thyroid, which catalyzes the outer-ring deiodination of T4 to T3. This process accounts for approximately 80% of circulating T3 production in hyperthyroid conditions, and its activity intensifies during acute illness, leading to disproportionately high T3 levels relative to T4. Glucocorticoids and antithyroid drugs like propylthiouracil can inhibit D1, underscoring its role in sustaining hormone excess.
Circulating free hormone fractions are further elevated by reductions in thyroid hormone binding proteins, such as thyroxine-binding globulin (TBG), transthyretin, and albumin. Acute stressors like hypoalbuminemia from illness or inflammation decrease protein binding capacity, thereby increasing the unbound, bioactive free T3 and T4 available to tissues; normally, over 99% of thyroid hormones are protein-bound. Certain drugs, including salicylates or androgens, can also lower TBG levels, exacerbating free hormone availability.
Tissue-level amplification occurs through enhanced sensitivity to thyroid hormones, driven by mechanisms including upregulation of beta-adrenergic receptors in critical organs such as the liver, heart, and brain. This receptor sensitization heightens responses to thyroid hormones, promoting excessive gene expression for metabolic and thermogenic pathways without necessarily requiring further rises in hormone concentrations.
Thyroid storm is distinguished by severely elevated free T4 and free T3 levels, markedly higher than in milder hyperthyroidism; however, the rapid rate of increase and clinical context, rather than absolute values alone, correlate with severity.

Sympathetic nervous system involvement

Thyroid storm involves significant adrenergic hyperactivity, characterized by heightened sympathetic nervous system activation that amplifies the effects of excess thyroid hormones. This manifests primarily through increased sensitivity of beta-1 adrenergic receptors in the cardiovascular system, leading to pronounced tachycardia and hypertension as key clinical features. The surge in catecholamine responsiveness contributes to the hypermetabolic state, where even modest endogenous catecholamine levels elicit exaggerated physiological responses.
A critical feedback loop exists wherein thyroid hormones, particularly triiodothyronine (T3), upregulate the density and sensitivity of beta-adrenergic receptors on cardiac and vascular tissues, thereby potentiating sympathetic signaling. This sensitization enhances the binding affinity of catecholamines to beta-1 receptors, perpetuating a cycle of increased heart rate, contractility, and vascular tone. Additionally, proinflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-6 (IL-6) and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-), elevated in the context of precipitating stressors like infection, further augment sympathetic tone by promoting central and peripheral neural activation.
Clinically, this sympathetic overdrive results in an exaggerated response to stressors, often mimicking pheochromocytoma due to overlapping symptoms of severe hypertension, tachycardia, and diaphoresis. Studies have documented elevated plasma catecholamine levels in a substantial proportion of thyroid storm cases, underscoring the role of adrenergic excess in the syndrome's pathophysiology. The efficacy of beta-blockers in rapidly alleviating these symptoms provides further evidence of sympathetic involvement, as these agents effectively mitigate the hyperadrenergic state without directly addressing thyroid hormone levels.

Systemic organ effects

Thyroid storm induces widespread multi-organ dysfunction due to the hypermetabolic state overwhelming physiological adaptive mechanisms, leading to decompensation across multiple systems.
In the cardiovascular system, excess thyroid hormones drive high-output heart failure through increased cardiac output, tachycardia, and enhanced contractility, which can progress to atrial fibrillation in up to 20% of cases and, in severe instances, cardiogenic shock from myocardial ischemia or dilated cardiomyopathy.
Hepatic involvement manifests as acute liver dysfunction, with elevated transaminases such as AST and ALT often exceeding three times the upper limit of normal due to direct hepatotoxicity from thyroid hormones and congestive effects from heart failure; jaundice develops in 17-21% of patients, reflecting cholestasis and hyperbilirubinemia that worsens prognosis.
Renal effects include acute kidney injury, primarily from prerenal azotemia secondary to dehydration caused by fever, diaphoresis, and gastrointestinal losses, compounded in some cases by rhabdomyolysis leading to myoglobin-induced tubular damage and reversible renal failure.
Hematologic complications arise in severe thyroid storm, with disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) occurring rarely but critically, triggered by endothelial damage and hypercoagulability, resulting in thrombocytopenia, prolonged prothrombin time, and increased bleeding risk.
This systemic decompensation aligns with the allostatic overload model, wherein repeated stressorssuch as precipitating infections or surgeryexhaust adaptive responses of the thyrotropic feedback control, culminating in failure to maintain homeostasis and multi-organ collapse.

Diagnosis

Diagnostic criteria

Thyroid storm is suspected clinically in patients with known or suspected hyperthyroidism who present with fever and tachycardia, often accompanied by signs of multiorgan dysfunction. A high index of suspicion is essential, as the condition can overlap with sepsis or other critical illnesses, necessitating prompt evaluation to differentiate and initiate treatment.
The Burch-Wartofsky Point Scale (BWPS), developed in 1993, is a widely used clinical scoring system to aid in the diagnosis of thyroid storm by quantifying the severity of thermoregulatory, cardiovascular, gastrointestinal-hepatic, central nervous system (CNS), and precipitating factors. The Japanese Thyroid Association (JTA) criteria, established in 2012 and updated in the 2016 guidelines, provide an alternative diagnostic framework emphasizing the presence of a precipitating factor, underlying thyrotoxicosis (elevated free T4 or free T3), and manifestations of organ dysfunction. Definite thyroid storm (TS1) requires thyrotoxicosis plus either (1) CNS effects (e.g., delirium or coma) combined with at least one of fever 38C, tachycardia 130 bpm, heart failure, or gastrointestinal/hepatic dysfunction (e.g., jaundice with bilirubin 3 mg/dL), or (2) at least three of the latter manifestations without CNS involvement. Suspected thyroid storm (TS2) includes thyrotoxicosis plus two such manifestations or meets TS1 criteria without confirmatory thyroid hormone levels.
Challenges in diagnosis include the overlap of symptoms with conditions like sepsis, which may delay recognition without a high index of suspicion. Recent guidelines from the American Thyroid Association (ATA), including the 2016 hyperthyroidism management update and 2021 recommendations, incorporate the BWPS for clinical scoring, while the European Thyroid Association (ETA) aligns with similar approaches in broader thyrotoxicosis guidance without specific 2020s thyroid storm updates.

Laboratory and imaging findings

Laboratory findings in thyroid storm confirm underlying thyrotoxicosis and reveal multisystem involvement. Thyroid function tests typically show suppressed thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) levels below 0.01 mU/L, with markedly elevated free thyroxine (free T4) often exceeding 50 pmol/L and total triiodothyronine (T3) above 10 nmol/L, though free T3 levels may be disproportionately lower in critically ill patients due to non-thyroidal illness syndrome.
Additional laboratory abnormalities include leukocytosis, reflecting stress or precipitating infection; hyperglycemia exceeding 200 mg/dL from impaired insulin secretion and increased glycogenolysis; and elevated liver enzymes such as aspartate aminotransferase (AST) and alanine aminotransferase (ALT), often accompanied by hyperbilirubinemia due to hepatic congestion or direct thyroid hormone effects.
Inflammatory biomarkers like C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) are frequently elevated, indicating systemic inflammation, while cardiac troponin levels may rise in cases of myocardial stress or injury.
Imaging studies support evaluation of complications rather than directly diagnosing thyroid storm. Thyroid ultrasound may reveal an enlarged goiter, nodules, or increased vascularity consistent with hyperthyroidism. Chest X-ray can detect cardiomegaly, pulmonary edema indicating heart failure, or infiltrates suggesting pneumonia as a precipitant. Electrocardiography (ECG) commonly demonstrates sinus tachycardia, atrial fibrillation, or other arrhythmias.
Diagnostic pitfalls include potentially normal or only mildly abnormal thyroid function tests in early stages or among elderly patients, where apathetic presentations may mask typical derangements; thus, clinical suspicion should guide urgent management without awaiting confirmatory labs.

Differential diagnosis

Thyroid storm presents with acute hypermetabolic symptoms including fever, tachycardia, and altered mental status, necessitating differentiation from other life-threatening conditions that mimic these features. Key mimics include sepsis, pheochromocytoma, malignant hyperthermia, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, and acute coronary syndrome.
Sepsis often manifests with fever and tachycardia due to systemic inflammation, but lacks evidence of thyroid hormone excess on laboratory testing; procalcitonin levels are typically elevated in sepsis (>0.5 ng/mL) while remaining normal in uncomplicated thyroid storm.
Pheochromocytoma can cause paroxysmal hypertension, tachycardia, and sweating from catecholamine excess, but is distinguished by elevated plasma or urinary metanephrines, with normal thyroid function tests.
Malignant hyperthermia and neuroleptic malignant syndrome present with hyperthermia, rigidity, and autonomic instability, often linked to anesthetic or antipsychotic drug exposure, respectively; both feature markedly elevated creatine kinase levels (>1000 U/L) due to muscle breakdown, unlike thyroid storm where creatine kinase is normal or only mildly increased.
Acute coronary syndrome may mimic thyroid storm through chest pain, tachycardia, and hemodynamic instability, but is identified by electrocardiographic changes (e.g., ST-segment elevation) and elevated cardiac biomarkers like troponin, without thyroid dysfunction.
The primary differentiators across these conditions are thyroid function tests revealing suppressed TSH and elevated free T4/T3 in thyroid storm, alongside clinical response to antithyroid therapy, which is absent in mimics.

Treatment

Initial stabilization

Upon suspicion of thyroid storm, immediate stabilization focuses on supporting vital functions to mitigate the risk of rapid decompensation from multi-organ involvement, including severe tachycardia exceeding 140 beats per minute. The primary step involves assessing and securing the ABCsairway, breathing, and circulationwith particular attention to airway patency, as altered mental status or respiratory distress may necessitate intubation and mechanical ventilation. Supplemental oxygen is administered via nasal cannula or mask to maintain saturation above 94%, addressing potential hypoxia from increased metabolic demand.
Dehydration is common due to fever, diaphoresis, and gastrointestinal losses, warranting aggressive intravenous fluid resuscitation with 1-2 liters of normal saline initially to restore intravascular volume and support cardiac output. Hyperthermia, often surpassing 40C, requires prompt cooling to prevent further metabolic derangement; acetaminophen is used for antipyresis at doses up to 650-1000 mg every 6 hours, while salicylates must be avoided as they increase free thyroid hormone levels by displacing thyroxine from binding proteins. External cooling measures, such as ice packs to the axillae and groin, tepid sponging, fans, or cooling blankets, are employed concurrently to reduce core temperature without inducing shivering.
Patients should be admitted to an intensive care unit for continuous telemetry monitoring of vital signs, including heart rate, blood pressure, and cardiac rhythm, with vasopressor support initiated if hypotension persists despite fluid resuscitation. Agitation and delirium, which can exacerbate hemodynamic instability, are managed with benzodiazepines such as lorazepam 1-2 mg intravenously every 4-6 hours as needed, titrated to effect while avoiding oversedation. These interventions in the first 1-2 hours are critical to stabilizing the patient and preventing irreversible organ damage.

Thyroid hormone inhibition

Thyroid hormone inhibition in thyroid storm primarily involves the use of antithyroid drugs to block hormone synthesis and peripheral conversion, iodine to inhibit hormone release, and beta-blockers to mitigate sympathetic effects. These therapies are administered in a specific sequence to rapidly reduce circulating thyroid hormone levels and control hypermetabolic symptoms.
Antithyroid drugs, such as propylthiouracil (PTU) and methimazole, are the cornerstone of treatment as they inhibit thyroid peroxidase-mediated iodination of thyroglobulin, thereby blocking new thyroid hormone synthesis. PTU is preferred in thyroid storm due to its additional inhibition of peripheral deiodination of thyroxine (T4) to the more active triiodothyronine (T3), which can reduce serum T3 levels by up to 45% within 24 hours. The recommended regimen for PTU is a loading dose of 500-1000 mg orally or via nasogastric tube, followed by 250 mg every 4 hours; if oral administration is not feasible due to gastrointestinal symptoms, rectal administration or intravenous compounding may be used. Methimazole serves as an alternative, particularly after initial stabilization or in cases of PTU intolerance, with dosing at 60-80 mg daily divided every 4-6 hours, though it lacks significant peripheral conversion inhibition and reduces T3 levels more modestly (10-15% in 24 hours). Both drugs carry risks of hepatotoxicity (higher with PTU) and agranulocytosis, necessitating baseline liver function tests and monitoring. In hepatic impairment, PTU should be avoided if transaminases exceed three times the upper limit of normal, with a switch to methimazole preferred.
Iodine therapy, administered as Lugol's solution or saturated solution of potassium iodide (SSKI), exploits the Wolff-Chaikoff effect to transiently block organification and release of thyroid hormones from the gland, potentially reducing hormone secretion within hours. It is given at least 1 hour after antithyroid drugs to prevent iodine from fueling additional synthesis before inhibition takes effect. Typical dosing includes 5-10 drops of Lugol's solution (equivalent to 50-100 mg iodine) or 5 drops of SSKI (250 mg iodide) every 6 hours orally. This approach is particularly effective in hyperthyroidism due to excess hormone production but requires caution in iodine-sensitive patients.
Beta-blockers provide rapid sympathetic blockade to control tachycardia, tremor, and anxiety associated with thyroid storm's hyperadrenergic state, while high doses of certain agents like propranolol also inhibit peripheral T4-to-T3 conversion. Propranolol is commonly used at 60-80 mg orally every 4-6 hours or 0.5-1 mg intravenously every 10 minutes (up to 10 mg total) for acute control. In patients with heart failure or requiring intensive care, esmolol is favored as a short-acting intravenous infusion starting at a loading dose of 250-500 mcg/kg followed by 50-100 mcg/kg/min, allowing titration and rapid discontinuation if needed. For reactive airway disease, cardioselective agents like atenolol or metoprolol may be substituted. In renal impairment, beta-blocker dosing requires adjustment based on glomerular filtration rate, with reduced intervals or doses for propranolol in severe cases (e.g., creatinine clearance <30 mL/min).
The standard treatment sequence prioritizes antithyroid drugs first to halt synthesis, followed by iodine 1 hour later to block release, with beta-blockers initiated concurrently or as needed for hemodynamic stability. This multimodal approach, supported by clinical guidelines, aims to achieve euthyroidism within days while minimizing complications from organ dysfunction.

Adjunctive therapies

Adjunctive therapies in the management of thyroid storm include agents that support thyroid hormone inhibition, address relative adrenal insufficiency, and enhance hormone clearance, particularly in cases refractory to primary treatments. Corticosteroids are routinely administered to inhibit the peripheral conversion of thyroxine (T4) to the more active triiodothyronine (T3) and to provide stress-dose glucocorticoid support for potential adrenal suppression induced by excessive thyroid hormones. Hydrocortisone is typically given at a dose of 100 mg intravenously every 8 hours, while dexamethasone at 2 mg intravenously every 6 hours serves as an alternative, with therapy continued until clinical stabilization.
Cholestyramine, a bile acid sequestrant, is used to interrupt the enterohepatic circulation of thyroid hormones, thereby accelerating their fecal excretion and reducing serum levels more rapidly. The recommended dose is 4 g orally every 6 hours, often administered via nasogastric tube in severe cases. This therapy is particularly beneficial in hyperthyrotoxic states where enhanced clearance is needed alongside antithyroid drugs.
For refractory thyroid storm unresponsive to medical therapy, plasmapheresis or plasma exchange is employed to directly remove protein-bound thyroid hormones from the circulation, typically requiring 2-3 sessions to achieve biochemical improvement. This extracorporeal technique is reserved for life-threatening cases, such as those with multiorgan failure, and may bridge to definitive surgery like thyroidectomy.
Other adjunctive options include lithium carbonate, administered at 300 mg every 6-8 hours as an alternative to iodine-containing solutions in scenarios like pregnancy or iodine hypersensitivity, where it inhibits thyroid hormone release without the risk of fetal goiter. Activated charcoal is rarely utilized, primarily in cases of acute thyroid hormone overdose to bind unabsorbed drug in the gut, but lacks established efficacy in standard thyroid storm management. These adjuncts are indicated when frontline therapies fail or in special populations, such as pregnant patients where propylthiouracil remains the preferred antithyroid agent due to its safer profile.

Supportive measures

Supportive measures in the management of thyroid storm focus on addressing the systemic complications arising from severe thyrotoxicosis, including dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, and potential multiorgan failure. Intravenous isotonic saline is administered to correct dehydration resulting from fever, diaphoresis, gastrointestinal losses, and increased metabolic demands, while carefully monitoring for fluid overload in patients with concurrent heart failure. Electrolyte disturbances, such as hypokalemia due to transcellular shifts and gastrointestinal losses, require prompt correction with potassium supplementation to prevent arrhythmias and muscle weakness. Glucose supplementation (e.g., 5% or 10% dextrose) is added if hypoglycemia occurs, and serial monitoring of electrolytes, renal function, and cardiac status guides adjustments to avoid exacerbating heart failure.
Nutritional support is essential to counter the hypermetabolic state, with enteral feeding initiated if gastrointestinal dysfunctionsuch as nausea, vomiting, or diarrheaimpairs oral intake. Multivitamins, thiamine, and folate are provided to address potential deficiencies from poor nutrition and increased requirements. Patients should avoid iodine-rich foods, such as seafood and iodized salt, to prevent further stimulation of thyroid hormone synthesis.
Infection control is critical, as sepsis often precipitates thyroid storm; broad-spectrum antibiotics are empirically administered if infection is suspected, pending culture results from blood, urine, sputum, or other sites. This approach targets common sources like pneumonia or urinary tract infections while supporting overall recovery.
For organ support, mechanical ventilation is employed in cases of respiratory failure due to pulmonary edema or weakness, with close monitoring in an intensive care unit. Dialysis is reserved for acute kidney injury unresponsive to initial therapies, particularly when severe hyperthyroidism persists.
Ongoing monitoring includes serial laboratory assessments of electrolytes, renal function, liver enzymes, blood glucose, and thyroid hormone levels (free T4 and T3) every 24 hours to evaluate response to treatment. Transition to oral therapies typically occurs within 48-72 hours once the patient stabilizes, allowing for de-escalation of intensive interventions. Thyroid storm often involves systemic effects on multiple organs, such as cardiac strain and hepatic dysfunction, which these measures help mitigate.

Prognosis and prevention

Outcomes and mortality

Thyroid storm carries a significant mortality risk, estimated at 8% to 25% even with contemporary therapeutic interventions. This rate can exceed 30% in vulnerable populations, such as the elderly or those with underlying comorbidities like heart failure or renal impairment. In a national analysis of U.S. hospitalizations from 2016 to 2020, the overall in-hospital mortality was 5.3%, though it demonstrated an upward trend from 2.9% to 5.3% over the study period. Elderly patients over 60 years have significantly higher mortality rates, which can reach 30-50% in severe cases with central nervous system involvement or multi-organ failure.
Several factors predict poor outcomes in thyroid storm. Advanced age greater than 60 years, presence of coma or other neurological deficits at admission, cardiac arrhythmias such as atrial fibrillation, and delayed initiation of therapy are strongly associated with increased lethality. Additional adverse prognostic indicators include the need for mechanical ventilation, dialysis, or failure to promptly administer beta-blockers and antithyroid medications. Comorbidities like acute heart failure or renal failure further heighten risk.
Among survivors, clinical recovery typically occurs within 3 to 7 days of aggressive treatment, with most patients achieving hemodynamic stabilization and normalization of thyroid function in this timeframe. Long-term sequelae include a heightened risk of hypothyroidism following definitive treatments such as radioiodine ablation or thyroidectomy, affecting over 50% of patients within years post-event and often requiring lifelong levothyroxine replacement. Recurrence of thyroid storm is uncommon, occurring in less than 5% of adherent patients managed with appropriate underlying hyperthyroidism control.
Recent studies from the 2020s indicate potential mortality reductions in specialized centers employing protocolized care, with rates dropping below 15% through standardized multidisciplinary approaches emphasizing early recognition and bundled therapies. As of 2025 analyses, US in-hospital mortality has trended upward to approximately 7.6%, while a 2024 Japanese multicenter study reported 5.5% 30-day mortality.

Prevention strategies

Prevention of thyroid storm primarily involves proactive management of underlying hyperthyroidism and avoidance of known precipitants in at-risk patients. Patient education plays a central role, emphasizing adherence to antithyroid drug (ATD) therapy such as methimazole or propylthiouracil to maintain euthyroidism and prevent decompensation. Patients should be instructed to report early symptoms of worsening hyperthyroidism, including fever, tachycardia, or agitation, and to seek immediate medical attention, as abrupt discontinuation of ATDs can precipitate a crisis. Additionally, education on recognizing potential triggers, such as infections or medication non-compliance, empowers patients to mitigate risks through prompt intervention.
In the perioperative setting, particularly for thyroid surgery in patients with Graves' disease or other hyperthyroid conditions, preoperative preparation is essential to avoid storm induction. Patients should be rendered euthyroid using ATDs for several weeks prior to elective procedures, followed by potassium iodide (e.g., 5-7 drops of Lugol's solution three times daily) for 10 days to reduce thyroid vascularity and hormone release. Beta-blockers, such as propranolol, are routinely administered preoperatively to control adrenergic symptoms, with glucocorticoids added in urgent cases or ATD intolerance. For high-volume thyroid surgeons, this regimen significantly lowers complication rates in hyperthyroid patients.
Trigger avoidance focuses on minimizing exposure to factors that can exacerbate thyrotoxicosis. Infections, a common precipitant, should be addressed promptly in hyperthyroid patients through early antibiotic therapy or vaccination where appropriate, as untreated infections can rapidly escalate to storm. Careful use of iodinated agents, such as radiocontrast media, is critical; in patients with underlying hyperthyroidism, pretreatment with ATDs and beta-blockers is recommended before procedures involving these agents to prevent iodine-induced hormone release. Similarly, additional iodine exposure should be avoided in iodine-induced hyperthyroidism.
Regular monitoring of thyroid function is vital for patients with Graves' disease or other forms of hyperthyroidism to detect subclinical changes before they progress to storm. Thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), free T4, and total T3 levels should be checked every 4-6 weeks during ATD therapy, with more frequent assessment (every 2-6 weeks) in high-risk cases to guide dose adjustments and maintain euthyroidism. In clinical practice, electronic health records (EHRs) can incorporate alerts for patients with hyperthyroidism history, flagging potential storm risks during encounters for comorbidities like infections.
The American Thyroid Association (ATA) guidelines outline specific recommendations for high-risk groups, such as the elderly or those with cardiovascular disease, advocating aggressive pretreatment with beta-blockers and ATDs before interventions like radioactive iodine therapy or surgery to avert storm. In patients over 65 years or with heart disease, even subclinical hyperthyroidism (TSH <0.1 mU/L) warrants treatment to reduce decompensation risks. These strategies, when implemented, enhance outcomes by addressing vulnerabilities in susceptible populations.
