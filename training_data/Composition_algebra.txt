Split examples include RR\mathbb{R} \oplus \mathbb{R}RR (dimension 2) and the algebra of 222 \times 222 real matrices (dimension 4), both with zero divisors. These structures play a foundational role in the study of exceptional Lie groups, Jordan algebras, and the classification of quadratic forms, with applications in physics and geometry.
The concept originated in the work of Adolf Hurwitz in 1898, who proved that multiplicative identities for sums of squares hold only in dimensions 1, 2, 4, and 8, motivated by problems in invariant theory and quadratic forms. Subsequent developments by mathematicians like Max Zorn, Nathan Jacobson, and Tonny Springer extended the theory to arbitrary fields, incorporating automorphisms and connections to exceptional groups.

Definition and Fundamentals

Definition

A composition algebra over a field $ K $ with char(K)2\operatorname{char}(K) \neq 2char(K)=2 is defined as a finite-dimensional unital algebra $ A $ over $ K $, regarded as a vector space over $ K $ with a bilinear multiplication operation, that is equipped with a nondegenerate quadratic form $ N: A \to K $ satisfying the multiplicativity condition $ N(xy) = N(x)N(y) $ for all $ x, y \in A $. The quadratic form $ N $ arises from a nondegenerate symmetric bilinear form $ \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle: A \times A \to K $, defined by the polarization identity $ \langle x, y \rangle = \frac{1}{2} \big( N(x+y) - N(x) - N(y) \big) $, ensuring that the associated bilinear form permits the recovery of the quadratic form uniquely under the characteristic assumption.
The unital structure requires the existence of a multiplicative identity element $ e \in A $ such that $ ex = xe = x $ for all $ x \in A $ and $ N(e) = 1 $. This setup positions $ A $ as a left (or equivalently right) module over $ K $, with the multiplication being $ K $-bilinear, thereby integrating the algebraic and quadratic aspects into a cohesive framework.
Such algebras are restricted in dimension: over the real numbers, unital composition algebras exist only in dimensions 1, 2, 4, or 8, a fact that foreshadows deeper classification results without entering their proofs here.

Classification

Structure Theorem

A fundamental result in the theory of composition algebras is the classification of their possible dimensions. Over any field $ K $ of characteristic not equal to 2, every finite-dimensional composition algebra has dimension 1, 2, 4, or 8. This theorem, established by Nathan Jacobson, shows that no composition algebras exist in other dimensions under these conditions. The result extends to characteristic 2 via work of Tonny Springer, confirming the same dimensional restrictions.
For real composition algebras equipped with a positive definite norm, the dimensional constraint is captured by Hurwitz's theorem from 1923. This theorem asserts that the only possible dimensions are 1, 2, 4, and 8, as these are the sole cases where the maximum number of linearly independent squares $ n(\mathbb{R}^n) $ satisfies $ n(\mathbb{R}^n) \leq \rho(n) $, with $ \rho(n) $ denoting the Radon-Hurwitz number, which equals the maximum dimension of a subspace admitting a composition of quadratic forms. Earlier contributions by Frobenius in 1878 and Pfister in related contexts laid groundwork, but Hurwitz provided the definitive classification for positive definite cases.
The proof of the general structure theorem proceeds by induction on the dimension, leveraging the isotropy of the associated quadratic form and Witt decomposition. Specifically, for a composition algebra $ C $ over $ K $ (char $ \neq 2 $), the pure part $ \operatorname{Im} C $ admits a decomposition into isotropic and anisotropic components, allowing reduction to lower-dimensional subalgebras. Isotropy ensures the existence of non-zero elements with zero norm, facilitating the inductive step, while the alternative property preserves the composition relation. Zorn's vector matrix construction further demonstrates existence in dimensions up to 8 by embedding lower algebras into higher ones via structured multiplications.
Over algebraically closed fields, composition algebras of each allowed dimension are unique up to isomorphism. In dimension 1, it is the base field itself; in dimension 2, the split algebra $ k \oplus k $; in dimension 4, the split quaternion algebra; and in dimension 8, the split octonion algebra. This uniqueness follows from the classification of quadratic forms and the rigidity of the normed structure in such fields.

Division and Split Forms

A composition algebra is classified as a division form if its quadratic norm NNN is anisotropic, meaning N(x)=0N(x) = 0N(x)=0 if and only if x=0x = 0x=0. This ensures the absence of zero divisors and endows the algebra with the structure of a division algebra over its base field.
In contrast, split composition algebras admit nonzero isotropic elements, or null vectors, satisfying N(x)=0N(x) = 0N(x)=0 for x0x \neq 0x=0. These elements generate isotropic subspaces on which the restricted norm vanishes identically, with the dimension of a maximal isotropic subspace termed the Witt index. The presence of such subspaces distinguishes split forms and allows for nontrivial zero divisors within the algebra.
The norm NNN on a composition algebra AAA is a nondegenerate quadratic form, permitting a Witt decomposition into an orthogonal direct sum of an anisotropic kernel and a hyperbolic space composed of hyperbolic planes of dimension 2. Over R\mathbb{R}R, the norm on a composition algebra admits a Witt decomposition into an anisotropic part and a hyperbolic part. For division algebras, the norm is positive definite (anisotropic kernel is the algebra itself). For split algebras, the norm is hyperbolic (signature (d/2, d/2) where d is the dimension), with trivial anisotropic kernel; for instance, the split quaternions possess a norm of signature (2,2) and Witt index 2. Over R\mathbb{R}R, split forms exhibit indefinite norms.

Examples

Over the Real Numbers

Over the real numbers R\mathbb{R}R, unital composition algebras exist only in dimensions 1, 2, 4, and 8, with exactly seven isomorphism classes: the division algebras R\mathbb{R}R, C\mathbb{C}C, H\mathbb{H}H, and O\mathcal{O}O equipped with positive definite norms, and the split algebras Cs\mathbb{C}_sCs, Hs\mathbb{H}_sHs, and Os\mathcal{O}_sOs equipped with indefinite norms. This classification follows from Hurwitz's theorem on the possible dimensions for such algebras, extended to include split forms.
In dimension 1, the composition algebra is the field of real numbers R\mathbb{R}R itself, with the squared Euclidean norm N(x)=x2N(x) = x^2N(x)=x2, which is positive definite. This algebra is a division algebra, commutative, and associative.
In dimension 2, there are two composition algebras: the complex numbers C\mathbb{C}C as a division algebra with positive definite norm N(x+yi)=x2+y2N(x + yi) = x^2 + y^2N(x+yi)=x2+y2, which is commutative and associative; and the split-complex numbers CsRR\mathbb{C}_s \cong \mathbb{R} \oplus \mathbb{R}CsRR (also known as hyperbolic or perplex numbers), a split algebra with indefinite norm N(x+yi)=x2y2N(x + yi) = x^2 - y^2N(x+yi)=x2y2 of signature (1,1), also commutative and associative.
In dimension 4, the division algebra is the Hamilton quaternions H\mathbb{H}H with positive definite norm N(x0+x1i+x2j+x3k)=x02+x12+x22+x32N(x_0 + x_1 i + x_2 j + x_3 k) = x_0^2 + x_1^2 + x_2^2 + x_3^2N(x0+x1i+x2j+x3k)=x02+x12+x22+x32, which is associative but non-commutative; the split form is the split quaternions Hs\mathbb{H}_sHs (isomorphic to the algebra of 22 real matrices M2(R)M_2(\mathbb{R})M2(R)), a split algebra with indefinite norm of signature (2,2). Both are associative.
In dimension 8, the Cayley octonions O\mathcal{O}O form a division algebra with positive definite norm, which is alternative (satisfying the alternative laws) but non-associative; the split octonions Os\mathcal{O}_sOs are a split algebra with indefinite norm of signature (4,4) and Witt index 4, also alternative. Positive definiteness holds only for the division algebras R\mathbb{R}R, C\mathbb{C}C, H\mathbb{H}H, and O\mathcal{O}O, while associativity holds for all composition algebras in dimensions at most 4.

Over Other Fields

Over the complex numbers C\mathbb{C}C, which is an algebraically closed field, the composition algebras are classified into four types based on dimension as C\mathbb{C}C-vector spaces. The dimension-1 case is C\mathbb{C}C itself, a division algebra. The dimension-2 case is the split algebra CC\mathbb{C} \oplus \mathbb{C}CC. For dimension 4, the algebra is isomorphic to the matrix algebra M2(C)M_2(\mathbb{C})M2(C), which is split. The dimension-8 case consists of the complex octonion algebra ORC\mathbb{O} \otimes_{\mathbb{R}} \mathbb{C}ORC (bioctonions), a split algebra. These exhaust the possibilities, as established by Hurwitz's theorem adapted to this setting via the Cayley-Dickson construction.
Over finite fields Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq with qqq odd, composition algebras have dimensions 1, 2, 4, or 8 as vector spaces over Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq. The dimension-1 algebra is Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq, and the dimension-2 algebras are either a quadratic field extension (division) or the split form FqFq\mathbb{F}_q \oplus \mathbb{F}_qFqFq. Quaternion algebras (a,b)Fq(a,b)_{\mathbb{F}_q}(a,b)Fq of dimension 4 are always split, isomorphic to M2(Fq)M_2(\mathbb{F}_q)M2(Fq), since no non-trivial central division algebras exist over finite fields by Wedderburn's little theorem. For dimension 8, only the split Cayley algebra exists; there are no division octonion algebras, as finite alternative division rings are associative (hence fields) by the Artin-Zorn theorem.
Over ppp-adic fields Qp\mathbb{Q}_pQp, the classification of quaternion algebras (dimension 4) follows the Brauer-Witt theorem: a quaternion algebra (a,b)Qp(a,b)_{\mathbb{Q}_p}(a,b)Qp is division if the Hilbert symbol (a,b)p=1(a,b)_p = -1(a,b)p=1 and split otherwise. Octonion algebras (dimension 8) are obtained via the Cayley-Dickson construction from quaternion algebras and can be either unramified (ramification index eC=1e_C = 1eC=1) or ramified (eC=2e_C = 2eC=2), with the latter occurring when the underlying quaternion algebra is division. The local invariants, including ramification index eCe_CeC, residue degree fCf_CfC, and trace exponent rCr_CrC, determine the isomorphism classes.
In general, over a field kkk of characteristic not 2, every composition algebra has dimension 1, 2, 4, or 8, and its isomorphism class is determined by the anisotropic kernela maximal anisotropic subalgebraand the Hasse invariant of the associated Pfister quadratic form on the kernel. Split forms correspond to trivial invariants, while division forms arise when the kernel is a division composition algebra with non-trivial invariant.

Applications

In Pure Mathematics

In number theory, quaternion algebras over number fields are central to class field theory, where the structure of their maximal orders and ideal classes provides key insights into abelian extensions. Specifically, the Hilbert class field of a quaternion algebra BBB is an extension in which every two-sided ideal of BBB becomes principal, facilitating computations of class numbers and ramification behavior through the Artin reciprocity map. This connection extends to the Brauer group, where quaternion algebras represent 2-torsion elements that define the Brauer-Manin obstruction to the Hasse principle for rational points on algebraic varieties. For instance, on conic bundles or K3 surfaces, unramified quaternion algebras can obstruct the existence of rational points even when local solubility holds everywhere, as the pairing with the Nron-Severi group captures global inconsistencies.
In coding theory, the multiplicative norm on composition algebras yields tight bounds for sphere packings in Euclidean space, leveraging the preservation of the norm under multiplication to construct dense lattices. A prominent example is the Leech lattice in 24 dimensions, constructed as the set of triples (x,y,z)(x, y, z)(x,y,z) of integral octonions satisfying certain norm and orthogonality conditions derived from the E8 root lattice via octonionic extension; this lattice achieves the optimal sphere packing density in its dimension and underpins bounds for error-correcting codes like the Golay code. Additionally, over finite quaternion rings such as the Lipschitz or Hurwitz integers modulo a prime, cyclic codes can be defined using the quaternion Mannheim distance, enabling non-commutative error correction with decoding algorithms that correct up to a specified number of errors via idempotent generators and syndrome computations.
Jordan algebras benefit significantly from composition algebras, particularly through Albert's construction of the exceptional 27-dimensional simple Jordan algebra, known as the Albert algebra, as the space of 33 Hermitian matrices over the octonions equipped with the symmetrized product xy+yxxy + yxxy+yx. This algebra is formally real and arises uniquely from the octonionic structure, providing a cubic norm form that encodes its exceptional properties and connections to cubic hypersurfaces. Complementing this, Freudenthal triple systemsternary algebras defined on modules over composition algebras with a trilinear product preserving a symmetric bilinear formfacilitate the structuration of exceptional Jordan algebras and their derivations, as seen in systems built from octonion modules that yield the full automorphism structure.
The automorphism groups of composition algebras generate the exceptional Lie algebras and groups. The derivation algebra of the octonions is the 14-dimensional Lie algebra g2\mathfrak{g}_2g2, whose automorphism group G2G_2G2 preserves the octonionic multiplication table and the standard norm. Extending this, the automorphism group of the 27-dimensional Albert algebra over the octonions yields the 52-dimensional Lie algebra f4\mathfrak{f}_4f4, while constructions involving Freudenthal triple systems over octonion modules produce e6\mathfrak{e}_6e6 (78 dimensions), e7\mathfrak{e}_7e7 (133 dimensions), and e8\mathfrak{e}_8e8 (248 dimensions), embedding the full chain g2f4e6e7e8\mathfrak{g}_2 \subset \mathfrak{f}_4 \subset \mathfrak{e}_6 \subset \mathfrak{e}_7 \subset \mathfrak{e}_8g2f4e6e7e8.
In geometry, composition algebras underpin the analysis of Clifford modules through their relation to quadratic forms and spin structures, where the multiplicative norm enables explicit computation of spinor norms in the Clifford group. For quadratic spaces modeled on composition algebras like the quaternions or octonions, the spinor norm map from the orthogonal group to the multiplicative group factors through the Clifford algebra's units, providing obstructions to lifting orthogonal transformations to spin representations and clarifying isotropy in spinor modules over rings.

In Physics

Composition algebras, particularly the octonions, play a significant role in theoretical physics through their connections to exceptional Lie groups such as G2G_2G2, F4F_4F4, and E8E_8E8. These groups arise naturally from the algebraic structure of octonions and have been proposed in grand unified theories (GUTs) to unify the fundamental forces of the Standard Model. For instance, the automorphism group of the octonions is G2G_2G2, which embeds into larger exceptional groups like F4F_4F4 and E8E_8E8, providing a framework for models that incorporate all known particles and interactions under a single symmetry. In heterotic string theory, the E8E8E_8 \times E_8E8E8 gauge group emerges from octonionic constructions, offering a pathway to unify gravity with gauge interactions in ten dimensions, where octonions facilitate the embedding of fermion representations.
In supergravity and M-theory, octonions underpin compactifications of eleven-dimensional supergravity, enabling the emergence of exceptional symmetries. Compactifying on manifolds with G2G_2G2 holonomy preserves supersymmetry and leads to N=1N=1N=1 supergravity in four dimensions, with octonions providing the necessary algebraic tools for describing the geometry and fluxes. Similarly, the E8E8E_8 \times E_8E8E8 structure in heterotic M-theory arises from octonionic membranes, where the self-dual or anti-self-dual 4-form fluxes in eight dimensions connect to the Yang-Mills fields on boundaries, influencing low-energy effective theories. These constructions highlight octonions' role in resolving singularities and stabilizing moduli in string compactifications.
In quantum mechanics, quaternions describe the spin-1/2 representations of fermions, with the Pauli matrices corresponding to the imaginary quaternion units, providing a natural basis for spinor transformations under SU(2). This quaternion structure simplifies the representation of rotations for half-integer spins, as seen in the Dirac equation's non-relativistic limit. Split forms of composition algebras, such as split quaternions, extend this to indefinite metric spaces, relevant for relativistic quantum field theories like the Klein-Gordon equation, where the split signature accommodates Lorentzian geometry and indefinite inner products for scalar fields.
Recent developments include the 2023 construction embedding all Hurwitz algebras (division and split forms) within three-dimensional geometric algebras, leveraging Clifford algebra techniques to unify real, complex, quaternion, and octonion structures. A 2024 follow-up recovers composition algebras from 3D geometric algebras, further supporting applications in relativistic models by providing tools for spacetime descriptions and in quantum computing through efficient representations of quantum gates via geometric products.
Composition algebras also link to Clifford algebras, which generate Dirac operators whose spectra and index theorems classify anomalies and topological invariants in quantum field theories. The Hurwitz theorem on composition algebras determines the possible dimensions for Clifford module representations, directly influencing the index of the Dirac operator in even dimensions via Bott periodicity.

History

Early Developments

The study of composition algebras traces its origins to classical problems in number theory concerning representations of numbers as sums of squares. In the 3rd century CE, Diophantus of Alexandria explored equations involving sums of squares in his work Arithmetica, including problems that implicitly addressed decompositions into multiple squares, laying early groundwork for multiplicative norms in higher dimensions.
By the 17th century, Pierre de Fermat advanced these ideas with his theorem stating that an odd prime can be expressed as the sum of two squares if and only if it is congruent to 1 modulo 4, a result that highlighted the multiplicative properties of such representations and influenced subsequent investigations into norms over the integers.
In the 18th century, Joseph-Louis Lagrange proved in 1770 that every positive integer is the sum of at most four squares, establishing a foundational result on positive definite quadratic forms that served as a precursor to norm multiplicativity in four-dimensional algebras. Shortly thereafter, Leonhard Euler discovered the four-square identity in a 1748 letter to Christian Goldbach, demonstrating that the product of two sums of four squares is itself a sum of four squares, which linked dimension 4 to composition properties and motivated extensions to higher dimensions.
In 1898, Adolf Hurwitz proved that nondegenerate quadratic forms permitting composition exist only in dimensions 1, 2, 4, and 8 over the reals, providing a rigorous bound on possible algebra dimensions via bilinear identities for sums of squares.
The 19th century saw significant developments in explicit algebraic constructions. In 1843, William Rowan Hamilton introduced quaternions as a four-dimensional extension of complex numbers to handle three-dimensional rotations, where the normdefined as the sum of squares of componentsexhibits multiplicativity, satisfying the composition property for the algebra. Building on this, Arthur Cayley independently constructed the octonions in 1845, an eight-dimensional algebra with seven imaginary units that preserved norm multiplicativity despite losing associativity, further extending the pattern observed in lower dimensions.

Modern Advances

In the early 20th century, Leonard Eugene Dickson generalized the Cayley-Dickson construction through a doubling process for hypercomplex integers, enabling the recursive building of algebras like the octonions from lower-dimensional ones such as quaternions. This approach emphasized arithmetic properties and unit extensions, laying groundwork for formalizing composition algebras beyond associative cases.
By the mid-20th century, Max Zorn extended these ideas to alternative rings, introducing a parameter to construct split forms of composition algebras, where the quadratic form becomes isotropic, allowing zero divisors and yielding structures like split quaternions and split octonions. A.A. Albert further developed split octonions as 8-dimensional algebras over fields, linking them to exceptional Jordan algebras through isotopy and quadratic form compositions that preserve norms. Later, Nathan Jacobson comprehensively classified automorphisms of composition algebras, showing that their groups align with orthogonal, unitary, or exceptional types depending on dimension and field characteristics, thus unifying structural properties.
In the late 20th century, T.A. Springer advanced decomposition theorems analogous to Witt's for quadratic forms, applying them to composition algebras over fields of characteristic not 2 to decompose them into anisotropic and hyperbolic components, facilitating classifications over local fields. H.P. Petersson introduced non-unital composition algebras, such as Petersson algebras, which lack identity elements but retain multiplicative quadratic forms, expanding the theory to include 8-dimensional examples over rings and curves of genus zero. Susumu Okubo proposed further extensions like pseudo-octonion and Okubo algebras, non-unital 8-dimensional structures derived from symmetric compositions, offering new division algebra candidates with applications in particle physics models.
Entering the 21st century, John Baez highlighted connections between composition algebras and exceptional Lie groups, demonstrating how octonions underpin the automorphism groups G, F, E, E, and E through triality and Freudenthal constructions, influencing higher-dimensional gauge theories. Recent realizations embed all Hurwitz composition algebras within 3D geometric algebras using involutions to recover norms, providing computational frameworks for dimensions up to 8, including octonions and para-octonions. A 2024 study further explores such embeddings from 3D geometric algebras using reversion, inversion, and Clifford conjugation. Computational aspects have emerged in coding theory, where octonion-based lattices yield high-rate error-correcting codes with superior minimum distances over finite fields.
Post-2008 classifications over finite fields have refined structures, confirming that composition algebras are either split or division types isomorphic to Cayley-Dickson doubles, with explicit counts for small characteristics via anisotropic kernel analysis. In quantum information, non-unital variants like Okubo algebras model triality-symmetric states and autotopies in algebraic quantum field theory, enabling novel encodings for multipartite entanglement.
