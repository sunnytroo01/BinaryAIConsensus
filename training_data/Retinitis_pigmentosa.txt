 Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) is a group of rare, inherited retinal dystrophies characterized by the progressive degeneration of photoreceptor cellsprimarily rods and secondarily conesin the retina, the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye, resulting in gradual vision loss.
The condition typically manifests in adolescence or early adulthood with initial symptoms including night blindness (nyctalopia) due to rod cell dysfunction, followed by progressive loss of peripheral vision, reduced visual acuity, and difficulties with dark adaptation. In advanced stages, RP often leads to tunnel vision due to severe peripheral vision loss and can cause impairment of central vision, frequently resulting in legal blindness. However, complete blindness (total absence of light perception) is rare, and many patients retain some residual central vision even in later stages, with the rate and severity of progression varying widely among individuals and depending on the specific genetic form of the disease.
RP arises from mutations in over 90 genes that disrupt phototransduction, retinal structure, or the function of the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE), with the most common forms linked to genes such as RHO, USH2A, and RPGR. Inheritance patterns are heterogeneous, including autosomal dominant (3040% of cases, often milder progression), autosomal recessive (5060%, typically more severe), X-linked (515%, affecting males predominantly), and rare mitochondrial or digenic forms. Worldwide prevalence is estimated at 1 in 3,000 to 5,000 individuals, impacting more than 1.5 million people globally, with higher rates in isolated populations due to founder effects.
Diagnosis involves a comprehensive ophthalmologic evaluation, including fundus examination revealing characteristic bone spicule pigmentation, electroretinography (ERG) showing reduced photoreceptor responses, visual field testing to assess peripheral defects, and genetic testing to identify causative mutations. Differential diagnoses include other retinal dystrophies like Usher syndrome or Leber congenital amaurosis, which may overlap clinically.
Currently, there is no cure for most forms of RP, but management focuses on slowing progression and preserving function through supportive measures such as low-vision rehabilitation, and treatment of complications like cataracts or macular edema. High-dose vitamin A palmitate supplementation is not routinely recommended, as recent reviews find no high-quality evidence of benefit and note potential risks including liver toxicity. Gene therapies have emerged for specific subtypes; for instance, voretigene neparvovec (Luxturna), approved in 2017, targets RPE65 mutations via subretinal delivery to restore vision in eligible patients. As of 2025, ongoing clinical trials explore neuroprotective agents like N-acetylcysteine (NAC) in Phase 3, stem cell transplants, and additional gene therapies such as those for USH2A or RPGR mutations, offering hope for broader therapeutic options.

Overview

Definition

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) is a heterogeneous group of inherited retinal dystrophies characterized by the progressive degeneration of rod and cone photoreceptor cells in the retina. These disorders primarily affect the light-sensitive layer at the back of the eye, leading to gradual retinal cell loss over time. RP encompasses over 100 genetic subtypes, making it clinically and genetically diverse, with mutations in more than 90 genes implicated in its pathogenesis.
The degeneration in RP typically initiates in the rod photoreceptors, which predominate in the peripheral retina, before extending to the cone photoreceptors concentrated in the central macula. This sequential involvement results in a characteristic peripheral-to-central progression of retinal damage, distinguishing RP from other retinal diseases such as age-related macular degeneration, which predominantly impairs central vision early in its course.
The name "retinitis pigmentosa" derives from the distinctive bone spicule-like pigmentation observed in the fundus during clinical examination, a hallmark feature resulting from the migration of retinal pigment epithelial cells into the degenerating retina; this appearance was first systematically described in 1857 by Franciscus Cornelis Donders. In advanced stages, many individuals with RP progress to legal blindness, though the rate and extent vary by genetic subtype.

Classification

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) is broadly classified into non-syndromic and syndromic forms, reflecting its isolation to the retina or association with systemic conditions, respectively. Non-syndromic RP affects only the ocular structures, comprising the majority of cases and inherited primarily through autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, or X-linked patterns, with rare digenic inheritance involving mutations in two genes. Syndromic RP, accounting for 20-30% of cases, involves extraocular manifestations and is exemplified by Usher syndrome, characterized by RP combined with sensorineural hearing loss, and Bardet-Biedl syndrome, which includes RP alongside obesity, polydactyly, renal anomalies, and cognitive impairment.
Genetic classification of RP is based on over 90 identified causative genes, with mutations in these genes accounting for the disease's heterogeneity across inheritance modes. The rhodopsin gene (RHO) is a prominent example in autosomal dominant RP, responsible for 20-30% of such cases through more than 150 known mutations that disrupt phototransduction. In autosomal recessive and syndromic forms, the usherin gene (USH2A) is frequently implicated, causing non-syndromic RP or Usher syndrome type 2 via over 600 mutations that affect retinal and cochlear function.
Clinical subtypes further delineate RP variants by pattern of retinal involvement. Sector RP represents an atypical, often milder form limited to specific retinal quadrants, typically the inferior nasal region, and is more prevalent in autosomal dominant cases with slower progression. Reverse RP, also termed inverse or central RP, deviates from the classic peripheral onset by initially affecting the macula and central vision, resembling cone-rod dystrophy patterns in some genetic contexts.
Leber congenital amaurosis (LCA), a severe early-onset retinal dystrophy, is sometimes included in the broader RP spectrum due to overlapping genetic causes and photoreceptor degeneration, though it manifests with profound vision loss from infancy.

Clinical presentation

Symptoms

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) is characterized by a progressive degeneration of the retina that manifests initially through subtle visual impairments, most commonly beginning with night blindness, or nyctalopia. This early symptom typically emerges in childhood or adolescence, where affected individuals experience significant difficulty seeing in low-light conditions or adapting from bright to dim environments, often leading to challenges in activities such as driving at dusk or navigating dimly lit rooms.
As RP advances, patients report a gradual constriction of peripheral vision, which progressively narrows the visual field and culminates in tunnel vision. This loss of side vision can impair spatial awareness, increasing the risk of bumping into objects and complicating tasks requiring broad visual scanning, such as walking in unfamiliar settings or playing sports. The progression of this symptom varies widely, with some individuals noticing subtle changes in their teens while others experience more rapid deterioration.
In later stages, central vision impairment becomes prominent, accompanied by reduced visual acuity, photopsia (flashes or sparks of light), and in severe cases, profound vision loss approaching legal blindness. These symptoms can profoundly affect reading, face recognition, and overall independence. The onset of RP symptoms shows considerable variability, ranging from congenital presentations in rare cases to initial complaints in mid-adulthood, with the rate of progression influenced by genetic factorssuch as faster deterioration in autosomal recessive forms.

Physical signs

The physical examination of patients with retinitis pigmentosa (RP) typically reveals characteristic changes in the posterior segment of the eye, particularly during funduscopy. The classic triad of findings includes bone spicule-like pigmentation in the mid-peripheral retina, attenuation of retinal arterioles, and waxy pallor of the optic disc. These pigmentary changes consist of intraretinal clumps of melanin-laden cells that migrate from the retinal pigment epithelium into the retina, creating a spicule appearance primarily in areas previously rich in rod photoreceptors. Retinal vessel attenuation reflects vascular narrowing due to chronic retinal hypoxia, while the optic disc pallor arises from axonal degeneration in the optic nerve. Fundus findings are often highly symmetric between the two eyes.
In advanced stages of RP, macular involvement becomes evident and can contribute to central vision impairment. Common features include cystoid macular edema, characterized by fluid accumulation in the macula forming cystic spaces visible on optical coherence tomography, and epiretinal membrane, a fibrocellular proliferation on the inner retinal surface that may distort the macula. These changes occur in a significant proportion of patients and are associated with progressive visual decline.
Lens opacities are a frequent anterior segment finding in RP, with early-onset posterior subcapsular cataracts being particularly common, affecting up to 50% of patients by age 40. These cataracts appear as plaque-like opacities directly beneath the posterior lens capsule and can exacerbate visual impairment. In contrast, early anterior segment abnormalities, such as corneal or iris changes, are typically absent in RP.

Etiology

Genetic causes

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) is genetically heterogeneous, with more than 100 genes implicated in its pathogenesis, primarily those involved in phototransduction, retinal structure maintenance, or pre-mRNA splicing. These genes encode proteins essential for rod and cone photoreceptor function, ciliary transport, and visual signaling pathways, and mutations disrupt these processes, leading to progressive retinal degeneration. Other common genes include USH2A for autosomal recessive forms and RPGR for X-linked cases. For instance, mutations in the RHO gene, which encodes rhodopsina key protein in the phototransduction cascadeaccount for approximately 25% of autosomal dominant RP cases.
Common mutation types vary by gene and inheritance pattern. In RHO, missense mutations, such as the prevalent P23H variant, often cause protein misfolding, endoplasmic reticulum stress, and impaired trafficking to the outer segment, triggering photoreceptor apoptosis. For autosomal recessive forms, nonsense mutations in PDE6B, which encodes the beta subunit of the cGMP-specific phosphodiesterase 6 enzyme critical for phototransduction recovery, result in truncated proteins and loss of enzymatic function, affecting up to 5% of recessive RP cases. These examples illustrate how specific genetic defects target core visual processes, with over 3,000 distinct mutations reported across implicated genes.
Rare forms of inheritance include digenic RP, where compound heterozygous mutations in unlinked genes such as ROM1 and PRPH2 (also known as RDS) disrupt tetrameric protein complex formation in photoreceptor outer segments, leading to instability and degeneration. Mitochondrial inheritance is another uncommon mechanism, typically involving mtDNA mutations like m.8993T>G in the MT-ATP6 gene, which causes RP as part of the neuropathy, ataxia, and retinitis pigmentosa (NARP) syndrome.
Phenotypic variability in RP, even among individuals with identical primary mutations, is influenced by modifier genes that alter disease expression. For example, variants in ALG6, involved in protein glycosylation, have been shown to modulate severity in families with DHDDS (RP59) mutations by affecting protein stability and trafficking. Such modifiers highlight the complex genetic interactions underlying RP progression and underscore the need for comprehensive genetic profiling.

Inheritance patterns

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) is genetically heterogeneous and can be inherited through several patterns, with autosomal recessive being the most common, accounting for 50-60% of cases. Autosomal dominant inheritance represents 30-40% of cases, while X-linked inheritance comprises 5-15%. Approximately 10-20% of cases are sporadic, lacking a clear family history, and rare instances involve digenic inheritance requiring mutations in two different genes.
In autosomal dominant RP (ADRP), a single mutated allele from one parent is sufficient to cause the disease, often exhibiting variable penetrance where not all carriers develop symptoms and onset can vary widely within families. ADRP typically presents with later onset, often in adulthood, and a milder progression compared to other forms.
Autosomal recessive RP (ARRP) requires two mutated alleles, one from each parent, and is associated with more severe symptoms and earlier onset, frequently in childhood or adolescence. This pattern shows increased prevalence in populations with consanguinity, where related parents are more likely to carry the same mutation.
X-linked RP (XLRP) primarily affects males, who inherit the mutated allele on their single X chromosome from carrier mothers, leading to severe early-onset disease. Female carriers may exhibit mild symptoms due to mosaicism or skewed X-inactivation, such as subtle visual field defects. For example, mutations in the RPGR gene are a common cause of XLRP.
Overall, ADRP tends to have a milder course and better preserved central vision than ARRP or XLRP, which are associated with faster progression to severe vision loss.

Pathophysiology

Photoreceptor degeneration

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) primarily involves the progressive loss of rod photoreceptors due to genetic mutations that disrupt the phototransduction cascade, leading to their apoptosis. Mutations in genes such as RHO (encoding rhodopsin), the key protein in rod phototransduction, often result in protein misfolding, which triggers constitutive activation of downstream signaling pathways even in the absence of light. This aberrant signaling causes prolonged elevation of intracellular calcium and cyclic GMP levels, ultimately activating cell death pathways in rods. For instance, rhodopsin misfolding, as seen in mutations like P23H, leads to retention in the endoplasmic reticulum and impaired trafficking to the outer segment, exacerbating phototransduction defects.
Following initial rod loss, cones undergo secondary degeneration through mechanisms including oxidative stress, metabolic alterations, and interactions with dying rods. The death of rods reduces oxygen consumption in the outer retina, leading to hyperoxic conditions that generate reactive oxygen species (ROS), which damage cone cells via lipid peroxidation and protein oxidation. Metabolic stress arises as cones rely on glucose supplied by the retinal pigment epithelium; rod degeneration disrupts this supply chain, causing energy deficits and accumulation of toxic metabolites in cones. Additionally, rod-cone interactions, such as the release of inflammatory signals or deprivation of trophic factors from rods, accelerate cone apoptosis, with studies showing that antioxidants like N-acetylcysteine can mitigate this oxidative damage and preserve cone function in RP models.
Neuroinflammation also plays a significant role in photoreceptor degeneration, with early activation of retinal microglia leading to cytokine release and phagocytosis of viable photoreceptors, thereby accelerating rod and cone loss. This microglial response, while initially protective by clearing debris, often promotes chronic inflammation that exacerbates degeneration in RP models and human tissues.
The cell death of photoreceptors in RP involves both caspase-dependent and caspase-independent pathways, with calpain activation playing a prominent role, alongside emerging evidence for regulated necrosis such as necroptosis and ferroptosis. Caspase activation, particularly of caspase-3 and caspase-12, occurs downstream of endoplasmic reticulum stress induced by mutant proteins, leading to cytochrome c release and executioner caspase engagement that cleaves cellular substrates for programmed cell death. Calpain-mediated cell death, triggered by elevated cytosolic calcium from dysregulated phototransduction, proteolytically degrades structural proteins and activates pro-apoptotic factors like AIF (apoptosis-inducing factor), often independently of caspases in RP models such as the rd1 mouse. Necroptosis, mediated by RIPK1/RIPK3/MLKL signaling, and ferroptosis, characterized by iron-dependent lipid peroxidation, have been implicated in photoreceptor demise, particularly under conditions of oxidative and metabolic stress, offering new therapeutic targets as of 2025. This cross-talk between calpains, caspases, AIF, and necrotic pathways amplifies photoreceptor loss, with inhibitors demonstrating protective effects against degeneration in preclinical studies.
Degeneration in RP typically begins in the mid-peripheral retina, where rod density is highest, before progressing centrally and peripherally, initially sparing the macula to preserve central vision. This pattern reflects the vulnerability of mid-peripheral rods to genetic insults in the phototransduction cascade, with bone spicule pigmentation and atrophy first appearing in this region. Over time, the wave of cell death encroaches on the macula, but early-stage foveal cones remain relatively intact due to their lower reliance on rod-derived factors.

Retinal remodeling

Retinal remodeling refers to the complex, progressive structural and functional adaptations in the surviving retinal layers following the initial loss of photoreceptors in retinitis pigmentosa (RP). This process, initiated by deafferentation from dying photoreceptors, involves widespread cellular and synaptic changes that alter the retina's architecture and impair potential therapeutic interventions.
Mller cell gliosis represents a hallmark of early remodeling, where these radial glial cells hypertrophy and proliferate in response to photoreceptor degeneration, forming a gliotic scaffold that disrupts normal retinal lamination. This gliosis is characterized by upregulation of glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) and other intermediate filaments, leading to fibrous scarring and metabolic shifts that support surviving neurons but also contribute to circuit dysfunction. In parallel, neuronal rewiring occurs in the inner retina, with bipolar and horizontal cells extending ectopic neurites, forming abnormal synaptic connections, and undergoing translocation that reorganizes the inner plexiform layer. These changes, observed as early as adolescence in RP models, create dysregulated neural circuits that propagate aberrant signals to the brain, complicating visual processing. Microglial activation further contributes to remodeling by promoting inflammatory signaling and synaptic pruning in the inner retina.
Vascular attenuation accompanies these neural alterations, with progressive narrowing and loss of retinal blood vessels, particularly in the peripheral retina, due to reduced metabolic demand from degenerated photoreceptors. Concurrently, choriocapillaris loss manifests as focal flow deficits and atrophy in the choroidal capillary bed, detectable via optical coherence tomography angiography, which correlates with the extent of outer retinal damage and further exacerbates ischemia in surviving tissue.
Bone spicule pigmentation arises from the migration of retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) cells, laden with melanin, into the inner retina along perivascular routes, forming characteristic intraretinal deposits that mimic bone spicules on fundoscopy. This migration is triggered by breaches in Bruch's membrane and outer retinal integrity, allowing RPE cells to invade the neural retina and accumulate near vessels, a process confirmed histopathologically in RP eyes.
The formation of ectopic synapses during remodeling poses significant challenges for retinal prostheses, as aberrant wiring in the inner retina can lead to misdirected electrical stimulation, phosphene distortion, and reduced prosthetic efficacy. These ectopic connections, including novel neurite fascicles and microneuromas, emerge in surviving bipolar and ganglion cells, potentially generating noise in prosthetic outputs and limiting restoration of patterned vision.

Diagnosis

Clinical assessment

The clinical assessment of retinitis pigmentosa (RP) begins with a detailed patient history to identify potential genetic and symptomatic patterns suggestive of the condition. A thorough family history is essential, as RP is primarily inherited, with patterns including autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, or X-linked transmission, helping to guide suspicion of inheritance modes and prompt genetic counseling. Age of onset, often between childhood and early adulthood, and symptom progressiontypically starting with nyctalopia (night blindness) followed by gradual peripheral vision lossare key elements elicited to differentiate RP from other retinal disorders.
Ophthalmic examination commences with measurement of best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) using Snellen charts, which may be relatively preserved in early stages but often declines as macular involvement progresses. Confrontation visual field testing reveals characteristic concentric constriction, reflecting mid-peripheral scotomas due to rod photoreceptor loss. Slit-lamp biomicroscopy evaluates the anterior segment for posterior subcapsular cataracts, a common early comorbidity in RP that can exacerbate visual impairment.
Dilated funduscopy is pivotal, disclosing the classic triad of intraretinal bone spicule-like pigmentation, attenuation of retinal arterioles, and waxy pallor of the optic disc, particularly in the mid-peripheral retina. Color vision testing frequently demonstrates deficits, especially in blue-yellow discrimination, using tests such as the Farnsworth-Munsell 100-hue test. Contrast sensitivity assessment, using charts like Pelli-Robson, uncovers early functional impairments not evident in standard acuity measures, correlating with photoreceptor dysfunction.
These clinical findings raise suspicion for RP, which may be confirmed through specialized tests such as electroretinography.

Diagnostic tests

Electroretinography (ERG) is a key electrophysiological test used to assess retinal function in suspected cases of retinitis pigmentosa (RP). It measures the electrical responses of photoreceptors and bipolar cells to light stimuli, revealing characteristic abnormalities such as reduced amplitudes of the a-wave (originating from photoreceptors) and b-wave (from bipolar cells), along with delayed implicit times in RP patients. These findings confirm photoreceptor dysfunction and help differentiate RP from other retinal disorders, with full-field ERG often showing non-detectable responses in advanced stages.
Visual field perimetry evaluates the extent of peripheral vision loss, a hallmark of RP progression. Automated or manual perimetry typically demonstrates mid-peripheral ring scotomas that expand centrally and peripherally over time, leading to progressive constriction of the visual field. This test quantifies the severity of field defects and monitors disease advancement, with early detection of scotomas aiding in timely diagnosis.
Optical coherence tomography (OCT) provides high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the retina, essential for structural assessment in RP. It commonly shows thinning of the outer nuclear layer due to photoreceptor loss and may reveal cystoid macular edema in up to 50% of cases, appearing as intraretinal fluid cysts. These OCT features correlate with functional decline and assist in identifying complications like macular involvement.
Fundus autofluorescence (FAF) imaging highlights lipofuscin distribution in the retinal pigment epithelium, offering insights into RP progression. In RP, FAF often displays a parafoveal hyperautofluorescent ring that demarcates viable from degenerating retina, with surrounding hypoautofluorescence indicating advanced atrophy; this ring constricts over time as a biomarker of disease spread. Wide-field FAF further characterizes peripheral changes, enhancing diagnostic precision.
Genetic testing is crucial for confirming RP etiology by identifying causative mutations in over 80 known genes. Next-generation sequencing panels target common RP-associated genes like RPGR, RHO, and USH2A, enabling molecular diagnosis in approximately 60-70% of cases and informing inheritance patterns or eligibility for targeted therapies. This test is particularly valuable when clinical findings, such as fundus changes, suggest RP but require genetic corroboration.

Management

Supportive measures

Supportive measures for retinitis pigmentosa (RP) primarily focus on rehabilitation strategies to optimize the use of residual vision and mitigate factors that may accelerate disease progression. These interventions do not alter the underlying retinal degeneration but significantly enhance quality of life by addressing functional limitations. Low-vision rehabilitation, including the use of optical aids such as magnifiers and electronic devices, helps patients perform daily activities more effectively.
Orientation and mobility training are essential components of supportive care, teaching individuals with RP to navigate environments safely using canes, guide dogs, or environmental cues. Occupational therapy supports adaptation to vision loss by modifying home and work settings, such as improving lighting and contrast to maximize visual efficiency. These multidisciplinary approaches, often provided by specialized low-vision clinics, emphasize practical skills to maintain independence.
Lifestyle modifications play a key role in preserving vision, with recommendations to wear ultraviolet (UV)-blocking sunglasses to reduce phototoxic damage from light exposure, which can exacerbate retinal cell loss in RP. Smoking cessation is advised, as tobacco use increases oxidative stress and may hasten photoreceptor degeneration. Patients are encouraged to limit exposure to bright sunlight and avoid retinotoxic substances where possible.
For RP patients developing visually significant cataracts, which occur in up to 50% of cases and can further impair central vision, cataract surgery is often beneficial. Phacoemulsification with intraocular lens implantation can improve visual acuity and contrast sensitivity, though outcomes vary based on the stage of RP and intraoperative risks like posterior capsule rupture. Postoperative monitoring is crucial due to the elevated risk of complications such as cystoid macular edema (CME).
CME affects approximately 10-20% of RP patients and can cause central vision blurring; regular optical coherence tomography screening is recommended, particularly after surgery or during disease progression. If detected, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (CAIs) such as oral acetazolamide or topical dorzolamide are first-line treatments, with studies showing resolution or reduction in macular thickening in over 60% of responsive cases. These agents work by improving retinal fluid dynamics, though long-term use requires monitoring for systemic side effects.
Patients with RP should be referred to genetic counseling to understand inheritance risks and family implications, especially given the genetic basis of the condition.

Pharmacological interventions

Pharmacological interventions for retinitis pigmentosa (RP) primarily focus on nutritional supplements and medications to slow disease progression or manage associated complications, such as cystoid macular edema (CME). These approaches are supported by randomized clinical trials and are recommended for select patients based on genotype and disease stage, with regular monitoring to mitigate potential side effects.
Early studies suggested that vitamin A palmitate supplementation at a dose of 15,000 IU per day might slow the decline in retinal function in adults with typical forms of autosomal dominant and recessive RP. A multicenter randomized trial involving 601 patients demonstrated a modest reduction in the annual loss of cone electroretinogram (ERG) amplitude compared to untreated controls over 4 to 6 years. However, a 2023 re-analysis of the data found no significant clinical benefit on vision loss, and current guidelines from the American Academy of Ophthalmology (as of 2024) do not recommend high-dose vitamin A supplementation for RP due to insufficient high-quality evidence and potential risks such as hepatotoxicity and reduced bone density. Patients currently on this regimen should consult their physician regarding continuation, with baseline and annual liver function tests advised. This intervention is not recommended for those with liver disease, hypervitaminosis A, or certain genotypes like ABCA4-related RP.
Docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) supplementation, an omega-3 fatty acid, has shown limited and inconsistent benefits in slowing RP progression when added to vitamin A therapy. A 4-year randomized trial of 221 patients receiving 1,200 mg/day DHA found no overall slowing of visual field loss or ERG decline, though a subset analysis suggested modest benefits in the first 2 years for X-linked RP cases. As a result, DHA is not routinely recommended for most RP patients due to insufficient evidence of long-term efficacy.
N-acetylcysteine (NAC), an antioxidant agent, is under investigation as a potential neuroprotective therapy to slow RP progression by reducing oxidative stress in retinal cells. The Phase III "NAC Attack" trial (NCT05537220), a multicenter, randomized, double-masked, placebo-controlled study sponsored by Johns Hopkins University with support from the National Eye Institute and international collaborators, is evaluating the efficacy and safety of oral N-acetylcysteine (1800 mg twice daily) in slowing cone degeneration in patients with retinitis pigmentosa. The trial began on October 11, 2023, is active but not recruiting, with an enrollment of 483 participants, and has estimated primary and study completion dates in May 2029. No results have been posted as of the last update on January 12, 2026. Results are anticipated to guide future recommendations.
Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors, such as oral acetazolamide (250 mg twice daily) or topical dorzolamide, are used to treat CME, a common complication in up to 50% of RP patients that can impair central vision. A prospective masked trial in 12 RP patients with chronic CME reported significant reduction in macular thickening and improvement in visual acuity after 2 weeks of acetazolamide treatment compared to placebo. Oral acetazolamide is considered first-line for this indication, with topical alternatives preferred for patients intolerant to systemic side effects like paresthesia or metabolic acidosis; however, rebound edema may occur with prolonged use.
High-dose vitamin E supplementation (400 IU/day) should be avoided in RP patients, as it has been associated with accelerated disease progression. The same 1993 trial that supported vitamin A use found that vitamin E doubled the rate of ERG amplitude decline over 6 years, prompting recommendations against its routine intake beyond normal dietary levels.

Gene therapy

Gene therapy for retinitis pigmentosa (RP) aims to address the underlying genetic defects by delivering functional genes to retinal cells, primarily using adeno-associated virus (AAV) vectors to target photoreceptors or retinal pigment epithelium (RPE). This approach has shown promise in halting or reversing vision loss in specific genetic subtypes of RP, particularly those caused by mutations in genes like RPE65 and CEP290. Unlike traditional treatments, gene therapy seeks to provide a one-time intervention that restores protein function, potentially offering long-term benefits.
A landmark advancement is Luxturna (voretigene neparvovec-rzyl), the first FDA-approved gene therapy for an inherited retinal dystrophy, including RP associated with biallelic RPE65 mutations. Approved on December 19, 2017 by the FDA and in October 2020 by Health Canada, Luxturna uses an AAV2 vector to deliver a functional copy of the RPE65 gene via subretinal injection, enabling the production of the RPE65 protein essential for the visual cycle. Clinical trials demonstrated significant improvements in multi-luminance mobility testing (MLMT) scores, with patients able to navigate courses at lower light levels post-treatment, restoring or significantly improving night vision in RPE65-related cases. Patients have reported enhanced ability to see in low light, including seeing stars for the first time and navigating dark areas. Long-term follow-up has confirmed sustained benefits for up to four years in some patients, though efficacy is limited to those with viable retinal cells.
Optogenetic gene therapies represent another innovative strategy, particularly for advanced RP where photoreceptors are largely lost, by rendering surviving inner retinal cells light-sensitive regardless of the underlying mutation. Nanoscope Therapeutics' MCO-010, an AAV2-based therapy expressing a multi-characteristic opsin (MCO) in bipolar cells, targets late-stage RP. In the phase 2b RESTORE trial (completed in 2024), intravitreal administration of MCO-010 led to statistically significant improvements in best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) and full-field stimulus threshold (FST) compared to sham controls, with durable effects observed up to three years in follow-up studies. The therapy's mutation-agnostic design broadens its potential applicability across RP subtypes.
CRISPR-based gene editing offers a precise method for correcting mutations in vivo, moving beyond gene replacement to directly modify defective DNA sequences. For RP-related conditions like Leber congenital amaurosis type 10 (LCA10) due to CEP290 mutations, Editas Medicine's EDIT-101 employs CRISPR/Cas9 delivered via subretinal AAV5 to excise a deep intronic mutation (IVS26). The phase 1/2 BRILLIANCE trial, reported in 2024, showed EDIT-101 was well-tolerated with no serious adverse events related to editing, and dose-dependent improvements in pupillary light responses and mobility testing suggested enhanced photoreceptor function. While still in early stages, this approach holds potential for broader RP applications, including dominant mutations, pending further trials.
Despite these advances, gene therapy for RP faces significant challenges, including AAV vector tropism limitations that restrict efficient targeting of specific retinal layers, potentially reducing transgene expression. Immune responses to viral capsids can trigger inflammation, necessitating immunosuppression and limiting repeat dosing. Additionally, therapies are most effective in early disease stages when sufficient target cells remain viable, as advanced degeneration reduces the therapeutic window and efficacy. Ongoing research focuses on next-generation vectors and non-viral delivery to mitigate these hurdles.

Retinal prostheses

Retinal prostheses are implantable electronic devices designed to bypass degenerated photoreceptors in retinitis pigmentosa (RP) by electrically stimulating surviving inner retinal cells, thereby restoring a rudimentary form of vision. These systems typically consist of an external camera-mounted on glasses that captures visual information, processes it via a video processing unit, and transmits it wirelessly to an implanted electrode array that delivers patterned electrical pulses to the retina. For RP patients with advanced disease and bare or no light perception, such devices offer the potential for light detection, motion sensing, and basic object localization, though they do not replicate natural color or high-resolution sight.
The Argus II, developed by Second Sight Medical Products and now under Cortigent, is an epiretinal prosthesis approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 2013 under a humanitarian device exemption for adults aged 25 years or older with severe to profound RP and bare or no light perception. The system features a 60-electrode array surgically attached to the inner surface of the retina via a retinal tack, with an external inductive coil powering the implant and transmitting video signals. Implantation involves vitrectomy, retinal detachment to position the array, and external component placement behind the ear. Clinical trials demonstrated that Argus II enables patients to perceive light patterns, localize objects with nearly 100% improvement over no device, and perform motion discrimination in about 65% of cases, including basic recognition of large objects like doors or vehicles. Production was halted in 20202021 due to financial challenges. Cortigent acquired the technology in 2022 but has discontinued marketing Argus II, redirecting resources to next-generation visual prostheses such as the Orion cortical implant.
Another notable device is the PRIMA system, originally developed by Pixium Vision (now part of Science Corporation), which employs a subretinal photovoltaic array of 378 micropixels that directly converts incident light into electrical stimulation without requiring intraocular wiring or batteries. CE-marked in 2021 for use in Europe, PRIMA targets the subretinal space to stimulate bipolar cells more proximally than epiretinal approaches, potentially yielding higher resolution for tasks like reading large print or navigating obstacles. Early feasibility studies in RP and age-related macular degeneration patients showed restoration of form vision, with prosthetic acuity reaching up to 20/98 in some cases, allowing letter recognition and improved mobility.
Clinical outcomes from retinal prostheses like Argus II and PRIMA indicate modest improvements in functional vision, particularly navigation in low-light or structured environments, where patients can better detect edges and motion compared to off-device conditions. However, these devices do not provide fine visual acuity sufficient for activities like face recognition or detailed reading, with phosphene patterns often requiring interpretation as low-resolution grayscale images. Complications, while generally manageable, include conjunctival erosion (affecting up to 55% of Argus II cases, often in the inferotemporal quadrant), endophthalmitis, vitreous hemorrhage, and device migration, with most serious events occurring within the first year post-implantation.
Limitations of retinal prostheses stem from the need for an intact inner retina to transmit signals to the optic nerve, as extensive remodeling in late-stage RP can reduce efficacy; additionally, patients require extensive rehabilitative trainingoften months of visual and mobility exercisesto adapt to the unnatural phosphene-based vision and maximize utility.

Cell-based therapies

Cell-based therapies for retinitis pigmentosa (RP) aim to replace degenerated photoreceptors and retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) cells through transplantation of stem cell-derived or progenitor cells, potentially restoring visual function by integrating into the retinal architecture or providing neuroprotective support. These approaches leverage the regenerative potential of various cell types, including retinal progenitor cells (RPCs), bone marrow-derived stem cells (BMSCs), and induced pluripotent stem cell (iPSC)-derived photoreceptors, administered primarily via intravitreal or subretinal injection. While preclinical studies have demonstrated cell survival and partial functional recovery in animal models of RP, clinical translation remains challenged by issues of cell integration, immune compatibility, and long-term efficacy.
One prominent example is jCell (famzeretcel), an allogeneic RPC therapy developed by jCyte Inc., involving intravitreal injection of human fetal-derived retinal progenitor cells to deliver trophic factors and potentially migrate to the retina. A Phase I/IIa safety study published in 2025 reported that doses up to 5.5 million cells were well-tolerated in 21 adults with advanced RP, with no serious adverse events related to the injection and evidence of improved visual function in some participants, including better mobility and reading speeds. Building on this, the ongoing Phase 2b JC02-88 trial, initiated in August 2025, evaluates a higher dose of 8.8 million cells in up to 60 patients with RP, focusing on safety over six months and preliminary efficacy in visual acuity and contrast sensitivity. Early results suggest potential benefits in stabilizing or improving peripheral vision in late-stage disease.
Bone marrow-derived stem cells (BMSCs), often autologous and delivered intravitreally, have been explored for their neuroprotective effects through secretion of growth factors like BDNF and VEGF, which may slow photoreceptor degeneration without requiring direct cell replacement. A 2018 clinical study involving 17 RP patients treated with autologous BMSCs reported modest improvements in visual acuity and field in 70% of cases at 12 months, attributed to paracrine signaling rather than cell integration. However, Phase I/II trials, including a 2024 UC Davis study using CD34+ enriched BMSCs, have shown mixed results: while safe with no tumorigenicity observed, functional gains were inconsistent, with some patients experiencing transient inflammation but limited long-term vision restoration in advanced RP. These findings highlight BMSCs' role as a supportive therapy rather than a curative one.
iPSC-derived photoreceptors offer promise for autologous cell replacement, where patient-specific iPSCs are differentiated into rod and cone photoreceptors for transplantation, potentially addressing genetic heterogeneity in RP. Preclinical studies in RP mouse models have demonstrated partial integration of these cells into the outer nuclear layer and restoration of light responses, though challenges include low survival rates (less than 10% engraftment) and immature functionality due to epigenetic barriers. Clinically, early trials are emerging: a 2023 Japanese study transplanted allogeneic iPSC-derived retinal organoids subretinally in two advanced RP patients, confirming safety and stable graft survival for two years without immunosuppression, but with no significant visual improvement yet. In 2025, BlueRock Therapeutics' OpCT-001, an allogeneic iPSC-derived photoreceptor therapy, received FDA Fast Track designation for primary photoreceptor diseases like RP, with Phase I trials planned to assess integration in end-stage cases; similarly, a University of Wisconsin trial approved in late 2024 targets advanced RP using patient-derived iPSCs. These approaches emphasize the need for optimized maturation protocols to enhance efficacy.
Despite advances, cell-based therapies carry risks including immune rejection in allogeneic transplants, requiring immunosuppression that may lead to intraocular inflammation or infection; tumor formation from undifferentiated pluripotent cells, though minimized in differentiated populations; and limited efficacy in end-stage RP due to extensive retinal remodeling, which hinders graft-host connectivity. A 2021 review of over 20 clinical trials noted that while most reported no severe adverse events, 15-20% of patients experienced mild vitreoretinal complications, underscoring the importance of patient selection for earlier disease stages. Ongoing research focuses on mitigating these through encapsulation or genetic modifications for immune evasion.

Prognosis and complications

Visual outcomes

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) exhibits highly variable visual progression among patients, influenced primarily by the underlying genetic inheritance pattern. Autosomal dominant RP typically progresses most slowly, with mean visual field loss rates around 2.7% per year, while X-linked RP shows intermediate rates of approximately 7.2% per year, and autosomal recessive RP progresses the fastest at about 10.3% per year. In X-linked RP, patients are four times more likely to achieve visual acuity of 20/200 or worse (legal blindness) compared to autosomal dominant forms, with a median age of legal blindness at 45 years. Overall, across inheritance types, about 50% of patients reach legal blindness by age 55.
Central vision is generally preserved until the late stages of the disease in most RP cases, allowing patients to maintain reading and facial recognition abilities despite severe peripheral field loss. Complete blindness (absence of light perception) is uncommon in RP, as macular function typically preserves some residual light perception even after significant loss of visual acuity; no precise universal percentage is reported for complete blindness, which remains rare, with progression varying significantly by individual and genetic form. Legal blindness in RP is defined as best-corrected visual acuity of 20/200 or less in the better eye, or a visual field diameter of less than 20 degrees. A majority of patients become legally blind before age 60, though some retain useful vision into later decades depending on the subtype.
Key factors influencing visual outcomes include genotype, with X-linked forms often leading to more severe overall decline despite variations in specific progression measures. Secondary issues such as cataracts may accelerate vision loss if not addressed.

Associated complications

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) is associated with several ocular complications that can exacerbate visual impairment beyond the primary photoreceptor degeneration. Cystoid macular edema (CME) occurs in approximately 30% of patients, leading to fluid accumulation in the macula and potential central vision loss. Cataracts, particularly posterior subcapsular types, affect about 50% of RP patients by age 40, often requiring surgical intervention to improve visual acuity. The risk of retinal detachment is significantly elevated in RP, with prevalence rates up to several times higher than in the general population due to vitreoretinal interface abnormalities.
In syndromic forms of RP, additional systemic complications arise. Usher syndrome, which accounts for 10-20% of RP cases, is characterized by progressive hearing loss alongside retinal degeneration, affecting balance and communication in affected individuals. Bardet-Biedl syndrome, another syndromic variant, features truncal obesity and postaxial polydactyly in addition to RP, contributing to multisystem morbidity including renal and cognitive issues.
Vision loss from RP also predisposes patients to mental health challenges, with depression and anxiety occurring at rates 5-6 times higher than in the general population due to the psychosocial impact of progressive blindness. Recent 2025 analyses confirm an earlier onset of cataracts in RP patients, with a mean diagnosis age of 57.6 years compared to 64.0 years in controls, underscoring the need for proactive screening. Management of these complications, such as cataract surgery, can mitigate some effects but requires careful consideration of underlying RP progression.

Epidemiology

Prevalence and incidence

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) has a global prevalence of approximately 1 in 3,000 to 5,000 individuals, affecting over 1.5 million people worldwide as of 2025.
In the United States, prevalence stands at about 27 per 100,000 individuals as of 2023, with rates showing an increase from prior years due to improved diagnostic techniques and genetic testing.
Founder effects elevate prevalence in certain isolated populations; for instance, specific mutations in genes such as MAK and DHDDS are more common among Ashkenazi Jews, leading to higher rates of autosomal recessive RP in this group.

Geographic and demographic variations

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) exhibits notable geographic and demographic variations influenced by genetic inheritance patterns and population-specific factors. In regions with high rates of consanguineous marriages, such as parts of the Middle East, South Asia, and North Africa, the prevalence of autosomal recessive forms of RP is elevated due to increased homozygosity for recessive mutations. For instance, studies in Indian populations have identified consanguinity as a significant risk factor for RP and other inherited retinal disorders, with recessive variants appearing more frequently in such communities.
Ethnic variations in the genetic spectrum of RP are evident, with the distribution of pathogenic variants differing across populations. The PRPH2 gene (formerly known as RDS), associated with autosomal dominant RP, shows phenotypic and mutational differences by ethnicity; for example, RP is the predominant manifestation in Asian populations, whereas macular dystrophy is more common among Caucasians. While comprehensive data on African populations remain limited, the overall spectrum of RP-associated genes, including PRPH2, varies between ethnic groups, contributing to differences in disease presentation.
Age of onset in RP displays a bimodal pattern tied to inheritance mode. Autosomal recessive forms typically manifest in childhood or adolescence, often with more severe early progression, whereas autosomal dominant variants generally present later, in the second or third decade of life. X-linked RP, which accounts for a smaller proportion of cases, usually begins in the first decade.
Regarding gender, X-linked RP disproportionately affects males due to hemizygosity for the causative mutations on the X chromosome, leading to earlier and more severe disease in affected individuals. However, across all inheritance patterns, the overall gender distribution of RP is approximately equal, with autosomal dominant and recessive forms showing balanced male-to-female ratios, though a slight male predominance arises from the X-linked subset.

Research advances

Neuroprotective strategies

Neuroprotective strategies in retinitis pigmentosa (RP) focus on preserving the viability and function of surviving retinal cells, particularly photoreceptors, amid progressive degeneration. These approaches target secondary mechanisms of cell death, such as oxidative stress, which exacerbates rod and cone loss following initial genetic insults.
One prominent agent is N-acetylcysteine (NAC), a potent antioxidant that replenishes glutathione levels to mitigate oxidative damage in retinal cells. In RP, NAC has shown promise in preclinical models by reducing reactive oxygen species accumulation, thereby slowing photoreceptor apoptosis. The NAC Attack trial (NCT05537220), a Phase III, multicenter, randomized, double-masked, placebo-controlled study sponsored by Johns Hopkins University with support from the National Eye Institute and international collaborators, evaluates the efficacy and safety of oral N-acetylcysteine (NAC, 1800 mg twice daily) in slowing cone degeneration in patients with retinitis pigmentosa. The trial began on October 11, 2023, is active but not recruiting after enrolling 483 participants across approximately 30 sites in the Americas and Europe, and has an estimated primary and study completion in May 2029. No results have been posted as of the last update on January 12, 2026. The primary outcome measure is the progressive change in ellipsoid zone width (cumulative loss calculated as area above the curve) over 45 months, assessed via spectral domain-optical coherence tomography.
Ciliary neurotrophic factor (CNTF), a cytokine that supports neuronal survival, has been investigated via encapsulated cell technology (ECT) implants, which provide sustained intraocular release without immunosuppression. Preclinical studies in rodent and canine RP models demonstrated CNTF's ability to preserve photoreceptor layers by activating survival pathways like JAK/STAT signaling. A phase 2 randomized trial involving patients with early-stage RP implanted with CNTF-secreting ECT devices reported slowed cone photoreceptor loss over 18-24 months, as measured by adaptive optics imaging and electroretinography, though overall visual acuity improvements were not significant.
Rod-derived cone viability factor (RdCVF), a thioredoxin-like protein secreted by rod photoreceptors, promotes cone survival by enhancing glucose metabolism and reducing oxidative stress in preclinical models of RP. Studies in mouse models of rod degeneration showed that RdCVF administration preserved cone density and function for extended periods, with mechanisms involving increased thioredoxin activity and metabolic support to cones. While primarily in preclinical stages, recent biodistribution and safety data from viral vector delivery support its potential, though clinical translation remains pending.
Despite these advances, neuroprotective strategies face significant challenges, including optimizing delivery methods for sustained retinal exposuresuch as intravitreal injections or implants that risk inflammation or surgical complicationsand ensuring long-term efficacy beyond initial phases, where compensatory degeneration pathways may emerge. Ongoing research emphasizes combination therapies and biomarkers to address these hurdles for broader clinical impact.

Optogenetics and novel therapies

Optogenetics represents a promising restorative approach for retinitis pigmentosa (RP) by introducing light-sensitive proteins, such as channelrhodopsins, into surviving retinal cells to bypass degenerated photoreceptors and restore light responsiveness. This technique leverages adeno-associated virus (AAV) vectors to deliver opsin genes, enabling inner retinal neurons like bipolar or ganglion cells to detect light and transmit signals to the brain. Unlike traditional gene therapies that replace specific faulty genes, optogenetics is gene-agnostic, making it suitable for advanced RP where multiple genetic causes lead to widespread photoreceptor loss.
A leading example is Nanoscope Therapeutics' MCO-010, an AAV2-based optogenetic therapy expressing a multi-characteristic opsin derived from channelrhodopsin variants, which enhances sensitivity to ambient light without requiring external devices. In the Phase 2b RESTORE trial, a single subretinal injection of MCO-010 in 24 patients with advanced RP resulted in significant improvements in best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA), with average gains of approximately 0.3 logMAR (equivalent to 3 lines on eye charts), and mobility testing, with durable vision gains of about 3 lines sustained up to three years post-treatment, as reported in 2025 at EURETINA and the Retina Society Annual Scientific Meeting from the REMAIN extension study. Long-term follow-up from the Phase 1/2a EXTEND study confirmed safety over five years, reported November 2025, with no serious adverse events related to the therapy and enhancements in quality of life measures. As of July 2025, Nanoscope initiated a rolling Biologics License Application (BLA) submission to the FDA for MCO-010 in RP, building on prior Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy (RMAT) designation, while planning a Phase 3 registrational trial for related indications like Stargardt disease in 2025.
Mutation-specific gene replacement therapies have advanced in 2025, showing improvements in rod function and low-luminance vision for specific RP subtypes, thereby aiding night vision restoration or improvement.
eyeDNA Therapeutics' HORA-PDE6B, an AAV-based gene therapy delivering a functional PDE6B gene, demonstrated vision improvements in its Phase 1/2 trial for PDE6B-associated RP. Treated eyes exhibited stable BCVA over long-term follow-up (up to five years in low-dose patients) compared to decline in untreated eyes, with improved rod function indicated by blue light full-field stimulation testing, suggesting preservation or enhancement of night vision mediated by rods. The therapy continues evaluation in younger patients with earlier-stage disease.
Beacon Therapeutics' laru-zova (laruparetigene zovaparvovec), targeting X-linked RP due to RPGR mutations, reported positive interim Phase 2 DAWN trial results in 2025, including early improvements in low-luminance visual acuity (average 16 letters or about 3 lines in dim light) for treated fellow eyes and enhanced retinal sensitivity on microperimetry. These findings indicate potential restoration of rod and cone function, with benefits for low-light vision in XLRP patients. The therapy was well-tolerated.
Advancements in CRISPR-based gene editing offer targeted restoration for specific RP mutations, such as the P23H variant in the RHO gene, which causes autosomal dominant RP by producing misfolded rhodopsin leading to photoreceptor toxicity. Researchers at Columbia University have developed in vivo editing strategies using CRISPR/Cas9 to precisely excise or repair the mutant P23H-RHO allele in preclinical models, demonstrating reduced rod degeneration and preserved cone function without off-target effects. A September 2025 preprint detailed a related meganuclease approach (RHO1-2) that cleaves the human P23H rhodopsin sequence in induced pluripotent stem cell-derived retinal organoids, rejuvenating rod cells and maintaining cone viability in RP models. These preclinical efforts, ongoing as of 2025, highlight the potential for subretinal delivery of editing tools to halt progression and restore protein function in mutation-specific RP.
For Usher syndrome type 1B (USH1B), which manifests as RP with congenital deafness due to MYO7A mutations, AAVantgarde Bio's AAVB-081 employs a dual-AAV vector system to deliver the full-length MYO7A gene via subretinal injection, addressing the gene's size limitations of standard AAV capacity. The ongoing Phase 1/2 LUCE-1 trial (NCT06591793), initiated in 2024 and reporting updated data in 2025, enrolled patients aged 18-50 with USH1B-associated RP, evaluating three dose levels for safety and preliminary efficacy. As of September 2025, interim results from EURETINA showed the therapy was well-tolerated with no dose-limiting toxicities, alongside early functional improvements in visual acuity and retinal sensitivity measured by microperimetry. These findings support continued dose escalation in the multicenter, open-label study, positioning AAVB-081 as a novel dual-vector strategy for large-gene RP subtypes.
Beyond viral and editing approaches, cell-based innovations like photoreceptor transplantation aim to directly replace lost cells. BlueRock Therapeutics' OpCT-001, an investigational therapy using induced pluripotent stem cell (iPSC)-derived photoreceptor precursors, received FDA Fast Track designation in February 2025 for primary photoreceptor diseases including RP. The Phase 1/2a trial (NCT06789445) began dosing the first patient in July 2025, assessing subretinal transplantation in patients with advanced RP or cone-rod dystrophy to evaluate safety, engraftment, and visual function restoration. Preclinical data indicated that OpCT-001 cells integrate into the host retina, form synaptic connections, and respond to light, offering a regenerative path independent of genetic specificity. Another example is jCyte's jCell (famzeretcel), an allogeneic retinal progenitor cell therapy, which dosed its first patients in the Phase 2 JC02-88 trial in August 2025 at a higher dose than prior studies to improve vision in RP.
Complementing these, small-molecule and RNA-targeted therapies provide non-viral options for mutation-specific RP. PYC Therapeutics' VP-001, an antisense oligonucleotide designed to modulate splicing in PRPF31-mutated RP (RP11), demonstrated encouraging safety and efficacy in a Phase 1/2 trial reported at ARVO 2025, with improvements in retinal sensitivity and visual function in pediatric and adult patients; a November 2025 update confirmed sustained vision gains in treated patients. Following FDA alignment in June 2025 on registrational trial design, PYC plans to initiate a Phase 2/3 study of intravitreally administered VP-001 in late 2025, targeting the 5,000-10,000 individuals affected by this childhood-onset form of RP. This approach exemplifies how novel oligonucleotides can address RNA-level defects to preserve photoreceptor function.
