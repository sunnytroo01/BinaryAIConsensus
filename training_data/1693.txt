 1693 was a year of profound natural catastrophe and military confrontation during the Nine Years' War (16881697), highlighted by the Sicily earthquake on January 11the most powerful in recorded Italian history, with an estimated magnitude of 7.4, which devastated southeastern Sicily and southern Calabria, killing approximately 60,000 people and triggering the eruption of Mount Etna. In Europe, French forces under Louis XIV achieved a costly victory at the Battle of Landen (also known as Neerwinden) on July 29, where Marshal Franois-Henri de Montmorency, duc de Luxembourg, repelled an Allied army commanded by William III of England, Holland, and Orange, inflicting heavy casualties but failing to alter the war's strategic stalemate. Naval actions included the French capture of the Smyrna convoy in the Battle of Lagos on June 27, destroying or capturing over 90 merchant vessels worth approximately 1 million, representing a major blow to English merchant shipping.
In the American colonies, the College of William & Mary obtained a royal charter from King William III and Queen Mary II on February 8, establishing it as the second-oldest institution of higher learning in what would become the United States and advancing educational foundations amid colonial expansion. Other cultural milestones included the launch of The Ladies' Mercury on February 27, recognized as the first periodical targeted at women in England. The year witnessed births of influential figures such as British astronomer James Bradley, who later discovered stellar aberration, and clockmaker John Harrison, inventor of the marine chronometer; notable deaths encompassed French author Madame de La Fayette, whose works like The Princess of Clves influenced modern fiction.

Events

JanuaryMarch

On January 11, 1693, a magnitude 7.4 earthquake struck southeastern Sicily and parts of southern Italy, with its epicenter near Noto, causing the collapse of numerous structures and an estimated 60,000 fatalities across affected regions. A foreshock of lower intensity had occurred two days earlier on January 9, but the main shock lasted approximately four minutes, leveling over 45 towns and villages, including severe damage in Catania where between 16,000 and 18,000 people died. The disaster also triggered a tsunami that inundated coastal areas in the eastern Mediterranean, reaching as far as Malta and contributing to additional drownings.
On February 8, 1693, King William III and Queen Mary II of England issued a royal charter establishing the College of William & Mary in Williamsburg, Virginia, as a seminary of learning to educate youth in the Church of England doctrines and prepare them for ministry and public service. This institution became the second-oldest college in the American colonies, following Harvard, with the charter outlining its governance, curriculum, and perpetual endowment from colonial revenues.
On February 28, 1693, the inaugural issue of The Ladies' Mercury appeared in London, published by the Athenian Society as the first periodical directed explicitly at women, focusing on queries related to love, marriage, and domestic counsel. The short-lived publication, which promised anonymous advice from "experienced matrons," issued three subsequent numbers before ceasing on March 17, marking an early experiment in gender-specific print media amid the burgeoning English periodical press.

AprilJune

On 5 April, King Louis XIV of France established the Order of Saint Louis, a chivalric order intended to recognize military merit among officers irrespective of noble birth, marking the first such distinction in the French army.
During the Nine Years' War, French forces under Marshal the Duke of Noailles initiated the siege of Rosas, a key Spanish naval base in Catalonia, on 28 May; the port surrendered on 9 June after minimal resistance, providing France with a strategic foothold for further operations in the region.
On 27 June (8 July New Style), Admiral Anne-Hilarion de Tourville's French squadron intercepted the Anglo-Dutch Smyrna convoycomprising approximately 200 merchant ships laden with goods from the Levantoff Lagos, Portugal; in the ensuing Battle of Lagos, Tourville's 40 warships overwhelmed the convoy's escorts, capturing or destroying over 90 vessels and inflicting merchant losses estimated at 1 million, the war's most severe blow to English commerce.
In colonial Massachusetts, Governor William Phips ordered the release and pardon of remaining prisoners held on witchcraft charges by early May, effectively ending the Salem witch trials that had begun in 1692 and resulted in 20 executions.

JulySeptember

On July 4, French and English forces clashed with Dutch troops at Boussu-lez-Walcourt in the Spanish Netherlands during the Nine Years' War, marking an early skirmish in the summer campaign. This engagement preceded larger operations but resulted in limited strategic gains for either side, reflecting the ongoing attrition in the Low Countries.
The pivotal event of the quarter was the Battle of Landen (also known as Neerwinden) on July 29, near Landen in the Spanish Netherlands. Marshal Franois-Henri de Montmorency, duc de Luxembourg, commanded a French army of roughly 80,000, which engaged an Allied force of about 60,000 led by King William III of England-Dutch forces. Luxembourg's cavalry feints drew the Allies into a defensive position across the Geul River, where French infantry assaults, supported by artillery, overcame Allied entrenchments despite heavy resistance from English and Dutch units. William III sustained a shoulder wound but escaped capture, while the battle inflicted severe casualties: approximately 12,00020,000 Allied dead, wounded, or captured, against 5,00015,000 French losses, making it one of the bloodiest European battles of the 17th century. Though a tactical French victory, the high cost and failure to pursue effectively prevented a decisive advance, contributing to the war's stalemate and exacerbating France's resource strains amid poor harvests.
On July 8, the New York City Common Council enacted an ordinance requiring city watchmen to wear standardized uniforms of white wigs, red vests, and white drawers, establishing the first such regulation for law enforcement attire in the American colonies.
August saw no major pitched battles in Europe, with French forces consolidating after Landen while Allied armies regrouped amid logistical challenges and famine in France. In the broader Nine Years' War context, British naval preparations continued for colonial expeditions, including Sir Francis Wheler's fleet assembling for operations against French holdings in the Caribbean and North America, though departures occurred later.
In September, French troops under Melac devastated the Palatinate further, detonating 38 mines on September 6 to demolish remaining structures at Heidelberg Castle, part of Louis XIV's punitive strategy against Electoral Palatinate territories despite prior 1689 ravages. This action underscored France's scorched-earth tactics but yielded no lasting territorial control, as Allied counteroffensives loomed. Concurrently, the expedition under Wheler received final orders around mid-September for deployment to the West Indies, aiming to disrupt French commerce amid the war's global extension.

OctoberDecember

On October 4, French forces under Marshal Nicolas Catinat decisively defeated the Allied army commanded by Victor Amadeus II, Duke of Savoy, at the Battle of Marsaglia near Pinerolo in Piedmont during the Nine Years' War. The engagement arose as Catinat moved to relieve the besieged fortress of Pinerolo, employing disciplined infantry assaults, including one of the earliest recorded bayonet charges by a sustained line formation, which broke repeated Allied counterattacks. Allied losses exceeded 9,000 killed, wounded, or captured, compared to roughly 4,500 French casualties, preserving French control over key positions in northern Italy despite the broader strain of the conflict on Louis XIV's resources.
The following day, October 5, residents of Haarlem in the Dutch Republic rose in protest against acute food shortages and exorbitant grain prices amid the contemporaneous European famine, initiating a wave of popular price-fixing actions known as taxation populaire. This event, the first such documented disturbance in Holland since the early 17th century, reflected broader subsistence crises exacerbated by poor harvests and wartime disruptions, though it was quickly suppressed without escalating into widespread disorder.
In colonial Massachusetts, the Salem witchcraft trials reached their effective conclusion as public skepticism mounted against convictions reliant on spectral evidence and coerced confessions. On October 29, Governor William Phips disbanded the special Court of Oyer and Terminer, suspending further proceedings under its controversial rules; subsequent cases tried in the Superior Court of Judicature largely resulted in acquittals or dismissals after excluding "invisible world" testimony as insufficiently empirical. By December 16, the General Court ordered the release of most remaining prisoners upon payment of fees, marking a pivot toward recognizing the episode's evidentiary flaws, though full legislative reparations for victims' families would not pass until 1711.
Throughout November and December, hostilities in the Nine Years' War persisted in desultory fashion across fronts, with no major engagements recorded in Europe, while French naval efforts in the Caribbean under privateers continued to harass English shipping amid the ongoing colonial extension of the conflict.

Date unknown

A severe famine struck France in 1693, triggered by poor harvests from the preceding year, unseasonably cold weather associated with the Little Ice Age, and disruptions from the Nine Years' War, which strained agricultural labor and food distribution. This crisis, extending into 1694, is regarded as one of the deadliest in French history, with excess mortality estimates ranging from 1 to 2.5 million deaths across the kingdom, particularly affecting rural populations reliant on grain. Government responses under Louis XIV's ministers, including efforts to regulate grain trade and import supplies, proved insufficient against hoarding, speculation, and logistical failures, highlighting vulnerabilities in the ancien rgime economy.
Similar subsistence crises emerged elsewhere in Europe during 1693, linked to climatic extremes and post-earthquake devastation in Italy, where the January Sicily event compounded food shortages through 16931695. In the Massachusetts Bay Colony, the aftermath of the 1692 Salem witchcraft hysteria persisted into early 1693, with remaining accusations investigated and prisoners gradually released following provincial interventions, though no further executions occurred. Cotton Mather published a defense of the trials' proceedings in 1693, framing them as a divine warning against societal moral decay amid ongoing Puritan anxieties.
