Balsam

1x
 Balsam is an aromatic, oleoresinous substance exuded from the bark or stems of various trees and shrubs, typically consisting of a mixture of plant resins and volatile essential oils that are insoluble in water and often contain benzoic or cinnamic acids. These exudates harden upon exposure to air and serve protective functions in plants, such as sealing wounds to prevent decay and water loss. Botanically, balsams are distinguished from other resins by their high aromatic content and viscosity, with notable sources including species in the genera Abies, Myroxylon, and Styrax.
Historically, balsams have been valued since antiquity for their medicinal properties, originating from regions like the Middle East, Central America, and Southeast Asia, where they were used in ancient pharmacology for wound healing, as expectorants, and in religious incense. Key types include balsam of Peru (Myroxylon pereirae), a reddish-brown syrupy resin from Central American trees employed as an antiseptic and in modern cosmetics and perfumes; balsam of Tolu (Myroxylon balsamum), a yellowish plastic substance from South American sources used historically for cough remedies and as a flavoring agent; and Canada balsam (Abies balsamea), a clear viscous oleoresin from North American fir trees prized in microscopy for mounting specimens due to its refractive index matching glass. Other variants, such as benzoin from Styrax species, appear as solid tears and function as fixatives in perfumery and tonics in traditional medicine.
In addition to resins, the term "balsam" applies to certain plants, notably the balsam fir (Abies balsamea), a coniferous tree native to North America known for its resin and use in Christmas trees, and the garden balsam (Impatiens balsamina), an annual flowering herb from Asia cultivated for its ornamental seed pods that "explode" when touched. These botanical associations highlight balsam's dual role in natural products and horticulture, though the resinous form remains the most defining and economically significant. Contemporary applications extend to pharmaceuticals, where balsams like Peru balsam aid in treating ulcers and skin conditions, and to industrial uses in adhesives and varnishes, underscoring their enduring versatility.

Overview

Definition

Balsam refers to a type of oleoresin, which is a natural mixture of essential oils and resins secreted by various trees and shrubs, often exuding spontaneously or through incisions in the bark. These substances are characteristically aromatic, viscous, and yield essential oils upon distillation, distinguishing them as pathological products formed in response to injury.
Key properties of balsams include their solubility in alcohol and ether, while being insoluble in water, which sets them apart from water-soluble gums. They are typically soft and plastic in texture due to their higher oil content compared to harder resins, and their balsamic naturereferring to soothing, healing qualitieshas long been recognized in traditional applications.
Balsams differ from true resins, which are solid or semisolid plant secretions lacking significant essential oil components and thus more brittle. In contrast to gum-resins, which combine resins with water-soluble gummy substances, balsams consist primarily of resinous and oily fractions without substantial gum content.
Prominent examples of balsam-producing plants include genera such as Commiphora (from the Burseraceae family, yielding Balm of Gilead), Myroxylon (Fabaceae, source of Peru balsam), and Abies (Pinaceae, producing Canada balsam from fir species). These oleoresins often contain benzoic or cinnamic acid derivatives as key chemical components.

Etymology and Terminology

The term "balsam" derives from Semitic roots, particularly the Hebrew bem or baam, meaning "spice," "perfume," or "healing balm," which denoted fragrant substances used medicinally in ancient contexts. This Semitic origin is reflected in related Aramaic busma and Arabic baam or balasn, the latter referring to a tree yielding such resin. The word entered Greek as balsamon around the 1st century BCE, likely via trade routes, and was adopted into Latin as balsamum by the Roman era, emphasizing its aromatic and therapeutic qualities.
From Latin, "balsam" influenced European languages, appearing in Old English around 1000 CE in isolated forms but gaining widespread modern English usage in the 16th century to describe aromatic resins for healing wounds. In Arabic, balasn specifically named the product of a resin-yielding tree, further shaping its botanical associations across Mediterranean cultures. Ancient references to balsam in biblical and classical texts, such as those describing it as a prized import, underscore its early prestige, though detailed historical uses are explored elsewhere.
Terminologically, "balsam" specifically denotes an oleoresin or aromatic plant exudate with healing properties, distinguishing it from the broader "balm," which refers to any fragrant, soothing ointment or preparation, often derived from similar sources but not limited to resins. The adjective "balsamic" stems from the same root, originally meaning "restorative" or "aromatic" in reference to balsam-like substances, and later applied to vinegar for its purported health benefits, evoking a resinous, balsamic quality.
Regional variations highlight terminological nuances; for instance, "Canada balsam" names the resin from the balsam fir (Abies balsamea), valued in optics for its clarity, yet it is not a "true balsam" chemically, as those are defined by high benzoic or cinnamic acid content, unlike this coniferous oleoresin.

Chemical Composition

General Components

Balsams, as a class of oleoresins, are characterized by their primary chemical constituents, which include benzoic acid, cinnamic acid, and their esters such as benzyl benzoate, alongside terpenes and volatile oils. These components contribute to the aromatic and therapeutic qualities of balsams, with benzoic and cinnamic acids often present in free or esterified forms, while terpenesprimarily monoterpenes like -pinene and -pineneform the bulk of the volatile fraction.
The extraction of balsams typically begins with tapping trees through incisions or natural exudation to collect the raw oleoresin, followed by processes such as steam distillation to isolate volatile oils or solvent extraction using alcohol or ether to yield the resinous portion. This method preserves the natural blend of soluble resins and essential oils, avoiding excessive heat that could degrade sensitive esters.
Proportions of components vary significantly across different types of balsams. Analytical methods commonly employed include gas chromatography for the separation and quantification of volatile oils and terpenes, often coupled with mass spectrometry for compound identification, while spectroscopysuch as infrared or nuclear magnetic resonancefacilitates the precise determination of benzoic and cinnamic acids.
Specific variations in component ratios occur among different types of balsams, as detailed in subsequent sections.

Physical and Chemical Properties

Balsams are typically viscous liquids or semi-solids at room temperature, exhibiting a tacky consistency that ranges from fluid to more solid-like upon cooling or exposure to air. They display colors from light yellow or greenish-yellow to dark brown, often with a transparent or translucent appearance, and possess a distinctive aromatic odor described as balsamic, pine-like, or vanilla-woody. Their density generally falls between 0.98 and 1.17 g/cm, while the refractive index is typically in the range of 1.52 to 1.60, contributing to their optical clarity in applications like microscopy.
Chemically, balsams are characterized by their acidity, with acid values commonly ranging from 50 to 160 mg KOH/g due to the presence of free benzoic and cinnamic acids. Saponification values for these resins typically lie between 190 and 250 mg KOH/g, reflecting the ester content that can be hydrolyzed under basic conditions. They show a tendency to polymerize when exposed to heat or light, leading to increased viscosity and potential hardening over time.
In terms of solubility, balsams are fully miscible in ethanol, ether, chloroform, and glacial acetic acid, but only partially soluble in fixed oils and insoluble or precipitating in water, which underscores their non-polar, resinous nature. Regarding stability, they are prone to oxidation upon prolonged exposure to air, resulting in color darkening and odor changes, and maintain an acidic pH typically between 4 and 6. Proper storage in cool, dark conditions extends their shelf life to 2-5 years, preventing degradation.

Types of Balsams

Plant-Derived Balsams

Plant-derived balsams are viscous oleoresins exuded from the bark of specific tree species, primarily harvested through controlled incisions to collect the resin without severely harming the trees. These natural products are valued for their aromatic and therapeutic properties, with major types originating from tropical and temperate regions. Production involves tapping mature trees, often seasonally, yielding a global output for key variants like balsam of Peru and balsam of Tolu estimated at approximately 50-100 tons annually combined (as of the 1980s), though exact figures vary due to small-scale, localized harvesting.
Balsam of Peru, derived from the Myroxylon balsamum var. pereirae tree native to Central America, particularly El Salvador, Honduras, and Guatemala, is obtained by making V-shaped incisions in the trunk to collect the exuding resin, which is then boiled and filtered. This balsam is a dark, viscous liquid with a vanilla-cinnamon aroma, containing key compounds such as benzyl cinnamate (comprising up to 60% of its esters), benzyl benzoate, and cinnamein, a mixture of cinnamic and benzoic acid derivatives. It is widely used in perfumery for its fixative and warm, balsamic scent profile. Annual production from El Salvador, the primary exporter, reaches about 50 metric tons (as of the 1980s).
Balsam of Tolu, sourced from Myroxylon toluiferum (also known as Myroxylon balsamum var. balsamum) trees in South America, including Colombia and Venezuela, is harvested similarly through bark incisions, producing a softer, more fluid resin compared to its Peruvian counterpart. Richer in tolu resin acids and possessing a higher vanillin content, it features prominent compounds like free cinnamic acid (around 15%), benzoic acid, and benzyl esters, contributing to its sweet, vanilla-like fragrance with balsamic undertones. This variant is noted for its enhanced solubility and is produced in smaller quantities than balsam of Peru, primarily in Colombia.
Balsam of Mecca, or opobalsam, comes from the Commiphora gileadensis shrub or small tree endemic to the Middle East, including Saudi Arabia, Yemen, and southern Oman, where it is tapped via incisions to yield a rare, myrrh-like resin historically prized for its scarcity. The oleoresin contains monoterpenes such as linalool and geraniol, alongside sesquiterpenes, imparting a fresh, floral-lemon scent with balsamic notes. Due to overharvesting and limited natural habitat, production remains minimal and localized, making it one of the rarer plant-derived balsams today.
Benzoin, a solid resin from species of the Styrax genus such as Styrax benzoin native to Southeast Asia, is harvested by tapping the bark to collect the exudate that hardens into tears or blocks. It contains benzoic acid, benzyl benzoate, and cinnamic derivatives, offering a sweet, balsamic-vanilla aroma used as a fixative in perfumery and in traditional medicine for its antiseptic and expectorant properties. Production is centered in Indonesia and Malaysia, with sustainable harvesting practices increasingly adopted.
Canada balsam is extracted from the Abies balsamea fir tree, widespread in North America from Canada to the northeastern United States, where resin blisters on the bark are collected or trees are lightly tapped. This pale, transparent oleoresin is dominated by monoterpenes, particularly alpha-pinene (a major component providing its coniferous aroma), along with borneol and bornyl acetate. Renowned for its optical clarity and refractive index matching glass (approximately 1.52), it serves as a premier mounting medium in microscopy to preserve specimens without distortion. Production occurs on a modest scale in forested regions, supporting scientific and industrial applications.

Other Natural and Processed Balsams

Styrax balsam, derived from the resin of the Asian sweetgum tree (Liquidambar orientalis), contains significant amounts of styrene and derivatives of cinnamic acid, such as cinnamyl alcohol, contributing to its aromatic profile. This balsam has been utilized historically in incense formulations for its soothing and expectorant properties, often burned in religious and medicinal contexts to release a sweet, balsamic fragrance.
Copaiba balsam, obtained as an oleoresin from various Copaifera species native to South America, is characterized by high levels of sesquiterpenes, particularly -caryophyllene, which imparts anti-inflammatory attributes. Unlike more viscous, solid resins, copaiba exhibits a fluid, oil-like consistency due to its elevated volatile oil content, making it suitable for applications requiring easier dispersion.
Processed variants of balsams include rectified forms, where raw resins like balsam turpentine from fir trees are subjected to repeated distillation to achieve higher purity and remove impurities, resulting in a clearer solvent ideal for artistic and industrial uses. Imitation balsams, developed as synthetic alternatives in perfumery, typically blend compounds such as benzyl salicylate for its mild balsamic note and coumarin for a sweet, hay-like warmth, replicating the sensory qualities of natural balsams without relying on botanical sources.
Among mineral analogs, amber represents a fossilized form of aged tree resin, polymerized over millions of years into a hard, translucent gemstone material that retains faint balsamic aromas when heated. Historical animal-derived balms, such as those incorporating musk from deer glands, served similar fragrant and fixative roles in ancient formulations but differ fundamentally from true plant-based balsams, lacking resinous exudates.
Synthetic balsams emerged in laboratory production during the mid-19th century, coinciding with advancements in organic chemistry that enabled the creation of aroma compounds like coumarin in 1868, allowing for consistent replication of balsamic scents in the fragrance industry. These synthetics now dominate commercial applications, providing economical and stable alternatives to natural extracts in perfumes, cosmetics, and flavorings.

History

Ancient and Biblical References

Balsam, particularly the renowned "balm of Gilead," holds significant references in the Hebrew Bible, where it appears as a valuable healing substance. In Genesis 37:25 and 43:11, it is depicted as a commodity carried by Ishmaelite traders from Gilead, highlighting its role in ancient commerce and medicinal export from the region of Judea around 1000 BCE during the era of King Solomon. Jeremiah 8:22 further emphasizes its therapeutic reputation with the rhetorical question, "Is there no balm in Gilead; is there no physician there? Why then is not the health of the daughter of my people recovered?"illustrating its use for wounds and ailments. Ezekiel 27:17 also notes Judea's export of balm to Tyre, underscoring its economic importance in biblical narratives.
In ancient Egypt, balsam fruit is mentioned in the Ebers Papyrus from circa 1550 BCE, a medical text that includes references to it in remedies for abdominal issues, urinary conditions, and other ailments, often mixed with ingredients like honey and beer. Aromatic resins, including those from coniferous sources, were used in embalming practices to preserve bodies, providing antiseptic properties, though specific use of Judean balsam in mummification is not attested.
Greek and Roman sources further attest to balsam's esteem. Theophrastus, in his 3rd-century BCE work Historia Plantarum, describes cultivated balsam groves in Jericho, noting the plant's careful irrigation and extraction methods unique to the Judean region. Pliny the Elder, in Natural History (Book 12), praises its medicinal virtues, detailing its efficacy against snakebites, headaches, palsy, convulsions, and sores, often recommending it in milk for internal remedies. These accounts reflect balsam's transition from regional curiosity to a staple in classical pharmacology.
Balsam's trade routes connected Arabiaits likely originto the Mediterranean via Judean ports and overland paths like the King's Highway through Gilead, facilitating its spread to Egypt, Greece, and Rome. Highly valued, often worth its weight in silver or gold in later periods, it represented one of antiquity's most lucrative commodities, driving economic and cultural exchanges. In Judaism and early Christianity, the balm symbolized divine healing and restoration; Jeremiah's lament extends to spiritual metaphors for God's redemptive power, later echoed in Christian traditions as a type of Christ's salvific balm for the soul.

Medieval and Early Modern Developments

During the medieval period, the cultivation of balsam, particularly the variety known as Balsam of Matariyya, became centered in royal gardens near Cairo, Egypt, starting from the 9th century CE. These gardens, located in the suburb of Matariyya, were stocked with plants derived from ancient Jericho sources in the Jordan Valley, where the species Commiphora opobalsamum had been grown since antiquity. The Egyptian cultivation ensured a steady supply for medicinal and aromatic uses, with the gardens maintained under strict control by caliphs and sultans, often tended by Christian gardeners to preserve the tradition. By the 12th century, this balsam symbolized prestige amid conflicts like the Crusades.
In the Islamic Golden Age, balsam received detailed attention in medical literature, notably in Avicenna's (Ibn Sina) Canon of Medicine (completed 1025 CE), a seminal text that synthesized Greek, Persian, and Arabic knowledge. Avicenna described balsam as a potent remedy for healing ulcers and wounds, praising its ability to purify infections and remove bone fragments when combined with orris root; he also recommended it for fevers and chills associated with plagues and poisons, reflecting its broad therapeutic reputation. This prescription influenced medical practice across the Muslim world and later Europe, positioning balsam as a panacea for traumatic injuries and epidemic diseases.
The European Renaissance marked the widespread introduction of balsam to Western medicine, facilitated by returning Crusaders who brought knowledge and samples from the Levant and Egypt starting in the 12th century. By the 16th century, distillation techniques for extracting and purifying balsam resins were refined, enabling more consistent pharmaceutical preparations; physicians like Paracelsus advanced iatrochemistry by using distillation to isolate active essences from herbs and resins, including balsams, for treating ailments such as syphilis and wounds. This chemical approach shifted balsam from a rare import to a more accessible remedy in European apothecaries.
Colonial expansion in the 16th century introduced New World alternatives through Spanish and Portuguese trade networks in South and Central America. Explorers and colonists harvested Tolu balsam from Myroxylon balsamum trees in Colombia (near the town of Tolu) and Peru balsam from related varieties in El Salvador and Honduras, exporting them to Europe for medicinal use in cough syrups, wound salves, and perfumes. This trade, regulated by Spanish crown monopolies, boomed due to demand but led to early overharvesting, depleting local forests as extraction methods damaged tree bark without sustainable replanting.
By the 19th century, the traditional Mecca balsam (also known as Balm of Gilead or Judean balsam) had declined sharply, with wild sources in the Near East exhausted due to centuries of overexploitation and failed cultivation attempts. The Egyptian Matariyya gardens, once a key production site, fell into disuse by the late 18th century amid political instability and neglect, prompting a full shift to New World substitutes like Tolu and Peru balsams, which became the primary commercial sources.

Uses and Applications

Medicinal and Therapeutic Uses

Balsams, particularly those derived from trees like Myroxylon balsamum and Abies species, have been employed in traditional medicine for their antiseptic properties in wound healing. Peruvian balsam, for instance, has a long history of use as an antiseptic agent to promote tissue repair and prevent infection in cuts, burns, and ulcers. Similarly, balsam fir oleoresin has been traditionally applied topically to treat skin sores, grazes, and inflammatory conditions among Indigenous North American communities. For respiratory relief, Tolu balsam has been incorporated into cough syrups and lozenges since the 19th century to alleviate symptoms of bronchitis, catarrh, and chest congestion by acting as an expectorant.
The therapeutic effects of balsams are attributed to key active compounds such as cinnamates and benzoic acid derivatives. Benzyl cinnamate, a primary cinnamate in Peruvian and Tolu balsams, exhibits anti-inflammatory activity by inhibiting nitric oxide production and pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF- and IL-6 in cellular models. Benzoic acid and its esters, present in various balsams, demonstrate antifungal properties, with minimal inhibitory concentrations (MICs) against Candida albicans ranging from 100 to 500 g/mL depending on the derivative, strain, and formulation. These compounds contribute to the overall antimicrobial and wound-healing efficacy observed in traditional applications.
In modern pharmacology, Peruvian balsam is utilized in topical formulations for dermatological conditions, including FDA-listed inactive ingredient status in ointments for wound care and hemorrhoid suppositories, where it aids in soothing irritation and promoting healing. Copaiba balsam oil, derived from Copaifera species, has shown promise in clinical trials for anti-anxiety effects; a 2022 randomized controlled trial demonstrated that inhalation of copaiba oil odor reduced subjective anxiety, heart rate, and salivary alpha-amylase levels in adults under mental workload. These applications leverage balsam's natural resin properties for targeted therapeutic outcomes.
Dosage guidelines for balsams vary by type and route of administration, emphasizing short-term use to minimize risks. For topical applications, dilutions of 5-10% in a carrier base, such as ointments applied 2-3 times daily, are recommended for wound healing or skin conditions. For oral use, Tolu balsam has a traditional dosage of 0.3-0.6 g per day in tinctures or syrups for respiratory support, under medical supervision; Peruvian balsam is not recommended orally due to potential kidney toxicity.
Recent studies since the 2010s have explored balsams' potential against antimicrobial-resistant pathogens. For example, oleoresin from Abies balsamea (balsam fir) exhibited activity against methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) strains, with MIC90 values of 50 g/mL in vitro, supporting its role in combating bacterial resistance through membrane disruption. These findings build on traditional uses and highlight balsams as candidates for adjunctive therapies in infection management.

Industrial and Commercial Applications

Balsam resins, particularly Peru balsam (Myroxylon pereirae), serve as fixatives in perfumery, anchoring volatile top notes and enhancing longevity in compositions such as oriental and amber fragrances. These resins contribute balsamic depth and warmth, typically comprising small percentages of the overall formula to blend with floral, spicy, and woody accords without overpowering the scent profile.
In microscopy and optics, Canada balsam (Abies balsamea) functions as a standard mounting medium for preparing permanent slides, owing to its clarity and refractive index of approximately 1.52, which closely matches that of glass coverslips and slides to minimize light distortion. This property ensures optimal visualization of specimens under transmitted light, making it a preferred choice in histological and biological applications since the 19th century.
Balsams have historically been integral to varnishes and adhesives, especially in luthiery during the Stradivarius era (late 17th to early 18th century), where resins from the pine family were mixed with turpentine to create durable, flexible coatings that protect wood while allowing acoustic resonance in string instruments. These mixtures formed spirit varnishes or grounds that penetrate the wood surface, providing adhesion and a glossy finish resistant to environmental wear.
In food and flavoring, balsams such as Peru balsam hold Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) status from the U.S. FDA for use as essential oils in seasonings and flavorings, appearing in trace amounts to impart vanilla-like, balsamic notes in products including certain vanilla extracts.
Within cosmetics, balsams act as emollients in formulations like lip balms, offering softening and occlusive properties due to their resinous texture, though their incorporation has declined since the 2000s amid the rise of synthetic alternatives and International Fragrance Association (IFRA) restrictions limiting usage to 0.030.7% in leave-on products to address sensitization risks.

Safety and Regulation

Health Risks and Allergies

Balsams, especially balsam of Peru derived from Myroxylon pereirae, are known to elicit allergic reactions primarily through type IV delayed hypersensitivity mechanisms involving T-cell mediated responses to components such as cinnamic acid and its derivatives. These reactions occur when sensitized individuals come into contact with the resin, often via topical application in cosmetics, pharmaceuticals, or fragrances.
Sensitization prevalence varies, with patch testing in patients suspected of contact dermatitis showing positive reactions to balsam of Peru in 4% to 8% of cases, making it one of the most common allergens in dermatological evaluations. In the general population, fragrance-related allergies, which frequently cross-react with balsam components, affect approximately 1% to 2% of individuals. High-risk groups include those with pre-existing fragrance sensitivities, as balsam of Peru is a standard marker in fragrance mix patch tests, and asthmatics, who may experience worsened respiratory symptoms upon exposure.
Common symptoms from topical exposure manifest as allergic contact dermatitis, presenting with eczema-like rashes characterized by redness, swelling, itching, and vesicular eruptions, alongside urticaria in some cases. Inhalation of balsam vapors or aerosols can provoke respiratory irritation, including coughing, wheezing, and nasal congestion, particularly in sensitive individuals. Oral ingestion may lead to systemic contact dermatitis, with flares at distant skin sites or mucosal inflammation.
Toxicological assessments reveal low acute toxicity for balsam of Peru, with an oral LD50 exceeding 5,000 mg/kg in rats, indicating minimal risk from single high-dose exposures. However, chronic exposure in animal models has been linked to subtle hepatic changes, such as liver discoloration and potential enzyme elevations, as observed in studies of related balsam resins like benzoin.
Notable case studies from the 1990s in Europe highlighted outbreaks of allergic contact dermatitis attributed to adulterated balsam-containing cosmetics and topical preparations, affecting multiple patients and underscoring cross-reactivity with fragrances and spices. These incidents often involved systemic reactions in sensitized individuals consuming or applying contaminated products.

Handling and Regulatory Guidelines

Balsam resins and oils, such as those derived from Myroxylon pereirae (Peru balsam), require careful handling to prevent degradation and minimize exposure risks. During manipulation, personnel should wear protective gloves and ensure adequate ventilation to avoid inhalation or skin contact, as direct exposure may cause irritation. For storage, these substances should be kept in tightly sealed amber glass containers to shield them from light and heat, ideally in a cool, dry environment below 25C (77F) and away from ignition sources or oxidizing agents.
In the European Union, under the REACH regulation and Cosmetics Regulation (EC) No 1223/2009, Peru balsam extracts and distillates are restricted to a maximum concentration of 0.4% in leave-on cosmetic products to mitigate potential sensitization, a limit established by the Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety (SCCS) in 2006. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) classifies balsam of Peru (CAS 8007-00-9) as a permitted cosmetic ingredient, subject to general safety and labeling standards, and as generally recognized as safe (GRAS) for use as a direct food additive (flavoring agent).
Labeling requirements for cosmetics containing balsam emphasize allergen disclosure under the International Nomenclature of Cosmetic Ingredients (INCI). For instance, Peru balsam oil must be declared as "Myroxylon Pereirae (Balsam Peru) Oil" if present above trace levels, and in the EU, its presence at concentrations exceeding 0.001% in leave-on products triggers mandatory listing of related fragrance allergens like benzyl cinnamate on the product label to inform consumers of potential sensitivities.
Best practices for safe use include diluting balsam oils to below 1% (with a maximum of 0.4% recommended for sensitive applications like topical skincare) in carrier mediums to reduce irritation potential, particularly for individuals with known fragrance sensitivities. In case of accidental exposure, immediate protocols involve washing affected skin with mild soap and water, seeking medical advice if symptoms persist, and avoiding undiluted application altogether.
