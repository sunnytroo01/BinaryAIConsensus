Angiotensin

1x
 Angiotensin is a family of peptide hormones that play a central role in the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), a key regulatory pathway for maintaining blood pressure, fluid volume, and electrolyte balance in the body. The renin-angiotensin system was first described in 1898 with the discovery of renin by Tigerstedt and Bergman, and angiotensin was isolated in the late 1930s, with the name adopted in 1958. Produced primarily through enzymatic cleavage of the precursor protein angiotensinogen, which is synthesized in the liver, the main forms include angiotensin I (an inactive decapeptide generated by renin from the kidneys) and angiotensin II (the active octapeptide formed by angiotensin-converting enzyme, or ACE, mainly in the lungs). Angiotensin II exerts potent physiological effects, such as vasoconstriction to elevate blood pressure, stimulation of aldosterone release from the adrenal glands to promote sodium and water retention in the kidneys, and enhancement of sympathetic nervous system activity. These actions occur primarily through binding to angiotensin type 1 (AT1) receptors, while angiotensin type 2 (AT2) receptors mediate opposing effects like vasodilation and tissue protection.
The RAAS is activated in response to low blood pressure, reduced renal perfusion, or decreased sodium delivery to the distal tubules, initiating a cascade where juxtaglomerular cells in the kidney release renin to cleave circulating angiotensinogen into angiotensin I. Further processing by ACE converts angiotensin I to angiotensin II, which not only directly constricts vascular smooth muscle but also triggers the release of antidiuretic hormone (ADH) from the pituitary and increases thirst to support fluid homeostasis. Beyond its classic pressor effects, angiotensin II contributes to inflammation, fibrosis, and oxidative stress in various tissues, linking overactivation of the system to pathological conditions such as hypertension, heart failure, chronic kidney disease, and atherosclerosis.
In addition to the classical RAAS axis involving angiotensin II and AT1 receptors, an alternative protective pathway exists, where ACE2 degrades angiotensin II into angiotensin (1-7), which binds to MAS receptors to promote vasodilation, anti-inflammatory responses, and natriuresis. This counter-regulatory mechanism helps balance the system's effects and has gained attention in contexts like COVID-19, where dysregulation of ACE2 impacts disease severity. Therapeutically, the RAAS is a major target for interventions; ACE inhibitors (e.g., lisinopril) block angiotensin II formation, angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs, e.g., valsartan) antagonize AT1 receptors, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (e.g., spironolactone) mitigate aldosterone's effects, all of which are cornerstone treatments for cardiovascular and renal disorders. Angiotensin II itself has been approved for intravenous use in distributive shock to rapidly restore mean arterial pressure.

Overview

Definition and Role in RAAS

Angiotensin refers to a family of peptide hormones derived from the precursor protein angiotensinogen, primarily functioning as potent vasoconstrictors and key regulators of fluid and electrolyte balance in the body. These peptides typically range from 8 to 10 amino acids in length, with angiotensin II (Ang II) being the most biologically active form, characterized by the sequence Asp-Arg-Val-Tyr-Ile-His-Pro-Phe. Angiotensinogen is mainly produced in the liver and serves as the substrate for the formation of these peptides within the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS).
In the RAAS, angiotensin plays a central role in maintaining blood pressure and volume homeostasis, activated in response to conditions such as low blood pressure or reduced renal perfusion. The cascade begins when decreased blood pressure or sodium levels stimulate the release of renin from the kidney's juxtaglomerular cells, which cleaves angiotensinogen to produce angiotensin I; this inactive decapeptide is then rapidly converted to the active octapeptide Ang II, primarily by angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) in the lungs. Ang II subsequently promotes the secretion of aldosterone from the adrenal cortex, which enhances sodium and water reabsorption in the kidneys, thereby expanding blood volume and restoring pressure. This integrated hormonal pathway ensures rapid physiological adjustments to hemodynamic stress.
The general functions of angiotensin, particularly Ang II, encompass vasoconstriction through binding to AT1 receptors on vascular smooth muscle, leading to increased peripheral resistance and elevated blood pressure. It also stimulates thirst and the release of antidiuretic hormone (ADH) from the hypothalamus and pituitary, promoting water retention and intake to support fluid balance. Additionally, via aldosterone induction, angiotensin facilitates electrolyte balance by increasing renal sodium reabsorption and potassium excretion. Counter-regulatory aspects exist, such as vasodilation and natriuresis mediated by AT2 receptors or metabolites like angiotensin-(1-7), which oppose the primary pressor effects of Ang II.

Historical Discovery

The discovery of renin, a key enzyme in the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), traces back to 1898 when Robert Tigerstedt and Per Bergman at the Karolinska Institute identified a pressor substance in rabbit kidney extracts that elevated blood pressure upon intravenous injection; they named it renin after its renal origin. This finding laid the groundwork for understanding renal contributions to hypertension, though the full mechanism remained elusive for decades. In 1934, Harry Goldblatt advanced the field by demonstrating that clamping renal arteries in dogs induced sustained hypertension, linking renal ischemia to pressor activity and prompting searches for the responsible factors.
By the late 1930s, independent research teams isolated a potent hypertensive peptide from kidney extracts. In 1939, Irvine H. Page and O. M. Helmer at the Cleveland Clinic extracted a vasoconstrictor substance from ischemic kidney perfusates, initially calling it angiotonin for its vessel-constricting effects. Concurrently, Eduardo Braun-Menndez and colleagues in Buenos Aires identified a similar pressor agent from renal vein blood, naming it hypertensin and recognizing it as the product of renin acting on a plasma substrate. These parallel discoveries highlighted the peptide's role in RAAS but led to nomenclature disputes; in 1958, Page and Braun-Menndez compromised on "angiotensin," derived from "angio" (vessel) and "tension," to unify the field.
The 1950s brought critical elucidation of angiotensin's forms and biosynthesis. Leonard T. Skeggs and colleagues at Case Western Reserve University isolated two distinct peptides in 1954angiotensin I (a decapeptide) and angiotensin II (an octapeptide)demonstrating that renin cleaved angiotensinogen to form the inactive angiotensin I, which was then converted to the active angiotensin II by a serum enzyme they termed converting enzyme (later identified as angiotensin-converting enzyme, or ACE). This two-step process clarified RAAS activation, with Skeggs' 1956 work confirming ACE's role in the conversion. In the 1970s and 1980s, further studies refined ACE's characterization, including its purification and identification as a zinc metalloprotease primarily in pulmonary endothelium, solidifying its central position in angiotensin II production and blood pressure regulation.
The 1990s marked the identification of angiotensin receptors, distinguishing AT1 and AT2 subtypes through subtype-selective antagonists like losartan (for AT1) developed in 1986, which revealed AT1's mediation of vasoconstriction and aldosterone release while AT2 opposed these effects. Cloning of the AT1 receptor in 1991 and AT2 in 1993 enabled molecular insights into their signaling pathways. Entering the 2000s, research expanded RAAS complexity with the recognition of angiotensin (1-7) , first noted in 1988 for its vasodilatory actions but gaining prominence after 2003 when Robson A. Santos and colleagues identified the Mas proto-oncogene as its receptor, establishing an counter-regulatory axis that modulates AT1 effects.

Biosynthesis and Metabolism

Precursor: Angiotensinogen

Angiotensinogen is a glycoprotein classified as an 2-globulin, consisting of 485 amino acids in humans, including a 33-amino-acid signal peptide that is cleaved during processing. The mature protein has a non-glycosylated molecular weight of approximately 53 kDa, but glycosylation at four asparagine residues increases this to 6575 kDa, contributing to its serum stability and function as a precursor. It is primarily synthesized in the liver by hepatocytes, where it constitutes a significant portion of circulating plasma levels under normal conditions.
The human angiotensinogen gene (AGT) is located on the long arm of chromosome 1 at position 1q42.2, spanning about 12 kb with five exons that encode the full-length precursor protein. Genetic variations in AGT, such as the M235T polymorphism (rs699) in exon 2, which substitutes threonine for methionine at position 235, have been associated with increased risk of essential hypertension; the T allele correlates with higher plasma angiotensinogen concentrations and elevated blood pressure in multiple populations. This variant influences transcriptional efficiency and protein levels, highlighting AGT's role in blood pressure regulation.
Hepatic synthesis of angiotensinogen is tightly regulated by hormonal signals, including stimulation by estrogens, which enhance transcription and explain higher plasma levels in females; glucocorticoids, acting via glucocorticoid response elements to induce gene expression; and thyroid hormones, which modulate production through metabolic effects on hepatocytes. Additionally, endogenous angiotensin II provides positive feedback by upregulating hepatic angiotensinogen mRNA and protein synthesis, maintaining systemic levels in a sodium-dependent manner. Circadian rhythms also influence its production, with plasma and urinary angiotensinogen exhibiting diurnal variations that align with the broader renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system oscillations.
Renin specifically cleaves angiotensinogen at the peptide bond between leucine at position 10 and valine at position 11 from the N-terminus, a process facilitated by the conserved His-Pro-Phe motif at positions 68, which ensures substrate specificity across species. This sequence forms critical interactions with renin's active site, enabling the release of the decapeptide angiotensin I as the initial step in the cascade.

Production Pathway

The production pathway of angiotensin within the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) initiates with the enzymatic cleavage of angiotensinogen by renin, an aspartyl protease secreted primarily from the juxtaglomerular cells of the kidney, yielding the inactive decapeptide angiotensin I. This renin-mediated step represents the rate-limiting process in the classical RAAS cascade and occurs in response to physiological signals maintaining blood pressure and fluid balance.
Subsequently, angiotensin I is rapidly converted to the biologically active octapeptide angiotensin II through the action of angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), a zinc metallopeptidase predominantly expressed on the luminal surface of vascular endothelial cells, especially in the pulmonary circulation. ACE catalyzes the removal of the two C-terminal residues (histidine-leucine) from angiotensin I, enabling angiotensin II to exert potent effects such as vasoconstriction and aldosterone stimulation.
Alternative non-ACE pathways contribute to angiotensin II formation, particularly in tissue microenvironments; for instance, chymase, a serine protease abundant in mast cells and cardiac tissues, directly hydrolyzes angiotensin I to angiotensin II, accounting for up to 90% of local angiotensin II production in the heart under pathological conditions like ischemia. Further downstream, angiotensin III, a heptapeptide with similar potency to angiotensin II at certain receptors, arises from the removal of the N-terminal aspartate residue of angiotensin II by aminopeptidase A, an enzyme expressed in various tissues including the kidney and brain. Additionally, the heptapeptide angiotensin (1-7), which opposes many angiotensin II actions, forms via ACE2-mediated cleavage of angiotensin II (removing the C-terminal phenylalanine) or through neprilysin degradation of angiotensin I or II in endothelial and renal cells.
Regulation of the pathway centers on renin secretion from juxtaglomerular cells, triggered by reduced renal perfusion pressure (detected by baroreceptors), decreased sodium delivery to the macula densa, sympathetic nervous system activation via 1-adrenergic receptors, or hypokalemia. Tissue-specific RAAS production amplifies these processes locally; for example, components like renin and ACE are expressed in the heart and kidney, enabling autocrine/paracrine angiotensin generation independent of circulating levels.

Degradation and Half-life

Angiotensin II (Ang II) is primarily degraded through hydrolysis by angiotensinases, which include aminopeptidases such as aminopeptidase A (APA) and aminopeptidase N (APN), removing the N-terminal aspartate residue to form angiotensin III (Ang III). Further degradation of Ang III proceeds via additional aminopeptidases and dipeptidyl peptidases (e.g., DPP III) to yield smaller fragments like Ang IV, ultimately leading to inactive peptides. Concurrently, angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2), a carboxypeptidase, cleaves the C-terminal phenylalanine from Ang II to produce angiotensin (1-7) , which exerts counter-regulatory effects. Neutral endopeptidase (NEP), also known as neprilysin, contributes by cleaving internal bonds in Ang II, such as between Tyr4 and Ile5, facilitating additional breakdown pathways.
The plasma half-life of Ang II is approximately 30 seconds, reflecting its rapid enzymatic degradation and clearance, while angiotensin I (Ang I) exhibits a similarly brief half-life of about 10 seconds due to conversion by ACE and peptidase activity. In contrast, tissue half-lives are extended: Ang II persists for 15-30 minutes in organs like the heart, kidney, and adrenal gland, primarily due to binding to angiotensin II type 1 (AT1) receptors and other proteins that protect it from immediate hydrolysis. This disparity underscores the distinction between circulating and local angiotensin levels, with tissue-bound forms enabling sustained paracrine signaling.
Clearance of angiotensin peptides occurs predominantly in the lungs, liver, and kidneys, where vascular endothelium and parenchymal cells express high levels of degrading peptidases. Pulmonary endothelium plays a key role in initial uptake and breakdown during circulation, while hepatic and renal peptidases handle substantial metabolic loads, contributing to overall systemic elimination.
Stability of angiotensin peptides is modulated by environmental factors, including pH, which influences peptidase activity, and pharmacological inhibitors such as phosphoramidon, which blocks NEP and prolongs Ang II half-life. These elements highlight the dynamic regulation of angiotensin bioavailability, with implications for elevated local concentrations in pathological states like hypertension.

Angiotensin Peptides

Angiotensin I

Angiotensin I is a decapeptide hormone consisting of the amino acid sequence Asp-Arg-Val-Tyr-Ile-His-Pro-Phe-His-Leu, commonly abbreviated as DRVYIHPFHL. This sequence is generated through the proteolytic cleavage of the N-terminal region of angiotensinogen by the enzyme renin, which acts as the rate-limiting step in the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS). The cleavage occurs specifically between the Leu-Val bond of angiotensinogen, releasing the intact decapeptide with high efficiency under physiological conditions.
As the immediate product of renin action, angiotensin I circulates in human plasma at concentrations typically ranging from 25 to 143 pg/mL in healthy individuals, reflecting the balance between its formation and rapid further processing. Despite its structural similarity to active angiotensin peptides, angiotensin I is biologically inert and lacks direct binding affinity to known angiotensin receptors, such as AT1 or AT2. Its primary function is to serve as a prohormone substrate for angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), which removes the C-terminal dipeptide (His-Leu) to produce the potent octapeptide angiotensin II.
Although largely inactive, angiotensin I may exhibit minor weak effects in specific tissues through non-ACE-dependent pathways, such as chymase-mediated processing or other local enzymatic conversions that bypass traditional pulmonary ACE activity. These potential roles are tissue-specific and contribute modestly to local angiotensin generation, particularly in conditions like inflammation or vascular injury where alternative proteases are upregulated. Angiotensin I is swiftly converted to angiotensin II primarily via ACE in endothelial cells, underscoring its transient precursor status in the RAAS cascade.

Angiotensin II

Angiotensin II is an octapeptide hormone consisting of the amino acid sequence Asp-Arg-Val-Tyr-Ile-His-Pro-Phe (DRVYIHPF), formed from angiotensin I through enzymatic cleavage. As the primary active component of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), it demonstrates the highest biological potency among the angiotensin peptides, exerting profound effects on blood pressure regulation and fluid homeostasis.
The key functions of angiotensin II include potent vasoconstriction, which increases peripheral resistance and elevates blood pressure. It also stimulates aldosterone secretion from the adrenal cortex to promote sodium retention and potassium excretion in the kidneys, enhances antidiuretic hormone (ADH) release from the posterior pituitary, and induces thirst to maintain extracellular fluid volume. Additionally, angiotensin II serves as a growth factor, driving hypertrophy and proliferation in vascular smooth muscle cells, contributing to vascular remodeling.
In normal physiological conditions, plasma concentrations of angiotensin II are maintained at low levels, typically ranging from 10 to 20 pg/mL, reflecting tight regulation by the RAAS. Tissue concentrations, however, are often substantially higher than plasma levelsup to 1000-fold in renal interstitial fluidand become further elevated in pathological states such as hypertension, where local RAAS activation amplifies these effects.
The signaling initiated by angiotensin II involves G-protein-coupled receptor activation, which stimulates phospholipase C (PLC) to hydrolyze phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate into inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate (IP) and diacylglycerol (DAG). IP then triggers the release of calcium ions (Ca) from intracellular stores, elevating cytosolic Ca levels and activating downstream pathways that mediate vasoconstriction, cell growth, and other responses. This pathway underscores angiotensin II's role as a central mediator in acute and chronic cardiovascular regulation.

Angiotensin III

Angiotensin III (Ang III) is a heptapeptide derived from angiotensin II (Ang II) through the enzymatic removal of the N-terminal aspartic acid residue by aminopeptidase A (APA), resulting in the amino acid sequence Arg-Val-Tyr-Ile-His-Pro-Phe (RVYIHPF). This modification occurs primarily in tissues such as the kidney and brain, where APA activity is prominent.
In terms of physiological functions, Ang III exhibits approximately 40% of the vasoconstrictor activity of Ang II, contributing to modest pressor effects at higher concentrations. However, it demonstrates enhanced potency in stimulating aldosterone secretion from the adrenal zona glomerulosa, with reported activity ranging from 100% to 200% that of Ang II in both rats and humans. This disparity arises partly from Ang III's preferential binding to the angiotensin type 2 (AT2) receptor, for which it has affinity similar to Ang II, while showing lower affinity for the AT1 receptor. Through AT2 receptor activation, Ang III promotes natriuresis and vasodilation, counterbalancing some hypertensive effects.
Formation of Ang III is tissue-specific, with elevated concentrations observed in the brain and adrenal glands compared to systemic circulation. In plasma, Ang III levels are typically around 10% of those of Ang II, measured at approximately 2.9 fmol/ml in healthy human subjects.
Research highlights Ang III's involvement in blood pressure regulation, particularly through central and renal mechanisms that modulate vasopressin release and sodium excretion. Additionally, its potent aldosterone-stimulating effects suggest potential implications in adrenal disorders, such as primary aldosteronism, where dysregulated Ang III signaling may contribute to pathophysiology without substantially elevating blood pressure.

Angiotensin IV

Angiotensin IV (Ang IV) is a hexapeptide with the amino acid sequence Val-Tyr-Ile-His-Pro-Phe (VYIHPF), formed by the further degradation of angiotensin III through aminopeptidase activity.
Ang IV primarily exerts its effects by binding to the AT4 receptor, identified as insulin-regulated aminopeptidase (IRAP), a membrane-bound enzyme predominantly expressed in the brain. This interaction facilitates cognitive processes, including enhanced memory acquisition, retention, and retrieval, as demonstrated in rodent models where central administration of Ang IV improved performance in spatial learning tasks such as the Morris water maze. Additionally, Ang IV promotes localized vasodilation in cerebral microvasculature, potentially supporting neuronal function by increasing regional blood flow.
Ang IV is predominantly localized in the brain, with high concentrations in regions critical for cognition, such as the hippocampus, neocortex, and entorhinal cortex, where it is synthesized locally via the brain renin-angiotensin system. In contrast, circulating plasma levels of Ang IV remain low, typically below detectable limits in standard assays, underscoring its role as a primarily central peptide rather than a systemic hormone.
Research on Ang IV has highlighted its potential therapeutic implications since the 1990s, beginning with the discovery of the AT4 receptor in 1992 and subsequent studies showing its facilitation of long-term potentiation in hippocampal slices. In Alzheimer's disease models, Ang IV analogues like Dihexa have reversed scopolamine-induced amnesia and reduced amyloid-beta pathology, suggesting a role in countering cognitive decline through IRAP inhibition and enhanced synaptic plasticity. These findings have spurred development of metabolically stable Ang IV mimetics for dementia treatment, with preclinical evidence indicating improved learning without the hypertensive effects of other angiotensin peptides.

Angiotensin (1-7)

Angiotensin (1-7), often abbreviated as Ang-(1-7), is a heptapeptide with the amino acid sequence Asp-Arg-Val-Tyr-Ile-His-Pro (DRVYIHP). This structure arises primarily through enzymatic cleavage within the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS). It is generated from angiotensin II by the action of angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2), which removes the C-terminal phenylalanine residue, or alternatively from angiotensin I via hydrolysis by neprilysin (also known as neutral endopeptidase), yielding the same heptapeptide product. These pathways position Ang-(1-7) as a key component of the protective arm of the RAAS, counterbalancing the vasoconstrictive effects of angiotensin II.
Ang-(1-7) exerts vasodilatory and anti-proliferative effects that directly oppose those of angiotensin II. It promotes vasodilation by stimulating nitric oxide release from endothelial cells and by potentiating the actions of bradykinin, thereby enhancing endothelial function and reducing vascular tone. Additionally, its anti-proliferative properties inhibit smooth muscle cell growth and extracellular matrix remodeling, contributing to reduced vascular and cardiac hypertrophy. These actions are mediated through binding to the Mas receptor, a G protein-coupled receptor identified in the early 2000s as the primary receptor for Ang-(1-7), with seminal work demonstrating its role in kidney binding and signaling.
Tissue levels of Ang-(1-7) are notably elevated in the kidney and heart, where it supports local protective mechanisms against fibrosis and inflammation. Administration of ACE inhibitors further increases circulating and tissue Ang-(1-7) levels by shunting more angiotensin I toward alternative degradation pathways, amplifying its beneficial effects. Recent research highlights its cardioprotective role, including attenuation of myocardial remodeling post-injury, and anti-inflammatory properties that suppress cytokine production in various tissues. Post-2010 studies have linked Ang-(1-7) to COVID-19 pathophysiology, suggesting that enhancing this peptide via Mas receptor agonists could mitigate virus-induced inflammation and endothelial dysfunction by restoring RAAS balance.

Receptors and Signaling

AT1 Receptor

The AT1 receptor, encoded by the AGTR1 gene, is a member of the G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) superfamily, featuring seven hydrophobic -helical transmembrane domains that form the ligand-binding pocket and facilitate signal transduction across the cell membrane. This structure is conserved across species and enables the receptor to interact with heterotrimeric G proteins upon activation. In terms of distribution, the AT1 receptor is widely expressed in key tissues involved in cardiovascular and renal regulation, with high levels in vascular smooth muscle cells, adrenal cortex, kidneys, heart, and liver. Specifically, it is abundant in the zona glomerulosa of the adrenal gland, renal proximal tubules and glomeruli, and endothelial and smooth muscle layers of arteries and veins, underscoring its central role in blood pressure homeostasis and fluid balance.
Rodents express two distinct subtypes of the AT1 receptorAT1A and AT1Barising from separate genes, whereas humans possess a single AT1 receptor isoform that is most homologous to the rodent AT1A subtype. The AT1A subtype predominates in vascular smooth muscle, liver, and kidneys, mediating responses such as sodium retention and vasoconstriction, while AT1B is more prominent in the adrenal gland and pituitary, contributing to hormone secretion. This subtype differentiation in rodents has facilitated targeted genetic studies, revealing nuanced tissue-specific functions, though the unified human AT1 receptor integrates these roles comprehensively.
The primary signaling cascade of the AT1 receptor involves coupling to the heterotrimeric Gq/11 protein family upon angiotensin II binding, which activates phospholipase C- (PLC-) to cleave phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP) into inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate (IP) and diacylglycerol (DAG). IP subsequently binds to receptors on the endoplasmic reticulum, triggering rapid release of Ca from intracellular stores, which elevates cytosolic calcium levels and activates calmodulin-dependent myosin light chain kinase to promote smooth muscle contraction. Concurrently, DAG recruits and activates conventional and novel isoforms of protein kinase C (PKC), which phosphorylate downstream targets to sustain signaling. Additionally, AT1 receptor activation engages mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) pathways, including extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK1/2), c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK), and p38 MAPK, through transactivation of receptor tyrosine kinases like epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR), ultimately driving gene expression changes that induce cellular hypertrophy in vascular smooth muscle and cardiomyocytes.
The AT1 receptor accounts for the majority of angiotensin II's physiological effects, particularly potent vasoconstriction in resistance vessels and stimulation of aldosterone biosynthesis and secretion from the adrenal zona glomerulosa, which enhances sodium reabsorption and potassium excretion in the kidney. These actions collectively elevate blood pressure and maintain extracellular fluid volume, with vasoconstriction mediated primarily by the calcium-dependent pathway and aldosterone release involving both Gq/PLC signaling and -arrestin-dependent mechanisms.

AT2 Receptor

The angiotensin II type 2 receptor (AT2R) is a seven-transmembrane G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) that shares approximately 34% amino acid sequence homology with the AT1 receptor. This structural similarity places it within the same receptor family, yet its distinct signaling profile contributes to counter-regulatory effects in the renin-angiotensin system. Recent crystallographic studies have revealed unique features, such as helix VIII, which blocks traditional G protein and -arrestin recruitment, allowing for sustained signaling without rapid desensitization.
AT2R expression is notably high during fetal development, where it predominates in various tissues, but it declines sharply after birth, resulting in low levels in most adult organs. In adults, it is primarily found in the brain (including the locus coeruleus and amygdaloid nucleus), kidney, adrenal gland, uterine myometrium, and skin, often upregulated in pathological conditions such as tissue injury or inflammation. This pattern reflects a developmental shift, with AT2R dominating in fetal life to support growth and differentiation, while AT1R expression increases postnatally to assume primary roles in homeostasis.
Signaling through AT2R primarily involves coupling to inhibitory G proteins (Gi/o), which activates downstream pathways including protein phosphatases such as mitogen-activated protein kinase phosphatase-1 (MKP-1), protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A), and Src homology 2 domain-containing phosphatase-1 (SHP-1). This leads to increased production of nitric oxide (NO) and cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP), promoting anti-proliferative and pro-apoptotic effects that oppose growth-promoting signals. Additional pathways, including phosphoinositide 3-kinase (PI3K)/Akt, further modulate these outcomes, contributing to protective cellular responses.
Physiologically, AT2R activation mediates natriuresis in the kidney through NO/cGMP-dependent mechanisms in proximal tubules, enhancing sodium excretion. It also promotes vasodilation, particularly via its higher affinity for angiotensin III compared to angiotensin II, leading to relaxation of vascular smooth muscle. Beyond these, AT2R supports tissue repair by modulating stem cell activity and extracellular matrix remodeling, while exerting anti-inflammatory effects through inhibition of nuclear factor-B (NF-B) translocation and cytokine release. These functions underscore its protective role in development and injury response, often counterbalancing AT1R-mediated actions.

Mas Receptor and Others

The Mas receptor, also known as MAS1, is a G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) that serves as the primary receptor for angiotensin (1-7) , mediating its protective effects within the renin-angiotensin system (RAS). Identified in 2003 by Santos et al. through functional studies demonstrating Ang(1-7)-induced arachidonic acid release in Mas-transfected cells, it was originally cloned in 1986 as a proto-oncogene but later linked to RAS signaling. The receptor exhibits ubiquitous expression, with particularly high levels in the brain and testes, and notable presence in the heart, kidneys, lungs, vasculature, adipose tissue, and skeletal muscle. Upon Ang(1-7) binding, Mas activates signaling pathways such as the PI3K-Akt axis, which promotes vasodilation by enhancing nitric oxide (NO) production and inhibits fibrosis through suppression of pro-inflammatory mediators like NF-B and p38 MAPK. These actions position Mas as a counter-regulatory element to angiotensin II-mediated effects, contributing to cardiovascular and renal protection.
The AT4 receptor, identified in the 1990s as a high-affinity binding site for angiotensin IV (Ang IV), was later confirmed to be insulin-regulated aminopeptidase (IRAP), a membrane-bound enzyme with peptidase activity.00240-3) Seminal work by Harding et al. in 1992 described the Ang IV binding site in brain membranes, while Albiston et al. in 2001 cloned IRAP and demonstrated its identity with AT4 through pharmacological and structural analyses.00240-3) IRAP is predominantly expressed in the brain, with high densities in memory-related regions such as the hippocampus, basolateral amygdala, cerebral cortex, and septum, though lower levels occur in peripheral tissues like the kidney and placenta. Ang IV binding to IRAP enhances long-term potentiation (LTP) in the hippocampal CA1 region, a key neuroplasticity mechanism underlying learning and memory consolidation, as evidenced by electrophysiological studies showing increased synaptic efficacy at concentrations around 1 M. This receptor's role extends to cognitive enhancement, independent of its enzymatic function, highlighting its brain-specific contributions to RAS modulation.
Beyond Mas and AT4/IRAP, emerging receptors in the RAS include the Mas-related G protein-coupled receptor D (MrgD), which binds alamandinea heptapeptide derived from angiotensin A or Ang(1-7)to exert vasorelaxant and anti-remodeling effects in local tissue RAS. First linked to the RAS in 2013 by Lautner et al., who identified alamandine's NO-dependent vasodilation in MrgD-expressing cells, this receptor is distributed in dorsal root ganglia, testis, arteries, heart, lungs, and atherosclerotic plaques. MrgD signaling involves adenylyl cyclase activation, cAMP elevation, and protein kinase A, promoting peripheral and central blood pressure regulation while countering fibrosis in cardiac tissue. These findings underscore the expanding complexity of non-classical RAS receptors in tissue-specific homeostasis.

Physiological Effects

Cardiovascular System

Angiotensin II (Ang II) primarily exerts its effects on the cardiovascular system through activation of the angiotensin type 1 (AT1) receptor, leading to potent vasoconstriction of vascular smooth muscle cells. This constriction increases peripheral vascular resistance, a key mechanism for elevating blood pressure during acute physiological stress. The AT1 receptor-mediated signaling pathway involves G-protein-coupled activation, resulting in calcium influx and smooth muscle contraction. Sustained Ang II exposure can promote chronic vasoconstriction, contributing to vascular remodeling and endothelial dysfunction.
In the heart, Ang II via AT1 receptors induces cardiomyocyte hypertrophy and interstitial fibrosis, which are adaptive responses to pressure overload but can progress to maladaptive remodeling. Hypertrophy involves increased protein synthesis and sarcomere reorganization in cardiomyocytes, while fibrosis arises from fibroblast proliferation and excessive extracellular matrix deposition. These changes enhance cardiac contractility in the short term through positive inotropic effects but ultimately impair diastolic function and promote atherosclerosis by accelerating plaque formation in coronary arteries. Local cardiac Ang II production further amplifies these effects, linking AT1 activation to oxidative stress and inflammatory pathways.
The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) activation by Ang II rapidly raises blood pressure through vasoconstriction and, over time, sustains hypertension via structural vascular and cardiac remodeling. Acute elevations occur within minutes of RAAS stimulation, restoring hemodynamic balance, whereas chronic exposure leads to thickened vessel walls and increased afterload on the heart. This dual acute-chronic regulation underscores Ang II's central role in cardiovascular homeostasis.
Counter-regulatory actions within the angiotensin system mitigate these effects, particularly through angiotensin (1-7) acting on the Mas receptor to promote vasodilation. Ang(1-7) stimulates endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) activation via Akt-dependent pathways, increasing nitric oxide (NO) bioavailability and relaxing vascular smooth muscle. This opposes AT1-mediated constriction, providing a protective balance against excessive blood pressure elevation and vascular injury. Mas receptor blockade abolishes this NO-dependent vasodilation, highlighting its specificity in endothelial function.

Renal System

Angiotensin II (Ang II) exerts significant effects on glomerular function in the kidney, primarily by constricting the efferent arterioles, which helps maintain glomerular filtration rate (GFR) during fluctuations in renal perfusion pressure. This selective vasoconstriction increases intraglomerular pressure, thereby stabilizing GFR and preventing excessive filtration under conditions of reduced blood flow. Additionally, Ang II modulates tubuloglomerular feedback (TGF), a key autoregulatory mechanism where macula densa cells sense changes in tubular sodium chloride concentration and adjust afferent arteriolar tone; Ang II enhances this feedback loop, particularly through actions on the connecting tubule, to fine-tune GFR and protect against hyperfiltration.
In terms of sodium handling, Ang II promotes sodium reabsorption in the proximal tubule by stimulating the Na+/H+ exchanger 3 (NHE3), which facilitates the exchange of luminal sodium for intracellular hydrogen ions, thereby increasing bicarbonate and fluid uptake. This effect accounts for a substantial portion of proximal tubular sodium recovery, up to 50-70% of filtered load, and is mediated via angiotensin type 1 (AT1) receptors on tubular epithelial cells. Ang II's actions synergize with aldosterone to enhance overall renal sodium retention, particularly in distal segments, amplifying conservation during volume depletion.
The local renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) within the kidney plays a crucial role in autoregulation, independent of systemic levels, by producing angiotensin peptides intratubularly and in the interstitium to regulate renal blood flow and pressure natriuresis. Elevated intrarenal Ang II contributes to the progression of diabetic nephropathy by promoting glomerular hyperfiltration, mesangial expansion, and proteinuria through sustained vasoconstriction and inflammatory pathways.
In contrast, angiotensin (1-7) , acting via the Mas receptor, provides protective counter-regulatory effects by promoting natriuresis and diuresis, which counteract sodium retention and support fluid balance. Furthermore, Ang(1-7) attenuates renal fibrosis by reducing extracellular matrix deposition and inflammation in models of kidney injury, offering potential therapeutic benefits in fibrotic conditions.

Adrenal Glands

Angiotensin II (Ang II) primarily exerts its effects on the adrenal cortex by binding to the angiotensin type 1 receptor (AT1R) on cells of the zona glomerulosa, the outermost layer responsible for mineralocorticoid production. This binding activates the phospholipase C (PLC) signaling pathway, leading to an increase in intracellular calcium (Ca) levels, which in turn stimulates the enzymatic steps of aldosterone biosynthesis, including the conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone and subsequent transformations to aldosterone. The elevated Ca also promotes the rapid secretion of newly synthesized aldosterone into the circulation, contributing to sodium retention and blood pressure regulation.
Angiotensin III (Ang III), a metabolite of Ang II, is approximately as potent as Ang II in stimulating aldosterone release from the zona glomerulosa, acting through both AT1R and AT2R, with a partial contribution from AT2R due to Ang III's greater affinity for it. This results in Ca signaling and aldosterone production, primarily mediated by AT1R.
In the zona fasciculata, the inner layer that produces glucocorticoids such as cortisol, Ang II has weaker direct stimulatory effects compared to its actions in the zona glomerulosa, but it can synergize with adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) to enhance cyclic AMP production and glucocorticoid secretion. This interaction amplifies the steroidogenic response during conditions of stress or volume depletion, though ACTH remains the dominant regulator.
The influence of Ang II on adrenal function is both acute and chronic: acutely, it rapidly induces steroidogenesis via Ca-dependent pathways, leading to immediate hormone release; chronically, it promotes trophic effects, including hyperplasia and hypertrophy of zona glomerulosa cells, thereby increasing the adrenal's biosynthetic capacity for sustained aldosterone output. These long-term adaptations involve transcriptional regulation of genes encoding steroidogenic enzymes and growth factors.

Central Nervous System

Angiotensin II (Ang II) exerts significant influence within the central nervous system (CNS), particularly in circumventricular organs lacking a blood-brain barrier, such as the subfornical organ (SFO), where it modulates autonomic functions and behaviors essential for fluid and electrolyte homeostasis. In the SFO, Ang II binding to angiotensin type 1 (AT1) receptors stimulates thirst and salt appetite, driving ingestive behaviors in response to hypovolemia or hyperosmolality. This action involves neural projections from the SFO to hypothalamic and brainstem regions, enhancing water and sodium intake to restore plasma volume and osmolality. Studies in transgenic rats with targeted Ang II overexpression in the SFO demonstrate markedly increased water and salt consumption, which is attenuated by AT1 receptor blockade, underscoring the receptor's pivotal role.
Beyond ingestive drives, Ang II in the hypothalamus promotes the release of arginine vasopressin (AVP, also known as antidiuretic hormone or ADH) from the posterior pituitary, aiding in water conservation by the kidneys. This effect is mediated primarily through AT1 receptors on paraventricular and supraoptic nuclei neurons, where Ang II enhances AVP synthesis and secretion in response to osmotic or volume stimuli. Electrophysiological evidence shows that Ang II directly excites AVP-producing neurons, increasing AVP release by up to 268% at physiological concentrations, thereby contributing to antidiuresis and blood pressure maintenance.
In the brainstem, particularly the nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS), Ang II modulates the baroreflex to regulate sympathetic outflow and cardiovascular homeostasis. Acting via AT1 receptors, Ang II attenuates baroreflex sensitivity, facilitating increased sympathetic nerve activity to the heart and vessels, which elevates blood pressure during stress or hypovolemia. This central modulation overrides peripheral baroreceptor inputs, as seen in models where intracerebroventricular Ang II infusion impairs baroreflex gain and boosts sympathetic drive, contributing to hypertension pathogenesis. AT2 receptor activation in the same region can counterbalance this by enhancing baroreflex function and reducing sympathetic outflow.
Angiotensin IV (Ang IV), a metabolite of Ang II, plays a distinct role in cognitive processes, particularly memory formation, through its interaction with the AT4 receptor, identified as insulin-regulated aminopeptidase (IRAP). In the hippocampus, Ang IV binding to IRAP inhibits the enzyme's activity, enhancing synaptic plasticity and long-term potentiation, which are critical for learning and memory consolidation. Central administration of Ang IV rapidly improves novel object recognition and spatial memory in rodent models, with effects linked to hippocampal AT4/IRAP expression, suggesting therapeutic potential for cognitive deficits. This mechanism involves modulation of neurotransmitter release and neuronal excitability without affecting thirst or vasopressin pathways.

Adipose Tissue

Adipose tissue expresses a local renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), with adipocytes producing key components such as angiotensinogen, renin, angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), and angiotensin II (Ang II), enabling autocrine and paracrine regulation of fat cell function. This local RAAS is upregulated in obesity, contributing to adipocyte hypertrophy and dysfunction independently of the systemic RAAS.
Ang II, acting primarily through the AT1 receptor on adipocytes, promotes lipogenesis by upregulating enzymes like fatty acid synthase and lipoprotein lipase while downregulating lipolysis, thereby favoring fat accumulation. AT1 receptor activation also induces inflammation in adipose tissue by stimulating pro-inflammatory cytokine release from adipocytes and infiltrating macrophages, exacerbating local and systemic inflammatory states. Furthermore, Ang II via AT1 impairs insulin signaling in adipocytes, leading to reduced glucose uptake and insulin resistance, a hallmark of obesity-related metabolic disturbances.
In obesity, adipose angiotensin levels are elevated, particularly during adipocyte hypertrophy, where enlarged fat cells overexpress RAAS components, amplifying local Ang II production. This heightened adipose RAAS activity contributes to hypertension in metabolic syndrome by increasing sympathetic outflow and vascular tone, linking fat expansion directly to cardiovascular complications.
In contrast, angiotensin (1-7) , acting via the Mas receptor, exerts protective counter-effects by inhibiting adipocyte differentiation and reducing adipocyte size in models of diet-induced obesity. Ang(1-7) also enhances insulin sensitivity in adipose tissue, improving glucose tolerance and lowering insulin resistance indices without altering food intake.
RAAS expression is tissue-specific within adipose depots, with higher angiotensinogen and ACE activity observed in visceral fat compared to subcutaneous fat in humans, correlating with greater metabolic risk. Ang II via AT1 negatively regulates peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma (PPAR) in adipocytes, suppressing its expression and thereby impairing anti-inflammatory and insulin-sensitizing functions; AT1 blockade reverses this, elevating PPAR levels and promoting healthier adipose remodeling.

Clinical Significance

Role in Diseases

Chronic activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) plays a central role in the pathophysiology of hypertension, where upregulated angiotensin II (Ang II) levels promote vascular smooth muscle proliferation and extracellular matrix deposition, leading to arterial remodeling and increased vascular resistance. This sustained RAAS activity also enhances sodium retention in the kidneys through aldosterone-mediated mechanisms, exacerbating blood pressure elevation. Genetic variations, such as the M235T polymorphism in the angiotensinogen (AGT) gene, have been associated with increased plasma AGT levels and higher susceptibility to essential hypertension in multiple populations.
In cardiovascular diseases, Ang II contributes to heart failure by inducing myocardial fibrosis through the activation of cardiac fibroblasts and the promotion of collagen synthesis, which stiffens the ventricular wall and impairs diastolic function. Similarly, in atherosclerosis, Ang II accelerates plaque formation and instability by stimulating oxidative stress, inflammation, and smooth muscle cell migration within arterial walls, increasing the risk of rupture and acute events like myocardial infarction. Endogenous Ang II elevation has been shown to shift plaque composition toward a vulnerable phenotype, characterized by a thinner fibrous cap and larger necrotic core.
Ang II exacerbates chronic kidney disease by causing glomerular hypertension, which increases intraglomerular pressure and leads to podocyte injury and proteinuria through enhanced filtration of proteins across the glomerular barrier. This effect is mediated by preferential constriction of the efferent arteriole, amplifying local Ang II actions and promoting progressive renal damage independent of systemic blood pressure.
In COVID-19, severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) infection disrupts the balance of the RAAS by downregulating angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2), reducing conversion of Ang II to the protective peptide Ang(1-7) and leading to unchecked Ang II accumulation that drives inflammation and endothelial injury. This imbalance correlates with repressed Ang(1-7) levels, which are inversely associated with disease severity and contribute to multi-organ dysfunction through heightened pro-inflammatory signaling.
Dysregulation in diabetes features reduced Ang(1-7) production, which impairs endothelial function by diminishing nitric oxide bioavailability and exacerbating oxidative stress in vascular tissues, thereby promoting microvascular complications. The resulting imbalance favoring Ang II over Ang(1-7) is linked to increased vascular inflammation and dysfunction in diabetic patients, highlighting a shift away from protective RAAS counter-regulation.

Pharmacological Targets

The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) serves as a primary pharmacological target for managing hypertension, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease (CKD) through drugs that interrupt angiotensin production or action. These agents, including angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors, angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs), renin inhibitors, and neprilysin inhibitors, reduce vasoconstriction, aldosterone release, and sodium retention while mitigating RAAS-driven pathology.
ACE inhibitors, such as captoprilthe first approved in 1981and enalapril, competitively inhibit the angiotensin-converting enzyme, preventing the conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II and thereby reducing vasoconstriction and aldosterone secretion; they also increase bradykinin levels, enhancing vasodilation. These drugs are indicated for hypertension, heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), post-myocardial infarction, and diabetic nephropathy, with captopril demonstrating reduced mortality in heart failure trials and enalapril showing blood pressure reductions of 10-15 mmHg systolic in hypertensive patients. Common side effects include cough due to bradykinin accumulation and hyperkalemia, but they remain first-line for many patients per guidelines.
ARBs, exemplified by losartanthe first approved in 1995selectively antagonize the angiotensin II type 1 (AT1) receptor, blocking its effects on vasoconstriction, cell proliferation, and inflammation without affecting bradykinin, thus avoiding the cough associated with ACE inhibitors. Losartan and similar agents like valsartan are used for hypertension, HFrEF, and CKD, particularly in diabetic patients, where they slow proteinuria progression by up to 30% compared to placebo. In heart failure, ARBs reduce hospitalizations by 13-24% in landmark trials like Val-HeFT, serving as alternatives or add-ons to ACE inhibitors.
Direct renin inhibitors like aliskiren, approved in 2007, bind to renin and prevent the cleavage of angiotensinogen to angiotensin I, suppressing the entire RAAS cascade upstream of ACE and AT1 receptors. Primarily indicated for hypertension, aliskiren lowers systolic blood pressure by 10-14 mmHg as monotherapy and is often combined with other agents, though its use is limited in patients with diabetes or CKD due to risks like hyperkalemia observed in trials such as ALTITUDE.
Angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitors (ARNIs), such as sacubitril/valsartan approved in 2015, combine ARB action with neprilysin inhibition to enhance natriuretic peptides and angiotensin-(1-7) levels, promoting vasodilation and natriuresis while countering RAAS overactivation. In HFrEF, the PARADIGM-HF trial showed sacubitril/valsartan reduced cardiovascular death and heart failure hospitalizations by 20% versus enalapril, establishing it as preferred over ACE inhibitors for many patients.
Emerging therapies targeting the Mas receptor, activated by angiotensin-(1-7), include investigational agonists like AVE0991, which demonstrate cardioprotective effects in preclinical models of diabetes and hypertension by reducing fibrosis and improving insulin sensitivity, though no agents have reached widespread clinical approval.
In clinical practice, RAAS inhibitors are foundational for hypertension management, with the 2025 AHA/ACC guideline emphasizing combination therapiessuch as ACE inhibitors or ARBs with diuretics or calcium channel blockersto achieve blood pressure goals below 130/80 mmHg in most adults, particularly those with CKD or diabetes, reducing cardiovascular events by 20-30%. For heart failure, guidelines recommend initiating ACE inhibitors or ARBs early, transitioning to ARNIs when tolerated, and using renin inhibitors selectively in resistant cases.
