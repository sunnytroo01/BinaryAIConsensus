Dry matter
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Dry matter (DM), also known as dry weight, is the mass of a substance remaining after the complete removal of water, typically achieved by drying a sample at 100105C until it reaches a constant weight. This measure excludes moisture and includes all non-aqueous components, such as organic compounds (e.g., carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and fibers) and inorganic elements (e.g., minerals and ash), providing a standardized basis for evaluating the composition and nutritional value of biological materials.
In agriculture and animal nutrition, dry matter is essential for assessing feed quality and formulating balanced rations, as varying moisture levels in forages and concentrates can dilute nutrient concentrations; rations are thus calculated on a DM basis to ensure consistent delivery of energy, protein, and other essentials. Dry matter intake (DMI) directly influences animal productivity, with cattle typically consuming 24% of their body weight in DM daily, depending on diet quality and physiological state. Measurement methods include oven drying, microwave drying, or near-infrared spectroscopy, with on-farm testing recommended for silage and hay to optimize fermentation and storage.
In plant biology and crop science, dry matter accumulation reflects photosynthetic productivity and resource allocation, with over 90% of a plant's DM derived from carbon dioxide fixed during photosynthesis, while the remainder comes from soil nutrients like nitrogen and minerals. It is used to gauge growth rates, yield potential, and residual biomass management in rangelands, where maintaining adequate residual dry matter (e.g., 8001,200 kg/ha) prevents soil erosion and supports regeneration. In horticulture, higher DM content in fruits like pears (>1416%) correlates with improved flavor, texture, and storage quality, often enhanced through cultural practices such as pruning.
In food science and processing, dry matter determines product stability, sensory attributes, and compliance with labeling standards, as it indicates the concentration of solids that affect shelf life and digestibility; for instance, low-DM foods like fresh produce require adjusted processing to prevent spoilage. Common analytical techniques mirror those in agriculture, ensuring accurate reporting of nutrient profiles on a DM basis for comparative purposes across wet and dry products.

Fundamentals

Definition

Dry matter refers to the portion of a substance remaining after the removal of all water content, encompassing the solid components such as carbohydrates, proteins, fats, minerals, and fibers. This non-aqueous fraction represents the nutrient-bearing elements of materials like plant and animal tissues, excluding moisture that can vary widely depending on environmental and physiological factors.
The percentage of dry matter in a sample is mathematically expressed as:
%DryMatter=(massofdriedsampleoriginalmass)100
\% \text{ Dry Matter} = \left( \frac{\text{mass of dried sample}}{\text{original mass}} \right) \times 100
%DryMatter=(originalmassmassofdriedsample)100
This formula quantifies the proportion of solids relative to the initial wet weight. Measurements on a wet basis reflect the composition including water, whereas dry basis calculations normalize values to the water-free solids, enabling consistent comparisons across samples with differing moisture levels. For instance, in natural materials such as fresh produce, dry matter content typically ranges from 5% to 30%, corresponding to water levels of 70% to 95%.
The concept of dry matter developed in agricultural chemistry during the mid-19th century, coinciding with the development of proximate analysis methods for evaluating feed quality, such as the Weende system introduced in the 1850s1860s. These early analytical approaches emphasized drying samples to isolate non-volatile solids, laying the foundation for standardized assessments in plant and soil sciences. Dry matter serves as a basis for standardizing nutrient comparisons, as detailed in subsequent sections.

Significance

Dry matter serves as a fundamental metric in nutrition, agriculture, and food processing by eliminating the variability introduced by differing water contents in samples, thereby enabling accurate and standardized comparisons of nutrient profiles, quality, and performance across diverse materials. In animal nutrition, for instance, expressing feed rations on a dry matter basis ensures that fluctuations in moisturecommon in silages or foragesdo not skew assessments of energy, protein, or mineral availability, allowing for precise diet formulation that optimizes livestock health, milk production, and reproductive outcomes. Similarly, in food processing and supplement manufacturing, this approach standardizes ingredient potency and nutritional value, facilitating reliable quality control and batch-to-batch consistency, as required by industry monographs from bodies like the United States Pharmacopeia (USP) and European Pharmacopoeia (EP).
Economically, dry matter analysis profoundly influences pricing, yield assessments, and regulatory compliance in agricultural and food sectors. When buying or selling feeds and forages, transactions are often adjusted to a dry matter basis to avoid overpaying for excess water, ensuring fair valuation based on actual nutrient content rather than total weight; for example, a 10% variation in dry matter can significantly alter the effective cost per unit of digestible energy. In crop production, yields are frequently reported on a dry matter basis to provide a consistent measure of productivity, aiding farmers in optimizing harvest timing and resource allocation for maximum economic return. Regulatory frameworks further mandate minimum dry matter standards in contracts and specifications to enforce compliance with quality thresholds, preventing disputes and supporting transparent trade in commodities like grains and dairy products.
In environmental management, dry matter quantification is essential for evaluating solid content in waste streams, promoting sustainable practices in sludge treatment and composting. Sewage sludge and biosolids are assessed per ton of dry matter to determine treatment efficacy, nutrient recovery, and disposal impacts, aligning with circular economy principles by enabling the reuse of organic matter while minimizing landfill volumes and pollutant releases. Composting operations rely on dry matter metrics to monitor decomposition progress and ensure the final product meets environmental safety standards for soil amendment, reducing greenhouse gas emissions compared to incineration or landfilling.
Beyond food and agriculture, dry matter plays a critical role in pharmaceutical applications, where drying processes remove moisture to enhance drug stability and extend shelf life. Therapeutic proteins and active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) are often formulated into dry powders to prevent degradation caused by residual water, which can trigger hydrolysis or microbial growth; techniques like freeze-drying or spray-drying achieve this by targeting low moisture levels, ensuring long-term efficacy at ambient temperatures. This standardization is vital for regulatory approval and global distribution, as it mitigates variability in product stability across manufacturing batches.

Determination Methods

Drying Techniques

Drying techniques for determining dry matter content involve the physical removal of water from a sample through controlled heating or evaporation processes, serving as foundational laboratory methods in analytical chemistry and quality control. These methods are destructive, requiring the sample to be heated until a constant weight is achieved, indicating complete water removal. Among them, oven drying remains the most widely adopted reference standard due to its reliability and simplicity for a broad range of materials, including foods, feeds, and biological samples.
The standard oven drying method, often conducted at 105C, is recommended by the Association of Official Analytical Chemists (AOAC) for most samples to ensure thorough dehydration without excessive degradation. The procedure typically begins with accurately weighing a known mass of the sample (usually 2-5 grams) into a pre-dried, weighed aluminum dish or crucible. The sample is then placed in a forced-air convection oven preheated to 105C and dried for 3-6 hours, or until consecutive weighings after 30-minute intervals show no further weight loss (typically less than 0.5 mg change). After cooling in a desiccator to room temperature, the sample is reweighed, and the dry matter percentage is calculated as the ratio of the final dry weight to the initial wet weight, multiplied by 100. This method's time requirement can vary from 4 to 24 hours depending on sample moisture content and thickness, making it suitable for routine analysis but less ideal for high-throughput needs.
For faster assessments, particularly in animal feed analysis, microwave drying offers a rapid alternative that can complete in 5-10 minutes, though it requires calibration against the oven method to account for potential over-drying. In this approach, a sample is placed in a microwave oven at a controlled power level (e.g., 600-1000 W) and heated in short bursts with intermittent weighing until constant weight is reached; studies have shown correlations exceeding 0.95 with oven results when properly standardized for feed types like silage or hay. Calibration curves are established by comparing microwave results from multiple feed samples to oven-dried references, adjusting for factors such as particle size and initial moisture. This technique is particularly valued in agricultural settings for its efficiency, despite needing validation to minimize errors from uneven heating.
Vacuum drying and freeze-drying are employed for heat-sensitive materials, such as pharmaceuticals or certain biological products, to preserve volatile components while removing water at lower temperatures. Vacuum drying operates at reduced pressure (e.g., 50-100 mbar) and temperatures around 60-80C, accelerating evaporation and typically requiring 2-8 hours; it is detailed in pharmacopeial standards for ensuring dry matter accuracy in drug formulations without thermal decomposition. Freeze-drying, or lyophilization, involves freezing the sample at -40C to -80C followed by sublimation under vacuum (below 0.1 mbar), which can take 12-48 hours but minimizes structural damage and loss of heat-labile substances, making it essential for high-value pharmaceuticals. These methods yield dry matter results comparable to oven drying for sensitive samples, with recovery rates often above 99% for water removal.
Despite their utility, drying techniques have limitations, including the potential loss of volatile compounds other than water, which can lead to underestimation of dry matter by 0.5-2% in samples rich in essential oils or solvents. Error sources also encompass incomplete drying if the endpoint is not reached, influenced by sample homogeneity or oven uniformity, and over-drying in methods like microwave, which may degrade organic matter and inflate apparent dry matter by up to 1-3%. To mitigate these, analysts often perform duplicate or triplicate runs and use desiccators to prevent moisture reabsorption during cooling. For non-destructive alternatives, instrumental methods such as near-infrared spectroscopy are increasingly used, as detailed elsewhere.

Instrumental Methods

Instrumental methods for determining dry matter content emphasize rapid, non-destructive techniques that enable real-time analysis in various industries, contrasting with traditional drying by avoiding sample alteration and lengthy processes. These approaches rely on physical properties such as light absorption, refraction, or dielectric responses to estimate dry matter indirectly, often calibrated against reference standards like oven drying.
Near-infrared spectroscopy (NIR) is a widely adopted non-destructive method for estimating dry matter in solid samples like grains and animal feeds, utilizing the absorption of near-infrared light (typically 7002500 nm) by molecular bonds in water and organic compounds. Calibration models, often developed using partial least-squares regression, correlate NIR spectra with dry matter percentages obtained from reference drying methods, achieving prediction accuracies with coefficients of determination (R) exceeding 0.90 in many cases for undried samples. For instance, NIR has been applied to whole-plant corn forage, where portable devices provide on-site predictions with root mean square errors around 23% dry matter.
Emerging portable NIR devices have advanced field applications in agriculture since 2020, allowing farmers to scan crops like forages directly in the field without sample preparation, thus supporting timely decisions on harvest and quality. These handheld units, integrated with smartphone apps for data processing, leverage miniaturized spectrometers and machine learning algorithms to deliver results in seconds, with studies reporting R values of 0.850.95 for dry matter in undried forage samples.
Refractometry measures the refractive index of liquid samples to estimate dry matter, particularly in fruit juices and extracts, where the Brix scale (Bx) serves as a proxy for soluble solids content, which correlates strongly with total dry matter. The refractive index increases with higher concentrations of dissolved solids like sugars, enabling direct conversion to Brix using standardized tables, with correlations to dry matter often exceeding 0.90 in fruits such as apples and grapes. This method is especially useful for assessing fruit maturity, as factors enhancing dry matter accumulation proportionally elevate Brix readings.
Microwave density meters determine dry matter in industrial processes like sugar production by measuring the dielectric properties of liquids and massecuites, where microwaves interact with water molecules to gauge concentration and density. These inline sensors provide continuous monitoring of dry substance content (often reported as Brix), with accuracies within 0.10.5% in sugar syrups, facilitating precise control in crystallizers and evaporators. For example, systems like the MicroPolar Brix use microwave polarization to capture rotational changes in water, enabling non-invasive installation in pipelines for real-time dry matter assessment.

Applications in Food and Nutrition

Water Composition in Foods

Water content in foods varies significantly across categories, directly influencing the proportion of dry matter. Fresh fruits and vegetables typically contain 70-95% water, corresponding to 5-30% dry matter, due to their high cellular turgor and structural composition. For instance, leafy greens like lettuce have approximately 96% water (4% dry matter), while fruits such as apples exhibit about 86% water (14% dry matter). In contrast, low-water foods like grains maintain 10-15% water after drying, yielding 85-90% dry matter to ensure stability and prevent spoilage. Dry white rice, for example, contains roughly 12% water (88% dry matter), and wheat flour is similarly around 12% water (88% dry matter).
Several factors affect water composition in foods, including biological maturity and processing methods. Ripeness can alter water retention; as fruits mature, changes in cell membranes may slightly increase water content or affect its distribution, enhancing juiciness in items like bananas (75% water at ripeness). Processing, particularly drying, drastically reduces water levelsfor powdered milk, dehydration lowers moisture to less than 5% (over 95% dry matter) to extend shelf life.
Biologically, water serves as a universal solvent in foods, facilitating the dissolution and transport of nutrients, enzymes, and metabolites essential for metabolic processes. Conversely, dry matter contributes to caloric density, as it concentrates macronutrients like carbohydrates and fats without the diluting effect of water, which provides no calories. This inverse relationship underscores the use of dry matter basis for standardizing nutrient comparisons across foods with varying moisture levels.

Nutrient Analysis on Dry Matter Basis

Nutrient analysis on a dry matter basis standardizes the expression of nutrient content by excluding water, allowing for accurate comparisons across foods with varying moisture levels. This approach calculates the percentage of a nutrient relative to the total dry matter, which is the portion of the food excluding water. The formula for converting a nutrient percentage from a wet (as-fed) basis to a dry matter basis is:
Nutrient%ondrymatterbasis=(nutrient%onwetbasis1waterfraction)100
\text{Nutrient \% on dry matter basis} = \left( \frac{\text{nutrient \% on wet basis}}{1 - \text{water fraction}} \right) \times 100
Nutrient%ondrymatterbasis=(1waterfractionnutrient%onwetbasis)100
where the water fraction is the moisture content as a decimal (e.g., 87% water = 0.87).
For example, cow's milk typically contains about 3.5% protein on a wet basis and 87% water, resulting in 13% dry matter; applying the formula yields approximately 26.9% protein on a dry matter basis. This conversion is commonly used in food labeling and research to highlight the concentrated nutrient profile in high-moisture foods like milk.
The primary advantages of dry matter basis analysis include enabling fair comparisons between moist and dry products, such as fresh fruits versus dried equivalents, by eliminating moisture variability as a confounding factor. It also supports regulatory compliance in food standards, where nutrient levels for certain products, like milk powders, are specified on a dry matter basis to ensure consistency in quality and safety assessments.
This method extends to minerals and vitamins, which are adjusted similarly since they reside in the dry matter fraction; for instance, calcium or vitamin content in dairy is often reported this way in composition databases to facilitate precise dietary planning and international standardization.

Fat Content on Dry Matter Basis

The fat content on a dry matter (FDM) basis is a critical metric in the analysis of high-moisture dairy products, providing a standardized measure that accounts for varying water levels to ensure consistent quality and nutritional profiling. This approach is particularly essential in cheese production, where moisture content can range widely, influencing apparent fat percentages on a wet basis. By expressing fat as a proportion of the non-water solids, FDM enables accurate comparison across products and compliance with regulatory standards.
The formula for calculating FDM is given by:
FDM(%)=(fatpercentageonwetbasisdrymatterpercentage)100
\text{FDM (\%)} = \left( \frac{\text{fat percentage on wet basis}}{\text{dry matter percentage}} \right) \times 100
FDM(%)=(drymatterpercentagefatpercentageonwetbasis)100
where the dry matter percentage is 100 minus the moisture percentage. For instance, in a cheese sample with 50% moisture (thus 50% dry matter) and 25% fat on a wet basis, the FDM would be (25 / 50) 100 = 50%, indicating half of the solids are fat. This calculation is routinely applied during manufacturing to adjust milk composition, such as by adding skim milk or cream, to target specific FDM values for desired cheese varieties.
International grading standards, such as those from the Codex Alimentarius, classify cheeses based on FDM thresholds to denote fat levels: full-fat cheeses require at least 45% FDM but less than 60%, while high-fat varieties exceed 60%. In the United States, federal regulations under 21 CFR Part 133 similarly mandate minimum FDM levels, such as 50% for Cheddar cheese, to ensure product identity and quality. These standards facilitate quality control in cheese production by verifying that formulations meet compositional requirements, preventing adulteration and maintaining uniformity.
FDM plays a key role in determining cheese texture and shelf life, as higher fat levels in the dry matter enhance creaminess and meltability while influencing microbial stability. For example, reduced FDM correlates with increased chewiness and firmness due to higher protein-to-fat ratios, affecting sensory attributes in processed and natural cheeses. In quality control, monitoring FDM helps predict these properties, optimizing ripening conditions and packaging to extend shelf life by mitigating fat oxidation and moisture-related spoilage.

Applications in Animal Feed and Agriculture

Feed Formulation and Quality

In animal feed formulation, the dry matter basis (DMB) standardizes nutrient comparisons across feeds by excluding water content, enabling accurate assessment of energy, protein, and other components regardless of moisture levels. For instance, hay typically contains about 90% dry matter, allowing direct nutrient evaluation, while silage often has only 30% dry matter, necessitating DMB adjustments to avoid underestimating its nutritional value. This approach ensures balanced rations by focusing on the non-water portion, which represents the actual digestible material.
In total mixed rations (TMR), dry matter plays a critical role in adjusting ingredient proportions to meet livestock energy and protein requirements, as varying moisture levels can otherwise dilute nutrient delivery. Formulators account for dry matter content when mixing feeds, increasing the volume of wetter components like silage to maintain targeted nutrient levels in the final ration. This adjustment prevents imbalances that could affect animal performance, such as reduced milk production in dairy cows.
Feed quality is often evaluated using dry matter thresholds to ensure suitability for storage and minimize spoilage risks. Hay, for example, should ideally exceed 85% dry matter at baling to facilitate aerobic stability and reduce mold growth during storage. Lower dry matter levels increase moisture-related issues, such as bacterial fermentation or nutrient loss, compromising feed efficacy.
Modern feed optimization integrates dry matter data into software tools like Spartan or CPM-Dairy, which automatically recalibrate rations based on updated moisture analyses for cost-effective, nutrient-precise formulations. These programs enable real-time adjustments, enhancing overall feed efficiency in commercial operations.

Dry Matter Intake in Livestock

Dry matter intake (DMI) refers to the total amount of feed consumed by livestock on a moisture-free basis, typically expressed as a percentage of body weight, which standardizes feed evaluation by excluding water content that varies widely across forages and concentrates. In ruminants like cattle, DMI is a critical metric for assessing nutritional adequacy, as it directly influences energy availability for growth, maintenance, and production without the dilution effect of moisture.
Typical DMI rates for cattle range from 2% to 3% of body weight daily, though this can vary; for instance, mature beef cows often consume around 2-2.5% of their body weight in dry matter, while lactating dairy cows may reach 3-4% due to higher energy demands. These rates provide a benchmark for ration planning, ensuring animals meet requirements without over- or under-consumption that could lead to health issues or inefficiency. Similar principles apply to non-ruminants, such as pigs (2-5% of body weight) and poultry, where DMI influences growth and egg production.
DMI is calculated by adjusting as-fed (wet) feed intake for its dry matter content, using the formula DMI = as-fed intake (dry matter percentage / 100), where dry matter percentage is determined as 100 minus the moisture percentage of the feed. This adjustment is essential in mixed diets, as forages like silage may contain 60-80% moisture, while grains are drier, allowing accurate nutrient delivery estimates.
Several factors influence DMI in livestock, including animal species, physiological stage, and environmental conditions; for example, lactation in dairy cows increases DMI by up to 50% to support milk production, while heat stress can reduce it by 10-20% through altered feeding behavior. Body weight also plays a role, with larger animals capable of higher absolute intake, and diet characteristics like neutral detergent fiber (NDF) content impose physical limits, as cattle typically consume no more than 1.2% of body weight in NDF to avoid rumen fill constraints. Species differences are notable, with sheep and goats often exhibiting 2-4% body weight DMI, adjusted for their smaller size and browsing habits.
Monitoring DMI is vital in livestock management, particularly in dairy farming where it serves as a primary predictor of milk yield, with studies showing a 1 kg increase in DMI correlating to 2 to 2.5 kg additional milk production daily. Common tools include internal markers such as indigestible neutral detergent fiber (iNDF), which is measured in feed and feces to estimate intake and digestibility without direct observation, offering a non-invasive alternative to total collection methods. These markers enable precise tracking in grazing systems, where visual estimation is unreliable, helping farmers optimize feed allocation and reduce waste.
Recent studies from the 2020s have advanced DMI monitoring through precision feeding technologies, such as machine learning models that predict intake using phenotypic data like body weight and water consumption, with improved accuracy over traditional equations in feedlot settings. These approaches, including AI-driven monitoring systems, are being applied to enhance sustainability and efficiency in livestock production, including grazing systems.

Broader Applications

In Soil and Plant Science

In soil and plant science, dry matter serves as a fundamental metric for evaluating plant biomass yield, representing the mass of plant material excluding water content, which is particularly useful for assessing harvested portions such as grains or tubers. This measurement allows researchers to quantify the efficiency of resource allocation in crops under varying environmental conditions. A key indicator derived from dry matter is the harvest index (HI), defined as the ratio of dry seed or grain weight to the total dry plant weight above ground, which reflects the plant's ability to partition photosynthates toward economically valuable parts. For instance, in cereals like wheat and maize, HI values typically range from 0.4 to 0.6, highlighting breeding goals to enhance this ratio for improved yields without excessive vegetative growth.
Soil dry matter, specifically the organic fraction obtained after oven-drying samples to remove moisture, is essential for assessing soil fertility and health. This organic matter, often comprising 1-5% of total soil dry weight, improves nutrient retention, water-holding capacity, and microbial activity. In fertility tests, soils with organic dry matter exceeding 3% are generally rated as high in quality, supporting robust crop productivity, whereas levels below 1% indicate degradation and reduced fertility. Standard determination involves drying soil at 105C until constant weight, followed by loss-on-ignition or chemical oxidation methods to isolate the organic component.
Applications of dry matter analysis extend to crop breeding programs, where selecting for high dry matter accumulation enhances overall biomass production and yield stability. Breeders target genotypes that maximize post-anthesis dry matter remobilization to grains, as seen in maize hybrids that increase dry matter partitioning by up to 16% under optimal conditions, contributing to sustainable intensification. Additionally, reductions in plant dry matter under drought stress serve as reliable indicators of water limitation, with sensitive declines in root and shoot biomass signaling early physiological impacts before visible wilting occurs. In sustainable agriculture, soil organic dry matter plays a pivotal role in carbon sequestration metrics, as increased levels through practices like cover cropping can store up to 0.15-0.5 tons of carbon per hectare annually, mitigating greenhouse gas emissions while bolstering long-term soil resilience.

In Biomass and Bioenergy

In the context of biomass and bioenergy, dry matter serves as a critical metric for assessing the productivity and viability of dedicated energy crops, enabling standardized comparisons of yield potential across different feedstocks. For instance, switchgrass (Panicum virgatum), a prominent perennial bioenergy crop, typically achieves annual dry matter yields ranging from 5.2 to 11.1 Mg/ha under established field conditions, with optimized management pushing yields above 10 Mg/ha in suitable environments. These yields are harvested as dry matter to maximize transport efficiency and storage, as fresh biomass often contains 50-70% moisture that dilutes energy density. Reducing moisture content to below 15% is essential for efficient combustion in bioenergy systems, as higher levels lead to incomplete burning, increased emissions, and reduced boiler performance; this threshold aligns with operational ideals for energy recovery plants processing solid biomass fuels.
The energy potential of biomass is fundamentally tied to its dry matter content, where the total energy value can be approximated by multiplying the dry matter mass by the material's calorific value. For wood-based biomass, a representative calorific value is approximately 18 MJ/kg on a dry basis, reflecting the net heating potential after accounting for inherent moisture effects.

This calculation underscores why dry matter basis reporting is standard in bioenergy assessments, as it isolates the combustible organic fraction from variable water content.
Processing techniques like torrefaction enhance the stability and utility of dry matter in biomass by subjecting it to mild pyrolysis at 200-320C in an oxygen-limited environment, resulting in a more hydrophobic, denser product with improved grindability and resistance to biological degradation. Torrefied biomass retains about 70-90% of its original energy content while losing 20-30% of its dry mass as volatiles, thereby increasing energy density to levels comparable to coal (around 20-25 MJ/kg) and facilitating easier handling in power plants. Standardization of dry matter determination is crucial for quality control, with ASTM E1756 providing a validated oven-drying method to measure total solids in biomass samples, correcting for volatiles and ensuring accurate reporting on an oven-dry basis.
Post-2020 EU bioenergy policies, particularly under the revised Renewable Energy Directive (RED III, Directive (EU) 2023/2413, which updated RED II in 2023), emphasize strengthened sustainability criteria for solid biomass fuels while implicitly requiring adherence to quality specifications that include dry matter metrics to support efficient energy conversion and at least 42.5% greenhouse gas savings. For woody biomass pellets, the ENplus certification schemewidely adopted across the EUmandates a maximum moisture content of 10% to guarantee combustion efficiency and compliance with RED III's criteria. These requirements build on earlier policies by integrating dry matter stability into broader supply chain standards, promoting reliable bioenergy contributions toward the EU's at least 42.5% renewable energy target by 2030.
