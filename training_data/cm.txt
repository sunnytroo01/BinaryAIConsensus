 CM& (also CM& Televisin) was a Colombian independent television production company and programadora, primarily known for producing the news program Noticiero CM&, one of the longest-running in the country. Founded on January 2, 1992, by journalist Yamid Amat, it created content mainly for Canal Uno, surviving industry crises and reforms until its programming was discontinued on November 14, 2024, following changes by Canal Uno's owner, Grupo Prisa.

History

Founding and early years (19921990s)

Compaa de Medios de Informacin (CM&), a Colombian programadora specializing in news production, was founded in late 1991 and launched its flagship program, Noticiero CM& (initially known as Noticiero CMI), on January 2, 1992. The newscast aired weekdays at 9:30 p.m. on Canal Uno (formerly Cadena Uno), operating within Colombia's mixed television system where independent programadoras produced content for state channels. Established by journalists Yamid Amat and Juan Gossan, the program emphasized rigorous reporting on national politics, security issues, and international affairs amid Colombia's turbulent early 1990s context, including escalating guerrilla conflicts and narcotrafficking violence.
Amat served as the primary director from inception, shaping the broadcast's format around live anchors, on-site correspondents, and in-depth analysis segments that distinguished it from competitors like Noticiero C newscast. In its first years, CM& quickly gained viewership by covering pivotal events such as the 1991 constitutional assembly and ongoing peace talks with groups like the FARC and ELN, prioritizing factual fieldwork over studio commentary. The programadora's early success stemmed from its operational model, leveraging a team of experienced reporters to deliver consistent evening bulletins, which helped it secure advertising revenue and establish credibility in a market dominated by a handful of programadoras. By the mid-1990s, Noticiero CM& had become one of Canal Uno's cornerstone programs, winning multiple India Catalina Awards for news excellence, reflecting its adherence to journalistic standards during a decade marked by media challenges from censorship pressures and advertiser caution toward controversial topics.
Throughout the 1990s, CM& expanded its contributions beyond prime-time news, producing supplementary segments and specials for Canal Uno while navigating the regulatory framework of the Comisin de Regulacin de Televisin. The company's resilience in the early years laid the groundwork for its later adaptations, as it focused on cost-effective production techniques, including regional bureaus in cities like Medelln and Cali, to cover Colombia's decentralized violence hotspots with verifiable on-the-ground footage. This period solidified CM&'s reputation for independence, though it occasionally faced scrutiny from political figures over its unfiltered depictions of state responses to insurgency.

Survival amid industry crisis (late 1990searly 2000s)

In July 1998, the launch of private channels Caracol Televisin and RCN Televisin disrupted Colombia's mixed television system, capturing substantial audience share from Canal A (renamed Canal 1 in 1999) and slashing advertising revenue for programadoras reliant on public airtime. This competition, exacerbated by a national economic recession with GDP contraction of 4.2% in 1999, triggered widespread insolvency among programadoras; by 2000, over a dozen had shuttered or sought government bailouts, as production costs outpaced declining income from hourly slots. The crisis intensified pressures on news-focused outfits like CM&, founded in 1992 by Yamid Amat and partners, which faced reduced budgets and viewer migration to flashier private news formats.
CM& navigated survival through fiscal restraint and strategic alliances, prioritizing its core Noticiero CM&a twice-daily bulletin emphasizing investigative reportingwhich retained niche loyalty amid broader audience flight from Canal 1's ratings drop to under 10% share by 2000. In 2001, CM& joined forces with peers including NTC, Colombiana de Televisin, Telecolombia, and Programar to collectively purchase foreign series rights, distributing costs and bolstering Canal 1's non-news programming to stabilize slot revenues. Amat's leadership, drawing on prior radio success, emphasized lean operations over expansion, avoiding the debt traps that doomed rivals like Producciones Punch; this resilience positioned CM& among the scant survivors, enabling contract renewals through 2003 amid ongoing public channel reforms.

Rebranding and operational changes (2000s2010s)

In the early 2000s, CM& undertook operational expansions to bolster its presence on Canal Uno amid heightened competition following the launch of independent channels by Caracol Televisin and RCN Televisin in 1998. From 2000 to 2001, the company introduced a weekday midday edition of Noticiero CM& airing at 12:30 p.m., replacing the prior 24 Horas newscast and providing additional news coverage to capture daytime audiences; weekend and holiday midday slots were handled collaboratively with NTC Televisin under the NTC Noticias banner, simulcast on Canal A. This scheduling adjustment reflected CM&'s strategy to diversify its output and maintain viewer engagement in a fragmenting market.
By 2002, following internal leadership stabilization, CM& further adapted its format with the 2004 launch of La Franja Informativa CM&, a dedicated two-hour news block from 8:00 p.m. to 10:00 p.m. on Canal Uno, incorporating the flagship Noticiero CM& alongside supplementary segments. This evolution extended the program's duration and integrated more analytical content, responding to shifts in Canal Uno's overall programming grid and aiming to consolidate CM&'s role as a primary news provider.
In the late 2000s, CM& pursued broader operational growth by entering the 2009 bidding process for Colombia's third private open television channel, partnering with Grupo Nacional de Medios and Grupo Prisa to expand beyond programadora constraints. However, CM& withdrew in 2010, citing insufficient guarantees in the process, which underscored the challenges of regulatory hurdles and market saturation for independent producers. Into the 2010s, the company refreshed its visual identity in 2017 as part of Canal Uno's administrative transition to Plural Comunicaciones, modernizing graphics and presentation to align with evolving viewer expectations for contemporary news delivery. These changes sustained CM&'s focus on rigorous journalism while navigating the transition toward digital broadcasting standards in Colombia.

Final years and discontinuation (2020s)

In the early 2020s, Noticiero CM& maintained its position as a key evening news program on Canal Uno, delivering daily broadcasts under the longstanding direction of Yamid Amat, who had shaped its format since the 1990s. The program continued to cover national and international events with a focus on investigative reporting and live updates, adapting minimally to digital streaming alongside traditional television.
By mid-2024, amid shifts in Canal Uno's ownership and programming strategy following the channel's acquisition by the Prisa Group, announcements emerged that CM&'s contract for the prime-time news slot would not be renewed. This decision reflected broader restructuring efforts by the new operators to reconfigure content production, prioritizing alternative formats over the established independent programadora model.
The final edition of Noticiero CM& aired on November 14, 2024, after precisely 32 years, 10 months, and 12 days of continuous transmission, totaling 12,005 days on air. In its closing broadcast, the team expressed gratitude to viewers and highlighted the program's role in Colombian journalism, evoking widespread sentiment among audiences who viewed its exit as creating an informational void after decades of reliable coverage. The discontinuation affected dozens of journalists and production staff, underscoring challenges faced by traditional programadoras in Colombia's evolving media landscape dominated by consolidated ownership.

Programming

Primary news production: Noticiero CM&

Noticiero CM& (initially Noticiero CMI), the flagship news bulletin produced by Compaa de Medios de Informacin (CM&), debuted on January 2, 1992, on Colombia's Canal 1, founded by journalists Yamid Amat and Juan Gossan as a commitment to independent, objective reporting amid the country's evolving media landscape. The program emphasized rigorous coverage of national politics, regional developments, security issues, and international affairs, prioritizing verifiable facts over sensationalism, with Amat serving as director until 1998 while maintaining influence over its editorial line. It initially aired in the evening, later expanding to weekday editions at 11:00 a.m. and 8:00 p.m. (GMT-5), delivering structured bulletins that integrated on-location reporting, expert analysis, and live updates to inform audiences on Colombia's complex socio-political dynamics.
The production format featured concise news segments bookended by anchor-led summaries, including the recurring "Uno, dos y tres CM&" for top daily headlines and "Entrevistas CM&" for in-depth discussions with key figures, fostering a model of investigative depth rare in commercial television at the time. CM& handled all aspects of production in-house, from scriptwriting and videography to editing, utilizing Bogot-based studios and a network of regional correspondents to ensure broad geographic coverage, which contributed to its reputation for reliability during events like Colombia's 1990s constitutional reforms and armed conflict escalations. Anchors evolved from originals Manuel Teodoro and Mara Cecilia Boterojoined by Csar Augusto Londoo for sportsto later teams including Luisa Tobo, Germn Arango, Claudia Palacios, and Margarita Ortega, maintaining a professional, non-partisan delivery that prioritized evidence-based narratives.
Technological adaptations in production included early adoption of satellite feeds for real-time international sourcing and digital archiving for fact-checking, allowing Noticiero CM& to sustain high production standards through economic downturns in the programadora sector. The program's independence was underscored by its avoidance of advertiser or governmental influence, earning accolades like the India Catalina Award for Best National Newscast, though it faced critiques for perceived elite perspectives in coverage. Production ceased on November 14, 2024, after 33 years, following the expiration of CM&'s contract with Canal 1 under new management by Spain's Prisa group, which opted for programming shifts rather than renewal, marking the end of a era in Colombian broadcast journalism.

Other content and contributions to Canal Uno

CM& produced interview and opinion programs for Canal Uno, extending its journalistic focus beyond standard news bulletins. A notable example was Pregunta Yamid, hosted by veteran journalist Yamid Amat, which featured in-depth conversations with prominent figures, exploring their personal lives, professional achievements, and viewpoints on current issues. The program aired within CM&'s allocated slots on the channel, providing viewers with extended analysis and dialogue not covered in routine newscasts.
In addition to such formats, CM& engaged in promotional efforts for entertainment-oriented content on Canal Uno during the mid-2010s, as evidenced by 2015 advertisements highlighting diversified programming to attract broader audiences. However, specific titles of these entertainment productions remain sparsely documented in public records, with the company's emphasis historically leaning toward news and informational genres. As a programadora, CM& also contributed institutional and specialized content, such as reports for public entities, which occasionally aired on the channel to fulfill programming requirements.
These contributions supported Canal Uno's mandate as a public-commercial broadcaster by filling time slots with credible, produced content amid competitive bidding processes for airtime. CM&'s non-news output, though secondary to its news dominance, helped maintain the channel's mix of information and engagement until the company's exit in November 2024.

Format evolution and technological adaptations

Noticiero CMI (rebranded as Noticiero CM& in June 1998) launched on January 2, 1992, featuring a conventional evening news format broadcast at 9:30 p.m. on Canal Uno, emphasizing live reporting, on-site journalism, and anchor-led segments under Yamid Amat's direction. Its initial visual style relied on analog-era graphics and standard-definition video, typical of early 1990s Colombian television production.
Over the decades, the program underwent periodic updates to its opening intros and on-screen graphics, reflecting broader shifts in broadcast aesthetics from static titles to dynamic animations and integrated lower-thirds for enhanced viewer engagement. These evolutions, documented from 1992 through the 2020s, incorporated progressive improvements in titling, color schemes, and motion design to maintain relevance amid competing newscasts. In June 1998, the newscast rebranded its name and adjusted its format structure to align with evolving audience preferences and channel requirements.
By August 14, 2017, CM& refreshed its overall image, including set design and graphic packages, in response to Canal Uno's programming overhaul, which aimed to modernize presentation without altering core content delivery. Further adaptations occurred in 2020 with a rename to Noticentro 1 CM&, integrating branding elements from the channel's unified news identity, before reverting to Noticiero CM& on October 25, 2024, to restore its original standalone appeal.
Technologically, CM& transitioned alongside Colombia's broader shift to digital terrestrial television, adopting improved signal processing and graphics software for sharper visuals and faster news integration by the 2010s, though specific HD implementation dates for its productions aligned with Canal Uno's gradual upgrades rather than pioneering standalone innovations. These changes prioritized reliability in live feeds and remote reporting over experimental formats, sustaining the program's focus on factual dissemination amid digital disruptions.

Leadership and personnel

Key founders and executives

CM& was established as a Colombian programadora on January 2, 1992, by journalists Yamid Amat and Juan Gossan, who launched Noticiero CM& as its flagship production for Canal Uno.
Yamid Amat, a veteran broadcaster with prior experience at Caracol Televisin, served as the primary founder, director, and majority shareholder, guiding the company's operations through its initial decades and maintaining control until its discontinuation in 2024. Amat directed the noticiero from 1992 to 1998, briefly stepped away during a period of leadership transitions, and resumed direction from 2002 onward, emphasizing investigative journalism and live coverage of national events.
Juan Gossan, an acclaimed radio and television journalist known for his work on RCN and Caracol, co-founded CM& but played a more limited executive role post-launch, focusing instead on his independent career while contributing to the program's early credibility through his reputation for in-depth reporting.
Interim directors during Amat's absences included Mauricio Vargas from 1998 to 1999, Ricardo Santamara from 1999 to 2001, Rafael Pardo from 2001 to 2002, and Cecilia Orozco in 2002, though these tenures were marked by efforts to stabilize the programadora amid broader industry challenges; Amat's return in 2002 solidified long-term leadership under his vision. The executive structure remained lean, with Amat as the dominant figure in strategic decisions, production oversight, and shareholder interests, reflecting the personalized management common in Colombia's programadora system.

Notable on-air talent and journalists

Yamid Amat, the founder and long-time director of CM&, also served as a key on-air presenter from the program's launch on January 2, 1992, until its discontinuation on November 14, 2024, shaping its emphasis on objective reporting and training subsequent generations of journalists. His involvement included co-directing the inaugural broadcast alongside Juan Gossan, establishing CM& as a cornerstone of independent news production in Colombia's programadora system.
Early presenters included Manuel Teodoro, who anchored from 1992 to 1995 and briefly from 2006 to 2007, alongside Mara Cecilia Botero and Mara Cristina Uribe, both active from 1992 to 1995, contributing to the noticiero's initial format of straightforward, fact-driven coverage. In later years, Claudia Palacios and Margarita Ortega emerged as central anchors from 2022 onward, delivering principal editions until the final broadcast, with Palacios also contributing columns to major outlets. Mnica Rodrguez co-anchored central segments, while Germn Arango specialized in sports commentary, earning recognition including an India Catalina award for best sports presenter in 2024. Other figures like Ilia Caldern (19972001) and Jaime Garzn, who provided satirical political commentary as "Heriberto de la Calle" from 1997 to 1998, added diverse perspectives to the program.

Internal structure and decision-making

CM&'s internal structure revolved around a compact organization tailored to news production, featuring editorial teams for story sourcing and verification, production units for scripting and assembly, and technical crews handling broadcast logistics. Decision-making was centralized under the director, who held authority over content prioritization, ethical guidelines, and adaptations to regulatory or market shifts in Colombia's programadora system. Co-founders Yamid Amat and Juan Gossan shaped initial processes upon the company's 1992 launch, focusing on independent journalism amid the state-controlled airtime allocation by Inravisin.
Yamid Amat, serving as director from 19921998 and 20022024, exemplified this model by personally influencing story selection and on-air presentation to emphasize political analysis and live reporting, often navigating pressures from advertisers and channel partners like Canal Uno. This hands-on leadership facilitated quick pivots, such as format tweaks during economic crises, but also drew scrutiny for perceived editorial biases tied to Amat's investigative style. Supporting roles included senior producers coordinating field reporters and anchors, ensuring compliance with broadcasting standards while competing for viewer ratings measured by systems like Nielsen.
In later years, as ownership ties deepened with groups like Prisa via Caracol affiliations, decision-making incorporated financial oversight from executives such as Carlos Hernando Ramrez, balancing creative autonomy with sustainability amid declining ad revenues and digital disruption. This evolution reflected broader programadora challenges, where internal hierarchies deferred to director-led editorial boards for daily operations but escalated strategic callslike program discontinuation in 2024to parent entities.

Reception and impact

Audience ratings and market position

CM&'s flagship program, Noticiero CM& (later branded as NotiCentro 1 CM&), consistently achieved the highest ratings within Canal 1's lineup, serving as the channel's primary audience driver amid broader struggles in the Colombian open television market. In 2021, its central evening edition recorded a rating of 1.50 points and a 13.2% share, reflecting a 46% growth in screen share over the prior week, while the channel overall saw a 14% audience increase. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the program grew 16% to a rating of 1.78 points, benefiting from heightened TV consumption that boosted open TV ratings by up to 30-35% nationally in early 2020.
Historically, Noticiero CM& peaked at shares like 9.6% nationally in 2017 and 6.52% in 2019 for its central emission, which reached 1.65 rating points and impacted over 3 million viewers in a single day. However, these figures remained modest compared to dominant private networks Caracol and RCN, which routinely secured double-digit ratings; Canal 1's average hovered around 0.2-1.4 points, with Noticiero CM& often hitting 0.9-1.0 points as the channel's top performer. Post-2024 discontinuation, Canal 1's ratings plummeted to a historic low of 0.1 points on November 19, underscoring CM&'s role in sustaining viability.
In market position, CM& occupied a niche as one of the last surviving programadoras in Colombia's hybrid public-private TV system, producing news and content for Canal 1 since 1992 and weathering the late-1990s financial crisis that eliminated most competitors. It held a stable but marginal share in the news segment, trailing Caracol's Noticias Caracol and RCN's Noticias RCN, which commanded higher trust and viewership per industry metrics. CM&'s focus on investigative journalism contributed to Canal 1's third-place standing, though the channel captured under 5% of the national audience, constrained by advertising revenue shortfalls and competition from streaming platforms. Financial pressures, including unrenewed contracts under Canal 1's new ownership by Grupo Prisa, led to its 2024 exit after 33 years, highlighting vulnerabilities in the declining programadora model.

Influence on Colombian journalism

Noticiero CM&, produced by the programadora CM& under the leadership of Yamid Amat, exerted significant influence on Colombian journalism by establishing benchmarks for rigorous, impartial reporting during its 33-year run from January 2, 1992, to November 14, 2024. Emerging amid President Csar Gaviria's 1990s television expansion policy, which fostered pluralism by introducing multiple independent newscasts on public channels like Canal Uno, CM& provided an alternative to emerging commercial broadcasters such as Caracol TV and RCN, emphasizing in-depth analysis and ethical standards over sensationalism. Its commitment to objectivity and truthevident in coverage of armed conflicts, peace processes, and political upheavalshelped shape public discourse and inform national events with a focus on factual depth rather than ideological alignment.
The program set agendas in Colombian news by prioritizing investigative segments and interviews, such as Amat's "Pregunta Yamid," which probed influential figures on complex issues, thereby bridging elite perspectives with public scrutiny and fostering accountability. This approach contributed to a "before and after" in news presentation standards, as noted by observers, by giving voice to societal problems and promoting compassionate yet truthful reporting amid media polarization. CM&'s resilience in maintaining traditional formats amid digital shifts underscored its role in preserving professional ethics, with 63% audience trust reported in 2023 surveys of major TV news outlets.
Through Amat's mentorship, CM& functioned as a training ground for generations of journalists, instilling practices of verification and balance that influenced broader industry norms. As one of the last survivors from the pluralistic public TV era, it democratized information access and countered commercial media dominance, leaving a legacy of substantive journalism that prioritized causal analysis of events over narrative-driven coverage. Its discontinuation highlights ongoing tensions in Colombia's media ecosystem, where economic pressures challenge such models, yet its standards continue to inform expectations for credibility in reporting.

Criticisms, controversies, and biases

CM& has faced accusations of political bias in its polling activities, particularly through partnerships with the Centro Nacional de Consultora (CNC). In September 2023, a CM& poll on Bogot mayoral candidates was criticized by opponents as manipulated to favor figures like Miguel Uribe Turbay's allies, with social media and political commentators alleging it was "made for Pinedo and Dumek" to boost their standings amid a competitive race.
Academic analyses have highlighted tendencies toward sensationalism in CM&'s reporting, notably during the COVID-19 pandemic. This approach drew implicit critiques for prioritizing viewer engagement over empirical proportionality, aligning with broader patterns in Colombian broadcast news where ratings pressures incentivize alarmism.
Operational controversies include workforce reductions amid financial strains. In September 2019, CM& initiated layoffs affecting journalists and production staff, sparking union complaints of abrupt terminations without severance negotiations, amid declining Canal Uno viewership and competition from digital platforms. These cuts were attributed to the programadora system's economic model, but critics linked them to mismanagement rather than market inevitability, given CM&'s established position.
Regarding ideological biases, CM& has been perceived by left-leaning observers as aligned with traditional elite interests, reflecting Colombia's media oligopoly where programadoras like CM& historically favored centrist or establishment narratives over populist reforms. However, empirical content audits, such as those by media watchdogs, show no systemic deviation from fact-based reporting, though selective framing in political scandalse.g., understating government accountability in corruption caseshas fueled claims of soft-pedaling elite malfeasance. Right-leaning critics, conversely, have occasionally accused CM& of undue scrutiny on conservative figures, as in 2022 coverage of electoral disputes, but such instances lack aggregated evidence of intentional skew. Overall, while not immune to the left-tilting pressures evident in Colombian academia and NGOs influencing source selection, CM& maintained a reputation for journalistic rigor under presenters like Nstor Morales, with controversies more tied to structural incentives than overt partisanship.

Legacy

Role in programadora system

CM& functioned as an independent programadora within Colombia's mixed television system, which operated from the 1980s until the early 2000s and relied on private producers to bid for and fill airtime slots on public channels through competitive tenders. Established in 1992, it specialized in news and informational content, launching the Noticiero CM&initially titled Noticiero CMIon January 2, 1992, for daily broadcast at 9:30 p.m. on Cadena Uno (later Canal Uno), thereby securing a prominent slot in the system's allocation of programming hours.
The programadora's model emphasized self-financed production of high-impact journalism, including debates and investigative segments, which distinguished it amid competition from over 30 other entities vying for limited channel time. By 1998, amid systemic shifts toward greater channel autonomy, CM& evolved into CM& Televisin to directly produce content for Canal Uno, becoming one of the channel's largest concessionaires and adapting to the declining viability of pure programadora bids. This transition allowed it to maintain output of programs like Agenda CM& and La Telepolmica, sustaining independent voices in a framework increasingly dominated by in-house channel production.
Unlike most programadoras that collapsed during the late 1990s financial crisistriggered by mounting debts, regulatory changes, and the 1998 government decree enabling channels to produce their own contentCM& endured through strategic alliances, such as joining Plural Comunicaciones in 2017 alongside entities like NTC Televisin and RTI. Its longevity, spanning over three decades until the Noticiero's final broadcast on November 14, 2024, underscored the resilience of specialized news producers in the system, even as it participated in later licitation attempts, including a 2009 bid for a third private channel that it withdrew in 2010 due to perceived risks. This role highlighted CM&'s contribution to journalistic pluralism before the program's full privatization in the 2000s.

Post-discontinuation status

The Noticiero CM&, the flagship news program produced by CM& Televisin, concluded its daily broadcasts on November 14, 2024, after 32 years and 10 months of continuous airing since its inception as Noticiero CMI on January 2, 1992. The decision stemmed from changes in the programming schedule of Canal Uno, where the program aired, following the entry of Grupo Prisa into content production slots, which displaced independent programadoras like CM&.
Post-discontinuation, CM& Televisin has maintained operations as an independent production company, emphasizing services such as television program development, content creation, and project implementation for various platforms, drawing on over 29 years of experience. The firm, founded by journalist Yamid Amat, no longer holds a regular broadcasting slot on national television but continues to leverage its expertise in investigative journalism and production infrastructure.
In the final broadcast, Amat delivered an emotional address, publicly offering forgiveness to former critics and adversaries while expressing gratitude to viewers for sustaining the program through decades of political and media shifts in Colombia. Co-anchor Margarita Ortega and other on-air talent displayed visible emotion, marking the end of an era for one of Colombia's longest-running independent news outlets, known for its focus on in-depth reporting and political analysis. No immediate plans for digital pivots or revivals have been announced by the company as of late 2024.
