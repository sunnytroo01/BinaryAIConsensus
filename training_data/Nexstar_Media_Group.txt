 Nexstar Media Group, Inc. (NASDAQ: NXST) is an American diversified media conglomerate founded in 1996 by Perry A. Sook through the acquisition of a single television station in Scranton, Pennsylvania, and headquartered in Irving, Texas.
The company has grown into the largest owner and operator of local television stations in the United States, controlling over 200 owned or partner stations across 116 markets that reach approximately 220 million people, or about 70 percent of U.S. television households.
Nexstar produces and distributes local and national news, sports, and entertainment content via broadcast, digital platforms, and national assets including the cable news network NewsNation, the political newspaper The Hill, and minority stakes in networks such as The CW Television Network and TV Food Network.
Its expansion, driven by major acquisitions like Media General in 2017 and Tribune Media in 2019, has solidified its dominance in local broadcasting and generated $5.4 billion in revenue for 2024, primarily from advertising, retransmission fees, and digital services.
In August 2025, Nexstar announced a $6.2 billion agreement to acquire TEGNA Inc., which would further expand its station portfolio pending regulatory approval amid ongoing scrutiny over media consolidation's effects on local news coverage and viewpoint diversity.
Empirical analyses indicate that Nexstar's ownership tends to increase local political news coverage compared to competitors like Sinclair, though broader consolidation raises causal concerns about reduced competition and potential homogenization of content.

History

Formation and Early Growth (19962010)

Nexstar Broadcasting Group was founded in the summer of 1996 by Perry Sook, a broadcast industry executive, with financial backing from ABRY Broadcast Partners II, which held a 78% stake. The company commenced operations with the acquisition of its first station, WYOU-TV in Scranton, Pennsylvania, purchased for approximately $23 million. This initial purchase marked Nexstar's entry into local television broadcasting, targeting mid-sized markets ranked 50 to 150 where competition was less intense and operational efficiencies could be realized through focused management.
In 1997, Nexstar established its early growth strategy by forming the industry's first local marketing agreement duopoly with Mission Broadcasting, Inc., allowing operational control of WYOU-TV alongside another station in the same market to bypass FCC ownership restrictions. The company expanded rapidly that year, acquiring stations in Terre Haute, Indiana; St. Joseph, Missouri; Joplin, Missouri; Wichita Falls, Texas; Beaumont-Port Arthur, Texas; Erie, Pennsylvania; and Wilkes-Barre, Pennsylvania. By 1999, further acquisitions included WROC-TV in Rochester, New York, for $46 million, and three CBS affiliates in Peoria, Springfield, and Champaign, Illinois, from Mid-West Television for $110.7 million. In 2000, Nexstar purchased KMID in Midland/Odessa, Texas, for $10 million, and KTAL in Shreveport, Louisiana, for $35 million, while joining the Broadcasters' Digital Cooperative to advance digital transition efforts.
The early 2000s saw continued expansion and financial milestones. In 2001, Nexstar secured $225 million in financing from Banc of America and formed additional duopolies, such as in Peoria and Bloomington, Illinois. The company filed for an initial public offering (IPO) in April 2002 on NASDAQ, targeting over $130 million, and invested $9 million in digital transmitter upgrades. By 2003, headquarters relocated to Irving, Texas; acquisitions included stations in Little Rock, Arkansas, and Dothan, Alabama, from Morris Multimedia for $90 million, and the landmark $230 million purchase of Quorum Broadcast Holdings, which added ten stations and effectively doubled Nexstar's portfolio to over 20 properties through official and virtual duopolies. The IPO closed in November 2003, raising $140 million to fuel further growth. In 2004, Nexstar formed duopolies in Utica, New York, and Rockford, Illinois, and acquired stations in Fort Smith/Fayetteville, Arkansas, and San Angelo, Texas. By 2005, the company pioneered retransmission consent negotiations, pulling signals from providers like Cable One and Cox in Texas and Missouri to demand fees of 30 cents per subscriber, establishing a revenue model that enhanced financial stability.

Expansion via Acquisitions and Disputes (20112019)

During the period from 2011 to 2019, Nexstar Broadcasting Group (later renamed Nexstar Media Group) expanded its holdings through targeted acquisitions of local television stations, capitalizing on regulatory changes and market opportunities to increase its market reach from approximately 50 stations in 2011 to over 200 by 2019. This growth strategy involved purchasing assets from smaller owners and consolidating operations via shared services agreements, which allowed Nexstar to operate stations without full ownership in some cases to navigate FCC ownership limits. Key smaller-scale deals included the 2014 acquisition of 15 stations from Communications Corporation of America, Meredith Corporation, and Landmark Media Enterprises, adding affiliates in markets such as Charleston, SC, and Terre Haute, IN.
A pivotal expansion occurred with the $4.6 billion acquisition of Media General, announced in September 2016 and completed on January 17, 2017, which added 62 owned stations and nine operated via agreements, bringing Nexstar's total to 171 stations across 100 markets and renaming the company Nexstar Media Group. The deal faced U.S. Department of Justice scrutiny over potential anticompetitive effects in advertising and retransmission consent markets, resulting in required divestitures of certain stations to maintain competition. Following this, Nexstar announced on December 3, 2018, its $6.4 billion acquisition of Tribune Media, including 42 stations and assets like WGN-TV in Chicago, completed in September 2019 after FCC approval and divestitures of 19 stations to address overlap concerns in 15 markets.
Amid this expansion, Nexstar encountered disputes, particularly over retransmission consent fees and affiliations. In 2011, Nexstar engaged in a protracted negotiation with Fox over reverse compensation terms, where affiliates pay networks for programming rights; the impasse led Fox to drop Nexstar's CW-affiliated WTVW-TV in Evansville, Indiana, effective July 1, 2011, temporarily replacing it with a subchannel feed from another affiliate. Separately, in Fort Wayne, Indiana, Nexstar's Fox affiliate WFFT-TV lost its affiliation to Granite Broadcasting's WPTA-TV in July 2011, prompting Nexstar to file an antitrust lawsuit in July 2011 alleging monopolization of local ad sales; the suit settled in February 2013, restoring the Fox affiliation to WFFT. These conflicts highlighted tensions in affiliation agreements and fee structures as broadcasters sought higher revenues from cable and satellite providers amid rising programming costs.

Launch of NewsNation and Network Investments (2020Present)

In September 2020, Nexstar Media Group rebranded its cable network WGN America and launched NewsNation as a prime-time news programming block, debuting on September 1 with a three-hour newscast from 8 p.m. to 11 p.m. ET, available to approximately 75 million U.S. television households. The initiative stemmed from Nexstar's strategy to create a national news outlet emphasizing factual reporting amid perceived polarization in cable news, leveraging the company's extensive local station resources for content sourcing.
Nexstar subsequently invested in expanding NewsNation's programming and infrastructure, increasing original weekly live news hours from 21 at launch to 61 by early 2022 through additions like daytime and weekend shows. In August 2022, the network announced key editorial hires, including experienced journalists for investigative and political coverage, alongside expansions to production facilities in Chicago to support enhanced reporting capabilities drawing from Nexstar's 5,500 local journalists across 110 newsrooms. By June 1, 2024, NewsNation transitioned to a full 24/7 news format, incorporating early-evening weekend editions and reaching nearly 67 million households, reflecting ongoing capital commitments to staffing, technology, and content distribution despite industry cord-cutting trends.
A significant network investment occurred in 2022 when Nexstar acquired a 75% controlling stake in The CW broadcast network, closing the deal on October 3 after an agreement announced on August 15, with Paramount Global and Warner Bros. Discovery retaining minority interests. This move positioned Nexstar to integrate The CW's programming with its local stations and NewsNation for cross-promotion and revenue synergies, including sports and entertainment content distribution. These efforts have contributed to NewsNation's audience growth, with prime-time viewership increases noted in periods like August 2025, underscoring Nexstar's focus on national platforms amid local broadcasting dominance.

Business Model and Operations

Local Broadcasting and Affiliate Strategy

Nexstar Media Group's local broadcasting operations center on owning and operating a portfolio of over 200 television stations across 116 U.S. markets, encompassing affiliations with major networks such as ABC, CBS, NBC, FOX, and The CW. This structure positions the company as the largest local TV station owner in the United States, with stations reaching approximately 220 million people through a combination of outright ownership and partnerships that enable operational control via shared services agreements. The strategy emphasizes market dominance in mid-sized and smaller designated market areas (DMAs), where local advertising and content production yield higher margins compared to larger metros dominated by network-owned outlets.
Affiliate agreements form the core of Nexstar's programming strategy, providing national content while reserving time slots for local news, weather, and sports to drive viewer loyalty and revenue from both advertising and retransmission consent fees. The company maintains the largest affiliation footprint among the Big Four networks and The CW, with renewals structured as multi-year deals to stabilize revenue streams; for example, a January 2025 agreement with NBCUniversal covered 33 NBC-affiliated markets, ensuring continued carriage of network programming alongside local insertions. A July 2024 renewal with Paramount Global extended CBS affiliations across 42 markets owned by Nexstar and its partner Mission Broadcasting, highlighting the use of affiliated entities to expand reach without direct ownership exceeding FCC caps.
To optimize costs and coverage, Nexstar pursues duopoly structures in select markets, operating paired stations (e.g., a Big Four affiliate alongside an independent or secondary network outlet) that share newsrooms, sales teams, and facilities for efficient local content production. Partner companies like Mission Broadcasting and Vaughan Media function as "sidecar" entities, nominally holding licenses while Nexstar provides programming, advertising sales, and management under local marketing agreements (LMAs), circumventing federal ownership limits that restrict control to 39% of a market's audience. This model supports robust local journalism, with many stations producing high-rated newscasts tailored to regional events, while positioning Nexstar for further consolidation; an August 19, 2025, agreement to acquire Tegna for $6.2 billion aims to add over 60 stations, potentially reaching 80% of U.S. TV households pending regulatory approval.

Retransmission Consent and Revenue Generation

Nexstar Media Group derives a substantial portion of its revenue from retransmission consent agreements, which allow its owned and operated television stations to negotiate compensation from multichannel video programming distributors (MVPDs) such as cable, satellite, and telecommunications providers, as well as virtual MVPDs and direct-to-consumer platforms, for the right to carry local broadcast signals. These agreements are negotiated on a per-subscriber basis, typically under 3-year terms, with fees escalating over multi-year contracts. Nexstar's extensive portfolio of over 200 local stations in 116 markets, reaching approximately 70% of U.S. TV households, including affiliates of major networks like ABC, CBS, NBC, and Fox in key markets, provides leverage in negotiations, enabling the company to secure higher per-subscriber rates compared to smaller station groups. Industry estimates for retransmission fees for major affiliates range from $3.50$6.25 per subscriber per month in some markets and disputes. Local news viewership appeals broadly across age groups, with consumption rates of 4785% by age bracket, contributing to the stations' overall value despite no direct public linkage between fees and specific customer demographics.
In 2024, distribution revenue, primarily from retransmission consent fees, totaled $2.928 billion, representing 55% of Nexstar's total revenue of $5.407 billion, underscoring its role as the company's primary growth driver amid declining linear advertising. This marked an increase from $2.18 billion in retransmission consent fees in 2022, reflecting successful renegotiations and affiliation advantages. Distribution revenue, which encompasses retransmission and related carriage fees, comprised 55% of overall revenue in the 2023-2024 period, with the remainder from advertising (43%) and other sources (2%).
To enforce favorable terms, Nexstar has engaged in high-profile carriage disputes, often resulting in temporary blackouts of its stations to pressure providers. In January 2025, 63 Nexstar stations in 42 markets, along with NewsNation, went dark on Altice USA's Optimum service after contract expiration, affecting roughly two million subscribers until a new multiyear agreement was reached on January 18, restoring access. Similar tactics were employed in a 2019 dispute with DirecTV, blacking out 120 stations in 97 markets. As of October 2025, negotiations with Verizon Fios TV faced a deadline on October 24, highlighting ongoing tensions over fee hikes amid cord-cutting trends.
These strategies have sustained retransmission revenue growth for Nexstar despite industry-wide subscriber erosion, though broader projections indicate flat national retrans revenue through 2028 due to MVPD declines. Nexstar executives have argued that current fees undervalue stations' ratings contributions, estimating potential 44% upside if compensated proportionally.

Digital and Syndicated Content Initiatives

Nexstar Media Group's digital operations encompass management of 138 local websites and 229 mobile applications, which deliver hyper-local news, sports, and entertainment content tailored to specific markets. These platforms support verticals such as product reviews through BestReviews, enabling consumer guides and affiliate marketing opportunities. The company's digital properties collectively rank among the top ten in the United States according to Comscore metrics, reflecting substantial audience engagement across publishing, video, and advertising services.
In streaming initiatives, Nexstar expanded access to local content by launching free ad-supported apps for ABC, CBS, FOX, and NBC affiliates in February 2025, available on Roku, Fire TV, and Apple TV devices. These apps provide live local news streams, on-demand video libraries, and push notifications for breaking alerts, with further market rollouts announced in March 2025. Complementing this, NewsNation introduced connected TV applications in February 2023, integrating with platforms like YouTube TV, Hulu, and FuboTV to broaden national news distribution. Overall, Nexstar generates over 316,000 hours of programming annually across broadcast, streaming, and digital channels, prioritizing monetization through targeted advertising and audience data analytics.
Regarding syndicated content, Nexstar stations primarily acquire and air established programs such as Jeopardy!, Wheel of Fortune, Judge Judy, and Family Feud to fill non-news daytime and fringe time slots, enhancing viewer retention in local markets. While the company licenses both its original local productions and third-party syndicated material to external outlets for additional revenue, its core strategy under founder Perry Sook emphasizes localism over reliance on nationally syndicated fare. This approach includes selective syndication of hyper-local content across its network of stations, though production of new original syndicated series remains limited compared to broadcasting peers.

Assets and Holdings

Television Stations and Markets

Nexstar Media Group maintains the largest portfolio of local television stations in the United States, encompassing more than 200 owned or partner stations operating in 116 markets and reaching approximately 70% of U.S. television households. This extensive footprint positions Nexstar as the leading provider of affiliated programming for major networks such as ABC, CBS, NBC, Fox, and The CW, with the company holding a controlling 75% interest in The CW since its 2022 acquisition from prior owners. The strategy emphasizes duopoly ownershipcontrolling two stations per market in over 100 instancesto consolidate local news production, optimize advertising sales, and leverage retransmission consent fees from cable and satellite providers.
In top-tier designated market areas (DMAs), Nexstar's holdings include prominent stations like WPIX (channel 11, CW affiliate) in New York, NY; KTLA (channel 5, CW affiliate) in Los Angeles, CA; WGN-TV (channel 9, independent) in Chicago, IL; KDAF (channel 33, CW affiliate) in Dallas-Fort Worth, TX; and WPHL (channel 17, MyNetworkTV affiliate) in Philadelphia, PA. These urban outlets often feature high-volume local news cycles, with WPIX producing 49 hours weekly in the nation's largest market, supplemented by syndicated content and sports programming. Further examples include KIAH (CW affiliate) in Houston, TX; WDCW (CW affiliate) in Washington, D.C./Hagerstown, MD; KRON (independent) in San Francisco-Oakland, CA; and WFLA (NBC affiliate) alongside WTTA (MyNetworkTV) in Tampa-St. Petersburg, FL, illustrating Nexstar's blend of network ties and independent operations in high-revenue markets.
Nexstar's mid-sized and smaller market stations, such as WCIA (CBS) in Champaign-Springfield-Decatur, IL, and WOOD (NBC) in Grand Rapids-Kalamazoo, MI, prioritize hyper-local news and weather coverage to capture community engagement, often through shared services agreements that extend operational efficiencies across affiliates. As of October 2025, the company's portfolio faces potential expansion via a pending $6.2 billion acquisition of TEGNA Inc., announced August 19, 2025, which would add 64 stations and elevate coverage to nearly 80% of households if approved by regulators, though opposition from lawmakers citing antitrust concerns persists.

National Networks and Digital Properties

Nexstar Media Group owns a 75% stake in The CW Network, which it acquired from Paramount Global and Warner Bros. Discovery in October 2022, positioning the network as the fifth major U.S. broadcast outlet with a focus on entertainment, sports, and original programming. The CW distributes content across Nexstar's affiliated stations and has expanded into live sports rights, including ACC college football and basketball games starting in 2024, with projections for profitability by 2026 driven by such deals.
The company operates NewsNation, a cable news channel rebranded from WGN America on March 1, 2021, which expanded to include five hours of weekday primetime news programming at launch and now offers 24/7 coverage emphasizing straight news from its Chicago headquarters. NewsNation reaches over 75 million households via cable and satellite distribution as of 2025.
Nexstar's national multicast networks include Antenna TV, a digital subchannel service launched in 2011 that broadcasts classic sitcoms from the 1950s through 1990s across more than 180 affiliates, and Rewind TV, introduced on September 1, 2021, specializing in 1980s and 1990s family-oriented programming on subchannels of Nexstar-owned stations. These networks leverage over-the-air digital signals to deliver free, ad-supported content without subscription fees.
Among digital properties, Nexstar acquired The Hill, an independent political news website and video platform based in Washington, D.C., for $130 million in August 2021, integrating it to complement NewsNation's coverage with Capitol Hill reporting and analysis. The company's broader national digital assets, including websites and apps tied to its networks, form a portfolio that ranked among the top ten U.S. digital media companies by user engagement in Comscore metrics as of 2025, supported by video advertising and content syndication. Nexstar also maintains connected TV applications and mobile platforms for NewsNation and The CW, enhancing multi-platform distribution.

Financial Performance

Revenue Composition and Historical Trends

Nexstar Media Group's revenue is predominantly generated from distribution fees paid by multichannel video programming distributors (MVPDs) and online video distributors (OVDs) for retransmission consent, alongside advertising sales and ancillary sources. For the 2023-2024 period, distribution fees constituted 55% of total revenue, non-political advertising 38% (with local advertising comprising 69% of that segment and national 31%), political advertising 5%, and other revenue streams including digital and syndication 2%. This composition reflects a strategic emphasis on stable, affiliation-based distribution incomeprimarily retransmission consent feeswhich has increasingly offset volatility in advertising amid audience fragmentation and the rise of streaming services.
Historical revenue trends demonstrate accelerated growth following key acquisitions, particularly the 2019 purchase of Tribune Media, which expanded Nexstar's station portfolio and market reach. Annual net revenue rose from $4.581 billion in 2019 to $5.211 billion in 2022, before a post-election-year decline to $4.933 billion in 2023 and a rebound to $5.407 billion in 2024a 9.61% year-over-year increase driven by elevated political spending and distribution escalations. Distribution revenue specifically trended upward consistently, increasing from $2.571 billion in 2022 to $2.727 billion in 2023 and $2.928 billion in 2024 (representing 55% of total net revenue), underscoring the reliability of carriage agreements amid broader industry shifts toward pay-for-content models.
Advertising revenue exhibits cyclical patterns tied to economic conditions and election cycles, with political advertising peaking in even-numbered years; for instance, it reached $492 million in 2024 compared to subdued levels in off-years. In 2022, an election year, the breakdown showed distribution at 49.3% of total revenue, core (non-political) advertising at 33%, political advertising at 9.7%, and digital/other at 8%, highlighting a transitional period where distribution's share began surpassing advertising dominance post-acquisition integrations. Overall, revenue expansion has been fueled by scale from mergers, contractual fee hikes, and diversification into digital platforms, though challenged by linear TV viewership erosion.

Market Share and Competitive Position

Nexstar Media Group commands the largest share of the U.S. local television market, owning or operating 114 stations that reach 39.1% of households under FCC ownership metrics and covering approximately 70% of the country's population across 116 markets. The company's scale enables it to affiliate with major networks like ABC, CBS, NBC, and Fox in key designated market areas (DMAs), providing broad audience access for news, sports, and entertainment content.
In fiscal year 2024, Nexstar generated $5.4 billion in revenue, outpacing primary competitors Gray Television ($3.5 billion) and Sinclair Broadcast Group ($3.1 billion), with retransmission consent fees accounting for 54% of its total, or about $2.9 billion. This revenue leadership underscores Nexstar's competitive edge in negotiating carriage deals with multichannel video programming distributors (MVPDs), where its extensive affiliate portfolio yields higher per-subscriber fees compared to smaller groups.
The pending $6.2 billion acquisition of TEGNA Inc., announced on August 19, 2025, positions Nexstar to further consolidate its dominance, potentially adding stations in 9 of the top 10 DMAs and 82 of the top 100 if regulatory approval is secured. As of October 2025, the deal faces scrutiny from the FCC and Department of Justice over antitrust implications, including DMA overlaps and national reach exceeding FCC caps without divestitures.
Despite these strengths, Nexstar's position is challenged by structural shifts in media consumption, as local TV's share of total viewing has declined amid streaming's rise to 46% of TV time by June 2025, pressuring advertising revenues that comprise roughly 40% of Nexstar's income. Nexstar mitigates this through diversification into digital properties and national networks like NewsNation, but competitors such as Sinclair leverage similar strategies with varying emphases on syndicated content and political programming. Overall, Nexstar's market leadership derives from its unmatched station footprint and revenue diversification, enabling resilience against cord-cutting trends eroding smaller broadcasters' viability.

Leadership and Ownership

Key Executives and Governance

Perry A. Sook has served as Chairman and Chief Executive Officer of Nexstar Media Group since founding the company in 1996 through the acquisition of WYOU in Scranton, Pennsylvania. With more than 43 years of experience in television and radio broadcasting, Sook has directed the company's growth into the largest U.S. owner of local television stations, expanding to over 200 stations across 116 markets and achieving annual revenues surpassing $5 billion as of 2025.
The executive leadership team includes Michael Biard as President and Chief Operating Officer, overseeing daily operations and strategic initiatives; Lee Ann Gliha as Executive Vice President and Chief Financial Officer, managing financial planning and reporting; and Rachel Morgan as Executive Vice President, General Counsel, and Corporate Secretary, handling legal affairs and corporate governance compliance. Additional senior roles encompass division presidents such as Andy Alford for Broadcasting, Sean Compton for Networks, and Dana Zimmer for Distribution and Strategy, supporting Nexstar's core operations in local media, content distribution, and advertising.
Nexstar's Board of Directors comprises nine members, with Perry Sook as Chairman and a majority of independent directors providing oversight on strategy, risk, and fiduciary duties. Independent directors include Geoff Armstrong (Audit Committee Chair, with broadcast finance expertise); Jay M. Grossman (Compensation Committee Chair, media investment background); John R. Muse (Nominating and Corporate Governance Committee Chair, private equity experience); Bernadette S. Aulestia and Tony Wells (Compensation Committee); Ellen Johnson and Lisbeth R. McNabb (Audit Committee); and Thomas McMillen (Nominating and Corporate Governance Committee). The board maintains standing committees to address audit, compensation, and governance matters, ensuring alignment with shareholder interests and regulatory standards through annual charters and stockholder approvals, as reaffirmed at the June 2025 annual meeting.

Institutional Ownership and Shareholder Influence

Institutional investors hold approximately 102% of Nexstar Media Group's outstanding shares, a figure exceeding 100% due to short selling and derivative positions reported in regulatory filings. As of mid-2025, 633 institutions manage these holdings, valued at over $5.8 billion collectively. This high concentration reflects Nexstar's status as a mid-cap media stock attractive to index funds and large asset managers.
Key institutional stakeholders include Vanguard Group Inc. and BlackRock Inc., which exert influence through passive investment vehicles. For instance, Vanguard's Total Stock Market Index Fund holds about 3.02% of shares, while BlackRock's iShares Core S&P Mid-Cap ETF owns 3.03%, based on September 2025 data. Other notable holders encompass Dimensional Fund Advisors LP, Neuberger Berman Group LLC, and Charles Schwab Investment Management, comprising the bulk of ownership via diversified portfolios rather than concentrated stakes.
Shareholder influence remains predominantly passive, with no major activist campaigns or contested proxy fights reported for Nexstar in recent years. At the 2025 annual meeting, shareholders unanimously approved all management proposals, underscoring alignment between institutional voters and executive leadership. Insider ownership, at 6.44% and largely attributable to CEO Perry A. Sook, provides internal stability but does not offset the external institutional dominance. Absent aggressive interventions, governance decisions prioritize long-term operational strategies over short-term shareholder activism.

Controversies

Regulatory and Antitrust Scrutiny

Nexstar Media Group has faced antitrust scrutiny from the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) for anticompetitive conduct, including a 2018 settlement resolving allegations that the company exchanged competitively sensitive information with rival broadcasters regarding retransmission consent fees, which the DOJ deemed likely to facilitate collusion and raise prices for multichannel video programming distributors (MVPDs). Under the agreement, Nexstar committed to ceasing such exchanges and implementing antitrust compliance measures, avoiding further litigation without admitting wrongdoing.
The company's $6.4 billion acquisition of Tribune Media, announced in December 2018 and completed in September 2019, underwent rigorous review by both the DOJ and Federal Communications Commission (FCC). The DOJ conditioned approval on July 31, 2019, requiring divestitures of broadcast stations in three marketsPhiladelphia, PA; Huntsville, AL; and Spokane, WAto address horizontal overlaps that would substantially lessen competition in spot advertising markets, as alleged in a complaint covering thirteen designated market areas (DMAs). The FCC approved the transfer on September 16, 2019, by a 3-2 vote, imposing conditions including divestitures of stations reaching over 110 million viewers and ongoing compliance with local ownership limits, amid concerns from dissenting commissioners about excessive concentration post-merger.
FCC enforcement actions have targeted Nexstar's sidecar arrangements with entities like Mission Broadcasting, where Nexstar provides operational services and financing suggestive of de facto control. In April 2024, the FCC imposed a $1.775 million forfeiture and ordered divestiture of WPIX in New York after finding apparent violations of the duopoly rule prohibiting common ownership of the top-four stations in a market, as Nexstar's influence over Mission exceeded permissible levels despite nominal independence. A related antitrust lawsuit by DirecTV against Nexstar, Mission, and White Knight Broadcasting, alleging coordinated carriage negotiations to inflate fees, was dismissed in March 2024 for failure to state a claim.
As of August 2025, Nexstar's proposed $6.2 billion acquisition of TEGNA, announced on August 19, faces heightened antitrust and FCC review, with the combined entity projected to exceed the FCC's 39% national audience reach cap (even with the UHF discount) and control stations in over 100 DMAs, prompting divestiture discussions in overlapping markets like Phoenix and Denver. Filings with the DOJ and FCC were due by September 30, 2025, with closure anticipated in the second half of 2026 pending approvals; critics, including state attorneys general and senators, have urged scrutiny for potential violations of state antitrust laws and risks to local journalism diversity, while Nexstar cites synergies and a deregulatory environment under the incoming Trump administration.

Carriage Fee Disputes and Blackouts

Nexstar Media Group has engaged in multiple carriage fee disputes with multichannel video programming distributors (MVPDs), primarily over retransmission consent fees for its local television stations, leading to several high-profile blackouts affecting millions of subscribers. These conflicts stem from Nexstar's demands for increased compensation reflecting the value of its affiliated network programming (including ABC, CBS, Fox, NBC, and CW affiliates) and rising production costs, contrasted with MVPDs' efforts to curb escalating affiliate fees amid cord-cutting trends.
One of the largest blackouts occurred with Dish Network starting December 2, 2020, when 164 Nexstar stations in 115 markets went dark for approximately 5.4 million subscribers after contract expiration, marking the most extensive broadcast affiliate blackout in U.S. television history. Dish accused Nexstar of rejecting a short-term extension and forcing the outage to pressure for higher fees, while Nexstar countered that Dish's unwillingness to pay fair market rates deprived viewers of essential local news and network content, including WGN America. The dispute resolved on December 24, 2020, with a multiyear agreement restoring access.
A protracted conflict with DirecTV began July 2, 2023, blacking out over 150 Nexstar stations for more than 10 million subscribers across dozens of markets, with DirecTV claiming Nexstar sought fees more than double prior rates for unchanged content. Nexstar maintained the demands aligned with industry standards and the stations' role in delivering must-have programming, especially during events like NFL preseason games. Temporary restorations occurred in September 2023 and February 2024 to facilitate negotiations, culminating in a multiyear deal on September 18, 2023; DirecTV's subsequent antitrust lawsuit alleging price-fixing was dismissed by a federal judge in March 2024.
More recently, on January 10, 2025, 63 Nexstar stations in 42 markets, including NewsNation, went dark on Altice USA's Optimum service after failed renewal talks, impacting local affiliates in key areas. Optimum highlighted Nexstar's fee hike demands as unsustainable, while Nexstar emphasized the programming's value to subscribers. The blackout ended January 20, 2025, with a new carriage agreement. As of October 22, 2025, Nexstar faced a potential blackout with Verizon Fios TV, with its contract expiring October 24 amid disputes over "exorbitant" retransmission fee increases.
These incidents underscore Nexstar's leverage as the largest U.S. broadcast station owner, using blackouts as negotiation tactics despite consumer backlash and regulatory scrutiny, though no federal mandates for blackout mitigation have been enacted as of 2025.

Content Control and Political Influence Allegations

Nexstar Media Group has faced allegations of exerting centralized control over content across its network of over 200 local television stations, including the use of pre-scripted segments distributed to affiliates, which critics argue undermines local journalism autonomy. For instance, in early 2025, multiple Nexstar-owned stations aired identical scripted reports promoting Amazon initiatives, highlighting a pattern of syndicated content that prioritizes corporate interests over independent reporting. Such practices, similar to those employed by competitors like Sinclair Broadcast Group, have drawn scrutiny for contributing to media consolidation effects, where national ownership leads to homogenized news narratives rather than community-specific coverage.
In its cable news venture NewsNation, launched in 2020 as a purportedly neutral alternative to partisan outlets, Nexstar has encountered claims of conservative political influence. Left-leaning watchdog Media Matters accused NewsNation of featuring evening opinion programming with "racism, sexism, and anti-immigrant hate" akin to right-wing networks, citing specific segments in 2023. However, independent bias evaluators, including Media Bias/Fact Check and Ad Fontes Media, have rated NewsNation as minimally biased or center-leaning with high factual reliability as of April 2025, attributing any rightward tilt to efforts at balance amid perceived left-wing dominance in mainstream media. Internal turmoil in 2021, including staff departures, stemmed from allegations of imposed conservative viewpoints, with Nexstar CEO Perry Sook defending the network's direction by noting that true neutrality might appear right-leaning given overrepresentation of liberal perspectives in journalism.
A prominent 2025 incident involved Nexstar's decision to preempt "Jimmy Kimmel Live!" on its 28 ABC affiliates following host Jimmy Kimmel's September 17 remarks falsely linking conservative activist Charlie Kirk to a rally with neo-Nazis. Nexstar cited the comments as inflammatory and opted not to air the September 23 episode, prompting backlash from Democratic lawmakers who launched a probe into the company and Sinclair for potential suppression of dissenting views. Nexstar defended the move as protecting viewer trust amid ABC's ongoing suspension of Kimmel, while critics, including Rep. Jim McGovern, framed it as a threat to free speech influenced by political pressures. This episode underscored broader concerns about station group owners wielding influence over network programming, particularly in polarized contexts, though Nexstar maintained its actions aligned with editorial discretion rather than partisan directives.
