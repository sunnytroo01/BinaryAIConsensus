 Ytterbium is a chemical element with the symbol Yb and atomic number 70, classified as a lanthanide rare earth metal in the f-block of the periodic table. It appears as a soft, silvery-white, malleable, and ductile solid at room temperature, with a density of 6.97 g/cm, a melting point of 824C, and a boiling point of 1200C.
Discovered in 1878 by Swiss chemist Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac while analyzing a sample of gadolinite from Ytterby, Swedenafter which the element is namedytterbium was initially isolated from what was thought to be the element yttrium but was later distinguished as a separate lanthanide. In 1907, French chemist Georges Urbain further separated it from lutetium, confirming its identity through spectroscopic analysis. The metal was first produced in 1937 by Wilhelm Klemm and Francis Bonner, though impure. Pure ytterbium metal was obtained in 1953 at the Ames Laboratory.
Ytterbium occurs naturally in rare earth minerals such as monazite and xenotime, comprising about 0.03% of monazite sand, and is extracted through processes involving reduction with lanthanum metal, solvent extraction, or ion exchange. It has seven stable isotopes, with Yb being the most abundant at 31.8%, and its electron configuration is 4f 6s, making it relatively stable but reactive with water, acids, and air over time. Physically, it exhibits three allotropes: alpha (hexagonal close-packed up to -13C), beta (face-centered cubic from -13C to 795C), and gamma (body-centered cubic above 795C), and under high pressure (around 16,000 atm), it becomes a semiconductor.
Key applications of ytterbium leverage its optical and mechanical properties. It is added to stainless steel to enhance grain refinement, strength, and other mechanical attributes, and isotope Yb serves as a gamma ray source in portable X-ray machines for non-destructive testing. In optics and electronics, ytterbium is used as a dopant in fiber optic cables for signal amplification, in high-powered fiber lasers for marking and engraving, and in superconductors and atomic clocks due to its stable isotopes. Ytterbium compounds also act as catalysts in organic chemical reactions, particularly for transformations in the production of pharmaceuticals and plastics. Though not highly toxic, handling requires care due to its reactivity and potential for tarnishing.

Properties

Physical properties

Ytterbium is a lanthanide element with atomic number 70 and electron configuration 4f144f^{14}4f14 6s26s^{2}6s2. Its standard atomic weight is 173.045 u.
As a rare earth metal, ytterbium exhibits typical metallic characteristics, appearing as a bright silvery-white solid that is soft, malleable, and ductile. It is one of the softer lanthanides, with a Vickers hardness of approximately 206 MPa, comparable to or slightly softer than neighboring elements like thulium and lutetium. The density of ytterbium is 6.90 g/cm at 20 C, significantly lower than that of thulium (9.32 g/cm) and lutetium (9.84 g/cm), reflecting its unique divalent tendency in the metallic state.
Ytterbium has a relatively low melting point of 824 C and boiling point of 1196 C among the lanthanides, contributing to its smallest liquid range. It displays three allotropic forms: the -phase (close-packed hexagonal) stable below about -13 C, the -phase (face-centered cubic) stable from -13 C to 795 C (the room-temperature form), and the -phase (body-centered cubic) above 795 C. The specific heat capacity is 156 J/(kgK) at 25 C.

Chemical properties

Ytterbium is highly reactive, tarnishing slowly in air to form ytterbium(III) oxide (YbO) and burning readily when ignited in oxygen. It reacts slowly with cold water to produce ytterbium hydroxide and hydrogen gas but dissolves rapidly in dilute acids, liberating hydrogen and forming the corresponding ytterbium salts.
As a lanthanide, ytterbium primarily exhibits +3 and +2 oxidation states, with +3 (Yb) being the most stable and common due to its favorable electronic structure, while +2 (Yb) is less stable and requires stabilizing conditions such as specific ligands or intermetallic environments. The electron configuration of neutral ytterbium is 4f 6s; upon oxidation to Yb, it loses the 6s electrons and one 4f electron, resulting in 4f, which is paramagnetic, whereas Yb retains the filled 4f shell, making it diamagnetic and more akin to alkaline earth metals in stability for certain compounds. The ionic radius of Yb is 86.8 pm (for coordination number 6), significantly smaller than that of Yb at 100 pm, contributing to the greater stability of the +3 state in most aqueous and solid-state environments due to better lattice fitting and higher charge density.
In coordination chemistry, ytterbium ions typically adopt coordination numbers of 6 to 9 in complexes, reflecting the lanthanide contraction and preference for high coordination to accommodate the large number of f-electrons and ligands. This is exemplified by the formation of stable +3 salts such as ytterbium(III) chloride (YbCl), which features octahedral or higher coordination around the metal center.
The redox potential for the Yb/Yb couple in aqueous solution is -1.05 V, indicating that reduction to the +2 state is thermodynamically unfavorable under standard conditions but can be achieved electrochemically or with strong reductants.
Ytterbium(III) salts generally exhibit good solubility in aqueous solutions, following trends typical of lanthanide ions where solubility decreases slightly with increasing atomic number but remains high for chlorides, nitrates, and sulfates. The oxide YbO is insoluble in water but displays amphoteric behavior, dissolving in strong acids to form ytterbium salts and in concentrated bases under certain conditions to yield ytterbiumates, though its basic character predominates due to the high charge density of Yb.

Nuclear applications

Ytterbium-169 serves as a gamma ray source in medical applications, particularly in brachytherapy due to its suitable emission spectrum and half-life of 32.03 days. The isotope emits gamma rays with energies ranging from 63 keV to 308 keV, including a prominent line at 177 keV, enabling its use in high-dose-rate brachytherapy sources activated via neutron irradiation of ytterbium-168. Additionally, its gamma and X-ray emissions, with a mean energy of approximately 93 keV (excluding photons below 10 keV), support calibration of gamma-X ray detectors in nuclear medicine imaging systems.
Enriched ytterbium-176 is increasingly utilized in nuclear technologies, including as a precursor for producing lutetium-177 via neutron capture for radiopharmaceutical applications in reactors. ASP Isotopes Inc. completed commissioning of its quantum enrichment facility in April 2025, achieving initial enrichment of ytterbium-176 and targeting up to 99.75% purity for commercial supply later in the year. This development supports broader nuclear sector needs, such as enhanced neutron detection in reactors, where enriched stable isotopes improve performance in low-energy neutron environments.
Certain ytterbium isotopes show potential in neutron capture therapy owing to their thermal neutron cross-sections, with 174Yb exhibiting a value of 70.5 4.2 barns for the (n,) reaction producing 175Yb. Research into neutron-activated ytterbium compounds, such as Fe-Pt-Yb2O3 nanoparticles, explores their use in targeted cancer therapies by leveraging capture-induced radioactivity for localized radiation delivery.
Historically, ytterbium-169 has been employed in portable X-ray sources as a radiation substitute in off-grid settings, providing a compact alternative to electricity-dependent equipment. The market for enriched ytterbium isotopes, including Yb-176, is projected to grow from $0.6 million in 2024 to $1.6 million by 2032, driven by demand in nuclear reactors and medical isotope production.

Occurrence

Natural sources

Ytterbium occurs naturally only in oxidized form within minerals, primarily as a trace component associated with other rare earth elements (REEs), and never as a free element due to its chemical reactivity. The principal minerals hosting ytterbium are monazite ((Ce,La)PO), a phosphate rich in light REEs but containing small amounts of heavy REEs like ytterbium, xenotime (YPO), which is enriched in heavy REEs including ytterbium, and gadolinite ((Ce,La,Nd,Y)FeBeSiO), a silicate mineral found in pegmatites and granites. These minerals form in igneous, metamorphic, and sedimentary environments, often concentrated in placer deposits or carbonatite complexes.
Major deposits of ytterbium-bearing ores, as of 2025, are located at Bayan Obo in Inner Mongolia, China, the world's largest REE deposit primarily mined for iron but yielding significant REE byproducts including ytterbium; Mount Weld in Western Australia, a carbonatite-hosted site rich in heavy REEs; and Mountain Pass in California, USA, a bastnsite-monazite deposit that supplies ytterbium as part of mixed REE concentrates. Trace occurrences of ytterbium are also reported in accessory minerals such as apatite and zircon within granitic rocks and pegmatites, though these are not economically viable sources. In monazite ores, ytterbium typically comprises about 0.03% of the total rare earth oxides, reflecting its minor role relative to dominant light REEs like cerium and lanthanum.

Abundance

Ytterbium occurs at a concentration of 3.2 parts per million (ppm) in the Earth's crust, corresponding to a Clarke number that ranks it approximately 41st in elemental abundance. This value reflects its distribution primarily in igneous and sedimentary rocks, where it associates with other rare earth elements. In comparison, the solar abundance of ytterbium is estimated at 1.6 ppm (normalized to silicon), based on analyses of carbonaceous chondrites and solar photospheric spectra, indicating its primordial cosmic prevalence.
In oceanic environments, dissolved ytterbium concentrations average around 1 10^{-9} g/L in surface and mid-depth waters, with nutrient-like profiles showing depletion at the surface due to biological scavenging and enrichment in deeper layers from upwelling. Concentrations are notably higher in deep-sea ferromanganese nodules, where ytterbium can reach levels up to about 100 ppm, reflecting adsorptive enrichment from seawater.
Geochemically, ytterbium partitions preferentially into the heavy rare earth element (HREE) fraction during magmatic and hydrothermal processes, owing to its smaller ionic radius and higher charge density compared to light rare earths. In cosmic settings, the Yb/Lu ratio is approximately 1.5 in CI chondrites, underscoring ytterbium's relative enrichment over lutetium in primitive solar system materials.
Across the lanthanide series in the continental crust, elemental abundances exhibit a general decrease with increasing atomic number, from ~30 ppm for lanthanum to ~0.5 ppm for lutetium, with ytterbium fitting this trend at 3.2 ppm due to progressive incompatibility during crustal differentiation.

History

Discovery and isolation

The mineral gadolinite (originally called ytterbite), the primary source from which ytterbium was eventually isolated, was discovered in 1787 by Swedish army lieutenant Carl Axel Arrhenius in a quarry near the village of Ytterby, Sweden. This black stone contained several rare earth elements, marking the beginning of investigations into the complex rare earth family. In 1843, Swedish chemist Carl Gustaf Mosander analyzed gadolinite samples and succeeded in separating yttria into three distinct oxides: pure yttria, terbia, and erbia, using fractional precipitation techniques. These efforts laid the groundwork for further separations, as erbia was initially thought to represent a single element but proved to be a mixture.
In 1878, Swiss chemist Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac, working at the University of Geneva, closely examined erbia derived from gadolinite. By heating erbium nitrate to complete decomposition and extracting the residue, he isolated a new, nearly colorless earth that differed in solubility and atomic weight from erbia. Marignac named this new substance ytterbia, believing it to be the oxide of an undiscovered element, which he provisionally called ytterbium. Spectroscopic analysis supported this finding, revealing absorption bands distinct from those of erbium.
The true elemental nature of ytterbium was confirmed in 1907 by French chemist Georges Urbain, who demonstrated that Marignac's ytterbia was impure and contained two components. Through repeated fractional crystallizations of the ammonium double sulfates, Urbain separated ytterbia into neoytterbia (the oxide of ytterbium) and lutecia (later identified as lutetium oxide). He verified ytterbium's identity as a distinct element using emission spectroscopy to confirm its distinct spectral lines. This separation resolved lingering doubts about the purity of Marignac's sample and established ytterbium as the 14th rare earth element. The first preparation of metallic ytterbium occurred later, in 1937, when German chemists Wilhelm Klemm and Wilhelm Bommer reduced ytterbium(III) chloride with potassium vapor, yielding an impure metal sample.

Etymology

The name ytterbium derives from "ytterbia", the oxide isolated by Marignac, which in turn is named after the village of Ytterby in Sweden, where the source mineral gadolinite was discovered. The suffix "-ium" follows the convention for naming metallic elements.

Production

Extraction from ores

Ytterbium is extracted primarily from monazite sands, which contain about 0.03% ytterbium by weight. The process begins with acid digestion of the ore using sulfuric acid to produce a solution of rare earth sulfates. Thorium and other impurities are removed through precipitation. The rare earth elements are then separated into groups using solvent extraction with organophosphorus extractants, such as di-(2-ethylhexyl) phosphoric acid (D2EHPA), to isolate the heavy rare earth fraction containing ytterbium. Further separation of individual heavy rare earths, including ytterbium, is achieved through multi-stage solvent extraction or ion exchange chromatography.

Purification methods

The separated ytterbium is typically obtained as ytterbium oxide (YbO). To produce metallic ytterbium, the oxide is reduced with lanthanum metal in a high-temperature vacuum furnace:

Compounds

Halides and chalcogenides

Ytterbium forms trivalent halides such as ytterbium(III) fluoride (YbF), which adopts a hexagonal yttrium fluoride structure and is used in optical coatings due to its low refractive index and high stability. YbF is prepared by reacting ytterbium oxide with hydrofluoric acid or ammonium fluoride. Ytterbium(III) chloride (YbCl) is hygroscopic, crystallizes in a hexagonal form, and is synthesized by direct combination of elements or from oxide and HCl.
Among chalcogenides, ytterbium(II) sulfide (YbS) has a cubic NaCl structure and exhibits semiconductor properties with a bandgap of ~1.6 eV, while YbS (trivalent) is also known. These are prepared by reacting ytterbium with sulfur or from oxides. Ytterbium selenide (YbSe) and telluride (YbTe) show similar divalent behavior, with potential in thermoelectric applications.

Oxides and hydroxides

Ytterbium(III) oxide, YbO, is the primary oxide of ytterbium and adopts a cubic bixbyite structure in the Ia-3 space group. This compound exhibits a high melting point of 2350 C, contributing to its stability in high-temperature applications. YbO is commonly prepared by calcining ytterbium carbonate precursors at elevated temperatures, a method that yields pure oxide nanoparticles suitable for materials synthesis. Ytterbium monoxide, YbO, features Yb ions and crystallizes in a monoclinic structure, though it is less stable and often studied in thin-film or epitaxial forms.
Ytterbium(III) hydroxide, Yb(OH), possesses a hexagonal crystal structure and displays amphoteric behavior, dissolving in both strong acids and bases. Its low solubility in water is quantified by a solubility product constant (Ksp) of approximately 2.5 10-24. This hydroxide is typically synthesized via precipitation from aqueous solutions of ytterbium salts, such as nitrates, by adding a base like sodium hydroxide:

Other inorganic compounds

Ytterbium forms several borides, including YbB, YbB, and YbB, which exhibit notable structural and electronic properties. YbB adopts the hexagonal AlB-type structure and is synthesized by arc-melting ytterbium metal with boron or through direct combination of the elements at elevated temperatures around 1400C. This compound is metallic, with ytterbium in the +3 oxidation state, and displays antiferromagnetic ordering below 5.6 K. YbB crystallizes in the cubic NaCl-type structure. These borides are characterized by high hardness, making them suitable for applications requiring wear resistance, though specific mechanical data for ytterbium variants emphasize their brittleness compared to lighter rare-earth analogs.
Ytterbium nitride, YbN, adopts the rocksalt (NaCl-type) crystal structure and behaves as an intrinsic semiconductor with antiferromagnetic exchange interactions. It is prepared via reactive sputtering or nitridation of ytterbium metal under nitrogen atmosphere. The carbide YbC features an acetylide structure with C dumbbells in a tetragonal lattice, formed by reacting ytterbium with carbon at high temperatures.
The hydride YbH crystallizes in the cubic fluorite (CaF-type) structure and demonstrates reversible hydrogen absorption and desorption, positioning it as a candidate for hydrogen storage applications due to its metallic conductivity and stability up to moderate temperatures. YbH is typically synthesized by direct hydrogenation of ytterbium metal under hydrogen pressure at 300500C.
Ytterbium phosphide, YbP, is a narrow-bandgap semiconductor with potential optoelectronic uses. Silicides such as YbSi have been investigated for high-temperature oxidation resistance; studies in the 2020s reveal that YbSi forms protective SiO layers upon exposure to air or steam at 12001400C, though it oxidizes more readily than Si-richer phases like YbSi due to lower Yb:Si ratios promoting volatile oxide formation. These silicides are prepared by arc-melting or solid-state diffusion of ytterbium and silicon.

Applications

Optical and laser uses

Ytterbium is widely used as a dopant in optical fibers, particularly in ytterbium-doped fiber amplifiers (YDFAs) for amplifying optical signals in telecommunications at wavelengths around 1 m, enabling high-gain, low-noise performance in fiber optic networks. Additionally, ytterbium-doped fiber lasers are employed in high-power applications such as material processing, including cutting, welding, marking, and engraving of metals and ceramics, due to their efficiency, compact design, and output powers exceeding 1 kW. These lasers also find use in biomedical research for fluorescence microscopy and spectroscopy.

Quantum technologies

Ytterbium plays a pivotal role in quantum technologies due to its stable isotopes, long coherence times, and versatile electronic structure, enabling applications in quantum computing, simulation, and networking. The isotope ^{171}Yb, with its nuclear spin of 1/2, is particularly favored for encoding qubits, while other isotopes like ^{176}Yb support specialized implementations. These properties allow ytterbium-based systems to achieve high-fidelity operations essential for scalable quantum information processing.
In trapped-ion quantum computing, ^{171}Yb^{+} ions serve as qubits, leveraging hyperfine clock states for exceptional coherence. Systems from IonQ and Honeywell (now Quantinuum) employ chains of these ions in microfabricated surface traps, achieving two-qubit gate fidelities exceeding 99.9%, with recent demonstrations reaching 99.99% above the Doppler limit. Gate times for two-qubit operations are on the order of 10 s, enabling efficient entanglement generation via phonon-mediated interactions. This architecture supports modular scaling, with IonQ's 30-qubit Forte system demonstrating benchmarked performance in 2024.
Neutral ytterbium atoms trapped in optical tweezers offer a complementary platform for scalable quantum computing, benefiting from identical particles and reconfigurable arrays. At the University of Michigan in 2025, researchers explored the metastable ^{3}P_{2} state for fast gate operations alongside the long-lived ^{3}P_{0} state for qubit storage, funded by a $1.5 million Army Research Office grant. This approach enables parallel processing in larger entangled arrays, with coherence times supporting robust quantum operations. Ytterbium's multiple valence electrons provide rich level structures for Rydberg excitations, facilitating high-fidelity two-qubit gates through controlled dipole interactions. Spectroscopy of ^{171}Yb Rydberg states, modeled comprehensively in 2025, validates energies and matrix elements for gate fidelities above 99.9%.
For quantum networks, ^{171}Yb atoms enable efficient entanglement distribution over fiber links. A 2025 scalable protocol uses one-dimensional atom arrays in optical tweezers, imaged onto fiber arrays for parallel photon collection at telecom wavelengths (1,389 nm). This mid-circuit networking scheme preserves qubit coherence while generating time-bin encoded entanglement between atoms and photons, achieving 99% fidelity with upgrades and negligible crosstalk across sites. Demonstrated over 40-m fibers, it paves the way for distributed quantum computing.
Ytterbium ions also advance quantum simulation of complex systems. In 2025, a single trapped ^{171}Yb^{+} ion simulated dynamic energy level shifts in organic molecules under light interaction, modeling vibronic couplings a million times more efficiently than classical methods. This proof-of-principle highlights trapped ions' potential for molecular spectroscopy beyond simple Hamiltonians. Rydberg states in ytterbium further support gate-based simulations of correlated materials.
Commercial advancements include isotope enrichment for quantum applications. ASP Isotopes Inc. commissioned its first quantum enrichment laser system in 2025, producing ^{176}Yb at up to 99.75% purity for qubit implementations requiring spinless nuclei to minimize decoherence. Initial commercial samples were shipped, targeting quantum computing demands.

Industrial and nuclear uses

Ytterbium is alloyed with stainless steel to enhance its mechanical strength, corrosion resistance, and durability, typically through the addition of ytterbium oxide (YbO) at low concentrations. This doping improves the material's performance in high-stress applications, such as in aerospace and automotive components.
In organic synthesis, ytterbium compounds, particularly ytterbium(III) triflate (Yb(OTf)), serve as efficient, water-tolerant Lewis acid catalysts for reactions including the addition of amines to carbodiimides and the three-component coupling for imidazole derivatives. These catalysts enable mild conditions and high yields in environmentally friendly processes, with recyclability enhancing their industrial viability.
Ytterbium oxide is utilized in nuclear reactors as a material for control rods due to its high neutron absorption cross-section, which helps regulate fission rates and improve reactor safety. The isotope Yb serves as a gamma ray source in portable X-ray machines for non-destructive testing, particularly in field applications where electrical power is unavailable. Additionally, the isotope Yb is enriched for the production of Lu via irradiation, supporting applications in nuclear technology, with advancements in enrichment processes enabling commercial-scale output by 2025.
The broader rare earth elements market, to which ytterbium contributes through demand in metallurgy and nuclear sectors, is projected to grow at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of approximately 12.6%, reaching $10.9 billion by 2029. Recent advances in the United States include the development of commercial-scale separation facilities for heavy rare earth elements like ytterbium, with facilities such as the Louisiana Strategic Metals Complex targeting operational expansion in 2025 to bolster domestic production.

Health and Safety

Biological effects

Ytterbium has no known biological role in living organisms.
At low doses, ytterbium salts such as ytterbium chloride can stimulate cellular metabolism, for instance by increasing mitotic activity and DNA synthesis in human breast cancer cells exposed to 0.1 mM/L for 16 months. This stimulation is associated with uptake primarily in bones and, to a lesser extent, the liver.
Ytterbium exhibits low acute toxicity, with an oral LD50 greater than 4,800 mg/kg in mice for ytterbium(III) chloride. Its compounds are irritants to the skin and eyes upon contact. In aquatic organisms, a 2025 study on rainbow trout juveniles demonstrated sublethal effects at concentrations as low as 60 g/L after 96 hours of exposure, including decreased lipid peroxidation (LPO) in the liver and gills, indicative of reduced reactive oxygen species (ROS) production due to mitochondrial depression.
Ytterbium bioaccumulates predominantly in bones, with trace amounts in the liver and kidneys following exposure. It has no established essentiality in mammals.
In medical applications, the radioisotope ytterbium-169 shows potential for nuclear imaging as a radiopharmaceutical complex with diethylenetriaminepentaacetic acid, offering improved resolution with reduced patient irradiation; however, its use remains limited by the element's rarity and production challenges.

Environmental impact

The extraction of ytterbium, primarily as part of rare earth element (REE) mining, involves acidic leaching processes that release ytterbium ions into tailings and wastewater, leading to significant water contamination. At major sites like China's Bayan Obo minethe world's largest REE depositthese operations have resulted in elevated ytterbium concentrations in surrounding soils and groundwater, with tailings ponds contributing to long-term pollution through acid mine drainage.
Ytterbium exhibits moderate bioavailability in ecosystems, accumulating in plants and aquatic organisms such as fish due to its uptake from contaminated soils and water. Studies on REEs, including ytterbium, show bioaccumulation factors in fish tissues that reflect environmental exposure levels, though ytterbium's accumulation is often lower than lighter REEs like neodymium owing to its geochemical speciation. Advancements in wastewater treatment, such as ion flotation techniques, enable efficient removal of ytterbium(III) ions from dilute solutions by forming insoluble complexes with surfactants, achieving recovery rates exceeding 90% under controlled pH conditions.
As a non-degradable heavy metal, ytterbium persists in the environment, contributing to long-term soil enrichment near mining sites without natural breakdown. Its low mobility in soils stems from strong adsorption in the +3 oxidation state to clay minerals and organic matter, resulting in high partition coefficients (Kd > 1000 L/kg) that limit leaching into groundwater.
Globally, rising demand for ytterbiumdriven by its use in quantum technologies and alloyshas projected REE market growth to over 200,000 metric tons annually by 2025, intensifying mining pressures and environmental releases. However, recycling rates for ytterbium and other REEs remain below 1%, exacerbating reliance on primary extraction. Unlike some elements, ytterbium has no direct impact on ozone depletion or climate forcing, though indirect effects arise from energy-intensive mining.

Handling precautions

Ytterbium metal is highly reactive with air and moisture, with fine powders capable of spontaneous ignition. It should be handled in an inert atmosphere, such as under argon or nitrogen, using glove boxes or sealed systems to prevent oxidation and fire hazards. Personal protective equipment (PPE) including chemical-resistant gloves, safety goggles, and respiratory protection is required, particularly in areas with potential dust generation. Work in well-ventilated fume hoods or enclosed processes to minimize inhalation risks. Avoid skin and eye contact, as direct exposure may cause irritation; wash thoroughly if contact occurs. Store in airtight, moisture-proof containers away from oxidizers, acids, and water. For ytterbium compounds, follow similar precautions, treating them as irritants and disposing of waste per local regulations for heavy metals.
