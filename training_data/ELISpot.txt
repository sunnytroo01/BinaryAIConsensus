ELISpot
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The enzyme-linked immunospot (ELISpot) assay is a highly sensitive immunoassay technique designed to detect and enumerate individual cells secreting specific proteins, such as cytokines or antibodies, by visualizing discrete spots on a solid-phase membrane that correspond to the secretory activity of single cells.
Developed in 1983 by Czerkinsky et al. as an adaptation of ELISA and hemolytic plaque assays, ELISpot was initially created to quantify antibody-secreting B cells but quickly evolved to assess T-cell functions, including the production of key cytokines like interferon-gamma (IFN-).
The assay's core principle involves coating multi-well platestypically with nitrocellulose or polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) membraneswith capture antibodies specific to the target analyte; upon addition of cells and stimulation, secreted molecules diffuse short distances and bind locally, forming visible spots after detection with enzyme-linked secondary antibodies and a precipitating substrate.
This method offers superior sensitivity, capable of detecting rare antigen-specific immune cells at frequencies as low as 1 in 100,000 to 1 in 1,000,000, making it indispensable for functional immunology studies.
ELISpot has broad applications in vaccine development, cancer immunotherapy monitoring, infectious disease diagnostics, including the FDA-approved T-SPOT.TB test for latent tuberculosis detection, and organ transplantation monitoring, where positive pre-transplant donor-specific IFN- ELISPOT results are associated with 37 times higher odds of acute rejection and a negative predictive value of ~81% for rejection, complementing humoral immunity assessments. with over 5,000 peer-reviewed publications underscoring its impact.
Variants like Fluorospot extend the assay's capabilities to multiplex detection of up to three analytes simultaneously, enabling more detailed profiling of immune cell subsets such as T cells, natural killer cells, and monocytes.

Overview

Principles

The enzyme-linked immunospot (ELISpot) assay is a highly sensitive technique designed to quantify the frequency of individual cells secreting specific molecules, such as cytokines or antibodies, by detecting their secretion at the single-cell level. Originally developed in the 1980s for the detection of antibody secretion from B cells, it was later adapted for cytokines such as interferon-gamma and has become a cornerstone for assessing cellular immune responses.
At its core, the ELISpot assay operates on the principle of immunochemical capture and enzymatic amplification, akin to a sandwich enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) but adapted for spatial resolution on a solid-phase membrane. Capture antibodies specific to the target analyte are pre-coated onto a hydrophobic membrane, typically nitrocellulose or PVDF, positioned at the bottom of a multi-well plate; upon secretion by cells placed in the wells, the analytes diffuse locally and bind to these immobilized antibodies, forming an antigen-antibody complex. This is followed by the addition of biotinylated detection antibodies that recognize a distinct epitope on the captured analyte, which in turn bind to enzyme-conjugated streptavidin (e.g., alkaline phosphatase or horseradish peroxidase); the enzymatic reaction with a chromogenic substrate then precipitates an insoluble colored product, amplifying the signal for visualization.
Spot formation in the ELISpot assay arises from the localized accumulation of this enzymatic precipitate around each secreting cell, creating a visible "spot" whose size and intensity correspond to the amount and duration of secretion from that individual cell. Each discrete spot thus represents the secretory product of a single responding cell, enabling direct enumeration of the frequency of antigen-specific or activated cells without the need for cell expansion or purification. This single-cell resolution distinguishes ELISpot from bulk assays like ELISA, which measure total analyte concentration in a sample.
Biologically, ELISpot is particularly suited to evaluating adaptive immune responses, where it quantifies cytokine secretion from T cellssuch as interferon-gamma (IFN-) or interleukin-2 (IL-2)in response to antigen-specific stimulation, reflecting the activation and effector functions of CD4+ helper or CD8+ cytotoxic T lymphocytes. For B cells, it detects immunoglobulin secretion, capturing the output of plasma cells or memory B cells upon antigenic challenge, thereby providing insights into humoral immunity and the differentiation of antigen-specific B cell clones. In both cases, the assay relies on the physiological process of rapid secretion following immune cell activation, often in the context of T-B cell interactions mediated by antigen-presenting cells.

Key Components

The ELISpot assay relies on specialized multi-well plates featuring hydrophobic membranes, such as polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF), which enable the capture and retention of secreted analytes directly on the membrane surface for subsequent visualization as distinct spots. These plates, typically in a 96-well format, provide high protein-binding capacity and low background staining, essential for detecting low-frequency immune responses at the single-cell level. The hydrophobic nature of PVDF membranes facilitates the immobilization of capture antibodies without diffusion, ensuring precise localization of secreted molecules.
Capture antibodies, specific to the target analyte such as cytokines like interferon-gamma (IFN-), are immobilized onto the membrane prior to the assay to bind and trap secreted proteins from individual cells. These monoclonal or polyclonal antibodies are selected for their high affinity and specificity, forming the foundation of the sandwich immunoassay format used in ELISpot. For instance, anti-IFN- antibodies (e.g., clone NIB42 for human or AN18 for mouse) coat the membrane to capture cytokine release from activated T cells.
Signal amplification in ELISpot involves biotinylated detection antibodies that bind to the captured analyte, followed by enzyme conjugates such as streptavidin-horseradish peroxidase (HRP) or streptavidin-alkaline phosphatase (AP), which link the biotin to an enzymatic reporter for enhanced sensitivity. The biotin-streptavidin interaction provides a robust, low-background amplification step, with examples including biotinylated anti-IFN- antibodies (e.g., clone 4S.B3 for human) paired with streptavidin-HRP. Alkaline phosphatase conjugates are alternatively used when compatibility with certain substrates is required.
Chromogenic substrates trigger the enzymatic reaction to produce visible spots, with 3-amino-9-ethylcarbazole (AEC) commonly employed for HRP-based detection to yield insoluble red precipitates that outline cytokine-secreting cells. AEC's precipitation properties ensure spot resolution and quantification, typically monitored for 5-30 minutes to optimize signal without overdevelopment. For AP conjugates, substrates like BCIP/NBT generate blue-black spots, offering flexibility based on the enzymatic system.
The assay utilizes effector cells capable of secreting the target analyte, most commonly peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) from humans or splenocytes from mice, which include T cells and antigen-presenting cells responsive to stimuli. PBMCs are isolated from blood and used at densities of 2 10^5 to 6 10^5 cells per well to detect antigen-specific responses, while splenocytes serve similarly in murine models at 1 10^5 to 3 10^5 cells per well. These cell types are critical for maintaining viability (80%) and functional integrity to ensure accurate enumeration of secreting cells.

History

Development

The ELISpot technique was invented in 1983 by Cecil Czerkinsky and colleagues at the University of Gteborg in Sweden, initially to enumerate specific immunoglobulin-secreting cells, such as those from the spleens of immunized animals, producing antigen-specific antibodies. This development built on earlier plaque-forming assays but introduced a solid-phase format using enzyme-linked detection to visualize individual secreting cells as distinct spots on a membrane or plate surface.
The method's initial description appeared in a seminal paper published in the Journal of Immunological Methods in 1983, adapting the principles of the enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA)which measures bulk protein secretionto the single-cell level for precise enumeration of rare antibody-secreting cells. The early motivation stemmed from limitations in existing techniques like hemolytic plaque assays, which relied on fragile red blood cell targets and offered poor quantitation; ELISpot addressed this by enabling sensitive detection of low-frequency antigen-specific cells activated post-vaccination or infection, where traditional bulk assays like ELISA failed to resolve cellular contributions.
By the 1990s, the technique gained practical accessibility through the introduction of the first commercial kits, with companies like Mabtech providing standardized reagents, pre-coated plates, and detection antibodies to facilitate widespread adoption in immunological research. In the 2000s, ELISpot evolved into the FluoroSpot variant, which uses fluorescent labels for multiplexed detection of multiple cytokines from single cells.

Milestones and Advancements

In the 1990s, the ELISpot assay underwent significant standardization for detecting T-cell cytokine secretion, particularly interferon-gamma (IFN-), which became a cornerstone for assessing cellular immune responses in infectious diseases such as HIV and tuberculosis. Seminal studies demonstrated its utility in enumerating antigen-specific T cells, enabling rapid diagnosis of latent tuberculosis infection even in immunocompromised individuals. This adaptation shifted ELISpot from primarily B-cell antibody detection to T-cell monitoring, with protocols refined for reproducibility across laboratories.
During the 2000s, the introduction of automated readers revolutionized ELISpot analysis by enabling precise, user-independent spot counting, thereby enhancing assay reproducibility and throughput. Instruments like the ImmunoSpot analyzers from Cellular Technology Limited (CTL), patented in the late 1990s and commercialized in the early 2000s, incorporated advanced image analysis algorithms to distinguish true spots from artifacts, reducing manual variability that had previously limited large-scale applications. These systems facilitated standardized data output, supporting multi-center studies in vaccine development and immune monitoring.
By 2010, ELISpot gained formal validation for use in clinical trials, including FDA-recognized immune monitoring endpoints for cancer vaccines, as outlined in joint workshops by the Society for Immunotherapy of Cancer (SITC), FDA, and National Cancer Institute (NCI). This acceptance established ELISpot as a reliable surrogate for T-cell responses in immunotherapy evaluations, with guidelines emphasizing its role in assessing vaccine efficacy against tumor antigens. The assay's integration into phase I/II trials for melanoma and prostate cancer vaccines underscored its clinical relevance, providing quantitative data on IFN--secreting cells post-vaccination.
In the 2020s, advancements in multiplexing have expanded ELISpot's capabilities, allowing simultaneous detection of multiple cytokines (e.g., IFN-, IL-2, and TNF-) from single cells via FluoroSpot variants, improving resolution of polyfunctional T-cell responses in vaccine and therapy assessments. Concurrently, integration of artificial intelligence for data analysis has automated complex image processing, with AI algorithms achieving over 95% accuracy in spot enumeration and classification, minimizing human bias in high-throughput settings. These innovations, validated in recent clinical protocols, enhance sensitivity for low-frequency responses in immunotherapy trials.
ELISpot's global adoption is evidenced by over 17,000 peer-reviewed publications as of 2025, reflecting its widespread use in immunology research and diagnostics. The Human ImmunoPhenotyping Consortium (HIPC) has contributed standardization guidelines, promoting harmonized protocols for functional assays like ELISpot within broader immunophenotyping efforts to ensure comparability across studies.

Mechanism

Standard ELISpot Procedure

The standard ELISpot procedure involves a series of sequential steps to detect and enumerate cytokine-secreting cells, such as T cells producing interferon-gamma (IFN-), using a colorimetric enzymatic detection system. Multi-well plates, typically featuring a hydrophobic polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF) membrane to enhance spot retention, are employed for optimal assay performance.
The process begins with coating the wells of a 96-well plate with capture antibodies specific to the target cytokine, diluted in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) at concentrations around 115 g/mL, and incubating overnight at 4C to allow passive adsorption onto the membrane. Prior to coating, the plate membrane is often pre-wet with 1570% ethanol for 12 minutes to improve antibody binding, followed by rinsing with sterile water.
Following coating, excess unbound antibodies are removed by washing, and the wells are blocked with a solution such as cell culture medium containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), bovine serum albumin (BSA) at 15%, or serum to minimize non-specific binding, typically for 12 hours at room temperature.
Antigen-specific or polyclonal stimulant-activated cells, such as peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs), are then added to the wells at densities of 12 10 cells per well in 100200 L of complete culture medium, and the plate is incubated for 1648 hours at 37C in 5% CO to permit cytokine secretion and capture by the immobilized antibodies.
After incubation, the plate is washed multiple times (35 times) with PBS or PBS containing 0.05% Tween-20 to eliminate cells and unbound material, followed by the addition of biotinylated detection antibodies that recognize a distinct epitope on the captured cytokine, with incubation for 12 hours at room temperature. Streptavidin conjugated to an enzyme, such as alkaline phosphatase (ALP) or horseradish peroxidase (HRP), is then applied for another 12 hours to bind the biotinylated antibodies.
Enzymatic development proceeds by adding a precipitating substrate, such as BCIP/NBT for ALP or TMB for HRP, which reacts to form insoluble colored spots at the secretion sites, with development times of 230 minutes until spots are visible, after which the reaction is stopped with water.
Spots are quantified either manually under a dissecting microscope or, more commonly, using automated imaging systems that analyze digital scans of the plate to count spot-forming units (SFU), standardized as SFU per 10 input cells to account for varying cell densities across experiments.

FluoroSpot Variant

The FluoroSpot assay is a fluorescence-based variant of the ELISpot technique, developed by Mabtech in 2008 to enable multiplex detection of secreted analytes from individual cells. This adaptation replaces the enzymatic amplification used in standard ELISpot with direct or indirect fluorescence labeling of detection antibodies, allowing for the simultaneous analysis of multiple cytokines or other proteins in a single well. By capturing secreted molecules on a membrane and visualizing them through distinct fluorescent signals, FluoroSpot provides enhanced resolution for studying polyfunctional immune cells, such as those secreting multiple factors in response to stimulation.
The procedure diverges from the standard ELISpot primarily in the detection phase: after cell incubation and washing, biotinylated detection antibodies are added, followed by fluorophore-conjugated secondary reagents or streptavidin for signal development. This indirect labeling approach, often supplemented with a FluoroSpot enhancer to boost signal intensity, supports multi-color detection via laser scanning instruments equipped with filters for specific wavelengths, enabling the quantification of up to four analytes concurrently. For instance, kits are available for simultaneous measurement of IFN- (typically in one channel), IL-2, IL-4, and granzyme B, revealing the co-secretion profiles of T cells or other effectors that would require multiple separate assays in the enzymatic format.
Readout involves digital fluorescence imaging with automated scanners, such as the Mabtech IRIS, followed by analysis using software like Mabtech Apex that employs RAWspot technology to count spots, measure their intensity, and assess colocalization of fluorescent signals within the same cellular footprint. Key reagents include spectrally distinct dyesFITC for green emission, PE for yellow-orange, and APC for redcoated onto plates or applied post-detection, which facilitate flow cytometry-like multi-parameter evaluation of secretion patterns without the need for cell dissociation. This multiplexing advantage supports detailed functional profiling of immune responses, such as identifying bifunctional or trifunctional cells, while maintaining the high sensitivity of the ELISpot platform.

Applications

Immunological Research

The ELISpot assay plays a pivotal role in immunological research by enabling the sensitive detection of antigen-specific T-cell responses, particularly in vaccine development where it quantifies CD8+ T-cell secretion of cytokines like interferon-gamma (IFN-) in response to viral peptides. For instance, in evaluating peptide-based vaccines against viruses such as HIV or hepatitis C, ELISpot measures the frequency and magnitude of cytotoxic CD8+ T-cell activation, providing insights into vaccine efficacy and immune memory formation. This approach has been standardized for high-sensitivity monitoring, often detecting responses as low as 1 in 100,000 T cells, which is crucial for assessing correlates of protection in preclinical and early-phase trials.
In studies of autoimmune diseases, ELISpot facilitates the enumeration of cytokine profiles to dissect T-helper cell imbalances, such as the Th1/Th2 shift in experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis (EAE), a model for multiple sclerosis. Researchers use it to quantify IFN- (Th1-associated) and interleukin-4 (IL-4, Th2-associated) secreting cells from splenocytes or lymph node cultures stimulated with myelin antigens, revealing how therapies modulate pathogenic responses. Similarly, for post-infection memory cell quantification, ELISpot, often in a cultured format, identifies persistent antigen-specific memory T cells against pathogens like cytomegalovirus (CMV) or influenza, tracking their decline or maintenance over years. In CMV research, it detects memory CD8+ T cells producing IFN- after peptide restimulation, correlating with long-term viral control.
ELISpot supports high-throughput screening for epitope mapping by testing large peptide libraries, allowing identification of immunodominant T-cell epitopes from pathogens or tumors. Techniques like pooled peptide matrices or deconvolution strategies enable screening thousands of overlapping peptides in a single experiment, accelerating discovery for vaccine design. This has been instrumental in over 300 studies on SARS-CoV-2 T-cell responses by 2025, where ELISpot quantified spike and nucleoprotein-specific CD4+ and CD8+ cells across infection and vaccination cohorts, informing variant escape and booster strategies.

Clinical and Diagnostic Uses

ELISpot assays play a crucial role in monitoring immune responses during cancer immunotherapy, particularly in tracking tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) activity in melanoma trials. In phase I-II clinical trials involving metastatic melanoma patients vaccinated with dendritic cell-based vaccines targeting antigens like gp100 and MELAN-A/MART-1, the assay quantified IFN--producing CD8+ T cells, revealing post-vaccination increases in spot-forming units (SFU) up to 1 log unit (e.g., from 1/2,600 to 1/200 cells), with one responsive patient showing clinical benefit. ELISpot also evaluates TIL potency by measuring cytokines such as IFN- and granzyme B against autologous tumors, aiding in the development and monitoring of TIL therapies like the FDA-approved lifileucel for advanced melanoma.
In vaccine efficacy assessment, ELISpot measures post-vaccination SFU counts to gauge T-cell responses, as seen in Bacillus Calmette-Gurin (BCG) trials for tuberculosis. Studies in cynomolgus macaques vaccinated with BCG demonstrated a peak IFN- response at 6 weeks post-vaccination, with SFU per 10^6 peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) declining thereafter but persisting for at least 18 weeks, though incorporating spot size improved discriminatory power over SFU alone.
For infectious disease diagnostics, ELISpot-based IFN- release assays like T-SPOT.TB detect latent tuberculosis (TB) by enumerating antigen-specific T cells responding to Mycobacterium tuberculosis peptides such as ESAT-6 and CFP-10. The World Health Organization endorses interferon- release assays, including T-SPOT.TBthe only ELISpot-based version with cell normalizationfor latent TB infection screening, with recent FDA approval in 2025 for an automated platform reducing indeterminate results.
ELISpot aids in transplant rejection prediction by monitoring alloreactive T cells pre- and post-transplant. A meta-analysis of pre-transplant donor-specific IFN- ELISpot assays showed that positive results associate with 37 times higher odds of acute rejection, with a negative predictive value of approximately 81% for rejection, and positive results linked to lower graft function (e.g., reduced estimated glomerular filtration rate ) and worse overall outcomes. This assay complements humoral immunity tests, such as donor-specific antibody (DSA) detection, by focusing on cellular immunity. In specific studies of kidney transplant recipients, pretransplant IFN- ELISpot (cutoff: 20 SFU/10^5 PBMCs) predicted biopsy-proven acute rejection with 96% sensitivity, 41% specificity, and 97% negative predictive value, associating high donor-reactive T cells with elevated rejection risk (odds ratio 15.11). Post-transplant, the assay distinguishes rejection from other graft dysfunctions using IFN- or IL-21 cutoffs, with negative predictive values up to 98%.
Emerging applications include HIV reservoir sizing via variants like VIP-SPOT, an ELISpot adaptation that quantifies productive HIV-1 reservoirs by detecting p24 antigen-secreting CD4+ T cells after reactivation, correlating with intact proviral DNA (r=0.48) and enabling monitoring of cure strategies in antiretroviral-treated individuals. For COVID-19 vaccine durability, optimized cultured ELISpot assesses central memory T-cell IFN- responses to SARS-CoV-2 peptides, revealing persistent recall responses up to 10 days post-stimulation in vaccinated cohorts, with enhancements like IL-2 supplementation improving specificity for long-term immunity evaluation as of 2025.

Advantages and Limitations

Strengths

The ELISpot assay exhibits exceptional sensitivity, capable of detecting antigen-specific cells at frequencies as low as 1 in 100,000, allowing for the identification of rare immune populations without the need for prior enrichment. This high sensitivity stems from its ability to capture cytokine or other secreted products directly from individual cells during a short incubation period, typically 18-24 hours, enabling real-time assessment of cellular responses in contrast to methods requiring multi-day cell cultures for expansion. Such rapid detection preserves the functional state of ex vivo cells, providing insights into immediate immune activity.
A key strength lies in its single-cell resolution, which facilitates direct enumeration of cytokine- or antibody-secreting cells without the confounding effects of cell expansion or pooling, thereby maintaining the integrity of functional data from heterogeneous samples. The assay's output is inherently quantitative, measured in spot-forming units (SFU) per defined cell number, which supports robust statistical comparisons across experiments, donors, or treatment conditions.
ELISpot demonstrates remarkable versatility, adaptable to a wide array of analytes including cytokines like interferon-gamma and immunoglobulins, as well as applicable across species such as human and mouse through species-specific antibody kits. Furthermore, it is cost-effective for analyzing large cohorts, requiring fewer cells and smaller sample volumesoften derivable from as little as 1-5 mL of bloodcompared to resource-intensive alternatives like flow cytometry.

Challenges and Limitations

One major challenge in ELISpot assays is the inherent variability in spot counting, which can be subjective and influenced by factors such as cell viability, background noise, and operator interpretation, resulting in coefficients of variation (CV) often exceeding 20% near or below the limit of detection. This variability is exacerbated by inter-assay and inter-operator inconsistencies, particularly when manual counting is used, though automated systems can mitigate some subjectivity. Additionally, logistical issues arise from the preference for fresh cells, as frozen samples reduce sensitivity and increase variability.
Standard ELISpot assays are limited in multiplexing capability, typically detecting only one analyte per well or plate due to the reliance on a single chromogenic substrate, necessitating multiple parallel assays for comprehensive cytokine profiling. This constraint increases reagent costs and sample requirements, making high-throughput analysis inefficient compared to more advanced variants.
The assay is time-intensive, generally requiring 2-3 days to complete from cell preparation to spot enumeration, with significant hands-on time for setup, incubation, and development demanding skilled personnel. This extended timeline can delay results in clinical or research settings where rapid turnaround is essential.
False positives can occur due to non-specific antibody binding or inadequate blocking, leading to high background spots that mimic true responses, while false negatives may result from anergic cells failing to secrete cytokines despite antigen exposure. Such errors are particularly problematic in low-frequency responses, requiring stringent controls to maintain reliability.
In clinical applications, regulatory hurdles persist due to the lack of standardized harmonization guidelines, complicating validation and comparability across laboratories, as highlighted in bioanalytical recommendations from the 2020s. Efforts toward assay standardization, such as those from proficiency panels (e.g., Immudex T-cell ELISpot panels as of 20202021) and the 2022 Global CRO Council (GCC) white paper on validation parameters including precision below 25% CV, aim to address these issues but underscore the need for ongoing regulatory development.

Comparisons to Related Techniques

Versus ELISA

The enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) and the enzyme-linked immunospot (ELISpot) assay share foundational principles in their use of sandwich antibody capture formats, where a capture antibody is immobilized on a solid surface to bind the target analyte, followed by detection with a secondary enzyme-conjugated antibody that generates a measurable signal. However, ELISpot localizes cytokine secretion to individual cells by capturing analytes directly on a membrane or nitrocellulose substrate as they are secreted, forming visible spots that correspond to spot-forming cells (SFCs), whereas ELISA measures the average concentration of soluble analytes in bulk supernatants from entire cell populations.
A key advantage of ELISpot lies in its cellular-level resolution, enabling detection of rare antigen-specific cells at frequencies as low as 0.01%, which ELISA cannot distinguish due to its reliance on averaged population-level measurements that mask heterogeneityfor instance, ELISA may fail to identify a 1% responder subpopulation within a sample if the overall signal is diluted. This single-cell sensitivity makes ELISpot 10 to 200 times more sensitive than ELISA for cytokine detection in culture supernatants. In contrast, ELISA excels in high-throughput screening of large sample volumes, as it processes soluble extracts more rapidly and scalably without the need for live cell handling.
In vaccine trials, ELISpot is particularly valuable for quantifying the frequency of responding T cells, such as IFN-secreting cells post-immunization, providing a direct functional readout of cellular immunity, while ELISA assesses total cytokine levels or antibody titers in serum to gauge overall humoral responses.

Versus Flow Cytometry

The enzyme-linked immunospot (ELISpot) assay and flow cytometry, particularly intracellular cytokine staining (ICS), are both widely used for enumerating antigen-specific T cell responses by detecting cytokine production, but they differ fundamentally in their detection mechanisms and outputs. ELISpot captures cytokines secreted by individual cells onto a membrane, forming visible spots that represent the number of cytokine-secreting cells (spot-forming cells, SFCs), typically without requiring cell fixation or blocking secretion. In contrast, flow cytometry with ICS measures intracellularly accumulated cytokines after blocking secretion (e.g., using brefeldin A or monensin) and cell permeabilization, allowing multiparametric analysis of cytokine expression within phenotypically defined cell subsets. These differences lead to complementary but sometimes discordant results, with studies showing statistical correlations in response magnitudes for antigens like CMV pp65 and HIV-1 Gag, yet no exact numeric agreement between the assays.
ELISpot generally offers higher sensitivity for detecting low-frequency antigen-specific T cells, often identifying responses in 1 in 10^5 to 10^6 cells, making it preferable for rare event detection or binary (positive/negative) assessments in clinical trials and vaccine monitoring. For instance, in evaluating SARS-CoV-2 spike-specific T cells post-infection, ELISpot detected responses in 67% of paucisymptomatic patients (median 61 SFCs per million PBMCs), compared to 44% by ICS (median 0.12% of T cells), with ELISpot identifying positives missed by flow cytometry. It also excels in assessing natural secretion dynamics ex vivo, avoiding artifacts from secretion inhibitors, and supports high throughput (up to 96-384 wells per plate) with lower costs and fewer cells required per sample. However, ELISpot is limited to one cytokine per assay and provides no phenotypic information on the secreting cells, such as CD4+ vs. CD8+ subsets or memory status.
Flow cytometry, conversely, provides richer phenotypic and functional data, enabling simultaneous measurement of multiple cytokines (e.g., IFN-, TNF-, IL-2) alongside surface markers to characterize responder cells, such as effector memory CD4+ T cells dominant in SARS-CoV-2 responses. It offers a broader dynamic range for quantifying response magnitudes, often reporting higher values than ELISpot due to detection of synthesized but not necessarily secreted cytokines. Yet, ICS is more labor-intensive, expensive, and technically demanding, requiring sophisticated equipment, larger cell numbers (often 10^6 per sample), and complex optimization, which can introduce variability from fixation and permeabilization steps that preclude further cell use. Concordance between the assays varies, with 76% agreement (Cohen's kappa 0.55) observed in SARS-CoV-2 studies, but discrepancies arise from ICS's lower sensitivity for low-level responses and potential underestimation due to secretion blockade. Overall, ELISpot is favored for rapid, sensitive enumeration in resource-limited settings, while flow cytometry is essential for detailed mechanistic insights.
