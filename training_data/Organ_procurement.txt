 Organ procurement refers to the coordinated medical, legal, and logistical processes for identifying suitable donors, evaluating organ viability, and surgically recovering organs or tissues from deceased individualstypically after declaration of brain death or circulatory deathor from living donors, for transplantation into patients with irreversible organ failure. This practice, governed by national regulations and organizations such as the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS) in the United States, involves hospitals, procurement entities, and transport networks to ensure organs remain functional during the narrow window post-recovery, often mere hours. In 2023, global efforts yielded approximately 172,397 solid organ transplants from 45,861 deceased donors and additional living donors, reflecting a 9.5% rise from the prior year amid ongoing demand. Despite these advances, severe shortages persist, with over 103,000 patients on U.S. waiting lists as of 2025 and daily deaths among waitlisted individuals underscoring the supply-demand imbalance driven by factors including donor scarcity and geographic disparities. Ethical debates center on consent modelsexplicit opt-in versus presumed consentand the dead donor rule, which mandates procurement only after irreversible cessation of vital functions to avoid hastening death, while empirical evidence highlights inefficiencies in procurement performance and allocation that exacerbate wait times. Financial incentives for donation remain prohibited in many jurisdictions to preserve intrinsic motivations and prevent commodification, though shortages fuel underground markets in regions with weak oversight.

Overview and Principles

Definition and Scope

Organ procurement refers to the coordinated medical, surgical, and logistical process of identifying potential donors, securing legal authorization, and surgically recovering viable organs or tissues from deceased or living individuals for transplantation into recipients with organ failure. This procedure prioritizes preserving organ function through techniques such as rapid cooling and perfusion to minimize ischemic damage post-removal. In practice, it encompasses organs like hearts, lungs, livers, kidneys, pancreases, and intestines, as well as tissues such as corneas and skin when integrated into broader donation protocols.
The scope of organ procurement extends beyond isolated surgical acts to include systemic elements like donor screening for infectious diseases and compatibility matching, often managed by specialized entities such as organ procurement organizations (OPOs) in the United States, which are federally designated non-profits responsible for donor evaluation, family communication, and organ allocation coordination. Legally, it operates under frameworks like the National Organ Transplant Act of 1984, which established the Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN) to oversee equitable distribution while prohibiting organ commerce. Deceased donation predominates, deriving from brain-dead or donation-after-circulatory-death donors, whereas living donation is limited to paired organs like kidneys or partial livers to mitigate donor risk. Globally, procurement volumes vary, with the U.S. recovering approximately 40,000 organs annually as of 2023, though shortages persist due to supply-demand imbalances.
Ethical and regulatory boundaries define the scope, excluding coerced or compensated donations in most jurisdictions to uphold voluntarism, while emphasizing brain death criteria established in the 1968 Harvard Ad Hoc Committee report for irreversibility determination. Procurement excludes non-viable or ethically contested sources, such as executed prisoners in certain countries, due to coercion concerns documented in human rights reports. Oversight involves multi-stakeholder collaboration among hospitals, transplant centers, and regulatory bodies like the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS), ensuring compliance with conditions for coverage that tie reimbursement to performance metrics on donation conversion rates.

Types of Organ Donation

Organ donation is broadly classified into two primary categories: living donation, where a healthy individual donates an organ or tissue while alive, and deceased donation, where organs are procured after the donor's legal death. Living donation typically involves renewable or regenerable organs, such as a single kidney or a lobe of the liver, and accounted for over 6,900 transplants in the United States in 2023. Deceased donation, which supplies the majority of organs for transplantation, yielded approximately 39,850 transplants from deceased donors in 2022, reflecting a 4.6% increase from the prior year.
Living donation encompasses directed donation, where the donor selects a specific recipient, often a family member or friend; non-directed (or altruistic) donation, in which the organ goes to an anonymous stranger on the waiting list; and paired or chain donation, which facilitates exchanges among incompatible donor-recipient pairs through organized matching programs. Donors in these scenarios may provide one kidney, a portion of the liver, one lung, part of the pancreas, or a segment of the intestine, with kidneys and liver segments comprising the vast majority of cases due to their regenerative capacity in the donor. Approximately 6,500 living donor transplants occur annually in the U.S., offering advantages such as shorter ischemia times for the organ compared to deceased donation, though donors face surgical risks including infection, bleeding, and long-term health impacts like reduced kidney function.
Deceased donation is subdivided into donation after brain death (DBD) and donation after circulatory death (DCD). In DBD, death is declared based on irreversible cessation of all brain functions, including the brainstem, while mechanical ventilation maintains circulation to preserve organ viability; this pathway allows for multi-organ procurement, including hearts, lungs, livers, kidneys, pancreases, and intestines. DBD remains the predominant form, as it permits controlled organ recovery under ongoing support, though only about 3 in 1,000 deaths in the U.S. are suitable for deceased donation overall due to the need for rapid intervention and compatible medical conditions. In contrast, DCD involves withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment in critically ill patients, followed by declaration of death after irreversible cessation of circulatory and respiratory functions, typically after a mandatory observation period of 2-5 minutes to confirm asystole; this method, increasingly utilized since the 1990s, primarily yields kidneys and livers, with hearts and lungs recoverable via advanced perfusion techniques in select programs. DCD organs may experience warm ischemia, potentially affecting viability, but have expanded the donor pool without violating the dead donor rule, which requires death declaration prior to procurement.

Fundamental Principles

The dead donor rule constitutes a foundational ethical and legal principle in organ procurement, mandating that vital organs be removed only after the donor has been declared dead by established medical criteria, such as brain death or circulatory death, to prevent the procurement process from causing or hastening the donor's demise. This rule, formalized in medical practice since the 1960s following the Harvard criteria for brain death in 1968, safeguards against conflicts of interest between transplant teams and end-of-life care, ensuring that death determination precedes any surgical intervention for organ recovery. Violations of this principle could expose procurement to accusations of instrumentalizing patients, as evidenced by ongoing debates over practices like donation after circulatory death (DCD), where irreversible cessation of circulation is required prior to organ retrieval.
Respect for autonomy underpins consent mechanisms in procurement, requiring explicit prior registration by the donor (opt-in systems) or surrogate decision-making aligned with the donor's wishes, while prohibiting coercion or undue influence. In the United States, for instance, the Uniform Anatomical Gift Act of 1968, revised in 2006, enforces this by prioritizing documented donor intent over family objections in cases of conflict, promoting individual agency while addressing empirical data showing family override rates exceeding 50% in some opt-in jurisdictions. Altruism further reinforces this principle, framing donation as a voluntary gift of life without financial incentives, as commercialization risks exploiting vulnerable populations and eroding public trust, with studies indicating that paid systems in countries like Iran have not eliminated black markets or long waits.
Non-maleficence and beneficence guide donor evaluation and procurement techniques, ensuring organs are procured only from medically suitable donors to maximize transplant success ratesestimated at 20-30% utilization for deceased donors in high-volume centerswhile minimizing harm through rigorous screening for infections or malignancies. Justice demands equitable procurement processes, free from discrimination based on age, race, or socioeconomic status, as disparities in donor identification have been documented, with lower rates among minorities despite comparable eligibility. These principles collectively prioritize empirical outcomes, such as the 2023 global procurement of over 150,000 organs yielding survival benefits averaging 10-15 years post-transplant for kidneys, over ideological or politically motivated frameworks.

Historical Development

Early History and Milestones

The origins of organ procurement trace to early experiments in transplantation, with rudimentary attempts at removing and grafting tissues documented as far back as ancient Egypt around 1550 BCE, though these involved skin rather than solid organs. Modern procurement techniques emerged in the 20th century alongside surgical advancements, initially focusing on living donors to avoid rejection issues inherent in cadaveric sources. Prior to the 1950s, sporadic human kidney transplants from deceased donors, such as the first attempted allograft in 1933 by Yurii Voronoy, failed rapidly due to immunological incompatibility and inadequate preservation, limiting procurement to experimental contexts without standardized methods.
A pivotal milestone occurred on December 23, 1954, when surgeons at Peter Bent Brigham Hospital in Boston, led by Joseph E. Murray, successfully procured and transplanted a kidney from one identical twin donor to his sibling recipient, marking the first viable human solid organ transplant and demonstrating feasible surgical excision and vascular anastomosis under immunosuppressive support. This living donor procedure relied on immediate procurement and transplantation without cold storage, as preservation technologies were absent, and the recipient survived eight years post-procedure. Procurement from deceased donors gained traction in 1962 with the first successful kidney transplant from a non-heart-beating cadaver at Brigham Hospital, performed by Murray and David Hume, which functioned adequately despite early logistical challenges like warm ischemia.
Further advancements in the late 1960s revolutionized procurement by addressing donor viability. In 1959, Belgian physician Guy Alexandre coined "coma dpass" to describe irreversible brain failure, enabling the June 1963 procurement of a kidney from a brain-dead donor at Hpital Saint-Pierre in Brussels, the first such successful case. This was followed by Christiaan Barnard's 1967 procurement and heart transplant from a deceased donor in South Africa, where the organ was excised post-circulatory arrest. The 1968 Harvard criteria formalized brain death diagnosis, allowing procurement from ventilated, heart-beating cadavers and improving organ quality through reduced ischemia; that year also saw the U.S. Uniform Anatomical Gift Act standardize consent for posthumous donation, facilitating ethical procurement frameworks. These developments shifted procurement from ad hoc living extractions to systematic deceased donor recovery, though early yields were low due to imperfect immunosuppression and transport limitations.

Expansion in the Late 20th Century

The introduction of cyclosporine as an immunosuppressant in the late 1970s revolutionized organ transplantation by markedly reducing acute rejection rates and improving long-term graft survival, thereby expanding the viability of procured organs. Initial clinical successes with cyclosporine in kidney transplants were reported in 1978-1979, with its broader adoption in heart and liver procedures by the early 1980s following FDA approval in 1983. This pharmacological advance, combined with refined surgical techniques and organ preservation methods, shifted transplantation from experimental to routine practice, increasing procurement demand and success.
Legal standardization further propelled procurement growth. The Uniform Determination of Death Act (UDDA), adopted in 1981, established irreversible cessation of all brain functionsincluding the brainstemas a legal criterion for death, providing uniformity across U.S. jurisdictions and enabling ethical expansion of deceased-donor organ retrieval. The National Organ Transplant Act (NOTA) of 1984 addressed procurement inefficiencies by mandating the creation of the Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN), a national registry for matching donors and recipients, while formalizing standards for organ procurement organizations (OPOs) and prohibiting organ sales. UNOS assumed OPTN operations under federal contract in 1986, implementing computerized matching systems that enhanced allocation efficiency from regional to national scales.
These developments drove substantial increases in procurement and transplant volumes. In the U.S., annual solid organ transplants grew from approximately 7,000 kidneys in 1980 to over 13,500 cadaveric procedures by 1993, with cumulative totals surpassing 150,000 since 1980. Innovations in procurement techniques, such as the first split-liver transplant in 1988 and living-donor liver procedures in 1989, diversified donor sources and mitigated organ shortages. Internationally, similar expansions occurred, with networks like Eurotransplant scaling operations in Europe during the 1980s to coordinate cross-border procurement. By the 1990s, these systemic changes had transformed organ procurement into a coordinated, high-volume enterprise, though waitlists continued to outpace supply due to rising end-stage disease prevalence.

21st-Century Advancements

In the early 2000s, organ procurement benefited from refined protocols for donation after circulatory death (DCD), which expanded the donor pool by allowing recovery from donors declared dead based on irreversible cessation of circulation rather than brain death, with initial U.S. implementations around 2000 leading to increased kidney and liver recoveries. By the mid-2000s, hypothermic machine perfusion (HMP) saw renewed clinical adoption for kidney preservation, extending viable storage beyond static cold storage limits of 24-36 hours and reducing delayed graft function in marginal donors, as evidenced by randomized trials showing 20-30% improvements in outcomes for extended-criteria kidneys. These techniques addressed procurement challenges from brain-dead donors by mitigating ischemic injury during transport, with HMP devices becoming standard in Europe and North America by 2010.
Normothermic machine perfusion (NMP), introduced clinically in the 2010s, marked a paradigm shift by maintaining organs at body temperature with oxygenated blood, enabling real-time viability assessment and repairsuch as defatting steatotic liversprior to transplantation, with pilot studies from 2014 demonstrating up to 50% utilization rates for previously discarded livers. NMP's procurement integration allowed for dynamic evaluation during ex vivo phases, reducing discard rates by 15-25% in high-risk donors, as validated in multicenter trials across Europe and the U.S. Concurrently, aggressive donor management goals, including hemodynamic optimization and hormonal resuscitation, boosted multi-organ recovery yields from 2.5 to over 3 organs per donor in intensive care settings by the late 2010s.
A pivotal 21st-century development was normothermic regional perfusion (NRP) for DCD donors, first implemented in Spain in 2012 for abdominal organs, which restarts regional circulation post-death declaration using extracorporeal membrane oxygenation to minimize warm ischemia, yielding graft function comparable to brain-dead donors and increasing heart procurement feasibility. By 2020, thoraco-abdominal NRP extended this to lungs and hearts, with U.S. programs reporting 10-20% higher DCD organ utilization rates, though ethical debates persist over the 5-minute circulatory arrest observation period. These methods contributed to global procurement growth, with deceased donor transplants rising from approximately 70,000 in 2000 to over 145,000 by 2023, driven by technological rather than solely demographic factors.
Recent procurement records underscore these advancements: in 2023, U.S. organ procurement organizations recovered 41,354 organsa 4.6% increase from 2022facilitated by NRP and perfusion technologies amid stable donor demographics. Internationally, machine perfusion's scalability has enabled procurement from extended-criteria donors, including those over age 70, with discard rates dropping 10-15% in perfused cohorts versus cold storage. Despite these gains, procurement remains constrained by logistical bottlenecks, prompting ongoing innovations like portable NMP units for remote recovery.

Procurement Methods and Techniques

Donor Identification and Consent Processes

Potential deceased organ donors are primarily identified in hospital intensive care units (ICUs) among patients with severe neurological injuries, such as traumatic brain injury, stroke, or cardiac arrest, who meet criteria for brain death or donation after circulatory death (DCD). Hospital staff screen for imminent or declared death and refer cases to organ procurement organizations (OPOs) via automated electronic systems or manual notification, as mandated by regulations like the U.S. Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) conditions for organ donation. OPOs then conduct initial evaluations, including medical history review, physical examination, serological testing for infectious diseases (e.g., HIV, hepatitis B and C), assessment of organ function through labs and imaging, and checks for hemodilution to ensure sample validity. This screening excludes donors with active malignancies, uncontrolled infections, or high-risk social behaviors that could transmit diseases, prioritizing recipient safety.
Consent for deceased donation in the United States operates under expressed consent models governed by the Uniform Anatomical Gift Act (UAGA), where individuals provide first-person authorization via state donor registries, driver's licenses, or advance directives, making such decisions legally binding as irrevocable gifts. OPOs verify registry status electronically; if documented consent exists, procurement can proceed without surrogate override, though many OPOs seek family concurrence in practice to mitigate emotional distress or potential legal challenges, despite no legal requirement. In cases without prior registration (affecting about 25% of potential donors), families serve as surrogates using substituted judgment to align with the deceased's presumed wishes, often after counseling by OPO staff on the donation's benefits and risks. Refusals occur in approximately 40-60% of family discussions, attributed to grief, mistrust, or misconceptions about donation impacting funeral arrangements or medical care quality.
Living donors are identified through transplant center referrals, typically from relatives, friends, or altruistic volunteers compatible with a specific recipient, initiating a rigorous multidisciplinary evaluation to confirm eligibility. The process includes blood and tissue typing for compatibility, comprehensive medical assessments (e.g., cardiac stress tests, CT angiography for vascular anatomy), laboratory tests for organ function and infections, and psychosocial evaluations assessing motivation, support systems, and mental health risks. Candidates must be over 18, in good health without comorbidities like diabetes or hypertension, and free from transmissible conditions.
Informed consent for living donation requires a two-step process: initial agreement to evaluation risks, followed by surgical consent after suitability confirmation, emphasizing voluntariness, full disclosure of short-term complications (e.g., 0.03% mortality for kidney donation) and long-term risks like reduced renal function. An independent living donor advocate reviews the process to ensure coercion-free decisions, with documentation of alternatives like deceased donation and psychological impacts. This safeguards autonomy, as living donation involves irreversible procedures without direct medical benefit to the donor.

Surgical Procurement Procedures

Surgical procurement of organs from deceased donors typically occurs in brain-dead individuals under controlled conditions to minimize ischemia time and preserve organ viability. The procedure is performed by specialized surgical teams coordinated by organ procurement organizations, often involving simultaneous recovery of multiple organs such as the heart, lungs, liver, pancreas, intestines, and kidneys. Full systemic heparinization (300 IU/kg in adults) is administered prior to aortic cannulation to prevent clotting, followed by cold perfusion with preservation solutions like University of Wisconsin (UW), histidine-tryptophan-ketoglutarate (HTK), or Perfadex at 04C, alongside topical cooling with ice slush.
The standard incision for multi-organ procurement is a midline sternotomy and laparotomy extending from the suprasternal notch to the pubic symphysis, allowing access to both thoracic and abdominal cavities. Initial dissection mobilizes the aorta, inferior vena cava (IVC), and portal vein while avoiding injury to vascular structures; the supra-celiac and supra-iliac aorta are clamped, and cannulas are placed in the distal abdominal aorta for systemic perfusion and the inferior mesenteric or portal vein for hepatic/gut reperfusion. Thoracic organs are procured first: the pericardium is opened, the heart arrested with cardioplegic solution via ascending aorta cannulation, great vessels transected sequentially (aorta, pulmonary artery, venae cavae, pulmonary veins), and the organ removed with atrial cuffs intact; lungs follow with pulmonary artery cannulation, bronchial stapling after inflation, and retrograde perfusion if needed to clear blood.
Abdominal procurement proceeds en bloc after thoracic removal: the liver, pancreas, and duodenum are dissected with preservation of the celiac axis and superior mesenteric artery origins, followed by transection of the distal pancreas, portal vein above the duodenum, and infrahepatic IVC; the small bowel is divided proximally and distally with mesenteric vascular cuffs secured via staplers, while kidneys are removed last en bloc with aortic and IVC patches to maintain polar arteries and ureters. Bench surgery on a back table involves further dissection, vascular reconstruction (e.g., splitting the aorta for bilateral kidneys), and packaging in sterile bags with cold solution and ice for transport, ensuring core temperatures drop below 10C within minutes of perfusion. Techniques emphasize gentle traction to avoid parenchymal injury, ligation of variant vessels, and limited dissection during warm phases to reduce procurement time, typically 12 hours for abdominal organs.
In donation after circulatory death (DCD), procedures differ primarily in timing and organ selection, as recovery begins only after cardiac arrest and a mandatory stand-off period (usually 5 minutes) to confirm irreversible cessation of circulation, limiting procurement to abdominal organs like kidneys and liver due to warm ischemic injury risks exceeding 3060 minutes. Surgical steps mirror brain-dead donation but are expedited without systemic perfusion until after asystole, often using regional perfusion techniques post-mortem to mitigate ischemia, though heart procurement remains rare and experimental as of 2024.

Organ Preservation and Transportation

Organ preservation following procurement aims to minimize ischemic damage and maintain cellular viability until transplantation, primarily through cooling to reduce metabolic demand and using specialized solutions to prevent swelling and oxidative stress. Static cold storage (SCS), the conventional method since the 1960s, involves flushing the organ with a cold preservation solution such as University of Wisconsin (UW) solution or histidine-tryptophan-ketoglutarate (HTK), then packing it in ice at 0-4C to induce hypometabolism. This approach limits cold ischemia time (CIT)the duration from procurement to reperfusionto organ-specific thresholds, as prolonged CIT correlates with increased reperfusion injury, delayed graft function (DGF), and reduced long-term survival; for kidneys, viable CIT extends to 24-36 hours, while hearts tolerate only 4-6 hours and livers typically under 12 hours. However, SCS provides no active metabolic support or viability assessment, leading to higher discard rates for marginal donors, with studies showing CIT beyond 18 hours doubling primary non-function risk in livers.
Machine perfusion techniques have emerged as superior alternatives to SCS, particularly for extended criteria donors, by continuously perfusing the organ with oxygenated solutions to remove metabolic waste, deliver nutrients, and enable real-time assessment. Hypothermic machine perfusion (HMP), operating at 4C, reduces DGF rates by 50% compared to SCS in deceased donor kidney transplants, as evidenced by randomized controlled trials and meta-analyses, with benefits persisting in donation after circulatory death (DCD) cases. Normothermic machine perfusion (NMP), maintaining physiological 37C conditions with blood-based perfusate, further improves outcomes by mimicking in vivo states, allowing functional testing; a 2023 trial in DCD kidneys demonstrated lower DGF (6% vs. 24% with SCS) and better 1-year graft survival. For livers, hypothermic oxygenated perfusion (HOPE) significantly cuts biliary complications and early allograft dysfunction versus SCS, with hazard ratios for graft loss reduced by 0.6 in extended CIT scenarios. These methods extend preservation windowsup to 24 hours for high-risk liversand decrease discard rates by 20-30%, though adoption varies due to equipment costs and logistical demands.
Transportation logistics prioritize minimizing CIT through coordinated cold chain maintenance, with organs packaged in sterile, insulated containers surrounded by melting ice slurry or gel packs to sustain 0-8C temperatures, monitored via data loggers compliant with standards like those from the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS). In the U.S., organ procurement organizations (OPOs) arrange rapid ground or air transportoften via chartered flights for cross-country matchesreducing average CIT for hearts to under 3 hours in optimized systems, as delays beyond 4 hours elevate mortality risk by 20% in recipients. Multiple organs from a single donor may be transported together in shared aircraft to synchronize implantation, further curtailing cumulative ischemia, though aviation regulations and weather can extend times, contributing to 10-15% of discards. Emerging integrations like portable NMP devices enable en-route perfusion, preserving function during transit and potentially expanding donor pools by allowing longer distances without viability loss.

Legal and Regulatory Frameworks

International Guidelines and Standards

The World Health Organization (WHO) established the Guiding Principles on Human Cell, Tissue and Organ Transplantation in 1991, with a revised version endorsed by World Health Assembly Resolution WHA63.22 on May 21, 2010. These principles outline an ethical framework for procurement, emphasizing voluntary consent or authorization for donation from deceased donors, absence of any monetary payment or inducement, and equitable access without discrimination. Principle 1 specifies that cells, tissues, and organs may only be procured from deceased individuals with prior expressed consent or family authorization in the absence of such consent, while Principle 3 mandates that procurement procedures prioritize donor safety and organ viability without compromising care for the dying patient. Principle 8 requires all involved health facilities and professionals to adhere to these standards, with transparency in procurement processes to ensure public trust and prevent exploitation.
The Declaration of Istanbul on Organ Trafficking and Transplant Tourism, issued on August 30, 2008, by The Transplantation Society and the International Society of Nephrology, provides complementary international guidance specifically targeting unethical procurement practices. It declares organ trafficking and transplant tourism violations of human rights and medical ethics, prohibiting the purchase or sale of organs and mandating that procurement occur only within national self-sufficiency frameworks to avoid cross-border exploitation. An updated version in 2018 reinforced calls for criminalization of organ trafficking, promotion of deceased donation over living donation where possible, and rigorous oversight of procurement to ensure no coercion of vulnerable populations, such as the economically disadvantaged. The Declaration emphasizes that procurement systems must be transparent, with independent regulatory bodies verifying donor consent and preventing commercialization, influencing over 100 countries' policies by 2023 through endorsements and national adaptations.
These guidelines lack binding legal force but serve as normative standards adopted variably by member states; for instance, the WHO principles have informed legislation in regions like the European Union via the 2004 Directive 2004/23/EC on tissue and cell quality, which indirectly affects organ procurement safety protocols. The Transplantation Society's Madrid Resolution of 2010 further aligns with WHO by stressing national responsibility for increasing procurement rates through ethical deceased donor programs, guided by evidence-based allocation to maximize utility and justice. Compliance challenges persist due to varying enforcement, with reports indicating persistent organ trafficking in some low-regulation areas despite these standards, underscoring the need for enhanced global monitoring.

Consent Models: Opt-In vs. Opt-Out

Opt-in systems require individuals to explicitly register their consent to donate organs upon death, typically through driver's licenses, national registries, or other formal mechanisms, ensuring that donation occurs only with affirmative prior agreement. In contrast, opt-out systems, also known as presumed consent, default to organ donation unless individuals actively register an objection during their lifetime, shifting the burden from affirmative action to deliberate refusal. These models differ fundamentally in their approach to autonomy: opt-in prioritizes expressed individual choice, while opt-out presumes willingness based on the absence of dissent, often justified by claims of inertia in decision-making.
As of 2024, opt-in remains predominant in countries like the United States, Germany, and Canada, where deceased donor rates per million population (pmp) vary but average around 20-40 pmp, influenced by registration efforts and public campaigns. Opt-out systems operate in nations such as Spain (47.5 pmp in 2023), Austria, and the United Kingdom (switched in England and Wales in 2020), with some exhibiting higher rates, though Spain's success is attributed more to robust transplant infrastructure and family engagement protocols than the consent model alone. Empirical comparisons across OECD countries show opt-out jurisdictions averaging 18% higher donation rates historically, but longitudinal analyses of policy switches, including in Wales (2015) and Scotland (2021), reveal no statistically significant increases post-implementation when controlling for confounders like healthcare resources and brain death identification practices.
Evidence on effectiveness remains contested, with early cross-sectional studies suggesting presumed consent boosts supply by leveraging default biases, estimating 21-26% higher rates in adopting regions. However, recent rigorous evaluations, including a 2024 study of five European countries switching to opt-out (Austria 1997, Singapore 2004, UK regions 2015-2021, Greece 2018), found no sustained rise in actual donations, attributing apparent gains to parallel improvements in procurement systems rather than consent defaults. Cases like Brazil and France post-opt-out adoption even recorded declines, linked to inadequate public education and persistent family vetoes, which override presumed consent in 10-20% of potential cases globally.
Critics of opt-out argue it undermines true consent by exploiting status quo bias without evidence of majority support, as surveys in opt-in nations show 60-80% willingness to donate yet low registration due to hassle, not opposition. Proponents cite behavioral economics, positing defaults shape outcomes, but meta-analyses indicate policy isolation yields minimal impact without complementary measures like mandatory discussions or incentives. In practice, family consent often functions as a de facto veto in both models, with refusal rates exceeding 15 pmp in opt-in systems and persisting at similar levels in opt-out despite legal presumptions, underscoring that relational dynamics and trust in medical systems drive procurement more than legal defaults.

National Regulations and Oversight Bodies

In the United States, the National Organ Transplant Act of 1984 (NOTA) prohibits the sale of organs and establishes the Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN) as the national regulatory framework for equitable organ allocation and procurement policies. The OPTN, operated under contract by the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS), a private nonprofit, develops and enforces standards for the 56 designated Organ Procurement Organizations (OPOs), which are responsible for donor identification, evaluation, and organ recovery across designated service areas. Oversight is provided by the Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) within the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS), which monitors OPO performance through metrics like donation rates and transplant outcomes, with recent 2025 reforms mandating root cause analyses for procurement errors and enhanced safety protocols following investigations into premature donor declarations.
In the United Kingdom, the Human Tissue Act 2004 governs organ procurement, emphasizing consent and ethical standards, with the Human Tissue Authority (HTA), established in 2005, serving as the independent regulator for licensing and inspecting procurement activities to ensure compliance with quality and safety requirements. NHS Blood and Transplant (NHSBT) acts as the operational oversight body, coordinating deceased donor identification, family consent processes, and organ retrieval through specialist nurse teams, under a soft opt-out system implemented in England on May 20, 2020, which presumes consent unless individuals have registered objections or families decline. Devolved administrations in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland maintain similar but distinct frameworks, with Scotland adopting opt-out in 2021 and Wales in 2015.
European nations implement procurement regulations in alignment with EU Directive 2004/23/EC, which mandates standards for donation authorization, procurement practices, and traceability to prevent disease transmission, though enforcement occurs at the national level. Oversight bodies include the Agence de la Biomdecine in France, which authorizes transplant centers and monitors waiting lists under the 1994 bioethics laws, and the Organizacin Nacional de Trasplantes (ONT) in Spain, which coordinates procurement via regional teams and achieves the world's highest deceased donation rates through proactive hospital protocols. In Germany, the Bundesrztekammer provides ethical guidelines, while the Deutsche Stiftung Organtransplantation handles procurement under the 1997 Transplantation Act's opt-in model with family veto rights.
In China, the Regulations on Human Organ Transplantation, promulgated in 2007 by the State Council, require voluntary donation and prohibit commercial trading, with oversight nominally provided by the National Health Commission and the China Organ Transplant Response System (COTRS) for allocation and verification of donor sources. A 2024 regulation update aims to standardize procedures and enhance traceability, but independent assessments, including U.S. congressional hearings, have raised persistent concerns over inadequate transparency, unverifiable wait times, and evidence suggesting non-consensual procurement from prisoners, undermining claims of full reliance on voluntary donors since 2015. International bodies like The Transplantation Society have advocated for verifiable oversight to align practices with global ethical norms.

Ethical Considerations

The Dead Donor Rule and Determination of Death

The dead donor rule (DDR) stipulates that vital organs may only be procured from deceased individuals and that the act of procurement must not cause the donor's death. This ethical norm, formalized in phrasing by bioethicist John A. Robertson in 1987, emerged to safeguard against intentionally hastening death for transplantation purposes, drawing from longstanding prohibitions against homicide in medical practice. The rule underpins organ donation policies globally, ensuring that procurement follows independent certification of death to mitigate liability risks and preserve public trust in transplant systems.
Determination of death for organ procurement relies on two primary standards: neurological criteria (brain death) and circulatory-respiratory criteria. Neurological death requires irreversible cessation of all brain functions, including the brainstem, evidenced by deep coma, absence of brainstem reflexes (such as pupillary light response and gag reflex), and confirmed apnea after disconnection from ventilatory support. In the United States, the Uniform Determination of Death Act (UDDA) of 1981 equates brain death with cardiopulmonary death, mandating prerequisites like known catastrophic brain injury and exclusion of confounding factors such as hypothermia or drug intoxication before testing. Circulatory death, conversely, is declared after irreversible cessation of heartbeat and breathing, typically following a waiting period of 2 to 5 minutes of asystole to preclude auto-resuscitation, as in controlled donation after circulatory death (DCD) protocols.
Controversies arise in applying these criteria to procurement, particularly where timing and interventions blur the line between life-sustaining care and organ recovery. In DCD, ethical concerns center on premature death declaration to facilitate viable organ retrieval, potential conflicts of interest among care teams, and the risk of undetected circulation resumption, prompting calls to uphold the DDR strictly by prohibiting interventions that could hasten demise. Advanced techniques like thoraco-abdominal normothermic regional perfusion (ta-NRP), which restore circulation post-declaration to improve organ viability, have intensified debates; critics argue this restarts bodily functions, potentially violating the irreversibility of death and the DDR by simulating life after formal pronouncement. Proponents counter that such methods align with updated understandings of death if brain function remains absent, yet opposition from bodies like the American Medical Association highlights risks to donor integrity and systemic trust. Empirical reviews indicate rare but documented cases of incomplete brain death assessments leading to procurement from potentially viable donors, underscoring needs for rigorous, multi-physician confirmation to prevent errors.

Autonomy, Consent, and Family Involvement

Autonomy in organ procurement centers on respecting the deceased donor's prior expressed wishes regarding donation, often formalized through first-person authorization mechanisms such as donor registries or legal documents. This principle holds that an individual's competent decision to donate, made while alive, should supersede posthumous objections to preserve self-determination, as overriding such consent risks eroding trust in the procurement system and disincentivizing registration. Ethicists argue that prioritizing the deceased's autonomy aligns with deontological imperatives against treating persons as means to ends, even for societal benefits like alleviating organ shortages.
In practice, however, family involvement frequently complicates this autonomy, with procurement teams often seeking familial approval even when prior consent exists, due to concerns over emotional distress or legal repercussions. In the United States, where opt-in consent prevails under the Uniform Anatomical Gift Act, families can veto donation in approximately 10-20% of cases despite donor designation, as evidenced by studies showing refusal rates of 11.6% in interviewed families from 2008-2017. Similarly, in the United Kingdom, family consent rates dropped to 61% in the year ending April 2024, reflecting persistent deference to next-of-kin despite high public support (around 90%) for donation. This "family veto" phenomenon persists across opt-in and opt-out systems, as even in presumed consent jurisdictions like Spain, procurement may halt if families object strongly, underscoring a gap between legal frameworks and bedside ethics.
Critics of family overrides contend they undermine autonomy by substituting surrogate judgments that may reflect cultural, religious, or emotional factors rather than the donor's intent, with data indicating discrepancies in up to 50% of cases between registered donors and family preferences. Proponents of relational autonomy, particularly in collectivist cultures, counter that decisions involve interdependent family units, as seen in studies of Asian American donors where familial consultation shapes outcomes without fully negating individual agency. Nonetheless, empirical evidence links strict adherence to first-person consent with higher donation realization rates, as in systems minimizing vetoes, suggesting that bolstering legal enforcement of donor wishes could enhance both autonomy and procurement efficacy without coercion. Reforms advocating non-discretionary honoring of authorizations, as proposed in recent ethical analyses, aim to reconcile these tensions by mandating documentation verification prior to family discussions.

Incentives, Compensation, and Market Approaches

In various jurisdictions, non-monetary incentives aim to encourage organ donation without direct financial exchange, such as priority access to transplant waiting lists for registered donors or their families. For instance, Israel's 2008 Organ Transplant Law grants allocation priority to individuals who have signed donor cards or donated organs previously, resulting in increased donation rates from 44 per million population in 2007 to 111 by 2019. Similarly, some U.S. states provide tax deductions for funeral expenses related to deceased donors or mileage reimbursements for living donors, as permitted under amendments to the National Organ Transplant Act of 1984, which distinguish such measures from prohibited "valuable consideration." These approaches seek to mitigate disincentives like financial burdens on families, with studies indicating that covering costs such as lost wages or travel can boost living donation without raising coercion concerns.
Proposals for direct financial compensation to donors or families remain highly contentious, primarily due to fears of exploiting vulnerable populations and commodifying human body parts. In the United States, the 1984 Act explicitly bans payments for organs to prevent black markets, though recent legislative efforts like the 2024 End Kidney Deaths Act propose a $50,000 tax credit for living kidney donors to address the persistent shortage of over 100,000 patients on waiting lists as of 2023. Ethicists favoring compensation argue it could expand supply through market signals, akin to how paid plasma donation has sustained U.S. supplies without evident long-term harm to donors, potentially saving lives given that 17 patients die daily awaiting transplants. Critics counter that such systems risk undue inducement, particularly among the economically disadvantaged, potentially leading to coerced decisions and poorer post-donation health outcomes, as evidenced by surveys showing many potential donors prioritize altruism over payment.
Regulated organ markets represent the most explicit market-oriented approach, with Iran's system since the 1980s serving as the sole national example of legalized compensated kidney sales. Under this model, living donors receive government-regulated payments of approximately $1,200 to $4,500, supplemented by health insurance, which has eliminated Iran's kidney transplant waiting list by facilitating over 1,400 sales annually and performing around 2,500 transplants yearly. Empirical analyses, however, reveal mixed outcomes: while access improves for recipients, donorsoften from low-income groupsreport lower long-term quality of life, higher regret rates (up to 10-20% in some cohorts), and inadequate protections against broker exploitation, challenging claims of ethical viability despite the system's intent to curb illegal trafficking. Broader ethical opposition invokes commodification, asserting that treating organs as commodities erodes altruistic norms foundational to medicine, potentially exacerbating inequalities as wealthier buyers dominate access.

Global Practices and Variations

Practices in the United States

Organ procurement in the United States primarily occurs through a network of 55 federally designated, nonprofit organ procurement organizations (OPOs), each responsible for coordinating the identification, evaluation, and recovery of organs from deceased donors within specific geographic service areas covering the entire country. These OPOs collaborate with hospitals to screen potential donors, typically individuals declared brain dead or meeting criteria for donation after circulatory death (DCD), and facilitate the surgical recovery process in operating rooms to minimize ischemia time. The system operates under an opt-in consent model, where individuals can register as donors via state registries or driver's licenses, though authorized OPO representatives routinely seek family authorization even for registered donors to honor potential objections.
The deceased donor procurement process begins when hospital staff identify a potential donor, prompting an immediate referral to the local OPO, which assesses medical eligibility through tests confirming death and organ viability, excluding cases with active infections, malignancies, or hemodynamic instability. If consent is obtained, the OPO coordinates multidisciplinary recovery teamsoften including surgeons from multiple transplant centersto surgically excise organs such as hearts, lungs, livers, kidneys, and pancreases, followed by rapid preservation via cold perfusion with solutions like University of Wisconsin or histidine-tryptophan-ketoglutaramate to extend viability for transport. Organs are then entered into the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS) matching system, operated under contract with the Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN), for allocation based on medical urgency, tissue compatibility, and geographic proximity to optimize outcomes. Living donation, comprising about 14% of procurements, involves directed or non-directed donors undergoing laparoscopic nephrectomy or hepatectomy in transplant centers, with OPOs playing a supportive role in evaluation and pairing for kidney chains.
In 2024, U.S. OPOs facilitated a record 48,149 organ transplants from 16,988 deceased donors and over 7,000 living donors, yet more than 103,000 patients remained on the national waiting list, underscoring persistent supply constraints despite innovations like ex vivo perfusion. Procurement efficiency varies by OPO, with performance metrics tied to donor conversion ratestypically around 20-30% of eligible deathsand federal oversight via the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services evaluating outcomes like organs transplanted per donor to address underperformance in low-yield regions.

European Models

European organ procurement systems vary by country but increasingly favor opt-out (presumed consent) models, with 19 of 27 EU member states adopting this approach as of 2023, often correlating with higher deceased donor rates per million population (pmp) compared to opt-in systems. These models presume consent for donation from deceased individuals unless explicitly refused, though family veto power remains common in practice, particularly in "soft" opt-out variants. Organizational infrastructure, such as dedicated transplant coordinators and hospital protocols for early donor identification, plays a critical role in outcomes beyond consent defaults alone, as evidenced by Spain's sustained leadership.
Spain's model exemplifies effective procurement through presumed consent since 1979, managed by the National Transplant Organization (ONT), which trains hospital-based coordinators to identify potential donors promptly after brain death certification. This system achieved 49.38 deceased donors pmp in 2023, the highest globally, yielding over 112 transplants pmp including living donors. Success factors include mandatory donor screening in intensive care units, expanded criteria for marginal donors, and public trust fostered by transparent allocation and low refusal rates (around 15% family override), rather than consent legislation alone. Peer-reviewed analyses attribute gains to systemic procurement efficiency, not merely opt-out policy, with family discussions and cultural acceptance enhancing yields.
In contrast, the United Kingdom implemented soft opt-out consent across nations starting with Wales in 2015 and England in May 2020, allowing family objection to override presumption. Initial post-implementation data showed no significant rise in donation rates, with England at approximately 20 donors pmp in 2023, suggesting organizational challenges like specialist nurse shortages and pandemic disruptions outweighed default changes. 
France adopted presumed consent in 2017 via the Bioethics Law, requiring individuals to register refusal explicitly, coordinated by the Agence de la Biomdecine. This yielded around 28 donors pmp in 2023, bolstered by national registries and hospital mandates, though family consent is routinely sought and often decisive.
Germany retains an opt-in model, requiring explicit prior consent, resulting in lower rates of about 12 donors pmp as of 2023, hampered by public awareness gaps and ethical debates over autonomy that led to rejection of opt-out proposals in 2020. Longitudinal studies across Europe indicate opt-out systems elevate rates on average but require robust infrastructure to avoid reliance on family overrides, with no universal causal proof of defaults alone driving increases.

Asian and Other Regional Approaches

In China, organ procurement operates under a voluntary, opt-in system established in 2015, with the China Organ Transplant Response System (COTRS) managing allocation since then; however, the country reported 6,454 deceased donor organs in 2023, yielding a donation rate of 3.86 per million population in 2022, ranking 48th globally. Official regulations emphasize unpaid donations and prohibit procurement from executed prisoners, yet independent investigations and congressional testimonies have raised persistent concerns about coerced harvesting from detainees, including Falun Gong practitioners and Uyghurs, with estimates of 25,000 to 50,000 such procedures annually as recently as 2025. These allegations, documented in reports from transplant ethics bodies and tribunals, highlight discrepancies between stated volumesprojected at 50,000 transplants by 2023and verifiable voluntary sources, underscoring needs for greater transparency as urged by organizations like The Transplantation Society.
Japan maintains a strict opt-in model under the 1997 Organ Transplant Law, amended in 2010 to facilitate brain-dead donations while requiring explicit donor registration and family veto override only in limited cases; despite these reforms, deceased donation rates remain among the world's lowest at approximately 0.61 per million for donation after circulatory death in recent data, with just 4% of transplant waitlist patients receiving organs annually as of 2025. Cultural emphasis on bodily integrity and historical delays in accepting brain deathlegally defined only in 1997contribute to this stagnation, resulting in reliance on living donors for over 90% of kidney and liver transplants.
In India, the Transplantation of Human Organs and Tissues Act (THOTA) of 1994, amended in 2011, enforces an opt-in system with national and state registries, yet deceased donation rates have risen modestly from 0.27 to 0.81 per million population between 2013 and 2024, hampered by inadequate infrastructure, procedural delays, and shortages of trained personnel. Family consent remains pivotal, often overridden by misconceptions and logistical barriers in public hospitals, while illicit trade persists due to unmet demand, prompting calls for stricter enforcement and expanded non-profit networks like NOTTO to curb inequities.
South Korea adheres to an opt-in framework requiring family consensus for deceased donation, with no adoption of presumed consent despite discussions; brain-dead donations increased post-2000 reforms, but rates lag due to familial priorities and public hesitancy, leading to heavy dependence on living donors. Taiwan, pioneering Asia's first organ transplantation act in 1987, uses an opt-in model with a centralized registry, achieving higher deceased donation integration through family education, though overall Asian trends favor living donations at 50-61% for liver and kidney transplants.
Beyond Asia, Iran's regulated compensated living kidney donation system, operational since 1988, eliminates waiting lists by facilitating paymentstypically several thousand dollars via government-charity vouchersto unrelated donors, with all procedures in accredited centers and dialysis costs state-funded. This model, unique in permitting incentives under Islamic fatwas, has performed over 35,000 transplants, though critics note risks of coercion among low-income donors and ethical debates over commodification, with recent shifts emphasizing related donors to sustain equity. In Central Asian nations like Kazakhstan, procurement relies on family consent without presumed models, yielding low volumes amid resource constraints, while broader regional variations underscore cultural and infrastructural influences on global disparities.

Challenges and Controversies

Organ Shortages and Allocation Inequities

In the United States, as of May 2025, over 103,000 individuals await organ transplants on the national waiting list managed by the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS), with kidneys comprising the majority at approximately 85% of candidates. Despite a record 48,149 transplants performed in 2024a 3.3% increase from 2023the supply remains critically insufficient, as only about 3 in 1,000 deaths occur under conditions suitable for deceased organ donation, such as brain death in a hospital setting with mechanical ventilation. Globally, the disparity is even starker, with only 10% of transplant demand met annually, according to data from the Global Observatory on Donation and Transplantation, driven by low deceased donation rates in most countries outside high-performing systems like Spain's.
The persistent shortage stems primarily from a mismatch between demandfueled by rising end-stage organ failure from chronic diseases like diabetes and hypertensionand limited supply, as donation rates have not scaled proportionally despite public campaigns. In the U.S., factors include suboptimal procurement efficiency by organ procurement organizations (OPOs), with studies identifying failures in referral evaluation and family consent conversion, alongside a shift toward extended criteria donors (ECDs) and donation after circulatory death (DCD), which yield fewer viable organs per donor. Cultural and religious reservations about bodily integrity post-mortem further suppress registration and consent rates, particularly in minority communities where distrust of medical systems persists. Living donation, while increasing for kidneys (over 7,000 in 2024), cannot bridge the gap for solid organs like livers or hearts, leaving thousands to die annually17 per day in the U.S. alonewhile on the list.
Allocation inequities compound the shortage's impact, as UNOS policies prioritize medical urgency, tissue matching, and wait time via a points-based system, yet geographic disparities arise from the 58 Donor Service Areas (DSAs), which vary widely in population density, donation rates, and transport logistics, resulting in median wait times ranging from months to years across regions. For instance, patients in low-donation DSAs face prolonged waits and higher pre-transplant mortality, independent of clinical factors. Racial and ethnic minorities, who represent 60% of the U.S. waitlist (including 27.8% Black/African Americans), receive disproportionately fewer organs relative to need, partly because non-white donors supply only 35% of recovered organs, reflecting lower donation rates among these groups due to socioeconomic barriers and historical mistrust rather than inherent biological mismatches.
Gender-based disparities also persist, with women less likely to receive liver transplants than men after adjusting for race, geography, and comorbidities, attributed to lower average body size affecting organ suitability and potential biases in urgency scoring. Peer-reviewed analyses indicate that UNOS algorithms can disadvantage minorities and women through unaccounted social determinants, such as access to pre-transplant care that influences waitlist priority, exacerbating outcomes where Black patients experience higher burdens of conditions like heart failure yet face barriers at multiple procurement stages. These inequities highlight systemic failures in equitable distribution, where shortages amplify vulnerabilities for underserved populations, prompting calls for policy revisions like geographic redistribution incentives, though evidence on their efficacy remains mixed.

Organ Trafficking and Black Markets

Organ trafficking encompasses the illicit procurement and sale of human organs, often involving coercion, deception, or exploitation of vulnerable individuals, constituting a subset of human trafficking prohibited under frameworks like the UN Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons. Black markets thrive on persistent global organ shortages, where demand exceeds legal supply, leading to prices for kidneys ranging from $5,000 to $200,000 depending on the transaction layer, with donors typically receiving fractions of that amount. Estimates indicate that illegally sourced organs may supply up to 10% of transplants performed annually worldwide, though precise figures are elusive due to the clandestine nature of the trade.
Detection remains low, with the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) 2022 Global Report on Trafficking in Persons documenting trafficking for organ removal in only 0.2% of detected cases, totaling about 700 victims reported globally from 2008 to 2022, a figure likely underrepresenting the scale given brief victim-trafficker interactions and post-removal dispersal. Highest detection rates occur in North Africa and the Middle East, with cases involving migrants from sub-Saharan Africa tricked into "donations" under false pretenses, such as job offers leading to kidney extractions without informed consent. In South Asia, particularly India and Pakistan, impoverished individuals are frequently recruited via brokers promising financial relief, undergoing nephrectomies in unregulated clinics where follow-up care is absent, exacerbating health risks like infection and hypertension.
China represents a distinct scale of state-involvement allegations, with the independent China Tribunal concluding in 2019 that forced organ harvesting from detaineesprimarily Falun Gong practitioners persecuted since 1999, alongside Uyghurs, Tibetans, and Christianscontinues unabated, evidenced by transplant volumes reaching 60,000 to 90,000 annually (far surpassing official voluntary donation figures of under 20,000), wait times as short as days, undercover telephone verifications of availability from hospitals, and survivor testimonies of forced blood tests and ultrasounds for organ matching. United Nations human rights experts in 2021 raised alarms over credible reports of systematic extractions of hearts, kidneys, livers, and corneas from detained minorities, registered in national databases without consent or oversight, urging independent verification amid government denials and opaque data. These practices fuel transplant tourism, drawing patients from abroad to Chinese facilities offering rapid procedures.
International responses include the 2008 Declaration of Istanbul, which condemns organ trafficking and transplant tourism, alongside UNODC toolkits for investigations emphasizing victim identification and broker prosecutions. Despite such measures, challenges persist from medical professional complicity, jurisdictional gaps in transnational networks, and economic incentives in source countries, underscoring the need for enhanced transparency in transplant registries and penalties for illicit procurement.

Ethical Lapses in Procurement Processes

In the United States, a 2025 federal investigation by the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) revealed systemic failures in organ procurement organizations (OPOs), including instances where procurement processes were initiated on patients exhibiting signs of life, potentially violating the dead donor rule. The probe identified at least 28 cases where donors may not have met criteria for death at the time of organ recovery, with inadequate neurologic assessments and poor coordination between hospitals and OPOs contributing to these lapses. One prominent example occurred in Kentucky in October 2021, when a patient declared brain dead showed spontaneous movementssuch as scratching and kickingmoments before scheduled organ removal, prompting a federal inquiry into pressure exerted by a local OPO on hospital staff to proceed despite revival signs.
Further scrutiny in 2025 led to the decertification of the University of Miami's OPO after investigations uncovered unsafe procurement practices, including deficient staff training and a fatal error where a donor heart was transplanted despite known risks, resulting in recipient death. These incidents highlight conflicts of interest within OPOs, which receive funding tied to procurement volumes, incentivizing rushed evaluations over rigorous verification of death. A bipartisan Senate report documented OPOs exploiting loopholes in recertification metrics, prioritizing organ yields over ethical safeguards like comprehensive family consent verification.
Internationally, procurement in China has involved documented violations of consent, particularly from prisoners of conscience, with UN human rights experts reporting in 2021 that forced harvesting targets detained ethnic and religious minorities, often without familial notification or legal authorization. A 2017 analysis in BMC Medical Ethics detailed cases of organ extraction from executed prisoners prior to full cardiac arrest and without consent, undermining autonomy and raising causal concerns about state incentives for maintaining transplant volumes through detention systems. Such practices persist amid limited transparency, with procurement data discrepancies suggesting non-voluntary sources contribute significantly to China's transplant rates, which surged post-2000 despite low registered donor numbers.
In donation after circulatory death (DCD) protocols, ethical breaches arise from hastening terminal withdrawal to preserve organ viability, as noted in a 2013 Pediatrics review, where conflicts between end-of-life care and procurement timing have led to interventions that arguably prioritize transplant utility over patient-centered dying processes. These lapses, while less overt than non-consensual harvesting, erode trust by blurring lines between therapeutic withdrawal and organ recovery, with empirical data showing variable adherence to waiting periods post-circulatory arrest across institutions. Overall, such procurement failures underscore the need for stricter oversight to enforce verifiable death determination and uncoerced consent, as deviations have precipitated both legal challenges and public reluctance toward donation systems.

Recent Developments and Future Directions

Technological and Medical Innovations

Machine perfusion technologies represent a significant advancement over traditional static cold storage in organ procurement, actively delivering preservation solutions to organs post-recovery to mitigate ischemic damage and enable functional assessment. Hypothermic machine perfusion (HMP), which maintains organs at 410C while continuously pumping solutions through vasculature, has demonstrated reduced rates of delayed graft function in kidneys compared to cold storage alone, with randomized trials showing up to 20% improvement in utilization of marginal donors. Normothermic machine perfusion (NMP), operating at near-body temperatures (3437C) with oxygenated blood-based solutions, allows real-time metabolic monitoring and viability testing during transport, particularly benefiting high-risk livers by lowering discard rates; a 2025 analysis reported NMP adoption correlating with a decline in liver discards from extended criteria donors.
Normothermic regional perfusion (NRP) integrates extracorporeal membrane oxygenation to reinstate circulation in donors after circulatory death (DCD) prior to organ recovery, restoring hemodynamics and minimizing warm ischemia time. Implemented in procurement protocols since the early 2010s, NRP has expanded DCD organ yields, with a 2025 review of in situ technologies documenting improved outcomes for hearts, lungs, and livers, including graft survival rates exceeding 90% in select cohorts. In the United States, organ procurement organizations reported operational experience with NRP in over 1,000 cases by 2024, facilitating broader adoption despite ethical debates over its application in controlled DCD scenarios.
Portable preservation devices further enhance procurement logistics by maintaining precise temperature and pressure controls during transport. The Paragonix SherpaPak systems, designed for hearts, lungs, and abdominal organs, received European Union Medical Device Regulation approval in March 2025 for five models, incorporating digital tracking to monitor conditions en route and reduce preservation injury. These innovations collectively address procurement challenges by rehabilitating organs from older or hemodynamically unstable donors, with clinical data indicating up to 30% increases in transplantable organs from expanded criteria pools. Ongoing developments, such as additive-enriched perfusates with defatting agents for steatotic livers, continue to refine these processes, prioritizing empirical outcomes over historical limitations of cold ischemia.

Policy Reforms and Systemic Changes

In response to persistent organ shortages, several countries have implemented presumed consent (opt-out) systems for deceased donation, shifting from explicit opt-in requirements; however, a 2024 analysis of five nations (Argentina, Chile, Sweden, Uruguay, and Wales) that adopted opt-out policies found no statistically significant increase in deceased donor rates compared to pre-reform trends, attributing higher donation in countries like Spain primarily to robust transplant coordination and family engagement rather than consent models alone. Internationally, the United Nations General Assembly's Resolution 79/189 in December 2024 urged enhanced cooperation to combat trafficking in persons for organ removal, building on the Council of Europe's 2015 Convention against trafficking in human organs, which mandates mutual assistance and criminalization of illicit procurement. In the United States, the Stop Forced Organ Harvesting Act, reintroduced in the 119th Congress in 2025, authorizes sanctions and visa denials for individuals involved in coerced organ procurement, targeting practices observed in certain regimes.
Domestically, the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) finalized reforms in November 2020 to the conditions of participation for organ procurement organizations (OPOs), introducing objective performance metrics such as organ transplantation rates (requiring procured organs to be transplanted, not merely recovered) and donation rates from eligible donors, replacing self-reported data with verified figures from the Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) and Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). These changes mandate public reporting of OPO outcomes, with underperformers required to implement quality assessment and performance improvement (QAPI) programs and facing decertification if they rank in the bottom 25% over three years, fostering competition expected to replace approximately half of the 56 OPOs by 2026. In July 2025, the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS), led by Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., launched a reform initiative citing systemic ethical lapses in the transplant network, including inadequate oversight of donations after circulatory death (DCD) and prioritization of procurement volumes over patient sanctity, with plans to reinforce the Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN) through expanded living donor support via the National Living Organ Donor Assistance Center and stricter procurement protocols.
Further systemic updates include the OPTN's proposed 2025 comprehensive multi-organ allocation policy, which standardizes offer sequences across seven donor-specific tables covering 98% of deceased multi-organ cases, prioritizing medically urgent candidates (e.g., pediatrics, highly sensitized patients) while balancing access for single-organ recipients to mitigate geographic and demographic inequities. Advocacy efforts, such as those by the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS), push for automated donor referrals in hospitals, national organ tracking systems, and Medicare adjustments to incentivize utilization of marginal organs, addressing declining procurement efficiency amid a waitlist exceeding 90,000 patients. Discussions on financial incentives for donors persist but remain prohibited under the National Organ Transplant Act of 1984, with ethical analyses emphasizing risks of commodification outweighing potential supply gains without rigorous safeguards. These reforms collectively aim to enhance transparency, accountability, and ethical procurement, though empirical outcomes depend on implementation amid ongoing debates over causal drivers of donation rates.

The United States recorded approximately 38 deceased donors pmp, leading in total transplants with over 45,000 procedures annually, including a 9.5% increase in solid organ transplants worldwide to 172,397 from deceased and living sources. Spain maintained one of the highest rates at around 48 donors pmp, attributed to its presumed consent framework combined with a national coordination network established in the 1990s, which emphasizes hospital-level procurement teams and family engagement protocols.
