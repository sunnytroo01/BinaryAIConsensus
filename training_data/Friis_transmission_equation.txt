Friis transmission equation
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Friis transmission equation, developed by Danish-American electrical engineer Harald T. Friis in 1946, is a fundamental formula in antenna theory and electromagnetics that calculates the power received by one antenna from another under ideal free-space conditions. It relates the received power to the transmitted power, the gains of the transmitting and receiving antennas, the operating wavelength, and the distance separating the antennas, providing a simple model for signal propagation without obstacles, reflections, or atmospheric effects.
The equation is expressed as
Pr=PtGtGr(4d)2, P_r = P_t G_t G_r \left( \frac{\lambda}{4 \pi d} \right)^2, Pr=PtGtGr(4d)2,
where $ P_r $ is the power delivered to a matched load at the receiving antenna, $ P_t $ is the power input to the transmitting antenna, $ G_t $ and $ G_r $ are the power gains of the transmitting and receiving antennas (relative to isotropic radiators), $ \lambda $ is the wavelength of the signal, and $ d $ is the distance between the antenna phase centers. This form derives from Friis's original expression using effective antenna apertures $ A_e = \frac{G \lambda^2}{4 \pi} $, which yields $ \frac{P_r}{P_t} = \frac{A_t A_r}{\lambda^2 d^2} $ for the aperture-based version, highlighting the reciprocity between transmitting and receiving antenna performance. The derivation assumes the power density from the transmitter spreads uniformly over a spherical wavefront in the far field, scaled by antenna gains and captured by the receiver's effective area.
Key assumptions include operation in free space (vacuum or air with negligible absorption), far-field conditions where $ d \gg \frac{2D^2}{\lambda} $ (with $ D $ as the largest antenna dimension), co-polarized and aligned antennas, and a conjugate-matched receiver for maximum power transfer. Limitations arise in non-ideal environments, such as near ground planes or with multipath fading, requiring modifications like two-ray models for ground reflection or additional loss factors for atmospheric attenuation. Despite these constraints, the equation remains essential for link budget calculations in wireless systems, including satellite communications, point-to-point microwave links, and cellular networks, where it helps predict path loss and optimize antenna designs for reliable signal strength.

History and Development

Original Formulation by Harald Friis

Harald T. Friis, a Danish-American radio engineer employed at Bell Telephone Laboratories in Holmdel, New Jersey, formulated the transmission equation as part of his research on microwave propagation. His career at Bell Labs, spanning nearly four decades, focused on advancing radio systems, including pioneering work in antenna theory and noise measurement techniques.
In May 1946, Friis published the equation in the paper "A Note on a Simple Transmission Formula" in Proceedings of the IRE, volume 34, number 5, pages 254256 (manuscript received December 6, 1945). The work was motivated by the need for a straightforward formula to compute power transfer in free-space radio circuits, particularly addressing challenges in microwave propagation that emerged prominently after World War II, while building on foundational principles from radiometry. Friis emphasized the formula's utility for practical calculations across wavelengths up to several meters, assuming isotropic radiators or directive antennas in unobstructed space.
The original equation expresses the ratio of received to transmitted power as
PrPt=ArAtd22
\frac{P_r}{P_t} = \frac{A_r A_t}{d^2 \lambda^2}
PtPr=d22ArAt
where PrP_rPr is the power available at the output of the receiving antenna, PtP_tPt is the power supplied to the transmitting antenna, ArA_rAr and AtA_tAt are the effective areas of the receiving and transmitting antennas, respectively, ddd is the separation distance between the antennas, and \lambda is the wavelength. This formulation highlights the direct dependence on antenna effective areas, which capture the antennas' ability to intercept and radiate power efficiently.
Friis specified a key condition for the formula's validity: the distance ddd must satisfy d2a2/d \geq 2a^2 / \lambdad2a2/, where aaa represents the largest physical dimension of either antenna, ensuring the wavefront approximates a plane wave at the receiver. This original area-based expression laid the groundwork for subsequent reformulations, including the equivalent modern version using antenna gains.

Evolution to Contemporary Usage

Following its introduction in 1946, the Friis transmission equation gained rapid adoption in antenna theory literature during the late 1940s and 1950s, serving as a foundational tool for analyzing power transfer in radio systems. Early integrations appeared in seminal texts such as Samuel Silver's Microwave Antenna Theory and Design (1949), where it was applied to microwave propagation scenarios, emphasizing practical engineering computations for radar and communication links. By the 1950s, the equivalence between effective aperture area and antenna gainexpressed as $ G = 4\pi A / \lambda^2 $was firmly established in the field, allowing the formula to bridge aperture-based and gain-oriented descriptions; this relation was prominently featured in subsequent works, including Henry Jasik's Antenna Engineering Handbook (1961 first edition), which solidified its role in standard antenna design methodologies.
The equation's evolution continued into the 1960s with formal standardization efforts by professional bodies. The Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) incorporated the Friis formula into its antenna measurement procedures in IEEE Std 149-1965, IEEE Test Procedures for Antennas, using it as the basis for two-antenna and three-antenna gain-transfer methods while explicitly accounting for directivity (the pattern shape factor) and radiation efficiency (to distinguish lossless directivity from practical gain). This standardization promoted consistent application across engineering disciplines, integrating factors like mismatch losses and polarization alignment to enhance measurement accuracy in laboratory and field settings.
By the 1970s, practical engineering practice shifted predominantly toward gain-based notation for the Friis equation, driven by the advent of computational tools such as programmable calculators and early software for antenna simulation, which simplified gain calculations over explicit area computations. This transition facilitated broader use in system-level designs, particularly for emerging wireless technologies. A key milestone occurred in the 1980s when the formula was embedded in International Telecommunication Union (ITU) recommendations for satellite communications; for instance, the 1982 CCIR (predecessor to ITU-R) reports on fixed-satellite services utilized it in link budget analyses to predict signal strength over long distances, incorporating atmospheric effects alongside free-space loss.

Mathematical Formulation

Friis' Original Formula

The original Friis transmission formula expresses the ratio of received power to transmitted power in free space as a function of the effective areas of the transmitting and receiving antennas, the distance between them, and the wavelength.
The formula is given by
PrPt=AtArd22,
\frac{P_r}{P_t} = \frac{A_t A_r}{d^2 \lambda^2},
PtPr=d22AtAr,
where PrP_rPr is the power available from the receiving antenna, PtP_tPt is the power supplied to the transmitting antenna, AtA_tAt and ArA_rAr are the effective areas of the transmitting and receiving antennas, respectively, ddd is the distance separating the antennas, and \lambda is the wavelength.
In this formulation, the effective area AAA of an antenna represents the physical aperture through which the antenna captures (for reception) or from which it radiates (for transmission) the power flux in the incident plane wave, assuming far-field conditions where the wavefronts are approximately planar. The effective areas AtA_tAt and ArA_rAr quantify how efficiently each antenna interacts with the electromagnetic field, directly influencing the fraction of transmitted power that reaches the receiver.
This equation indicates that the power transfer efficiency scales directly with the product of the effective areas AtArA_t A_rAtAr, while decreasing inversely with the square of the separation distance d2d^2d2reflecting the spherical spreading of the wavefrontand inversely with the square of the wavelength 2\lambda^22, which accounts for the dilution of power density over larger wavefronts at longer wavelengths. For isotropic antennas, where radiation is uniform in all directions, the effective area simplifies to A=2/4A = \lambda^2 / 4\piA=2/4, leading to a path loss factor of (4d/)2(4\pi d / \lambda)^2(4d/)2. This area-based form is equivalent to gain-based expressions when applied to isotropic cases, highlighting the foundational role of aperture concepts in free-space propagation.

Modern Gain-Based Formula

The modern gain-based formulation of the Friis transmission equation provides a practical expression for the power ratio in free-space propagation, incorporating antenna gains directly. It states that the ratio of received power $ P_r $ to transmitted power $ P_t $ is given by
PrPt=GtGr(4d)2,
\frac{P_r}{P_t} = G_t G_r \left( \frac{\lambda}{4\pi d} \right)^2,
PtPr=GtGr(4d)2,
where $ G_t $ and $ G_r $ represent the power gains of the transmitting and receiving antennas relative to an isotropic radiator, $ \lambda $ is the wavelength of the signal, and $ d $ is the separation distance between the antennas. This version evolved from Friis' original 1946 work through the established relation between antenna gain and effective aperture area.
In engineering practice, the equation is often used in decibel (dB) form for ease of computation, especially in link budget analyses. The received power in dBm is calculated as
Pr=Pt+Gt+Gr+20log10(4d),
P_r^{} = P_t^{} + G_t^{} + G_r^{} + 20 \log_{10} \left( \frac{\lambda}{4\pi d} \right),
Pr=Pt+Gt+Gr+20log10(4d),
where the gains are expressed in dBi (decibels relative to isotropic). This logarithmic representation simplifies the handling of large dynamic ranges in power levels and distances, making it a staple in wireless system design tools and simulations.
The formula assumes operation in the far field, where $ d \gg \frac{2 D^2}{\lambda} $ (with $ D $ the largest linear dimension of either antenna) to ensure plane-wave approximation; perfect polarization matching between antennas; propagation through a lossless, isotropic, and homogeneous medium; and absence of multipath, scattering, or other environmental effects. These conditions limit its direct applicability to ideal line-of-sight scenarios but allow extensions for real-world adjustments.
For illustration, consider a 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi link with $ P_t = 0 $ dBm, $ G_t = G_r = 5 $ dBi antennas, and $ d = 100 $ m. Here, $ \lambda = c / f = 0.125 $ m, yielding $ 20 \log_{10} (\lambda / 4\pi d) \approx -80 $ dB and thus $ P_r \approx -70 $ dBm, indicating a typical received signal strength for short-range wireless communication.

Derivation and Theoretical Basis

Prerequisite Concepts in Antenna Theory

Antenna gain quantifies the ability of an antenna to concentrate radiated power in a particular direction compared to an isotropic radiator. It is defined as $ G(\theta, \phi) = 4\pi \frac{U(\theta, \phi)}{P_{rad}} $, where $ U(\theta, \phi) $ represents the radiation intensity in the direction specified by angles $ \theta $ and $ \phi $, and $ P_{rad} $ is the total radiated power. This measure accounts for both the directive properties and ohmic losses in the antenna system. Directivity, often considered the lossless counterpart to gain, equals gain when the antenna efficiency is unity, emphasizing the geometric concentration of power without dissipative effects.
The effective aperture, or effective area $ A_e $, describes the antenna's capacity to capture power from an incident electromagnetic wave, linking the antenna's gain to its physical interaction with the wavefront. It is given by $ A_e = \frac{\lambda^2 G}{4\pi} $, where $ \lambda $ is the wavelength and $ G $ is the antenna gain. This relation highlights how larger gains, achieved through directive designs, enhance power collection despite fixed physical sizes, assuming uniform illumination and lossless conditions. For practical antennas, $ A_e $ is typically less than the physical aperture due to inefficiencies in aperture distribution.
In antenna theory, the far-field region is crucial for accurate pattern and gain measurements, where the electromagnetic field behaves as a plane wave, simplifying analysis. This region begins at a distance $ d > \frac{2D^2}{\lambda} $ from the antenna, with $ D $ denoting the largest linear dimension (such as the diameter for aperture antennas) and $ \lambda $ the operating wavelength. Within this zone, spherical wavefronts from the source approximate plane waves, ensuring constant phase across the antenna under test and minimizing reactive field influences. Closer distances introduce phase errors that distort measurements, making the far-field criterion essential for reliable characterizations.
Polarization matching between transmitting and receiving antennas ensures maximum power transfer by aligning the electric field orientations of the incident and received waves. Mismatch occurs when polarizations are non-parallel, introducing a loss factor that reduces captured power, with complete orthogonality yielding zero transfer. In ideal analyses, this factor is assumed to be unity for co-polarized systems, but real-world deviationssuch as due to propagation effectscan degrade performance by several decibels. These concepts collectively underpin the power transfer mechanisms in link budgets, enabling predictions of signal strength between antennas.

This form assumes unity efficiency in both antennas and perfect polarization match.
An alternative radiometric derivation employs the Poynting vector to quantify the time-averaged power flux and the reciprocity theorem to equate transmitting and receiving properties. Integrating over the beam solid angles confirms the gain relation and yields the identical final equation, emphasizing conservation in lossless free space.

Polarization alignment and matched impedances are also presupposed for maximum power transfer.

Applications

In Wireless Communications

The Friis transmission equation plays a central role in the design and analysis of wireless communication links by providing the free-space path loss component essential for link budget calculations, which estimate the received signal power and subsequent signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). In these budgets, the equation is combined with transmitter effective isotropic radiated power (EIRP), receiver antenna gain, and noise contributionsincluding the system noise figureto determine achievable SNR levels, ensuring reliable communication under given constraints. For instance, in geostationary Earth orbit (GEO) satellite systems operating at Ka-band frequencies around 30 GHz, the Friis-derived path loss, often exceeding 200 dB due to vast distances, is integrated into link budgets to optimize power allocation and predict performance margins for high-data-rate services like broadband internet.
A practical illustration of its application appears in short-range wireless systems like Bluetooth, operating at 2.45 GHz. For a typical link with isotropic antennas (0 dBi gain) over 1 meter, the Friis equation yields a free-space path loss of approximately 40 dB, highlighting the minimal attenuation in near-field scenarios and aiding engineers in assessing coverage for personal area networks. This path loss term directly influences the overall link margin, allowing for adjustments in transmit power to maintain connectivity within battery-limited devices.
Beyond basic power estimation, the Friis equation integrates with modulation schemes to evaluate feasible data rates under power constraints in wireless systems. By informing the received power and thus the SNR, it helps determine the maximum constellation size or modulation ordersuch as from QPSK to 16-QAMthat can be supported without exceeding regulatory transmit power limits, thereby optimizing throughput in resource-constrained environments like mobile cellular networks. For example, higher path losses predicted by Friis at elevated frequencies necessitate lower-order modulations to preserve bit error rates, directly impacting achievable data rates.
Historically, following its 1946 formulation, the Friis equation found immediate application in the postwar expansion of microwave relay systems for long-haul telephony and television transmission in the late 1940s and 1950s. These line-of-sight networks, spanning hundreds of kilometers with repeater stations, relied on the equation to calculate signal attenuation between horn antennas, enabling reliable multi-hop propagation at 46 GHz frequencies and facilitating the first transcontinental microwave links in the United States.

In Radar and Sensing Systems

The Friis transmission equation forms the foundation for the radar range equation by accounting for the round-trip propagation of electromagnetic waves in sensing systems. In radar applications, the signal travels from the transmitter to the target and back to the receiver, effectively applying the Friis formula twice while incorporating the target's radar cross-section (RCS), denoted as \sigma, which quantifies the target's ability to scatter energy back toward the radar. For a monostatic radar where transmitter and receiver are co-located, the received power PrP_rPr is given by
Pr=PtGtGr2(4)3R4,
P_r = \frac{P_t G_t G_r \lambda^2 \sigma}{(4\pi)^3 R^4},
Pr=(4)3R4PtGtGr2,
where PtP_tPt is the transmitted power, GtG_tGt and GrG_rGr are the transmit and receive antenna gains, \lambda is the wavelength, and RRR is the range to the target. This derivation starts with the power density at the target using the one-way Friis path loss, multiplies by /(4R2)\sigma / (4\pi R^2)/(4R2) for the backscattered field, and applies the receive Friis formula, resulting in the R4R^4R4 dependence that limits detection range.
In synthetic aperture radar (SAR) systems, the radar range equation based on Friis enables high-resolution imaging by processing echoes over a synthetic aperture formed by platform motion. The equation determines the signal-to-noise ratio for distributed targets, where the normalized RCS 0\sigma_00 (backscatter per unit area) replaces \sigma, and the R3R^3R3 term arises for surface illumination geometry in side-looking configurations. SAR exploits Doppler variations across the beam to achieve azimuth resolution finer than the physical antenna beamwidth, allowing Earth observation and terrain mapping at ranges up to hundreds of kilometers.
Automotive millimeter-wave (mmWave) sensing radars, operating at 77 GHz, apply the Friis-derived range equation to detect vehicles and pedestrians for advanced driver-assistance systems (ADAS). With typical transmit powers of 10-20 dBm, antenna gains around 12-15 dBi, and RCS values of 1 m for compact cars, the equation predicts receivable signals as low as -120 dBm at 80-200 m ranges, enabling collision avoidance. For example, in weather radar operating at 10 GHz (X-band), the equation incorporates Friis-based attenuation due to rain scatter, allowing detection of precipitation echoes at 50 km with RCS equivalents of 10-100 m for rain cells, though actual performance requires corrections for atmospheric absorption exceeding 0.2 dB/km.
Modifications to the basic radar range equation address dynamic effects in scanning systems. Doppler shifts, arising from relative motion, introduce frequency changes up to f=2vf0/c\Delta f = 2v f_0 / cf=2vf0/c (where vvv is radial velocity and f0f_0f0 is carrier frequency), which are processed separately for velocity estimation but can modulate the effective RCS in the power budget for moving targets. For beam scanning, integration over the antenna beamwidth \theta (typically 1-2) accounts for varying illumination, multiplying the equation by a beam-filling factor to adjust for partial target ensonification in search modes.

Limitations and Extensions

Key Limitations of Applicability

The Friis transmission equation relies on the far-field approximation, which requires the separation distance $ d $ between the transmitting and receiving antennas to greatly exceed $ 2D^2 / \lambda $, where $ D $ is the maximum linear dimension of the larger antenna and $ \lambda $ is the wavelength. When $ d < 2D^2 / \lambda $, the equation no longer holds, as near-field effects dominate, including reactive coupling that causes impedance variations and non-radiative energy storage between the antennas, leading to inaccurate power predictions. This restriction confines the equation's validity to scenarios where the antennas are sufficiently distant for the electromagnetic fields to approximate plane waves.
The equation assumes idealized free-space propagation, neglecting real-world environmental influences that degrade signal strength. It ignores multipath effects from reflections off surfaces, which can cause constructive or destructive interference and fading not captured in the direct path loss model. Atmospheric absorption, such as oxygen attenuation peaking at around 15 dB/km near 60 GHz, introduces additional losses absent from the free-space model, limiting applicability in high-frequency bands like millimeter waves. Similarly, terrain scattering in non-line-of-sight environments, due to obstacles or ground reflections, further invalidates the equation by introducing diffuse propagation paths.
The formulation presumes perfect polarization alignment and impedance matching between antennas, as well as operation over an infinitesimal bandwidth where wavelength and gain remain constant. In practice, polarization mismatchsuch as a linearly polarized receiving antenna intercepting a circularly polarized waveincurs a 3 dB loss, halving the captured power. Bandwidth limitations arise because the equation treats the signal as monochromatic; over broader spectra, frequency-dependent variations in antenna gain and path loss deviate from predictions, requiring corrections for wideband systems.
In directed energy applications like wireless power beaming, the Friis equation overpredicts transmission efficiency for short wavelengths, where the ratio $ \tau = 2d / D $ (with $ d $ as separation and $ D $ as aperture size) becomes large. The plane-wave assumption leads to theoretical beam efficiencies exceeding 100%, which is unphysical, due to unmodeled effects like oblique power flow and mutual coupling at high frequencies. This anomaly, highlighted in Shinohara's 2010 analysis, underscores the equation's inadequacy for compact, high-directivity systems in power transfer.

Modern Extensions and Alternatives

To address polarization mismatches between transmitting and receiving antennas, the Friis equation has been extended to a vector form that incorporates a polarization loss factor $ L_p = |\hat{p}_t \cdot \hat{p}_r|^2 $, where $ \hat{p}_t $ and $ \hat{p}_r $ are the unit polarization vectors of the transmitter and receiver, respectively. This yields the modified power ratio $ \frac{P_r}{P_t} = G_t G_r L_p \left( \frac{\lambda}{4\pi d} \right)^2 $, enabling accurate prediction of received power in scenarios where antenna orientations lead to partial or cross-polarization losses up to 3 dB for orthogonal linear polarizations.
For environments deviating from free-space propagation, such as terrestrial links with ground reflections, the two-ray model supplements the Friis equation by accounting for the direct path and a reflected path off the ground, resulting in constructive or destructive interference depending on distance and height. In urban settings with multipath scattering and shadowing, the Okumura-Hata model serves as an empirical extension, providing path loss predictions that incorporate Friis-based free-space loss alongside terrain-specific corrections for base station height, frequency, and environment type, typically yielding exponents of 3.5 to 4 for non-line-of-sight urban propagation.
Recent advancements include generalizations of the Friis formula for multiport antenna systems in massive MIMO configurations, using active antenna available power to handle near- and far-field interactions in 5G/6G arrays, as derived in 2024 analyses that extend the classical scalar form to vector power gains. For optical quantum links, such as satellite-based quantum key distribution, the Friis equation has been adapted in 2025 studies to model quantum signal attenuation, incorporating atmospheric turbulence, pointing errors, and wavelength-specific gains for 850 nm channels, enhancing efficiency in free-space quantum communication. In millimeter-wave (mmWave) bands for 5G and 6G, ITU-R and 3GPP models extend Friis with frequency-dependent corrections for gaseous absorption and beam misalignment, as evidenced in 2023-2024 propagation studies showing path loss exponents of 2.0-2.5 in line-of-sight urban mmWave scenarios at 28-73 GHz.
As an alternative for near-field power beaming applications, where Friis assumptions break down due to reactive coupling, the Goubau relation provides a directive gain-based efficiency formula for phased-array rectennas, avoiding far-field approximations and enabling up to 80% efficiency in microwave wireless power transfer over short distances.
