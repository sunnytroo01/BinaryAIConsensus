Historical Context

The Jacobi symbol was introduced by German mathematician Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi in 1837 in his paper published in the Monatsberichte der Kniglichen Preussischen Akademie der Wissenschaften, amid his broader investigations into number theory, particularly his extensions of the reciprocity laws originally developed by Carl Friedrich Gauss. This innovation appeared in Jacobi's contributions to the study of quadratic forms, where he aimed to create tools for handling arithmetic properties beyond prime moduli.
The primary motivation behind the Jacobi symbol stemmed from the need to generalize Gauss's quadratic reciprocity lawfirst rigorously proved in 1801to scenarios involving composite moduli. These extensions were essential for deeper analysis of quadratic residues and enabled efficient computation of the symbol using reciprocity without requiring the prime factorization of the denominator in the evaluation algorithm.
Jacobi's symbol built directly on Adrien-Marie Legendre's foundational work from 1785, in which Legendre introduced an analogous notationthe Legendre symbolfor expressing quadratic residuosity modulo primes as part of his efforts to prove reciprocity. By adapting and expanding this framework, Jacobi enabled reciprocity-like properties to hold for composites, marking a key evolution in the field.
In its early applications, the Jacobi symbol facilitated Jacobi's own generalizations of quadratic reciprocity to composite arguments, streamlining computations in number-theoretic problems. It also supported investigations into Diophantine equations, especially those related to binary quadratic forms, by providing a multiplicative structure that simplified determinations of representability and class numbers.
Throughout the 19th century, the Jacobi symbol became integral to advancing number theory, influencing Dirichlet's work on L-functions and class number formulas while laying groundwork for later developments in algebraic number theory and ideal theory.
