Thermoluminescence dating
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Thermoluminescence dating (TL) is a scientific method employed in archaeology and geology to establish the age of crystalline materials, such as ceramics, burnt stones, and sediments, by quantifying the light emitted when these samples are heated after accumulating trapped electrons from environmental radiation since their last significant thermal or light exposure event. The technique measures the thermoluminescence signal, which arises from electrons excited by ionizing radiation (primarily from isotopes like uranium, thorium, and potassium-40) and trapped in crystal lattice defects, providing an absolute chronology for events like pottery firing or sediment deposition. Developed from early 20th-century observations of luminescence in minerals, TL dating was first proposed as a chronological tool in the 1950s for geological and cultural materials, with archaeological applications refined in the 1960s and 1970s through advancements in dosimetry and sample analysis.
The core principle of TL dating involves three stages: acquisition of trapped electrons due to natural radiation, storage in metastable states within minerals like quartz and feldspar, and release of that energy as visible light (typically in the blue-green spectrum) when the sample is heated to 450550C in a controlled laboratory setting. This light emission, recorded as a glow curve via photomultiplier tubes, allows determination of the paleodose (the total radiation dose accumulated since zeroing), which is then divided by the annual dose rateestimated from the sample's radioactive content, surrounding environment, and factors like moisture and cosmic radiationto yield the age in years: Age = Paleodose / Dose Rate. The zeroing event, critical for accuracy, occurs during high-temperature firing (above 450C for ceramics) or prolonged sunlight exposure for sediments, effectively resetting the internal clock by evacuating electron traps.
In archaeological contexts, TL dating is particularly valuable for dating fired clay artifacts like pottery, bricks, and terracotta sculptures, where it provides direct evidence of the last kiln firing event, often spanning from a few hundred to several hundred thousand years ago. It has been applied to authenticate ancient objects, such as European sculptures at institutions like the J. Paul Getty Museum since the 1980s, detecting forgeries by revealing modern production through unexpectedly low paleodoses. Beyond ceramics, the method extends to volcanic materials like lava flows and heated lithics, such as burnt flint tools, offering chronological insights where organic materials for radiocarbon dating are absent. In geoarchaeology, TL complements optically stimulated luminescence (OSL) for dating buried sediments, helping reconstruct site formation processes and human-environment interactions.
Accuracy typically ranges from 520% of the determined age, depending on factors like sample homogeneity, precise dose rate modeling, and avoidance of contamination, though errors can arise from incomplete zeroing, variable environmental conditions, or insufficient sample size (often requiring 100500 mg of material). Despite these challenges, TL dating remains a robust, non-destructive (when fine-tuned) tool in multidisciplinary studies, integrated with other methods like radiocarbon for cross-validation, and continues to evolve with improved instrumentation for smaller samples and higher precision.

Fundamentals

Physical Principles

Ionizing radiation from natural environmental sources, primarily the decay of uranium and thorium series isotopes as well as potassium-40, interacts with the crystal lattices of insulating minerals such as quartz and feldspar. This interaction displaces electrons from their valence band positions, creating free electron-hole pairs that migrate within the lattice. The process occurs continuously in buried materials, with the radiation dose rate typically ranging from 1 to 5 mGy/year depending on the mineral's surroundings.
The displaced electrons become trapped in metastable states, such as defect sites or impurities within the band gap of the insulator, while holes are captured at complementary sites like hole traps. These traps possess specific activation energies, often around 1.6 eV or greater below the conduction band, which determine their thermal stability. Over geological timescales, the population of trapped charges builds up proportionally to the total radiation dose absorbed since the last zeroing event, such as heating above 300500C, providing a natural dosimeter for dating purposes.

Thermoluminescence Phenomenon

Upon heating a sample containing trapped electrons, such as quartz, to temperatures of 500C or higher, the trapped electrons acquire sufficient thermal energy to escape from their defect sites within the crystal lattice. These freed electrons then migrate through the conduction band or via quantum tunneling and recombine with trapped holes at luminescence centers, resulting in the excitation of the centers and subsequent emission of photons, primarily in the visible spectrumtypically blue-green light around 480 nm for quartz. This process produces the observable thermoluminescence (TL) glow, which serves as the fundamental signal for dating applications.
The intensity of the emitted light is directly proportional to the number of trapped charges accumulated since the last heating or light exposure event that reset the signal. As the sample is heated at a constant rate (e.g., 56C/s), the TL intensity is recorded as a function of temperature, yielding a characteristic glow curve with multiple peaks; each peak corresponds to the release of charges from traps of specific depths, with deeper traps (higher activation energies) manifesting at higher temperatures, such as the prominent quartz peaks at approximately 110C, 325C, and 375C. Glow curve analysis involves integrating the area under specific peaks to quantify the signal strength, using methods like the initial rise techniquewhich extrapolates the low-temperature rising flank of a peak to estimate trap depth and charge populationor the total integral approach, which sums the entire peak area after subtracting background noise; these techniques also aid in identifying trap types through thermal activation parameters.
To calibrate the natural TL signal for dating, the intensity of the glow from the unperturbed sample is compared to glow curves produced by administering known laboratory doses (e.g., beta or gamma radiation) to aliquots of the same material, allowing interpolation of the equivalent dose (D_e)the laboratory dose that yields a signal matching the natural one, representing the radiation absorbed since the last reset. Factors influencing the TL signal include anomalous fading, where trapped charges in certain minerals like feldspars decay faster than expected due to quantum tunneling, leading to signal loss over time and potential age underestimation; this effect is particularly pronounced in K-feldspars, with fading rates of 210% per decade. Additionally, sensitivity changes can occur post-irradiation, where the luminescence response to a given dose increases (up to 2030%) after repeated heating or irradiation cycles, attributed to alterations in trap-recombination efficiency, necessitating normalization procedures in measurements.

Dating Procedure

Sample Collection and Preparation

Sample collection for thermoluminescence dating requires careful field procedures to preserve the trapped electron signal by avoiding exposure to light, which can optically bleach the charges. Sediments are typically extracted using opaque PVC tubes, approximately 12 cm long and 6 cm in diameter, hammered into freshly cleaned profiles or deposits to capture undisturbed material from the center of a homogeneous sphere (about 30 cm radius) for accurate dose assessment. For artifacts like pottery sherds or burnt flint, interior fragments are removed to represent the last heating event that reset the signal, while samples are immediately wrapped in black plastic or foil and stored in light-proof containers to prevent sunlight exposure. Modern analogue samples from the surface or subsurface are often collected simultaneously to evaluate residual thermoluminescence and environmental dose rates.
In the laboratory, all handling occurs under subdued red or orange illumination to minimize unintended bleaching of the signal. Samples are air-dried, weighed for moisture content, and gently disaggregated or crushed using ceramic jaw crushers and sieves (e.g., to <2 mm initially) to avoid mechanical heating or damage that could alter trapped charges. For fine-grain analysis, material is further processed to isolate silt-sized fractions (4-11 m) via wet sieving, Stokes settling, or centrifugation, while coarse grains (90-200 m) are separated by dry sieving.
Chemical pretreatments begin with immersion in 4 N hydrochloric acid (HCl) for over an hour to dissolve carbonates, followed by 30% hydrogen peroxide (HO) treatment (several hours to overnight) to remove organic matter, with repeated washing in deionized water and dispersion in sodium pyrophosphate solution via ultrasonication. Quartz-specific purification involves etching with 40% hydrofluoric acid (HF) for about 40 minutes to remove the outer 10 m layer affected by alpha radiation and any adhering feldspars, followed by a 32% HCl rinse to eliminate fluorides and a final methanol wash. Density separation using heavy liquids (e.g., sodium polytungstate at 2.62 g/cm) isolates quartz from denser minerals, ensuring >99% purity.
Preparation protocols differ by material type to optimize signal quality. Polymineral fine grains (4-11 m, including quartz and feldspars) are suspended in methanol and plated onto aluminum disks (1-2 mg per disk) for multi-aliquot thermoluminescence or infrared stimulated luminescence measurements, suitable for sediments like loess where feldspar signals contribute. In contrast, single-aliquot or single-grain quartz protocols target coarse sand-sized grains (90-125 m or larger), etched and mounted individually on stainless steel disks with silicone oil to enable precise selection of grains with bright, stable signals, reducing heterogeneity in complex deposits. Control samples for background radiation are prepared alongside, often from nearby unexposed material.
Historically, early thermoluminescence methods in the 1950s, pioneered by Farrington Daniels, focused on basic glow curve measurements from bulk mineral samples like limestone or pottery without standardized preparation for dating, emphasizing the potential of trapped charges in heated materials. By the 1980s, advancements shifted to refined protocols, including single-grain quartz analysis, where individual grains are extracted, etched, and tested sequentially to identify optimal ones for accurate age determination in heterogeneous contexts.

Measurement and Calculation

In thermoluminescence (TL) dating, the equivalent dose (DeD_eDe) absorbed by the sample since its last heating or bleaching event is quantified using automated TL readers, which typically incorporate photomultiplier tubes (PMTs) for detecting blue light emissions around 350-450 nm and built-in beta irradiation sources such as 90^{90}90Sr/90^{90}90Y for delivering known laboratory doses. These instruments, exemplified by the Ris TL/OSL-DA-20 reader, enable precise control of heating rates (up to 20C/s), temperature ramps (e.g., to 500C), and automated aliquot handling to minimize contamination and operator error during signal measurement.
The primary protocol for determining DeD_eDe in quartz grains is the single aliquot regenerative (SAR) dose method, which applies a series of regenerative doses to the same subsample, measures the resulting TL signal (often the 375C peak after a 300C preheat), and uses test doses to correct for sensitivity changes via sensitization factors. For older samples or those with low luminescence sensitivity, the multiple aliquot additive dose (MAAD) protocol is employed, where separate aliquots receive increasing additive doses and the DeD_eDe is extrapolated from the dose-response curve, though it requires more material and assumes linear growth. In sedimentary contexts, partial bleaching corrections may be applied if the TL signal indicates incomplete zeroing by sunlight, using models like the leading-edge or finite mixture approaches to isolate the well-bleached component.
The environmental dose rate (D\dot{D}D) is calculated from concentrations of uranium (U), thorium (Th), and potassium (K) in the sample and surroundings, typically measured via high-resolution gamma-ray spectrometry using NaI(Tl) or HPGe detectors, combined with contributions from cosmic rays (0.03-0.35 Gy/ka depending on depth and latitude). Internal dose from within the mineral grains is often negligible for quartz but included if alpha particles contribute; external beta and gamma doses are scaled by geometric factors (e.g., infinite matrix assumption for gamma). The age ttt is then computed as t=DeDt = \frac{D_e}{\dot{D}}t=DDe, with uncertainties propagated in quadrature to combine errors from DeD_eDe (dominated by Poisson statistics on photon counts in PMT detection, yielding relative errors of 2-10% for bright signals) and D\dot{D}D (typically 5-15% from radionuclide variability and moisture estimates). Tools like the DRAC calculator automate these steps, incorporating conversion factors from elemental concentrations to dose rates (e.g., 0.043 Gy/ka per % K).
Since the 1980s, advancements have integrated optically stimulated luminescence (OSL) with TL in hybrid protocols and instrumentation, allowing combined measurement of multiple signals from the same aliquots for improved accuracy in poorly heated sediments, as OSL bleaches more rapidly than TL and enables finer chronological resolution up to 200 ka.

Applications

In Archaeology

Thermoluminescence (TL) dating serves as a key chronometric tool in archaeology for determining the age of heated materials, particularly pottery fired to temperatures exceeding 400C, which resets the trapped electron signals, hearths, and burnt stones by effectively zeroing the TL clock at the time of heating. This method is especially valuable for artifacts lacking organic content suitable for radiocarbon dating, extending chronological coverage from the Paleolithic era, with reliable ages up to approximately 50,000 years, through to historical periods spanning several millennia.
The first archaeological application of TL dating was pioneered by Grgler et al. in 1958, who analyzed samples from Swiss lake dwellings, demonstrating the technique's potential for dating prehistoric wooden structures through associated burnt materials. Building on early theoretical work, subsequent refinements in the 1960s, such as those by Aitken et al., expanded its use to pottery from diverse regions, including the Middle East, confirming ages up to 8,000 years with accuracies around 10%.
Notable case studies highlight TL's role in verifying historical timelines. For instance, analysis of ceramics and baked soil from the Terra Cotta Army site in China yielded dates around 2,200 years ago, aligning with the Qin Dynasty construction circa 210 BCE. In archaeological practice, TL dating integrates seamlessly with stratigraphy to refine site chronologies, particularly where organic preservation is poor and radiocarbon methods are unavailable, as seen in Levantine contexts where heated artifacts corroborate layered deposits. This complementary approach enhances relative sequencing by providing absolute ages for key stratigraphic markers, such as burnt layers in tell sites.
TL dating is also widely used for authenticating ancient objects, such as ceramics and terracotta sculptures. Since the 1980s, institutions like the J. Paul Getty Museum have applied TL to detect forgeries in European sculptures by identifying modern production through low paleodoses.
Ethical considerations in TL dating emphasize minimizing sample size to preserve artifacts, with preferences for non-destructive alternatives where feasible, though the method typically requires small destructive extractions (e.g., 15 grams from pottery sherds). Post-excavation handling poses risks to accuracy, as unintended exposure to heat, light, or radiation can alter the TL signal, necessitating controlled storage and documentation protocols to maintain sample integrity.

In Geology and Sedimentology

In geology and sedimentology, thermoluminescence (TL) dating is primarily applied to unheated sediments to determine the time elapsed since their last exposure to sunlight or deposition, enabling reconstruction of geological events such as sediment transport and burial histories. This method targets minerals like quartz and feldspar in aeolian, fluvial, and glacial deposits, where sunlight exposure during transport is assumed to reset the TL signal by bleaching trapped electrons to near-zero levels, thus marking the onset of burial and subsequent dose accumulation from ambient radioactivity. For well-bleached sediments, such as wind-blown sands or loess, TL provides a direct chronological tool for events beyond the range of radiocarbon dating, assuming full or partial signal zeroing occurs within hours to days of daylight exposure.
The effective age range for TL dating of sediments typically extends from a few thousand years up to 100,000500,000 years, limited by signal saturation in quartz and anomalous fading in feldspars, though some studies report ages approaching 800,000 years in low-dose environments like loess sequences. Representative examples include dating Saharan dune systems in northeastern Nigeria, where TL on quartz grains yielded ages of 20,000100,000 years, linking dune formation to arid paleoclimate phases during the Late Pleistocene. Similarly, European loess deposits, such as those in the Middle Rhine area, have been dated to 50,000134,000 years using TL, providing timelines for glacial dust accumulation and paleoenvironmental shifts.
To address incomplete bleachingcommon in sediments with short or shaded transport pathscorrections employ the minimum equivalent dose (D_e) approach, selecting the lowest D_e values from single-grain or aliquot distributions to approximate the true burial dose, as validated in aeolian quartz studies.
Case studies highlight TL's integration with paleoclimate and geomorphic research; for instance, coastal sediments in South Australia have been dated to around 320,000 years, reconstructing interglacial sea-level highs and barrier formation during Marine Isotope Stage 9. In volcanic contexts, TL dating of glass shards in ash layers from sites like the Palouse Loess (USA) has established eruption ages of 80,000120,000 years, aiding correlation of tephra with sediment stratigraphy and climate records. These applications often combine TL with oxygen isotope stratigraphy to model paleoclimate variability, such as dune activation tied to glacial-interglacial cycles in Australian arid zones.
A key limitation unique to geological applications is the variability in sunlight exposure times during sediment transport, which can lead to heterogeneous bleaching and age overestimation by 2050% in fluvial or glacial settings where grains experience limited daylight, necessitating rigorous single-grain analysis to isolate well-reset populations.

Advantages and Limitations

Strengths

One of the primary strengths of thermoluminescence (TL) dating lies in its broad temporal range, which spans from a few hundred years to over 100,000 years, and in some cases up to several hundred thousand years, thereby addressing chronological gaps left by methods like radiocarbon dating that are limited to approximately 50,000 years. This extended timeframe is particularly valuable for dating late Pleistocene and Holocene events, where dose accumulation from environmental radiation provides a reliable chronological signal.
TL dating demonstrates material versatility by applying to a wide array of inorganic substances, including ceramics, burnt flint, and sediments containing minerals like quartz and feldspar, making it suitable for sites rich in such artifacts where organic remains are scarce. Its independence from organic content further enhances its utility, as it does not require preserved biomolecules, rendering it effective in challenging environments such as arid regions or areas affected by high temperatures that degrade organics.
Advancements in measurement techniques, such as the single-aliquot regenerative (SAR) protocol, have improved precision, yielding relative errors of 5-10% for well-behaved samples, with potential for non-destructive analysis through microsampling that minimizes artifact damage. Recent developments as of 2024 include isothermal TL (ITL) SAR protocols for dating speleothem growth up to 425 ka and luminescence methods for rock surface exposure dating of engravings. Additionally, TL dating serves a complementary role in multidisciplinary research, providing independent age validation that cross-checks results from other chronological methods and strengthens overall interpretive frameworks.

Challenges and Sources of Error

One primary challenge in thermoluminescence (TL) dating arises from incomplete resetting of the luminescence signal prior to the deposition or heating event of interest, which can result in age overestimation by incorporating residual trapped charge from previous irradiation cycles. In archaeological contexts involving heated materials such as ceramics or burnt flints, thermal resetting occurs during the firing process, but insufficient temperatures or durations may leave partial signals intact. Conversely, in geological and sedimentary applications, optical bleaching by sunlight resets the signal, yet brief or filtered exposurecommon in turbid or glacial environmentsoften leads to incomplete zeroing. To mitigate this, multiple-grain or single-grain aliquot analysis is employed, allowing identification of dose heterogeneity and application of statistical models like the minimum age model to isolate well-reset components.
Variability in the environmental dose rate represents another significant source of error, primarily due to fluctuations in water content and radionuclide migration within the surrounding matrix, which alter the effective ionizing radiation absorbed by the sample over time. Water acts as an attenuator for beta and gamma radiation, and historical changes in moisture levelssuch as those from fluctuating groundwater or precipitationcan reduce the dose rate by up to 20-30% in wetter conditions, necessitating retrospective modeling of past environmental conditions. Radionuclide disequilibrium, caused by uranium leaching or thorium ingress, further complicates beta and alpha contributions, potentially biasing ages by 10-15%. Accurate estimation of the time-averaged dose rate () often relies on Monte Carlo simulations to propagate uncertainties from these variables, incorporating site-specific sedimentology and hydrological data for robust predictions.
Anomalous fading poses a particular issue for feldspar minerals commonly used in TL dating, where athermal tunneling of electrons leads to spontaneous signal loss at room temperature, underestimating ages without correction. This phenomenon is quantified by the fading rate parameter g-value, typically ranging from 2-5% per decade for many K-feldspars, with higher rates in certain orthoclase varieties. Correction involves laboratory measurement of the g-value for the specific sample and application of empirical or athermal models, such as the Huntley-Lamothe approach, to adjust the equivalent dose, though residual uncertainties of 5-10% persist in high-fading samples. Quartz is generally less affected, making it preferable when available, but fading remains a key limitation for feldspar-based chronologies beyond 100 ka.
Instrumental errors during measurement can introduce systematic biases, particularly in glow curve analysis where inadequate background subtraction inflates the integrated TL signal, or in plateau testing where unstable high-temperature peaks are not isolated, leading to paleodose overestimation by 5-20%. Proper protocols require subtracting instrumental and environmental backgrounds from each aliquot's glow curve and verifying a stable plateau (typically 200-400C) across multiple heating runs to ensure reproducibility. Additionally, beta dose attenuation within fine grains (<100 m) reduces internal exposure due to self-absorption, requiring grain-size-specific correction factors (e.g., 0.7-0.9 for 90-125 m quartz) derived from empirical calibrations to avoid underestimating the total dose.
Recent advancements address ongoing challenges through standardization, notably the efforts of the RIS National Laboratory since the early 2000s, which have provided certified calibration quartz and beta-source protocols to ensure inter-laboratory consistency in dose recovery and sensitivity normalization. These initiatives, including the distribution of Ris calibration quartz batches, have reduced systematic errors in paleodose determination to <5% in controlled tests, facilitating global comparability.

Comparisons with Other Methods

With Radiocarbon Dating

Thermoluminescence (TL) dating and radiocarbon (C-14) dating exhibit significant temporal overlap but diverge in their effective ranges, with TL primarily suited for inorganic materials beyond the reliable limit of C-14. Radiocarbon dating is effective for organic remains up to approximately 50,00060,000 years old, relying on the decay of carbon-14 isotopes in once-living materials. In contrast, TL dating extends to around 500,000 years for silicates and ceramics, making it ideal for older archaeological contexts where organic preservation is poor. This overlap in the 30,00050,000-year range allows for cross-verification, while TL fills the gap for pre-50,000-year events in inorganic matrices.
The methods differ fundamentally in applicable materials, enhancing their complementary roles in mixed archaeological sites. C-14 dating requires carbon-based samples such as bone, wood, or charcoal, limiting its use to contexts with preserved organics. TL, however, targets crystalline minerals like quartz or feldspar in pottery and sediments, enabling dating of fired artifacts or heated stones even in organic-poor environments. For instance, TL has confirmed C-14 ages in sites with organic-tempered ceramics, providing independent chronology for non-carbon elements.
Both techniques necessitate calibration to account for environmental variables, though the processes differ markedly. Radiocarbon ages are calibrated against variations in atmospheric 14C/12C ratios using established curves derived from tree rings and corals. TL calibration involves measuring the environmental radiation dose rate from uranium, thorium, and potassium in surrounding sediments, often requiring in situ dosimetry. Cross-validation has proven effective in Neanderthal sites; for example, TL dating of burnt flints at Saint-Csaire yielded 36,300 2,700 years for layers with Neanderthal remains, aligning with radiocarbon estimates from associated organics in nearby Mousterian contexts.
TL dating is generally more lab-intensive and costly than C-14 due to sample preparation and dosimetry requirements, but its applicability to non-organic materials justifies the investment in sites lacking suitable carbon samples. Standard C-14 analysis via accelerator mass spectrometry costs around $650 per sample for wood or seeds, while TL involves destructive testing of small sherd portions, increasing procedural complexity. Historically, TL gained prominence in the 1960s1970s following advances in instrumentation, coinciding with refined C-14 calibration curves that expanded both methods' archaeological utility post-1960.
Discrepancies between TL and C-14 dates can arise from methodological assumptions, such as incomplete thermal resetting in TL, which may lead to age overestimation if samples were not fully heated during deposition. Conversely, C-14 dates from aquatic or reservoir-influenced samples can underestimate true ages due to older carbon incorporation, as seen in marine contexts. Such issues underscore the value of using both methods conjunctively to resolve ambiguities in late Pleistocene chronologies.

With Other Luminescence Dating Techniques

Thermoluminescence (TL) dating differs from optically stimulated luminescence (OSL) primarily in the method of stimulation: TL relies on thermal energy to release trapped electrons from minerals like quartz and feldspar that have been heated to high temperatures, such as in pottery or hearths, whereas OSL uses light exposure, typically green or blue wavelengths, to stimulate the signal in sediments last exposed to sunlight. This distinction makes TL particularly suitable for dating events involving complete thermal resetting, like firing of ceramics, while OSL excels in reconstructing the timing of sediment deposition through optical bleaching. OSL measurements are generally faster and less destructive to samples, allowing for single-aliquot or single-grain analysis with protocols like the single-aliquot regenerative (SAR) method, which minimizes sensitivity changes compared to TL's potential for thermal alteration during readout.
Infrared stimulated luminescence (IRSL), developed as an extension for feldspar minerals, employs infrared light (around 880 nm) to evoke luminescence, contrasting with TL's heat-based approach and offering advantages in detecting signals from deeper electron traps that may exhibit less anomalous fading under certain conditions. IRSL is often integrated into multi-method protocols alongside OSL since the 1990s to extend the age range and improve accuracy for polymineralic samples, as feldspars provide higher dose saturation levels than quartz in OSL. However, IRSL can suffer from slower bleaching rates and fading issues, sometimes requiring post-infrared IRSL (pIRIR) variants to isolate more stable signals, whereas TL's thermal stimulation can risk incomplete bleaching in non-heated contexts but provides higher sensitivity for specific high-temperature traps in volcanic or heated materials.
All these techniques share the fundamental principle of measuring radiation-induced trapped charges in insulating minerals, with the equivalent dose calculated similarly after environmental dose rate assessment, but TL's reliance on heating introduces greater potential for sensitivity changes during repeated measurements compared to the optical methods. The evolution from TL to OSL marked a significant advancement in 1985, enabling more efficient dating of unheated sediments that were previously challenging with TL due to incomplete bleaching. While OSL and IRSL generally offer superior bleaching efficiency in daylight-exposed environments, TL retains advantages in scenarios requiring assessment of thermal events, such as archaeology of fired artifacts.
Modern hybrid approaches combine TL with electron spin resonance (ESR) for dose verification, particularly in materials like tooth enamel or shells, where ESR measures stable radicals to cross-check TL's equivalent dose estimates and mitigate uncertainties from fading or incomplete resetting. This integration enhances reliability in Quaternary chronologies, though it requires careful sample selection to align the signals from both methods.
