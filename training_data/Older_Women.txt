Older Women
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Older women, generally defined as women aged 60 years and older, constitute a rapidly expanding demographic group worldwide, driven by rising life expectancy and declining fertility rates. By 2050, their numbers are projected to surpass 1 billion, representing the fastest-growing segment of the aging population. Women outnumber men in this age group due to greater longevity, with a ratio of approximately 117 women per 100 men aged 60 and older, escalating to 156 women per 100 men aged 80 and above (as of 2024). This shift underscores the intersection of gender and aging, where older women face unique biological, social, and economic dynamics that shape their health, independence, and societal roles.
Globally, the aging population is concentrating in low- and middle-income countries, where 80% of older people, including women, will reside by 2050. Older women often experience compounded discrimination from ageism and sexism, leading to heightened vulnerabilities in areas such as financial security and social isolation. For instance, they are twice as likely as older men to live alone, primarily due to widowhood, with 21% of older women residing solo compared to 10% of older men. Economic disparities are pronounced, as older women face lower pension coverage and adequacy than men, largely due to lower working-age contributory rates (26.3% for women vs. 38.7% for men globally), with nearly two-thirds of older persons lacking pensions being women, and bear the lifelong burden of unpaid care work, exacerbating poverty in later life.
Health challenges for older women are multifaceted, with non-communicable diseases like cardiovascular conditions, cancer, osteoporosis, and diabetes being prevalent. They also face elevated risks of geriatric syndromes such as frailty, falls, and dementia, compounded by factors like lower bone density and chronic pain from conditions including osteoarthritis. Mental health issues, including depression, affect a significant portion, often linked to life transitions like retirement and partner loss. Additionally, older women are more susceptible to elder abuse and violence, with reports indicating that 34% experienced such incidents during the COVID-19 pandemic.
Despite these obstacles, older women contribute substantially to families and communities, frequently serving as primary caregivers and sources of wisdom and support. International frameworks, such as the WHO's Active Ageing policy and the UN's Madrid International Plan of Action on Ageing, emphasize gender-sensitive approaches to promote their participation, health equity, and security. Addressing these issues requires life-course interventions that mitigate gender inequalities from earlier stages to ensure dignified aging.

Demographics

Global Population Trends

The global population of older adults is undergoing rapid transformation, with women increasingly comprising the majority due to longer life expectancies. According to United Nations projections, the proportion of the world's population aged 60 years and older is expected to nearly double from 12% in 2015 to 22% by 2050, reaching approximately 2.1 billion individuals. Within this group, women are projected to account for 54% of those aged 50 and over by 2030, rising to 60% of individuals aged 80 and older, reflecting the "feminization of aging." This shift is driven by women's higher survival rates across all age groups, as evidenced by the current global population of women aged 60 and older standing at 605 million in 2020 and forecasted to reach 1.14 billion by 2050.
In the United States, these trends are pronounced, with women making up 55% of the population aged 65 and older. As of 2022, this included 31.9 million older women compared to 25.9 million older men, totaling 57.8 million individuals in that age group. By 2024, the total U.S. population aged 65 and older had grown to 61.2 million, continuing the pattern of female predominance. Globally, women's average life expectancy exceeds men's by about 5 yearsin more developed countries, as of 2021, women reach 83 years compared to 78 for mencontributing to this demographic imbalance and the overall aging of populations.
In low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), older women are poised to dominate aging populations even more starkly. By 2030, an estimated 80% of the world's people aged 60 and older will reside in LMICs, where women aged 50 and over already represent a growing share due to persistent gender gaps in longevity and healthcare access. This concentration underscores the need for targeted policies, as older women in these regions will constitute the majority of the aging demographic by mid-century.

Regional and Socioeconomic Variations

In the United States, individuals aged 65 and older comprised 17% of the total population in 2020, a figure projected to rise to 22% by 2040. This aging trend is unevenly distributed geographically, with older adults outnumbering children under 18 in 11 states as of recent estimates, reflecting concentrated growth in regions like the Northeast and Florida. Among older women specifically, these patterns underscore regional variations, where states with higher proportions of seniors often see amplified demands on local resources tailored to female longevity.
Globally, variations between low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) and high-income countries highlight stark differences in the aging experience for women. In LMICs, older women are encountering accelerated population aging driven by rising life expectancy, yet constrained by inadequate infrastructure and support systems, leading to heightened vulnerabilities compared to their counterparts in wealthier nations. For instance, the global population aged 50 and older is expected to reach 2.3 billion by 2030, with women comprising 54% of this group, and the majority residing in LMICs where resource limitations exacerbate these shifts.
Socioeconomic divides further shape these demographics, particularly between urban and rural settings. Lower-income older women in urban areas often navigate dense populations with variable service availability, while rural counterparts frequently exhibit higher longevitypotentially linked to lifestyle factors in select LMICs like Indonesiayet endure significantly reduced access to healthcare and transportation. This urban-rural gradient amplifies inequalities, as rural older women in both high- and low-income contexts face isolation from essential amenities despite their demographic prominence.
Within the United States, ethnic and racial disparities add layers to these variations, with higher proportions of older Black and Hispanic women concentrated in demographics associated with socioeconomic challenges. Black older women, for example, represent a growing segment of the aging population at 9% nationally, often in regions with historical underinvestment, while Hispanic older women, at around 8%, show rapid increases in Southern and Western states, intertwining cultural resilience with structural barriers. These patterns emphasize the need for targeted demographic policies to address intersecting regional and identity-based inequities.

Biological and Physiological Aspects

Physical Changes in Aging

As women age, their bodies undergo various physiological transformations that affect structure and function, often becoming more pronounced after menopause. These include alterations in skin integrity, musculoskeletal health, sensory acuity, and body composition, contributing to visible and functional declines that impact mobility and daily activities. Such changes are driven by cumulative cellular and tissue-level processes, including reduced protein synthesis and increased oxidative stress.
Skin changes represent some of the most noticeable aspects of aging in women, characterized by the development of wrinkles and sagging due to the progressive loss of collagen and elastin fibers. This reduction in dermal matrix components leads to decreased elasticity and increased fragility, while the epidermis thins and becomes drier, exacerbating scaliness and vulnerability to injury.
Musculoskeletal alterations involve a decline in muscle strength through sarcopenia, the age-related loss of muscle mass and function, which affects 10-20% of older adults, with higher rates in women due to factors like menopause, and reduces physical capability. Bone density also diminishes, with osteoporosis disproportionately impacting postmenopausal women due to accelerated bone resorption and lower estrogen levels, increasing fracture risk. Concurrently, immunosenescencea gradual weakening of the immune responseoccurs, slowing reaction times to pathogens and impairing overall resilience. These shifts are partly influenced by hormonal changes during menopause.
Sensory declines commonly include vision and hearing impairments; for instance, presbycusis, or age-related hearing loss, gradually affects high-frequency sound perception in most individuals over 65. Women experience higher rates of age-related macular degeneration (AMD), a leading cause of central vision loss, with late-stage prevalence around 11% in women aged 85 and older, higher than in men overall.
Hair and body composition undergo notable shifts as well, with graying resulting from diminished melanin production in hair follicles, often starting in the 30s and becoming widespread by age 50. Thinning occurs due to reduced follicle activity and slower growth cycles, affecting scalp density. Additionally, fat redistribution in postmenopausal women favors central abdominal deposition over subcutaneous areas, contributing to altered body shape and metabolic changes.

Hormonal and Reproductive Transitions

Menopause marks the permanent cessation of menstrual cycles due to the natural decline in ovarian function, typically occurring between the ages of 45 and 55, with an average age of 51 in the United States. This transition is characterized by a significant drop in estrogen and progesterone levels, leading to vasomotor symptoms such as hot flashes and night sweats, which affect up to 75% of women and can persist for several years. The estrogen decline also contributes to an increased risk of cardiovascular disease, as the protective effects of estrogen on blood vessels and lipid profiles diminish, elevating the incidence of heart disease and stroke post-menopause.
In the post-menopausal period, prolonged low estrogen levels result in various physiological effects, including genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM), which encompasses vaginal atrophy, dryness, and irritation due to thinning of the vaginal mucosa. These changes can lead to urinary issues such as urgency, frequency, and recurrent infections, as the urogenital tissues become more susceptible to inflammation and impaired barrier function. Additionally, estrogen deficiency accelerates bone resorption, heightening the risk of osteoporosis and fractures, with postmenopausal women experiencing a 2-3% annual bone density loss in the first few years after menopause.
Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) remains a primary option for alleviating menopausal symptoms, with systemic estrogen therapiesadministered via pills, patches, or gelsbeing most effective for hot flashes and night sweats, often combined with progestin for women with an intact uterus to prevent endometrial hyperplasia. Topical low-dose estrogen creams or rings provide targeted relief for vaginal atrophy and urinary symptoms without significantly affecting systemic hormone levels. The Women's Health Initiative trial linked combined estrogen-progestin therapy to an increased breast cancer risk, particularly with long-term use beyond five years. As of 2025, guidelines, informed by recent FDA revisions removing certain warnings, recommend initiating HRT near menopause onset in healthy women for symptom relief, noting that benefits like reduced all-cause mortality often outweigh risks when started within 10 years of menopause, while advising limited duration for some.
Women experience an extended post-reproductive lifespan averaging 30-40 years after menopause, potentially linked to evolutionary advantages like the grandmother hypothesis involving kin support, though estrogen decline introduces vulnerabilities such as frailty from muscle and bone loss. This estrogen decline exacerbates sarcopenia and reduced physical resilience, increasing frailty indices in older women independently of other aging factors.

Health and Well-Being

Chronic Diseases and Physical Health Risks

Older women are disproportionately affected by chronic diseases, which contribute significantly to morbidity and reduced quality of life in later years. Arthritis, a leading cause of disability, impacts over 48% of women aged 65-74 and rises to approximately 54% for those 75 and older (as of 2019), often resulting in joint pain, stiffness, and limited mobility. Heart disease remains the primary cause of death among women in this age group, accounting for about one in five female deaths annually, with risk factors including postmenopausal hormonal shifts exacerbating cardiovascular vulnerabilities. Diabetes also prevails at around 19% among women aged 65 and older, heightening susceptibility to complications like neuropathy and cardiovascular events.
Health disparities persist despite some similarities in self-reported health status, where women aged 65-74 report fair or better health at 73.7% compared to 71.8% for men, and those 75 and older at 84.1% versus 83.2% for men; however, older women encounter greater barriers to care access, including transportation issues and provider biases, leading to delayed diagnoses and poorer management of these conditions. These disparities are compounded by caregiving responsibilities, as older women frequently serve as primary informal caregivers for spouses or family members, which correlates with elevated physical strain, fatigue, and higher incidence of stress-related illnesses such as hypertension and musculoskeletal disorders.
Recent trends as of 2025 indicate a post-COVID surge in mobility limitations among older women, with studies showing increased risks of long COVID symptoms like persistent fatigue and reduced physical function, affecting up to 30% of postmenopausal women recovering from infection and hindering daily activities. These physical health risks are further enabled by age-related biological changes, such as decreased bone density and muscle mass, which amplify vulnerability to chronic conditions.

Mental Health Challenges

Approximately one in four older adults experiences a mental health condition, such as depression, anxiety, or dementia, with these issues contributing significantly to disability in this population. Women face a disproportionately higher burden of depression compared to men, with prevalence rates of major depressive episodes around 5% for older women versus 3% for older men in the US (as of 2023). This gender disparity is often exacerbated by life events like widowhood, which significantly increases the risk of depression among older adults, particularly affecting women due to their longer average lifespan and social role expectations.
Dementia, especially Alzheimer's disease, disproportionately impacts older women, who account for nearly two-thirds of cases in the United States, totaling about 4.4 million women aged 65 and older living with the condition. This higher prevalence is attributed to women's longer life expectancy, which elevates cumulative risk, as well as hormonal factors such as estrogen fluctuations during menopause that may influence cognitive vulnerability. Research highlights that these biological and longevity-related elements contribute to women facing a lifetime dementia risk of one in five, compared to one in ten for men. Interventions such as doll therapy have shown benefits in improving emotional well-being for those with Alzheimer's disease or dementia, by reducing agitation and promoting comfort.
Loneliness and social isolation are prevalent among older women, with 2024 data indicating that 33% of older adults report feeling lonely some or often in the past year, a rate that remains high into 2025 and is higher among women. Recent studies (2020-2025) indicate that women over 65 often experience heightened loneliness, influenced by family trajectories, marital quality, and life events such as widowhood or living alone. Strong family relationships and social engagement (e.g., caregiving) can mitigate loneliness and support psychological well-being, while perceived discrimination or poor family ties increase it. Interventions like digital social robots have shown effectiveness in reducing loneliness among older women. This issue is particularly acute for older women living in poverty, where nearly one in ten face financial insecurity that compounds isolation, with rates around 25-33% overall and higher in high-risk groups. Similarly, women in post-caregiving phasesafter years of providing family careexperience elevated loneliness, as the end of caregiving roles often results in grief, reduced social networks, and persistent emotional isolation. Older women often experience heightened psychological needs for companionship, emotional connection, and social support to address loneliness. They exhibit higher levels of anxiety compared to older men, though they may report greater social support networks.
Substance abuse, particularly alcohol misuse, is increasingly recognized as a behavioral health challenge for older women, with rates of alcohol use disorder rising notably in this demographic. Between 2001 and 2013, alcohol misuse among older adults increased by 107%, a trend that has continued, especially among women, driven by factors like bereavement and stress. By 2022, one in 11 adults over 60 had a substance use disorder, with alcohol being the most common, and midlife and older women showing accelerated binge drinking patterns that heighten mental health risks. This growing issue underscores the need for gender-specific interventions to address underlying vulnerabilities. As of 2025, women aged 65 and older are more than twice as likely as men to use antidepressants (15.3% vs. 7.4%), reflecting higher treatment needs for depression.

Social and Economic Dimensions

Poverty and Financial Insecurity

Older women experience higher rates of poverty compared to older men, a disparity driven by lifelong gender inequities in earnings and employment. In the United States, as of 2022, the poverty rate for women aged 65 and older was 11.2%, compared to 9.0% for men in the same age group, according to data from the Federal Interagency Forum on Aging-Related Statistics. The official poverty rate for those aged 65 and older was 9.9% in 2024. This gap is largely attributed to the gender wage gap, where women earn approximately 83% of men's wages for full-time work, and career interruptions for caregiving responsibilities, which reduce lifetime earnings and pension accumulations. Such breaks often result in women receiving lower average Social Security retirement benefits$1,426 monthly for women versus $1,756 for men at age 65 as of early 2025exacerbating financial vulnerability in later life.
Globally, older women in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) face even more pronounced economic insecurity, comprising a disproportionate share of the elderly poor due to limited access to formal pensions and reliance on informal work. In LMICs, older women face higher risks of relative poverty than older men, with factors like lower labor force participation and widowhood amplifying risks. United Nations data highlights that older women suffer higher poverty levels than older men across both developed and developing nations, often due to unequal inheritance rights and exclusion from social protections. Globally, older women have lower pension coverage rates (26.3% versus 38.7% for men). In the U.S., projections indicate rising insecurity amid persistent inflation, with the Supplemental Poverty Measure for adults 65 and older increasing to 15% in 2024, disproportionately affecting women through eroded purchasing power for essentials.
Key contributing factors include reduced Social Security benefits from interrupted careers and escalating housing costs, which burden over 40% of older women renters by consuming more than 30% of their income. In 2023, 57% of older adult rentersmany of whom are womenfaced such housing cost burdens, limiting resources for healthcare and nutrition. These economic pressures compound vulnerabilities tied to demographic trends, such as longer life expectancies that extend periods of financial strain.
The impacts of poverty on older women are severe, linking directly to poorer health outcomes, including increased risks of early mortality and disability. Financial strain raises mortality risk by nearly 60% over five years for older women, independent of other health factors, due to barriers in accessing medical care and nutritious food. Poverty also accelerates disability onset, with low-income older adults facing higher rates of mobility impairments and chronic conditions, perpetuating a cycle of health decline and economic dependence.

Employment, Retirement, and Caregiving

Older women aged 55 and above frequently encounter age discrimination in the labor market, which manifests through employer biases regarding productivity, adaptability, and training needs, resulting in fewer job opportunities and interview callbacks compared to younger or male counterparts. In OECD countries, the average employment rate for women aged 65-69 stands at 23.7% as of 2024, compared to 36% for men in the same age group, reflecting persistent gender disparities in labor force participation among the oldest workers. Many older women who remain employed turn to the gig economy or self-employment for flexibility, with 27.9% of women aged 69 classified as self-employed in 2020-2023, often to accommodate health limitations or family responsibilities.
Retirement patterns for older women are shaped by earlier exits from the workforce, driven by health issues and caregiving demands, leading to reduced lifetime earnings and pension accumulations. Women typically receive about 81% of the Social Security benefits that men do in the United States, owing to career interruptions and lower wages, which exacerbate financial vulnerabilities in later life. Amid rising pension insecurity and economic pressures, 2025 data indicate a trend toward delayed retirement, with OECD employment rates for workers aged 55-64 reaching 64% in 2023, up from previous decades, as older women extend working years to bolster savings.
Unpaid caregiving responsibilities disproportionately affect older women, with approximately 60% of family caregivers in the United States being women, many of whom are over 50 and managing care for aging relatives alongside their own employment. This often places women in the "sandwich generation," caring for both older adults and younger family members, which contributes to heightened stress, health declines, and career setbacks such as reduced hours or job loss, resulting in an estimated average annual income loss of over $300,000 over a lifetime for some. 
Policy frameworks for supporting caregivers remain inadequate globally, with 112 countries lacking any paid leave provisions for caring for aging family members as of recent assessments, forcing many older women to forgo income without compensation in most nations. In OECD countries, while some offer flexible arrangements, comprehensive paid caregiving leave is limited, highlighting gaps that perpetuate gender inequities in employment and retirement security.

Cultural Representations

Historical Perceptions

In many pre-20th century indigenous cultures, older women were revered as wise matriarchs and custodians of knowledge, serving as leaders who made key decisions for their families and communities. For instance, in various Native American societies, elder women held authoritative roles, guiding spiritual, social, and administrative matters due to their accumulated wisdom and connection to ancestral traditions.
In contrast, European folklore often depicted older women negatively, associating them with witchcraft or as economic burdens on society. During early modern witch trials, such as those in 17th-century Finland, stereotypes portrayed elderly women as marginal figures causing harm, though actual cases involved prosperous individuals rather than the impoverished periphery. This imagery reinforced perceptions of older women as threats or dependents, embedding misogyny and ageism in cultural narratives.
The 19th century brought shifts influenced by industrialization, which portrayed older women as increasingly dependent within evolving family structures and wage economies. Medical discourse during this period linked post-menopausal aging to decline, rendering older women socially invisible and unfit for productive roles beyond reproduction, as their value was tied to fertility rather than ongoing contributions. By the post-World War II era, an emphasis on youth culture further exacerbated this invisibility, with societal norms prioritizing vitality and beauty, marginalizing older women through stereotypes of irrelevance and devaluation in media and functionalist theories of aging.
Historical research on women's aging exhibited significant gendered neglect until the 1970s feminist movements, which began addressing the oversight in liberal, radical, and socialist theories that had focused primarily on younger women. Prior to this, gerontological studies rarely examined gender-specific inequities affecting older women, such as poverty and caregiving burdens, due to demographic assumptions centered on shorter lifespans and reproductive priorities. This gap persisted in nursing and feminist scholarship, treating aging as a neutral or male-centric process until feminist critiques highlighted structural biases.
Globally, historical perceptions varied, with Confucian traditions in Asia emphasizing respect for elder women as embodiments of filial piety and maternal authority. Texts like the Lien zhuan (c. 18 BCE) celebrated older women's virtues, such as widows exerting influence over family decisions, elevating their status through rituals and imperial recognition. However, colonial contexts in Asia often led to their marginalization, as European powers disrupted traditional hierarchies, imposing patriarchal structures that diminished women's roles across ages and reinforced economic dependency.

Contemporary Media Portrayals

Contemporary media portrayals of older women continue to exhibit significant underrepresentation, with male characters aged 50 and older outnumbering female counterparts by ratios of 80:20 in films, 75:25 in broadcast television, and 66:34 in streaming content. This disparity contributes to a sense of invisibility among older women, as evidenced by AARP research indicating that many adults 50-plus perceive Hollywood as failing to accurately depict aging experiences. Persistent stereotypes, such as the "overbearing grandmother" or desexualized elder, reinforce ageist tropes that marginalize older women beyond familial roles.
Despite these challenges, there have been notable improvements in authentic representations since 2020, with 62% of women aged 50 and older reporting enhanced portrayals in media. Iconic series like The Golden Girls, originally aired in the 1980s but experiencing renewed popularity through streaming and cultural references, have influenced positive aging narratives by depicting older women as vibrant, sexually active, and resilient, challenging isolation and decline stereotypes. Recent analyses highlight how such shows promote successful aging through themes of friendship, independence, and humor, fostering broader acceptance of diverse older female experiences.
Ageism in AI-generated content and online platforms exacerbates gender-age biases, with 2025 studies revealing that older women are often depicted as distorted, invisible, or unrealistically youthful in images and videos. Research from the University of California, Berkeley, demonstrates that online media systematically portrays women as younger than men, a distortion amplified by AI models trained on these biased datasets, leading to skewed representations in hiring tools and digital advertising. Stanford analyses further confirm that large language models perpetuate stereotypes against older working women, influencing perceptions in professional and social contexts.
Visibility around menopause in media is emerging but remains limited, often prioritizing empowerment narratives over realistic depictions of aging bodies. While 2025 reports note increased discussions in advertising and social mediasuch as femvertising campaigns addressing symptomscoverage frequently avoids bodily changes like weight gain or skin alterations, instead promoting anti-aging products that emphasize perpetual youthfulness. This selective focus, as highlighted in cultural analyses, contributes to a cultural shift toward menopause awareness but perpetuates ideals of desirability tied to younger aesthetics.

Rights and Advocacy

Legal Protections and Policies

In the United States, the Older Americans Act of 1965, which is proposed for reauthorization in 2025 via S.2120, establishes a framework for delivering community-based services to older adults, including nutrition programs like Meals on Wheels that support independent living and address food insecurity, particularly among women who often serve as primary family caregivers. The proposed 2025 reauthorization aims to enhance support for caregivers by improving access to services, ensuring trauma-informed care, and bolstering preventive health initiatives, recognizing the disproportionate burden on older women in caregiving roles. As of November 2025, reauthorization efforts continue, with a Senate hearing held on November 5, 2025.
Internationally, the United Nations Madrid International Plan of Action on Ageing, adopted in 2002, promotes gender-sensitive policies to address the unique vulnerabilities of older women, such as poverty stemming from lifetime income disparities and limited access to social protections. In the European Union, directives like the Employment Equality Framework Directive (2000/78/EC), which prohibits age discrimination in employment, and the Recast Directive on Gender Equality (2006/54/EC), which ensures equal pay and treatment, provide safeguards against intersecting age and gender biases faced by older women in the workforce and beyond.
Specific protections include enhancements to Social Security benefits for widows under the Social Security Fairness Act of 2025, which repeals the Windfall Elimination Provision and Government Pension Offset, restoring full benefits to many widows previously reduced due to public sector employment or spousal pensions, with average monthly increases of around $360. The U.S. Elder Justice Act of 2010 further targets elder abuse by authorizing federal funding for prevention, detection, and prosecution of abuse, neglect, and exploitation, with a focus on vulnerable populations including older women who experience higher rates of such mistreatment.
Despite these advancements, significant gaps persist in long-term care policies, particularly in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), where rapid population aging outpaces the development of formal systems, leaving older women disproportionately exposed to inadequate care, financial strain, and health risks due to reliance on informal family support.

Key Organizations and Activism

The National Council on Aging (NCOA) is a leading U.S.-based organization advocating for health equity among older adults, including targeted efforts to address disparities faced by older women in aging processes. Through its Falls Free Initiative and chronic disease self-management programs, NCOA has engaged over 255,000 participants in ACL-funded falls prevention programs from 2014-2024, with 83% female, promoting preventive health measures and reducing gender-specific risks such as osteoporosis and chronic conditions more prevalent in women. In 2025, NCOA's Age+Action Conference highlighted innovative programs for aging services professionals, emphasizing equitable access to health resources amid evolving demographic shifts that disproportionately affect women.
The Global Alliance for the Rights of Older People (GAROP) operates as an international network comprising over 380 members across approximately 80 countries, dedicated to advancing a UN convention on the rights of older persons. GAROP coordinates civil society engagement at national, regional, and global levels to combat ageism and ensure protections for older individuals, including women who often face compounded discrimination based on gender and age. A landmark achievement in 2025 was the UN Human Rights Council's initiation of an intergovernmental process to draft this binding treaty, driven by GAROP's advocacy for dignity, autonomy, and inclusion in decision-making.
Justice in Aging employs legal advocacy to combat senior poverty and secure health rights, with a particular emphasis on older women who are disproportionately impacted by economic insecurity. Their 2014 special report, "Older Women and Poverty," details factors like caregiving responsibilities, wage gaps, and inadequate retirement benefits that drive women into poverty, recommending expansions in Medicare coverage for dental, vision, and hearing services, alongside strengthened Social Security credits for caregivers. The organization continues to litigate and lobby for equitable access to affordable health care, influencing policies that protect older women from financial barriers to long-term services and supports.
The Grandmothers Advocacy Network (GRAN), a Canadian volunteer-led group, focuses on upholding human rights for older women in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), particularly in sub-Saharan Africa, by addressing poverty, health access, and educational opportunities. GRAN campaigns for social protections that enable grandmothersoften primary caregivers for AIDS orphansto afford basic needs and healthcare, while promoting lifelong learning to empower women against gender-based violence and economic exclusion. Their work underscores the intersection of aging and gender in LMICs, advocating for policies that recognize older women's roles in community resilience and child welfare.
In 2025, activism for older women has intensified around Medicare expansions to include comprehensive long-term care and preventive services, led by groups like Justice in Aging and NCOA to mitigate post-retirement health costs. Efforts to combat ageism in media portrayals have gained traction through policy agendas calling for stronger enforcement of anti-discrimination laws and inclusive representations that challenge stereotypes. Older women's leadership in post-COVID recovery has been highlighted in advocacy, emphasizing their roles in community health and social justice amid ongoing disparities exposed by the pandemic.
