Before Sunrise
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Before Sunrise is a 1995 American romantic drama film directed by Richard Linklater and co-written by Linklater and Kim Krizan. The film stars Ethan Hawke as Jesse, an American traveler, and Julie Delpy as Cline, a French student, who meet by chance on a train in Europe and decide to spend one spontaneous night wandering Vienna together, sharing intimate conversations about life, love, and existential questions. With a runtime of 101 minutes, it explores themes of fleeting romance and human connection through dialogue-driven storytelling, eschewing traditional plot devices in favor of real-time character development.
Released on January 27, 1995, following its premiere at the Sundance Film Festival on January 19, the film was produced on a modest budget of approximately $2.5 million and grossed $5.5 million domestically and $6.6 million worldwide. It received widespread critical acclaim for its naturalistic performances, screenplay, and direction, earning a 100% approval rating from critics on Rotten Tomatoes based on 51 reviews. At the 45th Berlin International Film Festival, Linklater won the Silver Bear for Best Director, while the film was nominated for the Golden Bear. Though it received no Academy Award nominations, its influence on independent cinema and romantic storytelling has been enduring.
Before Sunrise serves as the first installment in Linklater's Before trilogy, which examines the evolving relationship between Jesse and Cline over nearly two decades, followed by Before Sunset (2004) and Before Midnight (2013). The film's emphasis on authentic dialogue and location shooting in Vienna, captured in long takes, has made it a benchmark for introspective, low-budget dramas, inspiring discussions on serendipity and the passage of time in modern relationships.

Synopsis and Characters

Plot

On a train traveling through Europe from Budapest toward Vienna, Jesse, an American in his early twenties heading home after a trip, notices Cline, a French student returning to Paris from visiting her grandmother. An altercation in a nearby compartment draws their attention, leading to an initial conversation about human behavior and relationships. As they chat, they share details of their livesJesse's recent breakup and nomadic lifestyle, Cline's studies and family dynamicsand bond over mutual frustrations with modern dating. With the train nearing Vienna, where Jesse must catch an early morning flight, he boldly suggests that Cline join him for the evening to explore the city, promising an adventure without expectations. After weighing the risks and her own curiosity, she agrees, and they disembark together at dusk, marking the start of their spontaneous 12-hour journey.
Exiting the station, they store their luggage and begin wandering Vienna's cobblestone streets under the evening sky, passing historic buildings and quiet alleys. They encounter a group of amateur actors performing a mock play who invite them to a later show at the Prater amusement park. Their dialogue flows effortlessly, touching on parental expectations, the regrets of youth, and the search for authentic connections amid superficial encounters. Seeking a break, they enter a dimly lit pinball arcade, where they compete in games while debating gender roles in romanceJesse expressing cynicism about women's choices, Cline countering with insights on emotional vulnerability. The real-time pace underscores their growing comfort, as the city's lights brighten with the advancing night.
Their stroll leads them to a small record store, where they retreat to a listening booth to sample albums, using the music as a catalyst for deeper talks on art's power to evoke memories and emotions. Emerging as night fully sets in, they head to the Prater, Vienna's historic amusement park, and board the iconic Ferris wheel for a panoramic ride. High above the glittering city, their conversation turns introspective, exploring themes of mortality and the fleeting nature of chance meetings, culminating in a moment of physical closeness that heightens their emotional intimacy. Descending, they continue through the park's lively atmosphere, the clock ticking toward midnight as their shared silences become as meaningful as their words.
Midnight finds them at a casual poetry reading in a local bar, where a street poet improvises verses based on a random word they provide"milkshake"prompting discussions on creativity, inspiration, and the regrets tied to unfulfilled dreams. Energized, they board a small boat for a serene ride along the Danube canal, the water reflecting the city's glow as they reflect on the night's revelations and the impermanence of their time together. Transitioning to a nearby bar, they share a slow dance amid locals, delving into candid exchanges about love's illusions, sexual desires, and the balance between passion and practicality, all while the hours slip away in the early morning stillness.
Seeking quiet, they enter an open church, its echoing halls inspiring talks on faith, existential doubts, and the human need for transcendence. Outside in a leafy park, they settle on a bench with a bottle of wine, continuing their philosophical dialogue on reincarnation, personal growth, and the regrets that shape identity, the night's chill deepening their reliance on each other's warmth. Later, at a row of phone booths, they stage imaginary calls to friends back homeJesse venting about past heartbreaks, Cline articulating her evolving feelingsvoicing unspoken thoughts in a playful yet revealing ritual. As the first light of dawn breaks, they make their way back to the train station, the full night's progression from strangers to confidants etched in their weary but animated steps.

Cast

The principal roles in Before Sunrise are played by Ethan Hawke and Julie Delpy, whose characters form the core of the film's intimate narrative. Hawke stars as Jesse, a 23-year-old American aspiring writer traveling through Europe on a Eurail pass following a recent breakup. Delpy portrays Cline, a 23-year-old French student at the Sorbonne returning to Paris after spending the summer in Budapest. The characters' differing nationalitiesJesse's American background and Cline's French heritageshape their cross-cultural exchanges on topics like love, philosophy, and society throughout the story.
Supporting roles are filled by Austrian actors who appear in brief but memorable scenes on the train and in Vienna, enhancing the film's sense of serendipitous encounters. These include Andrea Eckert as the wife on the train, Hanno Pschl as the husband on the train, Erni Mangold as the palm reader, and Dominik Castell as the street poet. Additional minor parts feature street performers, such as Tex Rubinowitz as a singer and Bilge Jeschim as a belly dancer, along with other uncredited extras portraying passersby and locals.

Production

Development

The development of Before Sunrise stemmed from director Richard Linklater's real-life encounter in 1989, when he met Amy Lehrhaupt in a Philadelphia toy store during a visit to his sister and spent the subsequent night wandering the city with her, engaging in deep conversations that sparked the idea for a dialogue-heavy romantic film about fleeting connection. The director's brief encounter was akin to an "amour d'un soir" (one-night love), though the film itself portrays a deeper emotional bond rather than a casual one-night stand. This experience, which Linklater described as a profound yet minimal bond without conventional resolution, formed the emotional core of the story, though the film's setting shifted to Europe for added romantic allure.
Linklater co-wrote the screenplay with Kim Krizan, an actress from his earlier films Slacker (1991) and Dazed and Confused (1993), beginning their collaboration around 1993 in an intensive 11-day writing session that produced a draft emphasizing authentic, stream-of-consciousness dialogue over plot-driven action. The script, finalized by summer 1994 ahead of principal photography, incorporated revisions for naturalism, including input from the leads, and was structured to support on-set adjustments that gave the impression of improvisation while remaining fully scripted.
Casting the protagonists required a nine-month search across the U.S. and Europe, prioritizing performers capable of conveying subtle chemistry over star power. Ethan Hawke, fresh from roles in films like Dead Poets Society (1989), was attached early through his agent's recommendation and secured the role of Jesse after chemistry reads confirmed his fit for the introspective American traveler. Julie Delpy, emerging from her acclaimed performance in Europa Europa (1990), auditioned and was cast as Cline for her ability to embody a thoughtful French intellectual, adding cultural authenticity to the character's perspective.
Envisioned as a low-budget independent project, Before Sunrise secured $2.5 million in financing from Castle Rock Entertainment, enabling Linklater's vision of an unadorned, conversation-focused narrative without major studio interference. This modest scale aligned with the film's intimate scope, allowing flexibility in pre-production planning while keeping costs low through practical locations and a small crew.

Filming

Principal photography for Before Sunrise took place over 25 days in the summer of 1994, primarily in Vienna, Austria, to align with the film's one-night timeline spanning from evening to dawn. The production adopted a low-budget approach with a modest $2.5 million expenditure, employing a small crew to facilitate an intimate, location-based shoot that emphasized the characters' real-time interactions.
Filming occurred in chronological order across authentic Viennese sites, including the Prater amusement park with its iconic Riesenrad Ferris wheel, the banks of the Donaukanal where the protagonists encounter a street poet, Kahlenberg hill for panoramic city views, and the Oak Grove in the Augarten for a quiet nighttime rest. Many scenes were captured without formal permits, relying on a guerrilla-style method to blend seamlessly into public spaces and avoid disruptions, which contributed to the film's spontaneous feel.
The film was shot on 35mm film by cinematographer Lee Daniel, who prioritized natural lighting to enhance realism during the extended night sequences and long takes that preserved the natural flow of dialogue between leads Ethan Hawke and Julie Delpy. This technical choice supported the minimalistic visual style, with minimal artificial illumination to capture the ambient glow of streetlights and early morning light.
Challenges included coordinating night shoots amid Vienna's variable summer weather, which occasionally forced improvisations, and the ongoing script revisionsoften done weekends or even the night before key scenesto maintain actor immersion without extensive rehearsals. Director Richard Linklater noted the logistical hurdles of public transport and attractions like trains and the Ferris wheel, stating, "We shot in chronological order and worked on the script every weekend throughout the shoot... the night before, we were up until 3 in the morning rewriting the final scene." These elements fostered a collaborative, adaptive environment that mirrored the film's themes of serendipity.

Music

The original score for Before Sunrise was composed by English avant-garde guitarist and composer Fred Frith.
Frith's score consists of minimal, ambient compositions primarily featuring processed guitar elements that provide understated emotional layering in the film's quieter, transitional moments.
This approach ensures the music remains in the background, complementing the dialogue-centric narrative without drawing attention from the characters' interactions.
The official soundtrack album was released in 1995 by Milan Records, compiling 11 tracks of featured music from the film, blending folk, alternative, and classical selections.
Notable inclusions are "Come Here" by Kath Bloom, an acoustic folk song that captures the intimacy of the protagonists' budding connection; "Je t'aime tant" and "A Waltz for a Night," both performed by Julie Delpy; and "Living Life" by Kathy McCarty, offering a raw, indie sensibility.
Classical pieces, such as the Andante from Johann Sebastian Bach's Sonata for Viola da Gamba in G Major, BWV 1027, performed by Laurence Dreyfus and Ketil Haugsand, add a timeless elegance to reflective sequences.
Other tracks like "The Human Pump" by Harald Waiglein and "Dancing with Da Rat" by Loud contribute eclectic, upbeat vibes to lighter moments.
In the film, Frith's non-diegetic score appears sparingly in transitional scenes, such as walks through Vienna's streets, to subtly evoke mood and passage of time while preserving the naturalistic flow.
Diegetic music plays a more prominent role in specific plot points, including the record store scene where Jesse and Celine share headphones to listen to songs like "Come Here," fostering their emotional bond through shared discovery.
Street buskers' live performances, featuring acoustic guitar and violin, also integrate organically during the characters' nocturnal wanderings, mirroring the spontaneous nature of their encounter.
Frith's involvement began during post-production, where he crafted the score to align with a rough cut of the film, emphasizing restraint to match its intimate, conversational tone.

Themes and Style

Themes

Before Sunrise explores serendipity and fate as central forces in romance, exemplified by the chance encounter between Jesse, an American traveler, and Cline, a French student, on a train bound for Vienna. This serendipitous meeting propels their decision to spend one night together, underscoring how fleeting opportunities can forge profound connections. Director Richard Linklater has described the film's premise as capturing "two people meeting and having a connection," drawing from his own real-life serendipitous encounter that inspired the story. The narrative posits that fate is not predestined but realized through bold choices, such as Jesse's impulsive invitation for Cline to disembark the train with him.
Existential questions about mortality and time permeate the characters' conversations, emphasizing the urgency of living in the moment amid life's impermanence. Jesse and Cline frequently reflect on death and the passage of time, using metaphors like time travel to contemplate regrets and unlived possibilities. These discussions highlight an awareness of mortality that intensifies their brief romance, prompting them to seize the present rather than dwell on future uncertainties. Linklater frames this as a meditation on "roads not taken," where the 24-hour timeframe mirrors broader philosophical inquiries into human existence.
Gender dynamics and personal growth emerge through the evolving interplay between Jesse and Cline, revealing vulnerabilities and self-discovery. Their dialogues challenge traditional romantic tropes, with Cline's introspective responses often subverting Jesse's more assertive overtures, fostering mutual growth. Co-writer Kim Krizan's involvement ensured balanced portrayals, avoiding clichs in depicting female perspectives on independence and desire. This dynamic illustrates personal evolution through honest exchange, as the characters confront their insecurities and aspirations.
The film delves into love and connection by contrasting instant intimacy with the realities of long-term relationships, infused with postmodern skepticism toward idealized narratives. Jesse and Cline's rapid emotional bond questions whether profound love can flourish in a single night or requires sustained commitment, reflecting doubts about enduring romance in a fragmented world. Linklater notes that the story probes "could this really be something more, something bigger, eternal?" while acknowledging the ambiguity of such connections. Their interactions blend friendship and romance, suggesting love thrives on shared understanding rather than grand gestures.
Cultural contrasts between American optimism and European introspection are highlighted in discussions on travel, identity, and worldview. Jesse embodies a youthful, exploratory American spirit, while Cline represents a more philosophical European sensibility, enriched by her French background. Their exchanges on these differencessuch as views on consumerism versus cultural heritageilluminate how national identities shape personal philosophies and romantic expectations. Linklater intentionally cast actors from different continents to authentically capture this transatlantic tension.
Recurring motifs reinforce these themes, with the train symbolizing life's unpredictable journey and the dawn evoking new beginnings tempered by parting. The initial train setting launches their odyssey, representing serendipity's rails, while the approaching sunrise marks both renewal and inevitable separation, echoing meditations on time's relentlessness. These elements underscore the film's philosophical core without overt symbolism, integrating seamlessly into the narrative flow.

Visual and Directorial Style

Richard Linklater's directorial approach in Before Sunrise emphasizes a collaborative process with actors Ethan Hawke and Julie Delpy, who contributed significantly to the dialogue through structured improvisation within key scripted beats alongside the script by Linklater and Kim Krizan, allowing for natural dialogue and character development. The film unfolds in real-time pacing, mirroring the progression of a single night in Vienna through chronological shooting and a runtime of 101 minutes that closely aligns with the story's temporal scope, enhancing the sense of immediacy and transience. This reflects Linklater's signature "slacker" realism, rooted in his earlier work like Slacker (1990), where aimless wandering and unhurried conversations capture the mundane poetry of everyday encounters without contrived drama.
Cinematographer Lee Daniel employed handheld shots and long takes to achieve an intimate, documentary-like feel, often averaging around two to three minutes per scene to sustain the flow of uninterrupted interactions between the protagonists. Shooting predominantly with available light supplemented by minimal artificial sources, Daniel captured Vienna's varied nocturnal illumination to maintain authenticity and consistent skin tones. The resulting visuals evoke the city's nighttime ambiance through cool blues in shadowed streets contrasted with warm amber glows from shop windows and arcades, underscoring the film's themes of fleeting connection in an urban twilight.
In editing, Linklater and his team minimized non-linear inserts, prioritizing seamless continuity in the characters' conversations to build emotional intimacy and temporal immersion, with the film's structure eschewing montages in favor of extended, unbroken sequences that echo the night's unhurried rhythm. This approach draws from European New Wave influences, particularly Franois Truffaut's emphasis on location authenticity and dialogue primacy, as seen in films like The 400 Blows (1959), where real urban settings and philosophical exchanges ground youthful idealism in everyday realism. Linklater adapts these elements to prioritize time as a dynamic force, using extended runtime and naturalistic visuals to make the passage of hours feel palpably alive.

Release and Reception

Premiere and Release

Before Sunrise had its world premiere at the Sundance Film Festival on January 19, 1995. The film's European premiere occurred at the Berlin International Film Festival on February 14, 1995, during the event held from February 9 to 22, where director Richard Linklater received the Silver Bear for Best Director.
The film was theatrically released in the United States on January 27, 1995, distributed by Columbia Pictures. It launched with a limited release in a small number of theaters, expanding throughout early 1995 based on festival acclaim and positive word-of-mouth. International distribution rolled out in spring 1995, including a release in the United Kingdom on April 20.
Home media options became available starting with a VHS release from Columbia TriStar Home Video on July 18, 1995._1995_VHS) The Criterion Collection issued a DVD edition on November 7, 2001. A high-definition Blu-ray version followed as part of The Before Trilogy set from the Criterion Collection on February 28, 2017.
The marketing campaign emphasized the film's independent romance sensibilities, with promotional posters centering on leads Ethan Hawke and Julie Delpy set against Vienna's skyline to evoke the story's intimate European setting.

Critical Reception

Upon its release in 1995, Before Sunrise garnered widespread critical acclaim for its naturalistic dialogue, intimate character study, and the palpable chemistry between leads Ethan Hawke and Julie Delpy. The film holds a 100% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes, based on 51 reviews, with an average score of 8.8/10; critics consensus describes it as "thought-provoking and beautifully filmed, an intelligent, unabashedly romantic look at modern love, led by marvelously natural performances." Roger Ebert awarded it three out of four stars, lauding the film's focus on unadorned conversation as a refreshing departure from cinematic clichs, noting that "there is no hidden agenda... It's mostly conversation, and like the best conversations, it moves in unexpected directions." On Metacritic, it received a weighted average score of 79 out of 100, based on 17 reviews, reflecting strong praise for its emotional authenticity and youthful exuberance.
The film's artistic merit was further recognized with the Silver Bear for Best Director awarded to Richard Linklater at the 45th Berlin International Film Festival, where it competed in the main section and was celebrated for its innovative approach to romantic storytelling. It also earned nominations at the 11th Independent Spirit Awards in 1996, including for Best Screenplay (Linklater and Kim Krizan), Best Male Lead (Hawke), and Best Female Lead (Delpy), underscoring its status as a standout in independent cinema.
In retrospective assessments, particularly around its 30th anniversary in 2025, Before Sunrise continues to be hailed for its timeless exploration of fleeting connections and romantic idealism. Publications like The Guardian described it as a film that "elegantly sports with the idea of unresolved sexual tension over such an extended stretch of time," affirming its lasting appeal in an era of digital disconnection. Similarly, Little White Lies emphasized its portrayal of "ultimate intimacy" without modern intrusions like smartphones, positioning it as a poignant reminder of unmediated human encounter. In French-language reviews and critiques, the film is frequently described as a "coup de foudre" (love at first sight), due to the protagonists' instant romantic connection during their one night in Vienna; it is also tagged under "Coup de foudre" on AlloCin. While some early reviewers noted the deliberate pacing and dialogue-heavy structure as occasionally testing for audiences accustomed to faster narratives, the consensus remains overwhelmingly positive, with the film's romantic realism enduring as a benchmark for the genre.

Box Office

Before Sunrise was produced on a budget of $2.5 million. The film earned $5.5 million in the United States and Canada. Internationally, it grossed approximately $1 million, leading to a worldwide total of $6.6 million.
It opened in a limited release across 363 theaters, generating $1.4 million in its first weekend. The film later expanded its theatrical run and maintained strong performance over multiple weeks, bolstered by positive word-of-mouth among audiences. For an independent production in 1995, these results significantly outperformed initial expectations, demonstrating its appeal as a low-budget romantic drama.
The movie's setting in Vienna contributed to its robust international reception, particularly in Europe, where cultural resonance helped drive overseas earnings. In the long term, consistent home video sales further enhanced its profitability for producer Castle Rock Entertainment.

Legacy

Sequels

The sequel to Before Sunrise, titled Before Sunset, was released in 2004 and directed by Richard Linklater, with the screenplay co-written by Linklater, Ethan Hawke, and Julie Delpy. It reunites the characters Jesse and Cline in Paris nine years after their initial encounter, unfolding in real time through extended conversations that explore their lives and lingering connection. The film has a runtime of 80 minutes and was produced on a budget of approximately $2.7 million, grossing $16 million worldwide.
The trilogy concluded with Before Midnight in 2013, also directed by Linklater and co-written by the same trio of Linklater, Hawke, and Delpy. Set in Greece, the story picks up another nine years later, examining Jesse and Cline's married life as parents facing the realities of long-term commitment. With a runtime of 109 minutes, it grossed $23 million worldwide.
Across the three films, the narrative advances roughly every nine years1995, 2004, and 2013while maintaining a shared improvisational dialogue style that delves into the evolving dynamics of romantic relationships. Although ideas for a fourth film have been discussed, none has been produced as of 2025; in October 2025, Ethan Hawke noted that a sequel remains a possibility but without firm plans.
The characters Jesse and Cline make a brief animated cameo appearance in Linklater's 2001 film Waking Life, bridging the visual and thematic elements of his earlier works.

Cultural Impact and Influence

Before Sunrise has been widely recognized in critical rankings, underscoring its enduring appeal as a romantic masterpiece. Entertainment Weekly included it in their list of the 25 best romance films of the 1990s, highlighting its innovative approach to love stories. Similarly, The Guardian ranked it third among the best romantic films of all time, praising its gentle fusion of intellectual dialogue and emotional intimacy. These accolades reflect the film's role in inspiring the indie romance genre, with its focus on authentic, conversation-driven connections influencing later works like (500) Days of Summer, which echoes its exploration of fleeting relationships and personal growth.
The film's cultural resonance persists, particularly in millennial and Gen Z discussions of transient romance and human connection, often quoted in media as the archetype for a profound "one-night stand" encounter free from modern distractions. In 2025, to mark its 30th anniversary, Before Sunrise received a theatrical re-release in the UK and Ireland, accompanied by screenings and reflective essays that emphasize its timeless charm in a smartphone-saturated era. Fan theories frequently speculate on its real-life inspirations, tracing back to director Richard Linklater's 1989 encounter with Amy Lehrhaupt in Philadelphia, a night of walking and talking that mirrored the film's premise and tragically ended with her death in 1994; Linklater dedicated the trilogy's third installment to her, noting that without this experience, the films would not exist.
Before Sunrise paved the way for dialogue-centric cinema, prioritizing extended conversations over plot-driven action to delve into philosophical and emotional depths. The trilogy as a whole is studied in film courses for its depiction of character evolution across decades, examining themes of time, love, and existentialism through a Kierkegaardian lens. On a broader level, the film boosted the careers of Ethan Hawke and Julie Delpy, establishing them as indie icons, and stands as an emblem of the 1990s indie cinema revival with its low-budget innovation and authentic storytelling.
