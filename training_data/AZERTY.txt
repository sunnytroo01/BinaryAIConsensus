AZERTY

1x
 AZERTY is a keyboard layout for typewriter and computer keyboards used primarily in France and other Francophone regions, named after the sequence of the first six letters (A, Z, E, R, T, Y) on its top alphabetic row, and designed to facilitate typing in French by providing easier access to accented characters and common punctuation. Developed in the late 19th century as an adaptation of the American QWERTY layout for French typewriters, it swaps the positions of A and Q, as well as Z and W, to prioritize frequently used letters in French while incorporating keys for diacritics like , , and . This arrangement became the de facto standard in France by the early 20th century and remained without formal specification until the 2019 NF Z71-300 standard.Today, AZERTY remains widely used in France, Belgium, Switzerland, and several African countries, though it has faced criticism for inefficiencies in programming and non-French languages due to limited symbol access. In response to these limitations, the French standards body AFNOR published an updated voluntary standard, NF Z71-300, in April 2019, introducing an optimized AZERTY model that adds around 60 new characters for better support of uppercase accents, ligatures (such as and ), and international symbols, while maintaining backward compatibility and improving ergonomics based on computational analysis of typing corpora and public input.

Origins and History

Early Development

The AZERTY keyboard layout originated in France during the last decade of the 19th century as a modification of the American QWERTY design, specifically adapted for typewriter use to better accommodate the French language's phonetic structure and frequent letter usage. Unlike QWERTY, which prioritized English letter frequencies, AZERTY repositioned keys for common French letters such as A, Z, and Eplacing them as the first letters on the top alphabetic row to enhance typing speed and reduce finger movement on mechanical devices. This rearrangement addressed the limitations of imported U.S. typewriters, which often jammed due to inefficient key sequencing for non-English texts.
A core design goal was to optimize support for French diacritics, including accents like , , , and , which are integral to the language but absent in standard English layouts. Early AZERTY implementations introduced dead keys and dedicated modifiers to generate these characters without requiring separate keys for every variant, thereby minimizing mechanical complexity and jamming risks on typewriters where typebars could collide during rapid input. French typewriter users and importers modified QWERTY machines to incorporate these features, driven by the need for efficient document production in administrative and literary contexts.
The precise inventor remains unknown, but the layout emerged through adaptations by French manufacturers responding to local demands, with the first commercial AZERTY typewriters appearing around the 1890s. American firms like Underwood later refined and produced AZERTY variants for the French market starting in the early 1900s, solidifying its mechanical reliability and contributing to its establishment as a practical standard.

Adoption in French-Speaking Regions

The AZERTY layout achieved widespread adoption in France during the early 20th century, becoming the de facto standard for typewriters amid a booming market that saw significant growth between 1900 and 1914. This period marked the transition from experimental adaptations of QWERTY to a layout optimized for French typists, with AZERTY's arrangement reflecting the frequency of letters like A, Z, and E in the language. By the outbreak of World War I, AZERTY-equipped typewriters dominated French offices, schools, and administrative settings, entrenching it as the preferred choice for official documentation and correspondence.
The French government played a key role in promoting AZERTY from the 1890s onward, encouraging its use on typewriters to better support French orthography, including accents and the cedilla. This institutional backing extended into the interwar period, where typing instruction in education and vocational training emphasized AZERTY proficiency, solidifying its position in public administration and commerce during the 1920s and 1930s. Despite occasional debates over alternative layouts, such as Albert Navarre's 1907 proposal, AZERTY's momentum proved unstoppable, with manufacturers like Underwood producing French-specific models that reinforced its dominance.
AZERTY remained the de facto standard into the digital age, with official standardization by the Association Franaise de Normalisation (AFNOR) only occurring in April 2019 through the NF Z71-300 specification. In other French-speaking regions, like Belgium and parts of Switzerland, similar adoption patterns emerged, with variants tailored to local dialects but retaining the core AZERTY structure for compatibility with French imports. AZERTY's cultural entrenchment endured QWERTY's global prevalence, supported by linguistic pride and resistance to Anglo-American influences, ensuring its role as the primary layout in French-speaking Europe and Africa.

Core Layout Features

Basic Character Arrangement

The standard AZERTY keyboard layout arranges the 26 letters of the Latin alphabet across three primary rows to facilitate French typing, differing from the QWERTY layout by swapping positions for A and Q, Z and W, among others. The top row consists of the keys A, Z, E, R, T, Y, U, I, O, P from left to right, followed by a dedicated key for the circumflex accent (^ unshifted). The middle row features Q, S, D, F, G, H, J, K, L, M, followed by the grave accent (). The bottom row includes the less-than symbol (<), then W, X, C, V, B, N, followed by comma (,), semicolon (;), and colon (:).
Above these letter rows lies the number row, which primarily displays symbols in the unshifted state, such as &, , ", ', (, -, , _, , , ), and =. Numbers are accessed via the Shift modifier, yielding 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 0, degree symbol (), and plus (+).
Key punctuation and symbols are integrated into these rows for efficiency; for instance, the semicolon (;) occupies the position typically held by the period in QWERTY layouts, while square brackets are produced by shifting the 5 key and the key to the right of 0, respectively.

Accented Letters and Dead Keys

In the AZERTY keyboard layout, dead keys enable the efficient production of accented characters by altering the output of the following key press without inserting a standalone symbol. When a dead key is pressed, the system awaits the next character input, combining the diacritic with the base letter to form the accented version; if a non-compatible key follows, the dead key may insert its symbol or produce an error tone. This approach, inherited from typewriter designs, optimizes space on the keyboard while supporting the rich diacritical system of French.
The primary dead keys correspond to the most frequent French accents: grave (`), acute (), circumflex (^), diaeresis (), and tilde (~). For instance, in traditional implementations, the circumflex dead key followed by 'e' yields , and the grave dead key followed by 'e' produces . The diaeresis is often accessed via a shifted circumflex key, as in Shift + ^ + e for . These combinations allow dynamic accent application to vowels in positions consistent with the base layout.

The cedilla () occupies a fixed position without needing a dead key, and the ligature is available via AltGr + o.

By prioritizing these mechanisms, AZERTY minimizes keystrokes for common orthographic needs compared to layouts requiring numeric Alt codes.

Modifier Keys and Functions

In the AZERTY keyboard layout, the Shift key primarily serves to produce uppercase versions of letters and access secondary symbols on designated keys. For alphabetic characters, pressing Shift in combination with a letter key yields its uppercase equivalent, such as Shift + a producing A. However, due to the layout's emphasis on French diacritics, the number row features symbols in their unshifted state (e.g., the key labeled "2 " outputs without Shift), while Shift activates the corresponding numerals or additional symbols (e.g., Shift + key outputs 2, and Shift + & key outputs 1). This arrangement facilitates efficient typing of common French punctuation but requires users to adapt for numerical input compared to QWERTY layouts.
The Alt key, particularly the left Alt, functions in combination with the numeric keypad to input ASCII and Unicode characters via decimal codes, a method standardized for international layouts including AZERTY. For instance, holding Alt and typing 0233 on the numeric keypad inserts the lowercase (U+00E9), while Alt + 0232 produces (U+00E8). This technique is especially useful for characters not directly accessible through the base layout or dead keys, though it requires a keyboard with a dedicated numeric keypad and Num Lock enabled.
The right Alt key, often denoted as AltGr, acts as a third-level modifier to access extended characters and symbols essential for multilingual French usage. Common combinations include AltGr + e for (euro sign), AltGr + a for (ligature), and AltGr + o for , with Shift + AltGr enabling uppercase variants like Shift + AltGr + a for . These mappings align with the European ISO standards adapted for AZERTY, enhancing support for typographic needs without altering the core letter arrangement.
The Ctrl key operates mainly as a modifier for system-level keyboard shortcuts, consistent across layouts but adapted to AZERTY's key positions. Examples include Ctrl + c for copy, Ctrl + v for paste, and Ctrl + z for undo, which interact with applications rather than directly producing characters. Other modifiers like the Windows key may combine with Ctrl or Alt for OS-specific functions, such as Ctrl + Alt + Del for task management.
Despite these capabilities, AZERTY modifiers do not provide direct hardware access to the full Unicode repertoire; characters beyond the layout's predefined mappings, such as non-Latin scripts, necessitate supplementary OS utilities like the Windows Character Map or input method editors. This limitation underscores the layout's focus on French-centric efficiency over universal character coverage.

Implementation in France

Windows and Standard Layouts

The French AZERTY keyboard layout on Microsoft Windows is available in both legacy and standard variants, with the standard version (identifier 0001040C) introduced in Windows 11 to better support common French characters. In the default configuration, the layout prioritizes accented letters and symbols essential for French typing, such as dedicated keys for , , , and on the main rows, while the number row features mappings like Shift+2 for the double quote ("), AltGr+2 for the at symbol (@), and AltGr+E for the euro symbol (). Multimedia functions are typically handled through the function keys (F1F12), which can be remapped via Windows settings or hardware Fn keys to control volume, playback, and other media operations, though the core AZERTY arrangement does not alter these defaults.
Certain programming and technical symbols are absent from direct key positions in the standard French AZERTY layout, requiring AltGr combinations for access. For instance, the pipe symbol (|) is produced with AltGr+6, while curly braces ({ and }) necessitate AltGr+, where square brackets () themselves are AltGr+5 and AltGr+ (the key right of 0), leading to multi-step workarounds like AltGr+AltGr+5 for { in some contexts. These omissions stem from the layout's focus on French diacritics over English-centric symbols, often prompting developers to use character maps or third-party remappers as alternatives.
As an ergonomic alternative within Windows, the AZERTY-BPO layout (identifier 0002040C) rearranges keys to reduce finger strain, similar to Dvorak principles adapted for French, and is natively supported in Windows 11 for users seeking improved typing efficiency. By 2025, Windows 11 version 24H2 has enhanced the standard AZERTY with better support for extended diacritics and ligatures, such as and , through an updated configuration that simplifies input for rare French characters without additional software.

Linux and Open-Source Variants

In Linux and open-source environments, the AZERTY keyboard layout is primarily configured through the X Keyboard Extension (XKB), which provides flexible mapping of keys for various layouts, including French AZERTY. The core configuration files are part of the xkeyboard-config package, where the French AZERTY symbols are defined in /usr/share/X11/xkb/symbols/fr, supporting the standard arrangement with dead keys for accented characters like , , and , as well as variants for the NF Z71-300 improved layout available in recent distributions. Administrators can apply the layout system-wide via files in /etc/X11/xorg.conf.d/00-keyboard.conf, specifying Option "XkbLayout" "fr" for X11 sessions, or use the setxkbmap -layout fr command for runtime changes.
Under Wayland, AZERTY support leverages libxkbcommon to interpret the same XKB data files, integrated into compositors like GNOME or KDE Plasma, allowing configuration through desktop settings without direct X11 dependencies. For instance, in GNOME-based systems, users enable the French AZERTY variant via Settings > Keyboard > Input Sources, selecting "French (AZERTY)" from the list. This approach ensures consistency across sessions, though some early Wayland implementations required workarounds for variant persistence, now resolved in modern releases.
A key advantage of Linux implementations over proprietary systems is enhanced handling of dead keys and Unicode input through the Compose key, which enables multi-step sequences for extended characters beyond standard AZERTY mappings. The Compose key, configurable as Right Alt or Menu in desktop environments, supports dead key combinations (e.g., ^ followed by e for ) and Unicode entry (e.g., Compose + c + o for ), drawing from locale-specific files like /usr/share/X11/locale/fr_FR.(/page/UTF-8)/Compose. This facilitates seamless typing of diacritics and symbols without layout switches, improving accessibility for French users.
In popular distributions like Ubuntu, the default keyboard layout for French locales is set to AZERTY ("fr") during installation, with variants such as "French (legacy, alt.)" or "French (no dead keys)" available via the Settings interface or gsettings commands for customization. Users can add or toggle layouts using Super + Space, and preview the AZERTY arrangement in the input source menu to verify key positions. Other distributions, such as Arch Linux or Fedora, follow similar XKB-based defaults but offer additional models like "pc104" for compatibility with standard hardware.
As of 2025, open-source input methods have seen significant enhancements for multilingual support, particularly integrating non-Latin scripts alongside AZERTY bases through frameworks like IBus and Fcitx5. Linux Mint's Cinnamon 6.4 release (as in Mint 22.2, September 2025), for example, unified keyboard layouts with input method editors under Wayland, enabling smoother transitions between AZERTY for Latin text and IMEs for scripts like Arabic or Chinese via a single configuration panel. These updates, building on libxkbcommon improvements, reduce latency in dead key processing and expand Compose sequences for over 1,000 Unicode points, benefiting users in diverse linguistic environments.

National Standards and Industrial Applications

The AFNOR NF Z71-300 standard, published on April 2, 2019, formally defines the key positions and symbols for the French AZERTY keyboard layout for the first time, aiming to standardize input for accented characters, ligatures, and other French-specific elements to improve typing accuracy and ergonomics. This voluntary standard specifies two primary layouts: an "improved AZERTY" that closely resembles the traditional version while repositioning less common symbols for better accessibility, and a BPO variant optimized for French frequency analysis to reduce finger travel. Developed with input from the French Ministry of Culture and computational ergonomics research, it addresses longstanding inconsistencies in prior non-standardized designs.
In industrial contexts, the AZERTY layout traces its origins to late-19th-century French typewriters, where it adapted QWERTY principles to accommodate diacritics, becoming the dominant configuration for mechanical office equipment through the mid-20th century. Today, it informs the production of modern hardware, including ergonomic keyboards designed for prolonged office use, with manufacturers ensuring compliance for French markets to support efficient data entry in administrative and commercial settings. These applications extend to specialized devices like point-of-sale terminals and industrial control panels in French-speaking regions, prioritizing native language support over international symbols.
The AZERTY layout is widely used in the French public sector for official correspondence, reports, and digital services, as well as in educational institutions for typing instruction and computer labs, to promote grammatically correct French. Compliance with the NF Z71-300 standard is encouraged in these areas to align with national linguistic preservation efforts.
One notable limitation of the AZERTY layout under this standard is its restricted direct access to programming symbolssuch as curly braces, square brackets, and the pipe characterwhich often require modifier keys or AltGr combinations, leading many software developers in France to temporarily switch to QWERTY configurations for coding efficiency.

National and Regional Variants

Belgian AZERTY Layout

The Belgian AZERTY layout serves as the primary keyboard standard in Belgium, building upon the French AZERTY arrangement while incorporating adaptations to reflect the country's multilingual environment, including influences from Dutch and Flemish usage. Key modifications include the euro symbol () accessible via AltGr + E, consistent with French AZERTY influences. Modern implementations often feature dedicated multimedia keys for functions like volume adjustment and media playback, enhancing usability in everyday computing tasks influenced by broader European standards.
Belgium employs distinct variants for its linguistic communities: the French-Belgian (fr-be) layout for Wallonia and bilingual Brussels, and the Dutch-Belgian (nl-be) layout for Flanders. These variants share the same core key mappings for efficiency but differ in keycap legendssuch as Dutch terms on modifiers in the nl-be versionand minor symbol adjustments to align with regional typing preferences.
Accent support in the Belgian AZERTY layout encompasses all standard French diacritics, including acute (), grave (), circumflex (), diaeresis (), and cedilla (), typically generated using dead keys or AltGr combinations for seamless input. It extends further with characters like (y with diaeresis) and (o with diaeresis), accessible via AltGr + y and AltGr + o respectively, to accommodate German-influenced dialects in eastern Belgium and cross-border linguistic needs.
This layout evolved from late-1980s adaptations of the French model to better support the nation's three official languages in bilingual professional and public settings.

Multilingual Adaptations

The AZERTY keyboard layout has been adapted for various non-French languages, particularly in regions with French colonial history or linguistic ties, to accommodate unique scripts, diacritics, and input directions while retaining core Latin character arrangements.
On Apple's macOS, the French AZERTY layout employs the Option key for efficient input of accented characters, such as pressing Option-e followed by a vowel for acute accents, and integrates access to emojis and symbols through the Character Viewer (invoked via Control-Command-Space). This system supports seamless typing in French and related languages without switching layouts.
In North African countries like Algeria, Morocco, and Tunisia, the Arabic (102) AZERTY variant combines Latin keys with Arabic script, enabling right-to-left text entry for Arabic while preserving AZERTY's base for French-Latin bilingual use; this layout is standardized in Windows for regional compatibility.
For the Tamazight (Berber) language in Algeria and Morocco, AZERTY-based extensions incorporate Tifinagh script characters, such as (representing "z" and a cultural symbol), via dedicated keys or modifier combinations, building on the French AZERTY foundation to support indigenous orthography.
Vietnamese adaptations of AZERTY, historically derived from typewriter designs, utilize dead keys to compose tones and diacritics, for example, a dead key for the breve accent to produce or the circumflex for , facilitating input of the language's complex vowel system.
In Senegal, Wolof adaptations extend AZERTY to include local Latin-script symbols like (eng), often via additional layers or remapped keys, reflecting French influence in the region; Windows provides a dedicated Wolof layout based on AZERTY for this purpose.
As of 2025, mobile implementations in French markets have seen enhancements, with iOS 18 enabling AZERTY as part of multilingual keyboard setups supporting up to three languages simultaneously, and Android's Gboard offering updated AZERTY French variants with improved glide typing and language switching for bilingual users.

Comparisons and Influences

Differences from QWERTY

The AZERTY keyboard layout primarily differs from the QWERTY layout in the arrangement of key letters to better suit French language input. In AZERTY, the top alphabetic row starts with A, Z, E, R, T, Y, reversing the positions of A and Q as well as Z and W compared to QWERTY's Q, W, E, R, T, Y sequence; specifically, Q occupies the position of A in QWERTY, while W takes Z's place.
From an ergonomic perspective, AZERTY optimizes key placements based on French letter frequencies, positioning more common French characters like Z (which appears about three times more often in French than in English) and A in prominent spots to reduce finger travel and fatigue for French typists. However, this adaptation disadvantages English users, as rarer letters like Z become easier to access while frequent ones like W and Q require more effort, potentially slowing typing speeds and increasing strain in non-French contexts. The 2019 NF Z71-300 standard further refines ergonomics through computational analysis of typing patterns.
Access to symbols in AZERTY is less intuitive than in QWERTY, particularly for punctuation and programming characters. Brackets ( { }) and the backslash () demand shift combinations or alternative keys, often located on less accessible positions like above numbers that require holding shift for numerals themselves, complicating rapid input.
AZERTY's regional focus on French-speaking countries contrasts with QWERTY's worldwide adoption as the de facto standard, limiting interoperability and contributing to its niche usage outside Europe. This disparity creates specific challenges for programmers, who frequently encounter difficulties with symbol placement during code writing, often leading them to remap keyboards or adopt QWERTY variants for international collaboration and tool compatibility.

Variations Across French and Belgian Versions

The French and Belgian AZERTY layouts, while sharing the core alphabetic arrangement optimized for Romance languages, diverge in symbol placement to accommodate national preferences and historical adaptations. In the French layout, the euro symbol () is accessed via AltGr + E, reflecting its integration with the letter e for efficient French typing, whereas in the Belgian variant, it is positioned on the 5 key (typically as AltGr + 5), aligning with broader European numeric conventions. Similarly, the less-than (<) and greater-than (>) symbols occupy different positions: in French keyboards, < is on the comma key with AltGr, and > is Shift + period, prioritizing French punctuation flow; Belgian layouts shift these to the key adjacent to the right Shift for compatibility with Dutch-influenced input.
Language support further highlights these variations, with the French AZERTYin its updated NF Z71-300 form from 2019emphasizing ligatures essential to standard orthography, such as (AltGr + O) and (AltGr + A), which facilitate direct entry of words like "uvre" or "ther" without dead-key sequences. In contrast, the Belgian AZERTY incorporates Dutch umlauts like and (via AltGr combinations on i and e keys, respectively), supporting Flemish bilingualism alongside French accents such as , to address Belgium's trilingual context without requiring layout switches. These adaptations stem from the Belgian layout's evolution in the 1980s to bridge French and Dutch needs, unlike the French standard's focus on monolingual efficiency as defined by AFNOR's NF Z71-300.
Hardware implementations reflect these linguistic priorities, with French keyboards typically monolingual and labeled solely in French characters to reinforce national linguistic norms. Belgian keyboards, however, are frequently bilingual, featuring both French AZERTY legends and English or Dutch overlays on the same keys, enabling seamless switching in multilingual environments like offices or public institutions.
In usage contexts, the French AZERTY dominates education and government sectors, where it is mandated in schools for teaching proper accentuation and adopted in official documents to uphold linguistic purity, as evidenced by the French Ministry of Culture's 2016 initiative to refine the layout for better orthographic accuracy. Belgian AZERTY, by comparison, prevails in EU multilingual settings, such as Brussels-based institutions, where its inclusive character set aids cross-lingual communication in supranational bodies like the European Parliament.
