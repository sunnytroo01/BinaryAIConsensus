ddrescue
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 GNU ddrescue is a free software data recovery tool that copies data from one file or block device, such as a hard disk or CD-ROM, to another, prioritizing the rescue of readable sectors first in the event of read errors to maximize data recovery from damaged media.
Developed by Antonio Daz Daz in the summer of 2004 to address the limitations of existing tools like the Unix dd command, which could exacerbate damage to failing drives through inefficient seeking, GNU ddrescue was initially created to recover data from a personal failing CD-ROM and has since become a standard utility in the GNU Project. It operates fully automatically, using a mapfile to track progress, avoid re-reading successfully rescued data, and enable resumption of interrupted operations, making it suitable for both novice users cloning failing drives and experts handling complex recoveries.
Key features include support for merging data from multiple partial copies of damaged media into a single complete file, a "fill mode" for selectively overwriting portions of the output with specified values (useful for marking bad sectors), and compatibility with any device supported by the operating system's kernel, without reliance on specific interfaces. The tool is distributed under the GNU General Public License version 2 or later, ensuring its freedom for modification and redistribution, and its latest version, 1.29.1, was released on March 21, 2025. Accompanying utilities like ddrescuelog allow manipulation of mapfiles, while integration with compression tools such as lzip enhances long-term data archiving and recovery workflows.

Introduction

Purpose and Capabilities

GNU ddrescue is a command-line data recovery tool developed as part of the GNU Project, designed to copy raw data blocks from failing block devicessuch as hard disks, CDs, DVDs, and USB drivesto another device or file. Its primary purpose is to facilitate the recovery of data from damaged storage media by employing an efficient copying strategy that prioritizes accessible sectors. Written in C++, ddrescue operates on Unix-like systems and plays a crucial role in minimizing data loss during physical media failures by avoiding prolonged stress on malfunctioning hardware.
A core capability of ddrescue is its ability to read readable portions of the source device first, temporarily skipping problematic areas to prevent further degradation of the failing media. This approach allows for the systematic rescue of intact data before attempting recovery from error-prone sections. One of its key strengths is that it handles any kernel-supported block device without dependencies on specific interfaces, making it versatile for various hardware types including ATA, SATA, SCSI, floppy disks, and flash media.
Unlike basic copying utilities such as dd, which may halt or cause additional damage when encountering read errors, ddrescue implements an intelligent strategy that enhances recovery rates from partially damaged media. By focusing on non-destructive data extraction, it enables users to salvage a greater proportion of files and structures from compromised drives, often serving as a foundational step in broader forensic or archival recovery efforts.

Development History

ddrescue was developed by Antonio Daz Daz to address the shortcomings of existing data recovery tools, particularly in handling failing storage media like CD-ROMs, where traditional utilities such as GNU dd often failed to skip bad sectors efficiently. The project originated in the summer of 2004, with its initial release on August 12, 2004, marking the beginning of its availability as open-source software. From its inception, ddrescue has been part of the GNU Project, hosted on GNU's Savannah forge and distributed through official GNU FTP mirrors.
The tool's licensing began under the GNU General Public License version 2 or later (GPL-2.0-or-later) upon its initial release in 2004. In June 2007, with the release of version 1.5 on June 30, 2007, the license was updated to GPL-3.0-or-later to align with the newly introduced GPL version 3. This change remained in effect until October 2014, when version 1.19, released on October 6, 2014, reverted to GPL-2.0-or-later for improved compatibility with a broader range of software ecosystems. Antonio Daz Daz has maintained sole authorship and development responsibility throughout, ensuring consistent updates and refinements.
Key milestones include the first official GNU announcement with version 1.0 on June 7, 2005, which introduced features like multi-part rescue support via a new logfile format. Over the years, ddrescue evolved into a standard tool in open-source data recovery, praised for its robustness in copying data from damaged block devices while minimizing further harm. The most recent stable version, 1.29.1, was released on March 21, 2025, incorporating enhancements such as improved handling of bad sector data. As of October 22, 2025, development continues actively under the GNU Project, with source code hosted on GNU servers and new versions announced via the bug-ddrescue mailing list.

Technical Features

Rescue Algorithm

The ddrescue rescue algorithm employs a structured, multi-phase strategy to copy data from failing storage devices, emphasizing the extraction of reliable sectors while deferring intensive efforts on problematic areas to minimize further degradation of the source media. This approach contrasts with linear copying methods by using adaptive block sizes and a non-sequential order, allowing ddrescue to prioritize accessible data and achieve efficient recovery even from heavily damaged drives.
The process unfolds in four distinct phases, each tailored to handle errors at increasing levels of granularity. In the initial copying phase, ddrescue rapidly reads large contiguous blocks of untried, good data, defaulting to a cluster size of 128 sectors (64 KiB for standard 512-byte sectors) to maximize throughput. Upon encountering read errors or slow areas, it skips ahead, marking the failed blocks as non-trimmed and non-scraped, then reverses direction for subsequent passesup to five in totalto cover the input efficiently without dwelling on faults. This phase focuses on bulk transfer of healthy regions, leaving damaged sections for later attention.
The trimming phase then targets the edges of those skipped bad blocks, retrying them sector by sector to delineate precise boundaries between recoverable and irrecoverable areas. By splitting oversized error zones into smaller segments, this step refines the map of the input device, isolating potential salvageable data at the fringes and preparing for deeper recovery efforts. Trimming proceeds in a forward pass, adapting block sizes downward as needed to pinpoint issues without redundant reads on confirmed good sectors.
Subsequently, the scraping phase addresses the remaining small non-scraped blocks, attempting to retrieve data one sector at a time in a single forward pass. Here, ddrescue employs direct I/O access when specified to circumvent kernel caching, which can otherwise slow operations on erratic media, and further reduces read sizes to the minimum (one sector) for stubborn errors. This meticulous retry mechanism salvages fragments that broader passes might overlook, enhancing overall yield.
An optional retrying phase concludes the algorithm if enabled, reattempting the persistent bad sectors with configurable parameters such as retry counts or reversed reading direction across multiple passes. This final effort applies intensified scrutiny to the hardest-to-recover areas, potentially with adjusted timeouts or skip sizes, but is disabled by default to avoid excessive wear unless explicitly invoked.
Central to the algorithm's effectiveness is its dynamic block-size adaptation: reads commence at optimal large sizes for speed on intact media and systematically shrinkhalving or reducing to one sector upon errorsto isolate and resolve issues without wasting time on verified regions. This granularity ensures minimal overhead while maximizing precision. Complementing this is the non-linear copying order, which skips faulty or sluggish sectors early on, rescues the bulk of accessible data first, and circles back only as necessary; such deferral not only accelerates progress but also curtails repeated stressing of vulnerable hardware components.
Since version 1.29.1 (March 2025), the --bad-sector-data=<file> option allows ddrescue to treat any sector whose contents match the specified file as a read error. This is particularly useful for drives that return predictable garbage data instead of properly signaling read failures.
Since version 1.30 (January 2026), the rescue algorithm has been significantly improved, providing orders of magnitude better performance in automatic recovery from drives with dead heads. Additionally, the new --no-sweep option has been introduced, allowing users to disable the reading of skipped areas.
Overall, the algorithm is engineered to optimize recovery rates by methodically escalating from coarse to fine-grained operations, often succeeding on damaged drives where simplistic tools stall or compound harm. Progress is quantified through output metrics like "pct rescued," indicating the copied data proportion, which underscores ddrescue's capacity for near-complete salvage in challenging scenarios. The mapfile supports this by logging phase statuses for resumable sessions.

Mapfile and Resuming

The mapfile in GNU ddrescue is a plain-text file that records the progress and status of data recovery domains, enabling precise tracking and resumable operations. It consists of three main parts: optional heading comments providing metadata such as the ddrescue version, command line used, timestamps, and a status message; a status line indicating the current position in hexadecimal, the status character of that position, and the current pass number; and a list of contiguous, non-overlapping data blocks (domains) each described by its starting position, size in bytes, and status character.
Each domain's status is denoted by a specific character: '?' for non-tried blocks that have not yet been attempted, '+' for finished blocks successfully read and written, '-' for bad-sector blocks that could not be read after retries, '*' for non-trimmed failed blocks identified in the trimming phase, and '/' for non-scraped failed blocks from the scraping phase. These statuses allow ddrescue to distinguish between successfully recovered data, problematic areas requiring further effort, and untouched regions.
The mapfile is created automatically if omitted from the command line, but specifying it explicitly as the third positional argument is essential for operations longer than a single session, as it prevents re-reading good sectors and ensures no data duplication or loss upon interruption. When resuming a rescue, ddrescue parses the mapfile to skip finished domains ('+'), focus on non-tried or failed ones, and continue from the position recorded in the status line, thereby supporting multi-session recovery where progress from prior runs is merged seamlessly. This mechanism integrates with ddrescue's phased rescue algorithm by updating domain statuses as blocks progress through copying, trimming, scraping, and retrying.
Due to the potentially large number of domains on failing media, the mapfile's size grows proportionally to the input device's capacity and fragmentation level, though its repetitive text structure makes it highly compressible for storage or backup. The -O option, which reopens the input file after errors, further aids resumability by allowing recovery to proceed without permanent halts from transient issues.

Fill Mode

Fill mode in ddrescue enables targeted overwriting of specific domains in the output file, reversing the typical data recovery process by writing patterns to selected areas without attempting to read from the failing input device. This mode is activated using the --fill-mode=types option, where types specifies the status characters from the mapfilesuch as - for bad sectors, ? for non-tried areas, or + for finished domainsto identify which blocks to overwrite. The mapfile, which tracks recovery progress, serves as the input for selecting these domains, allowing ddrescue to operate only on the specified bad or untried areas while updating the mapfile for resumability.
In operation, ddrescue reads the fill pattern from the input fileoften a small source like /dev/zero for zeros or a file containing random dataand writes it to the designated output blocks, implying the --complete-only behavior to skip already finished areas. An optional l suffix in the types (e.g., l-) can be used to embed location data such as sector positions into the filled blocks for later identification. While ddrescue lacks built-in support for multiple passes, users can script repeated invocations with varying patterns to achieve secure wiping, such as overwriting bad sectors multiple times with zeros, ones, or random data.
This functionality supports several practical applications, including securely wiping recovered data on the output file by targeting good sectors with erasure patterns, or marking non-recovered areassuch as bad sectorswith unique signatures to facilitate post-processing and identification of corrupted files upon mounting the image. For instance, filling gaps in a partially recovered image with zeros or a non-native string allows repairing the file structure for further analysis, while in forensics contexts, it aids in preparing disk images for safe examination by overwriting sensitive or damaged regions without risking further reads from the source. Introduced in version 1.6 of ddrescue in November 2007, fill mode has become essential for such targeted post-recovery operations.

Usage Guide

Installation and Setup

ddrescue can be obtained either by downloading the source code from the official GNU repository or through package managers on supported systems. The latest version is available as a compressed tarball in .lzip format from http://ftpmirror.gnu.org/ddrescue/, such as ddrescue-1.29.1.tar.lz, which requires the lzip utility for decompression.
To compile from source, first decompress the tarball using lzip, then follow the standard GNU build process: run ./configure to generate the makefile (a C++ compiler like g++ is required, with minimal additional dependencies), followed by make to build the executable, and sudo make install to install it system-wide.
On Linux distributions, ddrescue is included in most major repositories since its initial release in 2004 and can be installed directly via package managers. For Debian and Ubuntu-based systems, use sudo apt install gddrescue. On Fedora and related distributions, the command is sudo dnf install ddrescue. For FreeBSD, it is available through the ports collection or as pkg install ddrescue. On macOS, ddrescue can be installed using Homebrew with brew install ddrescue or MacPorts with sudo port install ddrescue.
Windows users can run ddrescue via compatibility layers such as Cygwin or Windows Subsystem for Linux (WSL), as there is no native Windows binary; compilation within these environments follows the same source-based process.
After installation, verify the setup by running ddrescue --version, which displays the installed version (e.g., 1.29.1). Operations involving block devices typically require root privileges, executed with sudo to access hardware directly.

Basic Recovery Process

The basic recovery process with ddrescue involves cloning data from a failing input device, such as a hard disk, to a safe output file or device, while minimizing further damage to the source. Users begin by identifying the input device (e.g., /dev/sda) and preparing an output target (e.g., rescue.img), ensuring the output is on a separate, healthy storage medium with sufficient spaceat least as large as the input, and preferably larger to accommodate any expansions. A mapfile is then specified to track progress, enabling interruption and resumption without data loss.
To initiate recovery, the command ddrescue input output mapfile is executed, where input is the failing device, output is the destination, and mapfile records copied, unfinished, and bad sectors. For enhanced reliability, options like -d (direct I/O to bypass caching) and -r3 (retry bad sectors up to three times) are commonly added, as in the example: ddrescue -d -r3 /dev/sdX rescue.img mapfile. This starts the process, with ddrescue automatically skipping unreadable areas initially to prioritize accessible data. During execution, the tool proceeds through phases that focus on copying good sectors first before attempting repairs on problematic ones.
Progress can be monitored using the --verbose option (-v), which displays real-time statistics such as rescued size, error rates, and estimated remaining time. If the process is interrupted (e.g., via Ctrl+C), ddrescue safely updates the mapfile, allowing resumption by rerunning the same command; this ensures no re-copying of already recovered data. Best practices include connecting the source device in read-only mode if possible to prevent accidental writes, unmounting any partitions on the input to avoid interference, and using a dedicated output device to isolate the recovery. The first pass emphasizes capturing intact data quickly, deferring intensive retries.
For a typical 1TB drive, recovery runtime varies from several hours for minimally damaged media to days for severely failing ones, influenced by error density and hardware speed. Success is evaluated through the mapfile's status, which details percentages of rescued, non-tried, and bad sectors, often confirming near-complete recovery (e.g., over 99% rescued) before filesystem repair tools are applied to the output.

On macOS

For users on macOS, the basic recovery process requires adaptations to account for the system's disk management tools and performance optimizations. The following steps outline a direct cloning procedure using ddrescue:

Connect the source (failing) and target (healthy) drives to the Mac, ensuring they are recognized by the system.

Prevent the system from sleeping during the potentially lengthy operation by running caffeinate -i in the Terminal or using a third-party application such as Amphetamine.

Identify the source and target disks using diskutil list; for example, the source might appear as /dev/disk4 and the target as /dev/disk6.

Unmount the target disk to prepare it for writing with diskutil unmountDisk force /dev/disk6.

Execute the ddrescue command with elevated privileges: sudo ddrescue -f -r3 /dev/rdisk4 /dev/rdisk6 ~/clone.log. Here, /dev/rdisk devices are used instead of /dev/disk for significantly improved read/write speeds by bypassing the buffer cache; the -f option forces overwriting the target, -r3 retries bad sectors up to three times, and the mapfile (clone.log) tracks progress.

Monitor the command output for statistics on rescued bytes, errors, and progress; if interrupted, resume by rerunning the same command, as ddrescue will reference the mapfile to avoid reprocessing completed areas.

Upon completion, safely eject the source disk with diskutil eject /dev/disk4 and store it securely to prevent further damage.

These steps align with general ddrescue practices but incorporate macOS-specific commands for disk handling and performance.

Advanced Command Options

ddrescue provides a range of advanced command-line options to fine-tune the data recovery process for complex scenarios, such as damaged optical media, slow-reading devices, or non-standard sector sizes. These options allow users to optimize performance, handle specific error conditions, and adapt to various storage types while interacting with the mapfile to track progress and enable resumable operations.
The -d or --direct option enables direct disc access for the input file, bypassing the kernel's buffer cache to improve efficiency on large devices, though it requires specifying the correct sector size and is not supported on all systems. Similarly, --idirect and --odirect apply the O_DIRECT flag specifically to input or output, respectively, ensuring aligned I/O operations. For output handling, the -S or --sparse option performs sparse writes, which skip allocation and writing of zero blocks to regular files, provided the output is empty or fully zeroed, thus saving time and space. The --reverse option inverts the direction of all reading passes, useful for media where errors are concentrated at the end, without altering the block size.
Error management is enhanced through options like -r N or --retry-passes=N, which specifies the number of additional passes to retry bad sectors (default 0, or -1 for unlimited), reversing direction each pass to attempt recovery from different angles. The --no-scrape option disables the final scraping phase, avoiding prolonged attempts on particularly stubborn areas to prevent excessive wear. To control timeouts and rates, --timeout=SEC sets the maximum interval since the last successful read before exiting (default infinity, e.g., 1.5s or 1m), while --max-error-rate=KB/s aborts the process if the error rate exceeds the specified bytes per second (default infinity). Additionally, --min-read-rate=KB/s skips areas reading slower than the given rate during the first two passes, prioritizing faster sections.
For specialized media, the -C CLUSTER or --cluster-size=CLUSTER option defines the number of sectors to read at once (default 64 KiB divided by sector size), optimizing for clustered reads on optical discs or similar. The -b bytes or --sector-size=bytes option sets the logical sector size (default 512 bytes), crucial for devices with non-standard sizes and for enabling features like direct access or subsector joining in the mapfile. These options update the mapfile to reflect statuses such as skipped or errored regions, allowing precise resumption in subsequent runs.

Comparisons

With GNU dd

GNU dd is a standard Unix utility designed for low-level copying and conversion of data between block devices or files, operating sequentially from beginning to end without inherent mechanisms for handling read errors beyond basic options. It typically halts upon encountering a read error or risks corrupting the output unless modified with parameters like conv=noerror,sync, which allow continuation but fill unreadable sectors with zero bytes, thereby altering the original data layout and potentially reducing recovery accuracy.
In contrast, GNU ddrescue employs a sophisticated rescue algorithm that prioritizes copying accessible data first by skipping bad or slow sectors, then intelligently retrying problematic areas in multiple passes to maximize recovery from failing drives. This approach minimizes further damage to the source device, unlike dd's fixed sequential read, which can exacerbate wear on failing hardware by repeatedly attempting the same errors without adaptation. ddrescue's use of a mapfile enables non-destructive multi-pass operations and seamless resuming after interruptions, features absent in dd that would require manual scripting or restarts from the beginning, often leading to incomplete recoveries.
A key difference lies in error mitigation: while dd's conv=noerror,sync option continues past errors by padding gaps with zeroseffectively losing the precise original contentddrescue preserves the exact data layout by marking unrecovered areas in the mapfile and filling gaps only on explicit request, ensuring the output remains as faithful as possible to the source. Furthermore, ddrescue supports adaptive block sizing, dynamically adjusting read sizes (e.g., starting large for good areas and shrinking to sector-level for bad ones), compared to dd's reliance on a fixed block size parameter (bs=), which lacks flexibility for optimizing recovery from irregular media errors.
dd is best suited for cloning healthy media where errors are unlikely, whereas ddrescue's targeted capabilities make it superior for damaged drives, often enabling higher yields through its phased retry strategy (copying, trimming, scraping) as outlined in its rescue algorithm.

With dd_rescue

dd_rescue is a distinct data recovery and data protection tool developed by Kurt Garloff, first released in 1999. Like the Unix dd command, it copies data from one file or block device to another, supporting features such as seeking and skipping positions, while handling errors by skipping bad blocks and allowing partial reads. It emphasizes data transformation through plugins for tasks like encryption (ddr_crypt) and compression (ddr_lzo), as well as secure deletion by overwriting files, partitions, or disks multiple times with pseudo-random numbers generated efficiently. The tool is hosted on SourceForge and remains actively maintained, with its latest release (version 1.99.21) dated March 14, 2025.
In comparison to GNU ddrescue, dd_rescue serves broader purposes including copying and wiping, but it is less optimized for recovering data from failing drives with extensive bad sectors. GNU ddrescue prioritizes robust error recovery from damaged media by skipping problematic areas, retrying reads with configurable attempts, and employing a sophisticated multi-phase algorithm: an initial forward pass to copy accessible blocks, a trim phase to fill gaps around errors from both directions, and a scrape phase to attempt recovery of remaining bad sectors with reduced speed. dd_rescue, by contrast, follows a linear sequential approach, progressively reducing block sizes upon errors without these specialized phases, making it simpler but potentially less effective for complex recoveries.
A key distinction lies in progress tracking: GNU ddrescue utilizes an advanced mapfile (logfile) to record copied, non-tried, and bad sectors, enabling precise resumption and avoidance of re-reading successful areas, whereas dd_rescue supports basic partial reads for interruption handling but lacks this comprehensive mapping mechanism. GNU ddrescue benefits from ongoing development with updates as recent as 2025, incorporating enhancements like improved performance on modern hardware, while dd_rescue's design positions it as particularly suitable for data sanitization tasks over intensive error-prone recoveries. Both tools operate on block-level copying, but GNU ddrescue's phased strategy and mapfile provide superior resilience for damaged storage media.
