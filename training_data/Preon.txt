 In particle physics, preons are hypothetical point-like fermions proposed as the fundamental constituents of quarks and leptons, the building blocks of matter in the Standard Model, aiming to provide a deeper layer of compositeness beyond current elementary particles. Introduced in the late 1970s, these models seek to unify the diverse charges, flavors, and generations of quarks and leptons through combinations of a small number of preon types, typically two or three, bound by new interactions at energy scales far above those accessible today.
The most prominent early preon model, known as the rishon model, was developed by Haim Harari in 1979, positing two spin-1/2 preons called "rishons"one with electric charge +1/3 (T) and one neutral (V)from which all quarks and leptons are constructed as bound states of three rishons, explaining phenomena like flavor mixing and family replication without invoking supersymmetry or grand unification at the preon level. A similar scheme by Michael Shupe independently proposed three preons to account for the same particles, including a charged lepton from a triplet combination. Subsequent variants, such as those incorporating supersymmetry or additional preon flavors, have explored connections to grand unified theories and cosmology, but all share the core idea of reducing the Standard Model's 48 elementary fermions (including antiparticles) to fewer preonic entities.
Despite theoretical appeal, preon models remain unverified and are increasingly constrained by experimental data, with no evidence for quark or lepton substructure observed in high-energy collisions. Collider experiments at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) have set lower limits on the compositeness scale for quarks above 2040 TeV, depending on the model and channel, implying that if preons exist, their binding interactions must occur at energies inaccessible to current accelerators. Similarly, lepton compositeness limits exceed 30 TeV in some cases, ruling out low-scale preon scenarios and shifting focus to potential indirect signatures in rare decays or cosmic observations, though mainstream particle physics views preons as speculative extensions without empirical support.

Introduction

Definition and Basic Concepts

Preons are hypothetical point-like particles proposed in particle physics as the fundamental subcomponents of quarks and leptons, with some models extending their role to the composition of bosons as well. This concept seeks to address the proliferation of elementary particles in the Standard Model by positing that the 12 fermion types (six quarks and six leptons) and associated bosons arise from combinations of a smaller set of preons, thereby reducing the inventory of truly fundamental entities from dozens to just a few building blocks. The term "preon" was coined by Jogesh C. Pati and Abdus Salam in their 1974 paper introducing lepton number as a fourth color quantum number, distinguishing these entities from other proposed substructures like cosmic strings.
At the core of preon models is the ambition to construct a "periodic table" for subatomic particles, analogous to the chemical elements formed from protons, neutrons, and electrons bound by electromagnetic forces. In this framework, preons would be confined within quarks and leptons by novel binding interactions, such as hypercolor forces, operating at energy scales far beyond those of quantum chromodynamics, corresponding to distances below 101810^{-18}1018 metersroughly a thousandth of a proton's diameter. These models predict that the observed spectrum of particles emerges from bound states of preons, mirroring how atomic nuclei and atoms form a diverse yet systematic array from simpler constituents.
Preons are typically classified as fermions possessing spin $ \frac{1}{2} $, endowing the composite particles with the necessary half-integer spins observed in the Standard Model. They are theorized to carry fractional electric charges, such as $ \pm \frac{1}{3} e $ or $ \pm \frac{1}{9} e $ (where $ e $ is the elementary charge), and exotic color-like quantum numbers that are not manifest at the quark-lepton level but facilitate the binding and replication of particle generations. This fractional structure allows preons to combine into integer-charged quarks and leptons while introducing a deeper layer of unification absent in the Standard Model.

Historical Development

The concept of preons emerged in the mid-1970s amid efforts to extend the Standard Model by positing substructure for quarks and leptons, drawing inspiration from grand unified theories (GUTs) that sought to unify fundamental forces. The term "preon" was coined by Jogesh Pati and Abdus Salam in their 1974 proposal for a gauged lepton number model within an SU(4) SU(2)_L SU(2)_R GUT framework, where preons served as basic constituents carrying both color and lepton number charges. This idea built on earlier GUT developments, such as Georgi and Glashow's SU(5) model, by addressing the proliferation of fermion generations and symmetries at high energies.
Interest in preon models surged during the late 1970s and 1980s, fueled by parallel advances in dynamical symmetry breaking theories like technicolor, which demonstrated how strong interactions could generate masses without elementary scalars and influenced preon frameworks for fermion compositeness. In 1979, Haim Harari introduced the rishon model, proposing two spin-1/2 preons (T and V) bound by a new "metacolor" force to form quarks and leptons while explaining charge and color quantization. This was followed by other notable contributions, including Fritzsch and Mandelbaum's 1981 fermion-scalar preon model and Pati and Salam's exploration of supersymmetry at the preon level in 1982, aiming to stabilize hierarchies and incorporate generations. Key events included dedicated symposia, such as the 1981 Workshop on Composite Models of Quarks and Leptons at Fermilab, and discussions at the 1984 International Conference on High Energy Physics, where preon ideas were debated alongside experimental constraints from accelerators.
By the 1990s, enthusiasm for preon models waned as precision electroweak measurements at facilities like LEP and SLC confirmed the Standard Model's predictions with no evidence for compositeness, while deep inelastic scattering experiments probed quarks and leptons as point-like down to scales of ~10^{-18} m, far smaller than typical preon binding lengths would allow. These results, combined with challenges in dynamically generating light fermion masses and avoiding excessive flavor-changing neutral currents, shifted focus toward alternatives like supersymmetry, leaving preon theories as a marginalized but occasionally revisited paradigm in beyond-Standard-Model physics.

Theoretical Background

Limitations of the Standard Model

The Standard Model of particle physics describes the fundamental particles and three of the four known interactionsstrong, weak, and electromagneticbut leaves several structural gaps unexplained. One prominent issue is the arbitrary structure of its fermion sector, which posits 12 types of fundamental fermions arranged in three generationstwo quarks, one charged lepton, and one neutrino per generationwith the neutrinos massless in the original formulation. This replication lacks a deeper theoretical justification, as the model treats the number of generations as an input parameter without predicting why there are exactly three rather than more or fewer.
A related concern is the hierarchy problem, which questions the vast disparity between the electroweak scale, around 100 GeV where the Higgs field acquires its vacuum expectation value, and the Planck scale of quantum gravity, approximately 10^{19} GeV. Quantum corrections from high-energy physics would naturally drive the Higgs mass toward the Planck scale unless the bare parameters are finely tuned to an extraordinary degreeon the order of 1 part in 10^{32}to maintain the observed weak scale, rendering the theory aesthetically unappealing and unstable under renormalization.
The Standard Model also fails to achieve full unification of the fundamental forces. While the electromagnetic and weak interactions are unified into the electroweak force at energies around 100 GeV, the strong force remains separate, and the three gauge couplings do not converge at a single scale without additional physics. Moreover, gravity is entirely excluded from the framework, as general relativity is incompatible with the quantum field theory structure of the model at high energies, preventing a complete description of all interactions.
The flavor problem further highlights these explanatory shortcomings, as the model provides no mechanism for the existence of three fermion generations or the hierarchical differences in their masses and mixing patterns. The Yukawa couplings, which generate fermion masses via the Higgs mechanism, introduce numerous arbitrary parameters without underlying symmetry principles to constrain them. The discovery of neutrino oscillations in 1998 necessitated ad hoc extensions, such as adding right-handed neutrinos and Dirac or Majorana mass terms, to accommodate nonzero neutrino masses, further complicating the flavor sector.

Motivations for Preon Models

Preon models seek to address the proliferation of fundamental particles in the Standard Model by positing that quarks and leptons are composite structures built from a small number of more basic constituents, typically 3 to 6 types of preons. This approach mirrors the historical success of the quark model in reducing the diverse hadron "zoo" to a manageable set of three quark flavors bound by quantum chromodynamics (QCD), thereby simplifying the ontology of particle physics. In preon frameworks, all 12 Standard Model fermions (six quarks and six leptons across three generations) emerge as bound states of these preons, enabling a conceptual "periodic table" of generations analogous to atomic elements, where combinations dictate properties like charge and flavor.
A key motivation is the natural generation of fermion masses through preon binding dynamics, such as confinement mediated by a new gauge interaction at very high energy scales, well above those probed by current accelerators, obviating the need for the 13 arbitrary Yukawa coupling parameters in the Standard Model Higgs mechanism. In these models, mass hierarchies arise from the stability and binding energies of composite states, with lighter leptons potentially resulting from weakly bound preons compared to the stronger bindings in quarks. This dynamical mass generation parallels the spontaneous symmetry breaking in QCD but extends it to explain why fermions acquire masses without fine-tuning, providing a more unified treatment of the mass spectrum.
Preons also offer potential for unifying the quantum numbers of the Standard Modelcolor, weak isospin, and hyperchargeby assigning these charges directly to the preons themselves, allowing the familiar SU(3)_c SU(2)_L U(1)_Y gauge structure to emerge as an effective low-energy description from a higher symmetry broken at the preon scale. For instance, preons could carry fractional values of these charges such that their combinations reproduce the integer or fractional charges of quarks and leptons, facilitating grand unification where all forces converge at energies above the preon binding scale. This unification resolves the ad hoc assignment of charges in the Standard Model by deriving them from fewer fundamental entities.
The replication of three fermion generations, an unexplained feature in the Standard Model, finds a dynamical explanation in preon models through varying binding configurations or excitations of preon composites, avoiding the need for multiple identical copies introduced by hand. Different combinations of the same preons can yield the sequential generations, with increasing complexity or energy levels corresponding to heavier particles like the top quark, thus providing a geometric or topological basis for the observed family structure.
Underlying these technical motivations is a philosophical commitment to simplicity and economy in fundamental physics, akin to how the quark model supplanted earlier hadron theories by reducing postulates and enhancing predictive power. Preon models embody Occam's razor by minimizing the number of elementary entities, potentially paving the way for a deeper theory that integrates gravity or other forces, much like how QCD's success inspired beyond-Standard-Model pursuits.

Preon Models

Rishon Model

The Rishon model, proposed by Haim Harari in 1979, posits that quarks and leptons are composite structures formed from three types of fundamental spin-1/2 preons known as rishons: T (truth) with electric charge $ Q = +1/3 $, V (vein) with $ Q = 0 $, and E (end) with $ Q = -1/3 $, where E functions as the antiparticle counterpart to T in terms of charge. These rishons carry an additional "metacolor" quantum number under an SU(3) hypercolor gauge group, analogous to the color charge in quantum chromodynamics (QCD), which confines them into bound states. Rishons and their antirishons (T-bar = E, V-bar) form the building blocks, with quarks and leptons emerging as color-singlet or metacolor-singlet triplets to ensure stability at low energies. The model aims to explain charge quantization and generational replication without introducing new fundamental scalars.

The binding mechanism relies on a strong metacolor force mediated by hypergluons, confining rishons at scales around $ 10^{-3} $ to $ 10^{-2} $ times the inverse proton mass, preventing observation of free rishons.
The model predicts the structure of three generations of fermions through successive bindings or excited metacolor states of the same rishons, yielding six quark flavors and six leptons (three charged leptons and three neutrinos) without ad hoc parameters. For instance, the second generation (charm, strange, muon, muon neutrino) arises from higher vibrational modes of the TTE, TEE, EEE, and VVV configurations, while the third follows similarly. Neutral bound states involving the V rishon, such as di-V or tri-V systems beyond the neutrino, have been proposed as stable, weakly interacting candidates for dark matter due to their zero electric charge and potential longevity under metacolor confinement.
Extensions in the 1980s incorporated weak interactions by treating W and Z bosons as metacolor composites (e.g., W as T T T V V V), naturally generating parity violation through V-V-bar mixing and predicting $ \sin^2 \theta_W = 1/4 $ for the Weinberg angle, approximately consistent with early electroweak data. These variants maintained anomaly cancellation and fermion number conservation at low energies while allowing baryon-lepton number violation at high scales.

Other Notable Models

The term "preon" was coined by Jogesh Pati and Abdus Salam in 1974 in the context of their grand unified theory based on the gauge group SU(4) SU(2)_L SU(2)_R, which unifies quarks and leptons by treating leptons as a fourth "color." Subsequent preon models have adopted this structure, positing preons as fundamental constituents transforming under this group to generate the observed fermion spectrum without additional elementary fields.
A similar early model to the rishon scheme was independently proposed by Michael Shupe in 1979, using three preon types with charges +1/3, 0, and -1/3 to compose quarks and leptons as triplets, including the charged lepton from three negatively charged preons, and explaining the particle spectrum through bound states under a confining force.
In 1981, Pierre Ramond proposed a preon model interpreting the U(1) U(1) binding forces as arising from hidden electric and magnetic type charges, providing a framework for composing quarks and leptons with a reduced number of preon types and focusing on bilinear preon condensates to replicate standard model generations while minimizing unobserved particles.
Technipreons, explored in hybrid models during the 1990s, integrate preon substructure with technicolor dynamics to achieve electroweak symmetry breaking through strong binding forces among preons rather than a fundamental Higgs scalar. These frameworks posit that preons carry technicolor charges, leading to composite technifermions that generate the W and Z boson masses via chiral symmetry breaking, while also addressing fermion mass hierarchies through extended interactions.
A non-standard approach appeared in 2025 with the braid topology model, which employs ribbon twisting to bind preons and represents SU(3) color confinement through knotted structures in a discrete spacetime lattice. This topological formulation maps preon composites to braided diagrams, providing a geometric basis for fermion representations and potentially resolving confinement without traditional gauge dynamics.
Also in 2025, the octonionic preon model leverages octonion algebra to describe spin-exchange interactions among preons, offering an alternative mechanism to the Higgs for generating W and Z boson masses through non-associative algebraic bindings. By encoding particle states as octonionic operators, the model predicts mass ratios aligned with observations and connects preon dynamics to higher-dimensional geometries without invoking elementary scalars.

Implications and Predictions

Composite Higgs Boson

In some preon models that incorporate Higgs compositeness, the Higgs boson emerges as a bound state of preons, often referred to as technipreons, where its mass originates from the condensation of these fundamental constituents at an energy scale on the order of the TeV. This compositeness resolves the naturalness issue of the Standard Model by replacing the elementary scalar with a dynamically generated state, avoiding fine-tuning in the Higgs mass parameter.
Electroweak symmetry breaking in these frameworks proceeds through dynamical mechanisms driven by strong preon interactions, mirroring the formation of light pion bound states in quantum chromodynamics (QCD). Preon condensates form at high energies, spontaneously breaking the underlying global symmetry and generating masses for the electroweak gauge bosons without invoking an ad hoc elementary Higgs field. The complementarity principle ensures consistency between the perturbative description with preon fields and the non-perturbative composite phase.
Such models predict modifications to Standard Model Higgs phenomenology, including enhanced couplings to the top quark arising from the partial compositeness of fermions and the Higgs itself, which can alter branching ratios in decays like $ H \to t\bar{t} $. Additionally, preon resonances may manifest as Higgs partner states, such as scalar or pseudoscalar particles, detectable in high-energy collisions at scales above the electroweak vacuum expectation value.
The historical development of composite Higgs ideas within preon models dates to the 1980s, spurred by efforts to mitigate the hierarchy problem through substructure beyond quarks and leptons, with early explorations emphasizing low compositeness scales and chiral symmetries preserved in the infrared.

New Particles and Phenomena

Preon models predict a variety of exotic particles arising from the composite nature of quarks and leptons, including excited states formed by preon bindings. For instance, in the rishon model, bound states of rishons and antirishons, analogous to positronium atoms and termed rishonium, are expected to exist as neutral composites with zero charge, baryon number, lepton number, and rishon number. These states could manifest as short-lived resonances in high-energy collisions, potentially observable if the preon binding scale allows excitation.
Leptoquarks, hypothetical bosons that couple quarks to leptons, emerge naturally in several preon frameworks as bound states of preons carrying both baryon and lepton quantum numbers. Such particles are predicted to have masses around the compositeness scale, with signatures including single-lepton plus jet events in electron-proton collisions, as explored in left-right symmetric preon models interpreting anomalies at HERA. Heavy vector bosons, arising from metacolor gauge interactions in preon theories, are also anticipated; the preon trinity model, for example, originally forecasted additional Z' and W' bosons with masses in the hundreds of GeV, potentially detectable at colliders like the LHC through dilepton or lepton-plus-missing-energy channels, though such low masses are now excluded by experimental data as of 2024.
Preon compositeness introduces observable phenomena at high energies, such as deviations from Standard Model predictions in deep inelastic scattering at momentum transfers Q exceeding several times 10 GeV (corresponding to current lower limits on the compositeness scale above 10 TeV), though no such effects have been observed to date. Rare decays, including flavor-changing neutral current processes like K_L e, may be enhanced due to preon-mediated mixing, though rates depend on the binding dynamics.
Implications for the CKM matrix arise from family mixing mechanisms involving ortho-para states of preons, where spin alignments of preon pairs generate intergenerational transitions and predict additional sources of CP violation beyond the Standard Model phase. These could manifest in enhanced asymmetries in B meson decays or electric dipole moments.
Neutral preons or their bound states are proposed as dark matter candidates, functioning as weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs) through nonperturbative condensates that produce pseudogoldstone bosons like familons with masses in the eV to keV range, consistent with cosmological relic densities. Overall, these models anticipate new physics emerging at energy scales of 1100 TeV, accessible via future colliders, where preon excitations and interactions would reveal compositeness. However, many of these predictions are constrained by experimental data from high-energy colliders, as discussed in the Current Status section.

Criticisms and Challenges

Experimental and Observational Conflicts

High-precision collider experiments have provided strong evidence for the point-like nature of quarks and leptons, conflicting with preon models that predict composite structure manifesting as deviations from point-like behavior at accessible energy scales. Data from the HERA electron-proton collider indicate that electromagnetic form factors of quarks and leptons remain consistent with unity, $ F(Q^2) \approx 1 $, up to momentum transfers $ Q^2 < 5 \times 10^4 $ GeV2^22, with no signs of compositeness. Recent LHC measurements from ATLAS and CMS set much stronger lower limits on the compositeness scale $ \Lambda $ exceeding 37 TeV for eeqq channels and 40 TeV for qq, based on the absence of deviations in cross-sections and angular distributions at 13 TeV. These results imply that any preon binding energies must lie above several tens of TeV, challenging models where confinement occurs at lower scales to explain observed particle masses and interactions.
Precision electroweak measurements further constrain preon compositeness through their consistency with Standard Model predictions, particularly at the Z-pole. Asymmetries such as the forward-backward asymmetry $ A_{FB} $ and the left-right asymmetry $ A_{LR} $, measured at LEP and SLD, agree with electroweak theory to within per-mille precision, limiting contributions from new physics like contact interactions to scales $ \Lambda > 10 $ TeV. In preon models, TeV-scale bindings would induce tree-level modifications to Z-boson couplings and propagators, leading to observable discrepancies in these observables, yet no such effects have been detected. This tension is particularly acute for rishon-like models, where preon exchanges could alter electroweak parameters beyond experimental allowances.
Neutrino scattering experiments provide additional null results for substructure. The NuTeV experiment's deep inelastic neutrino-nucleon scattering data show no evidence for quark or lepton compositeness, setting limits on neutrino-quark contact interactions with $ \Lambda > 5 $ TeV for left-handed couplings. Similarly, MINOS measurements of neutrino cross-sections on iron targets up to energies of several GeV exhibit agreement with point-like predictions, excluding substructure effects that would enhance scattering rates at high momentum transfers. These bounds rule out preon-mediated interactions in neutrino processes, which many models predict to appear at TeV scales.

Current Status

Experimental Searches and Constraints

Searches for preon signatures at high-energy colliders primarily target manifestations of quark and lepton compositeness, including contact interactions, excited fermions, and leptoquarks as potential bound states. The ATLAS and CMS experiments at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC), using proton-proton collision data at a center-of-mass energy of 13 TeV and integrated luminosities up to 140 fb, have found no evidence for such phenomena. Analyses of dijet angular distributions and high-mass dijet events constrain four-fermion contact interactions, setting 95% confidence level (CL) lower limits on the compositeness scale exceeding 21.8 TeV for left-left (LL) quark-quark models and up to 37 TeV for certain left-right (LR) configurations in dilepton + dijet channels. Similarly, dedicated searches for excited quarks exclude masses below 6.7 TeV for excited light quarks assuming dominant electroweak couplings, based on gamma + jet final states.
Leptoquarks, which could emerge in preon-based composite models through binding of preons carrying lepton and baryon numbers, have been probed in multiple decay channels. Recent ATLAS and CMS results from Run 2 data exclude scalar leptoquarks (e.g., S1 and S3 representations) with masses below 1.83 TeV, depending on the generation and coupling strength, in single- and pair-production modes such as cc and ditau final states. Vector leptoquarks face even stronger bounds, with exclusions up to 5 TeV for high couplings in dielectron or dimuon channels. These limits, derived from 137140 fb of data, represent the most stringent to date and disfavor low-scale preon compositeness.
Indirect constraints arise from precision flavor measurements at B-factories, where preon models predict enhanced flavor-changing neutral currents (FCNCs) due to mixing between standard model fermions and composite states. Belle II has reported evidence for the rare decay B K with branching ratio Br = (3.04 0.77) 10, exceeding the Standard Model prediction of ~1 10 by 2.4 using 362 fb of data (as of 2024). This excess provides constraints on new physics contributions, including leptoquark-mediated processes in preon scenarios. Measurements of b s transitions show tensions with SM expectations but remain consistent within uncertainties as of 2025. Non-collider experiments like the Pierre Auger Observatory have also yielded null results for anomalous ultra-high-energy cosmic ray air showers potentially arising from preon production, further tightening bounds at energies above 10 eV.
Prospective upgrades offer enhanced sensitivity. The High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC), commencing around 2029 with up to 3000 fb of data, is projected to improve compositeness limits to > 30 TeV through refined dijet and leptoquark searches. The Future Circular Collider (FCC), envisioned for the 2030s at s = 100 TeV, could probe scales up to 100 TeV, potentially accessing direct preon production if viable at those energies. As of November 2025, ongoing Run 3 analyses at 13.6 TeV with additional ~100 fb are expected to further strengthen these limits. Overall, the absence of signals as of November 2025 disfavors preon models below multi-TeV scales, rendering them increasingly challenged by empirical bounds.

Recent Theoretical Developments

In the 2020s, preon models have seen renewed theoretical interest through topological and algebraic innovations addressing longstanding issues in representation theory and mass generation. A notable advance is the braid topology framework, where preons are modeled as braided ribbons, or "helons," that encode group representations of the Standard Model. This approach, building on Bilson-Thompson's helon model and Lambek's 4-vector model, maps braid states to Lie algebra weights and polytope vertices, resolving gaps in chiral fermion representations by incorporating twists for spinors and charges under SU(3)_c U(1)_em. The model demonstrates CPT invariance through braid diagrams and introduces a 5-vector representation combining 4-vector interactions with helon chirality, providing a particle-centric mathematical foundation for preon compositions.
Parallel developments have explored hypercomplex algebras to explain electroweak masses without the Higgs mechanism. The octonionic preon model posits chiral preon pairs bound by strong spin-exchange couplings, using octonion multiplication and Gell-Mann lambda matrices to derive weak boson masses from internal dynamics and topological structures like the Hopf fibration. This framework predicts the W/Z mass ratio as 3/2 0.87, the Weinberg angle as 30, and the Higgs/W mass ratio as 7/3 1.53, all aligning closely with observed values and bypassing fine-tuning by eliminating adjustable parameters. Small discrepancies are attributed to perturbative weak interactions at ~3% strength.
Complementing this, the spin-exchange preon model treats W, Z, and Higgs bosons as composites of massless chiral preon pairs, with masses emerging from spin-exchange interactions modeled via lambda matrices and curl operators on Mbius tori. It yields precise predictions, including the W/Z mass ratio of 0.87 (vs. experimental 0.88), decay width ratio _W/_Z of 0.87 (vs. 0.84 0.02), and Higgs/W ratio of 1.53 (vs. 1.56), matching LHC measurements without fine-tuning or the Higgs vev. The model's parameter-free nature highlights preon interactions as a natural alternative for electroweak symmetry breaking.
Efforts to integrate preon models with string theory have advanced ultraviolet completions, treating preons as fundamental entities in a topological supersymmetric framework akin to heterotic E_8 E_8 strings. By comparing preon-string symmetries and phenomenological outcomes, these hybrid models posit preons over Standard Model particles as the basic constituents, enabling consistent unification of interactions.
Ongoing theoretical work focuses on incorporating neutrino masses and dark sector phenomena through preon asymmetries. Extensions of supersymmetric preon scenarios to the dark sector generate asymmetric dark matter candidates via preon bindings, while chiral asymmetries in preon composites offer mechanisms for seesaw-like neutrino mass generation, aligning with cosmological constraints.
