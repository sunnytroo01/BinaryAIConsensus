Desjardins Group
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Desjardins Group is a major Canadian cooperative financial institution headquartered in Lvis, Quebec, founded in 1900 by Alphonse and Dorimne Desjardins as the first caisse populaire to promote mutual aid and financial accessibility for working-class individuals.
As the largest cooperative financial group in Canada, it operates through a network of 199 caissesmember-owned cooperatives primarily in Quebec and Ontariosupported by three main business segments that deliver integrated services across personal, business, and institutional sectors.
The group provides a comprehensive range of financial products and services, including savings and checking accounts, mortgages, personal and business loans, credit cards, life and health insurance, group benefits, wealth management, mutual funds, ETFs, and retirement solutions, serving individuals, businesses, and communities with a focus on cooperative principles. In August 2025, Desjardins announced the acquisition of Guardian Capital Group Limited to strengthen its wealth management offerings.
With total assets of $511.9 billion as of September 30, 2025, Desjardins supports 7.8 million members and clients through 579 points of service, 1,303 ATMs (the largest network in Quebec), and approximately 57,200 employees, while generating $3.356 billion in surplus earnings and returning $557 million to members and communities in 2024.
Beyond Canada, it engages in international development in over 30 emerging countries via Desjardins International Dveloppement, emphasizing sustainable finance and cooperation, and has earned recognition as one of Canada's top employers, including in 2025.

History

Founding and early development

The Desjardins Group originated on December 6, 1900, when Alphonse Desjardins, a journalist and parliamentary reporter, and his wife Dorimne Roy Desjardins founded the first caisse populaire in Lvis, Quebec. Inspired by European cooperative credit models, such as those developed in Germany by Hermann Schulze-Delitzsch and in Italy by Luigi Luzzatti, the couple sought to address the financial exclusion faced by working-class French Canadians, who were often denied access to affordable loans from traditional banks dominated by English-speaking elites. Dorimne played a pivotal role by contributing to the initial planning and personally providing the first loan of $5 to a local mechanic unable to secure credit elsewhere.
The Caisse populaire de Lvis commenced operations on January 23, 1901, with an initial capital of $26.40 raised from 37 founding members through $5 shares each, underscoring the institution's emphasis on mutual aid, democratic member control, and provision of low-interest loans to foster economic self-sufficiency. This grassroots approach allowed ordinary workers, farmers, and small business owners to save and borrow collectively, with profits returned to members rather than shareholders.
In the early 1900s through the 1920s, the network expanded steadily across Quebec, growing from a single caisse to over 200 by 1920, as Alphonse Desjardins traveled tirelessly to establish new branches in rural and urban areas. This development gained formal legitimacy on March 5, 1906, when the Quebec Legislative Assembly passed the Act respecting industrial and provident societies (also known as the Loi sur les syndicats agricoles), which authorized the creation and operation of cooperative credit societies like the caisses populaires. Alphonse Desjardins served as the secretary-general of the burgeoning movement, managing administrative duties from his home in Lvis and advocating for its principles until his death on October 31, 1920.
Among the early milestones, the Fdration des caisses populaires Desjardins was formed in 1920 to coordinate the independent caisses, centralize liquidity management, and support collective initiatives. In 1941, the federation established its first insurance subsidiary, Socit d'assurance des caisses populaires (later renamed Desjardins General Insurance), to offer property and casualty coverage tailored to members' needs and aligned with the cooperative ethos.

Expansion and consolidation

Following World War II, the Desjardins Group experienced significant expansion in its network of caisses populaires, growing from nearly 900 outlets by the war's end to over 1,200 by the mid-1970s, driven by increasing membership and deposits in Quebec. This period marked a shift toward broader regional coverage, with assets surpassing C$2.5 billion in the early 1970s as the cooperative model attracted more rural and urban savers. Diversification efforts began in earnest, including the formation of Desjardins Securities in 1974, enabled by special Quebec legislation passed in 1971, which allowed the group to enter the securities brokerage market and offer investment services to members. In 1979, Desjardins expanded into Ontario through a merger with the Credit Union Central of Ontario, incorporating approximately 200,000 members and establishing a foothold outside Quebec.
The 1980s brought economic challenges, including Quebec's financial crises affecting savings institutions, to which Desjardins responded by supporting stabilization efforts under government interventions like the 1988 Savings and Credit Union Act, which restructured the sector into more integrated subsidiaries for greater resilience. Amid these pressures, the group pursued internal consolidations, reducing the number of caisses from 1,275 in the late 1980s to about 800 by the late 1990s through targeted mergers aimed at improving operational efficiency and economies of scale. In 1981, Desjardins Trust was established to handle specialized trust services, building on earlier acquisitions like the Socit de Fiducie du Qubec in 1963. By the 1990s, further diversification included entry into life insurance with the creation of Desjardins Financial Security Life Assurance Company in 1990, following the merger of Desjardins Life Assurance and La Sauvegarde, which expanded offerings in individual and group policies.
Key structural changes in the early 2000s solidified this consolidation, such as the 2002 merger of personal and commercial insurance operations under Desjardins Financial Security, integrating acquisitions like the Laurentian Group's insurance arms from 1994 to streamline product distribution. These efforts contributed to robust asset growth, from approximately C$72 billion in 2000 to C$172.3 billion by 2010, fueled by stable economic conditions, rising member deposits, and expanded service lines.

Recent developments and acquisitions

During the 20082010 global financial crisis, Desjardins Group achieved asset growth of approximately 13%, expanding from C$152.5 billion in 2008 to C$172.3 billion by 2010, which reinforced its reputation as a stable cooperative alternative to Canada's major banks amid widespread market volatility.
A key strategic expansion occurred in 2010 when Desjardins acquired Western Financial Group for Can$443 million in an all-cash deal, incorporating 121 branches and serving 500,000 clients to strengthen its footprint in Western Canada, particularly in property and casualty insurance. In 2014, the group further broadened its insurance operations by purchasing State Farm Canada's property/casualty and life insurance businesses, which included 1.2 million policyholders; the integration was completed with full rebranding to Desjardins by 2019.
In the realm of digital innovation, Desjardins launched its mobile banking app in the early 2010s as part of the AccsD platform, enabling secure transactions and account management on smartphones to meet evolving member needs. The group invested in fintech through the establishment of Desjardins Lab in 2016, a dedicated innovation center fostering collaborations with startups and internal teams to develop client-centric technologies. Into the 2020s, Desjardins emphasized sustainability by issuing green bonds starting in 2021 to fund environmental projects, aligning with broader commitments to renewable energy and climate initiatives.
In August 2025, Desjardins signed a definitive agreement to acquire Guardian Capital Group in a Can$1.67 billion take-private transaction, expected to close in early 2026, aimed at doubling its wealth management assets under management and accelerating growth in investment services. Amid 2025 economic pressures including persistent inflation, the group responded with member-focused initiatives such as increasing provisions for dividends to Can$113 million in the first quarter, up from the prior year, to support financial resilience for its cooperative base.

Organizational structure

Cooperative model and governance

Desjardins Group operates as a member-owned cooperative federation comprising 203 caisses populaires in Quebec and one caisse d'conomie in Ontario, serving approximately 7.8 million members and clients across Canada. This structure emphasizes democratic principles, with each member holding one vote regardless of share ownership, enabling participatory decision-making at the local caisse level through annual general meetings where surpluses are discussed and allocatedtypically reinvested in community initiatives or distributed as dividends to members. The cooperative model prioritizes collective benefit over profit maximization, fostering values such as solidarity, equity, integrity, and sustainable development to support financial inclusion and community empowerment.
Governance is structured to balance democratic input with effective oversight, featuring key bodies that ensure accountability and alignment with cooperative ideals. The General Council, composed of elected representatives from the caisses, serves as a consultative assembly for strategic orientation and policy input, convening during annual democratic assemblies to incorporate member perspectives. The Board of Directors, comprising 19 members including a mix of elected caisse representatives and independent external experts, oversees the federation's direction and includes the President and CEO, currently Denis Dubois, who assumed the role in September 2025. Supporting these are specialized supervisory committees, such as the Board of Ethics and Professional Conduct and risk management committees, which focus on upholding integrity, monitoring ethical practices, and mitigating operational risks in line with cooperative solidarity.
Regulatory oversight is primarily provided by the Autorit des marchs financiers (AMF) in Quebec, which supervises the caisses and federation as a domestic systemically important financial institution, ensuring compliance with sound management and prudential standards. Subsidiaries adhere to federal regulations through the Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI) where applicable, maintaining a framework that integrates environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors into operations. Annual democratic assemblies at both caisse and federation levels provide ongoing member input, reinforcing transparency and responsiveness.
Following the 2019 data breach, Desjardins implemented governance reforms at the AMF's direction to bolster risk management and board independence, including enhanced cybersecurity protocols, improved data governance training (with 88% completion rates by 2024), and structural changes such as the 2024 separation of the Chair and CEO roles to promote clearer oversight and decision-making. These measures strengthened the cooperative's resilience, emphasizing proactive ethics and risk committees to prevent future incidents while preserving democratic integrity.

Divisions and subsidiaries

Desjardins Group operates through a cooperative structure centered on three primary business segments: Personal and Business Services, Wealth Management and Life and Health Insurance, and Property and Casualty Insurance. These segments are supported by the network of 204 caisses in Quebec and Ontario, which serve as local delivery points for financial services, while centralized functions such as treasury, risk management, and development are coordinated by the Fdration des caisses Desjardins du Qubec. The overall organization encompasses more than 20 subsidiaries that handle specialized operations, all reporting ultimately to Desjardins Group Inc. as the parent entity.
In the Personal and Business Services segment, key subsidiaries include Desjardins Bank, National Association, which provides cross-border banking services for Canadians, particularly in the U.S., including online accounts in U.S. dollars. Desjardins Securities Inc. focuses on brokerage, wealth management, and financial planning services across Canada. Additionally, DuProprio, acquired in 2020, operates as a real estate brokerage platform enabling property sales without traditional agents, integrated under Desjardins' real estate services.
The Wealth Management and Life and Health Insurance segment features Desjardins Financial Security Life Assurance Company, which provides life and health insurance products to over 5 million clients and ranks fifth in Canada by direct premiums. Desjardins Global Asset Management Inc. manages approximately $122.1 billion in assets as of 2025, including segregated funds and investment products. In 2025, Desjardins announced the acquisition of Guardian Capital Group to enhance its investment management capabilities, with the deal expected to combine assets exceeding $280 billion upon closing in early 2026. The acquisition, if completed, will integrate Guardian into the structure, potentially expanding the wealth management division.
Property and Casualty Insurance is led by Desjardins General Insurance Group Inc., which serves 3.7 million clients nationwide and ranks third in Canada, including through brands like The Personal for home and auto coverage. Desjardins Capital de risque Inc., part of the broader ecosystem, invests in startups, growth, and business transfers, managing over $3 billion in assets to support local enterprises. Other supporting entities include Caisse Desjardins Ontario Credit Union Inc. for retail banking outside Quebec and Ollabria Financial Services Inc. for payment and credit card solutions. These subsidiaries interconnect through shared governance under the Fdration, ensuring alignment with the cooperative's member-focused objectives across Canada.

Business operations

Banking and financial services

Desjardins Group provides a range of personal banking products through its network of caisses, including savings and chequing accounts that offer easy access to funds and inter-account transfers, personal loans and lines of credit for various needs such as debt consolidation or emergencies, mortgages tailored to homebuyers with competitive fixed and variable rates, and credit cards with rewards programs and cash-back options. These services are accessible via over 579 points of service across 199 caisses primarily in Quebec and Ontario, emphasizing a member-owned cooperative model where eligible personal members receive annual dividends proportional to their usage of products and services, with $437 million provisioned for such payouts in 2024.
For business clients, Desjardins offers SME financing solutions, including term loans and lines of credit to support growth and operations, alongside commercial real estate financing and equipment loans that prioritize local economic development in communities served by its caisses. Additional services encompass payroll processing with direct deposit capabilities, treasury management tools for efficient payments, billings, and cash flow oversight through the AccsD Affaires online platform, serving approximately 425,000 entrepreneurs and businesses with customized advice to foster regional vitality.
Members access these banking services nationwide through a hybrid model integrating physical and digital channels, including the largest ATM network in Quebec with 1,303 machines for fee-free transactions like deposits and withdrawals, complemented by the digital-only capabilities of platforms such as AccsD for online banking and a mobile app utilized by 2.7 million users for bill payments, transfers, and cheque deposits. This network ensures seamless connectivity between caisse branches and digital tools, enabling hybrid service delivery for routine and complex needs.
In the 2020s, Desjardins has emphasized innovations for financial inclusion and sustainability in its banking offerings, such as the Desjardins Microcredit program providing small loans and mentorship to aspiring entrepreneurs from underserved communities to launch or expand ventures, and ESG-linked financing options like green loans for energy-efficient projects that align with environmental goals while supporting local development. These initiatives reflect the group's commitment to responsible banking practices, integrating social impact into core transactional services without overlapping into investment or protection products.

Insurance and wealth management

Desjardins Group provides comprehensive insurance and wealth management services tailored to individuals and businesses, focusing on risk protection and long-term asset growth through its specialized subsidiaries. These offerings emphasize personalized coverage and investment strategies to support financial security across life stages.
The property and casualty insurance segment, operated primarily through Desjardins Insurance, delivers coverage for auto, home, and business needs, including protection against accidents, property damage, and liability risks. As of recent data, this division serves over 4.4 million policies in force, underscoring its scale in the Canadian market. In Quebec, Desjardins holds a leading market share of approximately 25% in property and casualty insurance, reflecting its dominant position in the province.
Life and health insurance products are managed via Desjardins Financial Security, offering term life, critical illness, disability, and group benefits to safeguard against health-related financial disruptions. These services include flexible group plans for employers, covering employee wellness and retirement security. Desjardins Financial Security administers assets under management exceeding Can$50 billion, positioning it as a top provider of market-linked guaranteed investments in Canada and Quebec.
Wealth management is handled through Desjardins Securities and Desjardins Wealth Management, providing mutual funds, retirement savings plans such as Registered Retirement Savings Plans (RRSPs) and Tax-Free Savings Accounts (TFSAs), and personalized advisory services for portfolio optimization and estate planning. These solutions cater to diverse client needs, from individual investors to high-net-worth families, with expert guidance on tax-efficient growth. In August 2025, Desjardins announced its acquisition of Guardian Capital Group in a Can$1.67 billion deal, expected to close in early 2026, which will enhance high-net-worth offerings by integrating Guardian's investment expertise and doubling the platform's scale.
Desjardins integrates its insurance and wealth management products with broader financial services to promote holistic planning, such as bundling auto and home policies for discounts up to 15% or combining life insurance with retirement accounts for streamlined protection and savings. This approach facilitates comprehensive risk management and wealth accumulation. In 2023, the group adopted IFRS 17 for insurance contracts, effective January 1, improving transparency in reporting insurance liabilities and performance.

International activities and other ventures

Desjardins Group maintains a limited but strategic international presence, primarily through its U.S. subsidiaries and global development initiatives. In the United States, the group operates via key entities including Desjardins Bank, N.A. (DB N.A.), a national bank offering retail banking, small business lending, and mortgage services; Desjardins FSB Holdings, Inc., its holding company; and the Desjardins Florida Branch (DFLB), which facilitates cross-border commercial lending and transactions. These operations support cross-border services, with combined U.S. assets averaging less than $1 billion as of December 31, 2024, representing less than 0.3% of the group's total consolidated assets of $511.9 billion CAD as of September 30, 2025. The 2025 U.S. resolution plan underscores these subsidiaries' role in enabling orderly cross-border activities while minimizing systemic risks.
Through Desjardins International Development (DID), the group has engaged in partnerships across more than 30 developing countries since the 1970s, focusing on microfinance, financial inclusion, and literacy programs. DID has supported nearly 700 projects in 65 countries overall, investing over CAN$100 million in 50 microfinance institutions to empower disadvantaged populations, including rural entrepreneurs and women. Notable examples include initiatives in Africa, such as the PINKK project in Senegal for business development and financial education, and broader efforts in Latin America to promote access to tailored financial services for micro-businesses. These activities align with the group's cooperative ethos of global solidarity, as detailed in annual social responsibility reports that track impacts like the number of beneficiaries reached and institutions strengthened.
Beyond core financial services, Desjardins pursues diverse ventures in real estate, venture capital, and sustainability. In 2020, the group acquired DuProprio, a commission-free real estate brokerage platform that enables direct buyer-seller transactions, saving users on traditional agent fees while leveraging technology for visibility and support. Desjardins Capital de Risque provides venture capital to startups and growth-stage companies, focusing on innovative Quebec-based firms in sectors like technology and life sciences, with investments contributing to the broader Canadian VC ecosystem. On the sustainability front, the group launched impact investing initiatives, including the Aequitas Fund in 2021 with CAN$88 million to finance microfinance in Africa, Latin America, and Asia, targeting gender equality, climate resilience, and UN Sustainable Development Goals; this builds on earlier 2020 efforts like the Desjardins Sustainable Diversity Fund. These ventures reinforce Desjardins' mission by generating social impact metrics, such as support for millions of entrepreneurs and alignment with ESG principles, as reported annually.

Financial performance

Key metrics and results

As of September 30, 2025, Desjardins Group reported total assets of Can$511.9 billion, marking an 8.7% increase from December 31, 2024. For the first nine months of 2025, the group recorded surplus earnings before member dividends of Can$2.753 billion, an increase of Can$223 million compared to the same period in 2024. In the third quarter of 2025, surplus earnings before member dividends totaled Can$1.115 billion, up from Can$757 million in Q3 2024.
As of June 30, 2025, total assets stood at Can$501.3 billion, a 6.4% increase from December 31, 2024. For the first half of 2025, the group recorded total net revenue of Can$7.773 billion and surplus earnings before member dividends of Can$1.638 billion, reflecting a decline of 7.6% (Can$135 million) in surplus earnings compared to the same period in 2024, primarily due to higher claims in property and casualty insurance and elevated credit loss provisions amid economic pressures. Annualized based on H1 data, total net revenue would approximate Can$15.5 billion, compared to Can$14.66 billion for full-year 2024.
Historically, Desjardins Group's assets have shown steady expansion, growing from Can$397 billion at the end of 2021 to Can$511.9 billion by September 30, 2025, representing a compound annual growth rate of approximately 6% over 3.75 years. Surplus earnings before member dividends stood at Can$2.942 billion in 2021, declined to Can$2.259 billion in 2023, and rebounded to Can$3.356 billion in 2024 (a 48.6% increase from 2023), driven by organic expansion and strategic initiatives in insurance and banking segments. Performance moderated in early 2025 due to macroeconomic factors but improved in Q3.
Key performance ratios highlight Desjardins Group's operational efficiency and profitability. The return on equity for the first half of 2025 was 8.3%, down slightly from 9.0% in full-year 2024, while the productivity indexa measure akin to the efficiency ratiostood at 71.4% for personal and business services in the same period, indicating controlled expense growth relative to revenue. The group employs 52,517 full-time equivalents as of June 30, 2025, and serves 7.8 million members and clients, reinforcing its scale as North America's largest cooperative financial institution.
In Q2 2025, surplus earnings before member dividends totaled Can$900 million, a slight decrease from Q2 2024, offset by a 9.0% rise in net revenue to Can$4.091 billion. On January 1, 2025, the group redeemed Can$464 million in preferred shares and issued Can$200 million in new ones to optimize its capital structure. The adoption of IFRS 17 in 2023 continues to influence reporting, particularly in insurance segments, by enhancing transparency in contract liabilities (Can$34.7 billion as of June 30, 2025) and contractual service margins (Can$2.554 billion), though it contributed to transitional adjustments in earnings comparability.

Credit agency ratings

As of mid-2025, Desjardins Group maintains high investment-grade credit ratings from major agencies, reflecting its robust capitalization and the stability inherent in its cooperative structure. The Fdration des caisses Desjardins du Qubec, the group's central funding entity, holds the following key ratings: Aa1 (stable outlook) from Moody's for counterparty and deposit ratings; A+ (stable outlook) from S&P for counterparty and deposit ratings; AA (stable outlook) from DBRS Morningstar for long-term issuer and deposit ratings; and AA- (stable outlook) from Fitch for long-term issuer default ratings. These ratings were affirmed in June and July 2025 with no changes reported as of November 2025.

They also reinforce the group's financial health, supporting sustainable member dividends through consistent surplus earnings, and provide a competitive advantage in attracting institutional investors to its debt instruments.

Controversies

2019 data breach

In early 2019, Desjardins Group discovered unauthorized access to its systems by a single employee, who had exfiltrated personal information over the period from 2017 to 2019. The breach was publicly announced on June 20, 2019, and ultimately exposed data from approximately 4.2 million individuals and 173,000 businesses, totaling 9.7 million records. The compromised information included names, addresses, social insurance numbers, and transaction histories, but did not involve passwords, full account numbers, or other highly sensitive financial details.
The incident stemmed from an insider threat, where the employee exploited inadequate access controls and monitoring mechanisms within Desjardins' IT environment. An investigation by the Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada (OPC) revealed a series of organizational gaps, including insufficient employee training, weak oversight of data access privileges, and delayed implementation of advanced security tools like a robust security information and event management (SIEM) system. These vulnerabilities allowed the employee to share the data with external parties, leading to potential identity theft risks for affected members.
Desjardins responded swiftly by notifying affected individuals and businesses, offering two years of free credit monitoring and identity protection services to all 4.2 million members starting in December 2019. In October 2025, portions of the 2019 stolen data resurfaced on dark web forums via the ransomware group CoinbaseCartel, re-exposing information for over 1 million individuals, primarily Quebecers. The company incurred remediation costs exceeding Can$100 million, covering investigations, system upgrades, and member support. The breach prompted multiple class-action lawsuits in Quebec and Ontario, which were settled in 2021 for up to Can$201 million, providing compensation for time loss, anxiety, and identity theft-related expenses without requiring proof of harm.
In the aftermath, Desjardins implemented significant cybersecurity enhancements, including the deployment of advanced monitoring tools, stricter access controls, and improved employee training protocols to mitigate insider threats. Governance reforms were introduced, such as regular privacy impact assessments and collaboration with regulators like the OPC to ensure compliance with PIPEDA. As of November 2025, no further major data breaches have been reported at the organization.

Other legal and ethical issues

In the 2010s, Desjardins Group drew criticism from environmental organizations for its financing of fossil fuel-related projects, including oil sands development and pipeline expansions that contributed to carbon emissions and climate risks. In response to such concerns, the group suspended new investments in energy transportation infrastructure, such as pipelines, in 2017. By 2020, amid ongoing advocacy from groups like Greenpeace, Desjardins committed to divesting from fossil fuels across its 17 SocieTerra investment funds and portfolios, rendering them fully fossil-free to align with sustainable investment principles. These actions were part of broader efforts to reduce exposure to high-carbon sectors, with fossil fuels comprising less than 1% of its portfolio by that year.
To support environmental transitions, Desjardins has issued sustainable bonds, including a Can$500 million green bond in 2021 allocated to renewable energy and energy efficiency projects, and a 500 million sustainability bond in 2024 targeted at low-carbon initiatives in Europe. These issuances reflect commitments under its Sustainable Bond Framework, which adheres to international green bond principles and emphasizes transparency in fund allocation.
Regulatory scrutiny has included fines for compliance shortcomings. In 2021, Quebec's Autorit des marchs financiers (AMF) required Desjardins Financial Services Firm to pay a Can$1 million settlement for breaches in mutual fund sales practices, including inadequate client suitability assessments and risk management. In 2024, the Canadian Investment Regulatory Organization (CIRO) imposed a Can$225,000 fine and Can$623,925 in disgorgement on Desjardins Securities Inc. for failing to supervise registered representatives, leading to unsuitable investment recommendations. Although no specific 2022 AMF fine for anti-money laundering lapses was documented, Desjardins has reported ongoing compliance with Financial Transactions and Reports Analysis Centre of Canada (FINTRAC) requirements in its risk disclosures.
Ethical challenges have encompassed labor relations and governance in its cooperative framework. In 2023, Desjardins announced layoffs affecting around 400 positions across operations, prompting concerns about job security and support for employees within its member-owned structure. A related class action over unauthorized deductions from a negative vacation bank policy, affecting non-unionized workers, culminated in a Can$7 million settlement approved on November 3, 2025, providing compensation and policy reforms. Executive compensation has faced examination for compatibility with cooperative values, but a study found Desjardins' top executive pay to be comparatively moderate versus Canada's major banks, with structures tied to ESG performance metrics.
On social responsibility, Desjardins has advanced Indigenous reconciliation through commitments announced in 2021, including support for the National Day for Truth and Reconciliation and participation in dialogues on Indigenous rights via investment initiatives. The Aequitas impact investment fund, created in 2021 and focused on inclusive finance for underserved communities in developing countries in Africa, Latin America, and Asia, has funded projects aiding marginalized groups to achieve United Nations Sustainable Development Goals. These international efforts align with Desjardins' broader ESG strategies, which include domestic initiatives to enhance economic access for First Nations businesses, though specific equity funds for Indigenous enterprises have been integrated into overall ESG approaches rather than as standalone vehicles.
