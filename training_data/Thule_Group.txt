Thule Group
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Thule Group AB (publ) is a Swedish multinational corporation specializing in sports and outdoor products designed to facilitate active lifestyles, including roof racks, bike carriers, cargo boxes, trailers, bags, and luggage. Founded in 1942 by Erik Thulin in Hillerstorp, Sweden, as a manufacturer of fishing gear and metal products, the company evolved in the 1960s to focus on vehicle accessories like ski racks and expanded globally through innovations and acquisitions.
Headquartered in Malm, Sweden, Thule Group operates under the mission "Active Life, Simplified," developing safe, stylish, and easy-to-use solutions for families and outdoor enthusiasts, with a strong emphasis on sustainability and quality. The company is a global market leader in categories such as roof racks and bike carriers, offering a broad portfolio that also includes water sports carriers, child bike seats, strollers, and protective cases for electronics.
Key milestones include the introduction of its first ski rack in 1962 and the iconic TB-11 ski box in 1977, which helped establish its reputation in winter sports transport. Strategic acquisitions have driven growth, such as Case Logic and Sportrack in 2006 for electronics protection and cargo solutions, Chariot Carriers in 2011 for child trailers, Denver Outfitters in 2019 to bolster camping gear, and Quad Lock in 2024 for performance phone mount solutions. Thule Group went public on Nasdaq Stockholm in 2014, enabling further international expansion.
Today, the company employs approximately 3,100 people worldwide, as of September 2025, maintains 35 sales offices, and distributes products in 138 markets through nine production facilities, prioritizing innovation in eco-friendly materials and designs to support diverse activities from cycling and skiing to travel and family outings.

History

Founding and early development

Thule Group was founded in 1942 by Erik Thulin and his wife Gunhild in a laundry room in sterskog, Gnosj municipality (near Hillerstorp), Sweden, during World War II, when Erik, an avid outdoorsman, began producing practical items amid material shortages. The company's inaugural product was a pike fishing trap, known locally as a gddsax, designed to support Erik's passion for fishing and reflecting the resourcefulness of rural Swedish life. This humble start marked the beginning of Thule's commitment to durable outdoor gear, drawing from Scandinavia's harsh weather and active lifestyle.
In the 1950s, Thule transitioned from wartime necessities like belt buckles to metalworking for the burgeoning automotive sector, capitalizing on Sweden's post-war economic recovery and rising car ownership. A pivotal innovation came in 1955 with the launch of car headlight guards, aimed at protecting vehicle lights from road debris, which established Thule's early foothold in automotive accessories and demonstrated its focus on functional, weather-resistant designs. These products laid the groundwork for Thule's shift toward transportation solutions, emphasizing robustness suited to Scandinavian winters and outdoor pursuits.
The company's defining moment arrived in 1962 with the introduction of its first roof rack system, specifically a ski rack developed in response to an order from Swedish retailer Nordiska Biltillbehr, signaling a full pivot to outdoor transportation accessories. By 1964, Thule had relocated to larger facilities in Hillerstorp, enabling expanded production, and by 1969, it had streamlined operations to specialize exclusively in roof racks. Throughout the 1960s and 1970s, Thule experienced steady growth in the Swedish market, innovating with products like the lockable Thule Classic 68 ski carrier in 1973 and the Thule 1050 system in 1975, all engineered for reliability in extreme conditions and inspired by the region's love for skiing and nature exploration. This era solidified Thule's reputation for high-quality, user-friendly gear that enhanced active lifestyles without compromising on safety or durability.

Expansion and ownership changes

In 1979, the Thulin family sold Thule to Eldon, a Swedish publicly listed company, which facilitated broader distribution across Europe and supported continued organic growth and acquisitions in the outdoor equipment sector.
The company entered a series of private equity ownership phases starting in 1999, when EQT acquired Eldon and spun off Thule as an independent entity, enabling investments in manufacturing facilities, such as the establishment of production in Poland, and further European expansion. In 2004, Candover purchased Thule from EQT for SEK 4.15 billion, during which the company pursued aggressive growth through multiple acquisitions that diversified its portfolio and boosted sales to SEK 6.7 billion by fiscal year 2006. Nordic Capital then acquired Thule from Candover in 2007 for SEK 12.7 billion in a leveraged buyout, providing capital for additional international market penetration and operational enhancements.
Thule transitioned to public ownership with its initial public offering (IPO) on Nasdaq Stockholm in November 2014, raising approximately SEK 1.9 billion to fund ongoing expansion and innovation initiatives.
Following the IPO, Thule accelerated its international growth, with its U.S. headquarters in Seymour, Connecticut, strengthening North American operations and logistics. By 2015, the company had achieved net sales of SEK 5.3 billion, reflecting robust post-IPO performance driven by expanded global presence in Europe, the Americas, and emerging markets in Asia and Africa.

Products and brands

Core product categories

Thule Group's core product categories encompass a diverse portfolio designed to support active lifestyles and outdoor adventures, emphasizing durability, safety, and user-friendly integration into daily routines. The company's offerings are divided into four primary segments: Sport & Cargo Carriers, Packs, Bags & Luggage, Juvenile & Pet Products, and RV Products. These categories collectively enable consumers to transport gear, protect valuables, and facilitate family or pet-inclusive excursions while prioritizing environmental sustainability and ergonomic design.
The Sport & Cargo Carriers category includes roof racks, roof boxes, bike carriers, and cargo carriers engineered for seamless vehicle integration, allowing users to securely transport sports equipment, luggage, and other outdoor essentials. These products feature aerodynamic designs to minimize drag and fuel consumption, along with quick-release mechanisms for easy installation and removal, ensuring they withstand harsh weather conditions through corrosion-resistant materials like aluminum and reinforced plastics. By providing reliable transportation solutions, this category plays a crucial role in enabling road trips, cycling outings, and winter sports activities, positioning Thule Group as a global market leader in this segment.
Packs, Bags & Luggage form another key category, comprising backpacks, camera bags, travel packs, and protective cases made from weatherproof, water-repellent fabrics such as nylon and polyester, often incorporating padded compartments for electronics and ergonomic shoulder straps for comfort during extended use. Functionality is enhanced by features like adjustable dividers, anti-theft zippers, and expandable storage, making these items suitable for commuting, hiking, or photography expeditions. This category supports outdoor enthusiasts by offering versatile, portable storage that protects gear from elements and impacts, with some lines utilizing 100% recycled materials to promote eco-conscious travel.
Juvenile & Pet Products focus on child bike seats, strollers, bike trailers, and pet carriers, all prioritizing safety through five-point harnesses, impact-absorbing frames, and reflective elements for visibility, while incorporating lightweight, foldable designs for enhanced portability. These items are crafted with breathable fabrics and adjustable fits to accommodate growing children or various pet sizes, facilitating family-oriented activities like biking or walking in urban and natural settings. The category underscores Thule Group's commitment to inclusive outdoor experiences, with expansions into car seats and dog transport solutions further broadening accessibility for active families and pet owners.
RV and outdoor gear in the RV Products category includes tents, awnings, and rooftop tents tailored for recreational vehicles and camping, featuring quick-setup mechanisms, UV-resistant coatings, and ventilated interiors to provide shelter and comfort in diverse environments. These products emphasize durability against wind and rain, with modular attachments for customization, allowing users to extend living spaces during adventures. By supporting extended stays in nature, this segment enhances the camping experience for RV enthusiasts, where Thule Group holds a leading position in Europe.

Owned brands and portfolio

The Thule Group's flagship brand, Thule, has served as the core identity for premium outdoor carriers since its founding in 1942, offering high-quality, sustainable products such as roof racks, bike carriers, roof boxes, and child-related gear designed to facilitate active lifestyles for families and outdoor enthusiasts. Thule emphasizes safe, user-friendly, and stylish solutions that integrate seamlessly into everyday adventures, positioning it as the premium leader in the portfolio with a focus on innovation and environmental responsibility.
The group's acquired brands expand its offerings by targeting diverse segments of the active lifestyle market, providing complementary products across various price points and use cases. Case Logic, acquired in 2007, specializes in protective bags and cases for electronic devices, including laptop sleeves, tablet cases, and media storage solutions that prioritize durability and slim design for urban commuters and professionals. SportRack focuses on affordable vehicle accessories, such as roof racks, cargo boxes, and bike carriers, enabling budget-conscious consumers to access safe and practical outdoor transport options without compromising on reliability. Chariot, integrated since 2011 and rebranded under Thule in 2013, provides multifunctional child carriers that convert for biking, jogging, strolling, and skiing, catering to active parents seeking versatile, all-season family mobility. Yepp offers child bike seats with ergonomic designs for rear- or front-mounting, emphasizing lightweight construction, shock absorption, and safety features for daily urban rides and family outings. Tepui, acquired in 2018, produces durable rooftop tents for overlanding and camping, featuring UV-resistant materials and easy vehicle mounting to support extended outdoor expeditions. Quad Lock, acquired in late 2024, leads in performance phone mounts and accessories, delivering secure, vibration-resistant solutions for cyclists, motorcyclists, and adventurers to capture and navigate their experiences hands-free. Reacha, acquired in July 2024, specializes in sustainable, modular bike trailers designed for transporting water sports equipment and other outdoor gear, enhancing options for family and adventure transport.
Thule Group's portfolio strategy revolves around diversification to address a broad spectrum of active lifestyles, blending premium offerings from the Thule brand with more accessible alternatives from acquired brands to cover categories like vehicle transport, child mobility, protective gear, and adventure accessories. This approach allows the company to serve varied customer needsfrom high-end outdoor enthusiasts to everyday userswhile maintaining a unified commitment to quality, sustainability, and ease of use across price points.

Business developments

Major acquisitions and divestitures

Thule Group's growth strategy has involved targeted acquisitions to broaden its product offerings in outdoor and transportation accessories. In 2004, the company acquired Knig, a leading European provider of snow chains and related automotive accessories, enhancing its position in winter mobility products.
Subsequent deals focused on diversifying into bags and child-related categories. In 2007, Thule acquired Case Logic, a U.S.-based manufacturer of protective bags for electronic devices such as laptops and cameras, which strengthened its portfolio in consumer electronics storage and later contributed to the premium Thule Subterra line. In 2011, Thule purchased Chariot Carriers Inc., a Canadian leader in multi-functional child carriers including bike trailers and strollers, marking its entry into the active-with-kids segment and expanding family-oriented outdoor solutions.
Further acquisitions targeted niche outdoor gear. In 2018, Thule acquired Tepui Outdoors Inc., a North American rooftop tent manufacturer, for USD 9.5 million on a debt-free and cash-free basis, with potential earn-out up to USD 1.75 million; this move bolstered Thule's camping product line and integrated seamlessly with its roof rack systems. In 2024, Thule acquired Reacha, a small but leading German player in bike trailers for transporting water sports equipment and other gear, consolidated from July 2024 to enhance its sustainable transportation solutions. Most recently, also in 2024, Thule completed the acquisition of Quad Lock, an Australian company specializing in performance phone mounts for cycling, motorcycling, and other activities, for AUD 500 million (approximately SEK 3.6 billion) on a cash and debt-free basis; this deal introduced a new mobile accessories category and enhanced Thule's appeal to adventure enthusiasts.
On the divestiture side, Thule sold its snow chain business, including the Knig brand, to Austrian firm Schneketten AG in 2015 for an undisclosed amount, allowing the company to sharpen its focus on core outdoor and bags segments amid shifting market priorities toward year-round active lifestyles.
These transactions have strategically expanded Thule's geographic footprint, particularly in North America through additions like Chariot, Tepui, and Case Logic, while diversifying its product diversity from traditional roof systems to innovative carriers, tents, and tech-integrated accessories, driving overall revenue growth in premium outdoor markets.

Key milestones and timeline

Thule Group's evolution is marked by several pivotal events that shaped its growth from a niche manufacturer to a global leader in outdoor transportation solutions. In 1962, the company launched its first cargo carrier, the Thule Standard ski rack, which revolutionized the transport of sports equipment and laid the foundation for its product innovation.
A significant financial milestone occurred in 2014 when Thule Group went public through an initial public offering on Nasdaq Stockholm, with trading commencing on November 26 under the ticker "THULE," enabling broader access to capital markets and supporting international expansion.
The year 2015 saw strategic refocusing, including the divestiture of its snow chain business to Schneketten AG in September, allowing Thule to concentrate on core outdoor and bags segments. At that time, the company reached a peak workforce of approximately 2,200 employees across its global operations.(https://www.thulegroup.com/en/press/thule-group-increases-focus-outdoorbags-divests-s snow-chain-business-1368585)
Thule underwent a leadership transition in 2023 with the appointment of Mattias Ankarberg as CEO and President, effective no later than August 9, 2023, bringing expertise in consumer goods to navigate evolving market dynamics. In the third quarter of 2025, net sales reached SEK 2,528 million, reflecting resilience amid acquisitions like Quad Lock.
Post-2023 economic slowdowns presented challenges, including cautious consumer spending and flat organic growth in key regions, prompting Thule to adapt through product innovation and cost efficiencies to maintain profitability.

Corporate affairs

Leadership and governance

Thule Group's executive leadership is headed by Chief Executive Officer and President Mattias Ankarberg, who assumed the role in August 2023. Ankarberg brings extensive experience in consumer goods, having previously served as CEO of Byggmax Group and held senior positions at H&M Group, including roles in strategy and operations, as well as earlier work at McKinsey & Company. Under his leadership, the company emphasizes consumer-driven innovation and long-term sustainability, as evidenced by Thule Group's recognition as a global ESG leader by Sustainalytics in 2025.
The Chief Financial Officer position is held by Toby Lawton, who joined in January 2024. Lawton oversees financial operations, reporting, and related functions, drawing on over 25 years of experience in finance, primarily with Svenska Cellulosa Aktiebolaget (SCA, now including Essity), where he held various senior roles. His prior positions include CFO at Renewcell AB and Vinda International Holdings.
The Board of Directors, consisting of seven members, is chaired by Hans Eckerstrm since April 2022, a position he took after serving on the board since 2007. Eckerstrm's expertise spans retail and growth strategies, informed by his background as a management consultant at Arthur D. Little, partner at NC Advisory AB, and involvement in Nordic Capital's investments, including Thule Group's turnaround and listing. He currently chairs Profoto Holding AB and serves on the board of Swedbank AB. The board includes Anders Jensen, Helene Willberg, Johan Westman, Paul Gustavsson, Sandra Finr, and Sarah McPhee, selected for their diverse skills in finance, sustainability, and international business to guide the company's strategy. The board adheres to the Swedish Code of Corporate Governance, with a diversity policy targeting balanced gender representationcurrently 43% womenand conducts annual evaluations to ensure effective oversight of operations and long-term value creation.
Governance practices include specialized committees: the Audit Committee, chaired by Helene Willberg, focuses on financial reporting and internal controls, while the Remuneration Committee, chaired by Eckerstrm, handles executive compensation and policies. The Nomination Committee, formed annually from representatives of the four largest shareholders plus the board chairman, proposes board candidates, fees, and auditor appointments ahead of the Annual General Meeting; for the 2025 AGM, it comprises Anders Oscarsson (AMF Frskring och Fonder), Helen Fasth Gillstedt (Handelsbanken Fonder), Magnus Sjqvist (Swedbank Robur Fonder), Carina Silberg (Alecta), and Hans Eckerstrm.

Financial performance

Thule Group's financial performance has shown steady growth in recent years, driven by a combination of organic expansion and strategic acquisitions, though tempered by macroeconomic challenges. In 2024, the company achieved net sales of SEK 9,541 million, marking a 4.5% increase from SEK 9,132 million in 2023, with organic growth contributing 3.5% to this rise. This performance reflected recovery in key markets like biking, alongside contributions from new product launches and the partial-year impact of the Quad Lock acquisition, which added SEK 105 million in revenue.
Profitability remained robust in 2024, with operating income (EBIT) reaching SEK 1,522 million and an EBIT margin of 15.9%, slightly down from 16.5% in 2023 due to elevated costs from product development and acquisition-related transaction expenses of SEK 100 million. Net profit for the year stood at SEK 1,122 million. Building on post-IPO momentum, total assets grew to SEK 6,899 million by the end of 2015, supported by initial public listing proceeds and operational scaling, with equity at SEK 3,228 million at that time. By December 31, 2024, assets had expanded to SEK 14,957 million and equity to SEK 8,095 million, reflecting sustained investments and acquisition-driven growth, including SEK 2,327 million in goodwill from Quad Lock.
In the third quarter of 2025, net sales rose 7.9% year-over-year to SEK 2,528 million, bolstered by 17.0% growth from acquisitions, though organic sales declined by 4.0% amid economic pressures. EBIT increased 9.5% to SEK 453 million, yielding a margin of 17.9%, while net income grew 4.6% to SEK 314 million. As of September 30, 2025, total assets stood at SEK 14,478 million and equity at SEK 7,319 million, indicating continued balance sheet strength despite cautious consumer spending and retailer inventories in a tough market environment.
On November 19, 2025, Thule Group updated its long-term financial targets, setting an annual organic sales growth ambition of 7% or higher, while maintaining the EBIT margin target at 20% and dividend payout ratio at or above 75% of net income. The sustainability targets remain unchanged.

Sustainability and responsibility

Environmental initiatives

Thule Group publishes an annual Sustainability Report as part of its integrated Annual and Sustainability Report, with the 2024 edition adhering to the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS). The company has committed to science-based targets aligned with the Paris Agreement, including a net-zero greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions goal by 2050 through at least a 90% absolute reduction from 2019 levels, with residual emissions neutralized. Toward this, Thule aims for interim 2030 targets of a 46.2% reduction in Scope 1 emissions, a 28% reduction in Scope 3 emissions (from purchased goods and logistics), and 100% renewable electricity for Scope 2, all relative to a 2019 baseline; in 2024, it achieved a 23% overall emissions reduction, with Scope 3 down 23% to 201,932 tCOe and Scope 2 fully renewable. In February 2025, Thule Group was recognized as one of the top 50 most sustainable companies globally by Sustainalytics.
In product eco-design, Thule integrates life cycle assessments to minimize environmental impact, emphasizing recycled materials such as hydropower-produced aluminum and recycled plastics in roof boxes like the Thule Motion 3 and bike carriers like the Thule EasyFold Gen 3, which achieved 50% lower emissions than its predecessor. Bags and other accessories also incorporate sustainable materials, with 2024 packaging comprising 54% recycled plastic, 38% paper, and 9% textiles to enhance recyclability. The company targets a 30% reduction in COeq emissions for new products generating at least SEK 20 million in second-year sales compared to benchmarks, while optimizing packaging to reduce plastic use and waste.
The Thule Test Center in Hillerstorp, Sweden, supports these efforts by conducting rigorous durability, crash safety, and weather testing that exceeds regulatory standards, enabling efficient R&D to extend product lifespans and minimize material waste.
For supply chain management, Thule focuses on Scope 3 emissions reduction through annual audits of high-risk suppliers, achieving 98% coverage in 2024 (178 suppliers representing 90% of spend via the Carbon Disclosure Project) and an additional 46 assessments. Manufacturing sites, such as the zero-emission facility in Poland using 30 air-to-air heat pumps and green electricity, promote renewable energy adoption among suppliers, contributing to a 36% drop in air freight emissions from 3,163 tCOe in 2019 to 2,038 tCOe in 2024.

Social and community engagement

Thule Group actively engages in social initiatives that support vulnerable children and promote inclusivity, particularly through long-term partnerships aimed at enhancing active lifestyles for those with disabilities. Since 2010, the company has served as the main sponsor of RBU, Sweden's National Association for Disabled Children and Adolescents, providing financial and logistical support to enable sports participation and outdoor activities for disabled youth. This partnership includes organizing annual "Family Days" events, such as the 2024 gathering at Liseberg amusement park in Gothenburg, where Thule employees assist in creating accessible experiences for RBU families. These efforts underscore Thule's commitment to outdoor accessibility programs, fostering community inclusion beyond product sales.
In addition to sponsorships, Thule Group contributes through employee-driven donations and local charity support. For instance, in 2021, the UK team in Haverhill raised funds for disabled and vulnerable children in the area, demonstrating grassroots involvement in community welfare. The company also participates in broader product donation programs, including grants and volunteer activities that benefit underprivileged groups, aligning with its vision of an active life for all.
Thule Group's ethical labor practices emphasize fair treatment across its global workforce of approximately 2,800 employees, as of 2024. The company commits to eliminating forced labor, slavery, human trafficking, and child labor in its operations and supply chain, adhering to the UN Global Compact's principles. It conducts ongoing assessments to ensure equitable remuneration and fair wages, while promoting diversity with a target of gender balanceaiming for no gender below 40% representation in leadership by 2030; currently, women comprise 44% of the workforce. These policies support equal opportunities for professional development and participation, reflecting a dedication to social equity.
Product safety remains a cornerstone of Thule's social responsibility, particularly for juvenile and pet categories. Juvenile products, such as strollers and child bike seats, undergo rigorous testing exceeding ISO standards and hold certifications like EN 1888:2012 from TV Rheinland for the Thule Sleek stroller. Pet carriers, including dog crates, are crash-tested at the Thule Test Center and certified by TV SD (certificate Z1 013709 0039), ensuring compliance with standards for vehicle transport safety. These measures prioritize user protection, especially for families and pets, integrating ethical considerations into product design.
