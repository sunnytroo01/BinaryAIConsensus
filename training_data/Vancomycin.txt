 Vancomycin is a tricyclic glycopeptide antibiotic derived from the soil bacterium Amycolatopsis orientalis (formerly Streptomyces orientalis), primarily used to treat severe infections caused by gram-positive bacteria, including methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) and other multidrug-resistant strains. Discovered in 1952 from soil samples collected in the jungles of Borneo, it was introduced clinically in the late 1950s as a last-resort option for infections resistant to earlier antibiotics like penicillin.
Vancomycin exerts its bactericidal effects by binding to the D-alanyl-D-alanine (D-Ala-D-Ala) terminus of peptidoglycan precursors in the bacterial cell wall, inhibiting transpeptidation and transglycosylation steps essential for cell wall synthesis, which leads to bacterial cell death. This narrow-spectrum activity targets aerobic and anaerobic gram-positive organisms such as staphylococci, streptococci, enterococci, and clostridia, but it has no effect on gram-negative bacteria due to their outer membrane barrier.
Due to its poor oral bioavailability for systemic use, vancomycin is typically administered intravenously to achieve therapeutic plasma levels for serious conditions like endocarditis, bacteremia, pneumonia, bone and joint infections, and skin and soft tissue infections. Orally, it is employed for non-systemic gastrointestinal infections, particularly Clostridium difficile-associated colitis, where it remains active in the gut lumen without significant absorption. Alternative routes, such as intrathecal or topical, may be used for specific localized infections like meningitis or peritoneal dialysis-related peritonitis. Despite its efficacy, vancomycin use is monitored closely due to risks of nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity, and the emergence of resistance mechanisms, such as vancomycin-resistant enterococci (VRE) and vancomycin-intermediate S. aureus (VISA).

Medical uses

Indications

Vancomycin is primarily indicated for the treatment of serious or severe infections caused by susceptible Gram-positive bacteria, including methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) and methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus epidermidis (MRSE). When administered intravenously, it is approved for conditions such as septicemia, infective endocarditis, infections of the bone and joints, lower respiratory tract infections, and skin and soft tissue infections due to these organisms. Orally, vancomycin is specifically indicated for the treatment of Clostridioides difficile-associated diarrhea (CDAD) in both adults and pediatric patients.
In clinical practice, vancomycin serves as a cornerstone therapy for various Gram-positive infections, including complicated skin and soft tissue infections, bacteremia, endocarditis, osteomyelitis, septic arthritis, and hospital-acquired or ventilator-associated pneumonia suspected to involve MRSA. It is particularly valuable for patients allergic to beta-lactams or those with infections unresponsive to other agents. For surgical prophylaxis, guidelines recommend vancomycin in high-risk procedures such as cardiac, orthopedic, vascular, and neurosurgical interventions when there is known MRSA colonization, beta-lactam allergy, or high institutional MRSA prevalence, typically as an alternative to cefazolin.
The Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA) guidelines endorse vancomycin as a first-line agent for serious MRSA infections, including bacteremia and endocarditis (with or without prosthetic valves), complicated skin and soft tissue infections, pneumonia, and bone and joint infections. For optimal efficacy, vancomycin therapy often targets an area under the curve to minimum inhibitory concentration (AUC/MIC) ratio of 400. For CDI, the 2021 IDSA/SHEA guidelines suggest fidaxomicin as the preferred initial therapy for nonsevere and severe episodes, with oral vancomycin (125 mg four times daily) as an acceptable alternative; for fulminant cases, oral (or nasogastric) vancomycin (500 mg four times daily) is recommended as first-line, with both fidaxomicin and vancomycin outperforming metronidazole in clinical outcomes.
Off-label uses of vancomycin include treatment of susceptible enterococcal infections, such as endocarditis caused by vancomycin-susceptible Enterococcus faecalis or Enterococcus faecium, typically in combination with gentamicin or as an alternative for beta-lactam-intolerant patients. It may also be employed for other resistant Gram-positive infections where susceptibility is confirmed, though emerging resistance patterns necessitate careful microbiological guidance.

Spectrum of activity

Vancomycin exhibits potent activity against a broad range of Gram-positive bacteria, including staphylococci (such as methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus ), streptococci, enterococci, and Gram-positive anaerobes like Clostridioides species (e.g., C. difficile) and other Clostridium species. This selectivity arises because vancomycin inhibits cell wall synthesis by binding to the D-Ala-D-Ala terminus of peptidoglycan precursors, a process accessible in Gram-positive organisms lacking an outer membrane.
In vitro susceptibility testing demonstrates low minimum inhibitory concentrations (MICs) for most susceptible Gram-positive strains. For example, the MIC for S. aureus is typically 2 g/mL, with MIC90 values of 2 g/mL reported for MRSA isolates. Against streptococci, including Streptococcus pneumoniae, MICs range from 0.25 to 2 g/mL, while for vancomycin-susceptible enterococci, they generally fall between 1 and 4 g/mL. Vancomycin also shows strong anaerobic activity, particularly against Clostridioides difficile, with MICs for susceptible strains typically 0.52 g/mL, supporting its efficacy in this context.
Vancomycin is inherently ineffective against most Gram-negative bacteria due to its inability to penetrate the outer membrane, resulting in MICs exceeding 64 g/mL for pathogens like Escherichia coli.
The accuracy of in vitro susceptibility testing for vancomycin can be influenced by factors such as inoculum size and testing media. Higher inoculum levels may elevate MICs, particularly for staphylococci and enterococci, due to the inoculum effect, while cation concentrations in media (e.g., calcium and magnesium) can alter results.

Adverse effects

Common side effects

Vancomycin's common side effects are typically mild and route-dependent, occurring in 510% of patients overall, though gastrointestinal issues with oral use can affect up to 20%.
When given orally, primarily for treating Clostridioides difficile-associated diarrhea, vancomycin exhibits low systemic absorption, confining most effects to the gastrointestinal tract. Frequently reported reactions include nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and a bitter or unpleasant taste (dysgeusia). Clinical data indicate nausea and abdominal pain in 10% of patients receiving oral capsules, with these symptoms often resolving without intervention due to the drug's localized action.
Intravenous administration, used for systemic infections, commonly leads to infusion-related reactions such as chills, fever, nausea, vomiting, and phlebitis at the injection site. These effects, including histamine-mediated flushing (related to red man syndrome), have an incidence of 3.747%, influenced by infusion speed and dose. Phlebitis occurs in 110% of peripheral infusions, more frequently with rapid delivery.
Management strategies focus on prevention and symptom relief. For intravenous reactions, infusing doses over at least 60 minutes reduces incidence significantly, with antihistamines like diphenhydramine used prophylactically in high-risk cases if needed. Oral gastrointestinal effects are managed symptomatically, such as with antiemetics for nausea, and switching to liquid formulations may alleviate taste disturbances.

Serious side effects

Serious hypersensitivity reactions to vancomycin are uncommon, occurring in less than 1% of patients, but can be life-threatening and include anaphylaxis, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, and drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms (DRESS). Anaphylaxis, an IgE-mediated type I reaction, may present with urticaria, angioedema, bronchospasm, and hypotension shortly after administration. Stevens-Johnson syndrome and DRESS represent severe cutaneous adverse reactions involving mucocutaneous lesions, fever, and multi-organ involvement, often requiring immediate discontinuation of the drug and supportive care.
Hematologic adverse effects from vancomycin include thrombocytopenia, neutropenia, and eosinophilia, which are immune-mediated and typically reversible upon drug cessation. Thrombocytopenia, characterized by a platelet count below 150,000/L, has an incidence of approximately 3-5% and may lead to bleeding risks in vulnerable patients. Neutropenia, with an incidence of 2-8%, usually develops after prolonged therapy (beyond 7-12 days) and increases infection susceptibility. Eosinophilia often accompanies DRESS or other hypersensitivity states but can occur independently.
A prominent infusion-related reaction is red man syndrome (also known as vancomycin flushing syndrome), a non-IgE-mediated histamine release causing flushing, pruritus, nausea, vomiting, fever, chills, and sometimes hypotension or chest pain, primarily during rapid intravenous infusion. The incidence can reach 80-90% when vancomycin is infused at rates exceeding 10 mg/min (e.g., 1 g over 10 minutes), but it is largely preventable by extending infusion to 1-2 hours. This reaction typically resolves within 20 minutes to hours after slowing the infusion rate or pausing administration, and premedication with antihistamines may mitigate symptoms in recurrent cases.
Risk factors for these serious effects include higher vancomycin doses (>4 g/day), rapid infusion rates, multiple prior exposures, and concurrent use of drugs like neuromuscular blockers or other nephrotoxic agents, which may exacerbate hypersensitivity or hematologic issues. Patients with a history of atopy or previous reactions warrant close monitoring, as these factors can heighten the likelihood of severe outcomes.

Nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity

Vancomycin is associated with nephrotoxicity, primarily manifesting as acute kidney injury (AKI) through mechanisms involving oxidative stress and proximal tubular damage. This toxicity arises from high intracellular accumulation of the drug in renal tubular cells, leading to reactive oxygen species generation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and subsequent cellular injury. The incidence of vancomycin-induced nephrotoxicity varies from 5-35% depending on therapy duration and monitoring approach, with higher rates associated with area under the curve (AUC) values exceeding 650 mgh/L.
Ototoxicity from vancomycin is characterized by irreversible hearing loss or vestibular dysfunction, though it remains a rare complication with an incidence of less than 1%. This adverse effect is typically linked to elevated serum concentrations above 40 g/mL or extended exposure durations, potentially involving direct damage to cochlear hair cells or synergistic effects with other ototoxic agents.
Several risk factors heighten the likelihood of both nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity, including advanced age, obesity, dehydration, and concurrent use of other nephrotoxic or ototoxic agents such as aminoglycosides and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs). Pharmacokinetic interactions can exacerbate these risks; for instance, the combination of vancomycin with piperacillin-tazobactam has been shown to significantly increase the incidence of AKI compared to vancomycin monotherapy or with alternative beta-lactams. Therapeutic drug monitoring using AUC targets (400-600 mgh/L) is preferred for mitigating these toxicities, as it better correlates with efficacy and safety compared to traditional trough monitoring.

Administration and dosing

Routes of administration

Vancomycin is primarily administered intravenously for the treatment of systemic bacterial infections, as this route ensures effective distribution throughout the body while minimizing local tissue irritation when delivered via a secure intravenous line with slow infusion to prevent phlebitis and other infusion-related reactions.
Orally, vancomycin is used specifically for Clostridioides difficile-associated diarrhea, where its poor systemic absorption allows it to achieve high concentrations directly in the gastrointestinal tract without significant entry into the bloodstream.
Off-label inhaled administration involves aerosolization of the intravenous formulation via nebulizer, primarily for targeting methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus infections in the lower respiratory tract, such as in ventilator-associated pneumonia or cystic fibrosis, thereby providing high local lung concentrations with reduced systemic exposure.
Rectal administration as retention enemas is employed off-label in cases of severe Clostridioides difficile infection where oral intake is not feasible, such as with ileus, to deliver the drug directly to the colon.
In patients undergoing peritoneal dialysis, intraperitoneal administration of vancomycin is utilized for managing peritonitis, allowing direct delivery to the peritoneal cavity for optimal local efficacy.

Dosing considerations

Vancomycin dosing must account for patient-specific factors such as renal function, body weight, age, and the severity of infection to optimize efficacy while minimizing toxicity. For adults with normal renal function, intravenous administration typically begins with a loading dose of 20 to 35 mg/kg actual body weight for serious infections, followed by maintenance doses of 15 to 20 mg/kg every 8 to 12 hours. This approach targets an area under the curve to minimum inhibitory concentration (AUC/MIC) ratio of 400 to 600 mgh/L for invasive methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) infections, which correlates with improved clinical outcomes. Oral vancomycin, primarily indicated for Clostridioides difficile-associated diarrhea, is dosed at 125 mg every 6 hours for 10 days in adults.
Adjustments for renal impairment involve prolonging the dosing interval based on creatinine clearance (CrCl); for instance, patients with CrCl 50 to 80 mL/min may receive maintenance doses every 12 hours, while those with CrCl 10 to 50 mL/min require dosing every 24 to 48 hours. In obese patients, actual body weight should not exceed ideal body weight by more than 40% for calculations, or adjusted body weight can be used to prevent over-dosing and associated risks. Pediatric dosing for children over 1 month is generally 15 mg/kg intravenously every 6 hours, with total daily doses not exceeding 60 to 70 mg/kg in severe cases. For critically ill adults, higher loading doses of 25 to 35 mg/kg are recommended to account for increased volume of distribution and potential augmented renal clearance.
Vancomycin is formulated as a generic intravenous powder for reconstitution in vials (typically 500 mg, 750 mg, or 1 g), administered by intermittent or continuous infusion, and as oral capsules (125 mg or 250 mg) or an oral solution for gastrointestinal infections. Extended-infusion protocols, such as administering maintenance doses over 2 to 3 hours or via continuous infusion, have been adopted to better achieve target AUC values and enhance efficacy in serious infections.

Therapeutic drug monitoring

Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM) of vancomycin is crucial for ensuring therapeutic efficacy against serious infections, such as methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), while reducing the risk of adverse effects like nephrotoxicity. The 2020 consensus guidelines from the American Society of Health-System Pharmacists (ASHP), Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA), Pediatric Infectious Diseases Society (PIDS), and Society of Infectious Diseases Pharmacists (SIDP) emphasize TDM to guide dosing adjustments based on individual patient factors.
Key monitoring parameters include serum trough concentrations and the area under the concentration-time curve (AUC). For serious infections, trough levels are traditionally targeted at 1020 g/mL (measured just before the next dose), but the guidelines strongly recommend AUC monitoring with a target of 400600 mgh/L, as it better predicts clinical outcomes and toxicity risk. AUC calculation typically relies on Bayesian dosing software, which uses two serum levels (e.g., one shortly after infusion and one midway) and patient-specific data like renal function to estimate exposure. Peak concentrations are rarely monitored, as they offer limited value for efficacy or safety assessment.
Measurements should be obtained early in therapy, preferably within the first 24 to 48 hours after initiation, without requiring steady-state conditions when using AUC monitoring with Bayesian estimation. For trough-based monitoring in intermittent dosing, levels are often drawn before the fourth dose, but early AUC assessment is preferred. TDM is indicated for all patients receiving vancomycin for serious infections, as well as those on prolonged therapy (>35 days), with renal dysfunction, or at high risk for toxicity.
If monitored levels exceed targets (e.g., trough >20 g/mL or AUC >600 mgh/L), doses should be reduced or intervals extended to mitigate toxicity risks, with rechecking after adjustments. The ASHP/IDSA guidelines specify monitoring frequencydaily for unstable patients and every 12 days for stable onesand stress early intervention to prevent nephrotoxicity.

Pharmacology

Mechanism of action

Vancomycin exerts its bactericidal activity against Gram-positive bacteria by disrupting cell wall synthesis through specific binding to peptidoglycan precursors. It targets the D-Ala-D-Ala terminus of these precursors, particularly lipid II, which is essential for peptidoglycan assembly, thereby preventing the formation of the rigid cell wall structure required for bacterial survival. This inhibition leads to accumulation of uncross-linked peptidoglycan units and eventual osmotic lysis of the bacterial cell.
The structural basis of this interaction lies in vancomycin's glycopeptide framework, where its rigid heptapeptide core forms a binding pocket that engages the substrate via hydrogen bonding. Specifically, five backbone-to-backbone hydrogen bonds between the antibiotic's peptide residues and the D-Ala-D-Ala dipeptide provide high-affinity sequestration, with a dissociation constant in the nanomolar range. This precise molecular recognition sterically hinders the precursors from interacting with cell wall-synthesizing enzymes.
By occupying the D-Ala-D-Ala terminus, vancomycin inhibits two key enzymatic steps in peptidoglycan maturation: transglycosylation, which polymerizes glycan chains from lipid II units, and transpeptidation, which cross-links adjacent peptidoglycan stems to form a stable meshwork. These disruptions weaken the cell wall, rendering bacteria susceptible to lysis, particularly during active growth phases.
Vancomycin's activity is restricted to Gram-positive bacteria due to its inability to penetrate the outer membrane of Gram-negative organisms, a barrier that excludes large hydrophilic molecules like the antibiotic (molecular weight approximately 1,450 Da). This selectivity ensures targeted action against pathogens such as staphylococci and enterococci while sparing Gram-negative species.

Pharmacokinetics

Vancomycin exhibits negligible oral bioavailability, typically less than 5%, necessitating intravenous administration for systemic infections, while oral dosing is reserved for gastrointestinal indications such as Clostridium difficile-associated diarrhea.
Following intravenous administration, vancomycin distributes widely in body tissues with a volume of distribution ranging from 0.4 to 1 L/kg in adults with normal renal function. It achieves good penetration into bone and joint fluids, making it suitable for treating osteomyelitis and septic arthritis, but cerebrospinal fluid penetration is poor (less than 1% of serum levels) unless the meninges are inflamed. Protein binding is moderate, approximately 3055%, primarily to albumin.
Vancomycin undergoes no significant metabolism in the body and is excreted primarily unchanged in the urine via glomerular filtration, accounting for about 8090% of the dose in patients with normal renal function. Its clearance is closely correlated with creatinine clearance (CrCl), with an approximate relationship of vancomycin clearance (mL/min) 0.695 CrCl (mL/min) + 0.05. The elimination half-life in individuals with normal renal function is 46 hours.
In special populations, the half-life of vancomycin is prolonged in neonates (up to 1020 hours due to immature renal function), the elderly (often exceeding 6 hours owing to reduced glomerular filtration rate), and obese patients (where dosing adjustments based on ideal body weight are recommended to account for altered volume of distribution). Renal impairment significantly extends the half-life, reaching 7.5 days in anephric patients, underscoring the need for dose adjustments based on renal function assessments.

Antibiotic resistance

Vancomycin exhibits intrinsic resistance in Gram-negative bacteria primarily due to the impermeability of their outer membrane, which prevents the hydrophilic glycopeptide from accessing its intracellular target site on the peptidoglycan precursors. Additionally, certain species of enterococci, such as Enterococcus gallinarum and Enterococcus casseliflavus, display intrinsic low-level resistance to vancomycin through the expression of VanC gene clusters, which modify the peptidoglycan terminus from D-Ala-D-Ala to D-Ala-D-Ser, resulting in a modest reduction in drug affinity.
Acquired resistance to vancomycin is predominantly mediated by the VanA and VanB gene clusters in enterococci, leading to the production of vancomycin-resistant enterococci (VRE). These clusters encode enzymes that reprogram cell wall synthesis by replacing the D-Ala-D-Ala dipeptide terminus with D-Ala-D-Lac, which decreases vancomycin's binding affinity by approximately 1,000-fold and thereby inhibits the drug's ability to block transpeptidation. In Staphylococcus aureus, acquired high-level resistance manifests as vancomycin-resistant S. aureus (VRSA), typically arising from the horizontal transfer of the VanA operon from co-infecting VRE strains via plasmids, often in conjunction with the mecA gene for methicillin resistance. A related but distinct phenotype is vancomycin-intermediate S. aureus (VISA), characterized by heterogeneous resistance with minimum inhibitory concentrations (MICs) of 48 g/mL; this arises from metabolic adaptations, including thickened cell walls that trap the drug and reduce its effective concentration at the target site.
The prevalence of VRE among enterococcal isolates in U.S. hospitals is substantial, with approximately 30% of healthcare-associated enterococcal infections being due to VRE (as of 2021), while VRSA remains exceedingly rare, with about 16 cases reported in the US as of 2023. These resistance patterns have significant clinical implications, contributing to higher rates of treatment failure in severe infections such as bacteremia and endocarditis, and necessitating alternative therapies like daptomycin, linezolid, or tigecycline, though challenges persist due to emerging cross-resistance.

Chemistry and production

Chemical structure

Vancomycin is a complex tricyclic glycopeptide consisting of a linear heptapeptide backbone composed of seven amino acids, five of which are non-proteinogenic and cross-linked via phenolic and biaryl ether bonds to form a rigid tricyclic core structure. This core is substituted with two chlorine atoms at positions 2 and 6 on the aromatic rings, enhancing structural stability, and bears a disaccharide moiety at the para position of one aromatic ring, comprising -D-glucose linked to vancosamine, an unusual amino sugar. The molecular formula of vancomycin is C66H75Cl2N9O24, with a molecular weight of 1449 Da.
As a type I glycopeptide, vancomycin was originally isolated from the actinomycete bacterium Amycolatopsis orientalis (previously classified as Streptomyces orientalis), highlighting its natural origins through microbial biosynthesis. The compound exhibits amphoteric properties due to multiple ionizable groups, including amino and carboxylic acid functionalities, which influence its solubility profile. It is poorly soluble in water at neutral pH but readily dissolves in acidic or basic conditions, and it is most stable and commonly administered as the hydrochloride salt to improve aqueous solubility and prevent degradation.
Vancomycin is sensitive to heat, with thermal decomposition observed around 90C, and to light exposure, which can lead to photodegradation over extended periods, necessitating protection during storage and handling.

Biosynthesis

Vancomycin is produced by the Gram-positive actinomycete bacterium Amycolatopsis orientalis, formerly classified as Streptomyces orientalis. This soil-dwelling microorganism synthesizes the antibiotic as a secondary metabolite to inhibit competing bacteria.
The biosynthetic pathway is governed by a large gene cluster spanning approximately 150 kb and containing over 40 genes, which encode the necessary enzymes and regulatory elements. The core of the pathway involves non-ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPS) that assemble a linear heptapeptide backbone from seven modified amino acid building blocks, including non-proteinogenic residues such as 3,5-dihydroxyphenylglycine (DPG), 4-hydroxyphenylglycine (HPG), -hydroxytyrosine (-Hty), and L-asparagine. Three multifunctional NRPS enzymesdesignated VpsA, VpsB, and VpsCorganize seven catalytic modules in a 3-3-1 configuration, where each module activates, modifies, and condenses one amino acid unit in an assembly-line fashion. Modules 2, 4, and 5 incorporate epimerization domains to generate D-configured residues, ensuring the correct stereochemistry for subsequent folding.
Post-assembly modifications transform the linear heptapeptide into the mature vancomycin structure through a series of tailoring steps. Chlorination of phenolic rings in HPG and -Hty residues is catalyzed by a flavin-dependent halogenase (VhaA), introducing two chlorine atoms essential for activity. Cyclization occurs via three cytochrome P450 monooxygenasesOxyA, OxyB, and OxyCwhich form two aryl ether cross-links (between residues 2-4 and 4-6) and one biaryl linkage (between residues 5-7), creating the characteristic rigid, cup-shaped aglycone scaffold; OxyB initiates the process, followed sequentially by OxyA and OxyC. Additional oxidations, including formation of an epoxide ring on the seventh residue, are mediated by dedicated flavin-dependent oxidases. Glycosylation completes the maturation, with glycosyltransferase GtfD attaching an L-vancosamine sugar to the oxygen of the fourth residue's phenolic group, and GtfE subsequently adding a D-glucose moiety to the vancosamine via an -1,2 linkage; these steps enhance solubility and target binding.
Industrial production of vancomycin relies on submerged fermentation of engineered A. orientalis strains in large-scale bioreactors, typically under oxygen-limited conditions to induce the biosynthetic pathway and optimize yields. Classical mutagenesis and selection, combined with modern metabolic engineeringsuch as overexpression of precursor pathway genes (e.g., for shikimate-derived HPG) and pathway-specific regulatorshave increased titers from early isolates (around 1-5 g/L) to over 10 g/L in optimized processes, enabling commercial viability.

Total synthesis

The total synthesis of vancomycin is a landmark achievement in organic chemistry, complicated by the molecule's intricate architecture, which features seven amino acid residues, six peptide bonds, a disaccharide, and three macrocyclic rings, including biaryl ether linkages prone to atropisomerism. The primary challenges involve controlling the complex stereochemistry at 21 chiral centers and managing axial chirality in the atropisomeric biaryl units, where rotation barriers can lead to mixtures of diastereomers that compromise biological activity. These issues demand precise asymmetric synthesis and selective macrocyclization strategies to achieve the natural (3S,18S,32S,39S) configuration.
The first total synthesis of the vancomycin aglycon was accomplished by David A. Evans and coworkers in 1998, utilizing a convergent approach with asymmetric synthesis of the heptapeptide core, followed by sequential macrocyclizations via nucleophilic aromatic substitution to form the AB and CD rings, and a final diphenyl ether coupling to establish the DE ring. This route highlighted the use of protecting groups and conditions to favor kinetic control over atropisomer formation, yielding the aglycon in 22 steps from tyrosine derivatives. Independently, K. C. Nicolaou's group reported a total synthesis of the full vancomycin molecule in 1999, building on the aglycon synthesis through a triazene-directed biaryl ether formation for the key atropisomeric linkages and enzymatic glycosylation to attach the vancosamine and glucose units with high stereoselectivity. Their strategy involved 47 linear steps, emphasizing Pd-catalyzed cross-couplings and protecting group orthogonality to assemble the structure.
Subsequent efforts, including Dale L. Boger's 1999 synthesis, refined these methods but underscored the inefficiencies of full de novo synthesis, prompting a shift to semi-synthetic approaches using the aglycon or early biosynthetic intermediates as precursors for derivative modification. Modern semi-syntheses, such as those modifying the peptide backbone or sugar moieties via selective functionalizations, streamline production while allowing targeted alterations like N-methylation or lipophilic extensions. This evolution has enabled the creation of resistance-evading analogs, such as those with enhanced binding to D-Ala-D-Lac peptidoglycan precursors in vancomycin-resistant bacteria. Overall, these synthetic milestones not only validated vancomycin's structure but also facilitated analog design to restore efficacy against evolving pathogens.

Other applications

Use in plant tissue culture

Vancomycin serves as a selective antibiotic in plant tissue culture, primarily to control bacterial contamination, particularly from Gram-positive bacteria during genetic transformation protocols, and in combination with other antibiotics to control Gram-negative bacteria such as Agrobacterium tumefaciens. It is added to culture media to inhibit bacterial growth while sparing plant cells, as vancomycin binds to the D-Ala-D-Ala terminus of peptidoglycan precursors, disrupting cell wall synthesis in bacteriaa process irrelevant to plant cells, which lack peptidoglycan and instead possess cellulose-based walls. Although ineffective alone against Gram-negative Agrobacterium, vancomycin is often combined with antibiotics like cefotaxime to fully eliminate it post-transformation while minimizing impact on plant cells. This selectivity makes it valuable in micropropagation and Agrobacterium-mediated transformation, where bacterial overgrowth can compromise explant regeneration.
Typical concentrations range from 50 to 200 g/mL in solid or liquid media, though effective levels can vary from 10 to 400 g/mL depending on the plant species and bacterial strain; higher doses, such as 5001000 g/mL, have been used in early protocols for root explants but may reduce transformation efficiency. Its broad-spectrum activity against Gram-positive contaminants, combined with stability under standard culture conditions (pH 5.56.5, 25C), positions it as a reliable agent for maintaining sterility without frequent medium changes. Vancomycin is often combined with cefotaxime (a -lactam antibiotic effective against Gram-negatives) for enhanced control in transformation workflows, leveraging synergistic effects to eliminate Agrobacterium while promoting plant morphogenesis.
Despite its advantages, vancomycin exhibits limitations, including potential phytotoxicity at elevated concentrations, which can inhibit cell proliferation or reduce regeneration rates in sensitive species like chrysanthemum and tobacco. For instance, exposures above 500 g/mL have been linked to lower transformation success due to subtle deleterious effects on explant viability. In such cases, alternatives like cefotaxime alone or timentin may be preferred for their broader efficacy and reduced impact on plant growth.
Historical applications of vancomycin in plant tissue culture date to the late 1980s, with early adoption in Agrobacterium-mediated transformation of model plants like Arabidopsis thaliana, where it effectively countered bacterial persistence post-infection without hindering root-to-shoot regeneration. By the 1990s and 2000s, it became a staple in micropropagation protocols for crops and ornamentals, reflecting its integration into standard biotechnology practices for contamination-free cultures.

Veterinary uses

Vancomycin is employed in veterinary medicine on an off-label basis for the treatment of serious infections caused by methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) in companion animals, including dogs and cats, where parenteral administration is indicated for life-threatening systemic disease following culture and susceptibility testing. In equine medicine, oral vancomycin is used as a last-resort therapy for metronidazole-resistant Clostridioides difficile-associated enterocolitis, targeting enteric infections without systemic absorption. No formulations of vancomycin are approved for any veterinary species, limiting its application to cases where less critical antibiotics have failed.
Due to the risk of fostering vancomycin-resistant enterococci (VRE) and compromising human treatment efficacy, vancomycin is prohibited for use in food-producing animals by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and has been banned in the European Union since 1997 as part of broader restrictions on glycopeptide antibiotics. These measures stem from the historical use of the related glycopeptide avoparcin as a growth promoter, which accelerated resistance emergence.
In veterinary practice, alternatives to vancomycin for managing MRSA-like infections include linezolid for its activity against gram-positive pathogens or teicoplanin as another glycopeptide option, though both are similarly restricted to preserve their utility in human medicine.
Research on vancomycin resistance in animal pathogens has documented VRE isolates from livestock, companion animals, and zoo species, raising concerns about zoonotic transmission and the need for antimicrobial stewardship to mitigate spread to human populations. Studies emphasize that while vancomycin remains effective against many susceptible strains in animals, ongoing surveillance is essential to track resistance genes like vanA in enterococci from veterinary sources.

History

Discovery and development

Vancomycin was isolated in 1953 by organic chemist Edmund C. Kornfeld at Eli Lilly and Company from a soil sample collected in the interior jungles of Borneo, Indonesia, by missionary William Bouw; the antibiotic is produced by the actinomycete bacterium Amycolatopsis orientalis (originally classified as Streptomyces orientalis). The compound, initially known as 05865, was named vancomycin in reference to "vanquish," signifying its capacity to overcome resistant bacteria.
Early laboratory testing revealed vancomycin's potent activity against Gram-positive bacteria, particularly penicillin-resistant strains of Staphylococcus aureus, positioning it as a promising agent amid rising staphylococcal resistance in the 1950s. Initial chemical characterization, published in 1956, described it as a complex amphoteric tricyclic peptide with basic properties, though its full structure would not be determined until later decades.
Pre-clinical evaluations in animal models, including mice and dogs, confirmed vancomycin's efficacy against experimental staphylococcal infections, with effective doses protecting against lethal challenges; however, early impure formulations raised significant concerns over toxicity, notably nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity observed at higher doses.

Clinical introduction and evolution

Vancomycin received approval from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 1958 for intravenous administration to treat severe staphylococcal infections, marking its entry as a critical glycopeptide antibiotic for gram-positive bacterial infections resistant to other agents. This approval was based on open-label clinical data demonstrating efficacy against drug-resistant staphylococci, positioning vancomycin as a vital option in an era of emerging antibiotic challenges. Initially reserved for life-threatening cases due to early concerns over toxicity and impurities in the formulation, its use expanded cautiously in hospital settings.
By the 1980s, vancomycin's clinical application surged in response to the rising prevalence of methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) infections, which drove a dramatic increase in its prescription worldwide. This period saw vancomycin become a cornerstone therapy for serious MRSA cases, with usage rising over 100-fold by the 1990s in many regions. Concurrently, oral vancomycin emerged as a preferred treatment for Clostridium difficile-associated diarrhea (CDAD) following studies in 1978 that established its high efficacy against the pathogen, leading to its routine use for this indication despite poor systemic absorption.
In the 1990s and 2000s, growing concerns over vancomycin-intermediate S. aureus (VISA) and heterogeneous VISA strains prompted enhanced monitoring protocols to address resistance challenges. The FDA label warns of the risk of nephrotoxicity with vancomycin use, particularly with high doses or concurrent nephrotoxic agents. This led to the development of consensus guidelines by the American Society of Health-System Pharmacists (ASHP), Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA), and Pediatric Infectious Diseases Society (PIDS) in 2009, recommending therapeutic drug monitoring with trough levels of 15-20 mg/L for serious infections to optimize efficacy and minimize toxicity.
In the 2020s, clinical practice evolved further with updated ASHP/IDSA guidelines in 2020 advocating area under the curve (AUC)-based monitoring over trough-only approaches to better balance efficacy against MRSA and toxicity risks, reflecting advances in pharmacokinetic modeling. These recommendations aim to target an AUC/MIC ratio of 400-600 mgh/L for improved outcomes in resistant infections. Today, generic formulations dominate the vancomycin market, with multiple manufacturers supplying the majority of intravenous and oral products following the expiration of original patents, ensuring broad accessibility while maintaining quality standards.

Research directions

New formulations and analogs

To address limitations in the pharmacokinetics and delivery of vancomycin, researchers have developed advanced formulations such as liposomal and nanoparticle systems. These encapsulate the antibiotic to enhance targeted delivery, reduce systemic toxicity, and improve bioavailability, particularly for localized infections like ocular endophthalmitis or bone-related conditions. For instance, liposomal vancomycin composed of lecithin and cholesterol has demonstrated superior antibacterial efficacy in preclinical models by prolonging drug release and minimizing off-target effects. Similarly, surface-modified nanoliposomes loaded with vancomycin exhibit enhanced bone affinity, enabling better penetration into osteomyelitis sites while maintaining stability in physiological conditions.
Semisynthetic analogs of vancomycin, including oritavancin, dalbavancin, and telavancin, represent significant advancements with structural modifications that extend half-life and optimize tissue penetration. Oritavancin incorporates an additional sugar and a lipophilic side chain, allowing for once-weekly dosing in acute bacterial skin infections. Dalbavancin features a lipophilic dimethylaminopropyl amide side chain, supporting intravenous administration every 7-14 days with reduced frequency compared to standard vancomycin regimens. Telavancin includes a decylaminoethyl side chain and a phosphono group, which contribute to its prolonged plasma exposure and activity against Gram-positive pathogens. All three have received FDA approval for treating skin and soft tissue infections, leveraging these modifications for improved clinical utility.
Halogenated derivatives of vancomycin focus on modifying the existing aryl chloride moieties to strengthen interactions with bacterial cell wall precursors. Efforts involving selective dechlorination followed by cross-coupling at the C6 position have yielded analogs with altered binding profiles, potentially increasing potency through precise functional group introductions. These modifications, often enabled by total synthesis approaches, aim to fine-tune the molecule's affinity without compromising its core structure.
In terms of clinical progress, new oral formulations of vancomycin, such as FIRVANQ (vancomycin hydrochloride for oral solution), advanced through phase III trials for Clostridium difficile-associated diarrhea (CDAD) and were approved by the FDA in 2018. Liposomal nanocarriers for oral delivery of vancomycin derivatives like FU002 are under investigation, showing promise in preclinical studies for systemic absorption and reduced gastrointestinal side effects.

Strategies to combat resistance

One prominent strategy to restore vancomycin's efficacy involves chemical redesigns that enhance its penetration into bacterial cells, particularly against resistant strains. The vancomycin-arginine conjugate (V-R), which attaches a single arginine molecule to vancomycin, improves membrane permeability and targets cell-wall synthesis in Gram-negative bacteria like carbapenem-resistant Escherichia coli, as well as vancomycin-resistant enterococci (VRE). This conjugate has demonstrated reduced minimum inhibitory concentrations (MICs) against actively growing resistant pathogens in vitro and in vivo, inducing morphological changes such as loss of rod shape in bacteria, thereby regaining activity where native vancomycin fails.
Combination therapies pairing vancomycin with beta-lactam antibiotics, such as ceftaroline, exploit synergistic effects to overcome vancomycin-intermediate Staphylococcus aureus (VISA) and vancomycin-resistant S. aureus (VRSA). In clinical case series, this combination resolved refractory MRSA bacteremia in patients unresponsive to vancomycin monotherapy, with in vitro studies showing synergy against multiple VISA isolates by enhancing cell-wall disruption. Early administration of vancomycin plus ceftaroline has been associated with improved mortality outcomes in complicated MRSA infections compared to standard therapies. Similarly, pairings with daptomycin or other agents have shown promise in salvage treatment for persistent VRE infections.
Efforts to develop resistance inhibitors focus on disrupting key resistance mechanisms, such as the VanA enzyme complex that modifies peptidoglycan precursors in VRE. D-alanine acts as a substrate inhibitor of VanA ligase, reducing vancomycin resistance by competing with D-alanine-D-lactate incorporation into cell walls, thereby lowering MICs in resistant enterococci. Chlorobiphenyl vancomycin analogues, despite lacking substrate-binding capacity, inhibit the transglycosylase activity of penicillin-binding protein 1b (PBP1b), a target in VanA-resistant strains, restoring susceptibility without direct glycopeptide binding. Compounds targeting efflux pumps or VanA induction pathways are also under investigation to block resistance gene expression and antibiotic expulsion.
Global surveillance programs play a crucial role in tracking vancomycin resistance trends to inform targeted interventions. The World Health Organization's Global Antimicrobial Resistance and Use Surveillance System (GLASS) collects standardized data on vancomycin-resistant pathogens like VRE and VRSA from national laboratories, enabling the monitoring of prevalence and guiding policy on antimicrobial stewardship. As of 2025, GLASS reports indicate rising antibiotic resistance globally between 2018 and 2023, with an average annual increase of 5-15% in over 40% of monitored antibiotics, and vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus faecium listed as a high-priority pathogen in the WHO Bacterial Priority Pathogens List 2024, underscoring the ongoing need for enhanced diagnostic and therapeutic strategies.
Emerging future approaches include phage therapy and CRISPR-based engineering to bolster vancomycin's utility. Bacteriophages combined with vancomycin eradicate VRE more effectively than either alone, as phages lyse resistant cells while the antibiotic prevents regrowth, with studies showing complete clearance in murine models of infection. For antibiotic production, CRISPR-Cas9 editing in vancomycin-producing Amycolatopsis orientalis enables rapid modification of biosynthetic gene clusters, potentially yielding variants with improved activity against resistant strains. Additionally, CRISPR systems targeting the vanA gene in resistant bacteria have cured plasmids carrying resistance determinants, sensitizing VRE to vancomycin in preclinical settings.
