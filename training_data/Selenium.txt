Selenium

 Selenium is a chemical element with the atomic number 34 and the symbol Se, classified as a nonmetal in group 16 of the periodic table, known as the chalcogens. It exists in several allotropic forms, including gray metallic, red crystalline, and amorphous red powder, with the gray form being the most stable under standard conditions. Chemically similar to sulfur and tellurium, selenium has an atomic mass of 78.96 and plays essential roles in biological systems at trace levels while being toxic at higher concentrations.
Discovered in 1817 by Swedish chemist Jns Jacob Berzelius while investigating residues from a sulfuric acid factory, selenium was named after the Greek word for moon, selene, due to its resemblance to tellurium (from Latin tellus, meaning earth). Berzelius identified it as a new element after noting its distinct properties, such as emitting a garlic-like odor when burned, which aided in its identification and initial isolation from industrial residues in sulfuric acid production; it is now primarily obtained from copper refinery byproducts. This discovery marked an early advancement in analytical chemistry, highlighting selenium's presence in industrial processes despite its rarity in Earth's crust at about 0.05 parts per million.
Physically, selenium has a density of 4.81 g/cm for its gray allotrope, a melting point of 221C, and a boiling point of 685C; it is unique among nonmetals for its semiconductor properties, conducting electricity better when exposed to light, which underpins its use in photocells. Chemically, it exhibits oxidation states of -2, +4, and +6, reacting with halogens, oxygen to form selenium dioxide, and acids like concentrated nitric acid, but it is insoluble in water. Selenium occurs naturally in over 40 minerals, often associated with sulfide ores, and is commercially produced as a byproduct of copper refining, with annual global output of approximately 3,700 tonnes (as of 2024) from major producers including China, Japan, the United States, Germany, and Canada.
Selenium's applications span electronics, where it is used in photovoltaic cells, rectifiers, and photocopiers due to its photoconductive qualities; the glass industry, for decolorizing and imparting red hues; and alloys, rubber vulcanization, and pigments. In agriculture, sodium selenite supplements animal feeds to prevent deficiency-related diseases.
Biologically, selenium is an essential trace element incorporated into selenoproteins, which function as antioxidants, support thyroid hormone metabolism, DNA synthesis, reproduction, and immune response. The recommended dietary allowance for adults is 55 micrograms per day, primarily obtained from seafood, meats, grains, and Brazil nuts, with deficiency linked to conditions like Keshan disease (a cardiomyopathy) and increased oxidative stress. However, excessive intake above 400 micrograms per day can cause selenosis, characterized by hair and nail brittleness, gastrointestinal distress, and a garlic-like breath odor. Environmentally, selenium bioaccumulates in aquatic ecosystems from industrial discharges, potentially leading to reproductive issues in wildlife at elevated levels.

Properties

Physical properties

Selenium exists in several allotropic forms, each exhibiting distinct physical characteristics. The most stable and common allotrope is gray selenium, which appears as a metallic, lustrous solid with a crystalline structure. Other forms include red (monoclinic or amorphous), black (vitreous amorphous), and yellow (amorphous powder), with the red and yellow variants being less stable and often prepared under specific conditions.
The density of gray selenium is 4.81 g/cm at room temperature, while the red form has a density of 4.39 g/cm and the black vitreous form 4.28 g/cm. The melting point of the gray allotrope is 221 C, and its boiling point is 685 C; the red form melts at a similar 221 C, but the black form softens around 180 C before transitioning.
Gray selenium adopts a hexagonal crystal structure, consisting of helical chains of selenium atoms arranged in a trigonal lattice, which contributes to its metallic appearance and relative stability. In contrast, the red allotrope features a monoclinic structure, and the black form is amorphous without long-range order.
As a p-type semiconductor, gray selenium exhibits notable electrical conductivity that can increase by up to 1000 times upon exposure to light, a property known as photoconductivity. This behavior arises from its band gap of approximately 2 eV, allowing photoexcitation of electrons. Other allotropes, such as red and amorphous forms, are generally insulators.
Elemental selenium is insoluble in water and ethanol but dissolves in carbon disulfide (solubility of about 2 mg/100 mL) and concentrated sulfuric acid. It also shows solubility in concentrated nitric acid and certain organic solvents like ether.
The allotropes of selenium display varying stabilities, with the gray hexagonal form being thermodynamically the most stable at standard conditions. The red monoclinic and amorphous forms are metastable and undergo transitions to the gray phase upon heating: the vitreous black form converts around 180 C, and the alpha red form above 120 C. These transitions highlight the tendency toward the denser, more ordered hexagonal structure.

History

Discovery and naming

Selenium was discovered in 1817 by the Swedish chemist Jns Jacob Berzelius while investigating a reddish sediment produced during the manufacture of sulfuric acid from pyrite at a factory in Gripsholm, Sweden. Berzelius, who held shares in the plant, noted the deposit's garlic-like odor and initial resemblance to tellurium, a recently identified element, leading to early confusion about its identity. Through detailed chemical analysis, including blowpipe tests and precipitation reactions on a small sample obtained by roasting 200 kg of sulfur-rich sludge to yield about 3 g of precipitate, he confirmed it as a distinct new element in his Stockholm laboratory.
Berzelius named the element selenium after the Greek word seln (moon), drawing an analogy to tellurium, which had been named from the Latin tellus (earth) by its discoverer Martin Heinrich Klaproth. This nomenclature highlighted the perceived pairing of the two elements, much like the earth and moon. In his 1818 publication in Afhandlingar i Fysik, Kemi och Mineralogi, Berzelius detailed the element's properties and described 90 of its compounds, including selenic acid and hydrogen selenide, solidifying its place as element 34.
The discovery gained further validation in the 1820s through independent work by other chemists and corroborative analyses by contemporaries, which affirmed selenium's chemical distinctiveness and atomic weight, establishing it firmly within the periodic table of elements.

Industrial development

Commercial production of selenium began in the early 20th century as a byproduct of copper refining, with the first extraction in the United States occurring in 1910 from anode slimes, yielding approximately 5 metric tons. This process recovered selenium from residues generated during the electrolytic refining of copper, where it accumulates as an impurity substituting for sulfur in sulfide ores. Over 90% of selenium has since been derived from copper production, marking its initial economic viability tied to the expanding nonferrous metals industry.
During the 1910s and 1920s, selenium's industrial applications grew significantly in the glass and pigment sectors. In 1915, it was adopted as a decolorizer for glass to counteract green tints from iron impurities, with U.S. consumption reaching 50 metric tons by 1918 amid shortages of alternative materials like manganese during World War I. Pigments such as cadmium sulfoselenide also emerged for paints, ceramics, and red glass, driving further demand as these industries expanded with urbanization and consumer goods production. World War I accelerated this growth by disrupting supply chains for competing decolorizers, leading to excess selenium stockpiles from copper refining that were later processed in the interwar period.
The World Wars further intensified selenium's role in electronics and metallurgy. During World War II, demand surged for selenium rectifiersmetal-based devices invented in 1933 for converting alternating to direct currentused in military power supplies, communication systems, and radar equipment. These rectifiers offered advantages over vacuum tubes in size and reliability, with widespread adoption in wartime projects contributing to post-war civilian applications in radios and televisions. Additionally, selenium was alloyed with copper, lead, and steel to enhance machinability and strength, supporting munitions and machinery production.
After 1950, selenium's applications shifted toward electronics, with rectifier use peaking in the 1950s for consumer devices before declining due to silicon alternatives. A major milestone came in 1959 with the introduction of the first commercial xerographic copier by Xerox, utilizing selenium drums for photoconductivity in imaging, which became the largest single use of selenium through the 1970s and 1980s. In photovoltaics, early selenium-based solar cells from the 1880s were pioneering but achieved only low efficiencies of around 1%; by the 1960s, silicon-based cells had achieved significantly higher conversion rates up to 14%, leading to silicon's dominance. U.S. production peaked at 565 metric tons in 1969, reflecting this electronics boom.
In recent years, the selenium market has recovered, with global production estimated at approximately 3,700 metric tons in 2024, driven by electronics applications in semiconductors, sensors, and displays. This represents a projected compound annual growth rate of 4.26% through 2030, fueled by demand in high-tech manufacturing and renewable energy components.

Occurrence

Natural sources

Selenium occurs primarily in nature as selenide minerals, which are often found in hydrothermal deposits and low-sulfur environments. Notable examples include clausthalite (PbSe), a lead selenide that resembles galena and forms in hydrothermal veins, and naumannite (AgSe), a silver selenide typically associated with other silver minerals in epithermal deposits. Other selenides, such as berzelianite (CuSe) and crookesite ((Cu,Ag)Se), also contribute to its mineralogical presence, substituting for sulfur in analogous sulfide structures.
These selenide minerals are commonly associated with sulfide ores of copper, lead, and nickel, where selenium substitutes for sulfur in the crystal lattice, often at concentrations of several parts per million. For instance, selenium is recovered as a byproduct from copper anode slimes during electrolytic refining, with similar occurrences in lead and nickel sulfide deposits. This association underscores selenium's geochemical affinity for chalcophile elements in ore-forming processes.
In soils, selenium is predominantly present as soluble oxyanions, including selenates (SeO) and selenites (SeO), which result from the oxidation of selenides during weathering. Selenate predominates in alkaline, well-oxidized soils, while selenite is more stable in acidic or neutral conditions, influencing its mobility and availability in the pedosphere.
Volcanic emissions represent a minor but significant natural source, releasing selenium as volatile compounds during magmatic degassing and fumarolic activity. Similarly, fossil fuels such as coal and oil contain trace selenium, primarily as organic selenides or elemental forms, derived from ancient sedimentary deposition.
Global reserves of selenium are estimated at 92,000 metric tons, primarily tied to identified copper deposits. These reserves are distributed across major copper-producing regions, including Peru (16,000 tons), Russia (26,000 tons), and the United States (10,000 tons), though actual recoverability depends on associated metal production.

Abundance and distribution

Selenium occurs at low concentrations throughout Earth's geochemical reservoirs, reflecting its chalcophile nature and limited volatility during planetary formation. In the Earth's crust, its average abundance is 0.05 parts per million (ppm), which is three to seven times greater than that of the rare earth elements and similar to the abundances of rhenium, arsenic, and germanium, positioning it as a relatively rare trace element compared to more common metals like copper or zinc. This scarcity arises from selenium's affinity for partitioning into the core and mantle during differentiation, leaving residual amounts in the silicate crust primarily bound to sulfide minerals.
In oceanic environments, dissolved selenium concentrations average around 0.17 parts per billion (ppb), though they vary significantly with depth due to redox conditions and biological processes. Surface waters often exhibit depletion to about 0.05 ppb from phytoplankton uptake, while deeper layers (>1,000 m) show enrichment up to 0.17 ppb as remineralization releases bound selenium. Soil concentrations mirror crustal patterns but are modulated by weathering and parent rock composition, typically ranging from 0.1 to 2 ppm globally; elevated levels occur in seleniferous regions derived from selenium-rich shales, such as the Phosphoria Formation in Wyoming, USA, and black shales in Enshi, China.
Beyond Earth, selenium's cosmic abundance in the solar system is estimated at approximately 20 ppm by mass, based on analyses of CI carbonaceous chondrites that approximate primitive solar material. Atmospheric concentrations remain trace, at 0.1 to 1 nanograms per cubic meter, mainly as particulate selenites or volatile organoselenium compounds emitted from soils, oceans, and biomass burning. Selenium's distribution is dynamically influenced by its geochemical cycle, where mobility depends on oxidation states from -2 (selenide, immobile in reducing sediments) to +6 (selenate, highly soluble in oxic waters), enabling transport through weathering, volatilization, and deposition processes that link crustal, oceanic, and atmospheric reservoirs.

Production

Extraction methods

Selenium is primarily extracted as a byproduct from the processing of copper, lead, nickel, and zinc ores, with the majority originating from electrolytic copper refining where it concentrates in anode slimes. These slimes, containing 5-20% selenium, are collected from the bottom of electrolytic cells during the refining of impure copper anodes. Global production of refined selenium reached an estimated 3,700 metric tons in 2024, predominantly led by China (1,800 tons, approximately 50%) and Japan (710 tons).
In the dominant process from electrolytic copper refining, anode slimes are first roasted in a sulfation step at 500-600C to convert selenium to volatile selenium dioxide (SeO), which is captured from the off-gases. The SeO is then absorbed in water to form selenous acid and reduced to elemental selenium using sulfur dioxide (SO) gas in an acidic solution, precipitating high-purity selenium that can be filtered and dried. An alternative pyrometallurgical approach involves soda ash roasting of the slimes at 530-650C, converting selenium to soluble sodium selenate (NaSeO), which is leached and subsequently reduced to elemental form using charcoal under controlled air oxidation.
Selenium recovery from lead smelting operations occurs through treatment of refinery slimes or residues, which contain selenium from the processing of lead ores. These slimes are subjected to soda ash roasting, similar to the copper process, where sodium carbonate facilitates the conversion of selenium compounds to soluble selenates that are extracted via leaching, followed by reduction to elemental selenium. This method is applied to the sludges generated in lead electrorefining or smelting byproducts, ensuring efficient isolation before further processing.
Hydrometallurgical routes are employed for selenium extraction from nickel and copper ores, particularly in operations involving anode slimes or metallurgical middlings from nickel refining. These processes typically involve acid leaching of the slimes under pressure or oxidative conditions to dissolve selenium into solution as selenious acid, followed by selective precipitation using SO at elevated temperatures (around 70C) over several hours. The resulting selenium precipitate is then purified, often via vacuum distillation, to achieve grades exceeding 99.5%. Such methods are particularly relevant in integrated nickel-copper operations, where selenium co-occurs with base metals in processing residues.

Refining processes

Refining processes for selenium focus on purifying crude material recovered from copper anode slimes or other sources, targeting the removal of impurities such as tellurium, sulfur, mercury, and trace metals to achieve commercial grades typically exceeding 99.5% purity. These methods address contamination introduced during initial extraction, employing thermal, chemical, and electrochemical techniques to volatilize or separate impurities while minimizing environmental impact. Common challenges include handling volatile selenium compounds and ensuring efficient recovery to support industrial demands in electronics and alloys.
One established purification route involves the distillation of selenium dioxide (SeO) vapor, generated by roasting crude selenium or selenides with sulfuric acid and air at elevated temperatures to oxidize and volatilize SeO, which is then captured in scrubbers or condensers. The collected SeO is subsequently dissolved in water to form selenous acid (HSeO) and reduced aqueously using agents like sulfur dioxide (SO), hydrazine hydrate, or copper powder at 3040C to precipitate elemental selenium, yielding purities up to 99.99%. This process effectively separates tellurium and other non-volatile impurities, with sublimation variants conducted at atmospheric pressure around 380C enhancing selectivity for high-purity applications.
Carbon reduction represents another thermal method, where crude selenium is mixed with soda ash or sodium carbonate and roasted at 530650C to form soluble sodium selenate (NaSeO), followed by reduction with charcoal (carbon) to sodium selenide (NaSe) and reoxidation with air to elemental selenium. Performed in controlled furnaces, this step operates around 500600C to volatilize impurities like arsenic and antimony, producing commercial-grade selenium with reduced tellurium content. The process is energy-efficient for large-scale operations but requires careful control to avoid over-reduction.
For ultra-high-purity selenium (99.99% or greater), electrolytic refining employs crude selenium as an anode in an aqueous electrolyte, typically sulfuric acid or selenious acid solutions, where selenium dissolves and redeposits on the cathode, leaving impurities in the anode slime or solution. This electrochemical approach achieves 99.999% purity by selectively depositing selenium while rejecting metals like copper and iron, and is particularly suited for semiconductor-grade material. Vacuum distillation complements this for final polishing, removing residual volatiles at low pressures (e.g., 10100 Pa) and temperatures of 450550C.
Commercially, high-purity selenium powder (99.99%) is widely available through wholesale platforms such as Alibaba.com, which lists approximately 929 products of this specification, primarily from Chinese suppliers with additional offerings from the United States and Belgium. Prices range from approximately $40 to $450 per kilogram, depending on order quantity, minimum order requirements, and supplier. This form is marketed for industrial uses including glass manufacturing, electronics, chemicals, solar energy, and metallurgy.
Emerging recycling methods in 2024 target selenium recovery from spent catalysts and electronic waste, such as CIGS solar cells and optoelectronic devices, using closed-loop evaporation or hydrometallurgical leaching to achieve up to 98% selenium yield without generating secondary waste. These processes involve thermal evaporation at 500600C to vaporize selenium, followed by condensation and reduction, promoting circular economy practices amid rising e-waste volumes. In May 2025, RETORTE GmbH opened a new production facility in Germany, boosting capacity by approximately 20% to meet demand in high-purity sectors like pharmaceuticals. Safety considerations are paramount, especially regarding hydrogen selenide (HSe), a toxic byproduct potentially formed during reduction steps; it requires handling in ventilated enclosures with exposure limits of 0.05 ppm (OSHA PEL), positive-pressure respirators, and monitoring to prevent respiratory irritation or pulmonary edema.

Compounds

Chalcogen compounds

Selenium forms a variety of compounds with other chalcogens, primarily oxygen and sulfur, displaying chemical behaviors closely analogous to those of the corresponding sulfur compounds due to their shared group 16 position in the periodic table.
Selenium dioxide (SeO) is a prominent oxide featuring a bent, V-shaped molecular structure with a SeO bond length of approximately 1.68 and a bond angle of 111. It is commonly prepared by the direct combustion of elemental selenium in a stream of air or oxygen, yielding the white crystalline solid that sublimes readily at 315 C. In organic synthesis, SeO serves as a selective oxidizing agent, particularly for allylic and benzylic oxidations to introduce carbonyl groups or hydroxyl functionalities, often in the presence of peroxides to regenerate the oxidant.
Selenium trioxide (SeO) is a less stable higher oxide prepared by dehydration of anhydrous selenic acid (HSeO) with phosphorus pentoxide at 150160 C or by the reaction of sulfur trioxide with potassium selenate (KSeO). It is thermodynamically unstable with respect to the dioxide and decomposes to selenium dioxide and oxygen (2 SeO 2 SeO + O), with decomposition occurring above 50 C or upon mild heating, limiting its practical utility.
Hydrogen selenide (HSe) is a colorless, flammable, and highly toxic gas with a pungent odor, recognized as the most hazardous selenium compound due to its extreme irritancy to the respiratory tract and potential to cause pulmonary edema at concentrations as low as 0.05 ppm over an 8-hour exposure. It is typically prepared by the hydrolysis of aluminum selenide (AlSe) with water: AlSe + 6 HO 2 Al(OH) + 3 HSe. As a weak diprotic acid, HSe has pK values of approximately 3.89 (first dissociation) and 11.0 (second dissociation), making it a stronger acid than HS but still exhibiting similar reductive properties in aqueous solutions.
Polyselenide ions (Se, where n = 26) form in alkaline or reducing solutions, consisting of linear chains of selenium atoms analogous to polysulfide ions (S), and they participate in redox reactions and serve as intermediates in selenium deposition processes. These species are synthesized, for example, by dissolving selenium in solutions of selenide ions in liquid ammonia, yielding compounds like NaSe or NaSe that exhibit similar reactivity to their sulfur counterparts, including disproportionation and chain-length-dependent stability.
Mixed selenium-sulfur compounds, such as selenium disulfide (SeS), represent interchalcogen bonding and adopt polymeric structures composed of eight-membered SeS rings in the solid state, with a yellow-orange color and limited solubility in nonpolar solvents. SeS is prepared by fusing elemental selenium and sulfur or through gas-phase reactions, and it shares vulcanization-like properties with sulfur analogs, though it is more reactive toward nucleophiles due to the weaker SeS bonds.

Halogen compounds

Selenium forms binary compounds with the halogens fluorine, chlorine, bromine, and iodine, primarily in the +4 and +6 oxidation states for fluorides and +4 for the others. These halides are characterized by their volatility, which facilitates their handling and application in chemical synthesis, such as fluorination reactions. Preparation typically involves the direct combination of elemental selenium with the diatomic halogen under controlled conditions to control the oxidation state and prevent side reactions.
Selenium tetrafluoride (SeF) adopts a tetrahedral molecular geometry distorted by a lone pair on the central selenium atom, resulting in a seesaw shape. It exists as a colorless liquid at room temperature, boiling at 106 C, and is highly reactive toward moisture. SeF hydrolyzes violently with water, producing selenium dioxide and hydrogen fluoride according to the reaction SeF + 2HO SeO + 4HF. This compound is prepared by the direct fluorination of selenium with fluorine gas at elevated temperatures, often in specialized apparatus to manage the exothermic reaction. Its volatility and fluorinating ability make it valuable for introducing fluorine into organic molecules.
Selenium tetrachloride (SeCl) is a red solid that sublimes at approximately 180 C without melting, exhibiting a similar tetrahedral arrangement around selenium with a lone pair. It is moderately stable in dry conditions but decomposes in water through hydrolysis, forming selenious acid and hydrochloric acid (SeCl + 3 HO HSeO + 4 HCl). Like other tetrahalides, SeCl is synthesized via the direct reaction of selenium with chlorine gas, yielding a product that is commercially available for use in chlorination processes. Its sublimation property aids in purification and transfer during synthetic applications.
The dibromide (SeBr) and diiodide (SeI) represent selenium in the +2 oxidation state and are notably less stable than their higher-oxidation-state counterparts. SeBr is unstable, existing primarily in solution or as a volatile liquid in equilibrium with SeBr and Br, and is prone to decomposition and hydrolysis. SeI is even less stable, typically prepared in situ by reacting selenium with iodine in non-aqueous solvents, with poor thermal and hydrolytic stability limiting isolation. These compounds' reactivity underscores selenium's tendency toward higher oxidation states in halogen environments.
Higher fluorides, such as selenium hexafluoride (SeF), exhibit greater stability. SeF is a colorless gas with an octahedral structure, inert to hydrolysis even under forcing conditions due to the absence of lone pairs on selenium. It is formed by the reaction of selenium with excess fluorine at high temperatures, contrasting with the more reactive lower fluorides. This inertness makes SeF suitable for applications requiring a stable fluorinating atmosphere.

Metal selenides

Metal selenides are binary compounds formed between selenium and metals, exhibiting diverse crystal structures and properties that make them significant in mineralogy, materials science, and semiconductor technology. These compounds typically adopt either rock salt (NaCl-like) or wurtzite (hexagonal ZnS-like) lattices, depending on the metal's ionic radius and bonding characteristics, which influence their electronic and optical behaviors.
Iron selenide, particularly FeSe, is a layered compound known for its superconductivity at low temperatures, with a critical temperature of around 8 K under ambient conditions, which can be enhanced to over 30 K through doping or pressure application. This material has garnered attention in condensed matter physics due to its iron-based superconducting properties, analogous to high-temperature cuprates, and its potential in understanding unconventional superconductivity mechanisms.
Copper selenides, such as CuSe, function as p-type semiconductors with tunable electrical conductivity, arising from cation vacancies and non-stoichiometry in their structure. These compounds exhibit phase transitions between low-temperature monoclinic and high-temperature cubic forms, impacting their thermoelectric applications, though their semiconductor nature is fundamental to charge carrier transport.
Cadmium selenide (CdSe) is a II-VI semiconductor with a direct bandgap of approximately 1.74 eV, widely utilized in pigments for its bright red-orange color and in quantum dots for size-dependent optical properties due to quantum confinement effects. Its wurtzite structure enables efficient light emission and absorption, making it a cornerstone in nanoscale optoelectronics.
Zinc selenide (ZnSe) serves as an infrared-transparent material, leveraging its wide bandgap of about 2.7 eV and low absorption in the mid-infrared region, which stems from its zincblende crystal structure. This property positions ZnSe as a key component in optical lenses, windows, and laser systems operating beyond visible wavelengths.

Other inorganic compounds

Selenous acid, with the formula $ H_2SeO_3 $, is a colorless, crystalline compound that serves as a reducing agent in various chemical reactions due to its ability to donate electrons, particularly in the presence of oxidizing agents. It is typically prepared by the oxidation of elemental selenium with nitric acid, following the balanced reaction Se + 4 HNO HSeO + 4 NO + HO, which produces the acid alongside nitrogen dioxide gas. This acid exhibits moderate solubility in water and is unstable upon heating, decomposing to selenium dioxide and water.
Selenic acid, $ H_2SeO_4 $, is a highly corrosive, colorless to pale yellow liquid that acts as a strong oxidizing agent, comparable in strength to sulfuric acid but with greater reactivity toward organic materials. It is synthesized by the oxidation of selenous acid with hydrogen peroxide or by electrolytic oxidation of selenium in sulfuric acid solutions, yielding a compound that is miscible with water and forms stable salts known as selenates. Unlike selenous acid, selenic acid is thermally stable up to higher temperatures and is used in analytical chemistry for the determination of certain metals.
Selenites (SeO) and selenates (SeO) are the anions derived from selenous and selenic acids, respectively, forming salts with various cations. Selenites are generally more soluble and reducing, while selenates are stable oxidizing agents used in volumetric analysis and as oxidizing reagents in organic synthesis.
Selenocyanates, such as potassium selenocyanate $ KSeCN $, are inorganic salts featuring the pseudohalide ion $ SeCN^- $, which mimics the behavior of cyanide in coordination chemistry and precipitation reactions. These compounds are employed in analytical procedures for the gravimetric determination of metals like cobalt and palladium, where they form insoluble selenocyanate complexes that can be weighed for quantification. The ion's linear structure, with selenium bonded to carbon, contributes to its utility in spectrophotometric assays as well.
Selenium can serve as a ligand in coordination compounds, forming stable complexes with transition metals where it acts as a soft donor atom, often in organoselenium derivatives like phenylselenolate. For instance, tetrakis(phenylselenolato) complexes of the form $ $ (where M is a metal such as zinc or cadmium) exhibit tetrahedral geometries and are synthesized via ligand exchange reactions with metal halides. These complexes demonstrate selenium's preference for binding to soft metal centers, influencing their electronic and optical properties in materials science applications.

Organoselenium compounds

Organoselenium compounds feature carbon-selenium bonds and exhibit diverse reactivity due to selenium's position in the periodic table, bridging properties of sulfur and heavier chalcogens. These compounds are of significant interest in organic synthesis and medicinal chemistry for their enhanced nucleophilicity and redox properties compared to sulfur analogs.
Selenides, represented as RRR-Se-RR'R, where RRR and RR'R are organic groups, serve as key antioxidants by mimicking the activity of glutathione peroxidase enzymes. For instance, selenocysteine, the 21st proteinogenic amino acid with a $ -CH_2-SeH $ side chain, incorporates into selenoproteins to catalyze peroxide reduction, protecting cells from oxidative stress. These compounds demonstrate superior catalytic efficiency in thiol-dependent redox cycles over their sulfur counterparts.
Diselenides (RRR-Se-Se-RRR) function as structural and functional analogs to disulfides (RRR-S-S-RRR), but with weaker Se-Se bonds that facilitate easier cleavage and reformation in biological and synthetic contexts. This property enables diselenides to participate in reversible redox processes, such as thiol-diselenide exchange, which is exploited in designing responsive materials and therapeutics.
Selenoxides (R2Se=OR_2Se=OR2Se=O) play a pivotal role in the selenium variant of the Johnson-Corey-Chaykovsky reaction, where they generate ylides for the stereoselective formation of epoxides and cyclopropanes from carbonyl substrates. These intermediates offer milder conditions and higher yields in certain asymmetric syntheses compared to traditional sulfur-based methods.
Organoselenium compounds generally display higher toxicity and reactivity than sulfur analogs, primarily due to selenium's easier oxidation to higher states, such as from Se(II) to Se(IV), which promotes pro-oxidant effects in biological systems. This susceptibility to oxidation enhances their therapeutic potential as anticancer agents but necessitates careful dosing to mitigate cytotoxicity.
Recent advances as of 2025 have focused on sustainable C-Se bond formation using elemental selenium powder, enabling the direct synthesis of unsymmetrical diaryl selenides for pharmaceutical applications. For example, copper-catalyzed coupling of aryl halides, arylboronic acids, and selenium powder provides a green route to bioactive selenides with yields up to 90%, addressing scalability issues in drug development.

Applications

Glass production

Selenium plays a significant role in glass production, primarily as a decolorizing agent for soda-lime and flint glasses. Iron impurities in raw materials, such as sand, introduce a green tint due to the presence of ferrous ions (Fe), which absorb light in the red region. To counteract this, selenium is added in low concentrations, typically 1050 ppm, often in combination with small amounts of cobalt oxide (15 ppm) to achieve a neutral or clear appearance in container and tableware glass. This application has been standard since the early 20th century, leveraging selenium's ability to modify the glass melt's redox environment.
The decolorizing mechanism involves the oxidation of ferrous iron (Fe) to ferric iron (Fe) by tetravalent selenium (Se), which shifts the absorption from green to a pale yellow, nearly colorless hue. During melting and cooling, higher oxidation states of selenium (Se or Se) act as oxidants, while the reduced selenium forms species like iron selenide (FeSe), producing a complementary pinkish-brown color that masks any residual tint. This redox interplay depends on furnace atmosphere and iron content (usually 50200 ppm), with optimal results in mildly oxidizing conditions to minimize selenium volatilization (up to 8090% loss).
In addition to decolorization, selenium is employed for coloring, particularly in ruby red glass production. When combined with cadmium sulfide (CdS), it forms cadmium selenide (CdSe), a semiconductor pigment that imparts intense red hues suitable for decorative and signal glasses. Concentrations for coloring range from 0.1% to 0.5% selenium, with the ratio of CdS to CdSe controlling shades from orange to deep ruby. This process requires careful control to ensure uniform dispersion and stability in the glass matrix.
Historically, glass production dominated selenium consumption, accounting for approximately 30% of global use from the 1970s through the 1990s, driven by expanding flat and container glass industries. By the early 2000s, this share declined to around 25% due to alternatives like cerium oxide and stricter environmental regulations on selenium emissions. As of 2024, the share has further declined to 20%, reflecting continued substitution and reduced demand in major producers such as China, though it remains a key application.

Metallurgy and alloys

Selenium plays a significant role in metallurgy as a microalloying element, enhancing the machinability, castability, and forming properties of various metal alloys. In 2024, metallurgical applications, including the production of electrolytic manganese metal, accounted for approximately 40% of global selenium consumption. This usage stems from selenium's ability to modify metal structures at low concentrations, improving processing efficiency without substantially altering other mechanical properties.
In the electrolytic production of manganese metal, selenium dioxide is added to the electrolyte to promote efficient deposition, resulting in denser metal with reduced gas porosity. The selenium content in the resulting manganese metal typically ranges from 0.1% to 0.15%, which helps minimize defects like porosity during electrodeposition. This process is energy-efficient compared to alternatives like sulfur dioxide additives, though efforts are ongoing to reduce selenium usage due to environmental concerns.
For steel alloys, selenium is incorporated at levels up to 0.35% to enhance machinability, particularly in free-cutting grades like 303SE stainless steel, where it aids in chip formation and reduces tool wear during high-speed machining. This addition is especially effective in austenitic and ferritic steels, outperforming sulfur in some cases by maintaining ductility and surface finish.
In copper alloys such as brasses, selenium improves machinability while preserving strength and hot workability, often added in trace amounts to achieve free-cutting properties without significant loss in ductility. Additionally, selenium serves as a lead substitute in lead-free solders, such as tin-copper-selenium formulations, which offer low melting points around 410F (210C) and high tensile strength up to 7,130 psi for plumbing and electronics applications.

Electronics and photovoltaics

Selenium's semiconductor properties have made it a key material in electronics, particularly due to its photoconductivity, where exposure to light increases its electrical conductivity by several orders of magnitude. This property stems from its direct bandgap of approximately 1.8 eV in the gray (trigonal) allotrope, enabling efficient absorption of visible light. Amorphous selenium, with similar bandgap characteristics, was pivotal in early electronic applications.
In photoconductors, selenium enabled the development of xerography, the core technology behind photocopying. The first photoconductive drums for xerographic processes utilized thin films of amorphous selenium coated on conductive substrates, allowing electrostatic imaging by selectively discharging areas exposed to light. These selenium drums, introduced in the mid-20th century, revolutionized document reproduction by leveraging selenium's high photosensitivity and ability to retain charge in dark conditions. Stabilized amorphous selenium alloys further improved drum longevity and performance in commercial copiers.
Selenium also played a role in early rectification devices, serving as a dry rectifier in power supplies. Selenium rectifiers, consisting of a selenium layer on a metal base often involving copper selenide (CuSe) contacts, converted alternating current to direct current efficiently without vacuum tubes. These were widely used in high-current applications, including early battery chargers and eliminators, providing reliable rectification up to several amperes before the advent of silicon diodes in the 1960s.
In photovoltaics, selenium compounds form the basis of high-efficiency thin-film solar cells, notably copper indium diselenide (CuInSe, or CISe) and its gallium-alloyed variant, Cu(In,Ga)Se (CIGS). CISe, with a tunable bandgap near 1.0 eV, serves as the absorber layer in these cells, enabling strong near-infrared absorption. CIGS solar cells have achieved certified efficiencies exceeding 23% in laboratory settings, with a 2024 record of 23.64% for standard compositions. Narrow-bandgap variants, closer to pure CuInSe, reached a certified efficiency of 20.26% in 2024, demonstrating low open-circuit voltage deficits and suitability for tandem configurations. These efficiencies highlight CIGS's potential for scalable, flexible photovoltaics, with power conversion surpassing 20% in commercial prototypes.
Recent advancements in 2025 have explored perovskite-selenium hybrids to enhance stability and efficiency. Selenium-doped metal chalcogenide layers, such as in FASnI perovskite cells, improve charge transport and reduce toxicity, achieving power conversion efficiencies above 20% while addressing lead-free requirements. Hybrid structures incorporating selenium-based electron transport layers, like Ag-Au-Se chalcogenides, further boost fill factors and operational durability in flexible tandem devices.

Energy storage

Selenium plays a significant role in advanced rechargeable battery technologies, particularly as a cathode material in lithium-selenium (Li-Se) and sodium-selenium (Na-Se) systems, offering potential for high-energy-density energy storage solutions. In Li-Se batteries, elemental selenium serves as the cathode active material, undergoing a conversion reaction where selenium is reduced to lithium selenide during discharge. The overall reaction is given by:

This process yields a theoretical specific capacity of 675 mAh g for selenium, contributing to a theoretical gravimetric energy density of approximately 1400 Wh kg, though practical cell-level densities are targeted around 600 Wh kg due to limitations in cathode loading and electrolyte contributions. Despite these advantages, Li-Se batteries face challenges with cycle life, primarily from the dissolution of soluble polyselenides (LiSe, 4 x 8) into the electrolyte, leading to shuttle effects, active material loss, and capacity fading over repeated cycles.
To address stability issues, recent advancements in 2024 have focused on selenium-sulfide (SeS) hybrid cathodes, which combine selenium's high conductivity and density with sulfur's abundance to enhance electrochemical performance. These SeS cathodes demonstrate improved polyselenide anchoring and reduced shuttle effects, enabling higher energy densities and longer cycle life in non-aqueous lithium-sulfur battery configurations adaptable to Li-Se systems. For instance, operando spectroscopy studies have elucidated the multi-step reduction pathways in SeS, facilitating designs with enhanced rate capability and capacity retention exceeding 80% after 500 cycles.
Sodium-selenium (Na-Se) batteries emerge as a cost-effective alternative for large-scale grid storage, leveraging sodium's abundance and selenium's electrochemical properties for applications requiring stationary energy buffering. These systems operate via similar conversion chemistry to Li-Se but at lower cost, with cathodes often incorporating carbon hosts to mitigate volume expansion and polyselenide dissolution. Molten salt variants of Na-Se batteries, using electrolytes like sodium tetrachloroaluminate, enable operation at elevated temperatures (around 200300C), providing high energy densities suitable for long-duration grid storage while avoiding dendrite formation in sodium anodes.
The market for selenium-based batteries is projected to grow significantly by 2030, driven by integration into electric vehicles (EVs) seeking beyond-lithium-ion chemistries for extended range. Global selenium demand, partly fueled by Li-Se battery adoption, is expected to expand the overall market to USD 25.9 billion by 2032, with EV applications prioritizing high-volumetric-energy-density cathodes to meet regulatory targets for emissions reduction.

Other industrial uses

Selenium finds application in the production of pigments, particularly cadmium sulfoselenide (CdSSe), which imparts ruby-red hues to ceramics, glass, and plastics due to its stable color properties under high temperatures. This compound is synthesized by incorporating selenium into cadmium sulfide matrices, enabling vibrant pigmentation in industrial ceramics where thermal durability is essential.
In catalysis, selenium compounds serve as promoters to enhance selective hydrogenation reactions, notably in the production of adiponitrile derivatives for nylon precursors, where they improve catalyst efficiency and yield in industrial processes. For instance, divalent selenium additives facilitate the reduction of dinitriles to aminonitriles, minimizing side reactions in large-scale chemical manufacturing.
Trace amounts of selenium are employed as accelerators in rubber vulcanization, aiding cross-linking of polymer chains to enhance elasticity and durability, though less commonly than sulfur due to cost and toxicity considerations. These selenium-based accelerators, such as selenium dithiocarbamates, provide faster curing times in specialized formulations.
In water treatment, selenium is utilized in trace quantities to support biological denitrification processes, where it acts as a cofactor in microbial enzyme systems that reduce nitrates to nitrogen gas, aiding compliance with effluent standards in wastewater facilities. Denitrifying consortia enriched with selenium exhibit improved reduction rates for both nitrate and co-occurring selenium species.
Globally, chemicals and pigments accounted for approximately 5% of selenium consumption in 2024, reflecting its niche but essential role in these sectors amid broader metallurgical and agricultural demands.

Emerging biomedical applications

Selenium nanoparticles (SeNPs), typically synthesized in sizes ranging from 50 to 200 nm, have emerged as promising agents in biomedical applications due to their enhanced bioavailability and targeted therapeutic potential. These nanoparticles exhibit anticancer properties primarily through the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), which induce oxidative stress and apoptosis in cancer cells while sparing healthy tissues. For instance, SeNPs functionalized with paclitaxel have demonstrated efficacy in triggering ROS-mediated signaling pathways to inhibit tumor growth in preclinical models.
In therapeutic contexts, SeNPs have shown anti-inflammatory effects for conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis by downregulating pro-inflammatory cytokine mRNA synthesis and reducing joint inflammation in animal studies. Recent reviews highlight their potential in managing inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) through modulation of gut microbiota and barrier function, as evidenced in 2024 analyses of nanoparticle-based interventions. Additionally, SeNPs incorporated into antimicrobial coatings have demonstrated broad-spectrum activity against bacterial pathogens, offering applications in wound dressings and medical implants to prevent infections.
For diagnostics, Se-labeled SeNPs enable non-invasive imaging techniques, such as pancreatic scanning, by leveraging the radioisotope's gamma emissions for high-resolution detection of selenoprotein expression in vivo. These labeled nanoparticles facilitate tracking of selenium distribution in biological systems, enhancing the precision of disease monitoring.
SeNPs also serve as efficient delivery systems for chemotherapeutic agents, exemplified by conjugates with doxorubicin (Se-DOX), which improve drug solubility, tumor targeting via folate receptors, and controlled release to amplify anticancer efficacy while minimizing systemic exposure. Hyaluronic acid-coated Se-DOX nanoparticles, for example, have exhibited superior uptake in cervical carcinoma cells compared to free doxorubicin.
A key advantage of SeNPs in these applications is their reduced toxicity profile relative to inorganic selenium forms like selenite, as they exhibit lower genotoxicity and oxidative damage in murine models at equivalent doses, attributed to slower dissolution and controlled bioavailability. This safety margin supports their progression toward clinical translation. Organoselenium compounds, such as selenocysteine derivatives, complement SeNP bioactivity by enhancing antioxidant responses in therapeutic formulations.

Environmental impact

Sources of pollution

Anthropogenic activities represent the primary sources of selenium pollution in ecosystems, significantly elevating concentrations beyond natural baseline levels, which typically range from less than 0.1 to 100 g/L in surface waters. These inputs occur through industrial processes, energy production, and agriculture, leading to widespread contamination of water bodies, soils, and sediments. Unlike natural weathering of selenium-bearing rocks, human-induced releases often involve mobilized forms like selenate and selenite, which are more bioavailable and persistent in aquatic environments.
Mining and refining operations, particularly for copper and lead, are major contributors to selenium pollution via tailings and effluents. Selenium co-occurs in sulfide ores, and during extraction, it is released into waste streams, with concentrations in mining effluents ranging from 0.1 to 20 mg/L. Tailings from copper mining can leach selenium at levels up to 1.5 mg/L in associated waters, contaminating nearby rivers and groundwater. Phosphate and gold mining also generate high-selenium waste, exacerbating pollution in regions like the western United States and Canada.
Coal combustion for energy production releases substantial selenium through fly ash and stack emissions, accounting for over 50% of global anthropogenic selenium inputs. Globally, these activities emit several thousand tons of selenium annually (with coal contributing over 50% of anthropogenic inputs), primarily from the volatilization and leaching of selenium-enriched fly ash, which contains 73140 g/g of the element. In the United States alone, coal-fired power plants produce over 110 million tons of coal combustion residuals (including fly ash) yearly, with leachates reaching 501,500 g/L in disposal sites. This airborne and waterborne dispersal contaminates remote ecosystems far from combustion sources.
Agricultural runoff introduces selenium through irrigation drainage from seleniferous soils and the application of selenate-based fertilizers, such as sodium selenate, used to enhance crop nutrition. In arid regions, irrigation mobilizes soil selenium, resulting in drainwater concentrations up to 1,400 g/L, which flows into wetlands and rivers. Fertilizer overuse amplifies this, as selenate is highly soluble and leaches readily during rainfall or irrigation, contributing to nonpoint source pollution in agricultural watersheds.
Industrial processes, including glass manufacturing and electronics production, release selenium via wastewater and waste disposal. In glass production, selenium serves as a decolorizing agent, with emissions from melting furnaces volatilizing up to 75% of added selenium as SeO, contaminating air and effluents. Electronics waste, containing selenium in components like photovoltaic cells and semiconductors, leaches the element during improper disposal, with concentrations in e-waste leachates reaching ppm levels that pollute soils and groundwater.
In 2024, California irrigation districts emerged as critical hotspots, with west-side San Joaquin Valley runoff discharging elevated selenium levels into the San Joaquin River, San Francisco Bay-Delta estuary, and wildlife refuges, exceeding regulatory thresholds due to ongoing agricultural drainage. These localized inputs highlight the interplay of historical soil legacies and current practices in amplifying ecosystem exposure.

Ecological effects

Selenium bioaccumulates in aquatic and terrestrial food chains, posing significant risks to wildlife through trophic transfer from primary producers like algae to higher-level consumers such as fish and birds. In aquatic ecosystems, selenium enters the food web primarily via uptake by algae and periphyton, with enrichment factors from water to these primary producers ranging from 800 to 19,000 mL/g depending on site conditions. Trophic transfer factors then amplify concentrations: from periphyton to benthic macroinvertebrates (2-10 times) and from invertebrates to fish ovaries (up to 8 times), leading to elevated levels in top predators like trout and pikeminnow, where ovarian tissues can reach 12-21 mg/kg dry weight. The Se:Hg molar ratio plays a critical role in modulating overall toxicity during this process; ratios exceeding 1:1 generally mitigate mercury's neurotoxic effects by supporting antioxidant enzyme activity, while ratios below 1:1 heighten vulnerability to combined metal stress in wildlife.
In aquatic environments, selenium concentrations above 5 g/L in water trigger chronic toxicity, particularly impairing fish reproduction through maternal transfer to eggs, resulting in embryo deformities such as spinal curvature, craniofacial malformations, and reduced hatchability. These effects stem from selenium's disruption of oxidative balance and selenoprotein synthesis in developing embryos, with tissue thresholds for adverse outcomes including 4 g/g dry weight in whole-body fish and 8 g/g in muscle. Fish populations in selenium-enriched waters often exhibit population declines due to these reproductive failures, with complete failure possible at 10 g/L in reservoirs.
Terrestrial ecosystems face selenium toxicity, known as selenosis, in birds and mammals exposed to irrigated seleniferous soils, where drainage mobilizes the element into wetlands and forage. Irrigation of soils derived from marine shales dissolves selenium, concentrating it in runoff and leading to embryotoxic levels (>8 g/g) in bird eggs, causing deformities like twisted bills and reduced hatchability in species such as coots and stilts. Mammals, including livestock and wild herbivores, suffer hair loss, hoof deformities, and liver damage from chronic ingestion, with similar risks extending to predators consuming contaminated prey. Approximately 4,100 square miles of irrigated land in the western U.S. are susceptible, exacerbating these effects in adjacent habitats.
A seminal example of selenium's ecological harm occurred at Kesterson Reservoir in California's San Joaquin Valley during the 1980s, where agricultural drainage elevated selenium levels, causing massive bird die-offs and deformities. From 1983 to 1985, over 88% of bird deaths and teratogenic effects, including missing eyes and legs in embryos, were attributed to selenium bioaccumulation in the food chain, affecting thousands of migratory waterfowl and leading to near-total nesting failure. Recent studies highlight ongoing risks, such as persistent high selenium in fish tissues downstream of mountaintop coal mining sites, where levels remain toxic to aquatic wildlife years after mine closure, potentially extirpating sensitive populations.

Mitigation and regulation

Wetland bioremediation represents a key strategy for treating selenium-contaminated waters, leveraging constructed wetlands where plants hyperaccumulate the element from soil and water. Species such as Astragalus, known for their ability to tolerate and sequester high levels of selenium through assimilation pathways similar to sulfur, are particularly effective in these systems, reducing concentrations by bioaccumulation and volatilization. This approach integrates microbial activity in wetland sediments to convert selenate to less mobile forms, achieving removal efficiencies up to 80% in field applications.
Regulatory frameworks aim to limit selenium discharges and protect water bodies. In the United States, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) establishes a chronic aquatic life criterion of 5 g/L for selenium in freshwater, serving as a benchmark for surface water quality standards to prevent bioaccumulation in ecosystems. In the European Union, the REACH regulation requires registration and risk assessment of selenium compounds, classifying many as substances of very high concern (SVHC) due to their toxicity, with restrictions on their manufacture and use to minimize environmental release.
Effective monitoring of selenium pollution relies on bioindicators and analytical techniques to assess exposure and compliance. Aquatic birds like the American dipper serve as bioindicators, with selenium levels in their tissues reflecting contamination in lotic systems, enabling detection of hotspots where concentrations exceed safe thresholds. Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) provides precise quantification of total selenium in environmental matrices such as water and sediment, with detection limits below 0.1 g/L, as outlined in EPA Method 200.8 for trace element analysis.
In 2025, advancements in mining technology in Canada include commitments to zero-discharge systems for selenium, particularly in Alberta's coal operations, where water management practices eliminate effluent releases to protect headwaters. These technologies employ advanced treatment like zero-valent iron reactors to remove selenium before any potential discharge, aligning with forthcoming Coal Mining Effluent Regulations.
Recycling incentives for electronic waste (e-waste) promote selenium recovery from sources like photocopier drums and solar panels, reducing landfill pollution. Programs such as the Solar eWaste Solutions PV Payback offer financial rebates for bulk submissions, recovering selenium alongside other metals through hydrometallurgical processes, with global e-waste recycling rates incentivized to reach 20% for precious elements including selenium.

Biological role

Essential functions and enzymes

Selenium is an essential trace element required for the biosynthesis of selenoproteins, a class of proteins that incorporate selenium in the form of selenocysteine (Sec), recognized as the 21st amino acid in the genetic code. Unlike the standard 20 amino acids, Sec is encoded by the UGA codon, which typically signals translation termination but is recoded as Sec through a specialized mechanism involving a selenocysteine insertion sequence (SECIS) element in the mRNA and unique translation factors. This incorporation is crucial for the catalytic activity of selenoproteins, with humans expressing 25 distinct selenoproteins, many of which play vital roles in redox homeostasis and cellular protection.
Among the key selenoproteins are the glutathione peroxidases (GPx), a family of enzymes that function as antioxidants by reducing hydroperoxides and lipid hydroperoxides to protect cells from oxidative damage. Particularly, GPx4 plays a critical role in spermatogenesis by protecting developing sperm from oxidative damage and contributing to the structural integrity of the sperm midpiece. The Sec residue in GPx forms the active site, enabling efficient peroxide detoxification using glutathione as a cofactor. Thioredoxin reductases (TrxR) represent another critical group, maintaining the redox state of thioredoxin and other proteins, thereby supporting DNA synthesis, apoptosis regulation, and antioxidant defenses. Iodothyronine deiodinases (DIOs), particularly DIO1, DIO2, and DIO3, are essential for thyroid hormone metabolism, converting thyroxine (T4) to the active triiodothyronine (T3) or inactivating it, which is vital for metabolic regulation and development. The synthesis of Sec itself relies on selenophosphate synthetase (SPS2 in humans), which catalyzes the formation of selenophosphate from selenide and ATP, serving as the selenium donor for charging tRNA with Sec.
Selenoproteins exhibit evolutionary conservation across eukaryotes, underscoring their ancient origin and fundamental biological importance, though they are notably absent in certain anaerobic organisms where selenium availability may be limited or its incorporation poses toxicity risks in low-oxygen environments. The daily requirement for selenium in adults is 55 micrograms, sufficient to support selenoprotein synthesis and prevent deficiency-related impairments in these enzymatic functions.

Dietary sources and intake

Selenium is obtained primarily through dietary sources, with bioavailability influenced by the chemical form and soil selenium content in plant-based foods. In plants, selenium is predominantly present as selenomethionine and other organic forms, which are highly bioavailable (up to 90%) and incorporated nonspecifically into proteins. Animal products contain selenium mainly as selenocysteine, integrated into selenoproteins, with similar high bioavailability.
Rich plant sources include Brazil nuts, which can provide 50100 g per nut but vary widely due to soil selenium levels; for instance, one ounce (68 nuts) may contain up to 544 g. Low-oxalate alternatives to Brazil nuts for consistent selenium intake, particularly for those concerned with oxalate levels or thyroid health support, include tuna, sardines, shrimp, eggs, turkey, pork, mushrooms, and whole grains. Grains such as bread and cereals, along with vegetables like garlic, also contribute, though their selenium content depends on soil conditions in the growing region. For example, one cup of cooked oats provides 30-50% of the daily value. Animal sources are more consistent, with seafood like tuna offering about 92 g per 3-ounce serving (approximately 108 g/100 g) and other fish such as sardines providing 45 g per 3-ounce serving. Meats (e.g., beef or pork at 37 g per 3-ounce serving), poultry (e.g., turkey at 26 g per 3-ounce serving), eggs (15 g per large egg), and dairy like cottage cheese (20 g per cup) are reliable contributors. Consuming 3-4 eggs per day can provide approximately 100% or more of the daily value for adult men, while 1-2 cans of sardines per week can contribute around 100% of weekly requirements; combinations of these foods with grains like oats can exceed 200% of the daily value.
The recommended dietary allowance (RDA) for selenium is 55 g per day for adults, increasing to 60 g during pregnancy and 70 g during lactation to support fetal and infant development. The tolerable upper intake level (UL) is 400 g per day for adults to avoid potential adverse effects from excess intake. Supplements, often in forms like chelated organic selenium such as selenomethionine (with an absorption rate of about 90% and high bioavailability, though some studies indicate slightly lower storage and conversion efficiency compared to selenium yeast), sodium selenite, or selenium yeast, can provide additional selenium, particularly for those in low-soil-selenium areas, but dietary sources are preferred for balanced nutrition.
In regions with selenium-deficient soils, such as parts of Finland, national programs have fortified fertilizers with sodium selenate since 1984, initially at 6 mg Se/kg and later increased to 15 mg Se/kg, effectively raising crop selenium levels and population intake without exceeding safe limits. This approach has been unique to Finland and demonstrates a strategy to enhance dietary selenium through agriculture.

Deficiency symptoms

Selenium deficiency manifests in various clinical and subclinical forms, primarily in regions with low soil selenium content, leading to inadequate dietary intake. Severe deficiency is linked to endemic diseases such as Keshan disease, a dilated cardiomyopathy characterized by multifocal myocardial necrosis, heart failure, arrhythmias, and sudden death, predominantly affecting children and young women in selenium-poor areas of China.
Another prominent manifestation is Kashin-Beck disease, an endemic osteoarthropathy involving degeneration and necrosis of cartilage and bone, resulting in joint deformities, stunted growth, and restricted mobility, mainly observed in children and adolescents in low-selenium regions of China, North Korea, and eastern Siberia.
Subclinical selenium deficiency contributes to impaired immune function, including reduced T-cell proliferation and antibody production, as well as disrupted thyroid hormone metabolism, which can exacerbate conditions like hypothyroidism.
In animal models, selenium deficiency causes conditions such as white muscle disease (nutritional muscular dystrophy) in ruminants like sheep and cattle, characterized by skeletal and cardiac muscle degeneration; mulberry heart disease in pigs, leading to sudden cardiac failure; and liver necrosis (hepatosis dietetica) in swine. Additionally, selenium deficiency in animal models impairs testicular morphology, reduces testis weight, and disrupts spermatogenesis, leading to decreased sperm quality and fertility.
Globally, selenium deficiency risks affect an estimated 0.5 to 1 billion people, particularly in agricultural areas with low soil selenium concentrations, such as parts of China, Europe, and sub-Saharan Africa, where dietary variability from soil depletion heightens vulnerability.

Toxicity mechanisms

Selenium toxicity manifests in both acute and chronic forms, with the former resulting from high single doses and the latter from prolonged excessive intake. Primary exposure routes include ingestion and inhalation, as intact healthy skin shows minimal to no significant absorption of inorganic forms such as selenate and selenite, or selenium sulfide as used in shampoos. Acute selenosis typically occurs following ingestion of more than 15 mg/kg body weight of selenium compounds, such as sodium selenite, leading to moderate to severe symptoms including nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. In documented outbreaks, such as one involving a contaminated dietary supplement, affected individuals exhibited blood selenium levels exceeding 1000 g/L, accompanied by gastrointestinal distress and subsequent systemic effects.
Chronic selenium toxicity arises from daily intakes surpassing 400 g, the established tolerable upper intake level for adults, resulting in selenosis characterized by symptoms like hair and nail brittleness or loss, a garlic-like breath odor, fatigue, and dermatological changes. At the cellular level, excess selenium binds to thiol groups in proteins, leading to non-specific substitution of sulfur atoms and disruption of protein structure and function. This binding can cause protein misfolding, impairing essential enzymes that incorporate selenocysteine, and thereby contrasting with selenium's role in normal selenoprotein synthesis.
A primary mechanism of selenium-induced harm involves the generation of oxidative stress, where excess selenide or selenocysteine metabolites react with cellular thiols to produce reactive oxygen species, damaging lipids, proteins, and DNA. These processes contribute to broader cellular dysfunction, including apoptosis in sensitive tissues like the liver and kidneys. Detection of selenium toxicity relies on measuring levels in blood or urine using techniques such as atomic absorption spectrometry (AAS) or inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS), with normal blood concentrations ranging from 70150 g/L; elevated levels above 500 g/L indicate chronic exposure, while acute cases may exceed 1000 g/L.
Recent research highlights that nano-selenium formulations exhibit lower toxicity profiles compared to inorganic or organic forms, due to controlled release and reduced bioavailability at equivalent doses, as evidenced in 2024 studies on plant-derived nanoparticles showing enhanced biocompatibility without compromising bioactivity.

Health effects and recent research

Selenium has been investigated for its potential role in cancer prevention, with mixed results from large-scale trials. The Selenium and Vitamin E Cancer Prevention Trial (SELECT), involving over 35,000 men, found that selenium supplementation at 200 g/day did not reduce the overall incidence of prostate cancer and may have slightly increased risk in certain subgroups with higher baseline selenium levels. However, subgroup analyses indicated potential benefits for men with low baseline selenium, where supplementation reduced prostate cancer risk by up to 50%. For colorectal cancer, observational studies and smaller trials suggest a protective effect; for instance, the Nutritional Prevention of Cancer trial reported a 58% reduction in colorectal cancer incidence with selenium supplementation in participants with low baseline levels. A 2008 review highlighted selenium's anti-oxidative and anti-proliferative mechanisms as likely contributors to this potential in colorectal carcinogenesis.
In cardiovascular health, higher selenium status is associated with reduced risk of cardiovascular disease (CVD), primarily through its incorporation into glutathione peroxidase (GPx) enzymes that mitigate oxidative stress. A 2025 meta-analysis of cohort studies found that selenium intake above 55 g/day correlated with a 20-30% lower CVD risk, with GPx activity serving as a key mediator in endothelial protection. Serum selenium levels positively correlate with GPx activity, which detoxifies peroxides and prevents lipid peroxidation in vascular tissues. Physiologically high selenium concentrations have been linked to decreased CVD incidence and mortality in population studies.
Selenium supports immune function and thyroid health, with links to conditions like HIV and Hashimoto's thyroiditis. Clinical trials show limited benefits of selenium supplementation for immune outcomes in HIV patients, such as modest improvements in CD4 counts but no significant reduction in disease progression. In Hashimoto's thyroiditis, an autoimmune disorder, selenium supplementation at 200 g/day reduces thyroid peroxidase antibodies (TPOAb) by 20-40% and lowers thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) levels, potentially slowing hypothyroidism onset. These effects stem from selenium's role in selenoproteins that regulate inflammation and thyroid hormone metabolism. Regarding male reproductive health, clinical studies indicate that selenium supplementation can improve sperm motility and overall semen quality in men with infertility, often in combination with vitamin E.
Recent research from 2024-2025 highlights innovative applications of selenium. Selenium nanoparticles (SeNPs) have demonstrated strong antiviral activity against SARS-CoV-2, inhibiting viral replication by up to 90% in vitro through disruption of viral envelope proteins and enhancement of host antioxidant defenses, positioning them as potential adjuncts for COVID-19 treatment. A 2024 meta-analysis of trace elements in Alzheimer's disease (AD) patients revealed significantly lower serum selenium levels (by 15-25 g/L) compared to controls, suggesting selenium deficiency as a risk factor for cognitive decline. Another 2024 study linked higher selenium status to better global and attention-related cognitive function, with potential neuroprotective effects via reduced oxidative damage in the brain.
Selenium interacts with other nutrients, notably iodine and vitamin E, influencing health outcomes. Selenium deficiency can exacerbate iodine deficiency, increasing the risk of congenital hypothyroidism by impairing deiodinase enzymes needed for thyroid hormone activation. In synergy with vitamin E, selenium enhances antioxidant protection; combined supplementation regenerates vitamin E from its oxidized form, amplifying defense against lipid peroxidation in tissues. Selenium also synergizes with coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10), facilitating its recycling and supporting the formation of antioxidant enzymes like glutathione peroxidase; together, they enhance mitochondrial energy production, cell membrane protection, and broader defense against free radicals, inflammation, and age-related damage, with pronounced effects in selenium-deficient populations. These interactions underscore the importance of balanced intake to optimize selenium's benefits.
