Lebesgue covering dimension
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Lebesgue covering dimension is a topological invariant that assigns a non-negative integer (or infinity) to a topological space, representing its dimension based on the complexity of its open covers. It is defined as the smallest integer $ n $ such that every finite open cover of the space admits an open refinement where no point belongs to more than $ n+1 $ sets in the refinement; if no such finite $ n $ exists, the dimension is infinite. This definition captures the intuitive notion of dimension by ensuring that covers can be refined without excessive overlap, generalizing classical Euclidean dimensions to abstract spaces.
Originally introduced by Henri Lebesgue in 1911 to study the non-embeddability of domains in Euclidean spaces of different dimensions, the concept used coverings by "bricks" (rectangular sets) to define dimension for compact subsets of $ \mathbb{R}^k $. Lebesgue's work established that the dimension of such sets equals the minimal $ n $ where small-diameter closed covers have order at most $ n+1 $, with the Lebesgue covering lemma guaranteeing a positive scale for uniform refinements in compact sets. In 1933, Eduard ech extended this to arbitrary topological spaces via the modern covering dimension framework, making it applicable beyond metric settings.
In separable metric spaces, the Lebesgue covering dimension coincides with the small and large inductive dimensions, providing a unified measure; for example, the Euclidean space $ \mathbb{R}^n $ has dimension exactly $ n $. It satisfies additivity properties, such as $ \dim(X \times Y) \leq \dim X + \dim Y $ for normal spaces, and plays a crucial role in embedding theorems: a compact metric space of dimension $ n $ embeds into $ \mathbb{R}^{2n+1} $, but not always into lower dimensions. These features make it a foundational tool in dimension theory, influencing areas like algebraic topology and geometric analysis.

Introduction

Informal Description

The Lebesgue covering dimension offers an intuitive way to quantify the "size" or complexity of a topological space by examining how it can be covered with open sets, focusing on the minimal level of overlap needed in such coverings. Imagine trying to cover a straight line with small intervals: it is always possible to arrange them so that no point lies in more than two intervals at once, reflecting the one-dimensional nature of the line. In contrast, covering a plane requires allowing overlaps of up to three sets at any point, akin to laying bricks in a wall where no single point is shared by more than three bricks, capturing the two-dimensional essence. This dimension represents the smallest integer $ n $ such that covers can be refined to avoid excessive overlaps beyond order $ n+1 $, providing a topological measure that aligns with everyday geometric intuitions for familiar spaces.
Henri Lebesgue introduced this concept in the early 20th century as part of his foundational work in measure theory, where he sought a robust notion of dimension to extend the ideas of length, area, and volume to more abstract sets while ensuring consistency across transformations. Unlike geometric dimensions, which depend on specific coordinates or distances, the covering dimension is purely topological, remaining unchanged under homeomorphismscontinuous bijections with continuous inversesthat distort shapes without tearing or gluing. This invariance makes it particularly valuable for studying spaces where metric structures are absent or irrelevant, emphasizing intrinsic properties over embedded realizations.
In essence, the Lebesgue covering dimension bridges intuitive geometry with abstract topology, foreshadowing the formal definition in terms of open cover refinements, and has become a cornerstone for understanding spatial complexity without relying on numerical measurements.

Historical Context

The concept of Lebesgue covering dimension traces its origins to Henri Lebesgue's contributions in 1911, where he introduced covering-based arguments to analyze the dimensionality of sets in the context of measure theory and integration. Lebesgue, building on his earlier work in developing the Lebesgue integral, used these coverings to distinguish the dimensions of Euclidean spaces, demonstrating that the unit cube in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn could not be covered by fewer than n+1n+1n+1 sets of sufficiently small diameter without overlaps exceeding a certain order. This approach provided an intuitive topological characterization of dimension tied to the geometric properties essential for integration over irregular sets.
The formalization of the covering dimension within general topology began with Pavel Urysohn's work starting in 1922, who extended Lebesgue's ideas to compact metric spaces by defining a related inductive dimension invariant that aligned with covering properties. Urysohn's seminal papers "Sur les multiplicits cantoriennes" (Parts I and II), published posthumously in Fundamenta Mathematicae in 1925 and 1926, established foundational theorems on dimension, including its additivity and behavior under mappings, laying the groundwork for a rigorous topological theory independent of metric structures.
Independently, Karl Menger advanced this in 1926 through his paper "Allgemeine Rume und Cartesische Rume. I" in the Proceedings of the Royal Academy of Amsterdam, where he refined the covering approach by introducing the large inductive dimension and proving its equivalence to Urysohn's small inductive dimension in separable metric spaces, thus solidifying the covering dimension as a key topological invariant.
Post-1930s developments saw significant refinements, including Eduard ech's 1933 formalization of the covering dimension for arbitrary topological spaces. This culminated in Witold Hurewicz and Henry Wallman's 1941 monograph Dimension Theory, which systematically extended the Lebesgue covering dimension from metric spaces to arbitrary topological spaces and compiled proofs of its core properties. This work addressed limitations in earlier definitions and incorporated results from the 1930s, including the recognition of the dimension's homotopy invariancemeaning that continuous deformations preserve the covering dimensionestablished through contributions by figures like ech and others who linked it to cohomological characterizations. Hurewicz and Wallman's text became a cornerstone, influencing subsequent research by emphasizing the dimension's role in embedding theorems and product spaces.

Examples and Applications

Euclidean Spaces

The Lebesgue covering dimension of the Euclidean space Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn is nnn. This result follows from the fundamental theorem of dimension theory, which equates the small inductive dimension, large inductive dimension, and covering dimension for separable metric spaces like Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, and establishes that all three are equal to nnn. To verify that the dimension is at least nnn, note that Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn contains an open set homeomorphic to the open nnn-ball, whose covering dimension is nnn as established for the nnn-simplex using Sperner's lemma on triangulations to show the existence of a refinement with order at least n+1n+1n+1. That the dimension is at most nnn is shown by constructing explicit refinements of arbitrary open covers using partitions of unity and the Brouwer fixed-point theorem, ensuring that every open cover admits a refinement of order at most n+1n+1n+1.
For product spaces, the Lebesgue covering dimension satisfies dim(RmRk)=m+k\dim(\mathbb{R}^m \times \mathbb{R}^k) = m + kdim(RmRk)=m+k. This additivity holds because RmRk\mathbb{R}^m \times \mathbb{R}^kRmRk is homeomorphic to Rm+k\mathbb{R}^{m+k}Rm+k, whose dimension is m+km+km+k by the result for Euclidean spaces. More directly, the product theorem for covering dimension states that for separable metric spaces XXX and YYY, dim(XY)dimX+dimY+1\dim(X \times Y) \leq \dim X + \dim Y + 1dim(XY)dimX+dimY+1, but equality is achieved in this case via explicit refinement constructions: given an open cover U\mathcal{U}U of RmRk\mathbb{R}^m \times \mathbb{R}^kRmRk, one forms a refinement by taking products of open refinements of the projections onto Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm and Rk\mathbb{R}^kRk with orders at most m+1m+1m+1 and k+1k+1k+1, respectively, and using a partition of unity to shrink sets so that their intersections have controlled multiplicity not exceeding m+k+1m+k+1m+k+1.
Subspaces of Euclidean spaces illustrate how the Lebesgue covering dimension captures intrinsic topology rather than embedding. For the nnn-sphere Sn={xRn+1x=1}S^n = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^{n+1} \mid \|x\| = 1\}Sn={xRn+1x=1}, the dimension is nnn, despite being embedded in Rn+1\mathbb{R}^{n+1}Rn+1 of dimension n+1n+1n+1. This follows from the subspace theorem, which implies dim(Sn)n\dim(S^n) \leq ndim(Sn)n as SnS^nSn is a compact nnn-dimensional manifold, and the lower bound is established by noting that SnS^nSn contains open sets homeomorphic to Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, requiring covers of order at least n+1n+1n+1 in refinements. The contrast highlights that covering dimension is invariant under homeomorphisms and independent of the ambient space, unlike the embedding dimension.

Topological Manifolds

A topological manifold of dimension nnn is a Hausdorff space that is locally homeomorphic to Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, and under standard assumptions such as second countability, its Lebesgue covering dimension is exactly nnn. This follows from the fact that every point has a neighborhood homeomorphic to an open subset of Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, which has covering dimension nnn, and the use of partitions of unity allows for the construction of refinements of open covers that satisfy the dimension condition globally. More precisely, for metrizable manifolds, the Lebesgue covering dimension coincides with the small inductive dimension, confirming that dim(M)=n\dim(M) = ndim(M)=n for an nnn-manifold MMM.
Representative examples illustrate this invariance. The 2-torus T2T^2T2, obtained as the quotient R2/Z2\mathbb{R}^2 / \mathbb{Z}^2R2/Z2, is a compact 2-dimensional manifold and thus has Lebesgue covering dimension 2. Similarly, the Klein bottle, a non-orientable compact surface homeomorphic to the quotient of the square with opposite sides identified in a twisted manner, also has dimension 2, as its atlas consists of charts to open sets in R2\mathbb{R}^2R2.

Properties

Fundamental Properties

The Lebesgue covering dimension, denoted dimX\dim XdimX for a topological space XXX, is a topological invariant. Specifically, if f:XYf: X \to Yf:XY is a homeomorphism, then dimX=dimY\dim X = \dim YdimX=dimY. This follows directly from the definition, as homeomorphisms preserve the refinement properties of open covers. In normal spaces, this invariance is established in Theorem 1.6.7.
The dimension also exhibits monotonicity with respect to subspaces. For any subspace AXA \subseteq XAX, dimAdimX\dim A \leq \dim XdimAdimX. This property holds in regular spaces for the inductive dimension (Theorem 1.1.2) and extends to the covering dimension in normal and separable metric spaces (Theorems 1.7.7 and 4.1.7). Additionally, for the disjoint union of two spaces, dim(XY)=max(dimX,dimY)\dim(X \sqcup Y) = \max(\dim X, \dim Y)dim(XY)=max(dimX,dimY). This additivity for disjoint unions is a consequence of the covering refinement condition applying separately to each component (Theorem 1.5.2 for disjoint subspaces).
In separable metric spaces with dimX1\dim X \geq 1dimX1, the dimension is stable under removal of a single point: dim(X{p})=dimX\dim(X \setminus \{p\}) = \dim Xdim(X{p})=dimX for any pXp \in XpX. This result, given in Corollary 1.5.6, relies on the ability to refine covers while avoiding the point without increasing the order. For compact Hausdorff spaces, the Lebesgue covering dimension coincides with the small inductive dimension, providing a unified finite or infinite value that characterizes the space's topological complexity (Theorem 1.9.9). Moreover, if dimXn\dim X \leq ndimXn, then XXX admits an open cover that refines to one of order at most n+1n+1n+1, aiding paracompact decompositions in normal spaces with finite dimension.

Metric Space Extensions

In metric spaces, the existence of Lebesgue numbers for open covers of compact subsets facilitates the construction of uniform refinements that preserve the covering dimension. Specifically, for a compact metric space XXX, every open cover U\mathcal{U}U admits a Lebesgue number >0\delta > 0>0 such that any subset of diameter less than \delta is contained in some member of U\mathcal{U}U; this property ensures that refinements can be chosen with uniformly small mesh (maximum diameter) while maintaining order at most n+1n+1n+1 if dimXn\dim X \leq ndimXn, thereby extending the topological invariance of dimension to metric-specific uniform structures.
A key extension concerns product spaces. In general topological spaces, the Lebesgue covering dimension satisfies dim(XY)dimX+dimY+1\dim(X \times Y) \leq \dim X + \dim Y + 1dim(XY)dimX+dimY+1. For compact metric spaces, this inequality holds, and while additivity dim(XY)=dimX+dimY\dim(X \times Y) = \dim X + \dim Ydim(XY)=dimX+dimY occurs in many cases (e.g., for Euclidean spaces or manifolds), counterexamples exist where the product dimension is strictly less, though the +1+1+1 bound remains sharp overall.
The Lipschitz dimension provides another metric refinement, defined as the smallest integer nnn such that there exists a Lipschitz light map f:XRnf: X \to \mathbb{R}^nf:XRn, where "light" means that preimages of small-diameter sets have controlled diameter proportional to the original. For any metric space XXX, the Lipschitz dimension satisfies dimLip(X)dimX\dim_\text{Lip}(X) \geq \dim XdimLip(X)dimX, with equality holding for doubling spaces, where the metric structure allows bi-Lipschitz equivalence to spaces of matching topological dimension.

Inductive Dimension

The small inductive dimension, denoted ind(X), of a topological space X is defined recursively as follows: ind() = -1, and ind(X) 0 if for every point x X and every open neighborhood U of x, there exists an open neighborhood V of x with V U such that the boundary V = cl(V) \ V is empty. The large inductive dimension, denoted Ind(X), is defined similarly but applies to closed sets: Ind() = -1, and Ind(X) 0 if for every closed set F X and every open set U F, there exists an open set V with F V U such that V is empty. For any normal space X, the inequalities ind(X) dim(X) Ind(X) hold. A brief proof sketch proceeds as follows: the inequality ind(X) dim(X) follows from the fact that the small inductive dimension satisfies the conditions for order of open covers, allowing a refinement argument similar to the covering dimension definition; conversely, dim(X) Ind(X) arises because the large inductive dimension's boundary condition implies the existence of refinements with controlled order via normality, ensuring no overlaps exceeding the dimension bound. These three dimensions agree in separable metric spaces, where ind(X) = dim(X) = Ind(X).
The small and large inductive dimensions may differ in certain pathological compacta. For instance, V. V. Filippov constructed a compact Hausdorff space with ind(X) = 0 but Ind(X) = 1, resolving a problem of P. S. Aleksandrov by showing that the inequality ind(X) Ind(X) can be strict.

Other Dimension Theories

The Lebesgue covering dimension, also known as the topological dimension, provides a lower bound for the Hausdorff dimension in separable metric spaces. Specifically, for any separable metric space XXX, dimXdimHX\dim X \leq \dim_H XdimXdimHX, where dim\dimdim denotes the covering dimension and dimH\dim_HdimH the Hausdorff dimension. This inequality arises because the covering dimension captures the minimal number of coordinates needed to separate points via open covers, while the Hausdorff dimension accounts for metric scaling properties that can yield non-integer values greater than or equal to the topological one. Equality holds in cases like Euclidean spaces or smooth manifolds, where both dimensions equal the classical integer dimension.
For self-similar sets satisfying the open set condition, the Hausdorff dimension often equals the similarity dimension, though typically the Hausdorff dimension exceeds the topological one for fractals, as in the Sierpinski gasket where the topological dimension is 1 but the Hausdorff dimension is log231.585\log_2 3 \approx 1.585log231.585. However, strict inequality occurs in examples like the Cantor dust, the product of two middle-thirds Cantor sets in the plane, which has topological dimension 0 (being totally disconnected) but Hausdorff dimension 2log2/log31.26192 \log 2 / \log 3 \approx 1.26192log2/log31.2619.
In the context of fractal dimensions, the Lebesgue covering dimension serves as a lower bound for the box-counting dimension (also known as the Minkowski-Bouligand dimension) in metric spaces. The box-counting dimension, defined as lim0logN()log\lim_{\delta \to 0} \frac{\log N(\delta)}{-\log \delta}lim0loglogN() where N()N(\delta)N() is the minimal number of sets of diameter \delta covering the space, satisfies dimXdimBX\dim X \leq \dim_B XdimXdimBX, reflecting how topological separation requires at least as many boxes as the covering order suggests. Embedding dimensions, such as the minimal Euclidean dimension for isometric embedding, are similarly bounded below by the topological dimension, ensuring that fractals with low covering dimension can still require higher embedding spaces due to metric distortions.
The Lebesgue covering dimension relates closely to the cohomological dimension in algebraic topology, particularly for paracompact spaces. For a paracompact space XXX, dimXn\dim X \leq ndimXn if and only if the ech cohomology groups Hn+1(Y;G)=0H^{n+1}(Y; G) = 0Hn+1(Y;G)=0 for every closed subset YXY \subseteq XYX and every coefficient group GGG. In CW-complexes, a key structure in algebraic topology, the Lebesgue covering dimension coincides with the cellular dimension, defined as the highest index nnn such that the nnn-skeleton is non-empty. For a CW-complex XXX, dimX=n\dim X = ndimX=n if and only if XXX is homotopy equivalent to its nnn-skeleton, with no higher cells needed. This alignment facilitates homotopy theory applications, where the dimension bounds the range of non-trivial homotopy groups; aiding spectral sequence computations and embedding theorems.
