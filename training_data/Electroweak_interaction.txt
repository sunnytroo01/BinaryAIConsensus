Electroweak interaction
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The electroweak interaction is a fundamental interaction in particle physics that unifies the electromagnetic force, responsible for phenomena such as light and electricity, with the weak nuclear force, which governs processes like beta decay and neutrino interactions. Developed as part of the Standard Model of particle physics, the electroweak theory describes these forces as manifestations of a single underlying symmetry at high energies, broken at lower energies to yield the distinct electromagnetic and weak interactions observed today.
The theoretical framework was pioneered by Sheldon Glashow in 1961 with a gauge model based on the symmetry group SU(2) U(1), which incorporated both charged and neutral weak currents alongside electromagnetism. Independently, Abdus Salam and Steven Weinberg extended this in 19671968 by incorporating spontaneous symmetry breaking via the Higgs mechanism, allowing the mediating bosons to acquire mass while preserving gauge invariance and renormalizability. This mechanism, originally proposed by Peter Higgs, Franois Englert, Robert Brout, and others in 1964, introduces a scalar Higgs field whose vacuum expectation value breaks the electroweak symmetry, generating masses for the W, W, and Z bosons (approximately 80 GeV/c and 91 GeV/c, respectively) while leaving the photon massless.
Experimental confirmation began with the discovery of weak neutral currents in 1973 by the Gargamelle bubble chamber experiment at CERN, providing direct evidence for the Z-mediated interaction predicted by the theory. The W and Z bosons were subsequently discovered in 1983 at CERN's Super Proton Synchrotron by the UA1 and UA2 collaborations, with masses and properties matching theoretical predictions to high precision. The crowning achievement came in 2012 with the observation of the Higgs boson at the Large Hadron Collider by the ATLAS and CMS experiments, confirming the mechanism responsible for electroweak symmetry breaking and particle masses.
The electroweak theory has withstood rigorous tests through precision electroweak measurements at facilities like LEP and the LHC, which constrain parameters such as the weak mixing angle (sin_W 0.231) and verify the Standard Model's predictions to better than 0.1% accuracy in many observables. No significant deviations have been observed, underscoring its success, though ongoing research probes for subtle effects or extensions beyond the Standard Model, such as in rare decays or high-energy scattering.

Historical development

Early theoretical motivations

The weak interaction was first theoretically described by Enrico Fermi in 1933 through a four-fermion contact interaction model, which posited a point-like coupling between neutrons, protons, electrons, and neutrinos to explain beta decay processes. This theory successfully accounted for the basic kinematics of beta decay but suffered from fundamental limitations, notably its non-renormalizability, which led to unphysical infinities in higher-order perturbation theory calculations and prevented reliable predictions at high energies.
A major empirical challenge to early weak interaction theories emerged in 1957 with the Wu experiment, which demonstrated parity violation in the beta decay of cobalt-60 nuclei by observing an asymmetric emission of electrons preferentially in the direction opposite to the nuclear spin. This result implied that the weak force distinguishes between left-handed and right-handed chiralities, or "handedness," fundamentally breaking mirror symmetry and necessitating a revision of symmetry assumptions in weak processes.
Early attempts to unify electromagnetism with other forces, including precursors to the weak interaction, included Oskar Klein's 1926 extension of Theodor Kaluza's five-dimensional general relativity framework, which compactified an extra spatial dimension to recover four-dimensional electromagnetism alongside gravity. Independently, in 1954, Chen Ning Yang and Robert Mills developed non-Abelian gauge theories based on isotopic spin symmetry, providing a mathematical structure for local gauge invariance that extended beyond the Abelian U(1) symmetry of electromagnetism and laid groundwork for mediating interactions via vector bosons.
Building on these insights, Richard Feynman and Murray Gell-Mann proposed in 1958 that weak currents exhibit a universal vector-axial vector (V-A) structure, where the charged weak current couples to left-handed fermions with equal vector and axial-vector components, unifying leptonic and hadronic weak decays under a single framework. This V-A theory resolved discrepancies in decay rates and incorporated parity violation naturally, motivating further exploration of gauge symmetries as a pathway to broader unification.

Unification by Glashow, Weinberg, and Salam

In 1961, Sheldon L. Glashow proposed a gauge theory unifying the weak and electromagnetic interactions under the structure group SU(2)_L U(1)_Y, where SU(2)_L acts on left-handed fermion doublets and U(1)_Y on hypercharge. This framework introduced four massless gauge bosons in the unbroken phase: the SU(2)_L triplet W^1, W^2, W^3 and the U(1)_Y singlet B. The charged weak bosons W^ arise from linear combinations of W^1 and W^2, while the neutral sector involves mixing between W^3 and B to produce the photon and a would-be massive neutral boson. To account for the observed short range of weak interactions, Glashow initially assigned masses to the weak bosons through explicit symmetry breaking terms in the Lagrangian, though this approach compromised the gauge invariance of the theory.
Subsequent developments by Glashow, Weinberg, and Salam addressed the mass issue by incorporating spontaneous symmetry breaking via the Higgs mechanism, first proposed in 1964, without explicit violation of gauge invariance. In particular, they introduced a complex scalar Higgs doublet transforming as (2, 1) under SU(2)_L U(1)_Y to generate the necessary mass terms through its vacuum expectation value. This innovation ensured renormalizability and preserved the underlying symmetries at high energies.
In 1967, Steven Weinberg formulated a complete electroweak model for leptons using this setup, predicting massive charged W^ bosons and a neutral Z boson, alongside the massless photon emerging from the unbroken U(1)_EM subgroup after symmetry breaking. The model naturally incorporated parity-violating neutral weak currents mediated by the Z, a feature absent in purely vector-like theories. Independently, Abdus Salam developed an equivalent theory in 1968, emphasizing its unification of weak and electromagnetic forces and predicting the same neutral current interactions. These works collectively established the Glashow-Weinberg-Salam (GWS) model as the cornerstone of electroweak theory.
The orthogonal mixing in the neutral sector is governed by the Weinberg angle W\theta_WW, defined by tanW=g/g\tan \theta_W = g'/gtanW=g/g, where ggg and gg'g are the coupling constants for SU(2)_L and U(1)_Y, respectively; this angle determines the relative strengths of the weak and electromagnetic interactions, with the photon coupling e=gsinW=gcosWe = g \sin \theta_W = g' \cos \theta_We=gsinW=gcosW. The GWS framework was driven empirically by observations of parity violation in beta decay, such as the 1957 Wu experiment, highlighting the chiral nature of weak interactions.

Experimental confirmations and Nobel recognition

The detection of weak neutral currents provided the first major experimental confirmation of the electroweak unification theory. In 1973, the Gargamelle bubble chamber experiment at CERN's Proton Synchrotron observed neutrino-induced hadronic showers without accompanying charged leptons, indicating neutral current interactions mediated by the predicted Z boson. This discovery, announced in a CERN seminar in July 1973, ruled out alternative models lacking neutral currents and aligned with the theory's prediction of purely left-handed weak interactions, resolving concerns about the absence of right-handed currents in weak processes.
Further validation came with the discovery of the W and Z bosons at CERN's Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) collider in 1983. The UA1 and UA2 collaborations detected W bosons through their decay into electron-neutrino pairs, confirming a mass of approximately 80 GeV/c, while Z bosons were identified via lepton pair decays with a mass around 90 GeV/c. These observations, achieved using proton-antiproton collisions at center-of-mass energies up to 540 GeV, precisely matched the electroweak model's predictions for the intermediate vector bosons responsible for weak interactions.
The theoretical contributions underpinning these discoveries were recognized with the 1979 Nobel Prize in Physics, awarded to Sheldon Glashow, Abdus Salam, and Steven Weinberg for their unified electroweak theory that incorporated both electromagnetic and weak forces under a single SU(2) U(1) gauge symmetry. Subsequent early precision tests at the Large Electron-Positron (LEP) collider and the Stanford Linear Collider (SLC) in the late 1980s and early 1990s refined measurements of the weak mixing angle, yielding sin_W 0.23, consistent with the model's radiative corrections and further confirming the absence of right-handed neutral currents through asymmetry analyses in Z decays.

Theoretical foundations

Gauge group and symmetries

The electroweak interaction is described by a gauge theory based on the local symmetry group $ SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y $, where $ SU(2)_L $ is the non-Abelian group associated with left-handed weak isospin and $ U(1)_Y $ is the Abelian group corresponding to hypercharge. This structure unifies the weak and electromagnetic forces at high energies, with the $ SU(2)_L $ sector capturing the charged and neutral weak interactions through its three generators, while $ U(1)_Y $ accounts for the hypercharge quantum number that, when combined, yields electric charge conservation. The non-Abelian nature of $ SU(2)_L $ implies self-interactions among its gauge fields, leading to nontrivial dynamics absent in the purely Abelian $ U(1)_Y $.
The gauge bosons of the theory consist of three massive vector fields from the $ SU(2)L $ triplet, denoted $ W^1\mu $, $ W^2_\mu $, and $ W^3_\mu $, which mediate the weak interactions, and a single massless vector field $ B_\mu $ from the $ U(1)Y $ singlet, associated with hypercharge. In the low-energy limit, after electroweak symmetry breaking, the neutral bosons mix to form the physical photon $ A\mu $ and $ Z_\mu $ boson through the Weinberg angle $ \theta_W $, defined such that $ \sin \theta_W = g' / \sqrt{g^2 + g'^2} $, where $ g $ and $ g' $ are the respective coupling constants of $ SU(2)_L $ and $ U(1)_Y $. The mixing is given by

ensuring the photon remains massless and couples to electric charge $ Q = T^3 + Y/2 $, where $ T^3 $ is the third component of weak isospin and $ Y $ is the hypercharge.
The theory exhibits a chiral structure, with only left-handed fermions transforming under non-trivial representations of $ SU(2)_L $, organized into doublets such as $ \begin{pmatrix} \nu_e \ e \end{pmatrix}_L $ for leptons, while right-handed fermions are singlets under $ SU(2)L $ and carry hypercharge, e.g., $ e_R $ with $ Y = -2 $. This parity-violating assignment reflects the observed V-A nature of weak interactions and ensures that electromagnetic interactions, emerging from the unbroken $ U(1){EM} $, are vector-like and parity-conserving.
The interactions of fermions and gauge bosons are encoded in the covariant derivative,

This form guarantees the invariance of the Lagrangian under local $ SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y $ transformations, providing the foundation for the unified electroweak dynamics.

Spontaneous symmetry breaking

Spontaneous symmetry breaking occurs when the laws of physics, as encoded in the Lagrangian of a quantum field theory, exhibit a symmetry, but the ground stateor vacuumthat minimizes the energy does not share that symmetry. This phenomenon arises because the system selects a particular vacuum state among degenerate possibilities, effectively "choosing" a direction that breaks the symmetry, even though the underlying dynamics remain symmetric.
A key consequence of spontaneous symmetry breaking for continuous symmetries is the Goldstone theorem, which predicts the emergence of massless scalar particles, known as Goldstone bosons or Nambu-Goldstone bosons, corresponding to each broken generator of the symmetry group. These bosons represent excitations along the flat directions of the potential where the vacuum degeneracy lies, ensuring that the low-energy spectrum reflects the broken symmetry.
In the context of the electroweak interaction, the gauge symmetry group SU(2)_L \times U(1)Y undergoes spontaneous breaking to the electromagnetic subgroup U(1)\mathrm{EM}, resulting in three broken generators and thus three Goldstone modes. This breaking is essential for generating the masses of the W and Z bosons while keeping the photon massless, consistent with the observed structure of weak and electromagnetic interactions.
Analogous examples illustrate the concept in other physical systems. In superconductivity, Philip Anderson showed that the spontaneous breaking of gauge symmetry leads to the Meissner effect, where the photon acquires an effective mass inside the superconductor, akin to the Higgs mechanism but without true Goldstone bosons due to gauge invariance. Similarly, in quantum chromodynamics (QCD), the approximate chiral symmetry SU(2)_L \times SU(2)_R breaks to the diagonal SU(2)_V, producing the pions as nearly massless Nambu-Goldstone bosons, as modeled by Yoichiro Nambu and Gabriele Jona-Lasinio.
The scale of electroweak symmetry breaking is set by the vacuum expectation value (VEV) of the scalar field, denoted v \approx 246 GeV, determined from the Fermi constant G_F via the relation v = ( \sqrt{2} G_F )^{-1/2}. This value characterizes the energy scale at which the symmetry is broken and governs the masses in the electroweak sector.

Higgs mechanism

In the electroweak theory, the Higgs mechanism employs a complex scalar field \phi that transforms as a fundamental representation (doublet) under the SU(2)L_LL gauge group with hypercharge Y=1Y = 1Y=1. This choice ensures that the scalar field's vacuum expectation value (VEV) can generate masses for the weak gauge bosons while preserving the unbroken U(1)EM_\mathrm{EM}EM electromagnetic symmetry. The mechanism relies on spontaneous symmetry breaking, where the scalar field acquires a nonzero VEV in its neutral component, breaking the SU(2)L_L \timesL U(1)Y_YY gauge symmetry down to U(1)EM_\mathrm{EM}EM.

The charged W^\pm bosons and the neutral Z boson thus acquire mass, while the orthogonal photon field combination remains massless, mediating the unbroken electromagnetic interaction.

A key theoretical motivation for the Higgs mechanism is its role in maintaining perturbative unitarity at high energies. In the absence of the Higgs, longitudinal weak boson scattering amplitudes, such as WL+WLWL+WLW_L^+ W_L^- \to W_L^+ W_L^-WL+WLWL+WL, grow with energy and violate unitarity bounds above scales around 1 TeV. The s-channel exchange of the Higgs boson cancels these leading contributions, restoring unitarity and ensuring the consistency of the electroweak theory as an effective field theory up to higher energies.

Physical implications

Gauge bosons and their properties

The electroweak theory features three physical gauge bosons: the massless photon (), the charged W and W bosons, and the neutral Z boson, all characterized by spin 1 as required by the underlying SU(2) U(1) gauge symmetry. These bosons emerge after electroweak symmetry breaking, where the originally massless gauge fields acquire their distinct properties through the Higgs mechanism. The photon remains massless, ensuring the long-range nature of electromagnetism, while the W bosons have a mass of approximately 80 GeV/c and the Z boson has a mass of approximately 91 GeV/c, as precisely measured in high-energy collisions.
The charged W and W bosons mediate the charged-current component of the weak interaction, facilitating processes that change the flavor of quarks or leptons, such as the beta decay of a neutron into a proton, electron, and antineutrino. In contrast, the neutral Z boson mediates neutral-current weak interactions, which do not alter flavor and thus conserve charge while allowing elastic scattering, such as neutrino-electron scattering. The photon, post-unification, solely mediates the electromagnetic force between charged particles, unifying it with the weak interaction at high energies. The physical states of the W, Z, and photon arise from a rotation of the original gauge fields parameterized by the Weinberg angle _W, which determines the mixing between the weak isospin and hypercharge fields.
The weak bosons exhibit vector and axial-vector couplings to fermions, embodying the chiral structure of the weak interaction where left-handed fermions couple preferentially, leading to maximal parity violation. This V-A (vector minus axial-vector) form distinguishes the weak force from the purely vector-coupled electromagnetism mediated by the photon. The substantial masses of the W and Z bosons, far exceeding that of typical hadrons, confine the weak interaction to a short range of about 10^{-18} m, governed by the Yukawa exchange potential where the range scales inversely with the mediator mass. This brevity contrasts sharply with the infinite range of electromagnetic interactions.

Weak neutral and charged currents

In the electroweak theory, weak interactions are mediated through charged and neutral currents, which arise from the coupling of fermions to the W and Z gauge bosons at tree level. These currents embody the unification of electromagnetic and weak forces, with the charged currents responsible for flavor-changing processes and the neutral currents for flavor-diagonal interactions. The structure ensures parity violation, primarily involving left-handed fermions, while right-handed currents are suppressed.
Charged weak currents take the V-A (vector minus axial-vector) form, coupling left-handed fermion doublets via the charged W bosons. The interaction Lagrangian for these currents is given by

For quarks, the current involves Cabibbo-mixed states, such as $ J^\mu = \bar{u} \gamma^\mu (1 - \gamma_5) d' $, where $ d' $ is a linear combination of down-type quarks. A canonical example is muon decay, $ \mu^- \to e^- \bar{\nu}e \nu\mu $, mediated by the W boson at tree level, demonstrating the purely left-handed nature and resulting in maximal parity violation. Right-handed currents are absent at tree level due to the chiral structure of the theory, suppressing processes involving right-handed fermions.

Loop-level contributions, such as those from electroweak radiative corrections, modify these couplings at higher orders but do not alter the fundamental tree-level structure.

Fermion couplings and mixing

Quark mixing arises because the mass eigenstates of down-type quarks (d, s, b) do not align with the weak eigenstates, necessitating a unitary mixing matrix known as the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa (CKM) matrix VVV, which rotates the down-type fields: di=j=13Vijdjd'_i = \sum_{j=1}^3 V_{ij} d_jdi=j=13Vijdj. The CKM framework was introduced to accommodate three generations of quarks while incorporating CP violation through the irreducible phase in a renormalizable theory.
For leptons, a similar mixing matrix, the Pontecorvo-Maki-Nakagawa-Sakata (PMNS) matrix UUU, describes neutrino flavor mixing in charged-current interactions involving left-handed neutrinos, assuming massless or nearly massless neutrinos in the core electroweak model prior to extensions for neutrino masses. The PMNS matrix is unitary and parameterized analogously to the CKM with three angles and a possible CP-violating phase, though its role was initially motivated by neutrino oscillation hypotheses rather than direct electroweak coupling necessities. In the standard electroweak Lagrangian, charged leptons couple without mixing in the minimal setup, but neutrino mixing introduces flavor rotation upon mass generation.

CP violation in the electroweak sector originates primarily from the single complex phase in the CKM matrix, which induces differences between matter and antimatter processes in charged-current decays, such as K0K0K^0 \leftrightarrow \overline{K}^0K0K0 mixing and B meson decays, without requiring additional sources in the minimal model. This phase, 68\delta \approx 68^\circ68, is the sole parameter responsible for observed CP asymmetries, linking quark mixing to the baryon asymmetry of the universe through processes like electroweak baryogenesis.

Experimental aspects

Discovery of W and Z bosons

The discovery of the W and Z bosons occurred in 1983 at CERN's Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS), repurposed as a proton-antiproton collider with a center-of-mass energy of s = 540 GeV. The UA1 and UA2 collaborations independently observed these particles, confirming key predictions of the electroweak theory. The SPS collider, operational from 1981, enabled high-energy collisions necessary to produce these heavy bosons, with antiprotons accumulated in a dedicated storage ring and injected into the SPS alongside protons.
The W boson was discovered first by both collaborations during data collected in late 1982. UA1 identified six events of W e decays, characterized by a high-transverse-momentum (p_T) isolated electron accompanied by significant missing transverse energy (E_T) from the undetected neutrino. UA2 observed a similar signature in six events with single isolated electrons of high p_T and missing E_T. These topologies were crucial to distinguish the signal from abundant QCD backgrounds, such as two-jet events mimicking leptons. The measured production cross-section times branching ratio, _W BR(W e) 630 pb, aligned closely with electroweak predictions. The W mass was determined to be m_W 80.4 GeV, in agreement with theoretical expectations to within about 1%.
The Z boson discovery followed in spring 1983, with UA1 and UA2 analyzing data from April to May runs. UA1 detected four Z ee events and one Z event, while UA2 found three ee pairs, all featuring back-to-back high-p_T lepton pairs with an invariant mass peak around 90-95 GeV. Background challenges included cosmic rays and hadron misidentification, mitigated by stringent cuts on lepton isolation and transverse momentum. The Z width was estimated at _Z 2.5 GeV, consistent with the broad resonance expected from its short lifetime. The measured Z mass, m_Z 91.2 GeV, also matched theory to ~1%, with the cross-section times branching ratio _Z BR(Z ee) 74 pb. These masses aligned with predictions derived from the Higgs vacuum expectation value in the electroweak model.

Precision electroweak measurements

Precision electroweak measurements, conducted primarily at the LEP and SLC colliders in the 1990s and early 2000s, have provided stringent tests of the Standard Model through high-precision studies of Z-boson properties and related observables. These measurements, building on the initial discoveries of the W and Z bosons at CERN in the 1980s, focused on loop-level quantum corrections to refine predictions for electroweak parameters. At LEP1, operating at the Z resonance (s M_Z), experiments ALEPH, DELPHI, L3, and OPAL accumulated over 17 million Z decays, enabling determinations of the Z-boson total width _Z = 2.4955 0.0023 GeV, which constrains the number of light neutrino species to N_ = 2.9840 0.0082 and verifies the three-generation structure of fermions. Similarly, partial widths to fermions and hadronic cross-sections were measured with percent-level precision, serving as inputs for global fits.
Forward-backward asymmetries A_FB, measuring the angular distribution of decay products, were key to probing parity violation in neutral currents. At LEP1, lepton asymmetries yielded A_FB^e = 0.0145 0.0025, A_FB^ = 0.0169 0.0013, and A_FB^ = 0.0188 0.0017, while quark asymmetries were higher, e.g., A_FB^b = 0.0996 0.0016, sensitive to flavor-specific couplings. Complementary measurements at SLC, utilizing polarized electron beams, provided the left-right asymmetry A_LR = 0.15138 0.00216 from hadronic final states, offering a clean determination of the electron's weak coupling. These asymmetries, combined across LEP and SLC, extracted the effective weak mixing angle sin_W^eff = 0.23151 0.00016, a fundamental parameter encoding radiative corrections. The slight tension between LEP (sin_W^eff 0.2315) and SLC ( 0.2306) values, at about 2.3, highlights the precision achieved and motivates ongoing scrutiny.
Radiative corrections play a central role in interpreting these data, with the parameter quantifying custodial symmetry breaking from loops involving the top quark and Higgs boson. The top-quark contribution dominates quadratically, 0.00934 (m_t / 172.61 GeV)^2, where m_t = 172.61 0.58 GeV from direct measurements, while Higgs loops provide a milder logarithmic dependence. Prior to the 2012 Higgs discovery, these precision data imposed indirect bounds on the Higgs mass, favoring m_H 94^{+29}_{-24} GeV and excluding m_H > 152 GeV at 95% confidence level when combined with direct search limits of m_H > 114 GeV from LEP2. Extended analyses, including top-mass uncertainties, relaxed the upper limit to around 200 GeV, narrowing the allowed window and predicting a light Higgs.
Contemporary analyses, updated with LHC inputs on m_t and M_W = 80.385 0.015 GeV, continue to test electroweak consistency, with the preferred Higgs mass from precision data at 97^{+18}_{-16} GeV, slightly below the observed 125 GeV value. The 2024 CMS measurement of the W boson mass further confirmed consistency with Standard Model predictions, resolving previous tensions observed in earlier determinations. As of 2025, data from LHC Run 3 continue to refine these parameters without significant deviations. Anomalies in related observables, such as the muon anomalous magnetic moment, were previously noted; however, the final 2025 measurement from the Muon g-2 experiment at Fermilab achieved record precision and confirmed agreement with Standard Model expectations, resolving the long-standing tension. These results, alongside Z-pole legacies, underscore the enduring impact of precision electroweak measurements in constraining beyond-Standard-Model scenarios.

Role of the Higgs boson

The discovery of the Higgs boson in 2012 by the ATLAS and CMS experiments at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) provided direct evidence for the mechanism responsible for electroweak symmetry breaking. Both collaborations observed a new particle with a mass of approximately 125 GeV in the decay channels $ H \to \gamma\gamma $ and $ H \to ZZ^* \to 4\ell $, where \ell denotes electrons or muons, achieving significances exceeding 5\sigma with combined data from proton-proton collisions at center-of-mass energies of 7 and 8 TeV. Subsequent observations in the $ H \to WW^* \to \ell\nu\ell\nu $ channel further corroborated the particle's properties, consistent with the Standard Model (SM) Higgs boson expectations.
The primary production mechanisms at the LHC for this Higgs boson mass are gluon fusion (ggF), which dominates due to the strong coupling of gluons to the top quark loop, and vector boson fusion (VBF), involving the emission of W or Z bosons from quarks. These modes, along with associated production with vector bosons (VH), were identified through their distinct kinematic signatures in the observed events, enabling cross-section measurements that align with SM predictions within uncertainties. The total decay width of the Higgs boson, inferred indirectly from off-shell production and decay analyses, is bounded to be less than 22 MeV at 95% confidence level, compatible with the narrow SM width of about 4.1 MeV.
Measurements of the Higgs couplings to particles have verified their consistency with SM values. The coupling modifiers V\kappa_VV for vector bosons (W and Z) and f\kappa_ff for fermions are both consistent with unity (V1.010.08\kappa_V \approx 1.01 \pm 0.08V1.010.08, f0.980.07\kappa_f \approx 0.98 \pm 0.07f0.980.07 from combined ATLAS and CMS data), indicating no significant deviations from the predicted strengths proportional to particle masses. These results stem from analyses across multiple production and decay channels, including $ H \to bb $, $ H \to \tau\tau $, and $ H \to \gamma\gamma $, accumulated over LHC Run 1 and Run 2 datasets.

The theoretical prediction of the Higgs mechanism, proposed independently by Franois Englert, Robert Brout, and Peter Higgs in 1964, was recognized with the 2013 Nobel Prize in Physics awarded to Englert and Higgs for their work on the mechanism contributing to electroweak symmetry breaking. The observed Higgs mass of around 125 GeV has profound implications for the stability of the electroweak vacuum in the SM; combined with the top quark mass, it places the vacuum in a metastable state, stable over the universe's lifetime but potentially susceptible to tunneling to a lower-energy state at extremely high scales, motivating searches for new physics.

Extensions and open questions

Integration in the Standard Model

The Standard Model of particle physics is a gauge theory based on the local symmetry group $ SU(3)_c \times SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y $, where the electroweak sector corresponds to the $ SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y $ subgroup that unifies the electromagnetic and weak interactions, while $ SU(3)_c $ governs the strong force acting on quarks. This structure ensures that the electroweak interactions are described by a chiral gauge theory, with left-handed fermions transforming under non-trivial representations of $ SU(2)_L $ and right-handed fermions being singlets under this group, coupled to the hypercharge $ U(1)_Y $. The full integration embeds the electroweak theory seamlessly into the broader framework, allowing consistent predictions for particle interactions across energy scales.
A key feature enabling this integration is the cancellation of gauge anomalies, which would otherwise render the theory inconsistent at the quantum level. In the Standard Model, anomalies from the electroweak sector are canceled through contributions from quarks and leptons: each generation of fermions, including the three colors of quarks, balances the triangle anomalies associated with $ SU(2)_L^3 $, $ SU(2)_L^2 U(1)_Y $, and gravitational terms, ensuring the theory remains anomaly-free without additional fields. This cancellation relies on the specific hypercharge assignments and the equal number of quark and lepton doublets per generation, a nontrivial constraint that underscores the elegance of the model's fermion content.
The renormalizability of the electroweak theory within the Standard Model was rigorously established by Gerard 't Hooft in 1971, demonstrating that the spontaneously broken gauge symmetry leads to a finite perturbative expansion despite the presence of massive vector bosons. This proof, using dimensional regularization and the Brout-Englert-Higgs mechanism, allows for precise calculations of higher-order corrections, such as those to the muon anomalous magnetic moment or weak decay rates, confirming the theory's predictive power up to the electroweak scale.
The running of the gauge couplings in the Standard Model1\alpha_11 for $ U(1)_Y $, 2\alpha_22 for $ SU(2)_L $, and 3\alpha_33 for $ SU(3)_c $provides hints of unification at a high energy scale around $ 10^{15} $ GeV, where they converge in minimal grand unified theories like SU(5), motivating extensions beyond the Standard Model while preserving its internal consistency. Unlike the vector-like structure of quantum chromodynamics (QCD), where left- and right-handed quarks couple symmetrically to gluons, the electroweak theory's chiral nature distinguishes it by treating chiralities differently, leading to parity violation in weak interactions and enabling phenomena like CP violation in the quark sector.

Anomalies and beyond-Standard-Model physics

Electroweak precision tests serve as a cornerstone for validating the Standard Model (SM), with oblique parameters S, T, and U parameterizing potential new physics contributions to gauge boson self-energies, where SM predictions set S = T = U = 0. Experimental global fits as of 2024 yield S = -0.04 0.10, T = 0.01 0.12, and U = -0.01 0.09, showing consistency within uncertainties. Mild tensions persist in related observables such as the forward-backward asymmetry A_{FB} for b quarks (3.6% deviation) and the weak mixing angle determinations across scales. An earlier 2022 CDF measurement reported a W boson mass of 80.433 0.009 GeV, suggesting a ~7 discrepancy with the SM expectation of ~80.356 GeV, but subsequent 20242025 ATLAS and CMS measurements align closely with the SM prediction of 80.353 0.006 GeV (excluding CDF), resolving the tension and identifying the CDF result as an outlier. Similarly, the muon anomalous magnetic moment g-2 showed a ~4.2 tension in earlier data, but 2025 Fermilab final results and updated theory predictions demonstrate agreement with the SM, eliminating this discrepancy. These resolutions improve the overall fit's /d.o.f. closer to 1, though ongoing analyses probe for subtle effects. Flavor anomalies in b s ^+ ^- transitions, observed primarily at LHCb, continue to show 24 deviations from SM predictions as of 2025, in observables like R_{K^{()}} (0.80.9 versus SM 1) and P'_5 in B K^ ^+ ^-, suggesting possible new physics from leptoquarks or Z' bosons.
A prominent theoretical challenge in the electroweak sector is the hierarchy problem, stemming from quadratic divergences in quantum corrections to the Higgs mass parameter, which would otherwise drive the electroweak scale (246 GeV) toward the Planck scale (10^{19} GeV) unless counteracted by extreme fine-tuning. These divergences arise primarily from top quark loops, contributing terms (3 y_t^2 / (8 ^2)) ^2 to the Higgs mass squared, where is the ultraviolet cutoff and y_t 1 is the top Yukawa coupling, necessitating bare mass cancellations at the level of one part in 10^{30} to yield the observed Higgs mass of 125 GeV. This fine-tuning violates naturalness principles, as the electroweak scale should emerge without such precision adjustments in a fundamental theory.
Several BSM frameworks address the hierarchy problem while accommodating electroweak precision data. In supersymmetry (SUSY), superpartners like light stops cancel quadratic divergences from top quark loops, stabilizing the Higgs mass up to the SUSY breaking scale (TeV), with models featuring an accidental R-symmetry to suppress gaugino masses and NMSSM-like singlets to enhance the Higgs mass at tree level. Composite Higgs models, where the Higgs emerges as a pseudo-Goldstone boson from strong dynamics breaking a global symmetry (e.g., SO(5)/SO(4)), generate the electroweak scale via partial compositeness of fermions, yielding small positive contributions to S (1/(6)) and negative to T from the composite sector, offset by fermion effects to align with measurements like S = 0.06 0.08 and T = 0.15 0.06. Warped extra-dimension models, such as Randall-Sundrum, resolve the hierarchy through exponential warping in an AdS_5 geometry, localizing the Higgs near the infrared brane and predicting S 2 (2.4 v / M_{KK})^2 with M_{KK} 35 TeV for KK modes, improving global fits (p-value up to 55%) over the SM while respecting custodial symmetry to minimize T.
The observation of tiny neutrino masses (0.010.1 eV) necessitates BSM extensions to the electroweak sector, as the SM predicts massless neutrinos. The type-I seesaw mechanism introduces right-handed neutrinos with Majorana masses M_N v^2 / ( tan ) from electroweak symmetry breaking via a dimension-5 operator suppressed by a new physics scale v, generating light active neutrino masses m_ - m_D M_N^{-1} m_D^T through mixing with Dirac masses m_D y_ v / 2, implying heavy sterile neutrinos below the electroweak scale and testable lepton flavor violations. Leptoquarks, scalar or vector particles coupling quarks to leptons, can radiatively induce neutrino Majorana masses at one- or two-loop level in models like the 3-3-1 extension, with TeV-scale leptoquarks (e.g., triplets T and singlets S) yielding eV-scale active and sterile masses via Yukawa couplings of 10^{-4}10^{-1}, while impacting rare decays like B_s ^+ ^-.

Future experimental prospects

The High-Luminosity Large Hadron Collider (HL-LHC), expected to commence operations in mid-2030, will deliver an integrated luminosity of approximately 3000 fb^{-1} at 14 TeV center-of-mass energy, enabling unprecedented precision in electroweak measurements. This upgrade will facilitate the observation of rare Higgs boson decays, such as H and H Z, with sensitivities reaching branching ratios down to 10^{-4}, providing direct probes of Higgs-fermion and Higgs-gauge boson couplings. Additionally, HL-LHC data will enhance studies of vector boson scattering (VBS) processes, testing electroweak symmetry breaking at high energies through longitudinal W and Z boson interactions.
Linear electron-positron colliders, such as the International Linear Collider (ILC) at 250 GeV and the Compact Linear Collider (CLIC) up to 3 TeV, offer clean environments for precise measurements of electroweak parameters. At these facilities, direct determinations of Higgs boson couplings to electroweak gauge bosons, including the trilinear Higgs self-coupling, are projected with per-mille precision, surpassing hadron collider capabilities due to reduced backgrounds. CLIC's higher energy reach will particularly probe triple gauge couplings in WZ and ZZ production, constraining deviations from Standard Model predictions at the 1% level.
The Future Circular Collider electron-positron stage (FCC-ee), proposed as a circular lepton collider with a 100 km circumference, will operate at the Z-pole and higher energies to achieve electroweak precision at the 10^{-5} level for parameters like sin_W. By accumulating tera-Z events, FCC-ee will refine measurements of the weak mixing angle through asymmetries in fermion production, offering a tenfold improvement over LEP-era results and serving as a benchmark for global electroweak fits.
Muon colliders represent an emerging option for a Higgs factory at multi-TeV scales, with designs targeting 10 TeV center-of-mass energy in a compact footprint. At such energies, muon colliders enable resonant Higgs production with luminosities up to 10^{35} cm^{-2}s^{-1}, allowing percent-level precision on Higgs width and couplings while minimizing beamstrahlung effects inherent to electron colliders.
These future experiments will extend sensitivity to beyond-Standard-Model physics, potentially discovering TeV-scale supersymmetric particles through electroweakino production at CLIC or leptoquarks via muon-lepton signatures at muon colliders, with mass reaches up to 5-10 TeV depending on the model. Such probes are motivated by current electroweak anomalies, like persisting flavor tensions, which hint at new physics scales accessible in the TeV regime.
