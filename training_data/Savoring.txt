Savoring
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Savoring is a key concept in positive psychology, defined as the capacity to attend to, appreciate, and enhance the positive experiences in one's life. This process involves deliberate cognitive and behavioral strategies that allow individuals to generate, prolong, and intensify positive emotions through mindful engagement with rewarding moments, such as sensory pleasures, social interactions, or personal achievements.
Developed primarily through the foundational work of psychologists Fred B. Bryant and Joseph Veroff in their 2007 book Savoring: A New Model of Positive Experience, savoring is recognized as a form of emotion regulation that counters the natural tendency for positive feelings to fade quickly. Key processes include anticipating future joys to build excitement, immersing in the present to deepen absorption, extending positive emotions by sharing them with others, and reminiscing about past events to recapture their affective impact. These strategies can be applied across various contexts, from everyday routines to therapeutic settings, and are influenced by factors like mindset, attention, and cultural norms.
Research demonstrates that savoring promotes psychological well-being by increasing resilience, life satisfaction, and overall happiness while reducing symptoms of depression and anxiety. Empirical studies, including meta-analyses of interventions, show that savoring exercisessuch as guided imagery or journaling about positive eventseffectively amplify positive affect and contribute to mental health improvements, particularly in clinical populations like those with generalized anxiety disorder. As a proactive approach, savoring bridges cognitive appraisal and emotional experience, making it a versatile tool in positive interventions and mindfulness-based therapies.

Introduction and Background

Definition and Core Concepts

Savoring is defined as the capacity to attend to, appreciate, and enhance the positive experiences in one's life. This psychological process involves the deliberate prolongation and intensification of momentary positive events to amplify their emotional impact and contribute to greater subjective well-being. As a key construct in positive psychology, savoring emphasizes the active cultivation of joy, pleasure, and other uplifting feelings rather than their mere occurrence.
At its core, savoring encompasses three primary temporal components: anticipation, savoring in the moment, and reminiscing. Anticipation refers to the pre-experiencing of future positives, such as looking forward to an upcoming vacation by imagining its delights. Savoring in the moment involves fully focusing on the sensory and emotional aspects of a current positive event, like attentively noting the flavors and aromas during a shared meal. Reminiscing entails recalling past positives after the fact, for instance, replaying a cherished memory of a family gathering to evoke renewed happiness.
Unlike passive enjoyment, which occurs without deliberate effort, savoring requires active attentional strategies and meta-awareness to regulate and up-regulate positive emotions. This intentional focus distinguishes savoring as a regulatory mechanism that individuals can employ to deepen emotional experiences, such as by sharing the joy of a kind gesture with others to further amplify its effects.

Historical Development

The concept of savoring traces its early roots to ancient philosophical traditions, particularly Epicureanism in 4th century BCE Greece, where Epicurus emphasized the mindful pursuit of simple, natural pleasures as a path to tranquility and happiness, viewing pleasure not as excess but as the savoring of life's fundamental activities to achieve a stable state of ataraxia (absence of disturbance).
In modern psychology, savoring emerged as a distinct area of study in the 1980s through the work of social psychologist Fred B. Bryant, who began exploring it as a process for enhancing positive emotions and joy, differentiating it from coping mechanisms and subjective well-being in his initial empirical investigations. Bryant's early research, including a 1982 publication with Joseph Veroff, laid foundational hints by examining self-reports of positive emotional regulation, while his 1989 work further distinguished savoring as a unique attentional process for prolonging enjoyment. These efforts focused particularly on anticipation as a savoring strategy, where individuals derive pleasure from looking forward to future positive events.
A seminal advancement came with Bryant's 2007 book, Savoring: A New Model of Positive Experience, co-authored with Veroff, which formalized savoring as a comprehensive framework for understanding how people attend to, appreciate, and amplify positive experiences across past, present, and future orientations, establishing it as a core construct in the psychology of enjoyment. This publication marked a pivotal milestone, synthesizing decades of research and influencing subsequent studies on emotional enhancement.
Savoring gained broader traction following Martin Seligman's 1998 inauguration of positive psychology as a field, which shifted focus toward strengths and well-being, integrating savoring as a key practice for cultivating positive emotions amid the movement's emphasis on human flourishing. Research proliferated in the 2000s and 2010s, with empirical work expanding from basic processes to clinical applications, such as interventions for depression and anxiety that leverage savoring to boost emotional resilience in therapeutic settings.

Theoretical Foundations

Bryant's Savoring Model

Fred B. Bryant and Joseph Veroff's savoring model conceptualizes savoring as a dynamic psychological process that involves deliberately attending to, appreciating, and enhancing positive experiences to amplify positive affect and prolong emotional well-being. This model positions savoring as an active form of emotion regulation, distinct from mere passive enjoyment, where individuals use cognitive and behavioral strategies to intensify the emotional impact of positive events. Central to the model is the idea that savoring transforms fleeting moments of pleasure into more sustained sources of happiness by focusing attention on the positive aspects of life.
The model delineates three primary temporal orientations of savoring, each corresponding to a different timeframe relative to the positive experience. Anticipating involves future-oriented savoring, where individuals generate excitement through mental imagery and planning for upcoming positive events, thereby building anticipation that heightens eventual enjoyment. Immersing refers to present-focused savoring, emphasizing full sensory engagement and mindfulness during the ongoing positive experience to deepen its immediacy and intensity. Extending encompasses past-oriented savoring, achieved through reminiscence techniques such as sharing memories with others or documenting experiences (e.g., via photos or journals) to relive and prolong the positive emotions after the event has ended.
Key mechanisms underlying the model include attentional deployment and cognitive reframing. Attentional deployment entails strategically directing focus toward positive stimuli while minimizing distractions, such as by limiting multitasking during enjoyable activities to sustain immersion. Cognitive reframing involves reinterpretations that enhance appreciation, for instance, cultivating gratitude by reflecting on the rarity or fortune of the positive event, which can transform neutral or mild pleasures into profound ones. These mechanisms operate across the temporal phases to modulate and elevate emotional responses.
Individual differences in savoring are captured through trait-like orientations, distinguishing high savorerswho habitually employ these strategiesfrom low savorers, who may overlook or dampen positives. These orientations are assessed using tools like the Ways of Savoring Checklist (WOSC), a 60-item measure that evaluates the frequency of ten specific savoring strategies, such as sharing with others or self-congratulation, revealing how dispositional tendencies influence the capacity to derive pleasure from positive events.
Bryant and Veroff's 2007 model diagram illustrates savoring as a sequential flow: a positive event triggers initial positive emotion, which is then channeled through savoring strategies (e.g., attentional focus and reframing) to produce enhanced, prolonged affect, ultimately contributing to greater overall well-being. This schematic emphasizes the model's process-oriented nature, showing how interventions at the strategy level can interrupt or augment the pathway from event to emotional outcome.

Related Psychological Theories

Savoring intersects with Barbara Fredrickson's broaden-and-build theory of positive emotions, which posits that positive emotions broaden individuals' momentary thought-action repertoires and build enduring personal resources such as resilience and social bonds. In this framework, savoring prolongs and amplifies positive emotions, thereby facilitating the accumulation of psychological resources over time; for instance, savoring interventions have been shown to increase positive affect following stressors, aligning with the theory's emphasis on emotional expansion as a pathway to long-term well-being. This integration highlights savoring as a practical mechanism for leveraging positive emotions to foster adaptive coping and personal growth.
Savoring also connects to mindfulness-based theories, particularly those developed by Jon Kabat-Zinn in the 1990s, which emphasize non-judgmental awareness of the present moment to cultivate psychological health. While mindfulness involves broad attentional focus on current experiences, savoring applies this awareness specifically to positive events, enhancing their emotional impact without judgment. Research indicates that dispositional mindfulness and savoring interact synergistically to predict higher positive emotions and better psychological health, with savoring amplifying the benefits of mindful presence during joyful moments.
The concept of savoring relates to Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's flow theory, introduced in 1975, which describes optimal experience states characterized by deep immersion and intrinsic motivation in challenging activities. Savoring enhances these flow states by promoting conscious appreciation and prolongation of the associated positive feelings, thereby deepening engagement and enjoyment during immersive tasks. This overlap underscores savoring's role in intensifying the intrinsic rewards of flow, contributing to sustained motivation and fulfillment in positive psychology interventions.
Savoring overlaps with gratitude research, notably the work of Robert Emmons in the 2000s, where it serves as a core mechanism in interventions that boost well-being by focusing on positive life events. Emmons and colleagues have demonstrated that gratitude practices, such as journaling benefits, inherently involve savoring to recognize and amplify positives, leading to improved subjective well-being and emotional resilience. This connection positions savoring as an underlying process that explains the efficacy of gratitude exercises in shifting affective states.
Criticisms of these theoretical overlaps include the potential conflation of savoring with hedonic adaptation processes, where prolonged attention to positives might inadvertently accelerate emotional habituation rather than sustain benefits. Although savoring is often proposed as a counter to adaptationby actively up-regulating positive affect to delay return to baseline happinesssome argue that without varied strategies, it risks blending into the very adaptation dynamics it aims to mitigate, necessitating clearer distinctions in future models.

Empirical Research

Key Studies and Methodologies

One of the foundational contributions to savoring research came from Fred B. Bryant's early lab experiments in the late 1980s, which conceptualized savoring as a form of perceived control over positive emotional experiences. In a seminal 1989 study, Bryant proposed a four-factor model of perceived control that included savoring as the process of obtaining and prolonging positive outcomes through cognitive and behavioral strategies, such as anticipation of future joys. These experiments involved participants reflecting on positive events to differentiate savoring from other control mechanisms, laying the groundwork for later empirical work on enhancing joy via pre-event mental imagery.
Building on this, longitudinal research in the 2000s and 2010s tracked savoring habits over time to assess their stability and links to well-being. For instance, a 2017 cohort study by Bryant and colleagues followed older adults over multiple assessments, revealing that perceived savoring abilities remained relatively stable but varied with health perceptions, using repeated measures to capture temporal changes in beliefs about anticipation, present-moment enjoyment, and reminiscence. Similarly, Quoidbach et al.'s 2010 study, while primarily cross-sectional, incorporated predictive analyses from a large sample to demonstrate how savoring strategies prospectively influenced life satisfaction, informing subsequent longitudinal designs.
Key methodologies in savoring research include self-report scales like the Savoring Beliefs Inventory (SBI), developed by Bryant in 2003, which measures individuals' confidence in their ability to savor across anticipation, in-the-moment, and reminiscence domains through a 24-item Likert-scale questionnaire validated in multiple samples. Experimental manipulations often employ randomized designs to induce savoring, such as guided audio recordings prompting participants to focus sensory details on current positive stimuli, as in Hanley et al.'s 2023 study where audio-led savoring meditation was compared to neutral conditions. For capturing real-time dynamics, ecological momentary assessment (EMA) has been increasingly used, involving smartphone prompts to report savoring behaviors during daily positive events, as demonstrated in Ji et al.'s 2021 two-week study that examined reciprocal effects between positive emotions and savoring in naturalistic settings.
Cross-cultural studies from the 2010s highlighted variations in savoring practices, often comparing individualistic Western samples to collectivist Eastern ones. Miyamoto and Ma's 2011 experimental study, for example, found that participants from Eastern cultures (e.g., Japan) were more likely to dampen positive emotions through dialectical strategies, showing lower emphasis on anticipation-focused savoring compared to Western (e.g., U.S.) participants who amplified joy via prolonging techniques. These investigations typically used mixed-methods approaches, including scenario-based manipulations and cultural priming.
Most randomized controlled trials (RCTs) in savoring research feature sample sizes of 100-500 participants to ensure statistical power for detecting medium effect sizes in emotional outcomes, as seen in Quoidbach et al. (2010) with 282 participants and Hanley et al. (2023) with 42 adults. Qualitative interviews complement these designs by refining techniques, such as probing participant experiences post-intervention to adapt prompts for diverse groups, as in the development of multicultural savoring tools.

Major Findings on Effects

Research on savoring has demonstrated consistent positive effects on emotional outcomes, with meta-analyses indicating that savoring interventions significantly increase positive affect with large effect sizes (Hedges' g 0.7) and reduce negative rumination by promoting attentional shifts away from distressing thoughts. For instance, a 2014 meta-analytic review of savoring interventions found moderate effects on enhancing happiness and positive emotions while diminishing affective negativity.
In terms of broader well-being, studies from the 2010s have linked habitual savoring to higher life satisfaction and greater psychological resilience, with high savorers reporting enhanced ability to buffer stress and maintain positive outlooks during adversity. Additionally, longitudinal and cross-sectional research associates stronger savoring tendencies with lower symptoms of depression, as savoring appears to interrupt cycles of negative affect and foster emotional recovery. Recent meta-analyses (2024) in clinical populations report moderate effect sizes (g 0.5) for improving well-being and reducing negative affect.
Physiologically, neuroimaging evidence, including fMRI studies from the 2010s and 2020s, reveals that savoring activates key reward centers in the brain, such as the ventral striatum, which is involved in processing positive stimuli and reinforcing pleasurable experiences. Correlational data also suggest that savoring practices correlate with reduced cortisol levels, particularly through mechanisms like reminiscing on positive memories, which buffer acute stress responses and lower physiological arousal.
Individual differences play a notable role in savoring's impacts, with high savorers exhibiting superior emotion regulation skills, including better modulation of positive and negative affects in daily life. Age-related patterns show that savoring ability tends to decline in older adults, potentially due to attentional shifts or future time perspectives, though targeted training can mitigate this and restore benefits.
Overall effect sizes for savoring interventions are moderate, typically around Cohen's d 0.5, indicating meaningful but not transformative changes in emotional and well-being outcomes. Gender differences are minimal, with no robust evidence of systematic variations in savoring efficacy across sexes.

Practical Applications

Savoring Techniques

Savoring techniques are evidence-based strategies designed to heighten the awareness and intensity of positive experiences across different temporal phases: anticipation of future events, engagement in the present moment, and reflection on past occurrences. These methods, primarily outlined in the seminal work by Bryant and Veroff, enable individuals to actively modulate positive emotions through specific cognitive and behavioral actions. Additional practices build savoring as a trait and adapt it to particular contexts, supported by empirical research in positive psychology.
Anticipation Techniques
Anticipation involves savoring future positive events by mentally engaging with them to build excitement and motivation. One core approach is visualizing upcoming positives, such as imagining the sensory details of a planned vacation to amplify pre-event joy. Sharing excitement with others further enhances this phase; discussing anticipated pleasures with friends or family can intensify positive affect through social validation and collective enthusiasm. A related method, mental contrasting, combines visualization of desired future outcomes with realistic consideration of potential obstacles, fostering commitment and preventing unrealistic optimism that might dampen savoring.
In-the-Moment Strategies
During ongoing positive experiences, techniques focus on deepening immersion and expression to prolong enjoyment. Sensory sharpening entails deliberately attending to specific perceptual elements, such as the aroma and texture of a meal, to heighten appreciation. Verbalizing appreciations aloud, a form of behavioral expression, amplifies emotions by outwardly acknowledging the moment's value, like commenting on a beautiful view during a walk. Limiting distractions supports absorption, the state of full engagement; for instance, implementing phone-free meals allows undivided attention to the experience, reducing interference and enhancing presence.
Reminiscence Methods
Reminiscing extends positive emotions by revisiting past events through deliberate recall. Photo review involves examining images from joyful occasions to reconstruct and relive the associated feelings, thereby building lasting memories. Journaling positives, such as noting three beneficial events from the day and their causes, cultivates gratitude and reinforces emotional benefits over time. Sensory cues like listening to music linked to past events trigger vivid recollections, evoking the original positive states through auditory associations.
Trait-Building Exercises
To develop savoring as a habitual skill, individuals can engage in regular practices that strengthen the capacity over time. Daily savoring logs, such as recording positive moments and reflections at day's end, promote consistent awareness and have been shown to increase overall well-being. Guided practices, typically lasting 10-15 minutes, involve structured prompts to focus on positives, like a brief meditation on recent joys, helping to internalize the process.
Adaptations for Contexts
Savoring techniques can be tailored to specific settings for greater relevance. In nature, mindful walks encourage noticing environmental details, such as the rustle of leaves or sunlight patterns, to foster present-moment appreciation. For social events, emphasizing connection through focused interactions, like maintaining eye contact during conversations, sharpens relational positives and amplifies shared joy via sensory and emotional attunement.

Interventions and Real-World Uses

Savoring has been integrated into clinical interventions, particularly within cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) frameworks for anxiety disorders. In protocols developed during the 2010s and refined in subsequent studies, savoring modules are added to standard CBT to enhance positive emotion regulation, such as through smartphone-based ecological momentary interventions that prompt users to savor past, present, and future positive experiences. For instance, a randomized controlled trial of a savoring intervention for generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) reduced worry symptoms and increased positive mindsets like optimism, with effects sustained at follow-up. In PTSD therapy, savoring beliefsindividuals' confidence in their ability to prolong positive emotionsserve as a buffer against posttraumatic stress symptoms following adverse events, with higher savoring beliefs predicting lower PTS total scores and reduced hypervigilance or negative cognitions six months later. These approaches emphasize positive memory enhancement to counteract trauma-related avoidance, showing promise in mitigating depression and PTS clusters beyond traditional factors like neuroticism.
In educational settings, savoring interventions are incorporated into programs aimed at boosting student resilience, particularly in university curricula during the 2020s. Among Taiwanese university students, savoring beliefs positively predicted happiness through sequential mediation by resilience and meaning in life, with educators recommending positive psychology activities to foster these skills and reduce anxiety. Workplace training programs utilize savoring to prevent burnout, especially in high-stress roles like school principals. Positive psychology interventions teaching savoring strategies, such as in-the-moment focus on joyful events or retrospective journaling, have been shown to enhance well-being, increase life satisfaction, and mitigate work-induced stress by building a reservoir of positive memories.
For daily life applications, mobile apps incorporating savoring practices, such as Happify's modules for tracking and amplifying positive experiences, provide guided exercises to promote habitual engagement post-2015. Cultural practices like the Japanese concept of ikigai, which involves finding purpose through enjoyable activities, align with savoring by encouraging mindful appreciation of small joys to sustain well-being, though direct empirical links remain exploratory. Savoring is applied in both individual and group formats; individually, it supports self-guided reflection, while in group settings like couples therapy, relational savoringfocusing on shared positive connectednessstrengthens bonds and attachment security through exercises recalling moments of sensitivity and competence.
Recent applications as of 2025 include relational savoring interventions for bereaved older adults, which have demonstrated reductions in grief and improvements in well-being, and group-based programs for mothers of young children to enhance emotional connectedness and resilience. Savoring-based approaches, such as meditation targeting positive emotion contrasts, are also emerging for transdiagnostic emotional disorders.
In applied contexts, savoring interventions yield measurable outcomes, such as significant improvements in mood and depressive symptoms in structured programs. Eight-week programs, including those for older adults, report enhanced resilience and reduced negative affect, establishing savoring as a scalable tool for gains in positive mood metrics across diverse groups.

Challenges and Future Directions

Limitations and Criticisms

Much of the empirical research on savoring relies heavily on self-report measures to assess beliefs, strategies, and outcomes, which can introduce biases such as recall inaccuracies and social desirability effects. For instance, systematic reviews of savoring interventions highlight that outcomes like well-being and emotional regulation are predominantly evaluated through validated self-report scales, potentially limiting the objectivity of findings.
Early studies on savoring, particularly those predating the 2020s, often featured small sample sizes and underrepresented non-Western participants, with psychology research in general drawing from Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic (WEIRD) populations in approximately 96% of cases. This lack of diversity constrains the generalizability of results, as savoring processes may vary across cultural contexts.
Savoring research has been critiqued for its Western-centric focus, emphasizing individualistic strategies like anticipation of personal future rewards, which may not align with collectivist societies where dialectical beliefs lead to greater dampening of positive emotions to maintain balance. Recent 2020s studies, such as those adapting relational savoring for multicultural use, reveal mismatches in efficacy when applying Western-derived techniques in Eastern contexts, underscoring the need for culturally tailored approaches.
While savoring shows promise in reducing depressive symptoms in some clinical trials, evidence is limited and mixed for severe depression, with many interventions failing to outperform active controls and lacking long-term follow-up data. Systematic reviews indicate poor methodological quality in over two-thirds of relevant randomized controlled trials, including inadequate blinding and small samples primarily among older adults, restricting applicability to broader clinical populations.
Current summaries of savoring's empirical base often overlook 2020s advancements in neuroimaging, such as electrocortical studies showing that savoring enhances late positive potential responses to positive mental imagery, though these effects are moderated by depression severity. Additionally, research gaps persist regarding equity, with few studies examining access barriers for low-socioeconomic-status groups, despite broader psychological evidence linking lower SES to diminished responsiveness to positive rewards that could underpin savoring deficits.
Ethical concerns arise from the commercialization of techniques in positive psychology, including savoring, where unregulated apps and programs may promote unrealistic expectations of well-being without sufficient evidence, potentially exacerbating self-blame among users facing systemic challenges. Critics argue this commodification transforms psychological tools into profit-driven ideologies, sidelining social justice considerations and diverse population needs.

Emerging Research Areas

Recent advancements in technology are integrating digital tools with savoring practices to create more accessible and immersive interventions. Pilot studies post-2020 have explored AI-assisted and app-based platforms for guiding users through savoring exercises, particularly during disruptions like the COVID-19 pandemic, where online self-help interventions increased savoring beliefs and subjective well-being among participants. Virtual reality (VR) simulations represent a promising frontier, with immersive environments designed to enhance positive experiences; for instance, the e-SaVoR project explored savoring strategies via an online platform with plans for VR integration to boost positive emotions and well-being in emerging adults, demonstrating feasibility and preliminary efficacy in a 2022 trial. Similarly, a 2025 randomized controlled trial integrated immersive VR relaxation scenarios with savoring prompts for patients with chronic respiratory diseases, resulting in significant short- and long-term gains in emotional and psychological well-being compared to controls.
Neuroscientific research on savoring is shifting toward longitudinal designs to examine its impact on neural plasticity, with early 2020s studies using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) to identify correlates. These investigations reveal that savoring modulates activity in brain regions like the ventral striatum and prefrontal cortex, enhancing positive emotion processing and potentially countering habitual rumination patterns. Emerging 2025+ protocols aim to track savoring-induced changes over time via repeated brain imaging, building on evidence that willful savoring increases electrocortical responses to positive stimuli, which could inform plasticity-focused therapies.
Interdisciplinary expansions are linking savoring to environmental psychology, where it may foster appreciation for sustainable practices by amplifying positive experiences tied to eco-friendly behaviors, though empirical pilots remain nascent. In aging research, savoring interventions address hedonic declinethe diminishing intensity of positive emotions with ageby targeting reduced hedonic motivation; studies show that older adults with stronger savoring beliefs report higher well-being, suggesting potential to mitigate age-related emotional blunting through targeted training.
Equity-focused directions emphasize inclusive savoring studies across diverse populations, including cultural adaptations of relational savoring to ensure relevance in multicultural contexts, as demonstrated in 2024 interventions that heightened positive connectedness in varied groups. Longitudinal trials are prioritized to assess long-term efficacy in underrepresented communities, addressing gaps in prior research dominated by Western samples and promoting broader applicability.
