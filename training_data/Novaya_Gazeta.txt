 Novaya Gazeta is an independent Russian newspaper founded in 1993 by a group of journalists, including Dmitry Muratov, who left the state-affiliated Komsomolskaya Pravda to establish an outlet focused on investigative reporting. The publication gained prominence for its in-depth coverage of corruption, human rights abuses, and conflicts such as the Chechen wars, often challenging official narratives and incurring risks to its staff.
Under Muratov's long tenure as editor-in-chief, Novaya Gazeta earned international recognition, including the 2021 Nobel Peace Prize awarded to him for efforts to sustain freedom of expression in Russia amid mounting restrictions. The newspaper's journalists have faced severe repercussions, including assassinations like that of Anna Politkovskaya in 2006, attributed to her reporting on state abuses. In response to post-2022 invasion censorship laws prohibiting descriptions of the Ukraine conflict as "war," Novaya Gazeta suspended domestic operations in March 2022 to avoid penalties, leading to a court-ordered shutdown and license revocation later that year.
Exiled staff subsequently relaunched Novaya Gazeta Europe from abroad, continuing investigations while the original entity was labeled undesirable in Russia, effectively banning its dissemination. Despite its adversarial stance toward the Kremlin, which has resulted in multiple fines and operational curbs under foreign agent and fake news regulations, the outlet has contributed to exposing systemic issues through empirical reporting, though its selective focus has drawn critiques of partisanship from pro-government perspectives.

History

Founding and 1990s Development

Novaya Gazeta was established on April 1, 1993, by Dmitry Muratov and approximately 50 colleagues who had departed from the daily Komsomolskaya Pravda in protest against its increasing alignment with Kremlin interests. The founding group sought to create an independent outlet prioritizing investigative reporting and democratic values amid Russia's post-Soviet media liberalization. Former Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev contributed funding from his 1990 Nobel Peace Prize to support the launch, enabling initial operations as a pro-democratic publication.
In its early years, the newspaper operated on a limited schedule, initially as a weekly supplement before transitioning to bi-weekly issues, reflecting the economic constraints of the era's hyperinflation and media market instability. Muratov assumed the role of editor-in-chief in 1995, steering the outlet toward deeper scrutiny of corruption and power abuses. Despite these ambitions, Novaya Gazeta encountered severe financial difficulties in the mid-1990s, including near-bankruptcy, as advertising revenues plummeted and many nascent independent publications folded amid the "Wild East" economic chaos.
By the late 1990s, the newspaper had carved a niche as one of Russia's few surviving critical voices, sustaining operations through reader subscriptions and modest donor support while avoiding state subsidies that compromised peers. Its persistence contrasted with the broader trend of media consolidation under oligarchic influences, laying groundwork for future investigative rigor without yet facing the intensified state pressures of the 2000s.

2000s: Consolidation and Key Investigations

In the 2000s, Novaya Gazeta strengthened its investigative framework under editor Dmitry Muratov, focusing on corruption, military mishandling, and human rights abuses despite escalating state controls after Vladimir Putin's inauguration in 2000. The newspaper sustained a print circulation of roughly 100,000 to 200,000 copies, relying on subscriber support and international recognition to counter financial strains from advertising boycotts by state-aligned entities. This period marked a shift toward deeper, risk-laden probes, often verifying claims through fieldwork in conflict zones and bureaucratic leaks, contrasting with state media's narrative alignment.
Prominent investigations exposed flaws in the August 2000 Kursk submarine sinking, where Novaya Gazeta reporters alleged crew members survived for hours or days post-explosion and criticized delayed rescue efforts by Russian authorities. These reports, drawing on forensic discrepancies and witness accounts, faced defamation charges but were upheld by the European Court of Human Rights in 2017 as ethical journalism. Similarly, Yuri Shchekochikhin's series on the "Three Whales" scandal revealed a furniture smuggling ring evading millions in duties via complicit customs officials and security services, implicating figures close to power structures. His sudden illness in June 2003, diagnosed as an allergic reaction but suspected as poisoning, led to his death on July 3, with Novaya Gazeta demanding a criminal probe denied by prosecutors.
Regional corruption probes carried lethal risks, as seen with Igor Domnikov's reporting on illegal timber operations in Moscow Oblast's Shatura district, targeting organized crime ties to local officials. Assaulted with acid and metal bars in his building entrance on May 12, 2000, Domnikov lingered in coma before dying July 16; perpetrators were convicted in 2013, but motives linked to his exposs remained unprosecuted at the highest levels. Anna Politkovskaya advanced Chechnya coverage, documenting systematic abuses like filtration camps and abductions during the Second Chechen War's counterinsurgency phase, often interviewing victims and defectors. Her on-site efforts during the 2002 Dubrovka theater siege and 2004 Beslan school hostage crises highlighted excessive force, including lethal gas deployment causing civilian deaths beyond militants. Politkovskaya's October 7, 2006, assassination in her elevator, executed with professional precision, was tied by investigators to her regional critiques, though trials convicted only low-level actors.
These efforts, yielding outlets like the 2009 International Press Institute award for persistent in-depth work, came at high cost: six staff or contributors murdered from 2000 to 2009, with systemic impunity shielding principals. Novaya Gazeta's persistence influenced public discourse on accountability gaps, though official responses prioritized narrative control over substantive reform.

2010s: Digital Expansion and Cyber Threats

During the 2010s, Novaya Gazeta intensified its online operations to reach broader audiences amid tightening restrictions on print media, developing its website into a primary platform for real-time reporting and investigations. The outlet began leveraging social media, including a Facebook page that amassed over 25,000 followers by 2011, to disseminate content and engage readers directly. By mid-decade, it incorporated data journalism techniques, such as open data analysis for exposs on corruption and human rights abuses, marking a shift toward digital-native investigative methods.
This digital pivot, however, exposed the newspaper to heightened cyber vulnerabilities, with multiple distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attacks disrupting its website. In late January 2010, a sustained DDoS assault lasted a week, rendering the site inaccessible and coinciding with coverage of political unrest in Moscow. A separate hacker intrusion in February 2010 froze operations, attributed by staff to retaliation for critical articles. Further DDoS incidents struck in December 2010, targeting human rights-focused content, and April 2011, when the site was blocked for over a day amid reports on opposition activities.
Russian President Dmitry Medvedev publicly condemned the 2011 attack, calling it an assault on free speech, though investigations yielded no perpetrators and cybersecurity experts linked such incidents to coordinated efforts against independent outlets. These disruptions forced temporary reliance on mirror sites and social channels, underscoring the fragility of digital infrastructure for outlets challenging official narratives, yet Novaya Gazeta persisted in online publishing without conceding to censorship demands.

2020s: Suspension, Exile, and Fragmentation

In March 2022, following Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine, Novaya Gazeta faced intensified pressure from authorities enforcing new censorship laws that criminalized descriptions of the conflict as a "war" or reports deemed to discredit the military. On March 28, the newspaper's editorial board announced an indefinite suspension of operations within Russia after receiving a second warning from Roskomnadzor for allegedly failing to label content from "foreign agents," a designation increasingly applied to independent media and critics. Editor-in-chief Dmitry Muratov stated that the outlet could no longer function under these constraints, as publishing truthful coverage would violate laws carrying up to 15-year prison sentences, prompting the pause until the end of what Russian officials termed the "special military operation."
Subsequent legal actions formalized the outlet's domestic curtailment. In July 2022, a Moscow court ordered the revocation of one of its media licenses, citing non-compliance with registration changes amid the suspension. By September, Roskomnadzor suspended its print license for Novaya Rasskaz-Gazeta, a related publication, on grounds of failing to issue content for over a year, and Russia's Supreme Court terminated its online edition, effectively shutting down operations inside the country. Muratov described these moves as politically motivated without legal basis, reflecting broader crackdowns on remaining independent voices.
Amid the shutdown, a faction of journalists relocated abroad, launching Novaya Gazeta Europe in Riga, Latvia, in April 2022 to circumvent censorship; its inaugural print issue appeared on May 6, with digital publication resuming immediately. Operating from exile, the outlet continued investigative reporting on Russian affairs, including war-related abuses, while facing blocks in Russia and designation as an "undesirable organization" in June 2023, prohibiting any interaction with it domestically. Kirill Martynov, previously the political editor, assumed leadership, and by 2024, the group expanded with a Paris office to sustain operations.
This exile marked a fragmentation of the original entity: while Novaya Gazeta's Russian remnants, including Muratov, navigated limited, censored activitiessuch as occasional contributions under pseudonyms or affiliated projectsthe European edition pursued uncensored journalism, leading to internal divisions over strategy and risk. Russian authorities escalated targeting, fining Muratov as a "foreign agent" in 2023 and initiating criminal cases against Europe staff, like editor Martynov in September 2024 for operating an undesirable outlet. As of 2025, Novaya Gazeta Europe persists from Latvia and other EU bases, publishing reports on Russian policy and conflict impacts, though its separation from the domestic brand underscores the irreparable split enforced by state repression.

Editorial Stance and Operations

Investigative Journalism Model

Novaya Gazeta's investigative journalism model centers on original, in-depth reporting that prioritizes uncovering concealed facts through persistent fieldwork, insider sourcing, and rigorous verification, distinguishing it from commentary-driven outlets in Russia's media landscape. Under editor Dmitry Muratov, the newspaper emphasized "real journalism" involving direct exposure of corruption, human rights abuses, and state misconduct, often requiring journalists to operate in high-risk environments like conflict zones. This approach relied on building networks of confidential informants within power structures, analyzing leaked documents, and conducting on-site interviews, with publications typically structured as long-form articles that present evidence chronologically to demonstrate causal links between events and perpetrators.
Verification processes at Novaya Gazeta involved cross-checking claims across multiple independent sources, prioritizing documentary evidence such as official records or recordings over unconfirmed testimony, while acknowledging the necessity of anonymous sourcing in repressive contexts where named witnesses faced retaliation. Journalists like Anna Politkovskaya exemplified this by embedding in Chechnya to document atrocities firsthand, combining eyewitness accounts with forensic details to challenge official narratives. The model incorporated open-source intelligence (OSINT) techniques and data analysis when available, as explored in interviews with Novaya staff, though traditional methods like physical document trails predominated due to limited digital transparency in Russia. Muratov advocated for this persistence, urging reporters to "fight for a better world" by methodically dismantling falsehoods, even as the outlet paused publications temporarilysuch as in March 2022 amid Ukraine coverage threatsto protect staff. 
The model's efficacy stemmed from a lean editorial structure, with a core team of fewer than 100 staff handling both reporting and verification, fostering accountability but amplifying personal riskssix Novaya journalists were killed between 2000 and 2021 due to their exposs. This hands-on ethos contrasted with state media's reliance on scripted narratives, enabling breakthroughs like corruption revelations that prompted official inquiries, though systemic biases in Russian institutions often limited follow-through. Novaya's approach demanded ethical discipline, including impartial fact-presentation without editorializing, to maintain credibility amid accusations of opposition slant from pro-government critics. In exile operations post-2022, adaptations included encrypted communications and international collaborations for data access, preserving core verification standards.

Political Orientation and Claims of Independence

Novaya Gazeta has consistently positioned itself as critical of the Russian government, particularly during Vladimir Putin's tenure, through investigative reporting on corruption, human rights violations, and state policies such as the Chechen wars and the invasion of Ukraine. Its editorial stance aligns with liberal opposition views, emphasizing democratic accountability and opposition to authoritarian consolidation, as evidenced by coverage that challenges official narratives on political repression and elite misconduct. This orientation has led to its labeling as "critical of the government" by media monitoring outlets, reflecting a departure from state-aligned media's pro-Kremlin narratives.
The newspaper claims editorial independence, asserting autonomy from political affiliations and state influence, which it maintains through a model reliant on reader subscriptions, private contributions, and refusal of government funding. This stance was underscored by editor Dmitry Muratov's 2021 Nobel Peace Prize, awarded for efforts to safeguard freedom of expression in Russia amid increasing repression. Novaya Gazeta's persistence in publishing critical content, even after self-suspending operations in March 2022 due to war-related threats and later relocating as Novaya Gazeta Europe, bolsters its assertions of independence from Kremlin control.
Critics from pro-government perspectives have contested these claims, portraying Novaya Gazeta as ideologically biased toward Western liberalism and opposition figures, with Russian authorities designating it a "foreign agent" in 2022 and revoking its license, actions the outlet attributes to its defiance of state censorship rather than any lack of independence. Independent assessments, however, affirm its commitment to fact-based journalism, noting that its investigations often rely on primary sources and have prompted official inquiries, distinguishing it from propagandistic outlets. While systemic pressures in Russia's media landscapewhere state control over major broadcasters fosters uniformityundermine neutrality claims for aligned media, Novaya Gazeta's adversarial role against power structures supports its independence in a context of curtailed press freedoms.

Funding, Ownership, and Structural Biases

Novaya Gazeta was founded in 1993 with initial capital derived from a donation by Mikhail Gorbachev from his 1990 Nobel Peace Prize winnings, enabling the establishment of an independent print and later digital outlet free from direct state control. Ownership has been structured through a closed joint-stock company serving as the primary shareholder of its autonomous non-commercial entity, registered in Moscow in 2003, with no majority state involvement that characterizes much of Russia's media landscape.
Significant financial backing came from Russian billionaire Alexander Lebedev, who invested approximately $3.6 million by 2010 and held a shareholder stake, subsidizing operations amid low advertising revenues and high costs of investigative work. Lebedev ceased direct funding in 2015, citing unsustainable expenses, though he retained ownership interests, highlighting the outlet's vulnerability to individual benefactor decisions rather than diversified revenue streams. Ongoing pre-2022 funding relied on reader subscriptions, limited advertising, and internal resources, sustaining a model that avoided dependence on government subsidies but constrained scale compared to state-aligned media.
Following the March 2022 suspension of Russian operations due to coverage of the Ukraine conflict, the exiled Novaya Gazeta Europe edition launched in April 2022 without formal investors or bank accounts, depending instead on crowdfunding, private donations via entities like Friends of Novaya Gazeta Europe, and grants from Western organizations including the JX Fund (launched 2022 for exiled journalism) and the Foundation for Democracy and Liberal Values (grant awarded March 2025). This shift has led to recurrent funding crises, with appeals for public support underscoring operational fragility as of September 2025.
The outlet's funding and ownership structure, eschewing state ties in favor of private and post-exile international sources, has fostered editorial independence from Kremlin influence but introduced potential structural biases toward adversarial reporting on Russian authorities, as evidenced by consistent focus on corruption and human rights abuses with limited counter-narratives. Reliance on donors like Lebedev, whose personal political views include criticism of Putin, risked aligning coverage with benefactor priorities, though no direct editorial interference has been documented. In exile, dependence on Western grantsoften from entities promoting liberal valuesprompts scrutiny of subtle incentives for anti-Russian framing, a concern amplified by Russian designations of Novaya Gazeta Europe as "undesirable" in June 2023, which frame such support as foreign meddling rather than neutral aid. Critics from pro-government perspectives argue this model perpetuates a liberal-opposition echo chamber, selectively amplifying state failures while downplaying achievements or alternative viewpoints, though the paper's investigations have frequently been corroborated by independent verifications.

Key Personnel

Founders and Long-Term Editors

Novaya Gazeta was established on April 1, 1993, by approximately 50 journalists who had departed from the state-aligned Komsomolskaya Pravda newspaper amid disagreements over its editorial direction. The founding group included Dmitry Muratov, a former deputy editor at Komsomolskaya Pravda, who co-initiated the project to create an independent publication focused on uncensored reporting. Igor Kozheurov was appointed as the inaugural editor-in-chief, overseeing the newspaper's early operations until 1995.
Dmitry Muratov assumed the role of editor-in-chief in 1995, a position he held continuously until 2017, briefly relinquished to Kozheurov during an internal election, and resumed from 2019 until September 2023. This extended tenure, spanning over two decades in total, positioned Muratov as the defining long-term editorial figure, guiding Novaya Gazeta through periods of intense scrutiny and repression while prioritizing investigative work on corruption and human rights abuses. In 2023, following his designation as a "foreign agent" by Russian authorities, Muratov stepped down to contest the label legally, after which staff elected Sergey Sokolov as editor-in-chief.

Notable Journalists and Their Contributions

Anna Politkovskaya (19582006), a special correspondent for Novaya Gazeta, gained international recognition for her fearless reporting on the Second Chechen War and human rights violations in Russia's North Caucasus region. Her dispatches detailed extrajudicial killings, torture by security forces, and the plight of civilians, often drawing on firsthand accounts from conflict zones despite repeated threats and an attempted poisoning during the 2004 Beslan school siege coverage. Politkovskaya's work challenged official narratives, highlighting systemic abuses under Ramzan Kadyrov's regime and federal oversight failures, which contributed to heightened scrutiny of Chechen policies. She was murdered on October 7, 2006, in the elevator of her Moscow apartment building, an assassination widely attributed to her journalism though the masterminds remain unidentified.
Elena Milashina, a longtime Novaya Gazeta contributor specializing in the North Caucasus, has exposed widespread human rights abuses, including extrajudicial executions and the 2017 purge of gay men in Chechnya, where security forces allegedly detained, tortured, and killed dozens based on leaked documents and survivor testimonies. Her 2020 article on secret Chechen "gay death lists" prompted international outcry and investigations, while ongoing reports on enforced disappearances and political repression under Kadyrov have forced her into exile. Milashina survived a brutal axe attack on July 4, 2023, in Grozny alongside lawyer Alexander Nemov, suffering severe injuries including a fractured skull; Chechen authorities denied involvement, but the assault followed her coverage of local trials.
Roman Anin, who joined Novaya Gazeta in 2006 and led its investigative desk from 2011, uncovered high-level corruption through data-driven exposs, such as a 2010 series revealing inflated military contracts and embezzlement in defense procurement totaling billions of rubles, implicating top officials. His reporting on elite asset concealment, including a 2016 investigation into a state oil executive's $100 million yacht, spurred public debate and legal probes despite censorship pressures. Anin co-founded the independent outlet iStories after Novaya Gazeta's 2022 constraints, continuing probes into war profiteering and sanctions evasion; Russian authorities placed him on a wanted list in June 2024 for his Bucha massacre coverage.

Major Investigations and Impact

Reporting on Chechen Conflicts

Novaya Gazeta's coverage of the Chechen conflicts, spanning the First Chechen War (19941996) and especially the Second Chechen War (19992009), emphasized independent on-the-ground investigations into military operations and human rights violations, contrasting with state-controlled narratives that minimized Russian forces' abuses. The newspaper dispatched correspondents to document atrocities committed by both federal troops and Chechen militants, though its reporting drew particular scrutiny for highlighting systemic impunity in the Russian military, including torture and extrajudicial killings.
Anna Politkovskaya, who joined Novaya Gazeta in 1999 as a special correspondent, became the outlet's most prominent voice on the Second Chechen War, producing dozens of articles based on direct fieldwork in the region over two years. Her dispatches exposed widespread war crimes, such as the indiscriminate bombing of civilians and filtration camp detentions where detainees faced beatings and enforced disappearances, as detailed in her 2001 book A Dirty War: A Russian Reporter in Chechnya. Politkovskaya received the Russian Union of Journalists' Golden Pen Award in 2000 specifically for her Chechnya coverage, underscoring its impact despite official denials of the reported abuses. Her work extended to mediating crises, including an attempt to negotiate during the 2004 Beslan school siege linked to Chechen insurgents, though she criticized both sides' roles in perpetuating the conflict's brutality.
Following the formal end of major hostilities, Novaya Gazeta sustained scrutiny of Chechnya under Ramzan Kadyrov's rule, with journalist Elena Milashina leading investigations into ongoing repression. In March 2021, the newspaper published detainee testimonies alleging extrajudicial executions by Chechen security forces, prompting international calls for probes into these violations. Milashina's reporting on honor killings, torture, and suppression of dissent drew death threats from Chechen authorities, culminating in her severe assault in Grozny on July 4, 2023, where she suffered a brain injury and multiple fractures. This continuity in coverage highlighted a pattern of reprisals against the outlet, as Politkovskaya's 2006 assassinationwidely linked to her Chechnya exposshad already marked journalism on the conflicts as high-risk. In 2023, Novaya Gazeta Europe republished a Memorial Human Rights Centre report tying Chechen war crimes to broader Russian military patterns, reinforcing the newspaper's archival role in documenting impunity.

Exposs on Corruption, Human Rights, and State Power

Novaya Gazeta has conducted numerous investigations exposing systemic corruption within Russian state institutions and oligarchic networks, often revealing embezzlement schemes involving billions of rubles. In March 2023, the outlet detailed a money-laundering operation tied to Gazprom, Russia's state-controlled energy giant, which allegedly facilitated the diversion of funds through offshore entities and was linked to the mysterious death of a key figure in Montenegro's luxury hotel sector. Similar reporting in September 2023 highlighted rampant graft in the military-industrial complex, where Russia's Investigative Committee pursued over 8,700 corruption cases in 2022 alone, a 700-case increase from prior years, underscoring inefficiencies and theft amid wartime procurement. These exposs frequently traced funds to entities connected to President Vladimir Putin's inner circle, such as land theft networks involving bribery around a confidant-owned company.
On human rights abuses, Novaya Gazeta's journalism has spotlighted violations in detention facilities, forced conscription, and treatment of prisoners of war, drawing on witness testimonies and leaked documents. A June 2025 investigation revealed that recently exchanged Russian POWs were being redeployed to front lines in violation of international humanitarian law, based on interviews with affected soldiers and military insiders. Earlier work included probing torture and extrajudicial punishments in North Caucasus holding facilities, where former Chechen police testified to systematic beatings of detainees, contributing to broader documentation of state-sanctioned brutality. The outlet's coverage extended to Indigenous rights suppression, exposing government isolation tactics that marginalized ethnic minorities' land claims and cultural protections under the guise of national security priorities.
Regarding abuses of state power, Novaya Gazeta has dismantled narratives of institutional integrity by uncovering asset seizures exceeding 25 billion from private firms since the 2022 Ukraine invasion, framing these as arbitrary expropriations to consolidate control under Kremlin directives. Investigations into bureaucratic purges, such as the dismissal of nearly 100 senior officials by August 2025the highest in a decadeportrayed them as factional infighting rather than anti-corruption reforms, with embezzled defense funds recycled through loyalist networks. Reporting on surveillance and repression mechanisms, including FSB-orchestrated intimidation of critics, relied on archival traces of past regime crimes and real-time analysis of protest crackdowns, attributing these to a centralized power structure that prioritizes loyalty over accountability. Such pieces often prompted official denials but rarely led to prosecutions, illustrating the regime's resilience against independent scrutiny.

Coverage of the Ukraine Conflict and Related Events

Novaya Gazeta's coverage of the Russian invasion of Ukraine, which began on February 24, 2022, initially emphasized opposition to the conflict, with the newspaper referring to it explicitly as a "war" in defiance of official Kremlin terminology mandating the phrase "special military operation." On March 1, 2022, the outlet published "Russia, Explained. 'Nyet Voinye / No to War,'" an article documenting anti-war protests across major Russian cities like Moscow on the day of the invasion announcement and highlighting public dissent against the military actions. This stance aligned with editor Dmitry Muratov's public calls for peace, including his auctioning of the Nobel Prize medal in 2022 to fund Ukrainian humanitarian aid, though the coverage drew immediate regulatory pressure under new laws criminalizing dissemination of "false information" about the Russian armed forces, punishable by up to 15 years in prison.
Faced with repeated warnings from Roskomnadzor, Russia's media regulator, Novaya Gazeta self-censored by removing all Ukraine-related war reporting from its website and social media channels on March 4, 2022, stating it would shift focus to domestic repercussions such as economic downturns, mass emigration, and social impacts within Russia. The decision reflected a calculated effort to preserve operations amid threats of full shutdown, as articulated by Muratov, who noted the outlet's prior history of pauses during conflicts like Chechnya to avoid total suppression. By March 28, 2022, the newspaper announced a complete suspension of activities in Russia "until the end of the war," citing unsustainable censorship constraints that had already forced the deletion of content and risked staff prosecutions.
In the invasion's aftermath, Muratov personally faced reprisals linked to the outlet's prior criticism, including an April 2022 auction attack where red paintsymbolizing bloodwas thrown at him, which he attributed to pro-war nationalists enraged by Novaya Gazeta's anti-invasion reporting. Following the suspension, a successor publication, Novaya Gazeta Europe, operated from exile in Latvia and continued investigative reporting on the conflict, often highlighting Russian military shortcomings and Kremlin propaganda. For example, articles analyzed the Russian army's "abysmal" performance and logistical failures as of October 2025, questioned prospects for Russian victory, and documented a near-doubling of hate speech in pro-Kremlin media since the invasion's start in August 2025 analyses. This exile coverage maintained the outlet's tradition of empirical scrutiny but operated outside Russian jurisdiction, avoiding direct confrontation with domestic censorship while critiquing state narratives on events like Ukrainian counteroffensives and infrastructure attacks. Critics, including some analyses of opposition media, have argued that even such reporting sometimes underemphasizes the full scale of Russian-inflicted civilian casualties in specific strikes, potentially softening accountability for attribution to their "own army."

Repressions and Security Incidents

Attacks, Intimidation, and Journalist Deaths

Novaya Gazeta journalists have endured multiple fatal attacks, with Igor Domnikov, a reporter investigating regional corruption, beaten with an iron bar outside his Moscow home on July 12, 2000, and succumbing to his injuries on August 16, 2000. Perpetrators were convicted in 2003 for the assault, receiving sentences of 10 to 18 years, though questions persisted about who commissioned the hit.
Deputy editor Yuri Shchekochikhin died on July 3, 2003, at age 53, after a sudden onset of severe symptoms including skin lesions and organ failure while probing espionage scandals and official corruption. Colleagues and medical analyses suspected poisoning, possibly with thallium, but Russian authorities classified the death as non-criminal in 2009, citing insufficient evidence of murder.
Anna Politkovskaya, renowned for her Chechnya coverage, was shot four times in the elevator of her Moscow apartment building on October 7, 2006. A Chechen gunman was convicted of the murder in 2014 and sentenced to life, with accomplices receiving prison terms, but the masterminds behind the contract killing remain unidentified, fueling ongoing impunity concerns.
Beyond deaths, physical assaults have targeted staff, including editor Dmitry Muratov, who on April 7, 2022, was doused with red paint symbolizing blood and threatened with death on a train from Moscow to Samara, an incident U.S. intelligence attributed to Russian operatives. Reporter Elena Milashina suffered a savage beating on July 4, 2023, in Grozny, Chechnya, alongside advocate Alexander Nemov; attackers axed her hand and struck her head, leaving threatening notes, amid her reporting on local torture cases, with the investigation later suspended. 
The newspaper's offices faced a chemical attack on March 15, 2021, when an unknown substance was poured at the entrance with a note threatening all reporters: "You are our enemies. We will find and kill you." Journalists have reported persistent intimidation, including death threats and harassment, particularly during Chechnya assignments, as documented by international monitors. These incidents underscore a pattern of violence against the outlet's critical reporting, with limited accountability from Russian authorities.

Legal Actions, Fines, and Media Restrictions

In 2021, prior to Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine, Novaya Gazeta faced administrative fines for violations related to Russia's "foreign agent" legislation. On November 17, Moscow's Basmanny District Court convicted the newspaper and editor-in-chief Dmitry Muratov of "abusing freedom of mass information" under Article 13.15 of the Code of Administrative Offenses, for republishing content from designated foreign agents without proper labeling; the court imposed six fines totaling 120,000 rubles (approximately $1,636) on the outlet and 12,000 rubles (approximately $163) on Muratov. These penalties stemmed from articles citing groups such as Memorial and the Anti-Corruption Foundation, which Russian authorities had labeled foreign agents, requiring disclosure in any reference.
Following the March 2022 adoption of laws criminalizing the dissemination of "fake news" about the Russian military or "discrediting" its actionspunishable by up to 15 years in prisonNovaya Gazeta suspended domestic publication on March 28, announcing it could no longer operate without self-censorship under threat of prosecution. In July 2022, Russia's media regulator Roskomnadzor blocked access to the newspaper's website for allegedly publishing prohibited content on the Ukraine conflict, citing violations of the new wartime statutes. A Moscow district court subsequently fined the outlet between 300,000 and 350,000 rubles for spreading "knowingly false information" about the armed forces, as determined under Part 1 of Article 13.15.
Subsequent restrictions targeted Novaya Gazeta's exiled operations and personnel. In September 2023, a Russian court imposed a substantial fine on the newspaper for "discrediting the Russian army" through critical reporting, part of a broader enforcement wave that also led to Muratov's personal designation as a foreign agent by the Justice Ministry. On February 29, 2024, Moscow's Perovsky District Court fined the editor-in-chief of Novaya Gazeta's continuing editorial team, Kirill Martynov, 30,000 rubles (approximately $329) for an article published in December 2023 deemed to violate military discreditation laws. In April 2025, a Russian court sentenced the editor of Novaya Gazeta Europethe outlet's Riga-based successorin absentia to six years in prison on extremism charges related to anti-war coverage, further illustrating ongoing extraterritorial legal pressures.
These measures, enforced by Russian courts and regulators, effectively curtailed Novaya Gazeta's domestic distribution and online presence, with cumulative fines exceeding several hundred thousand rubles by mid-2023 and contributing to the outlet's operational relocation abroad.

Post-2022 Suspension and Undesirable Organization Status

On March 28, 2022, Novaya Gazeta announced the suspension of its print and online publications in Russia, citing unsustainable threats to its staff following Kremlin warnings against coverage of the ongoing military operation in Ukraine. The decision came after the outlet published detailed reports on civilian casualties and Russian military setbacks, which authorities deemed violations of new censorship laws enacted post-February 24 invasion, punishable by up to 15 years in prison for "spreading false information" about the armed forces. Editor-in-chief Dmitry Muratov stated the pause would last until hostilities ended, emphasizing the outlet's inability to continue without endangering lives amid escalating repressions.
In September 2022, Russia's media oversight agency Roskomnadzor revoked Novaya Gazeta's official media registration, first annulling its print license on September 5 following a court ruling, and then its online license on September 15. These actions, prompted by alleged repeated "fake news" dissemination on the Ukraine conflict, barred the newspaper from legal operations within Russia and blocked access to its archived website content. By late 2022, the outlet's domestic infrastructure had effectively ceased, with remaining staff either emigrating or facing individual prosecutions, such as Muratov's February 2024 fine of 500,000 rubles for "discrediting the military" via social media posts linked to prior reporting.
Exiled journalists relaunched operations as Novaya Gazeta Europe from Riga, Latvia, in April 2022, focusing on investigations into Russian governance and war impacts. On June 28, 2023, Russia's Prosecutor General's Office designated this entity an "undesirable organization," classifying its activities as a threat to constitutional order and national security. The label, under a 2015 law expanded in 2021, prohibits any Russian citizen or entity from funding, distributing, or engaging with the outlet, with violations carrying fines up to 300,000 rubles or up to five years' imprisonment for individuals, and up to 1 million rubles for organizations. As of 2025, Novaya Gazeta Europe continues publishing from abroad, but the designation has isolated it from domestic audiences and collaborators.

Awards and International Recognition

Domestic and Prestigious Global Honors

Novaya Gazeta has garnered several prestigious international honors for its independent reporting and defense of press freedom, though it has not received notable domestic awards from Russian state institutions, consistent with its adversarial relationship with authorities. In 2010, the newspaper was awarded the Freedom of Speech Award as part of the Four Freedoms Awards by the Roosevelt Stichting in Middelburg, Netherlands, recognizing its resilience against threats and commitment to uncensored journalism despite the murders of six of its reporters since 2000.
In 2012, Novaya Gazeta received the Charlemagne Medal for European Media from the city of Aachen, Germany, honoring its journalists' fight for press freedom in Russia amid increasing government pressure and the risks faced by its staff.
The newspaper's editor-in-chief, Dmitry Muratov, was presented with the Golden Pen of Freedom Award in 2016 by the World Association of Newspapers and News Publishers (WAN-IFRA) during the World Press Congress in Fiera di Bologna, Italy, for his leadership in upholding journalistic integrity against censorship and violence. This accolade, established in 1961, highlights outstanding defenses of press freedom globally.

Nobel Peace Prize and Symbolic Legacy

In October 2021, Dmitry Muratov, editor-in-chief of Novaya Gazeta, was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize, shared with journalist Maria Ressa, for his "efforts to safeguard freedom of expression in Russia, a country where journalists who expose corruption, abuse of power, and violations of human rights often pay a high price." The Norwegian Nobel Committee highlighted Novaya Gazeta's role in documenting state abuses, noting that six of its journalists had been killed since 2000 due to their reporting. Muratov dedicated the award to the memory of those slain reporters, including Anna Politkovskaya, emphasizing in his acceptance speech that the prize represented "an argument for the truth" amid escalating threats to independent media.
The award underscored Novaya Gazeta's origins and persistence: founded in 1993 using funds from Mikhail Gorbachev's 1990 Nobel Peace Prize, the outlet has maintained a tradition of investigative journalism critical of Russian authorities, often at personal risk to its staff. Muratov announced he would donate the entire prize sumapproximately 10 million Swedish kronorto support independent journalists facing repression, declaring he would "not keep a single cent" for personal use. This commitment extended to auctioning the Nobel medal itself in June 2022 at a New York benefit, raising $103.5 million for Ukrainian refugee aid in response to Russia's invasion, an act framed by Muratov as "driving a nail into the war's coffin."
Symbolically, the Nobel elevated Novaya Gazeta as an emblem of resilient journalism in authoritarian contexts, inspiring global advocacy for press freedom while exposing the human cost of dissentits journalists' deaths exceeding those of any other Russian outlet. Despite the recognition, the prize did not shield the newspaper from intensified state pressure; it suspended domestic operations in March 2022 after warnings over Ukraine coverage and was later deemed an "undesirable organization" by Russian authorities. Muratov's win and subsequent actions reinforced Novaya Gazeta's legacy as a bulwark against censorship, galvanizing international support for exiled Russian media and underscoring the causal link between unfettered reporting and accountability in power structures resistant to scrutiny.

Criticisms and Controversies

Accusations of Bias, Sensationalism, and Factual Errors

Russian authorities and pro-Kremlin outlets have frequently accused Novaya Gazeta of anti-government bias, portraying its investigative reporting as systematically aligned with opposition narratives and Western interests rather than objective journalism. These claims intensified after the newspaper's coverage of events like the Chechen wars and the 2022 Ukraine conflict, where critics alleged selective framing that exaggerated state abuses while downplaying security threats. Such accusations often emanate from state-aligned sources, which exhibit their own incentives to discredit independent media amid broader crackdowns on dissent.
Specific instances of alleged factual errors include a January 23, 2021, article by Tatyana Yurasova claiming that provocateurs were being trained at a Moscow sanatorium to incite violence during protests against Alexei Navalny's arrest; a magistrates' court ruled this disseminated "knowingly unreliable socially significant information" threatening public order, imposing a 200,000-ruble fine on May 19, 2021, despite the article's removal. Novaya Gazeta defended the piece as based on anonymous sources but complied with the takedown order without admitting fault. Similarly, the Russian Foreign Ministry in October 2023 rebutted a Novaya Gazeta article as "fabricated," citing unverified claims about Russian military actions and the outlet's refusal to correct prior errors in a July 30 piece titled "They Served Murder."
Sensationalism critiques have targeted the newspaper's dramatic style in conflict reporting, such as Anna Politkovskaya's accounts of atrocities in Chechnya and Beslan, which some pro-government voices labeled as exaggerated to provoke outrage. Libel rulings provide further examples of challenged accuracy: in Novaya Gazeta v. Russia (European Court of Human Rights, April 2, 2013), domestic courts upheld damages against the outlet for an unsubstantiated corruption allegation against a regional governor, deeming it a defamatory value judgment lacking sufficient factual basis; the ECHR found no Article 10 violation. While Novaya Gazeta has won some defamation suits, such as against Josef Stalin's grandson in 2009 over historical claims, these losses highlight recurring disputes over evidentiary rigor. Independent verification of these errors remains contested, as opposition-leaning outlets like Novaya Gazeta operate in an environment where state control over information limits counter-evidence.

Government and Pro-Kremlin Perspectives on Disinformation

Russian authorities, through Roskomnadzor, have accused Novaya Gazeta of disseminating "fake news" about the Russian Armed Forces, particularly in coverage of military operations in Ukraine, claiming such reports contain knowingly false information that discredits the military and contradicts official accounts of the "special military operation." In May 2022, Roskomnadzor initiated administrative proceedings against the outlet for these violations, leading to temporary suspensions and requirements to remove content deemed to spread unreliable socially significant information. Pro-Kremlin officials argue that Novaya Gazeta's reporting amplifies Western narratives, portraying Russian actions as aggressive invasions rather than defensive measures, thereby undermining national security and public morale during the conflict.
Further actions included multiple fines and blocks for "discrediting the Russian army," with a September 2023 penalty imposed after the outlet published materials challenging state versions of battlefield events, such as casualty figures and tactical decisions. In July 2022, Roskomnadzor sued to revoke Novaya Gazeta's media license, citing repeated instances of content that allegedly propagated disinformation harmful to military efforts, resulting in a court ruling in September 2022 stripping the outlet's ability to operate domestically. State Duma member Andrey Lugovoy, in April 2024, publicly demanded investigations into Novaya Gazeta European exile-based continuationfor engaging in "anti-Russian propaganda," framing its journalism as a deliberate effort to distort facts and incite opposition to government policies.
The designation of editor Dmitry Muratov as a "foreign agent" in September 2023 by the Justice Ministry reinforced these views, with officials asserting that Novaya Gazeta's funding ties and editorial stance indicate foreign influence aimed at subverting Russian sovereignty through fabricated stories on human rights abuses and military misconduct. Pro-Kremlin perspectives portray the outlet as part of a broader ecosystem of "undesirable" media that prioritizes sensationalism over verified information, contributing to societal division by echoing unconfirmed claims from Ukrainian or NATO sources without balancing them against Russian evidence. These accusations align with post-2022 legislation criminalizing dissemination of "false information" about the armed forces, which authorities defend as essential countermeasures against hybrid warfare involving informational sabotage.

Internal Divisions and Ethical Debates

Novaya Gazeta has experienced notable internal tensions over journalistic practices and ethical boundaries, particularly regarding the tone and focus of investigative reporting. Staff members, including poet Dmitry Bykov, criticized reporter Anna Politkovskaya's style as overly personal and hysterical, resulting in limited interaction with her in her later years. Similarly, investigative journalist Roman Anin voiced objections to Politkovskaya's critiques of the Russian military, sparking heated arguments in the newsroom cafeteria with editor-in-chief Dmitry Muratov and leading to extended periods of strained relations. These disputes highlighted broader debates on balancing emotional advocacy with objective analysis in coverage of conflicts like Chechnya.
Ethical dilemmas have also arisen concerning the scope of investigations and self-imposed limits to mitigate risks. Muratov consistently opposed delving into the personal lives of political figures, such as Vladimir Putin's family, arguing it crossed into unnecessary intrusion; this stance prompted Anin's departure in 2016 to co-found the independent outlet iStories, where such topics could be pursued without restraint. In response to internal advocacy, particularly from younger staff, Novaya Gazeta adopted an ethics code in recent years to address workplace harassment and establish a five-member ethics council, which curbed some of Muratov's historically informal management style described by critics as abrasive.
Self-censorship debates intensified around high-risk topics, exemplified by a 2009 decision to suspend Chechnya reporting for a year to safeguard a journalist's life, a move Muratov later reflected on as potentially compromising core principles. Similar hesitations delayed projects like an LGBTQ+ podcast due to fears of triggering "foreign agent" designations under Russian law.
The Russian invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 exacerbated divisions, leading to a strategic split among staff. While Muratov opted to suspend operations within Russia after official warnings to avoid criminal prosecutions under wartime censorship laws, a portion of the team relocated abroad and relaunched as Novaya Gazeta Europe from bases in Latvia and other European cities, continuing uncensored coverage without direct affiliation to the original entity. This divergence reflected ethical tensions over preserving a domestic presence for potential post-censorship revival versus immediate, uncompromised opposition from exile, with Muratov prioritizing staff safety and long-term viability in Russia. Internal democracy was also contested, as seen in a failed 2019 leadership challenge against Muratov by reporter Ilya Azar, who likened the paper's decision-making to authoritarianism.
