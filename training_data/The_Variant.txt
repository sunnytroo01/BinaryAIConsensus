 "The Variant" is the second episode of the first season of the Disney+ series Loki, a Marvel Studios production based on the Marvel Comics character of the same name. Premiering on June 16, 2021, the episode follows the God of Mischief after the events of Avengers: Endgame, as he navigates the bureaucratic Time Variance Authority (TVA) and confronts the concept of multiversal variants. Directed by Kate Herron and written by Elissa Karasik, it advances the series' exploration of time travel, identity, and the Sacred Timeline while blending procedural elements with high-stakes action.
In the episode, Loki (Tom Hiddleston) partners with TVA agent Mobius (Owen Wilson) to track an elusive Variant responsible for timeline incursions, investigating attacks by the Variant, including an ambush at a Renaissance Festival in Oshkosh, Wisconsin, in 1985, and hiding in apocalypses such as the eruption of Pompeii in 79 AD and a hurricane disaster at a superstore in Alabama in 2050. The narrative delves into Loki's psyche, revealing his manipulative tendencies and forcing him to question his place in the multiverse, while introducing key TVA figures like Judge Ravonna Renslayer (Gugu Mbatha-Raw) and Hunter B-15 (Wunmi Mosaku). The episode also features the voice of Tara Strong as the animated mascot Miss Minutes, adding a layer of quirky world-building to the TVA's operations.
"The Variant" received widespread critical acclaim for its inventive storytelling, character development, and expansion of the Marvel Cinematic Universe's lore, particularly its introduction of gender-bent variants and multiverse threats. On Rotten Tomatoes, it holds a 95% approval rating based on 39 reviews, with critics praising the chemistry between Hiddleston and Wilson, as well as the episode's balance of humor, mystery, and spectacle. The installment solidified Loki's reputation as a pivotal series in Phase Four of the MCU, setting up broader narrative arcs involving time manipulation and alternate realities.

Synopsis and Analysis

Plot

In the episode, Mobius interrogates Loki in the TVA library, using a TemPad projector to display the unaltered Sacred Timeline, which reveals Loki's destined role in the Avengers' history and his eventual redemption. Loki, unsettled by this glimpse of his future, agrees to assist in capturing the variant responsible for the TVA attack in the series premiere.
Mobius and Loki review footage of the variant's previous actions, including a more recent event in 2050 at a Roxxcart superstore in Haven Hills, Alabama, amid a catastrophic hurricane apocalypse; there, the variant detonated a stolen reset charge, pruning the responding Minutemen and escaping through a timedoor. These investigations highlight the variant's pattern of targeting TVA personnel during end-of-the-world scenarios to avoid detection.
Suspecting the variant's next move will occur at another apocalypse, the team identifies 1985 in Oshkosh, Wisconsin, during a Renaissance fair as the likely site, as the fair's temporal significance aligns with the pattern. Loki and Mobius arrive via timedoor and disguise themselves as fair performers to blend in, with Loki donning period attire and attempting minor illusions that fail due to the TVA's nullifying effects. They locate the variant, disguised as a puppeteer performing for children with marionettes, and signal the arriving TVA Minutemen led by Hunter B-15 to surround the area.
As the Minutemen close in, the variant activates her green enchantment magic, compelling the fair attendees to turn on the TVA agents in a chaotic assault. A fierce chase ensues through the fairgrounds, with the variant wielding dual daggers in combat against the agents, using her sorcery to evade captures and pruning several Minutemen with her retrieved device. During the skirmish, Loki confronts the variant directly, but she overpowers and enchants Mobius with her magic, forcing him to attack Loki briefly before she reveals her identity as Sylvie Laufeydottir, another version of Loki. Sylvie then activates her stolen TemPad to open a timedoor and escapes with the enchanted Mobius, leaving Loki and the surviving agents behind as the episode ends on this cliffhanger.

Themes

The episode "The Variant" delves into themes of identity and duality through the introduction of Sylvie, a female incarnation of Loki, which underscores the concept of alternate selves and gender fluidity rooted in the character's mythological origins. Director Kate Herron emphasized the importance of acknowledging Loki's gender fluidity, noting that "in the comics, the mythology, yeah, he is gender fluid," a trait reflected in Sylvie's presentation as a distinct yet connected variant. This reveal prompts Loki to confront his own villainous archetype, as Mobius compiles footage of Loki's predetermined life eventssuch as his role in the Avengers' conflictsmirroring Rashomon-style perspectives on his past actions and forcing introspection on what defines him. Herron framed the series' core inquiry as "Who are you?", bookending Loki's journey with this question to highlight ongoing struggles with self-perception amid multiversal variations.
Central to the narrative is a critique of authority and free will, embodied in the Time Variance Authority's (TVA) rigid enforcement of the Sacred Timeline against Sylvie's rebellion. Head writer Michael Waldron described the TVA's mechanism as monitoring deviations from a baseline path, where branches risk creating threats like unchecked variants, thereby questioning predestination versus agency. Moments like Mobius presenting unaltered timeline footage to Lokidepicting his inevitable betrayals and redemptionsdirectly challenge the character's sense of autonomy, illustrating how TVA oversight renders personal choices illusory. Herron connected this to broader explorations of free will, as seen in later TVA figures like Renslayer pursuing it beyond institutional control, reinforcing the episode's tension between enforced order and defiant individualism.
Themes of isolation and deception are prominently explored through Sylvie's protracted solo crusade against the TVA, symbolizing the loneliness inherent in rebellion. Herron portrayed Sylvie as a fugitive who has "dyed her hair" and adopted the name to evade capture, highlighting her use of enchantment magic for manipulation and survival over centuries. This contrasts sharply with Loki's coerced partnership with Mobius, which tests themes of trust amid deception, as Loki grapples with potential betrayal while Sylvie operates in perpetual solitude. The Renaissance fair setting, where agents pursue leads on the variant, evokes performative identities through its costumed revelry, paralleling Loki's shape-shifting heritage in Norse lore and Sylvie's adaptive disguises. Brief narrative triggers, such as the thwarted pursuits in 1985 Oshkosh and 2050 Alabama, amplify these motifs by underscoring the variant's elusive, self-reliant evasion tactics.

Production

Development

The development of "The Variant," the second episode of the Loki series, originated in the initial conceptualization phase led by head writer Michael Waldron, who began outlining key elements in 2019 to introduce the show's primary antagonist variant and expand on the multiverse theme central to the season's narrative. Waldron, drawing from Marvel Comics interpretations of Loki and multiverse concepts, envisioned variants as a means to explore Loki's fractured identity and the broader implications of branching timelines, positioning this episode as a pivotal setup for the series' procedural structure.
Kate Herron was attached as director in January 2020, chosen for her distinctive approach that merged time-travel procedural mechanics with intimate character drama, which lent the episode its investigative tone focused on unraveling the variant's motives. Herron's background in shorts like "Rest Stop," which incorporated time manipulation themes, aligned with Marvel's goal of balancing high-stakes action with emotional depth in the TVA's bureaucratic world.
A core decision in integrating the episode with the overall series arc was to introduce Sylvie as the variant, setting up her backstory and lifelong vendetta against the TVA for later exploration, foreshadowing revelations about the organization's enigmatic origins. Waldron crafted Sylvie as an original variant inspired by comic iterations like Sylvie Lushton and Lady Loki, using her history to humanize the multiverse's chaos and drive long-term threads like the TVA's foundational mysteries.
Pre-production planning intensified from August to September 2020, after a COVID-19-induced production halt earlier that year, with teams storyboarding the episode's time-jump sequencessuch as the variant's disruptions across historical catastrophesand the climactic reveal to ensure seamless visual continuity with the series' timeline-hopping aesthetic. This phase refined the episode's role in escalating the hunt for the variant while deepening Loki's alliance with Mobius.

Writing

The episode "The Variant" was written by Elissa Karasik, marking her first credited script in the Marvel Cinematic Universe as a staff writer on the series. Head writer Michael Waldron contributed to the development of the variant lore, building on his original pitch of Loki hunting a variant of himself to explore multiversal threats and timeline deviations.
Key elements of the script emphasize character-driven dialogue, particularly the banter between Loki and Mobius that probes Loki's self-perception and the illusion of free will. Notable lines include Mobius' assertion, "The universe wants to be fixed. It wants its branches trimmed," which encapsulates the TVA's bureaucratic enforcement of the Sacred Timeline and challenges Loki's chaotic worldview. The script also features a pivotal projection sequence in the TVA's Time Theater, visually expositing Loki's backstory through holographic recreations of his past actions to foster reluctant collaboration with Mobius.
Structured across three acts to fit the episode's approximately 54-minute runtime, the narrative balances investigative exposition within the TVA, an action-oriented pursuit of the Variant in 1985 Oshkosh, Wisconsin, and a climactic confrontation that reveals the antagonist's identity, prioritizing character development amid escalating tension.

Casting

Tom Hiddleston reprised his role as Loki from the Marvel Cinematic Universe films for the series, with the project officially announced at San Diego Comic-Con on July 20, 2019. Owen Wilson was cast as Mobius M. Mobius on January 31, 2020, bringing a grounded contrast to Hiddleston's portrayal of the trickster god. Wunmi Mosaku was cast as Hunter B-15 in August 2019. Gugu Mbatha-Raw joined as Ravonna Renslayer in a series regular role announced on February 11, 2020.
Sophia Di Martino was cast in a key series regular role on November 15, 2019, which was publicly described at the time without specifying her character as the Sylvie variant of Loki, maintaining secrecy around the gender-swapped interpretation until the episode's reveal. Di Martino underwent extensive stunt training starting in January 2020 to prepare for Sylvie's physical demands, including sword drills and brawling choreography that emphasized a street-fighter style distinct from Loki's more elegant combat. This preparation was crucial for scenes in "The Variant," where Sylvie's agility and combat prowess drive the episode's action sequences at the Renaissance fair.
Eugene Cordero portrayed Casey, the TVA clerk who aids Mobius and Loki in tracking the variant, as part of the series' supporting ensemble. For the episode's Renaissance fair setting, minor roles such as shoppers and performers were filled by local extras cast in Atlanta, with filming taking place at the Georgia Renaissance Festival grounds to capture the period ambiance.
Principal actors, including Hiddleston and Wilson, participated in chemistry reads and rehearsals to develop the Loki-Mobius dynamic, focusing on interrogation and alliance-building interactions central to "The Variant." These sessions helped establish the rapport evident in the episode's TVA headquarters scenes, where Mobius recruits Loki to hunt the variant.

Filming

Principal photography for the season, including scenes for "The Variant," the second episode of the Loki series, was conducted as part of the season's block shooting schedule, occurring from September to December 2020 primarily at Pinewood Atlanta Studios in Fayetteville, Georgia, and various sites in the Atlanta metropolitan area. This approach allowed for efficient production of multiple episodes simultaneously after the COVID-19-related shutdown earlier in the year.
The Time Variance Authority (TVA) interiors were constructed on soundstages at Pinewood Atlanta Studios to create the bureaucratic, timeless environments central to the episode's narrative. In contrast, the Renaissance fair sequences, set in 1985 Oshkosh, Wisconsin, were shot on a constructed set at the Georgia Renaissance Festival grounds in Fairburn, Georgia, where actors wore period-appropriate costumes including corsets, tunics, and faux medieval attire to evoke the era's reenactment culture.
Filming faced significant logistical challenges due to ongoing COVID-19 protocols, which restricted crew sizes, mandated frequent testing, and enforced social distancing on set to ensure safety during the pandemic. Additionally, night shoots for the episode's climactic fair confrontation incorporated rain machines to produce atmospheric effects, enhancing the tension of the scene amid the controlled outdoor environment. The Variant reveal sequence, featuring the dramatic unmasking and ensuing action, was captured over five days, relying on practical stunts for the dagger fights, coordinated by Second Unit Director Chris O'Hara to maintain authenticity in the physical choreography.

Visual effects

The visual effects for "The Variant" were overseen by Marvel Studios, with Industrial Light & Magic (ILM) contributing over 400 shots across the first season, including key elements in episode 2 that highlighted the Time Variance Authority's (TVA) retro-futuristic bureaucratic aesthetic and dynamic time portals. ILM's work integrated seamlessly with on-set practical elements from filming, such as TVA interiors, to add digital overlays for enhanced depth and otherworldly scale.
Several pivotal sequences relied on advanced digital techniques to bring the episode's time-travel and action to life. FuseFX handled the digital creation of the TemPad's holographic timeline projections in the TVA's time theater and library briefing areas, employing 3D particle systems inspired by natural phenomena like bird murmurations to simulate fluid, branching timelines. Sylvie's enchantment effects were rendered with glowing green magical auras, using compositing and dynamic simulations to convey ethereal energy during confrontations with TVA agents. Pruning disintegrations, a core TVA enforcement tool, utilized particle simulations for realistic energy propagation, disintegration, and lingering residual wakes, drawing from research into nebulae and interstellar effects.
Historical recreations demanded meticulous CGI integration to depict variance events. For the Pompeii destruction flashback in 79 AD, digital artists blended new renders of volcanic eruptions and ash clouds with period-accurate environments to illustrate the chaos amid the variant's escape. Similarly, the 2050 Alabama apocalypse sequence incorporated CGI enhancements to archival-style footage, combining simulated catastrophic weather and structural collapses to underscore the timeline deviation.
The Renaissance fair chase sequence allocated significant resources to VFX innovation, with motion capture data from Sylvie's acrobatic performances driving digital extensions for enhanced agility and portal interactions amid the period setting.

Music

The original score for "The Variant" was composed by Natalie Holt, who crafted a hybrid soundscape blending orchestral elements, vintage analog synthesizers, and Scandinavian folk instruments to evoke the episode's multiversal chaos and mythological roots. Orchestral swells and ticking clock samples build tension during Time Variance Authority (TVA) sequences, underscoring the bureaucratic oppression and temporal stakes, while folk motifs incorporating instruments like the Hardanger fiddle provide an earthy, playful contrast for the 1985 Renaissance fair setting. Custom sound design, including theremin glissandos for eerie dissonance, accompanies key effects such as the pruning of timelines and enchantment disguises, heightening the surrealism of variant pursuits.
The score was recorded remotely during the COVID-19 pandemic, with Holt composing in her London studio and collaborating with musicians worldwide, including the Budapest Scoring Orchestra for symphonic passages and a 32-voice choir in later cues. This approach allowed for innovative layering, such as Moog synthesizer pulses evoking dystopian unease in Loki's interrogations.
A notable licensed track, "Holding Out for a Hero" by Bonnie Tyler, plays during the climactic confrontation at the Renaissance fair, where the variant ambushes TVA agents, its anthemic energy amplifying the scene's ironic heroism. The music integrates thematically to highlight duality: dissonant strings and theremin underscore Loki's personal revelations and moral ambiguity, contrasting the upbeat, period-appropriate folk tunes of the fair to emphasize the episode's blend of whimsy and dread.

Promotion and Release

Marketing

The marketing campaign for "The Variant," the second episode of the Loki series, was integrated into the broader promotional strategy for the show, which emphasized the multiverse's mysteries to heighten viewer engagement ahead of its June 16, 2021, premiere on Disney+.
A key teaser appeared in the official season trailer released on April 5, 2021, featuring a cryptic silhouette of the elusive Variant to build suspense without revealing its identity, aligning with Marvel's approach to teasing timeline disruptions. Marvel amplified hype through social media on platforms like Twitter and Instagram, encouraging fan speculation and theories about the Variant's role in the TVA's pursuit.
Cross-promotional activities featured interviews in Entertainment Weekly during June 2021, subtly hinting at narrative twists involving the Variant without disclosing details, focusing instead on the episode's themes of deception and identity. Post-release, merchandise tie-ins were announced, including variant-themed Funko Pop figures of Sylvie, revealed as the episode's central antagonist, to capitalize on the mystery's resolution and extend fan interest.
The overall campaign centered on the episode's mystery element, strategically withholding the Sylvie reveal until airing to sustain suspense and boost Disney+ subscriptions by positioning the Variant as a pivotal enigma in the series' exploration of alternate timelines.

Broadcast

"The Variant" premiered exclusively on Disney+ on June 16, 2021, in the United States, with simultaneous availability in over 60 international markets where the service operated, including regions across Europe, Asia, and Latin America. The episode's release followed a weekly schedule for the Loki series, building on promotional teasers that heightened anticipation for the storyline's progression.
Running 54 minutes including credits, the episode is formatted as a standard television installment and carries a TV-14 rating due to its depiction of fantasy violence. Accessibility features, such as closed captions and audio descriptions, were integrated from launch to support diverse audiences.
Internationally, the episode aired on the same day in European and Asian territories via Disney+, ensuring global synchronization for subscribers. Dubbed and subtitled versions in multiple languages, including Spanish, French, and Hindi, were rolled out within 24 hours to accommodate non-English speakers. Unlike traditional broadcasts, there was no theatrical release or airing on linear television networks.

Home media

"The Variant," the second episode of the first season of the Marvel Studios series Loki, became available for streaming on Disney+ as part of the complete first season upon its premiere on June 16, 2021. It has remained accessible on the platform's standard subscription tier since then. Additionally, the episode is included in digital purchase options for Loki Season 1 on platforms such as Apple TV (via iTunes), Google Play, and Amazon Prime Video, with availability for purchase beginning in July 2021.
On physical media, "The Variant" is featured as part of Loki: The Complete First Season Blu-ray and DVD set, released by Marvel Studios on September 26, 2023. The set includes the full season's episodes along with bonus features such as deleted scenes, a gag reel, and featurettes on the production, including the design of the Time Variance Authority.
Special editions of the physical release encompass a 4K UHD Collector's Edition SteelBook, also launched on September 26, 2023, bundling the season in high-definition format with exclusive artwork. There is no standalone disc for individual episodes like "The Variant."
As of November 2025, no additional home media releases for Loki Season 1 have been announced, though the content, including "The Variant," has been fully integrated into Disney+'s ad-supported subscription tier since its introduction in December 2022.

Reception

Critical response

"The Variant" received positive reviews from critics, earning a 95% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 39 reviews, with an average score of 7.8/10. The episode's critics consensus highlighted its strong character work and the intriguing introduction of a new player, despite some table-setting after the premiere's exposition. On Metacritic, the first season of Loki holds a score of 74 out of 100, indicating generally favorable reviews.
Critics widely praised the episode's storytelling for the Sylvie reveal twist at the climax, which introduced Sophia Di Martino's character as a female Loki variant and escalated the narrative intrigue. IGN noted the twist as a "game-changing" moment that deepened the series' exploration of identity and mischief, awarding the episode a 7/10. Performances were a highlight, particularly the chemistry between Tom Hiddleston's Loki and Owen Wilson's Mobius, described by Den of Geek as forming "the best new MCU duo" through witty banter and emotional depth. Variety commended the episode's expansion of the TVA's world-building, emphasizing its bureaucratic absurdity and innovative time-travel mechanics as adding layers to the MCU's multiverse.
The action choreography in the Roxxcart sequence also drew acclaim for its tense, body-hopping confrontation between Loki and the Variant, blending physical combat with magical elements effectively. Collider praised Di Martino's debut as "fierce and charismatic," noting her portrayal brought a fresh, vengeful energy to the Variant role.
Some reviewers critiqued the pacing as occasionally slow during expository scenes, such as the library interrogation, which felt drawn out amid the episode's investigative setup. The Hollywood Reporter pointed to minor inconsistencies in the variant timeline logic, suggesting the rules of nexus events created small plot holes in the TVA's pursuit mechanics.
Overall, Collider graded the episode an A-, calling it "a thrilling escalation" that balanced humor, action, and MCU lore expansion.

Audience viewership

The second episode of Loki, titled "The Variant," contributed to the series' strong early viewership on Disney+. According to Nielsen, the first episode garnered 731 million minutes viewed in the U.S. during the week of June 713, 2021. In the following week of June 1420, which included the release of "The Variant," Loki episodes accumulated 886 million minutes viewed.
Samba TV reported that the series premiere reached 2.5 million U.S. households over the five days following its release on June 9, 2021. Globally, Loki quickly became the most in-demand original series across all platforms, topping charts faster than previous Marvel Disney+ series like WandaVision and The Falcon and the Winter Soldier.
