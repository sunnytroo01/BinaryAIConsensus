The Vapors
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Vapors are an English new wave and power pop band formed in Guildford, Surrey, in 1978. Best known for their international hit single "Turning Japanese", which peaked at number 3 on the UK Singles Chart and number 36 on the US Billboard Hot 100 in 1980, the band blended catchy melodies with post-punk energy during the late 1970s and early 1980s music scene.
The band's original lineup featured vocalist and guitarist David Fenton, lead guitarist Edward Bazalgette, bassist Steve Smith, and drummer Howard Smith. Discovered by The Jam's bassist Bruce Foxton while performing locally, they signed with Virgin Records and released their debut album, New Clear Days, in 1980, which reached number 44 on the UK Albums Chart. Followed by the 1981 album Magnets, their early career included tours across the UK, US, and Australia, but poor sales led to their disbandment in 1982.
After a 34-year hiatus, The Vapors reformed in 2016 with original members David Fenton, Edward Bazalgette, and Steve Smith, joined by drummer Michael Bowes. Bazalgette later departed and was replaced by Danny Fenton on guitar; Bowes was replaced by Mike Kelly in 2025 due to health issues. The reunion saw them release new material, including the 2020 album Together (peaking at number 87 on the UK Albums Chart) and the 2025 album Wasp in a Jar, as well as singles like "Nothing Can Stop Us Now" in November 2025. They continue to tour, with scheduled performances in 2026, maintaining a cult following for their sharp, satirical songwriting and enduring pop hooks.

History

Formation and early years (19781979)

The Vapors formed in 1978 in Guildford, Surrey, England, with David Fenton on lead vocals and rhythm guitar, Edward Bazalgette on lead guitar, Steve Smith on bass guitar and backing vocals, and Howard Smith on drums. The band, emerging from the local music scene amid the rise of punk and new wave, drew initial inspiration from the energetic pub rock circuit and began rehearsing as university students in the area. Their first performance took place in February 1978 at the Lakers Hotel in Redhill, marking the start of regular appearances at venues across Surrey.
Throughout 1978 and into 1979, The Vapors honed their sound through frequent gigs at local pubs and clubs, playing three or four shows a month at spots like the Coach & Horses in Farnham and the Holly Bush. These early performances showcased their tight power pop style, blending sharp guitar riffs with Fenton's introspective lyrics, and helped build a grassroots following in the region. By early 1979, the band had recorded their first demos, capturing tracks that highlighted their raw energy and melodic hooks. Their breakout came when bassist Bruce Foxton of The Jam spotted them at a Surrey gig in April 1979, leading to co-management by Foxton and John Weller (father of The Jam's Paul Weller), and a signing to United Artists Records later that year.
The band's debut single, "Prisoners," was released in October 1979 on United Artists, produced by Pete Wilson at Ridge Farm Studios in Surrey. The track, featuring Fenton's rhythm guitar and Bazalgette's angular leads over a driving beat, explored themes of entrapment and frustration, reflecting the alienation of suburban existencea recurring motif in Fenton's early songwriting. While it achieved modest airplay, including a BBC Radio 1 session for John Peel in July 1979 where the band performed it alongside other originals, "Prisoners" failed to crack the UK Singles Chart, receiving positive notices for its urgent, mod-inflected sound but limited commercial traction. This release positioned The Vapors on the cusp of wider recognition, setting the stage for their subsequent breakthrough.

Rise to fame (1980)

In June 1980, The Vapors released their debut album New Clear Days through United Artists Records. Produced by Vic Coppersmith-Heaven, the record showcased the band's punchy power pop style blended with mod revival and new wave elements, exploring themes of alienation, youth angst, and social observation across tracks such as "Spring Collection," "Turning Japanese," "Cold War," "America," "Trains," "Bunkers," "News at Ten," "Somehow," "Letter from Hiro," "Waiting for the Weekend," and "Sixty Second Interval." The album received attention for its energetic, guitar-driven sound and peaked at No. 44 on the UK Albums Chart, spending six weeks in the top 100.
The band's breakthrough came with the single "Turning Japanese," released in January 1980, which became their signature hit. Backed by "Disco in Dream," the track reached No. 3 on the UK Singles Chart and No. 36 on the US Billboard Hot 100, marking their highest commercial success. However, the song sparked controversy due to widespread misinterpretation of its lyrics as a euphemism for masturbation, with the title allegedly referencing contorted facial expressions during climax; frontman and songwriter David Fenton firmly denied this, explaining it as a metaphor for heartbreak, loneliness, and cultural alienation, drawing on clichs of emotional turmoil and regret following a breakup.
Later in the year, The Vapors issued the single "News at Ten," which charted at No. 44 in the UK, followed by "Waiting for the Weekend," which reached No. 48. The group supported their rising profile with extensive live performances, including a major UK headline tour starting in Bradford in June 1980 to promote the album, alongside support slots for acts like The Jam. Critics praised their vibrant power pop energy and concise songcraft, with coverage in publications such as NME and Sounds highlighting their role in the burgeoning new wave scene.

Second album and disbandment (19811982)

The Vapors released their second studio album, Magnets, on 9 March 1981, just nine months after their debut. Produced by David Tickle at Ridge Farm Studio in Surrey, the record featured 11 tracks and represented a sonic evolution toward more experimental new wave elements, incorporating denser arrangements and darker lyrical themes compared to the punchy power pop of New Clear Days. Despite this artistic ambition, the album received limited promotion from their new label, Liberty Records, and failed to chart in the UK.
The lead single, "Jimmie Jones"a pointed critique of cult leader Jim Jonesinspired by the 1978 Jonestown massacre, peaked at No. 44 on the UK Singles Chart in July 1981 after six weeks. It was the primary single extracted from Magnets, with no other tracks from the album achieving significant chart success. Critical reception was mixed; while some praised the album's sophisticated post-punk edge and unsettling atmosphere, others found its dense production and departure from the debut's immediate accessibility challenging.
Amid growing frustrations, including creative differences and insufficient label support following the underwhelming commercial performance of Magnets, internal tensions mounted within the band. These pressures, compounded by the demands to replicate their earlier hits, led to a final UK tour in early 1982. The Vapors officially disbanded that spring after issuing two albums and six singles, with frontman David Fenton opting to explore solo endeavors, including a 1983 single "Fresh Air" on Razor Records, alongside brief involvement in other musical projects.

Reformation and later career (2016present)

The Vapors reformed in August 2016 after a 34-year hiatus, announcing a short tour of four dates in the UK and Ireland for October and November of that year. The reunion was spurred by a decade of online fan demand for a revival of their new wave sound amid growing interest in 1980s acts. Initial performances included a warm-up show in Guildford and headline dates at venues like Dingwalls in London, marking their first live appearances since 1982. David Fenton's son, Dan Fenton, joined the band on guitar starting in 2017, contributing to subsequent concerts and helping sustain the group's momentum.
The band expanded their post-reunion activities with additional UK gigs in 2017, including festival appearances that showcased their classic hits to enthusiastic crowds. This led to the release of their third studio album, Together, on May 15, 2020, self-produced by the band and issued through Manmade Soul Ltd and The Vapors Own Records. The album featured 12 tracks blending their signature power pop with contemporary reflections, receiving positive reviews for revitalizing their style.
In 2024, The Vapors launched a crowdfunding campaign to fund their fourth album, enabling fan-supported production and exclusive perks. The resulting Wasp in a Jar was released on February 28, 2025, with singles such as "Forever & Ever" debuting in July 2024 and others like "Hit the Ground Running" following. The album's lyrics explore themes of aging, personal resilience, and social observation, maintaining the band's incisive new wave edge.
Throughout 2025, The Vapors have remained active with live performances, including a slot on the "Lost 80s Live" North American tour on August 24 at the Greek Theatre in Los Angeles, where they blend original hits like "Turning Japanese" with tracks from Wasp in a Jar. Additional UK dates and a new digital single, "Nothing Can Stop Us Now," released on November 8, 2025, underscore their ongoing commitment, with no announced plans for disbandment as of November 2025.

Band members

Original lineup

The original lineup of The Vapors consisted of four members who remained stable throughout the band's initial run from 1978 to 1982, with no personnel changes during that period.
David Fenton served as the lead vocalist and rhythm guitarist, while also acting as the band's primary songwriter. Born on March 4 in Redhill, Surrey, England, Fenton shaped much of the group's material, including key tracks like "Turning Japanese," where his contributions to the song's structure and lyrics were central. After the band's 1982 disbandment, he returned to the legal profession and specialized in music law.
Edward Bazalgette handled lead guitar duties, bringing an engineering background to his role; he later pursued a PhD in engineering and is a great-great-grandson of Sir Joseph Bazalgette, the Victorian engineer known for London's sewer system. His guitar work featured prominently in the band's mod-influenced new wave sound, such as the distinctive "oriental riff" on "Turning Japanese." Bazalgette left the band following its 1982 disbandment and transitioned into a career in television production and directing for the BBC.
Steve Smith played bass guitar and provided backing vocals, delivering melodic bass lines that supported the band's tight rhythm section, as heard in songs like "Trains." Despite sharing a surname with the drummer, Smith was not related to him. He has remained active in the music scene post-1982, including participating in later band activities.
Howard Smith rounded out the lineup on drums, contributing to the energetic, punk-inflected new wave style that defined the band's live and recorded performances during their original tenure. Following the 1982 split, he worked in music copyright for the BBC before opening his own record shop in Guildford.

Reunion lineup

The initial reunion lineup of The Vapors, re-established in 2016, consisted of original members David Fenton (lead vocals and guitar), Edward Bazalgette (lead guitar), and Steve Smith (bass and backing vocals), the only other original member besides Fenton to participate in the reformation, along with new drummer Michael Bowes.
Bazalgette departed after the initial tours to focus on his television directing career and was replaced by guitarist Dan Fenton, son of David Fenton, around 20172018; Dan brought fresh energy through his multi-instrumental contributions on electric and acoustic guitar.
Bowes, who had extensive session experience from collaborations with artists such as Nelly Furtado and Joss Stone, remained until September 2025, when he departed due to health concerns following a medical incident during a performance; he was replaced by drummer Mike Kelly for live dates, including the band's October 2025 show in London.
This configuration with David Fenton, Steve Smith, Dan Fenton, and Mike Kelly formed the active core as of November 2025, enabling consistent touring and the release of new material.

Musical style and influences

Genre and sound

The Vapors are primarily classified as a new wave and power pop band, blending punk and mod revival influences into their music. Their songs are characteristically short and hook-driven, typically averaging 2 to 3 minutes in length, emphasizing concise structures with immediate melodic appeal. Signature elements include jangling guitars, driving rhythms, and catchy choruses built around power chords, creating an energetic yet accessible sound. Frontman Dave Fenton's distinctive vocals deliver themes of frustration and irony, often exploring personal and societal tensions through sharp, ironic lyrics.
The band's sound evolved across their initial run, with their 1980 debut album New Clear Days showcasing energetic accessibility through raw, punk-influenced arrangements that prioritized live-wire intensity and melodic hooks, as heard in tracks like "Turning Japanese." Their 1981 follow-up Magnets adopted an edgier tone, incorporating synth-tinged experimentation and crunchier textures for a more introspective edge while maintaining core power pop drive.
In their reunion era starting in 2016, The Vapors retained their foundational new wave and power pop essence but introduced mature lyricism addressing contemporary reflections, evident in albums like Together (2020) and Wasp in a Jar (2025), which blend nostalgic hooks with broader emotional depth.
Original releases on United Artists and Liberty labels featured clean, radio-friendly production that captured the band's tight instrumentation through live overdubs and minimal compression for punchy clarity. Reunion albums in the 2020s adopted a more independent approach, self-releasing via platforms like Bandcamp with professional yet hands-on production emphasizing live instrumentation and digital mastering.

Key influences

The Vapors' music was profoundly shaped by the punk and new wave movements of the late 1970s, which emphasized DIY ethos and accessible songwriting. Frontman David Fenton has cited punk as the primary catalyst for the band's formation, noting that it "encouraged us to have a go, proving anyone could do it." This influence is evident in their adoption of fast tempos and witty, observational lyrics, drawing from bands like the Buzzcocks and the Stranglers, both of whom Fenton listed among his key inspirations. The Surrey post-punk scene, dubbed the "Sound of the Suburbs," further contextualized their development alongside acts like The Jam, fostering a raw energy born from suburban discontent in late-1970s Britain.
The band also drew from the mod revival wave, which revived 1960s British subculture aesthetics amid punk's decline. Fenton expressed admiration for mod-era groups such as the Small Faces and the Kinks, whose guitar-driven sound and sharp style informed The Vapors' energetic riffs and stage presence. Guitarist Edward Bazalgette echoed this with influences including The Who and The Jam, contributing to the band's mod-adjacent image despite their reluctance to be pigeonholed solely within the revival movement.
American power pop elements resonated through Fenton's affinity for 1960s British Invasion bands like the Beatles and the Rolling Stones, which instilled melodic hooks and concise structures in their work. While not explicitly citing U.S. acts, the band's sound echoed contemporaries like Cheap Trick and The Knack in its punchy, hook-laden approach, reflecting broader transatlantic exchanges in the power pop genre during the era.

Discography

Studio albums

The Vapors' debut studio album, New Clear Days, was released on 7 June 1980 by United Artists Records. Produced by Vic Coppersmith-Heaven, it consists of 11 tracks and peaked at No. 44 on the UK Albums Chart, spending six weeks in the top 100. The album features the band's breakthrough single "Turning Japanese" alongside "News at Ten," capturing their energetic new wave power pop sound with sharp social observations.
The band's second album, Magnets, followed on 9 March 1981 via Liberty Records. Produced by David Tickle, it includes 11 tracks and marked an experimental shift toward denser arrangements and thematic depth, though it did not chart in the UK. Standout track "Jimmie Jones" highlights the album's exploration of cult dynamics and psychological tension within the new wave framework.
After a 39-year hiatus, the Vapors returned with Together on 15 May 2020 through their own Manmade Soul Ltd imprint. Produced by Steve Levine, the self-released album comprises 11 tracks emphasizing reunion themes and melodic power pop revival, reaching No. 87 on the UK Albums Chart and No. 1 on the UK Independent Album Breakers Chart. It reflects the band's enduring chemistry post-reformation, with songs like "Crazy" nodding to their classic style.
The fourth studio album, Wasp in a Jar, arrived on 28 February 2025 via Vapors Own Records, crowdfunded by fans to support its production. Co-produced by Michael Giblin and the band, it features 10 tracks addressing social commentary through a punk-infused power pop lens, debuting at No. 47 on the UK Albums Chart. Initial reviews praised its raw energy and return to punky roots, exemplified by opener "Hit the Ground Running."

Singles

The Vapors' singles career began with their debut release in 1979, marking the start of their brief but impactful run in the new wave scene. Their early singles were issued on 7-inch vinyl by United Artists Records, with later ones on Liberty Records, and they achieved modest chart success in the UK, with one major international hit. Post-reunion singles have been released digitally, reflecting the band's return to recording in the 2010s.

Compilation albums

The Vapors have released several compilation albums that collect their original material from the late 1970s and early 1980s, often focusing on hit singles, album tracks, and previously unavailable recordings. These retrospectives have helped maintain interest in the band's new wave and power pop sound, particularly through reissues that highlight their brief but impactful career.
One early compilation is Anthology (1995), released by One Way Records as a CD featuring 20 tracks drawn primarily from the band's two studio albums, New Clear Days (1980) and Magnets (1981). It provides a broad overview of their original era output without additional rarities.
In 1996, EMI Records issued Turning Japanese: The Best of the Vapors, a 20-track CD that emphasizes the band's singles and key album cuts, including the title track "Turning Japanese" and "News at Ten." This collection serves as an accessible entry point for fans, compiling material from their United Artists and Liberty periods.
Vaporized followed in 1998 from Collectables, a single CD that combines the full contents of New Clear Days and Magnets into a 24-track retrospective. While not including new material, it repackages the band's core discography for modern listeners in a budget-friendly format.
A more recent effort, The Best of the Vapors (2003), is a 17-track compilation available on CD and digital formats, blending single versions, select album tracks like "Jimmie Jones," and some live recordings to showcase the band's energetic style.
The most comprehensive release to date is Waiting for the Weekend: The United Artists & Liberty Records Recordings (2021), a 4-CD clamshell box set from Cherry Red Records containing 76 tracks. It includes the original albums New Clear Days and Magnets, along with B-sides, single edits, and two discs of previously unreleased material from 19791982, such as demos (e.g., "Spring Collection" demo), alternative versions (e.g., "Turning Japanese" alternative), rough mixes, and live recordings from shows like the 1979 Guildford Civic Hall performance. This set highlights rarities and outtakes, offering deeper insight into the band's recording process and early live energy.
