 Diego de Almagro (c. 14751538) was a Spanish conquistador of obscure and lowly origins, reputedly a foundling raised in the town of Almagro in Castile, who rose through military service in the Americas to become a key partner in the conquest of the Inca Empire. Arriving in Panama around 1514, he collaborated with Francisco Pizarro and Hernando de Luque to organize expeditions southward starting in 1524, providing reinforcements and logistical support that enabled the capture of the Inca emperor Atahualpa in 1532 and the subsequent subjugation of Peru. Disappointed with his share of spoils and governorships, Almagro led a punishing 15351537 expedition across the Andes into Chile, enduring extreme hardships and clashes with indigenous forces that yielded no lasting conquest before he retreated northward. His ambitions clashed fatally with Pizarro's faction, sparking a civil war in which Almagro seized Cuzco, only to be defeated at the Battle of Las Salinas in 1538 and subsequently garroted in prison.

Early Life

Origins and Youth in Spain

Diego de Almagro was born around 1475 in the town of Almagro, located in the province of Ciudad Real within Castile, Spain. He was the illegitimate son of Juan de Montenegro and Elvira Gutirrez, adopting the name of his birthplace instead of his father's surname. Some historical accounts portray him as a foundling of uncertain origins, highlighting the scarcity of primary records from this period.
Shunned by his father, Almagro was raised initially by his mother or a family servant before being taken in by his maternal uncle, Hernn Gutirrez, in Almagro. At approximately age 15, he departed from his uncle's household and relocated to Seville, where he received assistance from local authorities, including the mayor Don Luis de Polanco.
Almagro's youth was characterized by independence and occasional conflict, including a reported brawl in which he wounded another man, resulting in a warrant for his arrest. Specific details on his occupations remain elusive, with possibilities including menial labor or brief service in the Spanish navy, though these are not firmly documented. By 1514, at around 39 years old, he joined Pedro Arias Dvila's expedition, marking the end of his time in Spain.

Migration to the Americas and Initial Ventures

Diego de Almagro emigrated from Spain to the New World in 1514, arriving at Santa Mara la Antigua del Darin in the Isthmus of Panama as part of the fleet commanded by Pedro Arias Dvila (Pedrarias), the newly appointed governor tasked with consolidating Spanish control over the region. This expedition, numbering several hundred settlers and soldiers, aimed to expand settlements and suppress indigenous resistance following Vasco Nez de Balboa's earlier discoveries. Almagro, then approximately 39 years old and experienced from prior service in the Spanish navy, sought opportunities for advancement amid the harsh frontier conditions of Darin, where disease and conflict claimed many lives.
Upon arrival, Almagro quickly engaged in the ongoing military campaigns to subdue native groups, including the Cueva people, participating in punitive expeditions under Pedrarias' orders that involved raids, enslavement, and establishment of outposts. In November 1515, he led an independent venture with around 260 men into the interior, founding the short-lived settlement of Villa de Acla as a base for further exploration and resource extraction, though illness forced his return and limited its permanence. These efforts earned him recognition for valor, as he supported operations in the Gulf of Panama, including those coordinated with Gaspar de Espinosa, which utilized Balboa's ships for coastal advances and helped secure Spanish dominance over the isthmus by 1519. During this period, Almagro forged key connections with figures like Balboa and Francisco Pizarro, while accumulating modest wealth through encomiendas and trade in pearls and provisions.

Alliance with Francisco Pizarro

Formation of the Conquistador Partnership

In Panama, where both men had established themselves as experienced frontiersmen amid the turbulent governance of Pedrarias Dvila, Diego de Almagro and Francisco Pizarro forged a partnership driven by shared ambitions for conquest and wealth following Hernn Corts's successes in Mexico. Almagro, having accumulated resources from prior ventures including the subjugation of indigenous groups in Central America, complemented Pizarro's exploratory drive; together, they sought to capitalize on vague reports of opulent southern empires relayed by passing ships and native informants. To secure financing, they enlisted Hernando de Luque, the vicar of Panama's cathedral, forming a triad that pooled Almagro's logistical expertise, Pizarro's command authority, and Luque's clerical influence and funds.
The agreement, formalized on March 10, 1526, stipulated equal division of conquests into thirdsone for each partnerwhile assigning Pizarro overall leadership of expeditions, Almagro responsibility for recruiting men and horses, and Luque oversight of provisioning from Panama. This contract, notarized amid the colony's administrative constraints, bypassed initial royal hurdles by framing the venture as private enterprise, though it anticipated seeking a formal capitulacin from the Spanish Crown for legitimacy and monopolies. The arrangement reflected pragmatic realism: Almagro's one-eyed resilience from earlier battles and Pizarro's navigational persistence offset the high risks of uncharted Pacific voyages, enabling the procurement of two ships for their inaugural probe southward.
This alliance marked a causal pivot from individual skirmishes to coordinated imperial ambition, as the partners' complementary skillsAlmagro's supply chains honed in Darin and Pizarro's coastal scoutingmitigated the logistical perils of transoceanic expansion without crown subsidies. Initial outflows totaled around 4,000 castellanos from Luque, underscoring the venture's speculative nature amid Panama's economic strains.

Preliminary Expeditions along the Pacific Coast

In November 1524, Francisco Pizarro initiated the first preliminary expedition southward along the Pacific coast from Panama, with Diego de Almagro playing a key logistical role by remaining in Panama to recruit additional men and gather supplies before following in a secondary vessel. Pizarro departed with roughly 114 volunteers and four horses across two ships, navigating treacherous waters and encountering hostile indigenous groups while probing as far as the region near present-day Ecuador, where signs of more advanced civilizations emerged through reports of gold-laden lands further south. The venture endured extreme hardships, including storms, disease, and starvation that claimed most of the crew, but Almagro's arriving ship bolstered efforts during skirmishes, notably at Atacames, where he sustained a permanent injurylosing his left eye to an arrow during a native attack.
By early 1525, the survivors, including Almagro, returned to Panama after charting approximately 400 leagues of coastline and capturing initial intelligence on southern empires, though Governor Pedro Arias Dvila initially blocked further ventures due to the high casualties and meager immediate gains. Almagro's contributions extended to advocating for persistence, leveraging his military experience to manage onboard discipline amid mutinies and provisioning shortages that reduced the effective force to under 20 men at points. This expedition yielded balsa rafts laden with gold ornaments from natives, confirming rumors of wealth beyond Panama's barren coasts and setting the stage for formalized partnership terms with priest Hernando de Luque.
The second expedition commenced in May 1526 under a ratified contract among Pizarro, Almagro, and Luque, granting them conquest rights; Almagro co-commanded the larger fleet of two ships carrying 160 men, horses, and provisions, pushing deeper south to around 9 south latitude near Tumbez, where they observed organized settlements, llamas, and textiles indicative of imperial wealth. Bartolom Ruiz, the pilot, preceded to capture indigenous interpreters and artifacts, while Almagro oversaw reinforcements and quelled dissent, though equatorial storms and native resistance again inflicted losses. Overwintering on Gallo Island amid governorial recall orders, Almagro returned to Panama in 1527 to secure more recruits despite opposition, rejoining Pizarro by 1528 after the core group relocated to Isla de la Gorgona, having amassed evidence of the Inca Empire's riches without full conquest.

Role in the Conquest of Peru

The 1531 Expedition and Initial Clashes

In early 1531, Francisco Pizarro departed from Panama with an expedition force of approximately 180 men, 37 horses, and three ships to conquer the Inca Empire, under the joint partnership established with Diego de Almagro and Hernando de Luque. Almagro, tasked with logistical support as the primary provisioner, remained in Panama to secure additional recruits, horses, arms, and supplies, financing much of the venture through his resources and efforts to obtain colonial licenses for manpower. This division of roles reflected the partners' agreement, where Pizarro led the advance party southward along the Pacific coast, while Almagro's contributions ensured the sustainability of the campaign amid Panama's limited enthusiasm for the risky enterprise.
Pizarro's fleet made landfall near Coaque (modern-day northern Ecuador) in April 1531, where the Spaniards encountered hostile indigenous groups and engaged in their first significant clashes. The local warriors, numbering in the thousands, attacked using spears and arrows, but the Spanish advantage in steel weapons, armor, and cavalryunfamiliar to the nativesresulted in a decisive victory with minimal Spanish losses, allowing capture of gold artifacts, emeralds, and native women for intelligence and labor. These encounters demonstrated the effectiveness of combined arms tactics against numerically superior foes, boosting morale and providing initial spoils to fund further advances. Almagro's preparatory work in Panama indirectly facilitated these successes by committing to reinforcements that would alleviate Pizarro's strained resources during the prolonged coastal march.
Further south, near Pun Island off the coast of modern Ecuador later in 1531, Pizarro's men faced fiercer resistance from Tumbez warriors allied with Inca forces, involving naval skirmishes and amphibious assaults where Spanish crossbows and harquebuses repelled attacks, though disease and attrition began to take a toll. By this point, the expedition had confirmed the wealth of the Inca domains through captured natives' accounts, validating the partnership's gamble, but Almagro's delayed reinforcementsdue to recruitment challengesmeant Pizarro proceeded with a diminished force, heightening the risks of these early confrontations. Almagro himself did not arrive in Peru until April 1533, bringing 150 men and essential supplies that stabilized the conquest effort post-Cajamarca.

Capture of Atahualpa and the Ransom

Diego de Almagro, having remained in Panama to recruit additional forces following the initial expedition's departure, was absent from Francisco Pizarro's ambush and capture of the Inca emperor Atahualpa on November 16, 1532, at Cajamarca, where Pizarro's approximately 168 men ambushed and defeated Atahualpa's entourage of several thousand, resulting in heavy Inca casualties but no Spanish deaths. Atahualpa, held captive in a room in Cajamarca, proposed a ransom for his release, offering to fill that roommeasuring roughly 22 by 17 feet to a height of 8 feetwith gold objects, and two adjacent rooms to similar height with silver, a commitment that Inca emissaries fulfilled over subsequent months by delivering vast quantities of melted-down treasures from across the empire.
By February 1533, Almagro arrived in Peru with reinforcements numbering around 150 men, joining Pizarro at Cajamarca amid the ongoing ransom collection, which ultimately yielded over 13,000 pounds of gold and more than 26,000 pounds of silver after smelting, representing the largest ransom in history and significantly enriching the Spanish forces. Almagro's timely arrival doubled the Spanish contingent's strength, stabilizing their hold during a period of vulnerability as Inca forces under Quizquiz threatened retaliation, and his recruits claimed equal shares in the ransom distribution alongside the original Cajamarca participants, reflecting the partnership agreement's terms despite Almagro's absence from the initial clash. After deducting the royal fifth (20 percent) for the Spanish crown, the remaining spoils were apportioned, with Pizarro receiving the largest portion as expedition leader, Almagro a substantial share as co-financier and partner, and the rest divided among officers and soldiers based on rankAlmagro's contingent benefiting directly from this windfall, which funded further conquest efforts.
Almagro advocated for Atahualpa's execution post-ransom, arguing it served Spanish interests and crown policy by eliminating a potential rallying figure for Inca resistance, influencing the July 26, 1533, trial and garrote strangulation that followed despite Pizarro's initial reluctance, thereby securing the treasures without releasing the emperor. This decision, amid reports of Atahualpa ordering his half-brother Huscar's death to consolidate power, underscored the pragmatic calculus of conquest, prioritizing control over negotiated freedom.

March to Cuzco and Division of Spoils

Following Atahualpa's execution on July 26, 1533, Francisco Pizarro departed Cajamarca in late September with approximately 500 men, including Spanish troops reinforced by native allies, to advance on the Inca capital of Cuzco amid ongoing civil strife between Atahualpa's general Quizquiz and supporters of the rival claimant Huscar. Diego de Almagro, having arrived at Cajamarca earlier in April 1533 with around 150 reinforcements recruited from Panama, contributed to the combined force's strength during this phase, bolstering logistics and cavalry for the southward push through hostile Andean terrain. The expedition encountered resistance, notably defeating Quizquiz's Inca army at battles near Jauja and Vilcashuamn, where Spanish steel weapons, horses, and firearms provided decisive advantages over Inca numerical superiority and stone-based arms.
Pizarro's troops entered Cuzco unopposed on November 15, 1533, installing Manco Incaa brother of Huscar and Atahualpaas a puppet ruler to legitimize control, while systematically looting the city's temples and palaces. The Coricancha (Temple of the Sun) alone yielded gold and silver artifacts, including wall coverings, statues, and vessels, which were melted into bars; combined with prior ransom treasures, the total haul exceeded 15 million pesos in value after assaying. Almagro participated in securing the city but received a comparatively modest portion relative to Pizarro's allocations to his brothers and key captains, as the partnership agreementoriginally equal among Pizarro, Almagro, and Hernando de Luqueprioritized Pizarro's governorship privileges.
The division adhered to royal ordinances: one-fifth (quinto real) reserved for the Spanish crown, followed by allotments scaled by rank, with common soldiers receiving about 100-200 pesos each, captains thousands, and Pizarro over 1 million as governor and expedition leader. Almagro's share, though substantial for his partnership stake, fell short of expectations given his recruitment efforts and late-arriving men's claims, fostering resentment over perceived favoritism toward the Pizarro faction; this grievance, rooted in the unequal distribution amid vast spoils, sowed seeds for later territorial disputes without immediate rupture. The process, completed by early 1534, underscored the conquistadors' reliance on Inca wealth for sustaining operations, as melting and assaying converted ceremonial items into portable currency amid threats from Inca loyalists.

Expedition to Chile

Grant of Governorship and Preparations

In 1534, amid ongoing disputes over the division of Inca treasures following the conquest of Peru, Diego de Almagro petitioned the Spanish crown for authority to explore and govern territories south of Francisco Pizarro's domain. On 21 May 1534, a capitulacin was issued granting Almagro the title of governor and adelantado of New Toledo, a vast region encompassing approximately 200 leagues (about 1,100 kilometers) southward from the southern boundary of Pizarro's New Castile, along the Pacific coast toward the Strait of Magellan. This agreement entitled Almagro to conquer, pacify, populate, and extract tribute from the area, with privileges including one-fifth of any precious metals found and rights to establish settlements, in exchange for bearing the expedition's costs and adhering to royal oversight.
The grant served to compensate Almagro for perceived shortfalls in his Peruvian spoils, redirecting his ambitions toward uncharted southern lands rumored to hold riches comparable to those of the Inca Empire. To facilitate organization, Almagro leveraged his influence in Peru to secure resources, including appeals to local governors for recruitment licenses and funding.
Preparations intensified in Cuzco, where Almagro assembled a force of several hundred Spanish participants, supplemented by indigenous auxiliaries for labor and transport, along with horses, armaments, and provisions suited for extended overland travel. On 12 June 1535, Almagro formalized a pact with Pizarro stipulating equal division of future southern discoveries, mitigating potential conflicts. The expedition departed Cuzco on 3 July 1535, marking the launch of the first major Spanish incursion into the territories now known as Chile.

Southward Advance and Andes Crossing

Diego de Almagro departed Cuzco in mid-1535 with 500 Spaniards, thousands of indigenous yanacona auxiliaries, horses, and llamas for transport and provisions. The force marched southward through the Collao region of Upper Peru, passing Lake Titicaca and reaching Tupiza amid growing scarcity of food and increasing cold at higher elevations.
The expedition then attempted the Andes crossing via the San Francisco Pass, navigating altitudes up to 13,000 feet in the Puna de Atacama. Harsh conditions included extreme freezing temperatures, intense snowstorms, altitude sickness (known as soroche), and barren landscapes devoid of sustenance, forcing the consumption of pack animals. Swollen rivers and sharp volcanic pebbles that injured horses' hooves compounded the ordeal, slowing advance and exacerbating starvation.
Casualties mounted rapidly during the traverse; roughly 10,000 indigenous auxiliaries died from exposure, exhaustion, and privation, while most horses perished, severely limiting mounted capabilities upon descent. This grueling passage, completed by early 1536, represented one of the most punishing overland treks in early Spanish exploration, yielding entry into northern Chile near the Copiap Valley but at immense human and logistical cost.

Exploration of Chilean Territories and Setbacks

Diego de Almagro's expedition departed from Cuzco on July 3, 1535, comprising approximately 570 Spanish soldiers, 200 horses, and several thousand indigenous auxiliaries, aiming to conquer and exploit the rumored riches of territories south of Peru. The force followed portions of the Inca road system before attempting a perilous crossing of the Andes mountains, enduring severe winter conditions including heavy snow, freezing temperatures, and scarcity of provisions, which resulted in significant losses of men and livestock.
Upon descending into northern Chile around mid-1536, the expedition traversed the arid Atacama Desert and reached the Copiap Valley, where initial encounters with local Diaguita indigenous groups yielded limited provisions but sparked armed skirmishes. Further south, in valleys such as Huasco and Coquimbo, Almagro's men noted fertile lands suitable for settlement but faced increasing resistance from native populations, including battles that depleted resources and morale. The explorers pushed toward the Maule River region, hearing tales of a wealthy kingdom called Quilac, yet discovered no substantial gold or silver deposits, contrary to expectations inspired by Inca legends.
Setbacks mounted as the expedition encountered fierce opposition from Mapuche warriors in central Chile, leading to bloody confrontations that inflicted heavy casualties on the overstretched force. Harsh terrain, including rugged mountains and swollen rivers, compounded logistical failures, with horses succumbing to starvation and men suffering from malnutrition and disease. Internal dissent and mutiny threats arose amid the absence of plunder, prompting Almagro to abandon further advances by late 1536; the battered expedition retraced its steps northward, recrossing the Andes and arriving back in Cuzco by April 1537, having achieved no permanent conquest or economic gain. This grueling venture, marked by over 75% attrition in some estimates, underscored the formidable natural and human barriers to Spanish expansion southward, tarnishing Chile's reputation among potential settlers for decades.

Rivalry and Civil Conflict in Peru

Territorial Disputes with the Pizarros

Following the conquest of Peru, tensions between Diego de Almagro and Francisco Pizarro escalated over jurisdictional boundaries established by royal capitulaciones. Pizarro's 1529 grant for New Castile encompassed approximately 200 leagues southward from Tumbes along the Pacific coast, extending inland to include Cuzco, though exact interior demarcations remained ambiguous due to reliance on coastal latitudes and uncharted terrain. Almagro, initially excluded from territorial governorship despite his partnership role, secured his own 1534 capitulacin from Charles V for New Toledo, defined as 200 leagues further south of Pizarro's southern boundary, aimed at regions including modern-day Bolivia and northern Chile.
Almagro's 15351537 expedition to Chile yielded no significant riches, prompting his return amid reports of Manco Inca's 15361537 siege of Cuzco, where Pizarro's half-brother Hernando governed as lieutenant. Upon arriving in April 1537 with around 570 men, Almagro relieved the weakening Inca forcesalready retreating due to seasonal rains and internal divisionsand entered Cuzco, asserting that the city fell within New Toledo's purview. His claim hinged on interpreting the grants' latitudinal boundaries strictly: Cuzco, at roughly 1331'S, lay south of Pizarro's purported coastal limit near 12S, rendering intervening sierra territories (highland interior) subject to New Toledo despite Pizarro's prior occupation and investment in the area.
Pizarro and his brothers contested this as an overreach, viewing Cuzco as core to New Castile based on conquest precedence and the original partnership's spoils division, which had allocated the city to their faction in 15331534. Almagro's captains, facing financial ruin from the barren Chilean venture, pressured him to enforce the claim, leading to Hernando Pizarro's arrest on charges of disobedience during the siege relief. Royal cedulas arriving later in 1537 urged Almagro to vacate Pizarro-held sites, but enforcement lagged, exacerbating the rift into open conflict. The dispute underscored causal frictions from imprecise colonial mappingrelying on estimated leagues and parallels without precise instrumentationand self-interested interpretations favoring immediate control of Cuzco's gold-laden environs over distant, unproven southern holdings.

Capture of Cuzco and Outbreak of War

Following the grueling return from his expedition to Chile, Diego de Almagro arrived near Cuzco in early April 1537 with approximately 500 men, many of whom were disillusioned and seeking recompense for their hardships and lack of spoils. Cuzco, the Inca capital, had endured a prolonged siege by Manco Inca's forces from May 1536 until its relief in March 1537, during which Juan Pizarro, one of Francisco Pizarro's half-brothers, was killed, leaving Hernando and Gonzalo Pizarro in command of the depleted Spanish garrison. Almagro asserted legal claims to the city based on his 1534 royal grant of governorship over the territory south of Cuzco (New Toledo), arguing it fell within his jurisdiction due to its latitude, and citing prior agreements with Francisco Pizarro that entitled him to a share of the region's wealth to offset his expedition's costs.
Tensions escalated rapidly upon Almagro's approach, as his troops, suffering from privation and perceiving the Pizarros as monopolizing Cuzco's riches, mutinied against Hernando Pizarro's authority. On the night of April 8, 1537, Almagro's forces stormed the city, capturing Hernando and Gonzalo Pizarro without significant resistance; Hernando was imprisoned in the Sacsayhuamn fortress, while Gonzalo was briefly held before negotiations. Almagro justified the seizure as a necessary enforcement of his rights and the demands of his loyalists (later called Almagristas), who viewed the Pizarros' control as unjust given the original conquest partnership's terms.
This coup precipitated the outbreak of open civil war between Almagro's faction and the Pizarros, fracturing Spanish unity in Peru and exacerbating local Inca resistance. Francisco Pizarro, based in Lima, denounced the action as treasonous and mobilized reinforcements, including from newly arrived settlers, to reclaim Cuzco; meanwhile, Almagro consolidated control, distributing spoils to his men and installing provisional governance. The conflict, rooted in disputes over territorial boundaries, spoils division, and authority under ambiguous royal capitulaciones, persisted until Almagro's defeat at the Battle of Las Salinas on April 26, 1538, but the April 1537 capture marked its violent inception, drawing in hundreds of Spaniards and undermining colonial stability.

Defeat at Las Salinas and Execution

The Battle of Las Salinas took place on April 26, 1538, approximately 10 leagues from Cuzco, marking the decisive clash in the civil conflict between Diego de Almagro's faction and the forces loyal to Francisco Pizarro, led by his brother Hernando Pizarro. Almagro's army, consisting of around 500-600 men primarily infantry under the field command of Rodrigo Orgez, confronted Hernando's comparable force of roughly 500 foot soldiers, 80 cavalry, and supporting artillery. The engagement, lasting about an hour, saw Orgez's aggressive tactics falter against the Pizarrists' effective use of mounted charges and light cannons, resulting in heavy casualties for Almagro's side, including the death of Orgez himself on the battlefield. Almagro, who was not directly commanding due to illness, was captured shortly after the rout as his remaining troops surrendered or fled.
Imprisoned in Cuzco following the defeat, Almagro faced a swift trial orchestrated by Hernando Pizarro, who accused him of treason against the Spanish Crown for seizing Cuzco and waging war on royal authority. The proceedings, lacking impartiality given Hernando's longstanding enmity toward Almagro, disregarded appeals for clemency and prior pardons extended by Francisco Pizarro. Despite Francisco's reported displeasure with the harsh judgment, Hernando upheld the death sentence, viewing Almagro's actions as a direct threat to Pizarrist control and imperial order.
On July 8, 1538, Almagro was executed by garrote in his dungeon cell in Cuzco, a method reserved for nobles to avoid spilling blood directly. His corpse was subsequently decapitated, and his head mounted on a pike and displayed in the city's main plaza as a warning to potential rebels. This act intensified factional divisions among the conquistadors, contributing to further unrest, though it temporarily secured Pizarrist dominance in Peru.

Family and Immediate Aftermath

Personal Relationships and Offspring

Diego de Almagro did not enter into any formally documented marriage during his lifetime. In Panama, where he resided from approximately 1514 onward, he formed a common-law partnership with Ana Martnez, an indigenous woman, who gave birth to his only acknowledged son, Diego de Almagro IIcommonly known as "el Mozo" (the youth)around 1520. This son, recognized as legitimate by royal decree in 1535 despite his illegitimate birth, was entrusted to Almagro's care and later accompanied him on expeditions to Peru, where he received education and military training under his father's oversight.
Historical records provide limited details on other personal relationships, with some genealogical sources suggesting Almagro had a daughter named Isabel from a liaison with an indigenous woman referred to as Mencia, though this remains unconfirmed by primary contemporary accounts and is not corroborated in major biographical treatments. Conflicting reports exist regarding el Mozo's maternity, with a minority of accounts attributing it to Almagro's former enslaved African woman Malgarida rather than Ana Martnez, but the latter identification prevails in most analyses due to consistency with Panama-era records. Almagro's family life was overshadowed by his peripatetic military career, leaving scant evidence of deeper domestic ties beyond these offspring and their mothers.

Diego de Almagro el Mozo's Rebellion

Diego de Almagro el Mozo (c. 15201542), the acknowledged illegitimate son of the conquistador Diego de Almagro and the indigenous woman Ana Martnez, inherited grievances stemming from his father's execution by Hernando Pizarro on July 8, 1538, following defeat at the Battle of Las Salinas. Resentment among Almagrist partisans over the Pizarros' dominance in Peru, coupled with el Mozo's claims to his father's governorship of the disputed territories around Cuzco, fueled agitation. By early 1541, el Mozo, then in his early twenties, had garnered support from loyalists disillusioned with Francisco Pizarro's rule, including figures like Juan de Rada, who sought to restore Almagrist influence.
The rebellion ignited on June 26, 1541, when approximately 2024 conspirators loyal to el Mozo stormed Francisco Pizarro's palace in Lima during dinner. Despite Pizarro's resistance, in which he killed several attackers, the assailants overwhelmed and fatally stabbed him multiple times; he died invoking divine mercy. This assassination, directly avenging the elder Almagro's death, marked the rebellion's violent outset and decapitated Pizarro leadership, though his brothers Hernando (imprisoned in Spain) and Gonzalo retained influence. El Mozo, based in Cuzco, was promptly proclaimed governor of Peru by his followers, who raised an army of several hundred Spaniards and indigenous auxiliaries to consolidate control.
The uprising expanded into open civil war as Almagrist forces seized key areas, but faced opposition from royalists and Pizarro allies. In response to reports of instability, King Charles V dispatched Cristbal Vaca de Castro as juez pesquisidor (royal investigator) in 1541 to probe the elder Almagro's execution and restore order; Vaca de Castro arrived in Peru by mid-1542, rallying about 500 loyal troops. El Mozo's army, numbering around 600, marched north but suffered from internal divisions and logistical strains. On September 16, 1542, Vaca de Castro decisively defeated them at the Battle of Chupas near Huamanga (modern Ayacucho), where Almagrist losses exceeded 200 killed, including key leaders like Rada.
Captured shortly after the rout, Diego de Almagro el Mozo was tried by Vaca de Castro's tribunal for treason, the assassination, and rebellion against royal authority. On September 16 or shortly thereafter, he was beheaded in Cuzco's main square, ending the immediate Almagrist threat; his head was displayed publicly as a deterrent. The suppression quelled the faction but exacerbated factionalism, contributing to Gonzalo Pizarro's later revolt in 1544, while Vaca de Castro's provisional governance stabilized Spanish control until Viceroy Blasco Nez Vela's arrival in 1544.

Legacy

Contributions to Spanish Exploration and Empire-Building

Diego de Almagro's partnership with Francisco Pizarro was instrumental in initiating the conquest of the Inca Empire. Formed around 1524, the alliance involved Almagro providing logistical support and funding from Panama while Pizarro explored the Pacific coast and obtained royal capitulations in Spain between 1528 and 1529. Almagro recruited reinforcements, dispatching them to Peru in 1534 to aid in suppressing Inca rebellions after Atahualpa's capture in 1532 and execution in 1533. His forces helped secure Spanish control over key areas, including the foundation of cities like Trujillo in 1535, laying groundwork for colonial administration in the newly claimed territories.
Almagro's most direct contribution to exploration came with his 15351537 expedition southward from Cuzco, authorized by the Spanish crown to conquer regions beyond Peru. Departing on July 3, 1535, with roughly 600 Spaniards, 1,000 indigenous auxiliaries, and 100 enslaved Africans, the party endured severe hardships crossing the Andes, including freezing temperatures, altitude sickness, and supply shortages that reduced effective fighting strength. Reaching the Copiap Valley in northern Chile after approximately 20 months, Almagro's men clashed with local Diaguita and Mapuche groups but found scant gold, prompting a retreat in 1537 amid mutinies and further losses.
Despite yielding no immediate conquest or riches, the expedition advanced Spanish empire-building by mapping over 1,000 miles of terrain, documenting rivers, valleys, and indigenous polities that informed future ventures. Almagro's routes and intelligence reports demonstrated the overland linkage between Peru and southern lands, enabling Pedro de Valdivia's 1540 expedition, which founded Santiago in 1541 and established enduring Spanish footholds in Chile. This extension of exploratory claims southward solidified Spain's hemispheric ambitions, integrating the Inca heartland with peripheral regions into a cohesive imperial framework despite Almagro's tactical failures.

Assessments of Leadership and Character

Diego de Almagro demonstrated notable bravery and determination during his 15351537 expedition into Chilean territories, leading approximately 570 Spaniards and thousands of indigenous auxiliaries across the punishing Andes and Atacama Desert despite severe starvation, cold, and hostile encounters, resulting in the loss of most of his force yet persisting in pursuit of rumored riches. This endeavor, though ultimately fruitless in yielding gold, underscored his resilience as a self-made conquistador from humble, illegitimate origins, rising through audacious ventures in Panama and Peru.
Historians assess Almagro's character as tough and ruthless, traits essential for survival amid the brutal conquests, where he participated in the subjugation of Inca forces and extraction of tribute, employing harsh measures common to his peers against native populations. His ambition, however, often veered into impulsiveness, fueling a bitter rivalry with Francisco Pizarro over territorial governorships; after returning impoverished from Chile, Almagro seized Cuzco in 1537, claiming rights denied him, but his lenient treatment of captives allowed Pizarro's brothers to regroup.
In the ensuing civil war, Almagro's leadership faltered strategically at the Battle of Las Salinas on April 26, 1538, where his outnumbered but fatigued forces, positioned defensively on high ground, were outmaneuvered by Hernando Pizarro's cavalry charges, leading to decisive defeat and his subsequent garroting on July 8, 1538. While contemporaries in Pizarro's camp decried him as ungrateful and treacherous for challenging the partnership, balanced views credit his exploratory zeal for extending Spanish knowledge southward, though critiquing his quarrelsomeness as exacerbating factional strife that weakened the nascent colony.

Enduring Controversies and Balanced Historical Views

Historians continue to debate the origins of Almagro's rupture with Francisco Pizarro, with some attributing it to Pizarro's deliberate undercutting of Almagro's claims under the 1529 capitulations, which ambiguously divided southern territories and rewards from the Inca conquest. Others emphasize Almagro's own impatience after the barren Chile expedition of 15351537, viewing his seizure of Cuzco on April 6, 1537, as an opportunistic overreach rather than justified entitlement, given Pizarro's royal governorship over Peru proper. This interpretation aligns with primary chronicles favoring the Pizarros, which may reflect victors' bias in portraying Almagro's faction as disloyal agitators, though Almagrists' exclusion from encomienda distributions exacerbated grievances among under-rewarded conquistadors.
Almagro's execution following defeat at Las Salinas on April 26, 1538, raises questions of procedural fairness, as the trial under Hernando Pizarro prioritized factional loyalty over impartial justice, yet his prior alliances with Inca rebels like Manco Inca during the 1536 siege of Cuzco lent credence to charges of sedition. Balanced assessments acknowledge Almagro's role in initial Inca subjugationrecruiting forces in Panama and enduring hardships in the 15241528 expeditionsbut critique his impulsiveness, such as rejecting royal mediation in 1537, as strategically inferior to Pizarro's calculated diplomacy with the Crown.
Contemporary and modern evaluations depict Almagro as a resilient self-made figurerising from obscure origins to co-conqueror despite losing an eye in Panama skirmishesyet driven by raw ambition that mirrored the era's causal dynamics of personal gain amid weak central oversight. His Chile foray, though disastrous with high casualties from Mapuche resistance and Andean crossings, pioneered Spanish awareness of southern frontiers, countering narratives of mere failure by highlighting empirical risks of uncoordinated exploration. Overall, historiography frames Almagro not as outlier villainy but as emblematic of conquistador civil strife, where mutual betrayals delayed Peru's stabilization until viceregal reforms post-1542, underscoring how individual agency amplified factional chaos over unified imperial advance.
