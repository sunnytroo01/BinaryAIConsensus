Mars is the most explored planet beyond Earth and the most likely destination for the first human settlement on another world. Named after the Roman god of war because of its distinctive blood-red appearance, Mars has captivated human imagination for centuries and has been the subject of some of humanity's most ambitious engineering achievements. As of the mid-2020s, multiple nations have sent spacecraft to Mars, including orbiters, landers, rovers, and even a helicopter that achieved the first powered flight on another planet. The ongoing exploration of Mars is simultaneously an engineering challenge, a scientific investigation, and a rehearsal for the next great chapter of human exploration.

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun, orbiting at an average distance of approximately one hundred and forty-two million miles, about one and a half times Earth's distance from the Sun. It has a diameter of approximately four thousand two hundred and twelve miles, about half of Earth's diameter, and a mass of approximately one-tenth of Earth's. Its day, called a sol, is approximately twenty-four hours and thirty-seven minutes long, remarkably similar to an Earth day. Its year is approximately six hundred and eighty-seven Earth days, nearly twice as long as ours.

The Martian atmosphere is thin and inhospitable. It is composed of approximately ninety-five percent carbon dioxide, with traces of nitrogen, argon, oxygen, and water vapor. The atmospheric pressure at the surface is less than one percent of Earth's, which means that liquid water cannot exist on the surface under current conditions, as it would immediately boil away. Temperatures on Mars range from about minus two hundred degrees Fahrenheit at the poles in winter to about seventy degrees Fahrenheit at the equator in summer, though the average temperature is about minus eighty degrees.

Despite these harsh conditions, Mars bears compelling evidence of a warmer, wetter past. The surface is carved with features that strongly resemble river valleys, lake beds, delta deposits, and coastal shorelines. The Mars rovers have discovered minerals that form only in the presence of water, including hematite, gypsum, and clay minerals. Orbital observations have revealed recurring slope lineae, dark streaks that appear on steep slopes during warm seasons and fade during cold seasons, which may be caused by briny water flowing just beneath the surface, though this interpretation remains debated.

The most dramatic evidence for Mars's watery past comes from the planet's topography. The northern lowlands of Mars are remarkably flat and smooth, consistent with having been the floor of an ancient ocean that may have covered up to one-third of the planet's surface. If this ocean existed, it would have contained enough water to cover the entire planet to a depth of several hundred feet. The disappearance of Mars's ocean is one of the great mysteries of planetary science. The leading hypothesis is that Mars lost its global magnetic field approximately four billion years ago, which allowed the solar wind to gradually strip away the atmosphere. Without a thick atmosphere to maintain surface pressure and trap heat, the water evaporated and was either lost to space or frozen beneath the surface as permafrost.

The exploration of Mars by robotic spacecraft has produced some of the most remarkable achievements in the history of engineering. The Viking landers, which reached Mars in 1976, were the first spacecraft to successfully land on Mars and return images and data from the surface. They conducted the first experiments designed to search for life on Mars, with ambiguous results that are still debated today.

The Mars Pathfinder mission in 1997 deployed Sojourner, the first rover to operate on another planet. Sojourner was small, about the size of a microwave oven, and traveled a total distance of approximately three hundred and thirty feet during its eighty-three-day mission. But it proved that a remotely controlled rover could navigate the Martian terrain and conduct scientific investigations.

The Spirit and Opportunity rovers, which landed in 2004, were designed for ninety-day missions but far exceeded expectations. Spirit operated for over six years and traveled approximately four point eight miles. Opportunity operated for nearly fifteen years and traveled over twenty-eight miles, discovering geological evidence that water once flowed across the Martian surface.

The Curiosity rover, which landed in 2012 in Gale Crater, is the size of a small car and is powered by a nuclear battery that will last for at least fourteen years. It has discovered organic molecules in Martian rocks, measured seasonal variations in methane levels in the atmosphere, and confirmed that Gale Crater was once a habitable lake environment with the chemical ingredients necessary to support microbial life.

The Perseverance rover, which landed in February 2021 in Jezero Crater, a former lake bed with a beautifully preserved river delta, is conducting the most sophisticated search for biosignatures, chemical or structural evidence of past life, ever attempted on Mars. It is also collecting rock samples in sealed tubes that will be left on the surface for a future sample return mission to bring back to Earth, where they can be analyzed with laboratory instruments far more powerful than anything that can be sent to Mars.

Ingenuity, a small helicopter that accompanied Perseverance to Mars, achieved the first powered flight on another planet on April 19, 2021. This was a genuinely historic moment: the first time a human-built aircraft flew in the atmosphere of another world. Ingenuity was designed for five flights as a technology demonstration but ultimately completed over seventy flights, proving that aerial exploration of Mars is feasible and opening a new dimension of planetary exploration.

The question of whether Mars ever harbored life is one of the most profound in all of science. If life arose independently on Mars, it would demonstrate that life is not a unique accident that happened only once on one planet but a natural outcome of chemistry given the right conditions, implying that the universe may be full of life. If Mars was habitable but life did not arise, it would suggest that the origin of life requires conditions or circumstances that are rarer than we thought.

Human missions to Mars are no longer science fiction. NASA's long-term plans include sending astronauts to Mars in the late 2030s or 2040s. SpaceX, founded by Elon Musk with the explicit goal of making humanity a multi-planetary species, is developing the Starship rocket system designed to carry one hundred passengers to Mars. China has announced plans for crewed Mars missions by 2033.

The challenges of sending humans to Mars are enormous. The journey takes approximately seven months each way using current propulsion technology. The crew would be exposed to cosmic radiation for the entire journey, with no magnetic field or thick atmosphere to protect them. Communication delay between Earth and Mars ranges from four to twenty-four minutes each way, meaning real-time mission control is impossible. The crew must be entirely self-sufficient for at least two years, the minimum duration of a round trip including the wait for the orbital alignment that allows the return journey.

Mars is the most promising candidate for a second home for humanity. Its day length is similar to Earth's. It has water ice in abundance at the poles and beneath the surface. Its soil contains the elements needed for plant growth, though it also contains toxic perchlorates that would need to be removed. Its atmosphere, though thin and carbon dioxide-rich, could potentially be processed to extract oxygen and produce rocket fuel. Its gravity, about thirty-eight percent of Earth's, is strong enough to prevent some of the bone and muscle loss associated with microgravity.

The exploration of Mars is not just a scientific endeavor. It is an existential one. By becoming a multi-planetary species, humanity would ensure that no single catastrophe, whether an asteroid impact, a pandemic, a nuclear war, or a climate disaster, could extinguish all of human civilization. Mars is the backup drive for the human species. And the fact that we are seriously planning to go there, that we have the technology, the knowledge, and the will to attempt it, is one of the most remarkable facts about the present moment in human history.
