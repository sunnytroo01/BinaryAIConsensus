 A landlocked country is a sovereign state lacking direct territorial access to the sea, being entirely surrounded by other countries or landmasses, which precludes independent maritime trade routes and naval capabilities. As of 2025, there are 44 such countries, representing approximately 17% of the world's sovereign states and home to over 640 million people, with the majority concentrated in Africa (16), followed by Asia (12) and Europe (10). These nations inherently face elevated trade and transit costs due to dependence on neighboring coastal states for port access, often exacerbating economic isolation and developmental hurdles, as evidenced by the United Nations' classification of 32 as landlocked developing countries (LLDCs) with persistently high poverty rates. Two are doubly landlockedLiechtenstein and Uzbekistanrequiring passage through at least two other countries to reach the sea, amplifying logistical dependencies. Despite these constraints, outliers like Austria and Switzerland demonstrate that robust institutions and regional integration can mitigate geographical disadvantages, achieving high prosperity through overland infrastructure and free trade agreements.

Definition and Characteristics

Geographical and Legal Definition

A landlocked country is a sovereign state whose territory is entirely surrounded by landmasses of other countries or internal waters, with no direct access to the open sea or ocean. This geographical condition excludes coastlines along endorheic basins or closed seas that do not connect to oceanic waters. As of 2025, there are 44 such countries recognized internationally, comprising approximately one-fifth of the world's sovereign states.
Legally, under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), a land-locked state is defined as one having no sea-coast. Article 125 of UNCLOS, adopted in 1982 and entered into force in 1994, grants landlocked states the right of access to and from the sea for exercising rights under the convention, including freedom of transit through the territory of one or more transit states on terms of mutual agreement. This transit must be provided without discrimination, aiming to facilitate international trade without undue delays or restrictions, though implementation depends on bilateral or multilateral agreements between the states involved. Landlocked states retain full sovereignty despite their lack of maritime boundaries, but they do not possess territorial seas, exclusive economic zones, or continental shelf rights inherent to coastal states.

Physical and Demographic Traits

Landlocked countries lack any coastline on oceans or seas with direct oceanic access, confining their territory entirely within the landmasses of neighboring states and barring claims to territorial waters or exclusive economic zones under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea. Forty-four sovereign states meet this criterion, spanning Europe, Asia, Africa, and South America.
Physically, these nations display varied topographies shaped by their interior positions, including expansive plains, deserts, and mountain ranges, with limited reliance on natural inland waterways for transit in many cases. Kazakhstan represents the largest by land area, encompassing 2,724,900 square kilometers of predominantly steppe and semi-arid terrain. Higher average elevations characterize numerous landlocked countries, particularly in Africa and Asia; for example, eight of Africa's sixteen landlocked states exceed 1,000 meters in mean altitude, contributing to harsher continental climates with greater temperature extremes than coastal counterparts.
Demographically, landlocked countries exhibit stark disparities in scale and settlement patterns. The Vatican City holds the smallest population, under 1,000 residents, while Ethiopia qualifies as the most populous landlocked state. In 2023, the 32 landlocked developing countries accounted for 577 million inhabitants, or 7 percent of the global population in less-developed regions. Population densities range widely, from sparse figures in vast territories like Mongolia (approximately 2 persons per square kilometer) to denser concentrations in microstates such as Liechtenstein, influenced by limited arable land and resource distribution in enclosed geographies.

Historical Context

Ancient and Pre-Modern Examples

The Kingdom of Urartu, flourishing from approximately 860 to 590 BC in the Armenian Highlands around Lakes Van, Urmia, and Sevan, exemplified an ancient landlocked polity centered in mountainous terrain without oceanic access, sustaining itself through advanced irrigation, metallurgy, and defensive fortresses against invaders like the Assyrians. Its economy depended on overland trade routes and agricultural surpluses from highland valleys, highlighting early adaptations to geographic isolation via centralized hydraulic engineering and tribute systems from peripheral vassals.
Similarly, the Neo-Assyrian Empire (911609 BC), rooted in northern Mesopotamia along the upper Tigris River, operated as a landlocked power devoid of direct sea coasts, channeling resources through river navigation to southern Babylonian ports and extensive road networks for military expeditions reaching as far as Egypt. This empire's expansion relied on iron weaponry, siege technology, and administrative deportation policies rather than naval forces, demonstrating causal links between inland positioning and emphasis on terrestrial dominance, with annual campaigns covering up to 1,000 kilometers overland.
In pre-modern Eurasia, the Mongol Empire under Genghis Khan and successors (12061368) emerged from the landlocked Mongolian steppes, forming history's largest contiguous land empire spanning 24 million square kilometers by prioritizing horse-mounted archery, relay postal systems (yam), and merit-based governance over maritime avenues. Lacking inherent sea access, it integrated conquered coastal regions like Korea and Persia but maintained core logistical dependencies on vast inland mobility, with trade facilitated via the Silk Road rather than ports, underscoring how landlocked origins fostered unparalleled overland connectivity across diverse terrains.
The medieval Kingdom of Hungary, founded in 1000 AD after the Magyars' settlement of the Carpathian Basin, persisted as a landlocked entity for nearly a millennium, bordered by the Holy Roman Empire, Poland-Lithuania, and the Balkans, with its 325,000 square kilometers supporting a population peaking at 4 million through Danube River commerce and fortified border defenses like the Drava line. Despite personal unions granting nominal Adriatic access via Croatia after 1102, Hungary's sovereign core remained sea-denied, prompting reliance on Black Sea aspirations during the rpd dynasty and later Ottoman conflicts, where geographic constraints amplified vulnerabilities during invasions like the 1241 Mongol incursion that depopulated up to 20% of its inhabitants.

Colonial and Post-Colonial Emergence

The partitioning of Africa during the European Scramble for Africa from the 1880s to the early 1900s established numerous inland colonies, protectorates, and administrative territories without maritime access, as imperial powers prioritized resource extraction and administrative control over geographic viability. For instance, British divisions separated inland regions like Bechuanaland (modern Botswana) and Basutoland (Lesotho) from coastal holdings, while French colonies such as Upper Volta (Burkina Faso), Niger, Chad, and Mali were delineated as interior zones focused on labor and agriculture rather than trade ports. These boundaries reflected arbitrary decisions by colonial authorities, often splitting ethnic groups and ignoring natural trade routes, setting the stage for post-independence landlocked status.
Decolonization waves from the 1950s to 1970s transformed these entities into sovereign states, with colonial borders largely preserved under the Organization of African Unity's 1963 charter endorsing uti possidetis juris to avert interstate conflicts over territory. This principle locked in the landlocked configurations, yielding 16 such countriesBotswana, Lesotho, Eswatini, Zambia, Zimbabwe, Malawi, Rwanda, Burundi, Uganda, Ethiopia (reasserting pre-colonial status post-Italian occupation), Central African Republic, Chad, Niger, Mali, Burkina Faso, and South Sudan (emerged 2011 from Sudan)home to roughly 300 million people. Key independences included the French Sahel states (Mali, Niger, Chad, Burkina Faso) on August 5 and August 11, 1960; Zambia from Northern Rhodesia on October 24, 1964; and Botswana from Bechuanaland on September 30, 1966, each inheriting enclaves reliant on transit through neighboring ex-colonies. 
Beyond Africa, analogous post-colonial dynamics affected Asia, where Laos achieved autonomy from French Indochina via the 1953 Geneva Accords and full independence in 1954, emerging landlocked amid partitioned territories. In the Americas, while most landlocked cases predated full decolonizationsuch as Bolivia's 1884 coastal loss to Chile following the War of the Pacificthese reinforced the pattern of territorial fragmentation prioritizing political expediency over economic access. Overall, this emergence stemmed from colonial legacies of divided hinterlands, perpetuating dependencies on coastal neighbors for trade without compensatory infrastructure development during imperial rule.

Classification by Territorial Encirclement

Singly Landlocked Countries

Singly landlocked countries are sovereign states lacking direct access to the sea but sharing a land border with at least one coastal nation, permitting sea access through the territory of that single neighbor. This contrasts with doubly landlocked countries, where all bordering states are also landlocked, necessitating traversal of at least two landlocked territories to reach a coastal state. Of the 44 recognized landlocked countries worldwide, 42 fall into the singly landlocked category, with Liechtenstein and Uzbekistan being the exceptions as doubly landlocked.
These countries are distributed across multiple continents, predominantly in Africa (16), followed by Asia (12), Europe (9, excluding the doubly landlocked Liechtenstein), and the Americas (2, Paraguay and Bolivia). Examples include Austria in Europe, which borders coastal states such as Italy and Slovenia; Switzerland, adjacent to Italy and France; and in Africa, Botswana, sharing borders with coastal Namibia and South Africa. In South America, Paraguay borders Argentina and Brazil, both with extensive coastlines.
A subset of singly landlocked countries consists of three enclaves entirely surrounded by one coastal nation: Lesotho, enclosed within South Africa; and San Marino and Vatican City, both embedded in Italy. This encirclement by a single state simplifies transit arrangements but heightens dependence on that neighbor's goodwill and infrastructure for export-import routes. Geographically, singly landlocked status mitigates some logistical barriers compared to doubly landlocked counterparts, as direct adjacency to coastal ports reduces the number of international transit agreements required. However, challenges persist, including higher freight costsestimated at 50% above those of coastal nations due to overland transportand vulnerability to neighboring policies on tariffs or border controls.
Empirical data indicates that singly landlocked countries often fare better economically than doubly landlocked ones, with easier access to regional trade hubs; for instance, Central European singly landlocked states like Hungary and the Czech Republic leverage proximity to Adriatic and Baltic ports via neighbors like Croatia and Germany. Nonetheless, effective mitigation relies on bilateral treaties guaranteeing freedom of transit, as enshrined in the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) Article 125, which mandates non-discriminatory passage for landlocked states through coastal neighbors' territories.

Multiply Landlocked Countries

Multiply landlocked countries, also termed triply or higher-order landlocked states, would be sovereign nations entirely encircled by doubly landlocked countries, necessitating at least three international border crossings to access an ocean coastline. No such countries exist worldwide as of October 2025. The two doubly landlocked countriesLiechtenstein, bordered by Switzerland and Austria, and Uzbekistan, bordered by Afghanistan, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, and Turkmenistando not collectively or individually surround any other state, precluding the possibility of a third layer of landlocking. Each of these doubly landlocked nations' neighbors includes singly landlocked countries with direct or indirect ocean access via coastal states, preventing further isolation.
This absence stems from historical and geographical factors: international borders evolved through conquest, treaties, and decolonization without creating nested enclaves beyond the double degree, unlike subnational divisions. For comparison, within the United States, Nebraska qualifies as triply landlocked, as any path to the Pacific, Atlantic, or Gulf of Mexico requires traversing at least three state boundaries, due to its central position amid singly and doubly landlocked states like Colorado and Wyoming. Similar higher-degree landlocking occurs in other federations, such as triple landlocked provinces in Canada or Australia, but sovereign states' larger scales and maritime access treaties under the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea further mitigate extreme isolation.
The non-existence of multiply landlocked countries underscores the rarity of progressive landlocking, with only 44 landlocked sovereign states total, two of which are doubly landlocked. Hypothetical multiply landlocked entities would face amplified trade barriers, higher logistics costs (potentially 20-50% above coastal peers per World Bank estimates for doubly landlocked), and greater dependence on multilateral transit agreements, but no empirical cases exist to verify such impacts.

Doubly Landlocked Countries

Doubly landlocked countries are sovereign states that lack direct access to the sea and are bordered solely by other landlocked countries, necessitating the crossing of at least two international borders to reach an ocean coastline. Only two such countries exist: the European microstate of Liechtenstein and the Central Asian republic of Uzbekistan.
Liechtenstein, with a land area of 160 km, lies in the Alps between Switzerland to its west and south and Austria to its east and north. Both neighbors are landlocked, rendering Liechtenstein doubly so. Its population stood at approximately 38,300 in 2023. The principality maintains a customs and monetary union with Switzerland, facilitating indirect access to North Sea ports via the Rhine River and Swiss infrastructure for international trade.
Uzbekistan borders five landlocked states: Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, and Afghanistan. Covering 447,400 km, it holds the distinction as the world's largest doubly landlocked country. The population was estimated at 31.4 million in 2023, with over half residing in urban areas such as the agriculturally vital Fergana Valley. Uzbekistan's doubly landlocked status emerged after the 1991 Soviet dissolution, which eliminated prior transit routes through Soviet ports. Trade relies on bilateral transit agreements and infrastructure like rail links through neighboring states to reach ports in the Persian Gulf or Black Sea. No sovereign triply landlocked countriesthose surrounded exclusively by doubly landlocked onescurrently exist, as geographic and political configurations preclude such encirclement.

Economic Impacts

Trade Barriers and Logistics Costs

Landlocked countries encounter elevated trade barriers stemming from their geographical isolation, which necessitates routing exports and imports through one or more neighboring coastal states, thereby introducing dependencies on transit infrastructure, policies, and bilateral agreements. These transit dependencies often manifest as non-tariff barriers, including administrative delays, unofficial fees, and varying customs procedures at multiple borders, which inflate overall transaction costs beyond mere transportation expenses. Empirical analyses indicate that such barriers can double the time required for goods to reach international markets compared to coastal economies, exacerbating supply chain vulnerabilities.
Logistics costs for landlocked developing countries (LLDCs) are substantially higher, with unit logistics expenses on exports averaging 63% greater than those in coastal counterparts, driven by compounded freight charges, domestic distribution inefficiencies, and inadequate regional infrastructure. For instance, transport costs in many LLDCs exceed 10% of import values, compared to lower thresholds in maritime-access nations, partly attributable to poor road networks accounting for up to 60% of total transport expenditures. Recent data from 2025 reveal LLDCs face trade costs 1.4 times those of coastal countries and transport expenses 50% above the global average, with import wait times twice as long, hindering competitiveness in global value chains.
These elevated costs are further amplified by regional factors such as political instability in transit neighbors and limited bargaining power of smaller landlocked states, leading to asymmetric negotiations over access rights and fees. World Bank assessments underscore that LLDCs' lower scores on the Logistics Performance Index reflect not only internal deficiencies but also external transit bottlenecks, resulting in reduced export volumesLLDCs account for just 1.3% of global exports despite comprising over 7% of the world's population. Mitigation requires robust international transit agreements, yet persistent gaps in enforcement perpetuate these barriers, as evidenced by slower convergence in logistics efficiency metrics relative to coastal peers.

Strategies for Economic Mitigation

Landlocked countries address geographic trade disadvantages primarily through investments in transport infrastructure, bilateral and multilateral transit agreements, and economic diversification beyond export-dependent commodities. These efforts aim to reduce logistics costs, which can exceed 50% of export values in some cases compared to coastal peers, by fostering "land-linked" connectivity via corridors to seaports. Empirical analyses indicate that such infrastructure upgrades, including railways and dry ports, have lowered transit times by up to 30% in select African and Asian LLDCs participating in regional initiatives.
Regional integration plays a causal role in mitigation, as evidenced by participation in trade blocs like the Southern African Development Community (SADC), where harmonized customs procedures have cut border delays and transaction costs. For instance, Botswana has leveraged rail links to South African ports and diversified from diamonds into tourism and beef exports, achieving GDP per capita growth from $4,700 in 2000 to over $7,000 by 2023 through prudent resource management and policy stability, outperforming many coastal African neighbors. Similarly, Austria's post-World War II economic model emphasized high-value manufacturing, financial services, and EU single-market access, yielding a GDP per capita of approximately $52,000 in 2023 despite no seaboard, with Rhine-Danube waterway integrations compensating for land barriers.
Digital infrastructure and trade facilitation complement physical assets; UNCTAD reports highlight that e-commerce platforms and simplified regulations in countries like Kyrgyzstan have boosted small-scale exports by enabling air freight and digital payments, circumventing some road bottlenecks. However, success hinges on governance quality, as poor institutional frameworks in many LLDCs amplify geographic penalties, per World Bank assessments showing that rule-of-law improvements correlate more strongly with trade volume gains than infrastructure spending alone. Switzerland exemplifies this, sustaining affluence through innovation in precision engineering and neutral diplomacy securing transit rights, with exports reaching $500 billion annually via efficient Alpine rail networks.
Pipeline and air transport serve niche roles for high-value or urgent goods, as in Kazakhstan's oil exports via Caspian links, reducing reliance on volatile trucking routes. Overall, while no strategy fully erases landlocked costsestimated at 20-30% GDP drag in developing casestargeted policies have enabled outliers like European microstates and resource managers to achieve parity or superiority in development metrics.

Political and Geopolitical Implications

International Law on Transit Rights

The principle of freedom of transit for landlocked states originated in the Barcelona Convention and Statute on Freedom of Transit, adopted on April 20, 1921, under the League of Nations, which affirmed that states lacking sea access should enjoy unrestricted transit through neighboring territories for persons, goods, and means of transport, subject to agreements ensuring non-discrimination and reasonable regulations. This framework emphasized facilitation by transit states while allowing them to impose safeguards for security and public order, but it lacked binding enforcement, relying instead on bilateral or multilateral pacts to operationalize transit routes.
The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), adopted in 1982 and entering into force on November 16, 1994, codifies these rights in Part X, particularly Article 125, granting landlocked states the right of access to and from the sea to exercise UNCLOS provisions, including freedom of transit through transit states via all transport modes such as rail, road, and pipelines. Transit states must accord this access on terms of mutual agreement, prioritizing efficiency of means and economy of transport, without erecting barriers or discrimination based on flags of carriers; however, landlocked states bear transit costs and assume responsibility for supervisory or protective measures requested by transit states. Article 126 explicitly excludes any obligation on transit states to finance transit infrastructure, underscoring that rights are facilitative rather than prescriptive.
Supplementary instruments include the 1965 Convention on Transit Trade of Land-locked States (New York Convention), which entered into force on June 2, 1967, and mandates transit states to permit passage of traffic across their territories and waters without customs duties on goods in transit, while allowing reasonable charges for services rendered. These conventions intersect with World Trade Organization rules under GATT Article V, prohibiting unfavorable treatment of transit goods compared to direct imports. In practice, enforcement hinges on bilateral or regional agreements, as UNCLOS Article 125(2) requires negotiation of specific terms and modalities, with no automatic enforcement mechanism; disputes may invoke UNCLOS compulsory procedures under Part XV, though participation varies, as major powers like the United States have not ratified the convention. 
Examples of implementation include the 2002 agreement between Ethiopia and Djibouti, which specifies port utilization, transit fees, and infrastructure sharing to ensure reliable access via Djibouti's ports, demonstrating how bilateral pacts fill gaps in general treaty obligations. Similarly, regional frameworks, such as those under the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe or African Union, have facilitated corridor agreements, but persistent challenges arise from unilateral restrictions by transit states, highlighting the law's dependence on reciprocal cooperation rather than unilateral guarantees. Landlocked states' effective access thus remains vulnerable to geopolitical tensions, as treaties impose no penalty for non-compliance beyond diplomatic recourse.

Territorial Disputes and Access Claims

Landlocked countries often pursue territorial claims or legal challenges to secure sovereign access to the sea, driven by economic imperatives and historical grievances, though international law primarily guarantees transit rights rather than sovereignty over coastal territory. Under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), Article 125 affirms landlocked states' right to access the sea through neighbors' territory via agreement, but disputes arise when transit arrangements falter or historical losses prompt demands for restored sovereignty. Such claims can strain relations, as seen in cases where former coastal territories were ceded post-conflict.
Bolivia's dispute with Chile exemplifies a persistent territorial access claim. Bolivia lost its 400-kilometer Pacific coastline, including the ports of Cobija and Antofagasta, to Chile following the War of the Pacific (18791884), a conflict over nitrate-rich territories that ended with the Treaty of Peace and Friendship in 1904, formalizing Chile's annexation. Bolivia has since maintained diplomatic and legal efforts to regain sovereign access, arguing Chile incurred a binding obligation to negotiate during 20th-century exchanges, including a 1975 pledge by Chilean President Augusto Pinochet. In 2013, Bolivia instituted proceedings at the International Court of Justice (ICJ), which in 2018 ruled it lacked jurisdiction to compel negotiations, finding no legal duty under customary international law or prior treaties. Despite the ruling, Bolivia declared a "Day of Sea" annually on March 23 to rally domestic support, and bilateral talks remain stalled, with Chile rejecting sovereignty cession while offering improved transit facilities at ports like Arica.
Ethiopia, landlocked since Eritrea's independence in 1993 following a 30-year war, initially retained access to Eritrean ports like Assab under transitional agreements until the 19982000 border war severed ties. Eritrea permitted Ethiopian use of Assab from 1993 to 1997 via legal arrangements, but hostilities led to port closure, forcing Ethiopia to route over 90% of trade through Djibouti at elevated costs exceeding $1 billion annually in fees and logistics. Ethiopia has pursued alternative access, including a 2024 memorandum of understanding with Somaliland for a 50-year lease of 20 kilometers of coastline near Berbera, granting military basing rights in exchange for recognition of Somaliland's autonomya move Eritrea views as provocative, citing historical Ethiopian claims to Red Sea access predating colonial partitions. This arrangement risks escalating regional tensions without conferring full sovereignty, underscoring landlocked states' reliance on bilateral deals amid sovereignty disputes.
Paraguay, reliant on the Paraguay-Paran waterway for 80% of exports, faces access frictions rather than outright territorial claims, rooted in the 18651870 War of the Triple Alliance against Argentina, Brazil, and Uruguay, which devastated Paraguay but preserved riverine rights under the 1973 Treaty of Asuncin and subsequent accords. Recent disputes center on Argentina's imposition of tolls on the waterway in 2023, contested by Paraguay and Brazil as violating freedom of navigation principles in the 1967 River Plate Basin Treaty; a temporary truce was reached for 60-day negotiations, but Paraguay threatened Mercosur arbitration, highlighting how infrastructure fees can mimic territorial barriers for landlocked navigation. 
Serbia, accessing the Black Sea via the Danube, contends with border ambiguities from Yugoslavia's dissolution, particularly a meandering Danube stretch with Croatia where river shifts created disputed islets and terra nullius zones, complicating navigation and fostering micronation claims like Liberland since 2015. The unresolved demarcation, pending ICJ adjudication requested by Croatia in 2017, affects Serbia's fluvial trade routes, illustrating how fluvial boundaries exacerbate landlocked access vulnerabilities without direct sea claims.

Security Vulnerabilities and Conflicts

Landlocked countries possess inherent security vulnerabilities arising from their dependence on contiguous states for maritime access, which constrains military logistics, naval capabilities, and trade continuity during hostilities. This reliance exposes them to coercive measures such as transit restrictions or effective blockades by neighbors, potentially isolating them economically and militarily without direct sea routes for resupply or power projection.
Historical conflicts illustrate how territorial losses have entrenched landlocked status and perpetuated disputes over sea access. In the War of the Pacific (18791884), Bolivia allied with Peru against Chile over nitrate-rich border regions; defeat resulted in the cession of Bolivia's 400-kilometer Pacific coastline, rendering it landlocked and igniting century-long claims for sovereign corridor restoration. Similarly, Ethiopia's 1993 recognition of Eritrean independence severed its Red Sea outlet, fostering the 19982000 border war that killed over 80,000 and continues to fuel 2025 tensions, with Ethiopia accusing Eritrea of war preparations amid demands for renewed port access.
Contemporary cases underscore persistent risks from blockades exploiting landlocked geography. Armenia, doubly landlocked and reliant on Georgia and Iran for external links, faced Azerbaijans December 2022 blockade of the Lachin corridorthe sole route to Nagorno-Karabakhcausing fuel, food, and medicine shortages for 120,000 residents until Azerbaijans September 2023 offensive dissolved the enclave's defenses. Afghanistans multiply landlocked position has amplified invasion vulnerabilities, with transit dependencies on Pakistan enabling disruptions during Taliban resurgence and post-2021 instability, contributing to humanitarian crises affecting 24 million in need. Roughly one-third of the 32 landlocked developing countries grapple with active insecurity or conflict, often exacerbated by such geographic constraints.

International Support and Recent Developments

UN Programmes and Global Initiatives

The United Nations Office of the High Representative for the Least Developed Countries, Landlocked Developing Countries and Small Island Developing States (UN-OHRLLS), established in 2001, coordinates international support for the 32 landlocked developing countries (LLDCs), which comprise 16 in Africa, 10 in Asia, four in Europe, and two in South America. UN-OHRLLS monitors progress on LLDC-specific challenges, such as high trade costs due to geographical isolation, and promotes synergies between the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development and tailored programmes of action.
The Vienna Programme of Action (VPoA) for LLDCs, adopted by UN General Assembly resolution 70/210 on 22 December 2014 following the Second UN Conference on LLDCs in Vienna, outlined priorities including infrastructure development, international trade and trade facilitation, regional integration, and structural economic transformation to address poverty and underdevelopment exacerbated by lack of sea access. Implementation reviews, such as those by UNCTAD and UNDP, highlighted persistent barriers like inadequate transit infrastructure and dependency on neighboring coastal states, with South-South cooperation aiding some corridor improvements but falling short of full realization.
Succeeding the VPoA, the Awaza Programme of Action (APoA) for LLDCs covering 20242034 was adopted at the Third UN Conference on LLDCs in Awaza, Turkmenistan, in 2024, emphasizing enhanced productive capacities, value addition, export diversification, and digital transformation while drawing lessons from prior shortcomings in governance and sustainable financing. The APoA aligns with 13 of the 17 Sustainable Development Goals, prioritizing trade capacity building and barrier reduction to foster equitable market access. UN-OHRLLS continues to oversee its rollout, integrating efforts with agencies like UNCTAD for transit policy reforms and UNDP for human capital and climate resilience initiatives.

Regional Integration Efforts

Landlocked countries participate in regional economic communities and trade agreements to overcome isolation from maritime trade routes, emphasizing infrastructure corridors, harmonized customs procedures, and preferential access to neighboring ports. These efforts aim to lower transit costs, which can constitute up to 50% of total trade expenses for some landlocked developing countries (LLDCs), by promoting cross-border cooperation and value chain integration.
In Africa, where 16 countries are landlocked, the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA)signed in 2018 and with guided trade initiating in May 2022seeks to unify markets across 54 nations, enabling LLDCs to bypass bilateral dependencies through continental corridors. The United Nations Economic Commission for Africa (ECA) has supported 13 African LLDCs, including Botswana, Burkina Faso, and Ethiopia, in crafting national AfCFTA strategies focused on value-added manufacturing and trade facilitation. Regional economic communities like the Common Market for Eastern and Southern Africa (COMESA) and Southern African Development Community (SADC) complement this by funding projects such as the Northern Corridor, which reduced Kenya-Uganda border crossing times from days to hours by 2025. Countries like Eswatini demonstrate high integration, with 88% of exports directed to African markets via SADC linkages.
In Central and South Asia, the Central Asia Regional Economic Cooperation (CAREC) program unites 11 countriesincluding LLDCs Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Uzbekistan, Afghanistan, and Mongoliawith development partners to advance six transport corridors totaling over 12,000 kilometers. Launched in 2001 and coordinated by the Asian Development Bank, CAREC has facilitated $36 billion in infrastructure investments by 2024, particularly benefiting double-landlocked Uzbekistan by enhancing rail and road connectivity to ports in Pakistan and Georgia. The program emphasizes digital trade facilitation and SME value chains, with cross-border trade volumes along CAREC corridors growing 15% annually from 2019 to 2023.
The 2024-2033 Programme of Action for LLDCs, adopted at the Third UN Conference on LLDCs in August 2025, prioritizes deeper regional integration through economic corridors and multilateral transit pacts, building on Vienna Programme outcomes by targeting a doubling of LLDC exports via such frameworks. In Eastern Europe and the Caucasus, LLDCs like Armenia and Moldova engage in Eurasian Economic Union (EEU) customs unions for streamlined transit, though geopolitical tensions have limited gains compared to EU-integrated states like Hungary.

Distribution and Examples

By Continent and Region

Africa contains the highest number of landlocked countries, with 16 such states accounting for roughly 30% of the continent's 54 sovereign nations. These include Botswana, Burkina Faso, Burundi, Central African Republic, Chad, Eswatini, Ethiopia, Lesotho, Malawi, Mali, Niger, Rwanda, Uganda, Zambia, Zimbabwe, and South Sudan. Among them, Lesotho stands out as the only sovereign country completely surrounded by a single nation, South Africa, making it doubly landlocked in terms of international borders. Many of these African landlocked states are classified as least developed countries by the United Nations, with economies heavily reliant on overland transit through neighboring coastal states for trade.
Europe features 14 landlocked countries, primarily clustered in Central, Eastern, and the microstates of Southern Europe. These comprise Andorra, Austria, Belarus, Czech Republic, Hungary, Kosovo (whose independence is recognized by over 100 UN member states as of 2023), Liechtenstein, Luxembourg, Moldova, San Marino, Serbia, Slovakia, Switzerland, and Vatican City. Unlike their African counterparts, several European landlocked nations such as Austria, Switzerland, and Luxembourg rank among the world's most prosperous economies, benefiting from advanced infrastructure, stable governance, and integration into regional trade blocs like the European Union. Liechtenstein and San Marino are notable as doubly landlocked, surrounded exclusively by other landlocked countries.
Asia has 12 landlocked countries, spanning vast distances from the Himalayas to the steppes of Central Asia. The list includes Afghanistan, Armenia, Azerbaijan, Bhutan, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Laos, Mongolia, Nepal, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, and Uzbekistan. Kazakhstan is the largest landlocked country by area, covering 2.7 million square kilometers, while Bhutan and Nepal are enclave-like states nestled in mountainous terrain bordering India and China. These nations often navigate complex geopolitical dependencies for access to ports in the Indian Ocean, Caspian Sea (which is endorheic), or Pacific, with Central Asian states particularly affected by post-Soviet border dynamics.
South America includes only two landlocked countries: Bolivia and Paraguay. Bolivia lost its Pacific coast to Chile in the War of the Pacific (18791884), leading to ongoing disputes over maritime access, while Paraguay relies on the Paran River for export routes through Argentina and Brazil. Both face higher transport costs but have pursued infrastructure projects like Bolivia's investments in Chilean ports and Paraguay's riverine trade enhancements to mitigate isolation.
North America and Oceania have no sovereign landlocked countries, as geographic and historical factorssuch as expansive coastlines and island geographiesprevent such configurations. Globally, the 44 recognized landlocked states represent about 20% of all countries, with the majority in developing regions.

De Facto and Disputed Landlocked Entities

De facto landlocked entities are territories that exercise effective control over their claimed areas without full international recognition as sovereign states, resulting in no direct maritime access and reliance on neighboring countries for trade and transit. These differ from recognized landlocked countries by their disputed status and limited diplomatic relations, often complicating access to ports under international law such as the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea. Common examples include breakaway regions in post-Soviet spaces, where ethnic conflicts led to self-proclaimed independence but persistent isolation from global sea routes.
Kosovo, declared independent from Serbia on February 17, 2008, functions as a de facto state with its own government, currency, and military, but lacks universal recognition, with only 114 of 193 UN member states acknowledging it as of 2023. Entirely landlocked and bordered by Serbia, Montenegro, Albania, and North Macedonia, Kosovo depends on transit agreements for exports via Albanian ports like Durrs, though Serbia's non-recognition has historically blocked Danube River access, exacerbating economic vulnerabilities. Its partial status underscores disputes over self-determination versus territorial integrity, with Russia and allies vetoing UN membership bids.
Transnistria, also known as Pridnestrovian Moldavian Republic, separated from Moldova following a 1992 ceasefire after armed conflict, controlling a narrow strip along the Dniester River between Moldova and Ukraine since May 1990. Unrecognized by any UN member, it operates independently with Russian military presence supporting its administration, yet remains landlocked without sea access, routing goods through Ukrainian ports like Odesa when relations allow, though post-2022 tensions have intensified isolation. Economic reliance on smuggling and outdated Soviet-era industries highlights the challenges of de facto statehood without formal transit rights.
South Ossetia, proclaiming independence from Georgia in 1992 amid ethnic violence, maintains de facto control over its territory north of the Caucasus Mountains, recognized solely by Russia, Venezuela, Nicaragua, and Nauru following the 2008 Russo-Georgian War. Bordered by Georgia and Russia, it lacks coastline and depends on Russian rail links to Black Sea ports like Sochi for trade, with GDP per capita under $2,000 as of 2020 estimates reflecting dependency and sanctions evasion. Ongoing disputes with Georgia prevent integration into broader Eurasian transport corridors.
The Nagorno-Karabakh Republic, self-declared in 1991 from Azerbaijani territory, existed as a de facto landlocked entity until its dissolution on September 20, 2023, after Azerbaijan's military offensive displaced over 100,000 ethnic Armenians. Enclaved within Azerbaijan and reliant on the Lachin corridor for supplies from Armenia, it never achieved recognition and faced blockades exacerbating humanitarian crises, including a 2022-2023 siege. Its collapse illustrates how disputed landlocked status can lead to absorption by the parent state, nullifying prior autonomy claims.
Other disputed landlocked territories, such as the West Bank under partial Palestinian Authority control since the 1990s Oslo Accords, involve fragmented governance amid Israeli oversight, with no independent sea access and reliance on Jordanian routes, though not constituting a de facto state due to absent full self-rule. These entities collectively demonstrate how lack of recognition amplifies landlocked disadvantages, fostering reliance on patron states or informal arrangements vulnerable to geopolitical shifts.
