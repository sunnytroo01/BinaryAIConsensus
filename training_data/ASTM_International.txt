ASTM International
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 ASTM International is a globally recognized leader in the development and delivery of voluntary consensus standards to industries worldwide. Founded in 1898 as the American Society for Testing and Materials by chemist Charles B. Dudley, the organization was renamed ASTM International in 2001 to reflect its global scope. Today, it operates as one of the world's largest international standards developing organizations, with over 13,000 standards that enhance product quality, improve safety, facilitate market access, and build consumer confidence across sectors such as construction, manufacturing, and consumer goods.
The mission of ASTM International centers on supporting industries, governments, and societies through the creation of technical standards via an open, consensus-driven process that ensures balanced input from diverse stakeholders. This process involves more than 35,000 technical experts and professionals from over 140 countries, organized into more than 150 technical committees that address specific industry needs. Membership is open to individuals and organizations for a modest fee, providing opportunities to contribute to standard development while gaining access to knowledge, networking, and professional development resources.
ASTM's global impact is evident in its alignment with World Trade Organization principles, which promotes fair trade and reduces technical barriers to international commerce. The organization maintains offices in Belgium, Canada, China, Peru, Singapore, the United Arab Emirates, and Washington, D.C., to support regional needs and foster international cooperation. Beyond standards, ASTM offers services including certification through the Safety Equipment Institute, technical training programs, and proficiency testing to ensure compliance and competence in various fields. Over its more than 125-year history, ASTM standards have played a pivotal role in advancements ranging from early 20th-century infrastructure like highways and automobiles to modern innovations in materials science and sustainability.

Overview

Mission and Purpose

ASTM International was founded in 1898 as a voluntary consensus-based organization dedicated to developing technical standards for materials testing, emerging from a meeting of seventy engineers and scientists in Philadelphia led by chemist Charles B. Dudley to address inconsistencies in railroad steel quality. This founding ethos emphasized collaborative development of standards through stakeholder agreement, prioritizing technical accuracy and practical application over regulatory enforcement to foster reliable testing methods in emerging industries.
Today, ASTM International's mission centers on advancing voluntary consensus standards to positively impact public health, safety, consumer confidence, and overall quality of life by enhancing industry performance and enabling global collaboration among diverse experts. The organization focuses on creating market-relevant standards that promote innovation, facilitate international trade, and support sustainability initiatives, all without the compulsion of legal mandates, allowing flexibility for adoption across sectors like construction, manufacturing, and environmental management. This approach relies on the input of technical committees to ensure standards evolve with technological and societal needs, bridging gaps between producers, users, and regulators.
In 2024, ASTM International introduced its brand promise, "Advancing Standards. Transforming Markets," underscoring its commitment to leveraging standards as a catalyst for economic growth and societal benefit through ongoing innovation and global outreach.

Scope and Global Impact

ASTM International develops and publishes over 13,000 standards organized across more than 80 volumes, spanning diverse fields such as aerospace, construction, biomedical engineering, petroleum products, and consumer goods. These standards address technical requirements, testing methods, and specifications that ensure quality, safety, and performance in materials and products used worldwide. By covering over 90 industry sectors, ASTM's work supports innovation and reliability in critical applications, from aircraft components to medical devices and everyday consumer items.
The organization's standards have a profound global impact, facilitating trade and regulatory alignment in more than 150 countries through partnerships with over 125 national and regional standards bodies via Memoranda of Understanding (MoUs). This international cooperation enhances market access, reduces technical barriers, and promotes economic efficiency, with ASTM standards referenced approximately 8,700 times in regulations and trade agreements globally. According to an OECD case study, ASTM's voluntary consensus process contributes to international regulatory cooperation by harmonizing specifications and lowering conformity assessment costs.
ASTM also advances sustainability through dedicated standards for recycled materials, environmental testing, and resource-efficient practices, particularly in construction and plastics sectors. For instance, Committee E60 on Sustainability develops guidelines that promote circular economy principles, such as evaluating the recyclability of plastics and assessing environmental impacts of building materials. These efforts align with global goals for sustainable development, helping industries minimize waste and resource use.
Access to ASTM's standards is enhanced by digital platforms like ASTM Compass, which provides 24/7 online availability to the full catalog, including historical versions dating back to 1932 for research and compliance purposes. This tool integrates standards with related publications, enabling efficient workflow for users in over 140 countries.

History

Founding and Early Years

ASTM International, originally known as the American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM), was founded in 1898 in Philadelphia by Charles B. Dudley, a chemist with the Pennsylvania Railroad Company. The organization emerged in response to frequent rail failures caused by inconsistent steel quality during the rapid expansion of the U.S. railroad network, with an initial group of 70 engineers, scientists, and industry professionals convening to standardize testing methods for materials. Dudley's leadership emphasized the need for uniform specifications to enhance safety and reliability in transportation infrastructure, marking the society's early commitment to collaborative technical standards.
In its formative years, ASTM concentrated on developing protocols for materials testing, particularly in the railroad and manufacturing sectors, where variability in steel properties posed significant risks. The society's first standard, ASTM A1 on "Specification for Carbon Steel Tee Rails," was issued in 1901, providing guidelines for chemical composition and physical testing to ensure rail durability and prevent accidents. This milestone established a precedent for consensus-based standardization, with early efforts expanding to include tensile strength tests and other mechanical evaluations essential for industrial applications. By the 1910s and 1920s, ASTM's work had broadened to encompass metals, cement, and construction materials, fostering greater uniformity across emerging industries.
A key development in the interwar period was the establishment of the Cement Reference Laboratory in 1929 at the National Bureau of Standards (now NIST), sponsored by ASTM's Committee C01 on Cement. This facility served as a central hub for verifying testing accuracy and proficiency among laboratories, addressing inconsistencies in cement quality assessments that affected building and infrastructure projects. As the organization grew, it published annual proceedings and standards books, solidifying its role in promoting reliable materials science practices.
Leading into World War II, ASTM accelerated its contributions by issuing emergency standards in 1942 to support wartime production, including specifications for critical alloys, fuels, and protective materials needed for military equipment. These provisional standards enabled rapid scaling of manufacturing while maintaining quality, demonstrating the society's adaptability to national exigencies. This period of intense activity laid the groundwork for ASTM's post-war expansion into a broader international framework.

Key Milestones and Modern Expansion

In 1962, ASTM standards played a pivotal role in the construction of Seattle's Space Needle for the World's Fair, where the structure's steel legs were built to comply with ASTM specifications for high-strength, low-cost carbon steel, demonstrating the organization's growing influence in architectural and engineering projects.
During the 1970s, ASTM expanded into environmental standards in response to key U.S. legislative milestones like the Clean Air Act and the establishment of the Environmental Protection Agency, developing consensus-based methods for testing and analyzing soil, water, and air quality to address emerging ecological concerns.
By 1995, ASTM relocated to a state-of-the-art global headquarters in West Conshohocken, Pennsylvania, enhancing operational efficiency and launching an early website to facilitate broader access to its standards. This move supported the organization's increasing international focus, culminating in 2001 with its renaming to ASTM International, a change that formally acknowledged the worldwide participation in its standards development process and the global adoption of its technical documents.
To further its global reach, ASTM International established regional offices starting with its first in Latin America in 2002, followed by an office in Beijing, China, in 2005, and a new office in Singapore in 2021, enabling localized support for standards implementation across diverse markets. In 2020, amid the COVID-19 pandemic, the organization provided no-cost public access to over two dozen standards related to personal protective equipment, such as masks, gowns, and sanitizers, aiding global manufacturing and response efforts. These developments underscored ASTM International's adaptability, as highlighted in its 2023 celebration of the 125th anniversary of its founding, which reflected on its evolution from a U.S.-centric body to a key player in international standardization. In 2024, NIST awarded ASTM International $15 million to establish the Standardization Center of Excellence (SCOE), enhancing U.S. engagement in international standards for emerging technologies.

Organizational Structure

Membership and Participation

ASTM International boasts over 36,000 volunteer members from more than 160 nations, representing a wide array of industries, governments, academia, and consumers who contribute to the development of standards. Membership is open to anyone interested in standardization, fostering a global community dedicated to advancing technical knowledge and innovation.
The organization offers several membership types tailored to different levels of engagement. Participating membership, priced at $115 annually, provides voting rights in technical committees and direct involvement in standards development. Organizational membership, at $400 per year, supports business expansion through enhanced visibility and networking opportunities. Informational membership, also $115 yearly, grants access to updates on standards activities without voting privileges. Student membership is free, offering educational resources and pathways to future involvement in the standards process.
Key benefits of membership include complimentary access to one volume of the Annual Book of ASTM Standards, a subscription to the Standardization News magazine, and discounts on publications, conferences, and training events. Members also gain networking opportunities through events and online platforms, enabling professional growth and collaboration across sectors.
Participation occurs primarily through engagement with ASTM's 150 technical committees and over 2,100 subcommittees, where members propose, develop, and revise standards. Individuals can join via the My Committees online portal, attending virtual or in-person meetings, symposia, and workshops to contribute expertise.
ASTM International emphasizes diversity, equity, inclusion, and belonging (DEIB) to ensure equitable participation among its global membership, integrating these principles into governance, staff practices, and community outreach to amplify diverse perspectives and drive inclusive innovation.

Governance and Technical Committees

ASTM International is governed by a Board of Directors comprising 23 members, including a chair, vice chairs, past chairs, and directors serving three-year terms, who provide strategic leadership and oversight of the organization's mission to develop consensus standards and innovative services. The board's executive committee exercises powers between full sessions, while standing committees such as the Finance and Audit Committee supervise financial operations. Senior staff, led by President Andrew G. Kireta Jr., support operational execution, with key roles including the Vice President of Technical Committee Operations and the Vice President of Global Policy. Annual board books detail member biographies, committee assignments, and strategic priorities, such as enhancing global standards implementation and organizational vitality.
The core of ASTM's operations lies in its more than 150 technical committees, each focused on a specific technical domain and supported by over 2,100 subcommittees addressing narrower topics. These committees engage over 35,000 volunteers from industry, government, and other sectors, who collaborate to draft, review, and ballot on standards proposals. Committee structure includes elected officersa chair, vice chairs, and secretaryserving biennial terms, along with an executive subcommittee for administrative oversight and technical subcommittees for detailed work. Participation is open to qualified members, who contribute through electronic balloting, peer review, and discussions to ensure standards reflect practical needs.
Consensus principles underpin committee activities, emphasizing openness, transparency, and a balance of interests among producers, users, and general interest groups, with annual reviews to maintain equilibrium. Balloting requires at least two-thirds affirmative votes from the main committee and handles negative votes through written justifications and opportunities for resolution. Public review periods allow external input via announcements in Standardization News and the ASTM website, promoting broad stakeholder involvement. Members receive training on these procedures through resources provided by ASTM headquarters to uphold procedural integrity.
Oversight is provided by bodies such as the Committee on Standards (COS), which reviews and approves standards for publication, ensures compliance with consensus processes, and handles appeals on procedural matters. The Committee on Technical Committee Operations (COTCO) develops and enforces regulations governing committee bylaws, operations, and balance requirements. These mechanisms collectively ensure quality assurance and alignment with ASTM's bylaws, as authorized by the Board of Directors.

Standards Development

Consensus Process

The consensus process at ASTM International is a structured, multi-stage methodology designed to ensure that standards reflect broad agreement among diverse stakeholders, fostering transparency, balance, and technical rigor in their development. This process begins with the submission of a proposal for a new standard or revision, typically initiated by an individual member, organization, or stakeholder group identifying a need not addressed by existing standards. Proposals are submitted to the relevant technical committee or subcommittee, where they are evaluated for scope, relevance, and alignment with ASTM's mission; if approved, a work item is registered in ASTM's digital system to track progress.
Following proposal acceptance, the core development occurs through task group work, where ad-hoc groups of volunteerscomprising experts from producers, users, consumers, and general interest categoriescollaborate to draft the standard. These task groups operate under subcommittee oversight and utilize digital tools such as ASTM SpecBuilder, an online platform that enables secure, real-time collaboration, version control, and document management to streamline drafting and revisions. The process emphasizes balance of interests, requiring classified committees to ensure that the combined number of voting members from user, consumer, and general interest categories equals or exceeds the number of producer members to prevent dominance by any single group and ensure fair input.
Once a draft is prepared, it advances to the subcommittee ballot stage, where all eligible members vote electronically over a minimum 30-day period. Approval requires at least 60% ballot return and two-thirds affirmative votes from those returned; negative votes must include substantive written justifications and are resolved through discussion, revision, or motions deeming them not persuasive or not related (requiring another two-thirds vote). If unresolved negatives persist, a confirming ballot may be issued. Successful subcommittee ballots proceed to the main committee ballot, following identical requirements for return and approval, providing broader scrutiny across the entire technical committee.
The final stage involves ASTM approval through society review, which includes a public comment period posted on the ASTM website for at least 30 days, allowing non-members to submit comments that are forwarded to the committee for consideration. The Committee on Standards then conducts a procedural review to verify compliance with regulations, leading to final approval and publication if all criteria are met. This open-access public review enhances global participation and accountability.
To maintain relevance, ASTM mandates periodic reviews of published standards every five years, during which committees assess need for reaffirmation, revision, or withdrawal via the same balloting process. Standards inactive for eight years are automatically considered for withdrawal, with notice published in Standardization News; withdrawn standards remain available for three years with rationale provided, allowing users to reference historical versions if needed. This systematic review ensures standards evolve with technological and industry changes while upholding the consensus-driven integrity of the process.

Types and Publication of Standards

ASTM International develops six primary types of standards, each serving distinct purposes in technical documentation and application. Test methods provide definitive procedures for obtaining, analyzing, and reporting measurements or test results, including statements on precision and bias. Specifications outline explicit requirements for materials, products, or systems, such as physical, mechanical, or chemical properties, often incorporating referenced test methods to verify compliance. Practices offer detailed instructions for performing specific operations or processes, like sample preparation or maintenance procedures, without producing a numerical test result. Guides compile information on options, alternatives, or best practices to increase awareness and aid decision-making, without mandating specific actions. Terminology standards define terms, symbols, and abbreviations essential for clear communication within a field. Classifications systematically group materials, products, or systems based on shared characteristics, such as composition or performance attributes.
These standards are organized and published in the Annual Book of ASTM Standards, comprising over 80 volumes that cover more than 13,000 active standards across diverse technical areas, from materials science to environmental testing. The volumes are structured by subject matter, facilitating targeted access for users in specific industries. In addition to ASTM's own standards, the collection includes adoptions and joint publications from international and national bodies, such as the American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials (AASHTO) for transportation infrastructure and the American Petroleum Institute (API) for oil and gas sectors, enhancing global interoperability. Historical standards, which are superseded versions no longer active but retained for reference, and withdrawn standards, discontinued by sponsoring committees due to obsolescence or replacement, are also accessible within this framework to support legacy applications and research.
Publication occurs through multiple digital and print platforms to ensure broad dissemination. The primary access point is ASTM Compass, a subscription-based online platform offering full-text standards, advanced search tools, and integration with related publications like journals and manuals. The ASTM Digital Library provides on-demand access to standards alongside over 1,700 eBooks, 60,000 papers, and chapters dating back to 1932, encompassing the organization's full historical archive. Redline versions, which highlight changes between current and previous editions using color-coded markup, are available via Compass to streamline review of revisions. Print editions of the Annual Book volumes remain an option for those preferring physical copies.
ASTM maintains currency by approving over 1,000 new or revised standards annually, reflecting evolving technologies and stakeholder needs; for instance, in 2024, the organization published 148 new standards and revised 1,706 existing ones. These updates follow the consensus development process outlined in ASTM's procedural guidelines and are promptly incorporated into publication platforms, with weekly online refreshes and email alerts available through the ASTM Standards Tracker tool.

Standards Compliance and Application

Certification and Training

ASTM International offers proficiency testing programs through its Cement and Concrete Reference Laboratory (CCRL), which traces its roots to the Cement Reference Laboratory established in 1929. These programs, operational since 1936, distribute proficiency samples for materials such as Portland cement, concrete, masonry, pozzolan, and steel reinforcing bars to nearly 1,600 laboratories worldwide, enabling participants to evaluate and improve their testing performance against ASTM standards. CCRL also conducts on-site laboratory inspections to monitor equipment, procedures, and compliance, supporting accreditation efforts by organizations following ISO/IEC 17025 and other quality systems. Overall, ASTM maintains over 60 ISO/IEC 17043-accredited proficiency testing programs across industries like petroleum, metals, textiles, and plastics (with accreditation valid until May 2029), helping laboratories maintain accreditation and benchmark their results globally.
In addition to proficiency testing, ASTM provides extensive training to educate users on the application of its standards, with 258 e-learning courses available through its learning management system (as of November 2025). These offerings include over 700 training products in formats such as self-paced online modules, live virtual webinars, and in-person seminars, tailored for industries including construction, metals, energy (petroleum), and environmental testing. Training emphasizes practical implementation of ASTM standards, awarding continuing education units (CEUs), professional development hours (PDHs), and CPD hours to support professional growth.
ASTM's certification programs verify personnel competency in performing, recording, and reporting ASTM standards through proctored exams and performance evaluations, issuing digital badges and certificates upon successful completion. Examples include credentials for additive manufacturing operators qualified in metal powder bed fusion processes, developed in collaboration with the Additive Manufacturing Center of Excellence (AM CoE). These programs incorporate ISO-compliant audits and quality management assessments to ensure adherence to international standards like ISO 17025 for laboratory validation.
To further support standards implementation, ASTM provides resources such as webinars on emerging topics, interactive e-learning modules for flexible access, and customized enterprise training delivered on-site or virtually. These tools integrate with certification pathways, enabling broader industry adoption by equipping professionals with verifiable skills in standards compliance.

Industry Adoption and Enforcement

ASTM International standards are developed as voluntary consensus documents, yet they are frequently incorporated by reference into mandatory regulations, transforming them into enforceable requirements. In the United States, over 2,215 ASTM standards (as of 2011) have been integrated into the Code of Federal Regulations, providing technical specifications for federal agencies in areas such as safety, materials testing, and environmental protection. Internationally, the World Trade Organization's Agreement on Technical Barriers to Trade (TBT) encourages the use of such international standards as a basis for regulations to minimize trade barriers, with ASTM standards playing a key role in facilitating global market access. While adoption in the European Union is more aligned with harmonized EN standards, ASTM specifications are occasionally referenced alongside them in directives, particularly for transatlantic regulatory convergence in sectors like energy and materials.
In commercial and procurement contexts, ASTM standards are routinely specified in contracts to ensure quality assurance and interoperability across supply chains. For instance, in the aerospace industry, they are embedded in supplier agreements for material testing and component performance, helping to mitigate risks in high-stakes manufacturing processes. Similarly, in healthcare procurement, ASTM standards such as those for personal protective equipment (e.g., ASTM F1671 for barrier properties) are mandated in contracts for medical devices and supplies to verify safety and efficacy during pandemics or routine operations. This contractual reliance promotes consistency in product specifications and reduces disputes over compliance in global transactions.
Enforcement of ASTM standards often involves third-party audits and inspections to verify adherence, particularly when referenced in building codes or product regulations. For example, U.S. building codes frequently cite ASTM specifications for materials like concrete and steel, with local authorities conducting audits to ensure compliance during construction; non-conformance can lead to project halts or rework. Liability challenges arise in legal disputes, where failure to meet incorporated ASTM standards may constitute negligence, as seen in premises liability cases involving slip-and-fall incidents on surfaces not compliant with ASTM guidelines for walkways (e.g., ASTM F1637). Courts have upheld these standards as reliable authority under evidence rules, increasing accountability for manufacturers and builders. Such enforcement mechanisms, supported by training programs, help maintain public safety but can pose administrative burdens for smaller entities navigating audits.
Global harmonization efforts further enhance the enforceability of ASTM standards through alignments with ISO and IEC frameworks, enabling mutual recognition agreements that streamline international trade. ASTM has established cooperation agreements with ISO and IEC, allowing joint development and adoption of standards to avoid duplication and support reciprocal acceptance in regulatory contexts. These partnerships, including memoranda of understanding, facilitate the use of ASTM standards in international conformity assessments, such as those under the International Laboratory Accreditation Cooperation (ILAC) mutual recognition arrangement, which promotes one-test acceptance worldwide. This alignment reduces non-tariff barriers and bolsters enforcement consistency across borders.

Specialized Centers and Initiatives

Additive Manufacturing Center of Excellence (AM CoE)

The Additive Manufacturing Center of Excellence (AM CoE), established by ASTM International in July 2018, serves as a collaborative hub integrating industry, academia, and government stakeholders to accelerate the development and adoption of additive manufacturing (AM) technologies through targeted research, standards advancement, and ecosystem building. Initial funding came from U.S. government sources, including a $300,000 investment from America Makes supplemented by in-kind contributions, enabling the launch of early initiatives to bridge research and standardization gaps in AM. Over time, the AM CoE has secured over $10 million in partnerships and grants from agencies like the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) and the Department of Defense, fostering a global network of more than 100 team members across the Americas, Europe, and Asia.
Key activities of the AM CoE include conducting over 30 research and development (R&D) projects since inception, focused on critical areas such as materials characterization, process optimization, and qualification/certification methodologies to support AM industrialization. These projects, prioritized through annual Requests for Ideas and Calls for Projects, generate technical data that directly informs ASTM Committee F42 on Additive Manufacturing Technologies, addressing more than 30 identified standards gaps. Complementing R&D, the center offers workforce development programs, including the Professional Certificate Course in Additive Manufacturing (PCCAM), a comprehensive training initiative that equips participants with core technical knowledge on AM practices, alongside role-specific courses for designers, quality engineers, and safety managers to meet industry demands for skilled personnel.
The AM CoE also organizes high-impact events and consortia to promote collaboration and knowledge exchange, such as the AM Innovation and Standards Summit held at Formnext 2025 in Frankfurt, Germany, which convened experts from government, industry, and standards bodies to discuss cross-sector certification challenges and shared standards. A flagship effort is the Consortium for Materials Data and Standardization (CMDS), launched in 2022, which unites organizations across the AM value chain to standardize materials data requirements, curate shared high-pedigree databases, and develop tools like physics-based models for predictive simulations.
In November 2021, ASTM International acquired Wohlers Associates, a leading provider of AM market intelligence, integrating its expertise into the AM CoE to enhance strategic insights and global outreach. This acquisition brought the renowned annual Wohlers Report under AM CoE oversight, offering detailed analyses of AM market trends, including a 9.1% industry growth to $21.9 billion in 2024, driven by advancements in materials, software, and services. The integration supports AM CoE's mission by providing data-driven forecasts on 3D printing adoption, informing R&D priorities and standards development for emerging applications.
In November 2025, the AM CoE announced several advancements, including seven global initiatives at Formnext 2025 to scale additive manufacturing, such as expanding the AM Certification Program in Europe with first certifications for KSB and launching the Additive Manufacturing for Semiconductors project in partnership with Additive Center B.V. to enhance quality assurance. Additionally, it deepened its partnership with America Makes by introducing a Platinum Membership tier and launching the WATCHLIST initiative, supported by NASA, to accelerate in-process monitoring and control technologies.

Standardization Center of Excellence (SCOE)

In October 2024, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) awarded ASTM International a $15 million grant over five years to establish the Advancing Standardization Center of Excellence for Critical and Emerging Technologies (ASCET CoE), aimed at bolstering U.S. leadership in international standards development for critical and emerging technologies. The center officially launched in January 2025. Led by ASTM International, the initiative involves collaboration with partner standards developing organizations (SDOs), including the American National Standards Institute (ANSI), Association for Advancing Automation (A3), American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME), Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE), UL Standards & Engagement, CSA Group, Accuris, and Nexight Group. This public-private partnership represents NIST's first such effort to enhance U.S. engagement in global standardization processes essential for market access and innovation.
The ASCET CoE's primary goals focus on pre-standardization activities to support small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) in participating in standards development, particularly those from underrepresented sectors. It also emphasizes workforce training programs to cultivate a new generation of standards professionals capable of addressing complex technological challenges. Additionally, the center will launch pilot programs in partnership with industry and NIST to identify gaps in existing standards and accelerate the creation of industry-driven norms for emerging technologies. In May 2025, ASCET CoE held its inaugural stakeholder workshop in Bethesda, Maryland, attended by nearly 200 participants, followed by the release of a workshop report on September 9, 2025, outlining pathways for U.S. leadership in critical and emerging technology standardization.
Key focus areas include critical and emerging technologies such as artificial intelligence (AI), quantum technology, and biotechnology, with additive manufacturing serving as one illustrative example among broader applications. To facilitate collaboration, the ASCET CoE will establish a data-sharing hub providing stakeholders with tailored tools, resources, and information to streamline standards coordination. These efforts align directly with the U.S. National Standards Strategy of 2023, which prioritizes standards as a foundation for economic competitiveness, national security, and innovation in global markets.
