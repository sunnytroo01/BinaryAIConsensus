Here is exactly how to negotiate your salary, step by step, whether you are negotiating a new job offer or asking for a raise at your current company.

Before the conversation. Research the market rate for your role in your area. Use sites like Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, Payscale, LinkedIn Salary, and the Bureau of Labor Statistics. Talk to recruiters and peers in your industry. You need to know the range: the low end, the median, and the high end for someone with your experience, skills, and location. This data is your foundation. Without it, you are guessing.

Know your number. Based on your research, determine three numbers. Your target: the salary you believe reflects your fair market value. Your walk-away: the minimum you would accept. Your aspiration: the highest number you could reasonably justify. Never share your walk-away number. Always anchor at or above your target.

When negotiating a new job offer, let the employer make the first offer if possible. When they ask "What are your salary expectations?", try to redirect: "I would love to learn more about the total compensation package before discussing a specific number. What is the budgeted range for this role?" If they insist, give a range based on your research with your target at the bottom of the range: "Based on my research and experience, I would expect a salary in the range of ninety to one hundred and ten thousand dollars."

When you receive an offer, do not accept immediately, even if it is great. Say: "Thank you so much for the offer. I am very excited about this opportunity. I would like to take a day or two to review the full package. Can I get back to you by Thursday?" This is expected and professional. Nobody loses an offer because they asked for forty-eight hours to think about it.

The counter-offer. When you come back, express enthusiasm first, then make your case with evidence. "I am thrilled about this role and I am confident I can make a significant impact on your team. Based on my eight years of experience in this field, my track record of exceeding revenue targets by twenty percent, and the market rate for this position in this area, I was hoping we could discuss a base salary of ninety-five thousand. I believe this reflects the value I would bring and is consistent with what comparable candidates are earning."

Negotiate the full package, not just salary. If the employer cannot move on base salary, ask about signing bonuses, equity or stock options, annual bonus targets, additional vacation days, flexible work arrangements, professional development budgets, relocation assistance, or earlier performance review dates. Many of these items are more flexible than base salary because they do not affect the permanent cost structure in the same way.

When asking for a raise at your current company, timing matters. Ask after a major accomplishment, during a performance review cycle, or when the company is doing well financially. Do not ask when the company just had layoffs or missed its targets.

Build your case with evidence. Document your accomplishments with specific, quantifiable results. "I led the product launch that generated three hundred thousand dollars in new revenue." "I reduced customer churn by fifteen percent through the retention program I designed." "I mentored three junior team members who all received promotions within eighteen months." Numbers are persuasive. Vague claims are not.

The conversation itself. Schedule a dedicated meeting. Do not ambush your manager in the hallway. Start by expressing your commitment to the company and the team. Then present your case: your accomplishments, the market data, and your request. "I love working here and I am committed to the team's success. Over the past year, I have taken on significant additional responsibilities and delivered results that I am proud of. Based on my contributions and the current market rate for my role, I would like to discuss a salary adjustment to eighty-five thousand dollars."

If they say no, do not get angry or threaten to leave. Ask: "Can you help me understand what I would need to accomplish to earn a salary increase? I would like to create a plan with specific milestones so we can revisit this in six months." This turns a rejection into a roadmap and demonstrates professionalism and maturity.

Common mistakes. Never say "I need more money because my rent went up" or "my friend at another company makes more." Your personal expenses and your friends' salaries are irrelevant to your employer. The only relevant arguments are your value to the company and the market rate for your skills. Never give an ultimatum unless you are genuinely prepared to follow through. Never lie about competing offers. And never apologize for asking. Salary negotiation is a normal, expected part of professional life. You are not being greedy. You are advocating for fair compensation for the value you provide.
