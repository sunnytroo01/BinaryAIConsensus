Consciousness is the most profound mystery in all of science and philosophy. It is the subjective experience of being aware, the felt quality of seeing red, tasting chocolate, hearing music, or feeling pain. It is what it is like to be you, the inner movie that plays behind your eyes from the moment you wake up to the moment you fall asleep, and perhaps even during your dreams. Consciousness is the one thing you know with absolute certainty exists, because the very act of doubting it proves it, as Descartes recognized with his famous declaration "I think, therefore I am." And yet, despite being the most familiar phenomenon in your entire existence, consciousness remains the least understood.

The hard problem of consciousness, articulated by philosopher David Chalmers in 1995, asks why there is subjective experience at all. We can explain how the brain processes visual information: photons enter the eye, stimulate the retina, generate electrical signals that travel along the optic nerve to the visual cortex, where they are processed into representations of edges, shapes, colors, and objects. But none of this explains why there is an experience of seeing. Why does the processing of visual information feel like something? Why is there a subjective quality to the experience of redness that is different from the subjective quality of blueness? A hypothetical zombie, a being that is physically identical to a human in every way but has no subjective experience, no inner life, no consciousness, would process visual information in exactly the same way. The hard problem is explaining why we are not zombies.

Several major theories of consciousness are currently being investigated. Global Workspace Theory, proposed by Bernard Baars, suggests that consciousness arises when information is broadcast widely across the brain through a global workspace. According to this theory, unconscious processes occur in specialized, localized brain regions. Consciousness occurs when information from these specialized regions is selected and broadcast to many other regions simultaneously, making it available for attention, memory, language, and decision-making. The theory explains why we are conscious of only a tiny fraction of the information our brains process: only the information that wins the competition for access to the global workspace enters consciousness.

Integrated Information Theory, developed by Giulio Tononi, proposes that consciousness is identical to a specific type of information processing. The theory defines a mathematical quantity called phi, which measures the degree to which a system integrates information, meaning how much the whole system is more than the sum of its parts. According to IIT, any system with a phi greater than zero has some degree of consciousness, and the higher the phi, the greater the consciousness. This leads to the counterintuitive implication that consciousness is not unique to brains. Any system that integrates information, including certain types of artificial systems, could in principle be conscious.

Higher-Order Theories suggest that consciousness requires not just processing information but having thoughts about those thoughts. You are conscious of seeing red not because your brain processes red wavelengths but because you have a meta-representation, a thought about the thought of seeing red. This meta-cognitive layer, the ability to be aware of your own awareness, is what distinguishes conscious experience from mere information processing.

Predictive Processing theories suggest that consciousness arises from the brain's constant effort to predict its own sensory inputs. The brain is not a passive receiver of information from the world. It is an active prediction machine that constantly generates models of what it expects to perceive and then compares those predictions against incoming sensory data. Consciousness, according to this view, is the felt experience of the brain's best current model of reality, updated moment by moment as predictions are confirmed or corrected.

The neural correlates of consciousness, the specific patterns of brain activity that correspond to conscious experience, have been extensively studied. Consciousness appears to require the integrated activity of large networks spanning multiple brain regions, particularly in the cerebral cortex. Damage to small, localized brain regions can eliminate specific aspects of consciousness, such as the ability to recognize faces or the ability to perceive color, while leaving other aspects intact. General anesthesia, which disrupts long-range communication between brain regions while leaving local processing intact, eliminates consciousness entirely, suggesting that it is the integration of information across the brain, not local processing, that gives rise to conscious experience.

The question of animal consciousness is both scientifically and ethically important. Most scientists agree that mammals and birds have some form of conscious experience, based on their behavioral responses to pain, their emotional expressions, and the similarity of their brain structures to ours. The question becomes more difficult for fish, reptiles, insects, and other organisms with simpler nervous systems. The Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness, signed by a group of prominent scientists in 2012, stated that many non-human animals possess the neurological substrates that generate consciousness and that the absence of a neocortex does not preclude the experience of affective states.

The question of machine consciousness is among the most pressing philosophical questions of the twenty-first century. As artificial intelligence systems become more sophisticated, the question of whether they could ever be conscious, whether there could be something it is like to be a large language model or a robot, becomes increasingly urgent. If consciousness requires biological neural tissue, then no machine can be conscious regardless of its computational capabilities. If consciousness arises from information processing of a certain type, then in principle, a sufficiently complex and appropriately organized artificial system could be conscious. We currently have no way to determine which of these possibilities is correct, and the implications of getting the answer wrong in either direction are profound.

Consciousness is the lens through which we experience everything. Every fact, every relationship, every sensation, every thought, every emotion, every moment of beauty or pain or wonder is mediated by consciousness. Without it, the universe would exist but there would be no one to know it. Consciousness is the universe experiencing itself, and the question of how and why it exists is the deepest question that a conscious being can ask.
