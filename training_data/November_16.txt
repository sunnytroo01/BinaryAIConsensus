November 16

1x
 November 16 is the 320th day of the year (321st in leap years) in the Gregorian calendar, with 45 days remaining until the end of the year.
Significant events associated with this date include the capture of Inca emperor Atahualpa by Spanish conquistador Francisco Pizarro on November 16, 1532, at Cajamarca, Peru, an ambush that enabled the subsequent Spanish conquest of the Inca Empire despite the Incas' numerical superiority. On November 16, 1776, during the American Revolutionary War, British forces under General Howe captured Fort Washington on Manhattan, resulting in the surrender of approximately 2,870 American troops and marking a major setback for the Continental Army. In 1945, the constitution of the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO) was signed in London by representatives of 37 countries, establishing the agency to promote collaboration in education, science, and culture toward peace.
The date also marks the launch of NASA's third and final crewed Skylab mission on November 16, 1973, which conducted extended scientific experiments in low Earth orbit aboard the United States' first space station. Since 1995, November 16 has been observed annually as the International Day for Tolerance, proclaimed by United Nations General Assembly Resolution 51/95 to advance mutual understanding and respect among peoples.

Events

Pre-1600

In 13 AD, Roman general Tiberius, future emperor, celebrated a triumph in Rome for his victories in Illyricum and Germania, marking a significant military honor under Augustus.
On November 16, 534 AD, the second and final edition of the Corpus Juris Civilis, Emperor Justinian I's comprehensive codification of Roman law including the Codex Justinianus, Digesta, and Institutiones, was promulgated, influencing legal systems for centuries.
In 951, forces of the Southern Tang kingdom, commanded by general Bian Hao, captured the Chu kingdom's capital Jiangzhou (modern Jiujiang), leading to Chu's annexation and the flight of its ruler Ma Xiguang, consolidating Southern Tang power during the Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period.
On November 16, 1491, in vila, Spain, nine Jews and converts were publicly burned at the stake, convicted in the ritual murder case of the "Holy Child of La Guardia," a notorious blood libel accusation amid rising anti-Jewish persecution under the Inquisition.
In 1519, Spanish settlers under Diego Velzquez relocated the city of Havana to its current site on the north coast of Cuba, seeking better defense and to escape lowland flooding and mosquito-borne diseases that plagued the original southern settlement founded earlier that year.

16011900

On November 16, 1632, Swedish forces commanded by King Gustavus II Adolphus engaged and defeated the Imperial army led by Albrecht von Wallenstein at the Battle of Ltzen in Saxony, Germany, during the Thirty Years' War; despite the Swedish victory, which halted the Imperial advance and preserved Protestant gains, Adolphus was killed in the fog-shrouded melee after becoming separated from his guards. The battle involved approximately 19,000 Swedish-Saxon troops against 12,000 Imperial forces, resulting in heavy casualties on both sides, with estimates of 6,000 Swedish and 5,000 Imperial dead or wounded.
On November 16, 1776, British and Hessian troops under General Sir William Howe captured Fort Washington on Manhattan Island from Continental Army defenders during the New York and New Jersey campaign of the American Revolutionary War, seizing 43 artillery pieces, supplies, and approximately 2,870 prisoners while suffering fewer than 100 casualties compared to American losses of over 50 killed and 90 wounded. The fort's fall, defended by about 2,800 men under Colonel Robert Magaw, exposed the Hudson River to British control and contributed to George Washington's subsequent retreat across New Jersey.
On November 16, 1821, American trader William Becknell and his party arrived in Santa Fe, New Mexico, after departing Franklin, Missouri, in September, marking the first successful overland commercial expedition on what became known as the Santa Fe Trail following Mexico's independence from Spain, which opened trade opportunities previously restricted by Spanish policy. Becknell's group sold goods profitably and returned via a southern route, establishing the trail's viability for future merchants transporting textiles, hardware, and other items in exchange for silver, furs, and mules, with annual caravans soon exceeding 100 wagons by the 1830s.

1901present

1907 The U.S. Congress passed the Oklahoma Enabling Act, leading to Oklahoma's admission as the 46th state on this date, doubling the number of states west of the Mississippi River and incorporating over 1.4 million residents, including significant Native American populations previously under tribal governance.
1933 President Franklin D. Roosevelt established full diplomatic relations with the Soviet Union, ending a 16-year U.S. non-recognition policy initiated after the 1917 Bolshevik Revolution, amid efforts to counter the Great Depression through expanded trade and counterbalance fascist threats in Europe.
1940 Nazi authorities in occupied Poland sealed the Warsaw Ghetto, confining approximately 400,000 Jewsabout 30% of the city's populationinto 1.3 square miles, initiating systematic isolation, starvation, and disease that killed over 100,000 residents by mid-1942 before deportations to extermination camps began.
1945 The United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO) was established with the signing of its constitution by 20 countries in London, aiming to promote peace through international collaboration in education, science, and culture in the postwar era; it now has 194 member states.
1959 The Broadway musical The Sound of Music, composed by Richard Rodgers with lyrics by Oscar Hammerstein II and book by Howard Lindsay and Russel Crouse, premiered at the Lunt-Fontanne Theatre in New York City, running for 1,443 performances and becoming one of the longest-running musicals of its time before its 1965 film adaptation.
1965 The Soviet Union launched Venera 3, the first spacecraft to reach another planet, which impacted Venus on March 1, 1966, though it failed to transmit data due to loss of contact during atmospheric entry; the mission weighed 960 kg and marked a milestone in planetary exploration.
1973 U.S. President Richard Nixon signed the Trans-Alaska Pipeline Authorization Act, approving construction of a 800-mile pipeline from Prudhoe Bay oil fields to the port of Valdez to transport up to 2 million barrels of oil daily, addressing the 1973 oil crisis and becoming operational in 1977 despite environmental concerns.
1988 Benazir Bhutto was elected Prime Minister of Pakistan, becoming the first woman to head a Muslim-majority country and serving until 1990, amid promises of democratic reforms following her party's victory in general elections after General Zia-ul-Haq's death.
2000 U.S. President Bill Clinton became the first sitting American president to visit Vietnam since the end of the Vietnam War in 1975, meeting with Vietnamese leaders in Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City to promote bilateral ties, trade, and resolution of lingering issues like POW/MIA accounting.

Holidays and Observances

Religious Observances

In the Catholic liturgical calendar, November 16 is the feast day of Saint Gertrude the Great, a 13th-century German Benedictine nun and mystic born around 1256 in Eisleben, who entered the monastery at Helfta at age five and later experienced profound spiritual visions that emphasized devotion to the Sacred Heart of Jesus, influencing later Catholic piety. Her writings, including The Herald of Divine Love, detail ecstatic encounters with Christ and advocate intercessory prayer for souls in purgatory, earning her recognition as a Doctor of the Church by Pope Benedict XVI in 2010 for her theological contributions on divine love and Trinitarian mysticism.
The date also commemorates Saint Margaret of Scotland (c. 10451093), an English princess who married King Malcolm III after fleeing the Norman Conquest, reforming Scottish church practices by founding monasteries, promoting education and charity, and exemplifying personal asceticism amid royal duties; she was canonized in 1250 for her piety and influence on monastic life.
Other saints observed include Saint Agnes of Assisi (11971253), younger sister of Saint Clare who co-founded the Poor Clares and upheld Franciscan poverty vows despite familial opposition, and Saint Edmund Rich (c. 11741240), Archbishop of Canterbury noted for his defense of ecclesiastical independence against secular encroachment. In Eastern Orthodox traditions following the Julian calendar, observances may align with different dates, but fixed Gregorian commemorations are less prominent. No major fixed observances occur in Judaism, Islam, or Hinduism on this Gregorian date, as their calendars are lunisolar or lunar.

Secular and International Observances

The International Day for Tolerance is observed annually on November 16, as proclaimed by the United Nations General Assembly in resolution 51/95 adopted on December 12, 1996, which invited member states to commemorate the date in line with UNESCO's 1995 Declaration of Principles on Tolerance. This observance emphasizes tolerance as "respect, acceptance and appreciation of the rich diversity of our world's cultures, our forms of expression and ways of being human," aiming to foster mutual understanding and combat intolerance, prejudice, and discrimination through education and awareness initiatives. UNESCO, which coordinates related activities, promotes events such as workshops, campaigns, and publications to highlight the societal benefits of tolerance in preventing conflicts and promoting human rights.
When November 16 coincides with the third Sunday of the monthas it does in years like 2025the date also marks the World Day of Remembrance for Road Traffic Victims, designated by United Nations General Assembly resolution 60/5 on October 26, 2005. This global observance honors the approximately 1.19 million annual road traffic deaths and the tens of millions injured, primarily advocating for improved road safety measures, victim support, and prevention of future incidents through international cooperation. Activities include memorials, vigils, policy discussions, and data-driven campaigns by organizations like the World Health Organization, which report road traffic injuries as a leading cause of death for individuals aged 529 years.
