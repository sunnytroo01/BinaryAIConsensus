Diapause
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Diapause is a genetically programmed state of dormancy in which the development, growth, and metabolic activity of many animals are temporarily suspended in anticipation of adverse environmental conditions, such as extreme temperatures, drought, or food scarcity. This endogenous response, distinct from environmentally induced quiescence, enables organisms to synchronize their life cycles with favorable seasons, enhancing survival and reproductive success. Diapause is widespread across invertebrates like insects, nematodes, and crustaceans, as well as some vertebrates including fish and mammals.
Diapause manifests at specific life stages, categorized primarily as embryonic, larval (or nymphal/pupal), and adult (often reproductive). Embryonic diapause occurs in early developmental stages, as seen in the eggs of silkworms (Bombyx mori) and brine shrimp (Artemia franciscana), where embryos halt progression until conditions improve. Larval diapause involves arrested juveniles, such as the dauer larvae of the nematode Caenorhabditis elegans or pupae of the flesh fly Sarcophaga crassipalpis, which enter a stress-resistant state to endure harsh periods. Adult diapause typically suppresses reproduction, exemplified by the reproductive arrest in fruit flies (Drosophila melanogaster) under short day lengths or in the Colorado potato beetle (Leptinotarsa decemlineata) during summer dormancy.
The induction, maintenance, and termination of diapause are regulated by a combination of environmental cues (e.g., photoperiod, temperature) and internal physiological factors. Hormonally, it often involves downregulation of juvenile hormone and insulin/insulin-like growth factor signaling (IIS), which promotes metabolic depression and stress tolerance through pathways like FoxO transcription factors. Molecular mechanisms include epigenetic modifications, such as histone methylation (e.g., H3K27me3), and repression of growth regulators like mTOR and Myc, as observed in annual killifish (Austrofundulus limnaeus) during embryonic diapause. These processes not only conserve energy but also confer resistance to stressors like desiccation and hypoxia, underscoring diapause's role in evolutionary adaptation and ecological persistence.

Definition and Characteristics

Core Definition

Diapause is a genetically programmed physiological state characterized by the suspension of development or reproduction in response to predictable adverse environmental conditions, such as seasonal changes in temperature or photoperiod. This dormancy allows organisms, particularly in the animal kingdom, to survive periods of environmental stress by halting growth and metabolic processes in a coordinated manner, rather than merely slowing metabolism as a passive response. Unlike immediate reactions to stress, diapause is anticipatory, initiated by environmental cues perceived well before the onset of unfavorable conditions, enabling proactive preparation for survival.
A defining feature of diapause is its reversibility; once triggered, the state persists until specific termination cuessuch as changes in day length or temperatureare detected, at which point development resumes. It occurs at specific, species-dependent life stages, ensuring synchronization with seasonal cycles. For instance, embryonic diapause arrests early development within eggs, as seen in the silkworm Bombyx mori, where embryos halt just before hatching to overwinter. Larval diapause suspends growth in immature stages, exemplified by the codling moth Cydia pomonella, whose larvae cease feeding and development during winter. In adults, imaginal diapause typically prevents reproduction, as in the convergent lady beetle Hippodamia convergens, where diapausing females accumulate fat reserves but delay ovarian maturation until spring.
The phenomenon was first described in the 1730s by the French naturalist Ren-Antoine Ferchault de Raumur, who observed honey bees entering a state of winter numbness in their hives, marking an early recognition of insect dormancy. The term "diapause," derived from the Greek diapausis meaning pause, was coined in 1893 by William Morton Wheeler to describe arrested egg development in katydids. Over time, research expanded its scope beyond insects to other invertebrates and vertebrates, highlighting its role as a conserved adaptive strategy across taxa.

Distinction from Other Dormancy Forms

Diapause is distinguished from other forms of dormancy primarily by its anticipatory and genetically programmed nature, which allows organisms to prepare for predictable seasonal challenges in advance. In contrast, quiescence represents a direct, reversible suppression of metabolic activity in response to immediate adverse environmental conditions, such as extreme temperatures or desiccation, without any preparatory phase or endogenous timing mechanism. Quiescence lacks the hormonal mediation and developmental arrest characteristic of diapause, resuming activity as soon as the stress is alleviated, often in a matter of hours or days. For example, embryonic quiescence in Aedes aegypti mosquito eggs occurs under low humidity or high temperatures, halting development until conditions improve, whereas diapause in aphids is triggered by shortening photoperiods to synchronize with winter.

Hibernation, a form of seasonal torpor primarily in mammals and birds, differs from diapause by emphasizing temperature regulation and energy conservation through drastic reductions in body temperature (often below 10C) and metabolic rate (less than 0.1 ml O g hr), rather than a complete halt in development. Unlike the developmental arrest in diapause, hibernation allows for periodic arousals and is more focused on surviving cold rather than anticipating it through prior physiological reprogramming. For instance, black bears enter hibernation in response to declining temperatures and food scarcity, maintaining some organ function, while diapause in the gypsy moth involves embryonic arrest programmed by photoperiod. Aestivation, conversely, is a summer dormancy adapted to hot, arid conditions, often involving quiescence-like responses such as cocoon formation for desiccation tolerance, but lacking the predictive programming of diapause. In snails, aestivation reduces activity during drought, resuming with rain, whereas summer diapause in certain mosquitoes (e.g., Anopheles coluzzii) is endogenously timed to avoid peak heat.
Cases of overlap exist, particularly where quiescence follows diapause as a transitional state; for example, post-diapause insects may enter temporary quiescence if conditions remain unfavorable, combining programmed arrest with reactive suppression. This distinction underscores diapause's role as a strategic, forward-looking adaptation in primarily invertebrate life cycles.

Occurrence Across Organisms

In Insects

Diapause is a widespread adaptation among insects, enabling survival through periods of environmental stress such as winter cold, summer drought, or seasonal food shortages by suspending development and reducing metabolic activity. This dormancy state has contributed significantly to the evolutionary success of insects across diverse latitudes and habitats, allowing them to bridge unfavorable conditions without permanent migration or extinction.
Prominent examples illustrate diapause's role in insect life cycles. In monarch butterflies (Danaus plexippus), late-summer generations enter adult reproductive diapause, halting egg development and facilitating long-distance migration to overwintering sites in Mexico, where they remain inactive for months. The migratory locust (Locusta migratoria) exhibits embryonic diapause, where eggs arrest development in response to short photoperiods, preventing hatching during harsh dry seasons and synchronizing outbreaks with favorable monsoon periods. Similarly, female mosquitoes like Culex pipiens undergo adult reproductive diapause in autumn, arresting ovarian maturation and seeking sheltered overwintering sites to endure cold temperatures.
Ecologically, diapause plays a critical role in insect persistence and distribution. It permits range expansion into temperate zones by allowing species to overwinter successfully, as seen in the rapid establishment of invasive Aedes albopictus mosquitoes in North America through evolved diapause traits enhancing cold tolerance. Additionally, diapause synchronizes insect generations with seasonal resource availability, preventing mismatched development that could lead to high mortality and ensuring population stability across annual cycles.
Insect-specific adaptations highlight diapause's flexibility, occurring at variable life stages tailored to species ecologyembryonic in locusts, pupal in some moths, or adult in beetles and mosquitoes. This variability often integrates with other strategies, such as migration in diapausing monarchs to reach milder climates or aestivation (summer diapause) in tropical mosquitoes like Anopheles species to survive dry periods by entering dormancy in moist refuges. Insects thus exemplify the prototypical model for diapause phases, from induction to termination, informing broader studies on dormancy.

In Non-Insect Animals

Diapause occurs in various non-insect animals, often as an embryonic or larval adaptation to endure environmental stresses such as desiccation or seasonal droughts. In crustaceans, particularly the brine shrimp Artemia franciscana, encysted embryos enter a profound diapause state that enables extreme tolerance to desiccation, allowing cysts to remain viable for years under dry conditions. These cysts feature multi-layered shells and molecular mechanisms, including late embryogenesis abundant (LEA) proteins, that protect against anhydrobiosis while suspending development. Similarly, in copepods like Calanus finmarchicus, diapause involves dormancy in late-stage juveniles, facilitating overwintering in deep waters with suppressed metabolism.
In nematodes, the model organism Caenorhabditis elegans enters the dauer larval stage as a facultative diapause response to unfavorable conditions such as food scarcity, high population density, or elevated temperatures. This stress-resistant form halts development, reduces metabolism, and enhances survival for weeks to months until conditions improve, regulated by insulin signaling and TGF- pathways.
In fish and amphibians, diapause manifests primarily in embryonic or larval stages to survive periodic water scarcity. African annual killifish, such as the turquoise killifish Nothobranchius furzeri, exhibit embryonic diapause where developing embryos enter suspended animation within egg capsules, tolerating complete desiccation for up to eight months during the dry seasonaestivation-like adaptation unique to these species. This state halts organogenesis and metabolic activity, resuming only upon rehydration, and has evolved convergently at least six times in killifish lineages.
Embryonic diapause is rare in mammals and birds but serves as a reproductive strategy for timing birth to favorable conditions. In roe deer (Capreolus capreolus), blastocysts enter obligate diapause for about five months post-fertilization, delaying implantation independent of environmental cues and distinct from hibernation. Kangaroos and other marsupials exhibit facultative embryonic diapause, where delayed implantation occurs during lactation, suspending development for weeks to months until resources improve. In birds, temperature-induced embryonic diapause has been observed in domestic chickens, where cooling halts early embryo development, mediated by PKC-NF-B-IRF1 signaling, to synchronize hatching with optimal seasonal timing.
Compared to the well-studied insect diapause, non-insect forms remain underexplored, with significant gaps in understanding molecular triggers beyond descriptive physiology. Recent post-2020 research has advanced knowledge of the genetic basis in copepods through transcriptomic analyses, revealing profound gene expression shiftssuch as upregulation of lipid storage and downregulation of reproductive pathwaysduring diapause in species like Calanus finmarchicus, though functional validation via gene editing remains limited. These studies highlight over 90% of the transcriptome exhibiting annual rhythms tied to dormancy phases.
Diapause-like states across these diverse non-insect taxa demonstrate evolutionary convergence, where independent origins in arthropods, vertebrates, and others yield similar survival benefits against predictable adversities like drought or cold, often involving conserved metabolic suppression despite varying developmental stages. This convergence underscores diapause as a flexible adaptation enhancing reproductive success in fluctuating environments.

Phases of Diapause

Induction and Preparation

Diapause induction is initiated by the perception of environmental signals, most commonly shortening photoperiods detected via sensory organs such as compound eyes and ocelli in insects. These signals serve as predictive cues for impending seasonal stress, triggering a commitment to diapause that arrests development and redirects resources toward survival preparations. In many species, this process involves neural integration of photoperiodic information, often mediated by clock genes like period and cryptochrome2, leading to irreversible developmental decisions within specific sensitive periods.
Following induction, the preparation phase entails an accumulative buildup of physiological reserves to endure dormancy. Organisms increase lipid storage for sustained energy, synthesize stress-protective proteins such as heat shock proteins, and progressively reduce metabolic rates to conserve resources. This phase typically spans days to weeks, depending on the species and environmental conditions, allowing for enhanced tolerance to stressors like cold through the accumulation of cryoprotectants.
Molecular mechanisms underlying these processes include targeted changes in gene expression, with upregulation of diapause-specific genes responsible for trehalose synthesis to bolster cellular protection against desiccation and freezing. In the cold-adapted fly Chymomyza costata, for instance, short-day photoperiods in third-instar larvae downregulate genes for 20-hydroxyecdysone biosynthesis during induction, while preparation involves elevated expression of trehalose and proline pathways over approximately six weeks.
A representative example of photoperiodic induction occurs in aphids such as Acyrthosiphon pisum, where short-day lengths (e.g., 10:14 light:dark) prompt maternal individuals to shift from parthenogenetic to sexual reproduction, preparing overwintering embryos for diapause through slowed morphological development and cryoprotectant accumulation.

Initiation and Maintenance

Initiation of diapause marks the entry into a state of irreversible developmental or reproductive arrest, primarily orchestrated by hormonal shifts that suppress key regulatory pathways. In many insect species, declining levels of juvenile hormone (JH) and ecdysteroids signal this transition, preventing further morphogenesis and reproductive maturation. For instance, low JH titers in adult insects like the lady beetle Coccinella septempunctata trigger diapause by inhibiting ovarian development, while suppressed ecdysteroid synthesis in the prothoracic glands halts pupal-adult transformation. These hormonal changes build on preparatory accumulations of energy reserves, ensuring the organism is equipped for the arrest.
During the maintenance phase, diapause is sustained through profound physiological suppression, including halted morphogenesis and a drastic reduction in metabolic activity. Oxygen consumption typically drops to 10-20% of nondiapausing rates, reflecting a hypometabolic state that conserves stored lipids and carbohydrates as primary energy sources. This metabolic depression is enzymatically regulated, with downregulated pathways for oxidative phosphorylation and upregulated lipid storage mechanisms, as seen in diapausing larvae of Helicoverpa armigera. Concurrently, enhanced stress tolerance develops, particularly cold-hardening via the synthesis of cryoprotectants like glycerol and antifreeze proteins, which lower the supercooling point and prevent ice crystal formation in freeze-avoiding species.
The duration of diapause maintenance varies widely, from weeks in short-cycle species to months or even years in prolonged cases, with facultative diapause permitting flexible extension based on ongoing environmental monitoring to optimize survival. In the Chinese oak silkworm Antheraea pernyi, pupal diapause exemplifies this phase, where hormonal suppression maintains the pupal state, arresting adult emergence and associated physiological processes.

Termination and Post-Diapause Development

Diapause termination is primarily triggered by external environmental cues that signal the arrival of favorable conditions, such as prolonged exposure to low temperatures (chilling) or shifts to longer photoperiods, which counteract the developmental arrest imposed during maintenance. In many insects, an endogenous physiological clock also contributes, ensuring a minimum duration of dormancy before responsiveness to these cues develops, preventing premature reactivation during transient environmental improvements. Hormonal reactivation plays a central role in this process; for instance, the resumption of juvenile hormone (JH) synthesis and release, often alongside ecdysteroids like 20-hydroxyecdysone, breaks the endocrine blockade, stimulating downstream pathways such as insulin signaling to restore developmental progression.
Following termination, many diapausing organisms enter a brief post-diapause quiescence, a secondary, environmentally responsive dormancy that acts as a safeguard against suboptimal conditions, delaying full development until sustained favorable signals are confirmed. This phase, distinct from true diapause by its direct sensitivity to external stimuli without an internal timer, typically lasts days to weeks and involves low metabolic activity, with enzymatic adjustments like increased trehalase activity to mobilize stored energy reserves in preparation for resumption. In insects such as flesh flies (Sarcophaga crassipalpis), this quiescence ensures coordinated exit from pupal diapause only under verified spring-like cues.
Resumption of post-diapause development involves a rapid ramp-up in metabolic rates, gene expression, and physiological processes, leading to the completion of the interrupted life stage, such as hatching from eggs, pupation, or adult reproductive maturation. This phase is marked by heightened activity in pathways like the PI3K/Akt signaling and ecdysone biosynthesis, facilitating tissue growth and energy mobilization from lipid and glycogen stores. A representative example is seen in mosquito eggs of Culex pipiens, where diapause termination requires chilling at approximately 5C for 46 weeks, akin to vernalization in plants, after which embryos hatch upon warming, resuming embryonic development within days.

Regulation Mechanisms

Environmental Cues

Environmental cues play a pivotal role in signaling the onset, maintenance, and termination of diapause in insects, allowing them to anticipate and respond to seasonal changes. Among these, photoperiodthe relative lengths of day and nightserves as the primary predictive cue, acting as a reliable timer for impending unfavorable conditions in temperate regions. Short-day photoperiods, characterized by long nights (typically more than 12-14 hours of darkness), induce diapause in many species, such as the European corn borer (Ostrinia nubilalis), where scotophases of 10-14 hours trigger larval diapause. Conversely, long-day photoperiods with short nights promote development or terminate diapause, as seen in spring-adapted insects like the bug Poecilocoris lewisi, which enters summer diapause under lengthening days. The critical photoperiod, often defined as the day length inducing diapause in 50% of the population, is genetically determined and varies by species and latitude.
Temperature interacts closely with photoperiod to fine-tune diapause responses, often modulating the sensitivity to day length through thermoperiodsdaily temperature fluctuations. Low temperatures reinforce short-day induction, while thresholds above 15-20C can prevent or terminate diapause; for instance, in the cricket Teleogryllus commodus, egg diapause ends at 20C. In the silkworm Bombyx mori, a critical night length of about 10 hours under short-day conditions induces embryonic diapause, but thermoperiods alter this threshold, with warmer cycles reducing the inductive effect. Chilling at 5-7.5C for weeks to months is required for termination in many overwintering species, such as Hyalophora cecropia needing 10 weeks at 5C.
Moisture availability and food resources act as supplementary cues, particularly in arid or variable environments, where drought or scarcity signals diapause to conserve energy. Low humidity induces developmental arrest in some species, while rehydration post-rain terminates it. Similarly, the sorghum stem borer (Chilo partellus) enters larval diapause during dry spells, resuming development after rainfall replenishes soil moisture. Food scarcity or declining quality, such as senescing host plants, prompts diapause in species like the Colorado potato beetle (Leptinotarsa decemlineata), which arrests as adults when foliage quality drops. These cues often integrate with photoperiod and temperature during the induction phase to ensure synchronized responses across populations.

Internal Physiological Controls

The internal physiological controls of diapause primarily involve the neuroendocrine system, which integrates signals to regulate developmental arrest through hormonal and neural pathways. In insects, brain neurosecretory cells release prothoracicotropic hormone (PTTH) that stimulates the prothoracic glands to produce ecdysone, a steroid hormone essential for molting and development; during diapause, reduced PTTH activity leads to low ecdysone levels, maintaining quiescence. The corpora allata, another key endocrine organ, synthesize juvenile hormone (JH), which in non-diapause conditions promotes larval growth but is suppressed during diapause induction to prevent progression to the next developmental stage. These components form a coordinated pathway where brain neurosecretory cells act as central integrators, responding to upstream inputs like environmental cues to modulate glandular activity and hormone titers.
Key hormones further execute diapause regulation by altering cellular processes. In moths such as Bombyx mori, diapause hormone (DH), a neuropeptide secreted from subesophageal ganglion neurosecretory cells, induces embryonic diapause by suppressing ecdysteroid synthesis in developing eggs, thereby inhibiting ecdysis and developmental progression. Insulin signaling pathways, conserved across insects, play a central role in metabolic reprogramming during diapause; reduced insulin-like peptide activity activates the transcription factor FOXO, promoting stress resistance, lipid storage, and suppressed growth to sustain the arrested state. These hormonal mechanisms ensure that diapause is not merely a passive response but an actively maintained physiological program.
At the genetic level, diapause involves differential expression of clock genes and transcription factors that underpin endogenous rhythmicity and developmental timing. Clock genes such as period (per) and timeless (tim) exhibit altered expression patterns during diapause, contributing to photoperiodic timing by forming transcriptional-translational feedback loops that synchronize internal states with seasonal cues. Diapause-specific transcription factors, including those downstream of insulin signaling like FOXO, regulate target genes for metabolic arrest. Recent studies in the 2020s have highlighted epigenetic modifications, such as histone methylation, as regulators of reproductive diapause in Drosophila melanogaster, independent of juvenile hormone pathways.
In non-insect animals, such as nematodes and vertebrates, diapause regulation shares conserved elements like downregulation of insulin/insulin-like growth factor signaling (IIS) and activation of FoxO, but lacks insect-specific hormones like PTTH and JH; for example, in annual killifish embryos, repression of mTOR and Myc pathways maintains diapause.
Feedback loops involving endogenous timers prevent premature termination of diapause, ensuring survival through adverse periods. These timers, often modeled as hourglass or oscillator mechanisms linked to circadian components, accumulate physiological changes over timesuch as progressive hormone titer adjustments or gene expression shiftsthat override initial arrest signals only after a critical duration, independent of external stimuli. In this way, the internal controls provide robustness, with neuroendocrine and genetic elements forming interconnected loops that maintain diapause until conditions favor resumption.

Variations in Diapause

Tropical and Aestival Forms

In tropical environments, particularly in regions with distinct wet-dry seasons, insects often enter a form of diapause known as aestivation to survive prolonged dry periods. This dormancy is typically induced by the onset of drought and is terminated by the arrival of rains, which replenish moisture essential for post-diapause development. For instance, larvae of the sorghum stem borer (Chilo partellus) diapause within dry plant stalks for 6-8 months during the dry season, pupating only after exposure to spring rains in regions like India and Africa. Similarly, the African maize stem borer (Busseola fusca) exhibits larval diapause in Cte d'Ivoire that ends with intensive rainfall, while the sorghum head borer (Coniesta ignefusalis) in Ghana requires continuous rain to terminate its dry-season dormancy. Butterflies such as Hypolimnas bolina in tropical Australia also demonstrate this pattern, with diapause breaking upon the first significant rains. These examples highlight how tropical diapause synchronizes with erratic rainfall cycles rather than fixed photoperiods, allowing insects to bridge unfavorable dry intervals.
Aestival diapause, a summer-specific dormancy, enables insects to avoid extreme heat, desiccation, or food scarcity in both tropical and subtropical zones, contrasting with the overwintering diapause prevalent in temperate regions. In lady beetles (Coccinellidae), such as Hippodamia convergens, adults enter reproductive aestivation during hot, dry summers when aphid prey is scarce, aggregating in sheltered sites to conserve energy. Another example is the nine-spotted lady beetle (Coccinella novemnotata) in central California, where spring-generation adults aestivate for several months before resuming activity in autumn. In collembolans like Sminthurus viridis and mites such as Halotydeus destructor in Australia, aestivation is triggered by declining food quality as host plants mature and dry out, often under long-day conditions. These forms of diapause are generally shorter in durationspanning weeks to monthscompared to temperate overwintering, and support multiple generations per year by permitting rapid post-diapause reproduction when conditions improve.
Unlike temperate diapause, which is predominantly temperature-driven and synchronized to predictable winter cold via short-day photoperiods, tropical and aestival forms rely more on humidity deficits, rainfall cues, and host availability, with less emphasis on low temperatures. This adaptability allows for facultative responses to variable seasonal stresses, enabling multiple annual cycles in stable but periodically harsh environments. Climate change may disrupt these patterns by altering seasonal cues like rainfall and temperature, potentially desynchronizing diapause with environmental conditions and host availability. For example, altered precipitation in wet-dry tropics could delay rain-induced emergence, leading to mismatched food resources and increased mortality.

Facultative Versus Obligate Diapause

Diapause in insects can be classified as facultative or obligate based on its responsiveness to environmental conditions and genetic programming. Facultative diapause is an optional developmental arrest induced by specific environmental cues, such as short photoperiods or low temperatures, allowing insects to continue development under favorable conditions and enter diapause only when survival demands it. This flexibility enables multiple generations per year in suitable environments, as the decision to diapause is not fixed but modulated by external signals like day length.
In contrast, obligate diapause is a genetically predetermined halt in development that occurs mandatorily at a specific life stage or generation, irrespective of environmental conditions, often resulting in a strictly univoltine life cycle with one generation per year. This form ensures precise synchronization with seasonal cycles, preventing mismatched timing that could lead to exposure to adverse conditions. Populations may exhibit hybrid forms, where both phenotypes coexist, reflecting genetic variation that influences diapause incidence across geographic ranges.
Evolutionarily, facultative diapause promotes opportunism by permitting additional generations in extended favorable periods, though it requires reliable cue detection and may incur costs from variable environmental predictability. Obligate diapause, while limiting reproductive output to one cycle annually, provides reliability in synchronizing emergence with optimal seasons, trading flexibility for reduced risk of off-season mortality. These trade-offs shape population dynamics, with facultative strategies favoring adaptability in fluctuating climates and obligate ones enhancing survival in predictable but harsh environments.
Representative examples illustrate these distinctions. In aphids, such as Aphis fabae, facultative diapause is induced by short-day photoperiods (8-12 hours), leading to sexual forms that overwinter, while long-day conditions (17-18 hours) promote asexual reproduction and prevent diapause. The Colorado potato beetle (Leptinotarsa decemlineata) exhibits facultative adult diapause triggered by short photoperiods and declining host quality, allowing 1-3 generations depending on latitude and climate, with some variants influenced by pesticide exposure altering induction thresholds. Conversely, the European corn borer (Ostrinia nubilalis) univoltine race undergoes obligate larval diapause, ensuring overwintering regardless of conditions to synchronize with corn growth cycles. In moths like the cinnabar moth (Tyria jacobaeae), obligate pupal diapause lasts approximately 270 days, enforcing a single annual generation.
