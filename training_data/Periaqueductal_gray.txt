Periaqueductal gray
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The periaqueductal gray (PAG), also referred to as the central gray, is a midline structure of gray matter in the midbrain that encircles the cerebral aqueduct of Sylvius, extending rostrocaudally from the posterior commissure to the locus coeruleus. It originates embryologically from the mesencephalon during the third to fourth week of gestation and is composed primarily of neuronal cell bodies with diverse neurotransmitter profiles, including GABAergic, serotonergic, and opioid-containing neurons. The PAG serves as a critical integrative hub for sensory, emotional, and autonomic processing, particularly in the modulation of pain, defensive behaviors, and physiological responses to stress.
Anatomically, the PAG is organized into four longitudinal columnsdorsomedial (dmPAG), dorsolateral (dlPAG), lateral (lPAG), and ventrolateral (vlPAG)each with distinct connectivity and functional specializations identifiable through techniques like resting-state fMRI. The dmPAG and dlPAG are associated with active defensive responses, such as fight-or-flight behaviors and fear/anxiety processing, via connections to the amygdala, prefrontal cortex, and hypothalamus. In contrast, the lPAG and vlPAG contribute to passive coping strategies, including analgesia and cardiovascular regulation, through projections to the rostral ventromedial medulla (RVM) and locus coeruleus for descending pain inhibition involving enkephalins, serotonin, and mu-opioid receptors. Additional roles encompass vocalization, lordosis in reproductive behaviors, and sympathetic activation for cardiopulmonary responses, with the dorsal columns mediating pressor effects and the ventrolateral column eliciting depressor responses.
Functionally, the PAG integrates nociceptive inputs from the spinal cord with higher-order emotional and cognitive signals, facilitating adaptive survival mechanisms like analgesia during stress or threat. Its intrinsic GABAergic circuitry provides tonic inhibition, which, when released, activates output pathways to coordinate these responses. In emotional regulation, subregions show sex-specific connectivity patterns; for instance, the vlPAG in females links more strongly to the anterior cingulate cortex and insula for pain-emotion integration, while in males, it connects to the midcingulate cortex and parahippocampal gyrus.
Clinically, dysregulation of the PAG is implicated in chronic pain syndromes, migraines (with associated iron deposition), anxiety disorders, depression, and panic attacks, as well as drug addiction phenomena like withdrawal-induced anxiety and relapse via altered dopamine and GABA signaling. Therapeutic targeting, such as deep brain stimulation of the PAG or opioid analgesia, has shown efficacy in pain management by enhancing descending inhibitory pathways.

Anatomy and organization

Location and gross structure

The periaqueductal gray (PAG) is defined as a midline column of gray matter that encircles the cerebral aqueduct within the midbrain tegmentum, spanning rostrocaudally from the diencephalic-mesencephalic junction to the pontomesencephalic junction. This structure forms a continuous ring-like sheath around the aqueduct, which serves as a conduit for cerebrospinal fluid between the third and fourth ventricles. In humans, the PAG measures approximately 10-15 mm in rostrocaudal length, mirroring the extent of the aqueduct itself, and exhibits a consistent cylindrical configuration that adapts to the aqueduct's narrow diameter of about 1 mm.
The PAG is positioned centrally within the midbrain, bordering the tegmentum laterally, the red nucleus ventrally, and the superior and inferior colliculi dorsally. This strategic placement situates it adjacent to key midbrain landmarks, including the oculomotor and trochlear nuclei, while remaining enveloped by white matter tracts such as the medial longitudinal fasciculus. Its gross morphology is characterized by a symmetrical, multilayered arrangement of neuronal somata and neuropil, forming a distinct boundary with the surrounding brainstem tissues.
Evolutionarily, the PAG is highly conserved across all vertebrates, maintaining a homologous position relative to the ventricular system and serving as an ancient hub for coordinating survival-related functions. On magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), the PAG appears as a thin, hypointense ring of gray matter encircling the even more hypointense cerebrospinal fluid-filled aqueduct, particularly evident on T1-weighted sequences due to its intermediate signal intensity compared to adjacent white matter. This central location facilitates the PAG's role in integrating diverse sensory and motor signals from higher brain regions.

Subdivisions and cytoarchitecture

The periaqueductal gray (PAG) is subdivided into four longitudinal columns surrounding the cerebral aqueduct: the dorsomedial (dmPAG), dorsolateral (dlPAG), lateral (lPAG), and ventrolateral (vlPAG). While originally delineated in rodent models, this columnar organization has been confirmed in humans using high-resolution neuroimaging techniques. This organization reflects distinct patterns of afferent inputs, efferent projections, and intrinsic interneurons that contribute to functional specialization along the mediolateral axis.
Cytoarchitecturally, the PAG comprises a heterogeneous population of neurons, including GABAergic inhibitory cells, glutamatergic excitatory neurons, and peptidergic elements expressing neuropeptides such as enkephalin and substance P. These neuronal types are interspersed within a dense neuropil rich in synaptic terminals, with notably high densities of mu-opioid receptors that vary across columns, particularly elevated in the vlPAG.
Along the rostrocaudal axis, the PAG exhibits gradients in cellular composition and connectivity, with somatotopic organization where anterior regions are more associated with face representation and posterior regions with body representation.
Sexual dimorphism is evident in the PAG, with higher densities of mu-opioid receptors in the vlPAG in females compared to males, differences attributable to gonadal hormone influences such as estrogen modulation of receptor expression.
Recent neuroimaging studies using 7T MRI have provided high-resolution evidence in humans confirming this columnar and rostrocaudal organization, revealing distinct activation patterns during cognitive tasks that underscore functional specialization beyond threat responses.

Neural connectivity

Afferent inputs

The periaqueductal gray (PAG) receives diverse afferent inputs from forebrain and brainstem structures, integrating sensory, emotional, and cognitive signals. Major sources include the hypothalamus, particularly the medial preoptic area, which provides inputs related to reproductive and homeostatic regulation through direct projections. The amygdala contributes fear- and emotion-related signals, projecting primarily to the ventrolateral PAG (vlPAG) to influence passive defensive responses. The prefrontal cortex supplies cognitive modulatory inputs, targeting the vlPAG to contextualize threat processing.
Nociceptive and sensory afferents arrive directly from the spinal cord dorsal horn (laminae I, II, and V) through the spinomesencephalic tract, conveying pain signals to various PAG columns. Orofacial pain inputs reach the PAG directly from the trigeminal nucleus caudalis, enabling localized somatotopic representation. Visceral sensations are relayed indirectly via the parabrachial nucleus, which projects to the PAG to integrate interoceptive information with threat detection. Neurotransmitter-specific inputs include serotonergic projections from the raphe nuclei, particularly the dorsal raphe, modulating PAG excitability during stress and analgesia. Noradrenergic afferents from the locus coeruleus enhance arousal-related signaling to the PAG, with high catecholamine concentrations noted in the vlPAG.
Afferent organization varies by PAG column: the dorsolateral PAG (dlPAG) receives more cortical and superior colliculus inputs for active escape behaviors, while the vlPAG is preferentially innervated by hypothalamic and amygdalar pathways for autonomic integration. Recent studies highlight bidirectional feedback between the PAG and medullary regions, such as the rostroventral medulla, in defensive behaviors; for instance, medullary inputs to the PAG facilitate opioid-mediated pain modulation during threat responses, underscoring reciprocal control in active and passive coping.

Efferent outputs

The periaqueductal gray (PAG) sends efferent projections to several key brainstem and spinal targets, facilitating descending control over various neural and physiological processes. Primary efferent targets include the dorsal raphe nucleus, where PAG neurons promote serotonin release implicated in mood regulation; the nucleus paragigantocellularis, contributing to autonomic control; and the spinal cord via the dorsolateral funiculus, enabling descending inhibition of nociceptive transmission.
Column-specific outputs from PAG subdivisions exhibit functional specialization. The lateral PAG (lPAG) projects to the central nucleus of the amygdala, supporting fear-related processing; the ventrolateral PAG (vlPAG) targets the rostral ventromedial medulla (RVM) to mediate analgesia; and the dorsomedial PAG (dmPAG) projects to brainstem regions such as the rostral ventrolateral medulla, influencing cardiovascular effects such as hypertension and tachycardia.
Autonomic efferents from the PAG extend to the Edinger-Westphal nucleus, regulating pupillary responses during stress, and to the parabrachial nucleus, modulating respiratory patterns in response to threats.
Recent studies have identified distinct PAG neuronal populations projecting to the medulla that differentiate active and passive defensive behaviors, with glutamatergic outputs driving aversion and escape under strong stimulation.
Feedback loops involving reciprocal connections between the PAG and hypothalamus support homeostatic regulation, allowing bidirectional integration of emotional and visceral signals.

Physiological functions

Pain modulation and analgesia

The periaqueductal gray (PAG) serves as a central hub in the descending inhibitory pain pathway, where its activation triggers the release of endogenous opioids such as enkephalins and endorphins within the PAG itself. These opioids disinhibit projection neurons that synapse in the rostroventromedial medulla (RVM), which in turn sends serotonergic and noradrenergic projections to the spinal dorsal horn, thereby suppressing nociceptive transmission from primary afferents to second-order neurons. This mechanism underlies endogenous analgesia, allowing the PAG to gate pain signals in response to higher cortical or limbic inputs.
Electrical stimulation or microinjection of opioids into the ventrolateral PAG (vlPAG) induces profound antinociception, often comparable to systemic opioid effects, by enhancing this descending inhibition. Such vlPAG activation contributes to stress-induced analgesia, where acute threats suppress pain perception, and has been implicated in placebo analgesia through expectation-driven modulation of the same circuitry. Mu-opioid receptors predominate in the vlPAG, mediating supraspinal analgesia; for instance, microinjections of mu-agonists like morphine or oxycodone produce dose-dependent pain relief via these receptors, while partial agonists like buprenorphine show limited efficacy at this site.
The PAG's pain modulatory functions interact with other neurotransmitter systems, including cannabinoids and serotonin, to fine-tune analgesia. Endogenous cannabinoids acting on CB1 receptors in the PAG enhance opioid-insensitive stress analgesia and can potentiate descending inhibition independently of mu-opioids. Serotonergic projections from the RVM, under PAG control, further amplify inhibition in the dorsal horn, while chronic opioid exposure leads to PAG adaptationssuch as reduced opioid sensitivity and enhanced excitatory signalingthat contribute to tolerance development. These interactions highlight the PAG's role in integrating multimodal analgesia, though dysregulation can impair overall pain control.
Recent research as of 2025 has identified excitatory/inhibitory imbalances in the PAG as a key factor in chronic low back pain (CLBP), with patients exhibiting reduced glutamate + glutamine (Glx) levels and elevated gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) concentrations, resulting in a lower Glx/GABA ratio compared to healthy controls. This dysregulation correlates with diminished conditioned pain modulation and heightened pressure pain sensitivity, suggesting impaired descending inhibition; targeting vlPAG neural mechanisms, such as restoring excitatory drive, may offer novel pathways for improved pain relief in CLBP. The PAG also briefly integrates emotional context from amygdala inputs to contextualize pain modulation, influencing the intensity of descending control based on affective states.

Defensive and instinctive behaviors

The periaqueductal gray (PAG) serves as a critical midbrain hub orchestrating defensive and instinctive behaviors essential for survival, integrating threat detection to trigger coordinated motor and emotional responses such as fight, flight, or freezing. This structure functions as a command center for innate behaviors, a role conserved across vertebrates from fish to mammals, where it relays forebrain signals to brainstem motor and arousal centers to execute species-typical defenses against predators or conspecific threats. Experimental studies in rodents demonstrate that PAG activation evokes ethologically relevant responses, while lesions disrupt threat avoidance, underscoring its necessity for adaptive survival strategies.
Columnar organization within the PAG specializes functions for distinct defensive modes: the dorsal PAG (DPAG) drives active defense, including aggression and ultrasonic vocalizations in rodents, often accompanied by tachycardia to support confrontational responses. The lateral PAG (LPAG) coordinates flight and escape, facilitating rapid locomotion away from danger, as seen in predatory pursuit or evasion behaviors. In contrast, the ventrolateral PAG (vlPAG) mediates passive coping, such as immobility or freezing, which minimizes detection by threats and is linked to hypotensive states for energy conservation. These specializations allow context-dependent switching between active and passive strategies based on threat proximity and intensity.
Neural circuits of the PAG integrate aversion learning by receiving inputs from limbic structures like the amygdala and hypothalamus, then projecting via descending pathways to brainstem regions for locomotion (e.g., via the cuneiform nucleus) and autonomic arousal (e.g., via the parabrachial nucleus and rostral ventrolateral medulla). This enables the PAG to blend innate instincts with conditioned fear, as vlPAG circuits modulate learned freezing responses during Pavlovian conditioning. Seminal optogenetic experiments reveal that stimulating DPAG glutamatergic neurons elicits running and vocalizations, while vlPAG activation induces freezing; conversely, inhibiting these populations impairs threat-specific behaviors without affecting locomotion alone. Lesions in PAG columns similarly abolish species-typical defenses, such as escape in elevated plus mazes.
Recent investigations (20242025) highlight PAG-medulla projections in delineating active versus passive defense: optogenetic activation of lateral/ventrolateral PAG neurons projecting to the medullary reticular formation evokes graded responses, with strong stimulation promoting active escape and weaker inputs favoring aversion and freezing in mice. Functional analyses in rodents confirm the brainstem's conserved role, showing DPAG hyperactivity in escape-prone species like deer mice during looming threats, integrating visual inputs for instinctive antipredator actions. These findings emphasize the PAG's evolutionary tuning for ecological demands, with brief overlap to pain suppression enabling unimpeded defensive execution during threats.

Autonomic and visceral regulation

The periaqueductal gray (PAG) serves as a key midbrain integrator for autonomic functions, coordinating involuntary responses through its columnar subdivisions and projections to brainstem and hypothalamic nuclei. Activation of specific PAG subregions modulates sympathetic and parasympathetic outflows, influencing cardiovascular, respiratory, and visceral systems to maintain homeostasis during physiological demands.
In cardiovascular regulation, the medial and lateral PAG (mPAG and lPAG) facilitate increases in heart rate and blood pressure through excitatory projections to the hypothalamus and rostral ventrolateral medulla (RVLM), enhancing sympathetic drive during stress-like conditions. Conversely, the ventrolateral PAG (vlPAG) promotes bradycardia and hypotension by engaging parasympathetic pathways, as seen in hypotensive responses to hemorrhagic shock. These effects arise from PAG's integration of sensory inputs to balance arterial pressure and cardiac output.
Respiratory modulation by the PAG involves its connectivity to pontine and medullary respiratory centers, such as the pre-Btzinger complex, allowing adjustment of breathing patterns in response to integrated signals. The dorsolateral PAG (dlPAG) drives tachypnea and active expiration, increasing respiratory rate to support heightened oxygen demands, while the dorsomedial PAG (dmPAG) elicits slower, deeper breaths for sustained responses. This control ensures adaptive ventilation without overt behavioral involvement.
Visceral control, particularly micturition, is regulated by PAG neurons that coordinate bladder and urethral sphincter activity via descending pathways to the pontine micturition center. Somatostatin (SST)-expressing neurons in the vlPAG promote voiding by facilitating bladder contraction and sphincter relaxation, while dlPAG sites inhibit these processes to prevent untimely urination. Electrical stimulation of ventral PAG regions evokes bladder contractions and postganglionic nerve firing, highlighting its therapeutic potential for urinary tract dysfunction.
The PAG contributes to homeostatic integration by linking with the parabrachial nucleus (PBN) to regulate thirst and temperature, processing visceral afferents for fluid and thermal balance. PAG-PBN circuits modulate ingestive behaviors and thermoregulatory responses, ensuring coordinated autonomic adjustments.
Recent research as of 2025 underscores the PAG's role in threat-related respiratory output, with network analyses revealing its orchestration of hyperventilation via brainstem pathways. Additionally, optogenetic and chemogenetic manipulation of PAG SST neurons has advanced understanding of interventions for urinary tract function, restoring coordinated micturition in preclinical models. These autonomic adjustments support broader defensive arousal without direct behavioral execution.

Reproductive and maternal behaviors

The periaqueductal gray (PAG) plays a key role in coordinating sexual behaviors, with its lateral (LPAG) and medial (MPAG) subdivisions receiving inputs from the ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH) to facilitate reflexive responses such as lordosis in female rats and mounting in males. Neurons expressing estrogen receptor (ER) in the VMH project densely to the LPAG, enabling estradiol-mediated motor control of lordosis, while opioid systems within the PAG modulate reward and sensory processing during copulation to enhance sexual motivation. In males, serotonergic projections from the ventrolateral PAG (vlPAG) to the nucleus paragigantocellularis inhibit ejaculation; lesions of these serotonergic neurons increase ejaculation frequency and reduce latency, demonstrating the PAG's inhibitory role in genital reflexes.
Hormonal fluctuations across reproductive cycles significantly influence PAG activity, with estrogen and testosterone receptors distributed throughout its caudal and mid-level regions, particularly in dorsomedial and lateral columns. Progesterone withdrawal during late dioestrus upregulates 41 GABAA receptors in the PAG, reducing inhibitory tone on output neurons and altering excitability linked to reproductive behaviors like lordosis. These changes contribute to the PAG's involvement in orgasmic responses and the post-ejaculatory refractory period, where opioid and serotonergic modulation in the PAG helps regulate sexual satiety.
In maternal behaviors, activation of the vlPAG promotes nursing and pup retrieval in rodents, with caudal PAG lesions disrupting suckling-induced kyphosis and impairing litter weight gain, while rostral PAG lesions hinder pup carrying during retrieval without affecting nursing posture. Endogenous -opioid tone in the rostrolateral PAG (rlPAG) supports caregiving instincts, as morphine administration inhibits pup retrieval and nest building, whereas -opioid receptor blockade shifts behavior toward predation, highlighting the PAG's role in prioritizing maternal over aggressive responses.
Sex differences in PAG structure and function underpin variations in reproductive and maternal behaviors, with females showing greater medial PAG volume associated with enhanced caregiving, and the PAG-rostral ventromedial medulla (RVM) circuit exhibiting dimorphism in GABAA signaling that influences opioid-mediated responses during reproduction. The PAG integrates with oxytocin systems, as parvocellular oxytocin neurons project to the vlPAG to modulate maternal attachment and analgesia during nursing.
Recent studies underscore the PAG's conserved role in instinctive reproductive drives across species, with medial preoptic area projections to the PAG mediating hormonal activation of parental behaviors in rodents and birds, revealing a core neural mechanism for species-typical mating and caregiving sequences.

Clinical and research significance

Role in pain disorders

The periaqueductal gray (PAG) plays a central role in descending pain modulation under normal conditions, but pathological alterations in its activity and connectivity contribute to the persistence and amplification of pain in various chronic disorders. In fibromyalgia, disrupted resting-state functional connectivity of the PAG, particularly reduced coupling with regions involved in affective processing such as the anterior cingulate cortex and prefrontal areas, is associated with central sensitization and heightened pain sensitivity. These connectivity deficits correlate with clinical symptoms like negative affect and widespread pain, suggesting impaired descending inhibitory control that exacerbates fibromyalgia manifestations.
In neuropathic pain, enhanced spontaneous activity of pain-responsive neurons in the ventrolateral PAG (vlPAG) has been observed, correlating with behavioral hypersensitivity to thermal stimuli in animal models. This hyperactivity contributes to central facilitation of nociceptive signals via pathways such as vlPAG projections to the rostral ventromedial medulla, where somatostatin-expressing neurons promote descending pain facilitation in male mice subjected to nerve injury. Such alterations indicate a shift from inhibitory to facilitatory PAG function, sustaining allodynia and hyperalgesia in neuropathic states.
Recent studies on chronic low back pain highlight excitatory/inhibitory imbalances within the PAG, leading to dysregulated descending pain inhibition and persistent nociception from deep tissue afferents. Functional neuroimaging reveals altered PAG activity in these patients, supporting a model where impaired balance favors excitatory neurotransmission, such as via glutamate over GABA, thereby maintaining chronic pain despite absent peripheral injury.
In migraine and related headaches, the PAG serves as a key modulator of the trigeminovascular system, where dysfunctional hyperactivity can generate throbbing pain through enhanced facilitation of nociceptive transmission from meningeal afferents to thalamic and cortical targets. Imaging studies further demonstrate hypoperfusion in the PAG during chronic migraine episodes, providing evidence of impaired pain gating that correlates with attack frequency and intensity.
Opioid-related disorders involve adaptive changes in the PAG that underlie withdrawal-induced hyperalgesia, including glial activation and release of proinflammatory cytokines like TNF, which enhance neuronal excitability through ERK/CREB signaling and increased AMPA/NMDA receptor function. These adaptations, observed during morphine withdrawal, lead to cAMP-dependent hyperexcitability of GABAergic neurons and reduced opioid-mediated inhibition, contributing to heightened pain sensitivity in addiction and abstinence states.
Functional MRI evidence in complex regional pain syndrome reveals altered PAG connectivity, notably reduced resting-state functional coupling between the PAG and left anterior insula, indicative of downregulated antinociceptive modulation and amplified pain perception. This connectivity disruption, observed in patient cohorts, underscores the PAG's role in maintaining central hypersensitivity despite resolution of initial injury.

Therapeutic applications

The periaqueductal gray (PAG) has been a primary target for deep brain stimulation (DBS) in the management of intractable chronic pain since the late 1970s, when initial studies demonstrated its role in eliciting endogenous opioid release to produce analgesia in refractory neuropathic conditions. Early applications focused on electrical stimulation of the PAG or adjacent periventricular gray to interrupt pain signals via descending inhibitory pathways, with clinical trials reporting sustained relief in patients unresponsive to conventional therapies. Recent advancements include frequency-modulated stimulation protocols, such as low-frequency (5 Hz) DBS in the ventrolateral PAG (vlPAG), which has shown enhanced antinociceptive effects in animal models of neuropathic pain by modulating serotonergic descending systems, offering potential improvements for cancer-related pain management.
Pharmacological interventions targeting the PAG have also proven effective for pain relief, particularly through opioids and cannabinoids that engage local receptors to amplify descending inhibition. Intrathecal administration of opioids, such as morphine, indirectly influences PAG-mediated analgesia by reducing tolerance development in supraspinal sites and enhancing overall postoperative pain control, as evidenced by studies showing preserved efficacy despite PAG microinjection tolerance. Cannabinoids exert their effects via CB1 receptors in the vlPAG, where inflammatory pain upregulates receptor expression and G-protein coupling to potentiate antinociception, providing a synergistic mechanism with opioids for conditions like neuropathic pain.
Emerging therapies leverage advanced neuromodulation techniques to target the PAG in preclinical models. Optogenetic stimulation of PAG circuits, including projections from the medial prefrontal cortex to the PAG, has alleviated depression-like behaviors in rodents by promoting active stress-coping responses and reducing avoidance, highlighting potential translational applications for mood disorders comorbid with pain. Similarly, sacral neuromodulation for urinary tract disorders restores PAG activity by modulating inhibitory afferent signals, improving bladder control in patients with neurogenic lower urinary tract dysfunction through enhanced coordination of the PAG-pontine micturition center pathway.
Despite these benefits, PAG-targeted therapies carry notable risks, including respiratory depression from opioid interactions or direct stimulation effects on brainstem respiratory centers, as well as potential for dyspnea or autonomic instability during DBS. Clinical outcomes for DBS in chronic pain show success rates of 50-70%, defined as at least 50% pain reduction, though long-term efficacy varies by etiology, with higher rates in neuropathic cases but diminishing over years due to tolerance or hardware issues.
As of 2025, research on vlPAG mechanisms has advanced pain relief strategies, with studies demonstrating that targeted inhibition of glutamatergic neurons in the dorsomedial PAG reduces excessive defensive responses in PTSD models, suggesting PAG stimulation could mitigate instinctive behavior disorders like trauma-related hyperarousal. These findings build on PAG's established role in pain disorders, emphasizing circuit-specific interventions for comorbid conditions.
