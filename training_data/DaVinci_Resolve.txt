 DaVinci Resolve is a professional non-linear editing application developed by Blackmagic Design that integrates video editing, color correction, visual effects, motion graphics, and audio post-production into a single software platform, available for macOS, Windows, Linux, and iPadOS via a dedicated app optimized for touch interfaces and Apple Pencil support. It offers a free version that provides professional-grade tools for editing, color grading, VFX, motion graphics, and audio post-production without adding any watermark to exports. As of March 2026, DaVinci Resolve (free version) is widely regarded as the best free video editing software overall and particularly the best for audio mixing capabilities. Its Fairlight page delivers professional-grade audio tools, supporting high track counts (up to 2,000 with appropriate hardware acceleration), real-time effects, EQ, dynamics processing, and mixing in stereo to immersive 3D audio formats (with some advanced AI tools like Voice Isolation and enhanced immersive options reserved for the paid Studio edition). It supports up to Ultra HD 4K at 60fps with multi-user collaboration and HDR features. It is available on Windows, Mac, and Linux. While it has a steeper learning curve and reserves some advanced AI tools (like Neural Engine features) for the paid Studio version, it remains the top choice for serious users and prosumer workflows. For beginners or social media creators, CapCut is a strong, user-friendly alternative with excellent mobile support and features like auto-captions and background removal. While no free video editor is universally superior to paid tools such as Adobe Premiere Pro or Final Cut Pro, which benefit from deeper ecosystem integration and specific workflow advantages, DaVinci Resolve's free version excels in key areas including color grading, audio mastering via Fairlight, visual effects through Fusion, and overall value, and is often preferred by users and reviewers for achieving professional results without cost. As of March 2026, the Studio version of DaVinci Resolve 20 offers advanced video noise reduction with temporal noise reduction and AI spatial noise reduction (UltraNR), widely regarded as highly effective for cleaning up footage while preserving detail. In contrast, Adobe Premiere Pro lacks comparable built-in advanced video noise reduction tools; it focuses instead on audio noise reduction with AI-powered Enhance Speech, and users often rely on third-party plugins for better video denoising results. The free version is considered the top choice for serious projects due to its advanced features and lack of restrictions on output quality. A paid DaVinci Resolve Studio edition unlocks advanced features such as higher resolutions, additional effects, advanced noise reduction (including temporal and AI-powered UltraNR), and GPU acceleration for enhanced performance.
Originally created by da Vinci Systems as a hardware-based color grading solution in the early 2000s, the software's roots trace back to the company's pioneering work in digital film color correction starting in 1984, with the first Resolve system released around 2004. In September 2009, Blackmagic Design acquired da Vinci Systems, enabling the transition to software-only versions and expanding its capabilities into a comprehensive post-production tool, with major updates like the introduction of integrated editing in version 10 (2013) and Fairlight audio tools in version 14 (2017). The latest release, DaVinci Resolve 20 (with update 20.1 released in August 2025), incorporates over 100 new features, including AI-assisted tools for transcription-based editing, object removal, and voice isolation, as well as support for vertical video resolutions (such as those used for TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts) in project and timeline settings and an optimized UI layout for vertical timelines that automatically rearranges the cut, edit, and color pages to maximize screen space, reposition tools and palettes, and reduce the need for constant zooming.
DaVinci Resolve has become an industry standard in Hollywood post-production, used for color grading and finishing on numerous Academy Award-nominated and winning films, including Oppenheimer (2023) and Alien: Romulus (2024). Its node-based compositing in the built-in Fusion page, advanced HDR grading tools, and collaborative multi-user features support high-end workflows for visual effects and sound design, making it a preferred choice for professionals handling wide color gamut and high dynamic range content. The software's free availability has also democratized access to professional-grade tools, fostering its adoption in independent filmmaking and education worldwide.

Development history

Origins at da Vinci Systems (20032009)

da Vinci Systems was founded in 1984 as a spinoff from Video Tape Associates, a production and post-production facility, with a primary focus on developing high-end hardware for film and video color correction. The company's early products, such as the da Vinci Classic introduced shortly after founding, established it as a leader in professional color enhancement technology, emphasizing hardware systems capable of precise primary and secondary corrections for broadcast and theatrical workflows. These systems were designed to address the limitations of analog telecine processes, providing digital control over color balance, contrast, and saturation in real-time environments.
In 2004, da Vinci Systems launched DaVinci Resolve, marking the introduction of its first software-based, resolution-independent color grading platform as part of the 2K Plus system tailored for digital intermediate (DI) workflows. Announced and demonstrated at NAB 2004, Resolve combined Emmy Award-winning color science with nonlinear editing capabilities, available in configurations such as Resolve DI for high-resolution film finishing, Resolve FX for visual effects integration, and Resolve RT for real-time 2K playback and correction. This release represented a shift toward software-driven grading on Linux-based hardware, enabling efficient handling of scanned film data in post-production pipelines while maintaining compatibility with existing da Vinci hardware. The 2K Plus system, which powered early Resolve implementations, supported 10-bit RGB processing at resolutions up to 2048 x 1080, a significant upgrade over prior 8-bit systems and essential for preserving dynamic range in DI processes.
Key technological advancements in Resolve during this period included real-time primary color correction tools for overall balance adjustments and advanced secondary grading features, such as power windows and qualifiers for isolating and modifying specific image regions or colors without affecting the whole frame. These tools facilitated creative flexibility in nonlinear workflows, allowing colorists to reorder scenes and refine grades based on final edit decisions while linking correction data across edits to reduce rework. Integration with film scanning and output scanning processes was a core strength, enabling seamless data flow from telecine scans to final film-out recording, which optimized the DI pipeline for efficiency in high-stakes productions. Between 2006 and 2008, system upgrades further enhanced 10-bit processing capabilities across Resolve installations, improving precision in shadow and highlight detail for demanding color work.
By 2009, DaVinci Resolve had achieved widespread adoption, with hundreds of 2K Plus and Resolve systems installed in post-production facilities across the globe, solidifying its role as the industry standard for color grading theatrical releases and major Hollywood films. These installations powered the finishing of numerous high-profile projects, underscoring Resolve's reliability in professional environments where accuracy and speed were paramount. In September 2009, Blackmagic Design acquired da Vinci Systems, paving the way for further evolution of the technology.

Blackmagic Design acquisition and evolution (2009present)

In September 2009, Blackmagic Design acquired all assets of da Vinci Systems LLC, integrating the DaVinci Resolve technology into its portfolio and marking a pivotal shift from high-end hardware-centric systems to more accessible software solutions. This acquisition enabled Blackmagic to leverage Resolve's color grading expertise while expanding its development toward cross-platform software availability, reducing barriers for independent filmmakers and post-production professionals previously limited by expensive proprietary hardware.
Following the acquisition, Blackmagic released DaVinci Resolve 7 in September 2010 as the first version under its stewardship, introducing a software-only edition for Mac OS X priced at $995, which significantly lowered the entry cost compared to prior hardware-based systems. In June 2011, version 8 launched with the introduction of DaVinci Resolve Lite, a free edition limited to HD resolutions, initially available for Linux and later expanded to Windows and Mac OS X, broadening accessibility and fostering widespread adoption among users worldwide.
Key milestones in Resolve's evolution under Blackmagic include version 12 in 2015, which integrated a full non-linear editing page and the Fusion visual effects and compositing page, transforming the software into a more comprehensive post-production tool. Version 14, released in 2017, added the Fairlight audio post-production page, further consolidating editing, color, effects, and sound workflows into a single application. By version 17 in 2020, remote multi-user collaboration features were introduced, allowing teams to work simultaneously on projects via shared project libraries (formerly known as databases), enhancing efficiency in distributed production environments. Version 18 in 2022 advanced the DaVinci Neural Engine, powering enhanced AI-driven tools for tasks like object detection and noise reduction, while maintaining compatibility across Mac, Windows, and Linux platforms.
More recent updates have continued this trajectory of innovation and refinement. Version 19, publicly released in August 2024 after beta testing, improved multi-user collaboration with better project locking and real-time syncing, alongside AI enhancements for subtitle generation and audio classification. DaVinci Resolve 20, launched in May 2025, introduced over 100 new features, including the AI IntelliScript tool for automated timeline assembly based on script matching and dialogue transcription, streamlining rough cut creation, as well as support for vertical video resolutions (such as those used for TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts) in project and timeline settings, and an optimized UI layout. When a vertical timeline or project is loaded, the cut, edit, and color pages automatically rearrange layouts to maximize screen space for vertical orientation, repositioning tools and palettes to eliminate wasted space and reduce the need for constant zooming. The subsequent update to version 20.2.3 in November 2025 added support for Blackmagic RAW SDK 5.1, enabling advanced metadata handling and decoding improvements, while delivering up to 4x faster performance for AI features like Magic Mask, Depth Map, and Super Scale on Intel GPUs.
Under Blackmagic Design, DaVinci Resolve has evolved into a versatile all-in-one post-production suite, supporting professional workflows from editing to delivery, with free annual major updates ensuring ongoing accessibility and feature parity for the non-paid edition across all major operating systems. This commitment to free updates and cross-platform support has democratized high-end tools, powering projects in film, television, and streaming media globally.

Core functionality

Video editing and color grading

DaVinci Resolve employs a page-based interface that separates video editing and color grading into dedicated workspaces, allowing users to switch seamlessly between tasks. The Cut page is designed for rapid assembly of rough cuts, featuring a source tape mode for quick media review, a graphical A/B trimmer for precise edit point adjustments, and integration with the media pool for asset organization and import. It also supports live multi-camera broadcast editing and replay, including Multi Source viewing of multiple live cameras or clips, real-time editing while cameras are still recording, and replay with speed control. Video outputs can be streamed to a remote client monitor via DeckLink hardware. These live broadcast editing capabilities are available in the free version of DaVinci Resolve. However, built-in direct live streaming to online platforms such as YouTube or Twitch is not supported, and external software such as OBS is typically required for internet live streaming.
The Edit page provides a traditional timeline-based environment for detailed non-linear editing, including multicam editing with angle synchronization and switching, dynamic zoom for automated reframing of shots, and speed warping via retime controls for smooth variable-speed adjustments without artifacts. The media pool supports import of still images alongside video, including direct handling of PSD files in the timeline to preserve layers for compositing layered stills, with full editing tools applicable to hybrid workflows.
In the Edit and Cut pages, the timeline toolbar includes a Position Lock feature. When enabled, Position Lock restricts changes to clip positions in the timeline, preventing clips from being inserted or moved at the playhead position. As a result, pasted clipsincluding those containing Fusion compositions or any other clipmay append to the end of the timeline or appear as disconnected clips instead of inserting at the playhead. Disabling Position Lock allows pasted clips to be inserted at the current playhead position.
In the Edit and Cut pages, which employ a traditional timeline-based workflow more akin to layer-based systems (e.g., stacking elements in timeline order), keyframes are applied to clip parameters (such as position, scale, opacity, or effects) and edited using the dedicated Keyframe and Curve Editor panel. Updated in version 20, this interface provides a graph for viewing and editing keyframe curves with Bezier splines, allowing users to adjust handles for easing, interpolation, and precise control over animation timing and value changes. This differs in UI and behavior from the spline-based tools in node-based workflows on the Color and Fusion pages.
DaVinci Resolve's master timeline frame rate, set in Project Settings under Master Settings, becomes locked and greyed out once a timeline is created or media is added to the project. This prevents inconsistencies in frame rates across the project that could cause playback, synchronization, or rendering issues. Individual empty timelines can sometimes be adjusted independently by right-clicking the timeline in the Media Pool, selecting Timeline Settings, unchecking "Use Project Settings," and modifying the Timeline Frame Rate. To change the locked master project frame rate, common user workarounds include temporarily deleting all clips (or cutting them to the clipboard), changing the frame rate, then undoing or pasting the clips back; or creating a new timeline with the desired frame rate and copying clips over from the original.
The Effects panel on the Cut and Edit pages provides access to various tools, including the Adjustment Clip for applying effects or corrections across multiple underlying clips in the timeline. In DaVinci Resolve 20, the Adjustment Clip is accessible via the Toolbox in the Effects panel (not in main Effects Library folders or standard search unless Toolbox is active). To access: open the Effects panel on the Edit or Cut page, select the Toolbox tab/button in the sidebar, then drag the Adjustment Clip (listed under Video or directly) to the timeline. Note that search (e.g., Shift+Space) may fail if Toolbox is not selected; selecting Toolbox resolves this. This behavior was reported in versions including 20.1 and addressed in updates such as 20.2.1.
The Color page focuses on professional node-based grading, where corrections are applied sequentially or in parallel through a visual node graph, enabling non-destructive modifications and efficient reuse across clips. This node-based workflow allows keyframing of parameters on individual nodes (such as color corrections or sizing) independently, with keyframes added to node controls and curves edited in the Graph Editor (also known as the Spline Editor) using Bezier-style handles to adjust easing, speed, interpolation, and overshoot for smooth animations. Multiple parameters can be viewed and filtered simultaneously in the editor.
This includes Hollywood-grade color grading for still photos with RAW image support and node-based adjustments, as well as support for applying .cube 3D LUTs to RAW files and still images. Users can load .cube LUTs from the LUT panel in the Color page and apply them to nodes by dragging or via the node context menu (right-click > LUT > select), enabling quick creative looks or technical color transformations. To preview and compare multiple LUTs, users can create separate gradessuch as applying different LUTs in dedicated nodeson the same source clip or image, capture stills of each graded version to the Gallery, and then view them side-by-side in the Gallery panel or toggle between them for direct comparison.
Core color grading tools in the Color page include primary wheels for adjusting shadows (Lift), midtones (Gamma), highlights (Gain), and overall offset, which allow intuitive control over luminance and chrominance in RGB or YRGB modes. Curves provide precise tonal mapping across luminance, RGB, hue-versus-saturation, and hue-versus-hue channels, with histograms displaying input and output signal distributions for accurate adjustments. A common technique for enhancing contrast in high-dynamic-range footage, such as sunny outdoor scenes, is to apply an S-curve using the Custom or Luma curve. Add a control point in the lower third of the curve and drag it slightly up and right to lift shadows and reveal details. Add a point in the upper third and drag it down and left to roll off highlights and prevent clipping in bright areas like skies. This increases midtone contrast while preserving dynamic range in high-contrast footage. Subtle adjustments are recommended, and HDR tools can be used for better highlight control if available.
Qualifiers enable targeted secondary corrections by isolating specific color ranges using HSL or 3D keying modes, refined with matte finesse tools like blur and edge softening. HDR grading is supported through a dedicated palette with extended dynamic range wheels and color space management, compatible with standards like Rec.709, P3-D65, and ST2084 PQ for up to 4000 nits. Resolve FX plugins, available in the Studio edition, offer effects such as film grain emulation, noise reduction, and stylization tools like tilt-shift blur and beauty enhancements. Additionally, the DCTL (DaVinci Color Transform Language) OpenFX plugin allows for custom color transforms. On macOS, the default installation path for .dctl or .dctle files is /Library/Application Support/Blackmagic Design/DaVinci Resolve/LUT; place files in this folder or a subfolder, restart DaVinci Resolve to load them, and select them via the DCTL OpenFX plugin in the Color page. Custom folders can be added in Preferences > General > LUT Locations. The default folder can be accessed via Project Settings > Color Management > Open LUT Folder.
Editing features extend across the Cut and Edit pages with robust media management via the media pool, which supports bin organization, metadata tagging, and proxy generation for efficient asset handling, as well as automatic audio synchronization to video clips based on waveforms, with options to attach audio, create compound clips, or multicam clips; batch alignment is facilitated via the Sync Bin in the Cut page. Still images integrated into timelines can be further processed in the Fusion page for compositing and effects, though audio post-production tools are primarily designed for sequences involving motion or sound elements. Multicam workflows allow automatic audio-based syncing of multiple angles, with on-the-fly switching during playback. Dynamic zoom automates keyframe-based scaling and panning to create cinematic movements from static footage, while speed warping uses optical flow algorithms in the Studio edition to maintain motion fluidity during retiming.
DaVinci Resolve provides Proxy Handling modes under Playback > Proxy Handling to determine whether proxy media or original source files are used for playback and editing. These modes include:

Disable All Proxies: Ignores all proxy files and forces use of the original source media for maximum quality (may reduce performance on demanding footage).

Prefer Proxies: Uses generated proxy files when available for faster playback and editing; falls back to originals if proxies are missing.

Prefer Camera Originals: Prioritizes the original camera source files (e.g., RAW formats) over proxies for the highest image quality and detail, even if proxies exist; useful for color grading or critical viewing.

These modes help balance performance during editing with quality during review or finishing.
In the Studio edition, DaVinci Resolve supports resolutions up to 32K, frame rates up to 120 fps, and professional formats including Blackmagic RAW for constant-quality decoding, Apple ProRes for high-efficiency editing, and Avid DNxHR for 10-bit workflows. These specifications enable real-time playback and grading of high-end productions without transcoding.
Playback performance can be further optimized by setting the viewer proxy mode to Half or Quarter via the dropdown in the viewer (Off/Half/Quarter). This reduces the display resolution in the viewer for smoother playback but does not affect the quality of grabbed stills, which are always captured from the full-resolution timeline data. On the Color page, users can capture a still frame with applied color grades by positioning the playhead, right-clicking in the viewer, and selecting Grab Still; the still appears in the Gallery panel, where right-clicking the thumbnail and selecting Export Still allows saving in formats such as PNG, JPEG, or TIFF to a chosen location. Additionally, starting in version 18.5, users can export the current frame directly as an image via File > Export > Current Frame as Still after positioning the playhead, selecting the save location, filename, and format (e.g., PNG, JPEG, TIFF); this method is available on multiple pages, including Media, Cut, Edit, and Color.
Workflow examples highlight the integration of tools, such as using power windowsshape-based masks like circles, polygons, or vignettesto apply targeted corrections to specific regions, with adjustable softness for feathering edges. Tracking features, including the Tracker palette and AI-assisted IntelliTrack, follow moving objects or faces to dynamically apply windows, qualifiers, or effects, ensuring consistent grading across motion. For instance, a power window can isolate a subject's skin for refinement while tracking maintains the mask during camera movement.

Audio post-production

The Fairlight page in DaVinci Resolve was introduced in version 14, released in 2017, providing a dedicated digital audio workstation (DAW)-like interface for professional audio post-production. This page features a multi-track timeline enabling subframe audio editing at sample-level precision and track-based mixing with support for up to 2,000 tracks, including real-time effects processing via the Fairlight Audio Accelerator.
A common issue when importing mono audio clips is that they may play only on one channel (typically left) when placed on stereo tracks or due to default channel mapping. This occurs because mono sources default to single-channel playback unless properly configured. To resolve this, right-click the clip in the Media Pool or timeline, select Clip Attributes, and in the Audio tab, set the Format to Stereo while mapping the mono source channel to both left and right output channels. Alternatively, select the clip in the timeline, open the Inspector (Audio tab), and adjust Balance/Pan to center (0.0). Another option is to right-click the track header and change the track type to Mono, ensuring mono clips play equally in both speakers. For manual stereo conversion, duplicate the clip and pan one instance fully left and the other fully right. Users should verify the source file plays correctly in external media players to rule out file issues.
Key tools in the Fairlight page include equalization (EQ) with a six-band parametric design per track and four-band per clip for precise frequency adjustments, dynamics processing such as compression, expansion/gating, and limiting to control audio levels and reduce unwanted noise, and reverb effects applied via auxiliary buses to simulate spatial environments and add depth. Noise reduction capabilities are handled through dedicated Fairlight FX plug-ins like De-Hummer and Noise Reduction, which target low-level artifacts and hum, while ADR (Automated Dialogue Replacement) workflows support cue creation, multi-channel recording up to 96 inputs, elastic wave retiming for synchronization without pitch alteration, and seamless editing and export of replacement takes.
Immersive audio support in Fairlight encompasses Dolby Atmos rendering for object-based 3D mixing with bed and object tracks, Auro-3D format compatibility for immersive soundscapes, and advanced spatial panning tools including a spherical panner for precise positioning in a panoramic sound field across formats like 5.1, 7.1, and beyond. These features enable multi-format delivery, including up to 22.2 channels, with real-time monitoring and automation for immersive deliverables.
Integration with the video timeline allows audio to sync automatically to edits using scrollers and elastic wave alignment, ensuring precise timing between picture changes and sound elements. Voice isolation via Track FX enhances dialogue clarity by suppressing background noise, and dialogue leveling tools, including the Dialogue Leveler and compression, automatically balance levels for consistent audio throughout the project.
Advanced features include a suite of Fairlight FX plug-ins such as Echo, Distortion, Modulation, and Chain FX for combining up to six effects per track, bus routing with user-defined Flex Buses for flexible signal flow and shared effects across submixes, and automation curves for precise control over volume, fades, and other parameters using keyframes. These elements support complex mixes with main, sub, and auxiliary buses, facilitating efficient workflows for feature films and broadcast projects.

Visual effects and compositing

The Fusion page in DaVinci Resolve provides a node-based compositing environment for creating visual effects, motion graphics, and advanced compositing tasks, integrated fully since version 15 in 2018 to streamline workflows within the all-in-one application. This integration allows users to access hundreds of 2D and 3D tools directly, supporting OpenFX plugin standards for extensible effects while maintaining a proprietary node graph system that connects inputs, processes, and outputs for precise control over image manipulation, including still images. The page's design emphasizes flexibility, enabling seamless transitions from editing and color grading to VFX without exporting to external software.
Core tools in Fusion include spline-based keyframing for animating parameters like position, scale, and rotation with smooth Bzier curves. The Graph Editor (also known as the Spline Editor) visualizes and edits these keyframe curves, where keyframes mark value changes at specific timeline points and curves define interpolation between them for smooth motion or effects. Curves are Bezier-style, with adjustable handles for easing, speed control, overshoot, and other refinements. Multiple parameters can be viewed and filtered simultaneously for coordinated animation. In the node-based workflow, parameters on individual nodes (e.g., Merge, Color Correction) are keyframed independently: select a node, add keyframes to controls via the diamond icon or auto-key, then edit the curves in the Spline Panel/Graph Editor. This enables modular, flow-based animation where effects chain through nodes, providing non-destructive, reroutable connections ideal for complex composites. In contrast to layer-based systems (e.g., After Effects or Premiere Pro), where keyframes apply to hierarchical layer properties in timeline stacking order with parent-child relationships, Fusion's node-based approach offers greater flexibility and modularity for intricate VFX and compositing tasks. particle systems via nodes such as pEmitter and pRender3D for simulating dynamic effects like fire, smoke, or debris in 3D space; 3D camera tracking using the Camera3D and Tracker nodes to analyze motion and align synthetic elements to live footage; and rotoscoping with tools like Polygon and B-Spline for creating animated mattes to isolate subjects frame by frame. These tools support both 2D planar operations and full 3D scene construction, with modifiers like the Planar Tracker automating repetitive tasks such as stabilization or corner pin alignment. The Planar Tracker requires a valid closed polygon pattern to initiate tracking; the "Track failed: invalid pattern polygon" error occurs when the pattern polygon is not properly definedmost commonly because it is not closed, lacks sufficient points, or forms a degenerate/invalid shape (e.g., zero area or self-intersecting). To resolve this, select the Planar Tracker node in the Fusion page, on the reference frame in the viewer place points outlining a high-contrast, planar surface (typically four clicks to form a rectangle/quadrilateral), double-click the last point to close the polygon ensuring it is fully connected and encloses a usable area, choose a large, unoccluded, high-contrast pattern with good trackable features for best results, set the reference frame if needed, and then track.
The effects library encompasses fundamental nodes like Merge for layering foreground and background images with modes such as Add, Multiply, or Screen; Transform for adjusting geometry, perspective, and depth; Blur for softening edges or creating depth-of-field effects; and Text+ generators for designing animated titles and motion graphics with options for 3D extrusion, beveling, and material mapping. Additional nodes handle vector graphics, lens flares, and procedural noise, allowing artists to build complex compositions iteratively by wiring nodes in a flowchart-like interface.
Fusion's workflow operates in clip-based mode for targeted effects on timeline segments, where a Fusion clip consolidates multiple layers into a single asset that renders back to the Edit page, or in full composition mode for standalone .fusion files that support unlimited resolution and versioning. Rendering is accelerated via GPU using APIs like Metal, CUDA, or OpenCL, enabling real-time playback and faster iteration on high-resolution projects, with options for optimized caching to manage complex scenes.
Representative examples of Fusion's capabilities include green screen keying with the Delta Keyer or Ultra Key nodes to remove chroma backgrounds and refine edges using spill suppression and matte controls; match moving, where 3D camera solves integrate CGI elements like animated logos onto practical shots; and stereoscopic 3D support through dual-camera setups and depth merging for immersive output in formats like side-by-side or anaglyph. These features make Fusion suitable for broadcast graphics, film VFX, and promotional content, often preparing elements that benefit from prior color grading adjustments in the Color page.

Project settings

Project settings in DaVinci Resolve are accessed within an open project by clicking the gear icon at the bottom right of the interface, selecting File > Project Settings from the menu bar, or using the keyboard shortcut Shift+9. These methods open the Project Settings window, where users can adjust project-wide parameters such as timeline resolution, frame rate, color management, and others.
In DaVinci YRGB Color Managed projects, for SDR workflows (as in versions current in 2025/2026, such as DaVinci Resolve 20), the recommended best practice is to set the timeline/working color space to DaVinci Wide Gamut Intermediate. This provides greater grading flexibility, preserves maximum color detail from log/RAW/HDR sources, and avoids clipping in wide gamut footage. The output color space should be set to Rec.709 Gamma 2.4 for SDR delivery. Using Rec.709 directly as the timeline color space is a more traditional approach but limits working gamut and grading latitude compared to DaVinci Wide Gamut Intermediate.
In DaVinci Resolve 20, released in 2025, Blackmagic Design introduced support for vertical video formats, including selections of square and vertical video resolutions (such as 10801920 for 9:16 aspect ratio) in project and timeline settings, tailored for platforms like TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, and Snapchat. When a vertical timeline or project is loaded, the cut, edit, and color pages automatically rearrange their layouts to optimize the workspace for vertical orientation, repositioning tools and palettes to maximize screen space, eliminate wasted space, and reduce the need for constant zooming. In Project Settings under Master Settings, the default timeline resolution can be set to vertical dimensions (e.g., 10801920). For individual timelines, users can override these defaults by right-clicking the timeline in the Media Pool and selecting Timeline Settings, unchecking "Use Project Settings", and selecting appropriate vertical resolution options, along with mismatched resolution handling such as "Scale full frame with crop" to fit horizontal footage appropriately. These configurations serve as prerequisites for AI-powered tools like Smart Reframe, which can then be used to automatically reframe landscape clips to vertical aspect ratios.
Users may encounter performance or playback issues after changing the timeline resolution to a vertical format such as 10801920, often because the timeline proxy resolution does not update correctly when switching aspect ratios, causing proxies to appear ineffective. A common workaround is to navigate to Playback > Timeline Proxy Resolution and re-select "Full" (even if it is already selected) to reset the proxy handling and cache. For improved performance in vertical workflows, it is recommended to set proxy generation to "Half" resolution in the Project Settings, regenerate proxies if needed, ensure proxy mode is enabled, and consider additional optimizations such as clearing the cache, using optimized media, or lowering the viewer playback resolution.

Deliver page

In DaVinci Resolve 19, the Deliver page renders the timeline using only enabled tracks (those with the enable button active in the timeline), excluding disabled tracks from the output. There is no separate "only visible tracks" option, as track visibility in the UI (e.g., scrolling) does not affect rendering; output is determined solely by the enabled/disabled state. This behavior is consistent with previous versions and unchanged in Resolve 19.

Advanced features and integrations

AI-powered tools

DaVinci Resolve introduced the DaVinci Neural Engine in version 16, released in 2019, as an advanced machine learning system that leverages deep neural networks to enhance various post-production tasks. This engine operates on Apple silicon processors or compatible GPUs, enabling real-time processing of AI-driven features across editing, color grading, and visual effects workflows. By utilizing artificial intelligence, the Neural Engine automates complex operations that traditionally required manual effort, significantly improving efficiency for professional users.
Among the core AI tools powered by the Neural Engine, Magic Mask isolates specific objects or subjects in video footage for precise adjustments, such as isolating a person from a crowd without manual rotoscoping. In DaVinci Resolve Studio (version 18+, with AI Magic Mask 2 in version 20+), users can refine Magic Mask selections using paint strokes. After clicking the subject in the viewer to create an initial mask and tracking if needed, select "Add Paint Stroke" from the toolbar in the AI Magic Mask palette. Adjust brush size (via slider or Ctrl/Command + drag), then paint over areas to add to the mask frame-by-frame. Use "Subtract Paint Stroke" or similar options for removal. This helps fix gaps or errors in the AI-generated matte. Face Refinement enhances skin tone detection and refinement, allowing for targeted color corrections on facial features while preserving natural appearances. Super Scale upscales lower-resolution footage to higher resolutions, such as from HD to 4K, using AI to add detail and reduce artifacts. UltraNR provides AI-powered spatial noise reduction in the Color page, complemented by temporal noise reduction, and is widely regarded as highly effective for cleaning up noisy footage while preserving detail; it can be combined with temporal noise reduction for enhanced results on footage with motion. This tool is exclusive to DaVinci Resolve Studio. Additionally, Relight enables dynamic HDR adjustments by simulating lighting changes on subjects, facilitating creative relighting in post-production. Smart Reframe, another Neural Engine-powered tool, automatically reframes landscape (horizontal) clips to vertical aspect ratios (e.g., 9:16 for social media) by analyzing the scene and applying pan and tilt adjustments via keyframes to keep subjects centered and visible in the new frame. This is particularly useful for repurposing content for platforms like TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube Shorts.
To use Smart Reframe on the Edit page for reframing landscape clips to vertical: Right-click the timeline in the Media Pool and select Timeline Settings; uncheck "Use Project Settings"; enable "Use vertical resolution" (e.g., 1080x1920); set Mismatched Resolution handling to an option such as "Scale full frame with crop". Select the desired clips in the timeline. In the Inspector under the Video tab, expand the AI Smart Reframe controls if necessary, and click the Reframe button (located in the Transform section). Choose a mode such as Auto (for full pan and tilt), Pan Only, or Tilt Only to control the AI-assisted reframing. Manually fine-tune position, scale, or keyframes in the Transform controls if needed.
The free version includes AI speech-to-text transcription supporting multiple languages, including Chinese. This feature enables text-based editing in the Cut page, where modifying or deleting transcribed text automatically adjusts corresponding video clips, and detects and removes silent parts via transcription-linked tools.
Version 20, released in May 2025, expanded the AI toolkit with several innovative features designed to streamline editing and audio workflows. AI IntelliScript automatically generates timelines from a provided text script by matching transcribed audio in media clips to the script, constructing a timeline from the best selected takes with alternative takes placed on additional tracks for review. AI Set Extender, primarily available on the Edit and Cut pages, generates extensions to existing scenes or fills in missing frame areas based on simple text prompts, auto-generating content such as new backgrounds behind foreground objects to expand shots without additional footage. The Fusion page does not feature a native equivalent to Photoshop's AI Generative Fill for text-prompt-based selection filling or inpainting; Fusion focuses on node-based compositing, deep image processing, vector warping tools, and traditional VFX without built-in generative AI for content creation. AI Animated Subtitles creates dynamic, synchronized captions that animate in time with spoken words, enhancing accessibility and visual engagement. AI Multicam SmartSwitch (also referred to as Multicam SmartSwitch) automates angle selection in multicamera setups by analyzing audio and video cues to detect active speakers and automatically switch to the most appropriate camera angle. In DaVinci Resolve 20, the feature is located on the Edit page and can be accessed by right-clicking a multicam clip in the timeline and selecting "Multicam SmartSwitch" from the context menu, which opens a dialog window to configure settings (e.g., angle switching, minimum edit duration, wide angle options) and initiate the AI analysis for automatic switching of camera angles based on audio and video cues. It may also be accessible via the SmartSwitch option in the multicam viewer after creating a multicam clip. AI Voice Convert facilitates dubbing by transforming one voice into another while preserving intonation and timing, useful for localization or creative voice swaps.
While tools such as IntelliScript and Set Extender incorporate text inputs to assist with timeline assembly and scene extension, they are not equivalent to full generative text-to-video capabilities that create complete videos solely from text prompts without pre-existing media. As of February 2026, DaVinci Resolve version 20 lacks a native text-to-video generation feature capable of producing full videos from text prompts alone.
Performance optimizations in version 20.2.3, released in November 2025, deliver up to 4x faster processing for Depth Map generation, Magic Mask tracking, and Super Scale operations specifically on modern Intel GPUs in Windows environments. These improvements rely on compatible Intel hardware and updated drivers, reducing render times for AI-intensive tasks without compromising output quality.
Advanced AI features, including the full suite of Neural Engine tools such as UltraNRan AI-powered spatial noise reduction tool complemented by temporal noise reduction, widely regarded as highly effective for cleaning up footage while preserving detailare exclusive to the DaVinci Resolve Studio edition, limiting access in the free version to basic functionality. In contrast, Adobe Premiere Pro lacks comparable built-in advanced video noise reduction tools, focusing instead on AI-powered audio enhancements like Enhance Speech, with users often relying on third-party plugins for better video denoising results. The AI Voice Convert tool, in particular, raises ethical considerations around voice synthesis, such as consent for voice replication and potential misuse in deepfake applications, as highlighted in industry discussions on AI audio tools.

Fairlight and Fusion pages

DaVinci Resolve's Fairlight and Fusion pages integrate seamlessly into the application's unified post-production workflow, enabling users to switch between audio mixing, visual effects compositing, and other tasks without disrupting the project structure. This integration relies on a shared timeline that spans the Edit, Color, Fairlight, and Fusion pages, allowing non-destructive modifications where changes made in one page automatically propagate to others while preserving the original media.
The Fairlight page provides professional audio post-production tools modeled after traditional mixing consoles, including console emulation for tactile control layouts that mimic hardware faders, EQs, and dynamics processors. It features multi-channel metering capabilities supporting formats up to 7.1.4 immersive audio, with visual indicators for levels, phase, and spectral analysis across multiple tracks. Additionally, Fairlight enables export to stems, allowing users to output isolated audio groupssuch as dialogue, music, and effectsas separate multichannel files for further processing or delivery.
In contrast, the Fusion page offers a node-based environment for visual effects and motion graphics, highlighted by its 3D workspace that supports geometry, lighting, cameras, and particle systems within an OpenGL-accelerated viewer. This workspace includes tools like Camera 3D and Render 3D for creating immersive scenes, with options for stereoscopic rendering and depth-of-field effects. Fusion leverages GPU acceleration, including OpenCL for parallel processing in certain operations, to enable real-time playback of complex compositions. Scripting support via Lua and Python allows automation of workflows, such as custom tool creation and batch rendering, enhancing efficiency for repetitive tasks. Fusion does not include a native equivalent to Photoshop's AI Generative Fill for text-prompt-based selection filling, inpainting, or generative content creation. It focuses on node-based compositing, deep image processing, vector tools, and traditional VFX without built-in generative AI for content creation. Common workflows involve performing generative fill externally (e.g., in Photoshop on static frames), then importing the results into Fusion for tracking and compositing into motion footage. DaVinci Resolve 20 introduces the AI Set Extender, a generative AI tool that extends scenes by filling missing frame areas based on text prompts, primarily available in the Edit and Cut pages rather than Fusion.
Cross-page functionality further unifies these tools through features like compound clips, which bundle multiple elements into a single editable unit that can be opened and modified in Fusion or Fairlight without altering the source timeline. Adjustment layers apply effects across timeline segments in a non-destructive manner, propagating updates dynamically across pages via the shared timeline structure. This dynamic linking ensures that edits, such as audio balances in Fairlight or VFX refinements in Fusion, reflect immediately in the Edit and Color pages, streamlining iterative workflows.
Evolutions in recent versions have enhanced these pages' integration and performance. DaVinci Resolve 18, released in 2022, introduced improved Fairlight console support, including VCA fader groups and bus spill functionality for the Fairlight Desktop Console, enabling more precise control over large-scale mixes. In DaVinci Resolve 20, launched in 2025, Fusion gained clip accelerators that optimize rendering of Fusion clips within the timeline, reducing playback lag for GPU-intensive effects through enhanced pipelining and vector-based tools. These updates, alongside brief AI enhancements like neural engine-assisted tracking in both pages, continue to refine the non-linear, page-agnostic editing paradigm.

Collaboration and workflow tools

DaVinci Resolve supports multi-user collaboration through its integrated project server and Blackmagic Cloud, enabling teams to work simultaneously on shared projects without conflicts. Introduced in version 18 in 2022, Blackmagic Cloud allows users to host project libraries remotely, sync media files, and facilitate real-time editing across locations by creating a Blackmagic Cloud ID and setting up shared libraries. This system extends earlier multi-user features from version 17, which provided basic shared access but lacked cloud syncing.
DaVinci Resolve stores projects within project libraries (formerly known as project databases). A project library is a database file that contains one or more projects, including timelines, grades, clip metadata, visual effects, and audio mixing, but does not include the original media files. The default local project library is still named "Local Database" due to historical naming in earlier versions of the software, though there is no functional difference. Project libraries can be local (on the computer or attached storage), network-based for multi-user collaboration, or cloud-based via Blackmagic Cloud for remote access and syncing.
Transferring projects to another computer can be accomplished using the Project Manager to export and import individual projects. This method is useful for moving projects between machines or sharing with others outside of cloud-based collaboration.
To transfer a project:

On the source computer, open the Project Manager (Shift-1), right-click the project, and select "Export Project" to save it as a .drp file (containing project data such as timelines and grades, but not original media). Alternatively, choose "Export Project Archive" to create a .dra file that bundles the project with media, cache, and other assets if needed.

Copy the exported file (.drp or .dra) and any associated media files (if not bundled in an archive) to the target computer via external storage or network transfer.

On the target computer, in the Project Manager, right-click in the project area and select "Import Project" (for .drp files) or "Restore Project Archive" (for .dra files), then choose the file to import.

If media links break due to path differences, open the project, go to the Media Pool, select the offline clips, and use the "Relink Media" option to locate and reconnect the source files.

Alternatively, to transfer or back up an entire project library (including all projects within it), use the Database tab in the Project Manager: select the library, click the Details button, and use the Back Up button to create a backup file, which can then be restored on another system using the Restore button.
Key collaboration tools include project locking to prevent overlapping edits, where timelines or bins can be locked by one user while others view or edit elsewhere in the project. Built-in chat and annotations enable direct communication, with markers and notes attached to clips or timelines for feedback during remote grading sessions. Version history tracks changes, allowing users to revert to previous states and review edit logs for accountability in team workflows.
Workflow enhancements streamline professional pipelines, such as project servers for centralized shared libraries that support multiple artists accessing the same assets. Proxy workflows generate low-resolution media for offline editing on slower machines or in collaborative environments, with automatic relinking to high-resolution originals upon reconnection. The Proxy Handling modes (found under Playback > Proxy Handling) allow users to control whether proxy media or original source files are used for playback and editing: Disable All Proxies ignores all proxy files and forces use of the original source media for maximum quality (which may reduce performance on demanding footage); Prefer Proxies uses generated proxy files when available for faster playback and editing, falling back to originals if proxies are missing; Prefer Camera Originals prioritizes the original camera source files (such as RAW formats) over proxies for the highest image quality and detail, even if proxies exist, which is useful for color grading or critical viewing. These modes support collaborative and offline workflows by enabling performance optimization during editing while preserving access to high-quality originals for review and finishing.
Render queues integrate distributed processing, distributing tasks across networked machines or cloud resources for faster output in collaborative environments.
Interoperability with other editing software, such as importing timelines from Adobe Premiere Pro via XML, is supported but can encounter performance issues in versions 19 and 20. Large or complex Premiere XML imports may cause DaVinci Resolve to hang, freeze, or become unresponsive, particularly with extensive projects, network storage, or path discrepancies. A common and effective workaround is to manually import all referenced media files into the Media Pool prior to XML import, then import the XML with the "Automatically import source clips into media pool" checkbox unchecked. This avoids simultaneous media import operations that often trigger hangs. Additional steps include relinking any offline media after import, ensuring timecode and file names match precisely between the XML and source files, updating DaVinci Resolve to the latest version for improved XML compatibility, and simplifying the timeline in Premiere Pro (e.g., removing unsupported effects) before export.
Recent updates have advanced these capabilities: version 19, released in 2024, introduced multi-user timelines for simultaneous editing on different sections without full project locks.
Security features ensure safe sharing, with encrypted cloud sync protecting media and project data during transmission, alongside role-based access controls that assign permissions like view-only or edit rights to team members.

Compatibility and performance needs

DaVinci Resolve 20 is compatible with macOS Ventura 13.0 or later, Windows 10 or Windows 11 (including ARM variants), and Linux Rocky Linux 8.6. External GPU (eGPU) support is available on macOS systems with Thunderbolt connectivity, enabling enhanced performance on compatible hardware.
Blackmagic Design does not publish strict minimum system requirements on their site, but performance for multicam 4K editing depends on hardware. For smooth real-time playback and editing in multicam 4K workflows, recommended hardware includes: a multi-core CPU, 32GB or more system RAM, and a powerful discrete GPU (NVIDIA CUDA or AMD OpenCL compatible) with at least 8GB VRAM; multiple GPUs are supported for enhanced real-time performance with professional 10-bit formats and high-resolution footage up to 32K.
Minimum system configurations for the free edition include, for macOS and Windows: 16 GB of system memory (32 GB recommended when using the Fusion page for compositing or for 4K workflows); for Linux: 32 GB of system memory. An integrated GPU (using shared system memory), or a discrete GPU with at least 4 GB of VRAM, supporting OpenCL 1.2 (AMD/Intel) or CUDA 12.8 (NVIDIA), and a modern CPU such as an Intel Core i5 or AMD Ryzen 5 equivalent running at 2 GHz or higher. SSD storage is strongly recommended for media caching and playback to avoid bottlenecks, particularly with high-resolution footage.
For the Studio edition and demanding workflows like multicam 4K editing, 4K editing, 8K RAW processing, or multi-layer Fusion compositing, recommended specifications include 32 GB or more of RAM, a discrete GPU with 8 GB or greater VRAM (such as NVIDIA RTX 30-series or Apple M1 and later chips), and a multi-core CPU like an Intel Core i7 or AMD Ryzen 7 for optimal rendering speeds. Multiple GPUs are supported for enhanced real-time performance with high-resolution footage up to 32K. These setups ensure smooth real-time playback and acceleration of GPU-intensive effects, with NVIDIA GPUs benefiting from CUDA 12.8 optimizations for features like H.265 4:2:2 encoding/decoding.
Version 20 introduces performance enhancements including full support for NVIDIA Blackwell GPUs via CUDA 12.8, expanded Apple ProRes encoding on Windows and Linux platforms (previously macOS-only), and improved GPU acceleration for tools like the PanoMap and Spherical Stabilizer in Fusion. These updates reduce processing times for high-frame-rate content, such as 120 fps workflows, when paired with sufficient storage bandwidth from NVMe SSDs.
Common performance issues in Resolve 20 often stem from outdated GPU drivers, particularly NVIDIA cards requiring the latest Studio drivers to avoid configuration warnings and enable CUDA mode in preferences. Storage bandwidth limitations can cause choppy playback at high frame rates like 120 fps, resolvable by using optimized media or proxy files on fast SSD arrays. Additionally, changing the timeline to a vertical 9:16 aspect ratio (e.g., 1080x1920) can cause performance or playback issues due to timeline proxy resolution not updating correctly, making proxies appear ineffective. A common workaround is to go to Playback > Timeline Proxy Resolution and re-select "Full" (even if already selected) to reset proxy handling and cache; set proxy generation to "Half" resolution for better results in vertical workflows, regenerate proxies if needed, ensure proxy mode is enabled, clear the cache, or lower playback resolution for smoother editing. Excessive VRAM usage during complex timelines may lead to throttling, mitigated by clearing cache regularly and selecting appropriate GPU processing modes under system preferences.
