Fibrosis
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Fibrosis is a pathological process defined by the excessive deposition of extracellular matrix (ECM) components, such as collagen and fibronectin, leading to the overgrowth, hardening, and scarring of connective tissues in various organs. This aberrant wound-healing response disrupts normal tissue architecture, impairs organ function, and can progress to irreversible organ failure if uncontrolled.
Fibrosis affects virtually every organ system, including the lungs, liver, heart, kidneys, skin, and gastrointestinal tract, where it manifests as conditions like pulmonary fibrosis, cirrhosis, cardiac fibrosis, and renal sclerosis. It arises from chronic insults such as persistent inflammation, tissue injury, infections, toxins, radiation, or autoimmune disorders, often triggered by an imbalance in repair mechanisms that favors ECM accumulation over resolution. Central to this process is the activation and differentiation of fibroblasts into myofibroblasts, which excessively produce and deposit ECM proteins, compounded by factors like transforming growth factor- (TGF-) signaling and immune cell involvement.
As a leading contributor to morbidity and mortality worldwide, fibrosis underlies numerous chronic diseases and accounts for substantial healthcare burdens, with prevalence increasing due to aging populations and environmental exposures. While organ-specific therapies existsuch as antifibrotic drugs like nintedanib and pirfenidone for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosisgeneral treatments remain limited, prompting research into shared mechanisms like epithelial-mesenchymal transition and senescence for broader interventions. Early detection via biomarkers, including ECM proteins and profibrotic cytokines, is crucial for managing progression and improving outcomes.

Definition and Overview

Definition

Fibrosis is the pathological thickening and scarring of connective tissue, characterized by excessive deposition of extracellular matrix (ECM) components such as collagen and fibronectin. This aberrant repair process disrupts normal tissue architecture, leading to increased stiffness and potential organ dysfunction. Unlike physiological wound healing, which restores tissue integrity, fibrosis represents a dysregulated response that persists beyond the initial injury phase.
The process is generally progressive, driven by the activation, proliferation, and differentiation of fibroblasts into contractile myofibroblasts that excessively produce and remodel ECM. In its early stages, fibrosis holds potential for reversibility, particularly through mechanisms that promote myofibroblast dedifferentiation back to quiescent fibroblasts.
Histologically, fibrotic tissues exhibit dense bundles of collagen fibers replacing normal parenchymal elements, a feature readily identified using special stains such as Masson's trichrome, which highlights collagen in blue against a red counterstain for cellular components. This staining technique underscores the hallmark ECM accumulation that defines fibrosis across various tissues.

Historical Context

The understanding of fibrosis as a pathological process began in the 19th century with early pathological observations of scar tissue formation in various organs. German pathologist Rudolf Virchow, a pioneer of cellular pathology, provided foundational descriptions of connective tissue changes, including the role of spindle-shaped fibroblasts in tissue repair and sclerosis, which laid the groundwork for recognizing excessive scarring as a disease mechanism beyond normal healing. These observations built on earlier work, such as Ren Laennec's 1819 coining of "cirrhosis" to describe tawny, fibrotic liver tissue, initially limiting the concept to hepatic scarring.
In the 20th century, the terminology evolved from organ-specific terms like "cirrhosis" to the broader "fibrosis," reflecting recognition of similar scarring processes across multiple tissues during wound healing and chronic injury. Influential studies in the 1960s on granulation tissue contraction in skin wounds identified specialized fibroblastslater termed myofibroblastsas key mediators, linking normal repair to pathological fibrosis when persistent. This period also saw the identification of idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) as a distinct entity; while initial cases were reported by Hamman and Rich in the 1930s and 1940s, the 1950s marked its characterization as a chronic interstitial lung disease, often termed chronic Hamman-Rich syndrome, distinct from acute forms.
Key molecular insights emerged in the 1980s with the discovery of transforming growth factor- (TGF-) by researchers Anita Roberts and Michael Sporn, who demonstrated its role in inducing fibroblast proliferation, collagen synthesis, and rapid fibrosis in vivo, establishing it as a central driver of fibrotic responses. The 2010s brought clinical advancements, including the approval of antifibrotic drugs like pirfenidone, first licensed in the European Union in 2011 and the United States in 2014, which slowed IPF progression by modulating fibrotic pathways. These milestones shifted fibrosis from a descriptive histological finding to a targetable disease process with unified classifications across organs.

Pathophysiology

Cellular and Molecular Mechanisms

Fibrosis arises primarily from the activation of resident fibroblasts, which differentiate into myofibroblasts, specialized cells marked by the expression of alpha-smooth muscle actin (-SMA) integrated into stress fibers. This transition confers enhanced contractile forceapproximately twofold greater than that of quiescent fibroblastsand drives persistent ECM deposition, distinguishing myofibroblasts as the central effectors of fibrotic remodeling.
A key process contributing to the expansion of the myofibroblast population is epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT), in which epithelial cells lose polarity and adhesion molecules while gaining migratory and fibrogenic traits. Endothelial-mesenchymal transition (EndMT) similarly transforms endothelial cells into mesenchymal-like fibroblasts, facilitating their recruitment and amplifying the fibrotic response in injured tissues.
Cellular senescence, a state of irreversible cell cycle arrest, also plays a pivotal role in fibrosis progression. Senescent cells, including epithelial cells, fibroblasts, and endothelial cells, accumulate in fibrotic tissues and secrete the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP), comprising profibrotic factors like TGF-, IL-6, and IL-8. This SASP promotes myofibroblast differentiation, sustains inflammation, and impedes tissue repair, exacerbating ECM accumulation. As of 2024, studies highlight senescence as a driver across organ fibroses, with senolytics showing promise in preclinical models.
Inflammatory cells, particularly macrophages, contribute significantly by polarizing to an M2 (alternatively activated) phenotype, which secretes pro-fibrotic cytokines such as transforming growth factor- (TGF-), platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF), and interleukin-4 (IL-4), thereby promoting fibroblast proliferation and myofibroblast differentiation. Transforming growth factor- (TGF-) serves as a primary trigger for these cellular transitions.
The extracellular matrix (ECM) in fibrosis becomes dysregulated, dominated by fibrillar collagens type I and III, which form stiff, cross-linked deposits that replace normal tissue architecture, while elastin fibers undergo proteolytic degradation, reducing tissue elasticity. This imbalance involves matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), a family of zinc-dependent endopeptidases that normally remodel ECM but exhibit altered expression in fibrosisoften with insufficient activity of degradative MMPs like MMP-1 and MMP-3 relative to their inhibitors (TIMPs), perpetuating net matrix accumulation.

Key Signaling Pathways

The transforming growth factor- (TGF-) pathway represents a central orchestrator of fibrosis, initiating a cascade that drives extracellular matrix (ECM) deposition and myofibroblast differentiation. In its canonical form, latent TGF- is activated in the extracellular space, often through integrin-mediated mechanisms or protease cleavage, allowing the mature ligand to bind to the type II TGF- receptor (TRII). This recruits and activates the type I receptor (TRI, also known as ALK5), leading to phosphorylation of receptor-regulated Smads (Smad2 and Smad3) at specific serine residues. The phosphorylated Smad2/3 forms a heteromeric complex with Smad4, which translocates to the nucleus and interacts with DNA-binding partners to induce transcription of profibrotic genes, including COL1A1 encoding type I collagen, fibronectin, and connective tissue growth factor (CTGF). This pathway is markedly upregulated in fibrotic tissues, with TGF- expression often showing 5- to 10-fold increases compared to healthy states, as observed in models of pulmonary and hepatic fibrosis.
Beyond TGF-, platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) signaling prominently contributes to fibrosis by stimulating fibroblast proliferation and migration. PDGF ligands, particularly PDGF-BB and PDGF-DD, bind to dimeric PDGF receptors (PDGFR/), triggering autophosphorylation and activation of downstream cascades such as PI3K/Akt and MAPK/ERK pathways. These effectors promote cell cycle progression via cyclin D induction and cytoskeletal reorganization, resulting in enhanced fibroblast replication and chemotaxis toward injury sites. In fibrotic contexts, PDGF signaling sustains myofibroblast expansion, amplifying ECM production in synergy with TGF-.
The Wnt/-catenin pathway further exacerbates fibrosis by promoting epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT), a process linking epithelial injury to fibrotic remodeling. Upon Wnt ligand binding to Frizzled and LRP5/6 co-receptors, Dishevelled inhibits the -catenin destruction complex (comprising Axin, APC, GSK3, and CK1), stabilizing -catenin for nuclear translocation. There, -catenin complexes with TCF/LEF transcription factors to upregulate EMT-associated genes like SNAI1, TWIST, and matrix metalloproteinases, facilitating loss of epithelial markers (e.g., E-cadherin) and gain of mesenchymal traits. This pathway's activation in chronic fibrosis heightens fibroblast-like behavior in epithelial cells, contributing to persistent tissue scarring.
In scenarios of chronic injury, the Hedgehog (Hh) pathway activates to perpetuate fibrogenesis. Hh ligands (Sonic, Indian, or Desert Hh) bind Patched (Ptch1), relieving inhibition of Smoothened (Smo), which in turn activates Gli transcription factors (primarily Gli1 and Gli2). Nuclear Gli drives expression of target genes involved in cell survival and proliferation, including TGF- and PDGF, thereby amplifying myofibroblast recruitment and ECM synthesis in injured tissues. Aberrant Hh signaling in chronic settings, such as liver or lung injury, correlates with sustained inflammation-to-fibrosis transitions.
Regulatory feedback loops maintain these pathways' profibrotic activity, notably through autocrine mechanisms in myofibroblasts. Activated myofibroblasts secrete TGF-, which binds back to their surface receptors, reinforcing Smad signaling and sustaining -smooth muscle actin expression and contractility, thus preventing apoptosis and promoting persistence even after injury resolution. MicroRNAs, such as miR-21, modulate these loops by targeting negative regulators like Smad7, thereby enhancing TGF-/Smad responsiveness and boosting fibrotic gene transcription in fibroblasts. This miR-21 upregulation, often 3- to 5-fold in fibrotic models, creates a feed-forward amplification that resists resolution signals.

Etiology and Risk Factors

Primary Causes

Fibrosis primarily arises from initiating factors that disrupt normal tissue repair, leading to excessive extracellular matrix deposition. These causes can be broadly categorized into injury-based, autoimmune, genetic, and idiopathic origins, each triggering a cascade of inflammation and fibroblast activation that perpetuates fibrotic remodeling.
Injury-based causes often stem from chronic inflammation induced by persistent insults such as infections, toxins, or radiation. For instance, chronic hepatitis C virus infection is a leading etiological factor for hepatic fibrosis, where viral persistence drives ongoing hepatocyte injury and inflammatory responses that promote stellate cell activation and collagen accumulation. Similarly, inhalation of asbestos fibers causes pulmonary fibrosis, known as asbestosis, by eliciting a prolonged inflammatory reaction in the lung parenchyma, resulting in diffuse interstitial scarring. Radiation exposure, such as in therapeutic contexts, can also initiate fibrosis through direct cellular damage and subsequent inflammatory signaling in affected tissues.
Autoimmune diseases contribute to fibrosis by generating aberrant immune responses that target self-tissues, fostering chronic inflammation and tissue remodeling. Systemic sclerosis, a prototypic autoimmune disorder, features widespread fibrosis as its hallmark pathology, driven by vascular injury and autoantibody-mediated activation of fibroblasts, leading to excessive collagen deposition in skin and internal organs. Rheumatoid arthritis similarly promotes pulmonary fibrosis through synovial inflammation spillover and cytokine release, such as TNF- and IL-6, which stimulate myofibroblast differentiation and extracellular matrix production in the lungs.
Genetic causes involve inherited mutations that impair cellular homeostasis, predisposing individuals to fibrotic disorders. In familial idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF), heterozygous mutations in the TERT gene, which encodes telomerase reverse transcriptase, are a key driver; these mutations shorten telomeres, leading to epithelial cell senescence and defective repair, thereby initiating fibrotic progression in the lungs. Such genetic defects account for a notable proportion of heritable fibrosis cases, highlighting the role of telomere biology in disease susceptibility.
Idiopathic cases represent scenarios where no specific initiating factor is identifiable, comprising approximately 1737% of pulmonary fibrosis instances, as seen in IPF, which by definition excludes known causes and manifests as progressive lung scarring without evident triggers. These cases underscore the complexity of fibrosis etiology, where subtle or undetected insults may amplify signaling pathways like TGF- to drive unchecked fibrosis.

Predisposing Factors

Predisposing factors for fibrosis encompass both non-modifiable demographic elements and modifiable lifestyle, comorbidity, and environmental influences that elevate susceptibility across various forms of the condition.
Among non-modifiable factors, advanced age represents a primary demographic risk, with peak incidence observed in individuals over 50 years, particularly for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF), where the majority of cases occur in those aged 60 years or older. Male sex also confers a higher predisposition in specific fibrotic disorders, such as IPF, where men exhibit greater incidence rates compared to women, potentially due to historical differences in exposure patterns though the exact mechanisms remain under investigation. Genetic predisposition, including common variants like the MUC5B rs35705950 polymorphism, also plays a significant role, increasing the risk of IPF by approximately 3- to 7-fold.
Lifestyle factors significantly contribute to fibrosis risk, with cigarette smoking strongly linked to an increased likelihood of pulmonary fibrosis; ever-smokers face approximately a 1.6-fold higher odds compared to non-smokers. Similarly, chronic alcohol abuse heightens the risk of hepatic fibrosis, acting synergistically with metabolic factors to promote progression toward advanced liver disease.
Comorbid conditions like diabetes and obesity further predispose individuals by accelerating extracellular matrix (ECM) deposition through mechanisms involving hyperglycemia-induced activation of transforming growth factor- (TGF-), a key profibrotic cytokine that drives tissue remodeling in multiple organs.
Environmental exposures, including occupational hazards, play a critical role as modifiable risks; prolonged inhalation of silica dust is associated with pulmonary fibrosis in industries like mining and construction, where it triggers chronic inflammation leading to silicosis. Additionally, certain chemotherapy drugs, such as bleomycin and others used in cancer treatment, can induce pulmonary and other organ fibrosis as a side effect, with risk influenced by cumulative dose and patient factors.

Organ-Specific Manifestations

Pulmonary Fibrosis

Pulmonary fibrosis is a type of interstitial lung disease (ILD) involving the progressive scarring and stiffening of lung tissue, impairing gas exchange and leading to respiratory compromise. It manifests as either idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF), a form of unknown origin primarily affecting older adults, or secondary pulmonary fibrosis triggered by identifiable causes such as connective tissue diseases including systemic sclerosis and rheumatoid arthritis. In IPF, the etiology remains elusive but is linked to recurrent alveolar epithelial injury, genetic predispositions like MUC5B gene variants, and environmental exposures such as cigarette smoking or occupational dusts, though no single cause is definitive. Secondary forms often arise in the context of autoimmune conditions where systemic inflammation drives fibrotic changes in the pulmonary interstitium.
Clinically, pulmonary fibrosis presents with insidious onset of exertional dyspnea, a hallmark symptom reflecting reduced lung compliance and oxygenation, alongside a persistent dry cough that disrupts daily activities. Physical examination may reveal digital clubbing in up to 50% of cases, particularly in IPF, alongside fine inspiratory crackles on auscultation. Radiologically, the condition is characterized by honeycombing on high-resolution computed tomography (HRCT), representing cystic spaces indicative of advanced fibrosis in the subpleural regions. These features distinguish pulmonary fibrosis from other interstitial lung diseases by their progressive nature and association with usual interstitial pneumonia (UIP) pattern, involving temporal heterogeneity in fibrotic lesions.
The disease progresses relentlessly, with the UIP pattern on histopathology or imaging predicting a more aggressive course, leading to declining lung function and eventual respiratory failure. Median survival after diagnosis is approximately 3-5 years in IPF, influenced by factors like age and comorbidities, though secondary forms may have variable prognoses depending on the underlying condition. The primary complication is hypoxic respiratory failure due to ventilation-perfusion mismatch and diffusion impairment from scarred alveoli, often culminating in cor pulmonale or acute exacerbations. This underscores the need for early recognition, as general mechanisms of fibrosis, such as myofibroblast activation, amplify lung architecture distortion specific to pulmonary involvement.

Hepatic Fibrosis

Hepatic fibrosis represents a wound-healing response to chronic liver injury, characterized by excessive deposition of extracellular matrix (ECM) proteins, primarily by activated hepatic stellate cells, which disrupts normal hepatic architecture over time. This process is a precursor to cirrhosis and is driven by persistent inflammation and hepatocyte damage. Unlike fibrosis in other organs, hepatic fibrosis uniquely impacts metabolic and synthetic liver functions, potentially leading to systemic complications if it advances.
The main etiologies of hepatic fibrosis are chronic infections with hepatitis B virus (HBV) or hepatitis C virus (HCV), non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH), and chronic alcohol abuse. HBV and HCV cause ongoing necroinflammation, activating fibrogenic pathways that promote ECM accumulation around portal tracts and central veins. NASH, linked to obesity, insulin resistance, and metabolic syndrome, induces lipotoxicity and oxidative stress, fostering steatosis progression to fibrosis in 20-30% of cases over decades. Alcoholic liver disease involves acetaldehyde-mediated toxicity and gut-derived endotoxins, accelerating fibrosis in heavy drinkers through similar inflammatory mechanisms.
Staging of hepatic fibrosis typically employs the METAVIR system, with F0 indicating no fibrosis, F1 portal fibrosis without septa, F2 portal fibrosis with few septa, F3 numerous septa without cirrhosis, and F4 bridging fibrosis with cirrhosis. Advanced stages (F3-F4) feature bridging fibrosis that connects portal and central areas, causing nodular regeneration and vascular distortion. This leads to portal hypertension, clinically evident as esophageal varices, ascites, and hypersplenism, which increase risks of bleeding and infection.
Progression to cirrhosis occurs in approximately 20-30% of chronic HCV patients over 20 years, with faster rates in advanced fibrosis (F3) influenced by factors like age, alcohol co-use, and viral load; similar patterns hold for NASH and alcoholic etiology, though annual increments are slower (0.1-0.2 stages/year). Early-stage fibrosis (F0-F2) is largely reversible upon etiology removal, such as alcohol cessation, due to stellate cell apoptosis and ECM degradation. In contrast, advanced cases show partial reversibility but persistent risk of decompensation. A hallmark of progression is synthetic dysfunction, manifesting as hypoalbuminemia (<3.5 g/dL), prolonged prothrombin time, and reduced clotting factor production, reflecting hepatocyte loss and impaired protein synthesis.

Renal Fibrosis

Renal fibrosis, a pathological process characterized by the excessive accumulation of extracellular matrix in the kidney interstitium, represents a common endpoint in chronic kidney disease (CKD) and significantly contributes to the progression toward end-stage renal disease (ESRD). It primarily affects the tubulointerstitium, leading to structural remodeling that impairs renal function. Among the leading causes are diabetic nephropathy, where hyperglycemia induces glomerular and tubular injury culminating in fibrotic changes; hypertensive nephrosclerosis, driven by sustained vascular damage and ischemia; and obstructive uropathy, resulting from urinary tract blockage that triggers inflammatory and fibrogenic responses. These etiologies often initiate with inflammatory triggers, such as cytokine release and immune cell infiltration, which exacerbate matrix deposition as detailed in broader pathophysiology discussions.
Key histological features of renal fibrosis include tubular atrophy, where renal tubular epithelial cells undergo degeneration and loss, and interstitial expansion due to fibroblast activation and proliferation, leading to collagen-rich scar formation. In advanced stages, glomerulosclerosis emerges, with sclerosis of glomerular tufts further compromising filtration capacity and perpetuating tubulointerstitial damage. These changes disrupt the normal architecture of the nephron, reducing the kidney's ability to maintain homeostasis and filter waste products effectively.
The progression of renal fibrosis is typically monitored through the decline in estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), a key indicator of worsening renal function, with rates of decline varying by underlying cause but often accelerating in untreated cases. For instance, in diabetic nephropathy with macroalbuminuria, approximately 50% of patients progress to ESRD within 10 years, highlighting the rapid trajectory toward irreversible failure. A unique aspect of renal fibrosis is the role of proteinuria as an early marker, where increased urinary protein excretion signals initial glomerular barrier dysfunction and precedes overt fibrotic changes, allowing for potential early intervention. This marker is particularly valuable in distinguishing renal fibrosis from other organ-specific fibrotic processes, as it directly reflects urinary and filtration impairments central to kidney pathology.

Cardiac Fibrosis

Cardiac fibrosis refers to the pathological accumulation of extracellular matrix proteins, primarily collagen, in the myocardium, resulting in tissue stiffening and impaired cardiac function. This process is a common response to myocardial injury and chronic stress, leading to structural remodeling that affects both mechanical and electrical properties of the heart. Unlike replacement fibrosis in acute injury, reactive fibrosis in chronic conditions diffusely alters the myocardial architecture, contributing to systolic and diastolic impairments.
The primary etiologies of cardiac fibrosis include post-myocardial infarction scarring, hypertensive heart disease, and various cardiomyopathies. Following myocardial infarction, fibrosis forms as scar tissue to replace necrotic cardiomyocytes, preventing rupture but creating non-contractile regions that disrupt normal ventricular geometry. In hypertensive heart disease, sustained pressure overload induces myocardial fibrosis through activation of fibroblasts, leading to concentric hypertrophy and eventual stiffness. Cardiomyopathies, such as dilated and hypertrophic types, also promote fibrosis as a maladaptive response to genetic or inflammatory insults, exacerbating contractile dysfunction. Myofibroblasts play a central role in this fibrotic deposition across these conditions.
Key features of cardiac fibrosis encompass interstitial fibrosis, which diffusely infiltrates the myocardial interstitium, and perivascular fibrosis, which encircles coronary vessels and impairs microvascular perfusion. These changes increase myocardial stiffness, manifesting as diastolic dysfunction with prolonged relaxation and reduced ventricular filling, often evident through elevated filling pressures. Perivascular fibrosis further exacerbates ischemia and contributes to heterogeneous tissue properties.
Cardiac fibrosis progresses to heart failure by compromising ventricular compliance and pump efficiency, transitioning from compensated hypertrophy to decompensated systolic or diastolic failure. In advanced stages, it heightens the risk of adverse outcomes, including an annual hospitalization rate for heart failure of approximately 10-20% in affected patients. A unique consequence is arrhythmogenesis, where fibrotic strands create conduction heterogeneities that support re-entry circuits, predisposing to ventricular arrhythmias and sudden cardiac death.

Neurological Fibrosis

Neurological fibrosis refers to the excessive deposition of extracellular matrix (ECM) and scar formation in the central nervous system (CNS), arising as a pathological response to injury or disease that impedes neural repair. Unlike fibrosis in peripheral organs, which typically involves fibroblast-derived myofibroblasts, CNS fibrosis is dominated by glial contributions, particularly from astrocytes, leading to a multicellular scar that blends reactive gliosis with fibrotic elements. This process isolates damaged areas but often hinders functional recovery by creating barriers to regeneration.
The primary causes of neurological fibrosis include traumatic brain injury (TBI), multiple sclerosis (MS), and Alzheimer's disease (AD)-related neurodegeneration. In TBI, mechanical trauma triggers immediate cellular damage and inflammation, initiating fibrotic scar formation within days to weeks post-injury. MS involves autoimmune attacks on myelin, resulting in repeated demyelination episodes that provoke chronic scarring through persistent neuroinflammation. In AD, accumulation of amyloid-beta plaques and tau tangles induces microglial activation and astrocytic responses, fostering fibrotic changes in affected brain regions like the hippocampus and cortex.
Characteristic features encompass astrocytic gliosis, where resident astrocytes undergo hypertrophy, proliferation, and process extension to form a dense glial scar rich in ECM components such as collagens, laminins, and chondroitin sulfate proteoglycans (CSPGs). This is compounded by blood-brain barrier (BBB) disruption, which permits influx of peripheral monocytes and fibrocyte-like cells that deposit additional ECM and amplify the fibrotic response. TGF- signaling, activated by injury-induced inflammation, drives astrocytic transformation and ECM production in the CNS. The scar's progression creates both physical obstruction and inhibitory molecular cues, such as CSPGs and Nogo-A, that block axonal sprouting and remyelination, thereby perpetuating neural circuit dysfunction. Neurological fibrosis significantly contributes to long-term disability following TBI, manifesting as motor deficits, cognitive impairment, and sensory loss.
A key debate in the field concerns whether CNS scarring represents true fibrosis or primarily reactive gliosis, as the brain lacks abundant fibroblasts and relies on astrocytes for ECM synthesis. Reactive gliosis provides neuroprotective benefits like cytokine sequestration and BBB repair but transitions to inhibitory fibrosis when fibrocyte-like infiltrates and persistent TGF- elevate ECM rigidity. This hybrid naturegliosis-dominant yet fibrotic in outcomehighlights the need for targeted interventions that preserve gliosis's protective roles while attenuating scar-mediated regeneration failure.

Other Sites

Skin fibrosis, particularly in the context of systemic sclerosis (scleroderma), is characterized by progressive thickening and hardening of the dermis due to excessive collagen deposition. This leads to restricted mobility and often begins distally in the extremities before potentially involving proximal skin areas. Raynaud's phenomenon, a hallmark feature, manifests as episodic vasospasm of the digital arteries, causing pallor, cyanosis, and pain in the fingers upon cold exposure or stress, affecting up to 95% of patients with systemic sclerosis.
Bone marrow fibrosis, as seen in primary myelofibrosis, involves the replacement of normal hematopoietic tissue with fibrous stroma, impairing blood cell production and leading to cytopenias. This condition, a myeloproliferative neoplasm, promotes extramedullary hematopoiesis, where blood cell formation shifts to sites like the spleen and liver, resulting in organomegaly. The fibrosis arises from dysregulated cytokine signaling, such as transforming growth factor-beta, contributing to megakaryocyte hyperplasia and collagen accumulation.
Peritoneal fibrosis manifests as encapsulating peritoneal sclerosis, a severe complication primarily in patients on long-term peritoneal dialysis, where repeated exposure to dialysate induces peritoneal membrane thickening and sclerosis. This leads to encapsulation of the small bowel, causing bowel obstruction and malnutrition, with an incidence of 0.5-2% in dialysis populations. The process involves chronic inflammation and peritoneal mesothelial cell transformation into myofibroblasts, promoting fibrotic bands.
Systemic multi-organ fibrosis can occur rarely in amyloidosis, where misfolded protein deposits in tissues trigger secondary fibrotic responses across organs like the heart, kidneys, and gastrointestinal tract. In light-chain amyloidosis, the most common systemic form, amyloid infiltration disrupts organ architecture and induces fibrosis, leading to progressive dysfunction in multiple sites. These shared mechanisms of extracellular matrix remodeling echo those in organ-specific fibrosis, though amyloidosis uniquely involves protein aggregation as the initiator.

Diagnosis

Clinical Assessment

The clinical assessment of fibrosis begins with a comprehensive history taking to identify potential etiologies, risk factors, and disease progression. Clinicians systematically inquire about environmental and occupational exposures, such as inhalation of silica dust or asbestos in pulmonary fibrosis, chronic viral hepatitis B or C in hepatic fibrosis, or exposure to nephrotoxic drugs and heavy metals in renal fibrosis. Family history is elicited to uncover genetic predispositions, including conditions like alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, which can contribute to both pulmonary and hepatic fibrosis. The temporal pattern of onset is also explored, with most fibrotic processes presenting as insidious and chronic over months to years, though superimposed acute eventssuch as infections or drug reactionsmay accelerate symptoms in predisposed individuals.
Laboratory investigations are integral to clinical assessment, providing supportive evidence for organ involvement and aiding in differential diagnosis. For hepatic fibrosis, liver function tests often reveal elevated transaminases (ALT/AST) and reduced platelet count due to hypersplenism, with non-invasive scores like FIB-4 or APRI used for initial risk stratification. In pulmonary fibrosis, pulmonary function tests (PFTs) demonstrate a restrictive pattern with reduced forced vital capacity (FVC) and diffusing capacity for carbon monoxide (DLCO), typically showing FVC <80% predicted and DLCO <50% in moderate disease. Renal fibrosis evaluation includes serum creatinine and estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), where eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m indicates chronic kidney disease stages associated with interstitial fibrosis. Cardiac fibrosis may involve elevated B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP) levels and electrocardiographic changes, while autoantibodies (e.g., ANA) are checked in suspected autoimmune etiologies across organs.
Physical examination targets organ-specific signs to support the suspicion of fibrosis while evaluating overall systemic involvement. In pulmonary fibrosis, auscultation of the lungs often reveals fine, velcro-like inspiratory crackles, particularly at the bases, representing early fibrotic changes. For hepatic fibrosis, abdominal palpation may detect hepatomegaly or a firm liver edge in moderate cases, progressing to splenomegaly and ascites as portal hypertension develops. Renal fibrosis assessment includes inspection and palpation for bilateral flank tenderness and evaluation of fluid status, with lower extremity pitting edema indicating advanced glomerular involvement. In cardiac fibrosis, examination may uncover signs of congestive heart failure, such as elevated jugular venous pressure, peripheral edema, or a third heart sound, reflecting impaired ventricular compliance.
Symptom clustering in fibrosis commonly includes nonspecific manifestations like profound fatigue and unintentional weight loss, driven by chronic inflammation, increased metabolic demands, and cytokine-mediated cachexia across affected organs. These are frequently accompanied by organ-specific patterns, such as progressive exertional dyspnea and nonproductive cough in pulmonary involvement, right upper quadrant discomfort or early satiety in hepatic cases, and nocturia or lower limb swelling in renal fibrosis. Such clusters help differentiate fibrosis from other chronic conditions and guide initial management priorities.
To standardize symptom evaluation, particularly for respiratory compromise in pulmonary fibrosis, validated scoring systems like the modified Medical Research Council (mMRC) dyspnea scale are utilized. This tool categorizes breathlessness on a 0-4 scale based on functional limitation during daily activitiesranging from no dyspnea at rest (grade 0) to severe impairment preventing any exertion (grade 4)providing a simple, patient-reported measure of disease impact and progression.

Imaging Modalities

Imaging modalities play a crucial role in the non-invasive detection, characterization, and staging of fibrosis across various organs, providing quantitative and qualitative assessments that guide clinical management. These techniques leverage differences in tissue stiffness, density, contrast enhancement, and metabolic activity to identify fibrotic changes without the need for invasive procedures. Common approaches include ultrasound-based elastography, computed tomography (CT), magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), and positron emission tomography (PET), each offering unique advantages depending on the affected organ and disease stage.
Ultrasound elastography, particularly transient elastography using devices like FibroScan, is widely employed for assessing liver fibrosis by measuring liver stiffness in kilopascals (kPa). This technique applies a mechanical pulse to the liver and quantifies the propagation velocity of shear waves through the tissue, where higher stiffness correlates with increased fibrosis. For instance, liver stiffness values greater than 7 kPa are indicative of significant fibrosis (METAVIR stage F2 or higher), with cutoffs escalating to 12-14 kPa for advanced cirrhosis. Elastography is particularly valuable in chronic liver diseases such as hepatitis C or non-alcoholic steatohepatitis, offering a rapid, bedside assessment with high reproducibility when performed by trained operators.
High-resolution computed tomography (HRCT) is the cornerstone for evaluating pulmonary fibrosis, especially in idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF), where it reveals characteristic patterns such as reticulation, traction bronchiectasis, and honeycombingclustered cystic spaces typically 3-10 mm in diameter, predominantly subpleural and basal. Honeycombing on HRCT is a key diagnostic feature for usual interstitial pneumonia (UIP) pattern, essential for confirming IPF without biopsy in many cases. In cardiac fibrosis, MRI with late gadolinium enhancement (LGE) excels at detecting myocardial involvement, where gadolinium accumulates in fibrotic scar tissue due to expanded extracellular volume, appearing as hyperintense regions on inversion recovery sequences. LGE is a reliable marker of irreversible fibrosis in conditions like dilated cardiomyopathy or post-myocardial infarction, quantifying extent and correlating with arrhythmic risk. MRI also aids in hepatic and renal fibrosis staging through T1 mapping or diffusion-weighted imaging, though less commonly than elastography.
Positron emission tomography (PET) imaging is an emerging tool for distinguishing active inflammation from established fibrosis in fibrotic diseases, particularly pulmonary conditions. Using 18F-fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG), PET highlights areas of heightened metabolic activity in inflammatory cells, such as alveolar macrophages in IPF, where standardized uptake values (SUV) reflect disease severity and progression. FDG uptake is typically more pronounced in inflammatory phases than in quiescent fibrotic tissue, aiding in the differentiation of reversible versus irreversible changes; for example, heterogeneous lung uptake patterns can predict response to anti-inflammatory therapies. Novel tracers like fibroblast activation protein inhibitors (FAPI) are under investigation for directly targeting fibrotic fibroblasts, showing promise in quantifying fibrosis burden in interstitial lung diseases.
Despite their utility, imaging modalities for fibrosis have notable limitations. CT, including HRCT, involves ionizing radiation exposure, which accumulates with repeated scans and poses risks, particularly in younger patients or those requiring serial monitoring. MRI, while radiation-free and superior for soft-tissue contrast, is hindered by high costsoften double that of CTand longer scan times, limiting accessibility in resource-constrained settings. Additionally, both techniques can be affected by patient factors like motion artifacts or obesity, potentially reducing diagnostic accuracy.

Histopathological Confirmation

Histopathological confirmation remains the gold standard for diagnosing fibrosis, providing direct visualization of tissue architecture and extracellular matrix deposition through biopsy examination. Biopsy types vary by organ to minimize invasiveness while ensuring adequate sampling. For hepatic fibrosis, percutaneous needle biopsy is commonly performed, allowing assessment of collagen accumulation in the liver parenchyma. In renal fibrosis, ultrasound-guided percutaneous needle biopsy targets the kidney cortex to evaluate interstitial and peritubular changes. Pulmonary fibrosis diagnosis often requires surgical lung biopsy, typically via video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery (VATS), to obtain lung tissue showing characteristic patterns. Cardiac fibrosis is confirmed through endomyocardial biopsy, where samples from the right ventricular septum are analyzed for myocardial interstitial expansion. These procedures are frequently guided by imaging modalities to enhance precision and reduce risks.
Under microscopic examination, fibrosis is identified by increased collagen deposition, which disrupts normal tissue structure. Masson's trichrome staining is widely used to highlight collagen fibers in blue, distinguishing them from cellular components and enabling quantification of fibrotic areas across organs. In liver biopsies, Ishak or METAVIR scoring systems stage fibrosis severity; the Ishak score ranges from 0 (no fibrosis) to 6 (cirrhosis), while METAVIR categorizes from F0 (no fibrosis) to F4 (cirrhosis), based on the extent of portal, periportal, and perisinusoidal involvement. For pulmonary samples, usual interstitial pneumonia (UIP) patterns feature temporal heterogeneity, fibroblast foci, and honeycombing, confirming idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis when integrated with clinical data. Renal and cardiac biopsies similarly reveal interstitial collagen expansion, with perisinusoidal fibrosis in the liver serving as an early diagnostic indicator of progression from steatosis to advanced disease. Quantitative histological assessment, when performed, may use point counting or digital morphometry to estimate fibrosis extent, but diagnosis primarily relies on pattern recognition and semi-quantitative scoring such as the Ashcroft scale for lungs (0-8).
Diagnostic criteria emphasize quantitative and qualitative thresholds for fibrosis confirmation. In lung tissue, along with honeycomb changes, the presence of UIP pattern supports a fibrosis diagnosis. Hepatic perisinusoidal fibrosis, characterized by collagen deposition around sinusoids in zone 3, indicates early fibrotic stages and correlates with disease progression. These criteria, combined with scoring, guide staging and prognosis, though interobserver variability necessitates standardized protocols.
Biopsies carry risks, including bleeding and infection, with major complication rates reported at 1-2%. Hemorrhage occurs in approximately 1% of cases, potentially requiring intervention, while infections are rare but can lead to sepsis. Patient selection and procedural expertise mitigate these hazards.

Treatment and Management

Pharmacological Therapies

Pharmacological therapies for fibrosis primarily target underlying pathological processes such as inflammation, fibroblast activation, and extracellular matrix deposition, with efficacy varying by organ and etiology. In idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF), two antifibrotic agents are approved: pirfenidone and nintedanib. Pirfenidone, an oral pyridone derivative, exerts antifibrotic effects by downregulating transforming growth factor- (TGF-) expression at transcriptional and translational levels, thereby inhibiting fibroblast proliferation and collagen synthesis. In phase III clinical trials, such as the ASCEND study, pirfenidone reduced the annual rate of forced vital capacity (FVC) decline by approximately 48% compared to placebo (adjusted mean change of -235 ml/year versus -429 ml/year; P<0.001), preserving 20-30% of lung function over 52 weeks and improving progression-free survival. Similarly, nintedanib, a small-molecule tyrosine kinase inhibitor targeting platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF), vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), and fibroblast growth factor (FGF) receptors, slows fibrotic progression by blocking profibrotic signaling pathways. The INPULSIS trials demonstrated that nintedanib reduced the annual FVC decline by 31-68% in IPF patients (from -115 ml/year to -79 ml/year; P<0.001), with benefits extending to reduced acute exacerbations.
For cause-specific fibrosis, such as hepatic fibrosis driven by chronic hepatitis C virus (HCV) infection, direct-acting antivirals like sofosbuvir have revolutionized treatment by achieving sustained virologic response (SVR) rates exceeding 95%, effectively curing the infection and halting or reversing fibrosis progression. In the NEUTRINO trial, sofosbuvir-based regimens yielded SVR rates of 89-100% for HCV genotypes 1, 4, 5, and 6, with liver fibrosis regression observed in up to 70% of patients post-cure, as measured by noninvasive fibrosis scores. This etiological approach underscores the importance of addressing infectious drivers to prevent fibrotic advancement in the liver.
Anti-inflammatory agents, including corticosteroids, are employed for managing acute flares in fibrotic interstitial lung diseases (ILDs), where they suppress excessive immune responses and inflammation. High-dose corticosteroids (e.g., methylprednisolone 1 g/day for 3 days followed by oral prednisone taper) are commonly used in acute exacerbations of IPF or non-IPF fibrotic ILD, potentially improving short-term oxygenation and survival in select cases, though randomized evidence is limited and mixed, with some studies showing no overall mortality benefit but reduced progression in non-IPF subtypes. In autoimmune-associated fibrosis, such as connective tissue disease-related ILD (e.g., systemic sclerosis or rheumatoid arthritis), immunosuppressants like mycophenolate mofetil or azathioprine are standard adjuncts to corticosteroids, targeting T-cell activation and cytokine production to mitigate inflammatory-driven fibrosis. Phase III trials, including the Scleroderma Lung Study II, reported that mycophenolate mofetil was as effective as cyclophosphamide in preserving FVC over 24 months in systemic sclerosis-ILD (mean change +1.6% vs +0.3%), with favorable safety profiles compared to cyclophosphamide. Overall, phase III data across these therapies indicate up to 50% reductions in disease progression for targeted fibrotic conditions, emphasizing personalized application based on etiology and disease stage.

Non-Pharmacological Interventions

Non-pharmacological interventions for fibrosis encompass lifestyle modifications, supportive therapies, procedural options, and rehabilitation programs aimed at slowing disease progression, alleviating symptoms, and improving quality of life across affected organs. These strategies are particularly emphasized in pulmonary, hepatic, and renal fibrosis, where they address modifiable risk factors and provide essential support when pharmacological options are insufficient.
Lifestyle interventions play a crucial role in managing fibrosis, with smoking cessation demonstrating significant benefits in reducing disease progression. In patients with idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF), quitting smoking is associated with a relatively good prognosis compared to continued smoking, which accelerates lung function decline. Similarly, in non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) progressing to fibrosis, smoking cessation aids in disease management by mitigating hepatic damage. For non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH)-related liver fibrosis, achieving sustained weight loss through dietary modifications exceeding 10% of body weight can lead to reversal of early fibrosis stages, with studies showing fibrosis regression in up to 45% of such patients. These changes, often combined with structured exercise, target underlying contributors like metabolic syndrome without relying on medications.
Supportive therapies focus on symptom relief and organ function preservation in advanced fibrosis. In pulmonary fibrosis, supplemental oxygen therapy improves exercise capacity and endurance, significantly increasing walk times and oxygen saturation levels during activity. For end-stage renal fibrosis leading to chronic kidney disease, dialysis serves as a vital supportive measure to replace lost kidney function, preventing immediate life-threatening complications until transplantation becomes feasible.
Procedural interventions are reserved for severe, irreversible fibrosis. Liver transplantation offers curative potential for advanced hepatic fibrosis due to cirrhosis, with 5-year patient survival rates averaging around 75%, though outcomes vary by underlying etiology such as NAFLD. In select cases of IPF with heterogeneous upper-lobe predominant emphysema, lung volume reduction surgery can improve lung function and quality of life by removing hyperinflated areas, though it is not widely applicable and requires careful patient selection.
Rehabilitation programs, particularly pulmonary rehabilitation, enhance functional outcomes in fibrotic lung diseases. These structured interventions, involving exercise training and education, typically improve the 6-minute walk distance by approximately 50 meters in IPF patients, alongside reductions in dyspnea and fatigue. Such programs are recommended for maintaining physical capacity and delaying disability in progressive fibrosis.

Emerging Therapies

Stem cell therapy, particularly using mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs), has shown promise in modulating inflammation and reducing fibrosis in preclinical and early clinical studies across various organs. MSCs exert anti-fibrotic effects by secreting factors that inhibit pro-fibrotic pathways and promote tissue repair, with investigations focusing on liver and pulmonary fibrosis models. In phase II trials for liver fibrosis associated with non-alcoholic steatohepatitis, MSC infusions have demonstrated reductions in fibrosis scores by approximately 15-20% in histological assessments, alongside improvements in liver function markers.
Gene targeting approaches, such as CRISPR/Cas9 editing of TGF--related genes, represent investigational strategies in preclinical stages to disrupt fibrogenic signaling. These techniques aim to knock out or modulate genes like TGF- receptors in fibroblasts and hepatic stellate cells, preventing excessive extracellular matrix deposition in models of liver and pulmonary fibrosis. Studies have shown that CRISPR-mediated editing of TGF- pathway components reduces myofibroblast activation and collagen production by up to 50% in vitro and in animal models. Antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) targeting TGF- or related profibrotic factors are also in preclinical development, where they promote mRNA degradation to attenuate fibrosis progression in hepatic and corneal models.
Biologic therapies, including monoclonal antibodies against key cytokines, are advancing in clinical trials for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF). Anti-IL-13 monoclonal antibodies, such as tralokinumab, have been evaluated in phase II trials, demonstrating safety and potential modulation of inflammatory pathways involved in IPF progression, though efficacy endpoints like forced vital capacity decline were not significantly met in earlier studies. As of 2025, similar biologics like MTX-463, an anti-WISP1 monoclonal antibody, are in phase II trials for IPF, targeting downstream mediators of TGF- signaling to inhibit fibroblast proliferation.
Despite these advances, emerging therapies face significant challenges, including efficient delivery to fibrotic tissues in organs like the lung and liver, where poor vascularization and extracellular matrix barriers limit therapeutic access. Ethical concerns also arise, particularly with gene editing technologies like CRISPR, regarding off-target effects, long-term genomic stability, and equitable access in clinical translation.

Prognosis and Complications

Prognostic Indicators

Prognostic indicators in fibrosis encompass a range of biomarkers, clinical scoring systems, and genetic factors that help predict disease progression and patient outcomes across affected organs such as the lungs, liver, and heart. These tools enable clinicians to stratify risk, guide management decisions, and estimate survival probabilities, though their accuracy can vary by fibrosis type and stage. Early identification of high-risk features is crucial, as timely interventions have been shown to positively influence prognosis.
Among biomarkers, serum Krebs von den Lungen-6 (KL-6) is a well-established indicator for pulmonary fibrosis, particularly idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF). Elevated levels exceeding 1000 U/mL are associated with poorer survival and faster disease progression, reflecting increased epithelial damage and fibrotic activity. In liver fibrosis, the Fibrosis-4 (FIB-4) index, calculated using age, aspartate aminotransferase, alanine aminotransferase, and platelet count, serves as a non-invasive prognostic tool. Higher FIB-4 scores (>3.25) correlate with advanced fibrosis and increased risk of liver-related mortality, cardiovascular events, and all-cause death.
Clinical scoring systems further refine prognosis. The Gender-Age-Physiology (GAP) index for IPF integrates gender, age, forced vital capacity (FVC), and diffusion capacity for carbon monoxide (DLCO) to predict 1-, 2-, and 3-year mortality risks, with higher stages (e.g., stage III) indicating over 50% 1-year mortality in validation cohorts. For cardiac fibrosis in the context of heart failure, the New York Heart Association (NYHA) functional classification assesses symptom severity and exercise tolerance; advanced classes (III-IV) are independently linked to higher mortality rates, with 5-year survival dropping below 50% in NYHA IV patients.
Genetic factors also modulate outcomes, notably the MUC5B promoter polymorphism (rs35705950 T allele), which is prevalent in IPF and paradoxically associated with improved survival despite conferring disease risk. Carriers of this variant exhibit longer median survival times, potentially due to altered mucin production influencing fibrotic remodeling. Early intervention, such as antifibrotic therapies in IPF, can extend survival; for instance, initiation within the first year of diagnosis has been linked to 2- and 5-year survival rates of 89% and 52%, compared to lower rates without treatment.
Overall, 5-year survival in fibrosis ranges from 20% to 40%, depending on the organ: approximately 20-30% for untreated IPF, higher (up to 50%) with therapy in pulmonary cases, variable in liver fibrosis (e.g., 80-90% for compensated early stages but 14-20% for decompensated cirrhosis), and around 50% in cardiac fibrosis within heart failure cohorts.

Associated Complications

Fibrosis can lead to organ failure as a direct consequence of progressive tissue scarring and loss of function. In pulmonary fibrosis, particularly idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF), respiratory failure develops in a significant proportion of patients, often as a terminal event, with up to 50% progressing to severe hypoxemia requiring mechanical ventilation, where mortality rates range from 50% to 90%. In hepatic fibrosis advancing to cirrhosis, hepatic encephalopathy occurs in 30% to 45% of cases, manifesting as cognitive impairment due to toxin accumulation from impaired liver detoxification.
Systemic complications extend beyond the primary affected organ, exacerbating overall morbidity. Pulmonary hypertension frequently arises secondary to lung fibrosis, with prevalence estimates of up to 40% in IPF patients, driven by vascular remodeling and destruction of the pulmonary capillary bed. Cachexia, characterized by involuntary weight loss and muscle wasting, affects approximately 15% of IPF patients and is linked to persistent chronic inflammation promoting catabolic processes.
Multi-organ involvement is evident in conditions like cardiorenal syndrome, where concurrent cardiac and renal fibrosis creates bidirectional dysfunction, with fibrosis acting as a central pathological driver in up to 76% of chronic kidney disease cases complicated by heart involvement. This interplay leads to worsened fluid retention, electrolyte imbalances, and accelerated decline in both organs.
Long-term risks include heightened susceptibility to malignancy, as fibrosis fosters a pro-carcinogenic microenvironment; for instance, the lifetime risk of lung cancer in IPF patients is elevated to 10-15%, with about 15% developing it within five years of diagnosis.
