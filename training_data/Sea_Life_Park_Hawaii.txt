Sea Life Park Hawaii

 Sea Life Park Hawaii is a marine mammal park, aquarium, and avian sanctuary located on the windward coast of Oahu in Waimnalo, Hawaii, offering interactive animal encounters, educational exhibits, and conservation programs focused on native Hawaiian species.
Established in 1964 as a shark research facility, the park has evolved into a major tourist attraction, drawing millions of visitors over its six decades of operation while emphasizing marine conservation, education, and rehabilitation for animals with medical needs. It utilizes a natural flow-through seawater system for its habitats, which include a 300,000-gallon Hawaiian Reef Tank housing over 2,000 specimens such as sharks, sea turtles, and rays, as well as the Ocean Theater for dolphin, sea lion, and penguin shows.
Key attractions feature hands-on experiences like swimming with dolphins and whitetip reef sharks, feeding sessions with baby sharks, and exhibits highlighting endangered species such as Hawaiian monk seals and green sea turtles (honu). The park also maintains a Seabird Sanctuary for native Hawaiian birds and participates in the Honu Conservation Project to support sea turtle recovery efforts. Complementing its marine focus, the Aloha Kai Luau provides cultural entertainment with Polynesian dances and Hawaiian cuisine, held several evenings a week.
Acquired by Herschend Family Entertainment in May 2025following prior management by Parques Reunidosthe park operates under a sublease from the Hawaii Pacific University Oceanic Institute and continues to blend entertainment with environmental stewardship along Oahu's scenic Makapuu Point.

History

Founding and early development

Sea Life Park Hawaii was established in 1964 as a marine research and educational facility on the shores of Waimnalo, Oahu, by Taylor "Tap" Pryor and Karen Pryor through the Pacific Foundation for Marine Research. Initially conceived as an extension of the nearby Oceanic Institute, the park focused on shark research and broader marine biology studies to advance understanding of ocean ecosystems and support conservation efforts. The project secured a state land lease in 1960 from the Hawaii Department of Land and Natural Resources (DLNR) for the Makapuu Oceanic Center site, which encompassed the park, an aquarium, and research facilities, marking an early effort to integrate scientific inquiry with public access to Hawaii's marine heritage.
The park's opening on September 13, 1964, highlighted its dual role in research and outreach, with initial animal acquisitions including dolphins and sea lions captured from local Hawaiian waters to populate exhibits and training programs. Karen Pryor, serving as head trainer, pioneered marine mammal training techniques, developing the first dolphin shows in Hawaii using behavioral science and positive reinforcement methods that emphasized animal creativity and welfare. These shows, featuring species like spinner and rough-toothed dolphins, served as an educational platform to demonstrate marine intelligence and foster public appreciation for ocean conservation, while contributing to early publications on cetacean behavior.
Early development faced challenges in balancing scientific objectives with growing public demand, leading to operational strains on the lease agreement and facility resources under DLNR oversight. The integration with the Oceanic Institute allowed for shared expertise in marine education, but financial challenges, including overextension in undersea mining and fish farming, led to bankruptcy and a reorganization in 1972, resulting in the spin-off of Sea Life Park as a separate entity from the institute. This foundational period established Sea Life Park as a pioneer in Hawaii's marine attractions, influencing subsequent ownership transitions.

Ownership changes and expansions

In 2008, Sea Life Park Hawaii was acquired by the Madrid-based Parques Reunidos Group for an undisclosed amount, marking a significant shift in its management as the park came under the operational oversight of Palace Entertainment, Parques Reunidos' U.S. subsidiary. This acquisition integrated the park into a broader portfolio of global attractions, leading to operational enhancements focused on visitor experience and maintenance, though specific financial details of the transaction remained private.
During Parques Reunidos' ownership from 2008 to 2025, the park faced several controversies related to animal welfare and operations. In 2015, the acquisition of a false killer whale and two dolphins without required federal permits drew criticism and legal scrutiny from animal rights groups and local residents. The park also incurred USDA fines for violations including improper handling and feeding of animals, and in 2018, it was investigated by the Hawaii Department of Labor & Industrial Relations following $130,000 in safety violation fines. Ongoing concerns about captive animal health, including deaths and injuries, highlighted debates over the park's conservation versus entertainment focus.
The park's lease structure, governed by the Hawaii Department of Land and Natural Resources (DLNR), has played a pivotal role in ownership transitions. Sea Life Park operates via a sublease from the Oceanic Institute, which merged with Hawaii Pacific University in 2013 and holds the master lease from DLNR for the 62-acre site, originally designated for marine research and public education, set to expire in 2027. The master lease, set to expire in 2027, required DLNR approval for subleases, and the 2013 merger prompted reviews of operational continuity, including consents for sub-subleases to ensure compliance with state land use regulations. No major legal controversies directly tied to the 2008 acquisition emerged, but the lease framework has necessitated ongoing state oversight for any ownership or structural changes.
Under Parques Reunidos' ownership, the park underwent key expansions in the 2010s and 2020s to modernize facilities and enhance animal habitats. In 2018, a $30 million improvement plan was announced, including renovations to the Shark Cave's reef tank completed in 2020, aimed at improving water quality and exhibit immersion. By 2023, further upgrades progressed, such as rebuilding the entry lobby with a Polynesian theme, constructing a climate-controlled Ocean Oddities Indoor Exhibit, and enhancing dolphin and sea lion lagoons with improved filtration systems and viewing areas. A five-year renovation plan outlined in 2023 also introduced new sea lion habitats and expanded educational spaces, reflecting a commitment to sustainability and visitor accessibility while adhering to DLNR lease terms.
In early 2025, Parques Reunidos sold its U.S. operations, including Sea Life Park, to Herschend Family Entertainment, the Atlanta-based operator of attractions like Dollywood, in a deal announced on March 18, 2025, and completed on May 27, 2025. This transition allows Herschend to broaden its family-oriented portfolio in the Pacific region, with plans for enhanced conservation programs and facility integrations, while Parques Reunidos refocuses on European markets. The sale required DLNR review due to the sublease structure, but no reported legal disputes arose, positioning the park for potential future expansions under new management.

Location and facilities

Site and grounds

Sea Life Park Hawaii is situated in Waimnalo on the windward coast of Oahu, Hawaii, at coordinates 211847N 1573947W, near Makapuu Point and approximately 15 miles east of Honolulu. The park occupies a coastal position along Kalanianaole Highway, providing direct oceanfront access and integrating with the island's rugged shoreline.
The site is nestled beneath the dramatic Koolau Mountains, offering panoramic views of surrounding natural landmarks including Koko Head, Sandy Beach, Hanauma Bay to the south, Mnana Island (Rabbit Island), and the Makapuu Lighthouse. This positioning enhances the park's connection to local ecosystems, with ocean breezes and proximity to native Hawaiian marine habitats emphasizing its role in coastal conservation. The grounds blend with the tropical landscape, featuring native vegetation and pathways that wind through open-air areas to promote an immersive experience in the windward region's biodiversity.
Spanning approximately 62 acres under a sublease from the Hawaii Pacific University Oceanic Institute, which holds the master lease from the State of Hawaii, the park's layout includes landscaped grounds with meandering walkways that facilitate exploration while preserving the natural topography. Environmental design incorporates sustainable elements, such as a flow-through seawater system drawn from on-site wells and filtered through lava rock and sand to replicate natural ocean conditions, minimizing ecological disruption. These features support habitat integration and include efforts like replanting native beach sandalwood to bolster coastal stability.

Infrastructure and amenities

Sea Life Park Hawaii features a prominent 300,000-gallon main aquarium tank that houses over 2,000 marine specimens, including sharks, moray eels, eagle rays, and colorful reef fish, providing visitors with an immersive underwater viewing experience. The park also includes several specialized lagoons, such as the Dolphin Lagoon designed for bottlenose dolphins and a snorkeling lagoon stocked with whitetip reef sharks and reef fish, which support interactive marine habitats while maintaining natural water flow and quality. Additionally, the Hale Manu Bird Aviary serves as a key structure, enclosing a serene environment for native and exotic bird species, including lovebirds and cockatiels, with open-air design elements that promote species observation. As of November 2025, the aviary is temporarily closed due to avian influenza concerns.
Visitor amenities at the park emphasize convenience and comfort, with ample free parking available in designated lots equipped with smoking areas. Restrooms are strategically placed throughout the grounds, including locations near the Rocky Shores exhibit, Hawaii Ocean Theatre, Sea Lion Habitat, and Beach Boy Lanai, ensuring accessibility during extended visits. Dining options include family-friendly restaurants offering affordable meals, such as those at the food court area, while gift shops like the main Gift Shop near the Honu exhibit and the Seaside Boutique overlooking the Sea Lion Feeder Pool provide Hawaii-inspired souvenirs, apparel, jewelry, and marine-themed items. Accessibility features comprise wheelchair rental services at the ticket office, along with paved paths suitable for mobility aids, and first aid stations for immediate support. Locker rentals and cash-to-card conversion machines are also provided at key points, such as the entrance and Shark Cave area, to facilitate a seamless experience.
Behind-the-scenes facilities support the park's operational needs, including a veterinary clinic where on-site veterinarians conduct routine health examinations and care for marine and avian residents. Water filtration systems are integral to maintaining habitat conditions, with aquarists performing regular quality checks and maintenance dives in the tanks and lagoons to ensure optimal salinity and cleanliness. Staff areas, though not publicly detailed, include operational quarters for the animal care team, which comprises certified scuba divers responsible for daily habitat upkeep. Following the park's acquisition by Herschend Family Entertainment from Parques Reunidos in 2025, initial priorities focused on operational continuity without immediate structural changes.

Exhibits

Marine life exhibits

Sea Life Park Hawaii features several exhibits dedicated to marine life, showcasing a variety of ocean-dwelling species in naturalistic habitats designed to educate visitors on their behaviors and conservation needs. The Dolphin Lagoon serves as a central attraction, housing 15 bottlenose dolphins, including both Atlantic and Pacific varieties distinguished by their size, coloration, and dorsal fin shapes. This expansive lagoon allows visitors to observe the dolphins' playful interactions and agile swimming from dedicated viewing areas, emphasizing their intelligence and social structures through trainer-guided presentations.
The sea lion and seal exhibits highlight pinnipeds native to Pacific waters, with the Sea Lion Habitat featuring California sea lions that engage in natural behaviors such as basking, diving, and vocalizing. Adjacent displays include the Hawaiian Monk Seal Habitat, home to an individual named Kekoa, a critically endangered species known for its expert diving abilities using four flippers, alongside other rescued California sea lions. These enclosures provide underwater and above-water viewing opportunities, accompanied by educational talks that detail differences between seals and sea lions, such as the absence of external ear flaps in seals.
Aquarium sections offer immersive views of reef ecosystems, including the Shark Cave, a 300,000-gallon tank inhabited by native Hawaiian species like sandbar sharks, whitetip reef sharks, and blacktip reef sharks. Complementary areas such as the Hawaiian Reef Lagoon and Discovery Reef Pool display vibrant Hawaiian reef fish, stingrays, and other shoreline creatures, with interactive elements like touch pools in select zones to illustrate biodiversity and ecological roles. These exhibits incorporate trainer-led discussions on shark feeding and ray gliding behaviors to foster understanding of marine food webs.
The Penguin Habitat provides a unique contrast with its focus on Humboldt penguins, flightless seabirds from the coasts of Chile and Peru that "fly" underwater at speeds up to 9 mph. This climate-adapted enclosure uses approximately 12 million gallons of fresh, nutrient-rich seawater pumped daily, mimicking their natural saltwater environment while allowing observation of their counter-shaded plumage, molting cycles, and diving adaptations. Daily talks highlight threats like overfishing and habitat loss, underscoring the penguins' threatened status and the importance of coastal conservation.

Avian and rehabilitation exhibits

The Sea Bird Sanctuary at Sea Life Park Hawaii serves as a dedicated refuge for native Hawaiian seabirds, providing a permanent home for injured or non-releasable individuals while emphasizing conservation efforts. This 500-square-foot facility houses species such as the Laysan albatross, black-footed albatross, wedge-tailed shearwater, Newell's shearwater, Hawaiian petrel, red-footed booby, brown booby, and great frigatebird, many of which are federally protected and face significant threats in the wild. The sanctuary features flight aviaries designed to mimic natural habitats, allowing birds to exhibit soaring behaviors with their long wings and countershading feathers adapted for marine life.
Complementing the sanctuary is the Hale Manu Bird Aviary, a serene enclosure showcasing a mix of native Hawaiian and exotic bird species, though it has been temporarily closed since October 25, 2025 due to avian influenza concerns on Oahu to prioritize resident bird health and safety. Visitors can engage in interactive bird encounters here when open, such as close-up observations that highlight the birds' unique adaptations like salt glands for expelling seawater and webbed feet for swimming. Educational signage throughout both exhibits explains Hawaiian seabird ecology, including nesting habits in burrows, trees, or colonies, shared parental duties, and anatomical features like strong bills for diving.
The park's rehabilitation programs focus on rescuing and treating injured marine birds. Previously operating a 24/7 Seabird Rehabilitation Facility that admitted 300 to 800 seabirds annually, with a success rate of 75 to 85 percent leading to release back into the wild, the facility is currently under renovation as of November 2025. During this period, reports of downed seabirds should be directed to partner organizations such as the Hawaii Wildlife Center or the Department of Land and Natural Resources. Since 2005, over 5,000 seabirds have been rescued, rehabilitated, and released, including notable efforts for endangered chicks like those of the wedge-tailed and Newell's shearwaters during peak fall migration seasons. The recovery process involves nursing birds back to health from injuries caused by disorientation from lights, predation by introduced species such as cats, dogs, and mongooses, or ingestion of plasticsthreats that endanger Hawaii's seabird populations. Non-releasable birds find lifelong care in the sanctuary, integrating rehabilitation with broader conservation themes through displays that educate on these ecological pressures and the importance of protecting native species.

Visitor activities

Shows and presentations

Sea Life Park Hawaii offers a series of scheduled shows and presentations that highlight the behaviors and biology of its resident marine animals, blending education with demonstrations of natural abilities such as swimming, diving, and feeding. These programs, held daily in dedicated habitats, aim to inform visitors about conservation efforts and the ecological roles of species like dolphins, sea lions, turtles, and sharks, while incorporating Hawaiian storytelling traditions known as "talk story" sessions.
The park's flagship presentation is the Aloha Naia show at the Dolphin Lagoon, performed daily at 12:15 p.m., where bottlenose dolphins demonstrate agile leaps, synchronized swims, and interactive behaviors guided by trainers to showcase their intelligence and social dynamics. This 20- to 30-minute program explores the cultural significance of dolphins, or "naia" in Hawaiian, emphasizing their historical connections to Polynesian voyaging and marine stewardship.
Sea lion presentations occur twice daily at 10:45 a.m. and 3:30 p.m. in the Sea Lion Habitat, featuring California sea lions performing vocalizations, flips, and dives to illustrate their prowess as members of the Pinnipedia order, which includes expert divers adapted with four flippers for propulsion. Titled Kupaa and Liis Journey, the talk integrates Hawaiian narratives about resilience ("kupaa") while educating on sea lion biology, conservation challenges, and occasional appearances by a Hawaiian monk seal to highlight endangered species recovery.
Daily Honu Turtle Feeding and Talk Story sessions at 10:15 a.m. and a follow-up talk at 1:45 p.m. in the Honu Habitat focus on the park's unique captive breeding colony of green sea turtles, the only active one under human care in the United States, where visitors observe feeding routines and learn about rehabilitation, annual releases of 200 to 800 hatchlings into the wild, and the turtles' cultural reverence in Hawaiian lore as symbols of longevity and navigation.
The Shark Cave Feeding Frenzy, held at 2:00 p.m. from the upper viewing level, provides insights into whitetip reef sharks from the Indian and Pacific Oceans as they exhibit hunting strategies and competitive feeding behaviors during a controlled demonstration, accompanied by explanations of Hawaii's shark diversity and myth-busting on their ecological importance.
Over time, the park's presentations have shifted emphasis toward educational content and cultural integration, moving from earlier entertainment-heavy formats to programs that prioritize animal welfare through rigorous training protocols and veterinary oversight, ensuring safe, low-stress interactions that align with modern conservation standards.

Interactive programs

Sea Life Park Hawaii offers a variety of hands-on interactive programs that allow visitors to engage directly with marine animals under the guidance of trained professionals, emphasizing safety and animal welfare. These experiences are designed for families and individuals, with specific age and health requirements to ensure participant suitability. All programs include general park admission and must be booked in advance online, with life vests provided for water-based activities to maintain safety standards.
Dolphin encounters provide immersive opportunities to interact with bottlenose dolphins, focusing on natural behaviors and enrichment activities led by animal care experts. The Dolphin Exploration program involves a deep-water swim where participants, ages 8 and older, can swim alongside the dolphins in their habitat, learning about their intelligence and care routines during a guided session. For a shallower option, the Dolphin Encounter allows waist-deep interactions for ages 4 and up, including touching and observing the dolphins as they perform behaviors in a controlled environment. The Dolphin Aloha is a dry-land experience suitable for ages 1 and older, offering a 20-minute behind-the-scenes look at dolphin enrichment without entering the water, ideal for younger children or those with mobility concerns. Health guidelines require participants to be free of open wounds, pregnant individuals to consult a doctor, and anyone with medical conditions to notify staff beforehand; all encounters prioritize non-invasive contact to avoid stressing the animals.
Sea lion interactions center on California sea lions, with trainer-guided sessions that highlight conservation efforts through feeding and touch opportunities. The Sea Lion Encounter is a 20-minute dry-land program for ages 8 and older, where participants feed the sea lions, touch their flippers, and engage in one-on-one and group interactions while learning about their behaviors and rescue stories. Safety is emphasized through direct supervision, with rules against sudden movements or direct eye contact to respect the animals' space; this program, introduced in 2022, underscores the park's commitment to rehabilitated sea lions.
The Hawaiian Reef snorkel program enables visitors to explore the park's 300,000-gallon lagoon habitat up close, snorkeling among Hawaiian reef fish and whitetip reef sharks in a secure, netted area. Available for ages 4 and older, this experience includes feeding tropical fish under trainer guidance, providing a hands-on introduction to reef ecosystems without direct shark contact. Participants receive a safety briefing on snorkel use and marine etiquette, such as avoiding coral touch, to protect the environment and ensure a controlled interaction.
Behind-the-scenes tours offer guided insights into animal care, integrated into select encounters like the Dolphin Aloha, where visitors observe preparation areas and learn operational details from staff. These tours, limited to small groups, require adherence to hygiene protocols, such as hand washing, and prohibit photography in sensitive zones to safeguard animal privacy.
Additional options include the Aloha Kai Luau, a cultural evening event that incorporates park access for daytime interactive animal experiences prior to the show, blending Hawaiian traditions with marine themes through storytelling about ocean connections. Held Wednesday through Sunday, it features family-friendly activities like lei-making alongside the buffet and performances, with bookings recommending arrival by early afternoon for animal program participation. Post-2022 additions, such as the baby shark feeding session during the Shark Cave Feeding Frenzy, allow supervised observation and basic interaction with young whitetip reef sharks at 2:00 p.m. daily, focusing on feeding techniques and shark biology for ages 4 and up, enhancing visitor engagement with reef species.

Conservation and education

Environmental initiatives

Sea Life Park Hawaii implements ocean sustainability programs aimed at reducing environmental impacts on Hawaiian marine ecosystems. The park has transitioned to using completely plant-based and compostable cups, plates, and cutlery across its facilities to minimize single-use plastics and microplastics entering the ocean. These efforts support broader advocacy for reef protection by promoting reduced waste that contributes to coral degradation.
The park collaborates with local organizations on habitat restoration and cleanup initiatives. In partnership with Waimnalo Limu Hui, a community-based group, Sea Life Park supports the restoration of native limu (algae species essential for maintaining healthy reef ecosystems in Waimnalo Bay. Additionally, it partners with the nonprofit 808 Cleanups to organize beach cleanup events, such as the January 2024 event at adjacent Kaup Beach, where volunteers remove marine debris to protect coastal habitats. These collaborations emphasize community involvement in preventing pollution and habitat degradation through educational outreach.
The Conservation Center features rotating exhibits and films that educate visitors on environmental challenges, including the impacts of climate change on Hawaiian waters, such as ocean acidification and rising temperatures affecting coral reefs. These interactive displays, incorporating hands-on elements and artwork, foster public awareness of sustainability practices and conservation needs.
A cornerstone of the park's initiatives is its long-term Hawaiian green sea turtle conservation program, which has released over 17,000 hatchlings into the wild since its inception more than 40 years ago. Annual releases of 200 to 800 juveniles occur between May and September, often in collaboration with partners like Mauna Lani Bay Resort and Maui Ocean Center, contributing to the species' status improvement from endangered to threatened. Hatchlings are tracked using passive integrated transponder (PIT) tags to monitor survival and migration patterns in Hawaiian waters.

Research and animal care

Sea Life Park Hawaii collaborates closely with the Oceanic Institute, an adjacent research facility affiliated with Hawaii Pacific University, on marine biology initiatives focused on species native to Hawaiian waters. This partnership supports studies on marine mammals, including bottlenose dolphins and false killer whales, particularly in the areas of hearing capabilities and responses to underwater noise pollution. For instance, in 2015, the University of Hawaii's Marine Mammal Research Program relocated three long-term research subjectsa false killer whale and two bottlenose dolphinsto the park after over two decades of study at the Hawaii Institute of Marine Biology, ensuring continued access to specialized care while advancing acoustic research. While specific shark research is less documented, the park's facilities enable observational studies on whitetip reef sharks as part of broader marine ecosystem projects through this collaboration.
The park has a long history of Hawaiian monk seal rehabilitation dating back to the 1980s, in partnership with NOAA Fisheries. Starting in 1987, it rescued and treated underweight or orphaned pups, such as the seal "Lambchop," who arrived emaciated from French Frigate Shoals and received comprehensive care including cataract surgery and husbandry training, ultimately living to 32 yearsbeyond the species' average lifespan of 25-30 years. Between 1989 and 1991, the program rehabilitated 16 pups for release to remote atolls like Kure and Midway, achieving notable success: births at Kure Atoll rose from one in 1986 to 14 in 1991, attributed to the influx of healthier, younger seals with a female-biased sex ratio that bolstered population recovery. Protocols emphasize strict quarantine with disinfectant measures and separate handling gear, comprehensive health screenings (including blood work, fecal parasite exams, and titers for diseases like distemper and toxoplasmosis), and nutritional rehabilitation via initial force-feeding of herring or tilapia until self-feeding is achieved (averaging 14-17 weeks), targeting a release weight of at least 150 pounds, with an average of 156 pounds met. Non-releasable seals receive lifelong care under 24-hour veterinary oversight to prevent human interactions in the wild.
Animal welfare at Sea Life Park Hawaii adheres to rigorous standards set by the Alliance of Marine Mammal Parks and Aquariums (AMMPA), of which it is a member, ensuring compliance with the U.S. Animal Welfare Act through regular inspections. These standards encompass specialized diets tailored to species needssuch as fresh fish for dolphins and seals, supplemented with vitaminsand behavioral enrichment programs, like puzzle feeders and habitat simulations, to promote natural behaviors and reduce stress. Health monitoring involves daily veterinary checks, routine blood analyses, and environmental controls for water quality and temperature, with the park cited for excellent overall compliance in federal reviews.
Recent efforts include conservation and breeding programs for Humboldt penguins, an endangered species from the coasts of Chile and Peru. Since the park's early days, it has maintained one of the few successful captive breeding colonies, expanding from an initial group of four in the 1970s to over ten individuals through optimized husbandry techniques, such as artificial burrows made from cement pipes to mimic natural nesting. These programs contribute to species management by studying reproductive success and health in controlled environments, supporting global conservation strategies amid declining wild populations due to habitat loss and overfishing. In addition, the park briefly supports turtle release programs as part of its broader rehabilitation work.

In popular culture

Film and television appearances

Sea Life Park Hawaii has served as a filming location for several notable films and television productions, highlighting its marine exhibits and scenic coastal setting. The park prominently features in the 2004 romantic comedy 50 First Dates, directed by Peter Segal, where protagonist Henry Roth, a marine veterinarian played by Adam Sandler, works at the facility. Key scenes, including the dolphin encounter involving characters portrayed by Sandler and Drew Barrymore, were shot on-site at the park's dolphin lagoons and surrounding areas on Oahu's windward coast. Interior aquarium shots, however, were filmed at Six Flags Marine World in California to accommodate production needs.
The park has also appeared in multiple episodes of the classic television series Hawaii Five-O (19681980), utilizing its underwater facilities and oceanarium for marine-themed sequences. For instance, in the 1972 episode "Cloth of Gold," investigators explore a poisoning case involving a rare shellfish, with scenes filmed in the park's aquarium where divers interact with exhibits. Similarly, the 1971 episode "The Burning Ice" incorporates the location for action sequences near the water tanks, showcasing the park's infrastructure as a backdrop for crime drama. Additional episodes, such as "The Ransom" (1970), also utilized the site. The park featured in episodes of Magnum, P.I. (19801988), including "Old Acquaintance" and "Rapture" (both 1985), for scenes involving its aquatic environments. These appearances leveraged the park's authentic aquatic environments to enhance the shows' portrayal of Hawaiian island life.
Filming at Sea Life Park has boosted its visibility in popular media, drawing fans of these productions to visit the site for its recognizable features. The exposure from 50 First Dates in particular has encouraged tourists to seek out the dolphin habitats and aquarium, associating the park with the film's romantic Hawaiian imagery. The park's media center continues to support such collaborations, facilitating commercial shoots that further promote its role in entertainment.

Other media references

Sea Life Park Hawaii appears prominently in travel literature as a symbol of accessible marine education and family-oriented ocean exploration on Oahu. The Ultimate Hawaii Islands Travel Guide 2025 recommends it for interactive wildlife encounters, including dolphin and sea lion programs that educate visitors on Hawaiian marine ecosystems. Similarly, the 2025 Hawaii Official Statewide Visitors' Guide portrays the park as an engaging, interactive site on Oahu's eastern coast, emphasizing its role in fostering appreciation for local sea life through exhibits and presentations. A 2001 publication, Sea Life Park Hawaii: Come Play With Us, details the park's habitats and conservation messaging, positioning it as a gateway to understanding Hawaii's underwater biodiversity.
The park's conservation initiatives have garnered mentions in environmental media, including news features on unique animal events that highlight its rehabilitative work. In 2005, the birth of a wholphina rare false killer whale-bottlenose dolphin hybrid calfdrew national attention, with NBC News reporting on the event as a milestone in marine mammal studies at the facility. Local outlets have covered animal releases and intakes, such as the 2022 relocation of two California sea lions requiring permanent care, noted by Hawaii Parent for underscoring the park's commitment to marine rescue operations. The facility's seabird rehabilitation efforts, which have resulted in over 5,000 releases since 2005, including more than 800 in a single season, are documented in park announcements and regional news.
In broader cultural contexts, Sea Life Park Hawaii connects to Hawaiian traditions through its luau programs, which incorporate storytelling and performances that echo folklore surrounding sea creatures. The green sea turtle, or honu, central to the park's breeding program, holds deep significance in Hawaiian lore as a symbol of longevity, peace, and navigation guidance for ancient voyagers, as highlighted in park educational content. These events blend traditional Hawaiian music and hula with marine themes, reinforcing the cultural reverence for ocean life.
