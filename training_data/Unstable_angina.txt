Unstable angina

1x
 Unstable angina is a form of acute coronary syndrome characterized by sudden, often severe chest pain or discomfort due to reduced blood flow to the heart muscle, typically resulting from a non-occlusive thrombus forming on a disrupted atherosclerotic plaque in a coronary artery, without evidence of myocardial necrosis. Its incidence has been declining in recent years due to improved diagnostic biomarkers that distinguish it from NSTEMI. It is distinguished from stable angina by its unpredictable onset, frequently occurring at rest or with minimal exertion, and its potential to progress rapidly to a myocardial infarction, making it a medical emergency requiring immediate evaluation. As part of the spectrum of coronary artery disease, unstable angina is a common acute manifestation of coronary artery disease, with over 500,000 hospitalizations annually in the United States alone, and is a leading cause of morbidity and mortality, with prompt intervention critical to prevent complications.
The hallmark symptom of unstable angina is angina pectoris, described as a pressure-like, squeezing, or burning sensation in the chest that may radiate to the arms, neck, jaw, shoulders, or back, lasting longer than 10-20 minutes and not fully relieved by rest or nitroglycerin. Accompanying features often include shortness of breath, diaphoresis, nausea, vomiting, dizziness, or anxiety, particularly in high-risk presentations where symptoms worsen progressively or occur with hemodynamic instability such as hypotension. Unlike stable angina, these episodes are new-onset, crescendo (increasing in frequency or severity), or occur at rest, signaling acute myocardial ischemia.
The primary cause of unstable angina is coronary atherosclerosis, where fatty plaques build up in the arterial walls, leading to partial obstruction; a plaque rupture then triggers platelet aggregation and thrombus formation, temporarily reducing blood flow without complete occlusion. Less common etiologies include coronary artery spasm (as in Prinzmetal's variant) or embolism, while increased myocardial oxygen demand from conditions like anemia, tachycardia, or hypertension can exacerbate ischemia in susceptible individuals. Risk factors include advanced age, male sex, smoking, diabetes mellitus, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, obesity, and a family history of premature coronary disease, all of which accelerate plaque formation and instability.
Diagnosis begins with a detailed history and physical examination to assess symptom pattern and rule out mimics like aortic dissection or pulmonary embolism, followed by an electrocardiogram (ECG) showing transient ST-segment depression, T-wave inversions, or normal findings in up to 20% of cases. Serial cardiac biomarkers, such as troponin levels, are essential to confirm the absence of necrosis and differentiate from non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI); elevated levels indicate infarction rather than pure unstable angina. Definitive evaluation often involves coronary angiography to visualize the extent of coronary artery disease, guiding risk stratification and therapeutic decisions.
Initial management of unstable angina focuses on hospitalization, oxygen supplementation if hypoxic, and anti-ischemic therapy including sublingual nitroglycerin, beta-blockers to reduce heart rate and contractility, and morphine for pain relief in severe cases. Antiplatelet agents like aspirin and P2Y12 inhibitors (e.g., clopidogrel), combined with anticoagulation (e.g., heparin), are administered to prevent thrombus progression, while statins address underlying atherosclerosis. For high-risk patients, early revascularization via percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stenting or coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) is recommended to restore blood flow, significantly improving outcomes compared to medical therapy alone.
The prognosis for unstable angina varies based on factors such as the number of affected coronary arteries, left ventricular function, and timeliness of treatment, with untreated cases carrying a high risk of myocardial infarction, heart failure, arrhythmias, or sudden death. With contemporary management, including dual antiplatelet therapy and lifestyle modifications (e.g., smoking cessation, diet, exercise), one-year risk of myocardial infarction or death is approximately 7-11%.

Definition and Background

Definition

Unstable angina is defined as a type of acute coronary syndrome (ACS) characterized by myocardial ischemia without evidence of myocardial necrosis, typically manifesting as new-onset severe angina, angina that is worsening in frequency or severity, or angina occurring at rest. This condition arises from reduced blood flow to the heart muscle due to coronary artery obstruction, often from plaque rupture and thrombus formation, but without the permanent damage seen in infarction.
It is distinguished from stable angina, which involves predictable chest pain triggered by exertion and relieved by rest, whereas unstable angina is unpredictable and can occur with minimal activity or at rest, signaling a higher risk of progression to myocardial infarction. In contrast to non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI), unstable angina lacks biochemical markers of necrosis, such as elevated troponin levels, though both fall under non-ST-elevation ACS.
In recent years, the widespread adoption of high-sensitivity cardiac troponin assays has led to a substantial decline in the incidence of diagnosed unstable angina, as minor myocardial injury is now frequently detected and classified as non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI) instead.
The concept of unstable angina has evolved historically to emphasize its acute and high-risk nature within coronary artery disease syndromes. A seminal contribution was the 1989 Braunwald classification, which stratifies risk based on clinical severity (e.g., rest vs. exertional pain), duration of episodes, and precipitating factors (e.g., secondary to conditions like anemia or primary without such triggers), aiding in prognostic assessment and therapeutic decisions.
Unstable angina is included in the broader ACS spectrum alongside ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) and NSTEMI, representing a continuum of ischemic events where prompt intervention is crucial to prevent adverse outcomes.

Classification

Unstable angina (UA) is classified as a form of acute coronary syndrome (ACS) characterized by transient myocardial ischemia without evidence of myocardial necrosis, distinguishing it from non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI), which involves troponin elevation indicating necrosis, and ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI), which features persistent ST-segment elevation on electrocardiogram due to transmural injury. In UA, cardiac troponin levels remain normal or show no significant rise, reflecting the absence of detectable myocardial damage, whereas both NSTEMI and STEMI exhibit elevated biomarkers.
In the International Classification of Diseases, 10th Revision, Clinical Modification (ICD-10-CM), unstable angina is coded as I20.0, which encompasses synonyms such as crescendo angina, accelerated angina, de novo effort angina, worsening effort angina, intermediate coronary syndrome, and preinfarction syndrome.
The Braunwald classification provides a clinical framework for categorizing UA based on severity, clinical circumstances, and treatment context. It divides cases into three main classes by severity: Class I encompasses new-onset or accelerated angina without rest pain occurring within the previous 2 months; Class II includes angina at rest that is subacute, having occurred within the past month but not within the preceding 48 hours; and Class III denotes severe angina at rest within the preceding 48 hours. Each class is further subdivided by clinical circumstances: subtype A for secondary UA triggered by extracardiac conditions such as anemia or tachycardia; subtype B for primary UA without such precipitants; and subtype C for postinfarction UA arising within 2 weeks of an acute myocardial infarction. Additionally, subscripts indicate responsiveness to treatment: 1 for rest angina without prior antianginal therapy, 2 for angina despite adequate conventional therapy, and 3 for angina despite maximal antianginal therapy including intravenous nitrates. While the Braunwald classification provided a foundational framework, current guidelines, such as the 2025 ACC/AHA ACS guideline, emphasize validated risk scores like TIMI and GRACE for prognostication and management decisions in unstable angina and NSTE-ACS.
Risk stratification in UA and NSTEMI often employs the Thrombolysis in Myocardial Infarction (TIMI) risk score, a 7-point tool predicting the 14-day risk of death, myocardial infarction, or urgent revascularization. The score assigns 1 point for each of the following: age 65 years; at least 3 risk factors for coronary artery disease (family history, hypertension, hypercholesterolemia, diabetes, or current smoking); known prior coronary stenosis 50%; ST-segment deviation on presenting electrocardiogram; at least 2 anginal events within the prior 24 hours; recent use of aspirin in the last 7 days; and elevated cardiac markers. Higher scores correlate with increased event rates, ranging from approximately 5% for scores of 0-2 to over 40% for scores of 5-7, facilitating decisions on invasive versus conservative management.
The Global Registry of Acute Coronary Events (GRACE) risk score complements TIMI by estimating in-hospital and 6-month mortality across ACS, including UA. It incorporates variables such as age, heart rate, systolic blood pressure, Killip class, cardiac arrest at admission, serum creatinine, elevated cardiac enzymes, and ST-segment deviation, with additional factors for postdischarge prediction like in-hospital percutaneous coronary intervention. Validated in diverse cohorts, the score demonstrates strong discriminatory power (c-statistic 0.70) for UA patients, enabling tailored risk assessment and resource allocation in both non-ST-elevation ACS and broader ACS contexts.

Epidemiology and Risk Factors

Epidemiology

Unstable angina, as a subset of acute coronary syndrome (ACS), contributes significantly to the global burden of cardiovascular disease, which caused an estimated 19.2 million deaths worldwide in 2023, accounting for approximately 32% of all global deaths. In the United States, approximately 150,000 patients are hospitalized annually with unstable angina, though recent trends indicate a decline due to improved preventive strategies and diagnostic reclassification. This condition represents about 10-15% of all ACS presentations, with higher incidence rates in developed countries compared to developing regions, where rates are rising due to urbanization and lifestyle changes in areas like Asia. Overall, the annual incidence in industrialized countries is approximately 6 per 10,000 persons. The incidence of unstable angina has declined in recent decades due to improved diagnostics, such as high-sensitivity troponin tests, which better distinguish it from NSTEMI.
Demographically, unstable angina predominantly affects individuals in the 50-70 age group, with a mean presentation age of 62 years. Age-adjusted rates are higher in men, with a male-to-female ratio of about 2:1 until age 75, after which the rates equalize as postmenopausal women experience increased risk. Black individuals tend to present at younger ages and with more risk factors, while women, comprising 39-40% of cases, are on average 5 years older at diagnosis and have higher rates of comorbidities such as hypertension and diabetes. Globally, prevalence varies by region, with higher burdens in Western populations but emerging increases in low- and middle-income countries, where over 75% of cardiovascular deaths occur.
Mortality trends for unstable angina show improvement over time, reflecting advances in acute care and prevention. In-hospital mortality ranges from 2% to 5%, lower than the 5-10% observed for non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI). Long-term outcomes indicate a 1-year mortality rate of approximately 10% without intervention, though overall ACS mortality has declined substantially since 2000 due to better risk factor management. Unstable angina accounts for a notable portion of the 371,506 coronary artery disease-related deaths in the US in 2022, underscoring its role in overall cardiovascular morbidity.

Risk Factors

Unstable angina shares risk factors with coronary artery disease (CAD), categorized as non-modifiable and modifiable. Non-modifiable risk factors include advanced age, with risk increasing after 45 years in men and 55 years in women due to progressive atherosclerosis. Male sex confers higher risk until menopause, after which rates converge between sexes. Family history of premature CAD in a first-degree relativediagnosed before age 55 in men or 65 in womenelevates susceptibility by 1.5- to 2-fold. Genetic predispositions, such as familial hypercholesterolemia, further heighten risk through inherited lipid abnormalities leading to early plaque formation.
Modifiable risk factors play a dominant role in unstable angina development and can be targeted for prevention. Smoking substantially increases risk, with current smokers facing 2- to 4-fold higher odds of acute coronary syndromes compared to nonsmokers, and a dose-response relationship evident as pack-years accumulate. Hypertension, defined as systolic blood pressure exceeding 140 mmHg, contributes to endothelial damage and plaque progression, raising CAD risk by approximately 1.5- to 2-fold. Dyslipidemia, characterized by low-density lipoprotein cholesterol above 130 mg/dL or low high-density lipoprotein levels, promotes atherogenesis and is associated with 2- to 3-fold increased risk of unstable events. Diabetes mellitus doubles to quadruples the risk of unstable angina through accelerated vascular inflammation and thrombosis. Obesity (body mass index >30 kg/m), sedentary lifestyle, and diets high in saturated fats exacerbate insulin resistance and lipid imbalances, each independently elevating risk by 1.5- to 2-fold.
Emerging risk factors include chronic kidney disease, which independently raises the likelihood of presenting with unstable angina rather than stable forms by promoting systemic inflammation and calcification. Air pollution exposure, particularly to fine particulate matter (PM2.5) and carbon monoxide, acutely heightens unstable angina risk, with short-term elevations linked to 10-20% increased odds of onset. Psychosocial stress, such as depression or chronic job strain, increases CAD risk by 1.5- to 2-fold according to meta-analyses, via heightened sympathetic activity and cortisol effects.
Attributable risks underscore the impact of key modifiable factors: smoking accounts for about 20-30% of CAD cases, while dyslipidemia contributes similarly, together explaining roughly 50% of unstable angina attributable to modifiable elements, with stronger effects in dose-dependent exposures.

Clinical Presentation

Signs and Symptoms

Unstable angina typically presents with substernal chest pain or pressure sensation, often described as tightness, heaviness, or discomfort, lasting more than 10 to 30 minutes, often longer than in stable angina and not fully relieved by rest or nitroglycerin. This symptom may radiate to the jaw, neck, shoulders, arms (left or both), back, or epigastrium and represents a change from prior stable angina, manifesting as new-onset severe episodes, a crescendo pattern with increasing frequency, duration, or intensity, or occurrence at rest.
Associated symptoms frequently accompany the chest discomfort and include shortness of breath (dyspnea), profuse sweating (diaphoresis), nausea, vomiting, lightheadedness, palpitations, or profound fatigue. These may predominate or occur without classic chest pain, particularly in high-risk scenarios.
Atypical presentations are common in women, the elderly, and individuals with diabetes mellitus, occurring in up to 40-60% of cases in these groups and featuring isolated jaw or neck pain, epigastric discomfort, upper abdominal pain, unexplained dyspnea, fatigue, or silent ischemia, including in some patients with diabetes. Precipitating factors differ from stable angina, with episodes triggered by minimal exertion, emotional stress, or occurring unpredictably at rest, including nocturnal awakenings; unlike stable angina, symptoms often persist despite rest and fail to resolve promptly with nitroglycerin. The crescendo pattern specifically involves an increase in frequency (e.g., more than three episodes per day), duration, or severity compared to prior stable angina over the preceding month.

Physical Examination

The physical examination in patients with suspected unstable angina often yields nonspecific findings, but certain objective signs can indicate myocardial ischemia or its complications. Vital signs may reveal tachycardia with a heart rate exceeding 100 beats per minute, reflecting increased sympathetic activity or underlying heart failure, particularly in elderly patients. Hypertension or hypotension can also be observed, with systolic blood pressure below 100 mm Hg signaling potential hemodynamic compromise. Tachypnea may accompany these changes due to associated pulmonary congestion or anxiety. In high-risk cases, signs of cardiogenic shock may emerge, including systolic blood pressure less than 90 mm Hg, cool extremities, and altered mental status, which demand immediate attention.
Cardiac auscultation frequently uncovers abnormalities suggestive of ventricular dysfunction or valvular involvement. An S3 or S4 gallop rhythm is commonly detected, arising from impaired left ventricular compliance during ischemia. A new or worsening mitral regurgitation murmur may be present, resulting from papillary muscle dysfunction due to regional ischemia. Paradoxical splitting of the second heart sound can occur secondary to delayed left ventricular ejection. Elevated jugular venous pressure and a dyskinetic or sustained apical impulse further support transient myocardial impairment.
General inspection often shows diaphoresis and pallor, indicative of autonomic activation and reduced cardiac output. Anxiety may manifest as restlessness, though it is an observable behavioral sign rather than a subjective report. Peripheral evidence of atherosclerosis is frequently noted, such as carotid bruits, diminished or absent pulses in the extremities, and xanthomas signaling hyperlipidemia. High-risk indicators include pulmonary edema, evidenced by rales extending beyond one-third of the lung fields, and overall hemodynamic instability, which correlate with adverse outcomes.

Pathophysiology

Atherosclerotic Plaque Instability

Unstable angina arises primarily from the chronic atherosclerotic process in the coronary arteries, where lipid-rich plaques progressively accumulate, leading to luminal narrowing and potential ischemia. These plaques develop through the infiltration of lipids into the arterial wall, promoting the formation of foam cells and extracellular lipid pools that expand into a necrotic core. Stable plaques typically feature a thick fibrous cap and smaller necrotic cores, maintaining vessel patency despite moderate stenosis. In contrast, unstable plaques are prone to disruption due to their structural vulnerability, characterized by a thin fibrous cap measuring 65 m in thickness, a large necrotic core comprising at least 40% of the plaque cross-section, and dense inflammatory cell infiltration by macrophages and T-cells that undermine plaque integrity.
Plaque rupture represents the most common trigger for unstable angina, accounting for 60-70% of cases in acute coronary syndromes, wherein fissures in the thin fibrous cap expose the thrombogenic necrotic core to circulating blood, precipitating partial thrombus formation and transient vessel occlusion. Plaque erosion, occurring in 25-30% of instances, arises without cap rupture through superficial endothelial denudation, often over pathological intimal thickening or fibroatheromas, similarly resulting in luminal compromise. These disruptions reduce coronary blood flow, but unlike myocardial infarction, the occlusion in unstable angina is typically incomplete, preserving myocardial viability while inducing demand ischemia.
Inflammation plays a pivotal role in plaque instability, with pro-inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-1 (IL-1) and tumor necrosis factor- (TNF-) recruiting and activating macrophages within the plaque, which in turn secrete matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) that degrade the extracellular matrix of the fibrous cap. This inflammatory milieu weakens the cap's collagen framework, increasing rupture susceptibility, and is reflected systemically by elevated levels of high-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hsCRP), a biomarker associated with plaque vulnerability and adverse outcomes in unstable angina.
The resulting partial occlusion from plaque disruption creates a supply-demand mismatch in myocardial oxygen delivery, where reduced perfusion fails to meet cardiac metabolic needs during stress, manifesting as angina without evidence of necrosis. Risk factors like dyslipidemia and smoking accelerate this atherosclerotic buildup, fostering the lipid-laden, inflammatory environment conducive to instability.

Thrombotic and Vasospastic Mechanisms

In unstable angina, the primary thrombotic mechanism involves the formation of a partial, non-occlusive thrombus superimposed on a disrupted atherosclerotic plaque, which reduces coronary blood flow and precipitates myocardial ischemia. This process begins with plaque rupture exposing subendothelial components such as collagen and von Willebrand factor (vWF), which rapidly activate circulating platelets under high shear conditions in the coronary arteries. Platelet adhesion to vWF via glycoprotein Ib-IX-V receptors and subsequent activation through collagen binding to glycoprotein VI lead to platelet aggregation and the release of pro-thrombotic mediators. Concurrently, tissue factor expressed on the plaque surface or from infiltrating inflammatory cells initiates the extrinsic coagulation pathway, forming a complex with factor VIIa that activates factor X to Xa; factor Xa then converts prothrombin to thrombin, which cleaves fibrinogen into fibrin to stabilize the growing thrombus as a mesh-like structure. The dynamic nature of this thrombusprone to intermittent growth, embolization, and partial dissolutionresults in the characteristic waxing and waning pattern of ischemic symptoms observed in unstable angina.
Vasospasm contributes to ischemia in a subset of unstable angina cases, particularly the Prinzmetal variant, which accounts for approximately 5-10% of episodes and involves focal or diffuse constriction of coronary arteries, often at sites of underlying atherosclerotic plaques. This spasm arises from endothelial dysfunction, which impairs nitric oxide-mediated vasodilation and promotes vasoconstrictor release, compounded by heightened alpha-adrenergic tone from sympathetic activation or catecholamine surges. Magnesium deficiency has also been implicated, as low levels reduce calcium channel blockade in vascular smooth muscle, exacerbating hypercontractility and spasm susceptibility in up to 45% of affected patients. These mechanisms can occur independently or synergistically with thrombosis, transiently narrowing the vessel lumen and intensifying ischemia without complete occlusion.
Microvascular involvement in unstable angina manifests as the no-reflow phenomenon, where distal embolization of thrombotic debris or platelet aggregates into small coronary vessels impairs perfusion despite patency of the epicardial artery. This obstruction, often accompanied by microvascular spasm due to released vasoconstrictors like thromboxane A2, leads to localized ischemia in the myocardial territory supplied by affected arterioles. Such events contribute to prolonged or refractory symptoms and are more prevalent in cases with significant plaque disruption.
Endogenous fibrinolysis plays a crucial role in limiting thrombus progression in unstable angina, often preventing full occlusion and distinguishing it from myocardial infarction. Tissue plasminogen activator (tPA), released from endothelial cells in response to thrombin or ischemia, converts plasminogen to plasmin, which degrades fibrin within the thrombus. Elevated tPA antigen levels during acute episodes reflect this compensatory response, helping to maintain partial vessel patency and resolve non-occlusive thrombi episodically. Impaired fibrinolysis, however, can prolong thrombotic burden and worsen outcomes.

Diagnosis

Clinical History and Examination

The clinical history is pivotal in suspecting unstable angina, characterized by a change in the pattern of chest discomfort that suggests acute coronary syndrome. Patients typically describe substernal pain or pressure that is new in onset, more severe, prolonged (often lasting more than 10-20 minutes), or occurring at rest, differing from prior stable angina episodes. The quality is usually described as squeezing, tightness, heaviness, or burning, potentially radiating to the arms, neck, jaw, back, or epigastrium, and may be provoked by minimal exertion, emotional stress, or occur unpredictably without triggers. Aggravating factors include physical activity or stress, while relief with rest or sublingual nitroglycerin is less consistent than in stable angina.
Associated symptoms often accompany the pain, including dyspnea, nausea, vomiting, diaphoresis, fatigue, lightheadedness, or palpitations, which may predominate in women, elderly patients, or those with diabetes, presenting as anginal equivalents without classic chest discomfort. The cardiovascular history should elicit prior episodes of angina, myocardial infarction, revascularization procedures, or heart failure, alongside risk factors such as hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia, and family history of coronary artery disease. Social history is essential, noting tobacco use, which increases risk, or recent cocaine or methamphetamine exposure, as these can precipitate vasospasm and ischemia even in younger patients without traditional risk factors.
Risk stratification incorporates history and clinical features using validated scores such as the TIMI and GRACE risk scores, which include age, angina frequency, known coronary disease, and hemodynamic status to identify high-risk patients. The GRACE risk score, which predicts 6-month mortality based on age, heart rate, systolic blood pressure, creatinine, cardiac arrest at admission, ST-segment deviation, and troponin elevation, is also recommended for comprehensive risk assessment.
The physical examination complements the history by assessing for acute distress, such as pallor, anxiety, or diaphoresis, and evaluating vital signs for tachycardia, hypotension (systolic blood pressure below 100 mm Hg), or hypertension indicating hemodynamic instability. Cardiac auscultation may reveal an S3 or S4 gallop, new murmurs suggesting mitral regurgitation, or rales indicating pulmonary congestion, though findings are often normal in early unstable angina.
Red flags in the history and examination prompt immediate action, including ongoing pain exceeding 20 minutes unresponsive to nitroglycerin, recurrent episodes at rest within 48 hours, or signs of compromise such as syncope, severe dyspnea, or vital sign derangements signaling potential progression to myocardial infarction.

Electrocardiography and Biomarkers

Electrocardiography plays a central role in the initial evaluation of suspected unstable angina, providing evidence of myocardial ischemia without infarction. Characteristic findings include transient ST-segment depression of 0.5 mm or greater or T-wave inversions in two or more contiguous leads, particularly during episodes of chest pain, which indicate subendocardial ischemia. These changes are often dynamic, resolving with symptom relief, and their presence helps differentiate unstable angina from stable presentations. In contrast, persistent ST-segment elevation is not typical and suggests ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) rather than unstable angina. Approximately 20% to 30% of patients with unstable angina present with a normal electrocardiogram (ECG) at initial evaluation, underscoring that a normal tracing does not exclude the diagnosis, especially if symptoms are suggestive.
Serial ECGs are recommended to capture evolving ischemic changes, particularly if the initial ECG is normal or nondiagnostic and clinical suspicion remains high. Guidelines advise repeating a 12-lead ECG every 15 to 30 minutes in such cases until symptoms resolve or diagnostic changes appear, with continuous ST-segment monitoring for high-risk patients to detect arrhythmias or progression to infarction. These dynamic alterations, such as new or worsening ST depression, are crucial for risk stratification and may prompt earlier invasive evaluation.
Cardiac biomarkers, particularly troponins, are essential for distinguishing unstable angina from non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI) within the spectrum of non-ST-elevation acute coronary syndromes (NSTE-ACS). Unstable angina is defined by normal levels of cardiac troponin I or T, below the 99th percentile upper reference limit, confirming the absence of myocardial necrosis despite ischemic symptoms. High-sensitivity troponin assays are preferred due to their superior ability to detect minor degrees of myocardial injury with high negative predictive value (>99.5%) when used in serial protocols, such as 0/1-hour or 0/2-hour algorithms. Initial measurement should occur at presentation, with repeat testing at 1-3 hours later using high-sensitivity troponin algorithms (e.g., 0/1-hour or 0/2-hour), or as guided by symptom onset and assay type; if unavailable, creatine kinase-MB (CK-MB) may serve as an alternative, though it is less specific.
The integration of ECG findings and biomarkers enhances risk assessment in unstable angina. Negative biomarkers alongside ischemic ECG changes confirm the diagnosis of unstable angina and support a lower-risk profile compared to troponin-positive NSTEMI. Tools like the Thrombolysis in Myocardial Infarction (TIMI) risk score incorporate these elementssuch as ST-segment deviation and elevated biomarkers (though absent in pure unstable angina)to predict short-term adverse events and guide therapeutic decisions, with scores of 3 or higher indicating benefit from early invasive strategies.

Imaging Modalities

Coronary angiography remains the gold standard invasive imaging modality for confirming coronary pathology in unstable angina, as it directly visualizes non-occlusive lesions, thrombus formation, or dynamic obstructions in the coronary arteries. In patients with high-risk features, such as a TIMI risk score of 3 or greater, elevated biomarkers, or ongoing ischemia, guidelines recommend an early invasive strategy with angiography performed within 24 hours to reduce major adverse cardiovascular events. For very high-risk cases with refractory angina or hemodynamic instability, immediate angiography within 2 hours is advised, while intermediate- or low-risk patients may undergo delayed imaging within 48 to 72 hours. This approach, classified as Class 1 with Level of Evidence A, enables precise identification of culprit lesions and guides subsequent revascularization decisions.
Non-invasive imaging options are primarily reserved for risk stratification in low- to intermediate-risk patients with suspected unstable angina, particularly when an invasive approach is not immediately feasible. Exercise stress electrocardiography is not recommended (Class III, No Benefit; Level of Evidence B-R) in low- to intermediate-risk patients due to limited utility; instead, imaging-enhanced stress testing or coronary CT angiography may be considered for functional assessment. Echocardiography is recommended to evaluate for regional wall motion abnormalities indicative of ischemia, especially in the setting of hemodynamic instability or to assess left ventricular function prior to discharge (Class 1, Level of Evidence B); it is the preferred initial modality due to its noninvasive nature and ability to detect complications like thrombus. Coronary computed tomography (CT) angiography offers high negative predictive value (>95%) for ruling out significant stenosis greater than 50% in intermediate-risk outpatients, positioning it as a viable alternative to invasive angiography in stable, low-risk settings per 2025 guidelines (Class IIa, Level of Evidence B-NR).
Advanced imaging techniques, such as intravascular ultrasound (IVUS) and optical coherence tomography (OCT), are employed during catheterization to provide detailed plaque characterization in unstable angina, assessing burden, fibrous cap thickness, and vulnerability features like thin-cap fibroatheromas. IVUS offers cross-sectional views of plaque composition and vessel dimensions, aiding in the identification of unstable lesions with high accuracy (Class 1, Level of Evidence B for guiding percutaneous coronary intervention in complex cases). OCT provides superior resolution (10-fold higher than IVUS) for detecting intraluminal thrombus, plaque rupture, or erosion, which are prevalent in unstable angina culprit lesions. Cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is considered when echocardiography is nondiagnostic, particularly for evaluating myocardial viability and perfusion defects in ischemic regions, though it is not routinely indicated (Class 2a, Level of Evidence B). The 2025 ACC/AHA guidelines emphasize early invasive imaging for high-risk unstable angina while endorsing CT angiography as a noninvasive alternative for low-risk outpatients to optimize resource use and patient outcomes.

Management

Initial Assessment and Stabilization

Upon presentation with suspected unstable angina, initial assessment prioritizes the airway, breathing, and circulation (ABCs) to ensure hemodynamic stability. Intravenous access should be established promptly for medication administration, and continuous electrocardiographic (ECG) monitoring is initiated alongside frequent vital sign assessments to detect arrhythmias, hypotension, or hypoxia. Supplemental oxygen is administered if oxygen saturation (SpO2) falls below 90% to maintain levels at or above 90%. A 12-lead ECG must be obtained and interpreted within 10 minutes of arrival, with repeat ECGs if initial findings are nondiagnostic or symptoms persist.
Aspirin is administered immediately as a loading dose of 162-325 mg non-enteric-coated, chewed orally, unless contraindicated, due to its proven reduction in mortality and major adverse cardiovascular events in acute coronary syndromes. This recommendation stems from evidence showing a 23% relative reduction in 5-week vascular mortality with early aspirin use in patients with suspected acute myocardial infarction, a benefit applicable to unstable angina as part of non-ST-elevation acute coronary syndromes (NSTE-ACS).
For pain relief, sublingual nitroglycerin is given at 0.3-0.6 mg every 5 minutes for up to three doses if systolic blood pressure exceeds 90 mm Hg and no contraindications such as recent phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitor use exist; intravenous nitroglycerin may follow at 5-10 mcg/min, titrated for ongoing ischemic pain. If pain remains unrelieved, morphine sulfate is administered intravenously at 2-5 mg doses, titrated as needed, though with caution due to risks of hypotension, respiratory depression, and potential delay in percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) or P2Y12 inhibitor absorption.
Risk stratification occurs concurrently using validated tools such as the TIMI or GRACE scores to estimate short-term mortality and ischemic risk based on clinical features like age, heart rate, and ECG changes. Patients with very high-risk features (e.g., refractory angina, hemodynamic instability) require immediate transfer to the catheterization laboratory (<2 hours). For other high-risk patients (e.g., ongoing pain, dynamic ECG abnormalities, or GRACE score >140), an early invasive strategy within 24 hours is recommended. These 2025 updates emphasize rapid ED pathways, including ECG and troponin testing within 10 minutes, to facilitate timely intervention in NSTE-ACS. In anticipation of PCI, a P2Y12 inhibitor loading dose is recommended, preferring ticagrelor 180 mg or clopidogrel 600 mg orally if angiography is delayed beyond 24 hours; prasugrel 60 mg should be administered at the time of PCI if proceeding to intervention and no contraindications.

Pharmacological Therapy

Pharmacological therapy forms the cornerstone of medical management for unstable angina, a form of non-ST-elevation acute coronary syndrome (NSTE-ACS), aiming to alleviate ischemia, prevent thrombotic complications, and stabilize plaques through anti-ischemic, antithrombotic, and lipid-lowering agents. These interventions are initiated promptly following initial stabilization, with evidence supporting their use in reducing major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) such as death, myocardial infarction, and recurrent ischemia. Guidelines emphasize individualized dosing based on patient factors like renal function and bleeding risk.
Anti-ischemic therapy targets myocardial oxygen demand and supply imbalance. Beta-blockers, such as metoprolol (initial intravenous dose of 5 mg every 5 minutes up to 15 mg, followed by oral 25-50 mg every 6-12 hours), are recommended to initiate within 24 hours in hemodynamically stable patients without contraindications, reducing reinfarction risk by approximately 18% as demonstrated in the COMMIT trial involving over 45,000 patients with acute myocardial infarction. Nitrates provide rapid symptom relief; sublingual nitroglycerin (0.3-0.4 mg every 5 minutes up to three doses) or intravenous infusion (starting at 10 g/min, titrated upward) is used for ongoing chest pain in patients without hypotension. For beta-blocker-intolerant patients or those with vasospasm, non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers like verapamil (initial oral 40-80 mg every 6-8 hours) or diltiazem (initial oral 30-60 mg every 6-8 hours) may be employed to control ischemia, supported by class IIa recommendations in guidelines.
Antithrombotic therapy prevents thrombus propagation and embolization. Parenteral anticoagulation with unfractionated heparin (bolus 60-70 units/kg intravenously, maximum 5,000 units, followed by infusion 12-15 units/kg/hour, adjusted to activated partial thromboplastin time 1.5-2.5 times control) or low-molecular-weight heparin like enoxaparin (1 mg/kg subcutaneously every 12 hours, reduced to 1 mg/kg daily if creatinine clearance <30 mL/min) is standard, reducing recurrent ischemic events as shown in trials such as ESSENCE and TIMI 11B. Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) combines aspirin (loading dose 162-325 mg orally, then 75-100 mg daily indefinitely) with a P2Y12 inhibitor; ticagrelor (180 mg loading, 90 mg twice daily) or prasugrel (60 mg loading, 10 mg daily; 5 mg if weight <60 kg or age 75 years) are preferred over clopidogrel (600 mg loading, 75 mg daily) in patients undergoing percutaneous coronary intervention, per 2025 guidelines, due to lower MACE rates in PLATO and TRITON-TIMI 38 trials. Glycoprotein IIb/IIIa inhibitors, such as eptifibatide (bolus 180 g/kg intravenously, then 2 g/kg/min infusion) or tirofiban (bolus 25 g/kg over 3 minutes, then 0.15 g/kg/min), are reserved for high-risk patients prior to intervention, reducing composite ischemic events by 10-20% in trials like PURSUIT and PRISM-PLUS.
High-intensity statin therapy is initiated at admission for plaque stabilization and long-term secondary prevention. Atorvastatin 80 mg daily (or rosuvastatin 20-40 mg) is recommended, achieving a 16% relative risk reduction in the primary composite endpoint (death, myocardial infarction, stroke, revascularization, hospitalization for unstable angina) at 2 years compared to moderate-intensity pravastatin 40 mg in the PROVE-IT TIMI 22 trial of 4,162 ACS patients. If low-density lipoprotein cholesterol remains 70 mg/dL despite maximum statin dosing, ezetimibe 10 mg daily may be added, further reducing events as per IMPROVE-IT. DAPT and statin durations are typically 12 months and lifelong, respectively, with adjustments for bleeding or ischemic risk.

Invasive Interventions

In patients with unstable angina, an early invasive strategy involving percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) is recommended within 24 to 48 hours for those at high risk, defined by a TIMI risk score of 3 or higher or a GRACE score greater than 140, to reduce the incidence of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE). This approach typically includes balloon angioplasty followed by stenting, with drug-eluting stents (DES) preferred over bare-metal stents due to their lower rates of restenosis, generally under 10%, and reduced risk of target vessel revascularization. For urgent cases with refractory angina or hemodynamic instability, PCI should be performed within 2 hours.
Coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) is indicated as a Class 1 recommendation in the 2025 ACC/AHA guidelines for patients with left main coronary artery disease, three-vessel coronary artery disease, or multivessel disease in the presence of diabetes, particularly when anatomy is complex and PCI is less suitable. The choice between PCI and CABG is guided by anatomical complexity, comorbidities, and patient preferences, with CABG offering durable revascularization in high-syntax score lesions.
The 2025 ACC/AHA guidelines strongly endorse complete revascularization over culprit-lesion-only treatment as a Class 1 recommendation for unstable angina with multivessel disease, involving treatment of severe non-culprit stenoses either during the index procedure or in a staged approach to minimize MACE. This strategy is supported by evidence from the FRAME-AMI trial, which demonstrated reduced rates of death, myocardial infarction, and revascularization with fractional flow reserve-guided complete PCI in acute coronary syndromes.
Invasive interventions carry risks, including periprocedural myocardial infarction in approximately 5% to 6% of PCI cases and bleeding complications associated with dual antiplatelet therapy and heparin use. Radial artery access is preferred over femoral access to lower vascular complications and bleeding rates, as per Class 1 recommendations. Pharmacological support, such as antiplatelet agents, is administered during procedures to mitigate thrombotic risks, as outlined in pharmacological therapy guidelines.

Prognosis and Prevention

Prognosis

Patients with unstable angina experience relatively low short-term mortality rates with contemporary management, approximately 0.5% during hospitalization. The incidence of myocardial infarction is approximately 5% to 10%, while recurrent ischemia affects 10% to 20% of cases in the acute phase. High-risk patients, identified by a TIMI risk score of 5 to 7, face an elevated 14-day event rate (composite of death, myocardial infarction, or urgent revascularization) of 15% to 20%.
Long-term prognosis improves with optimal medical and interventional therapies, with 1-year mortality rates of 2% to 5%. Five-year mortality reaches 15% to 25% in treated cohorts, though rates vary by patient age and comorbidities. Revascularization strategies, compared to medical therapy alone, reduce long-term mortality and major adverse cardiovascular events by 30% to 50%, as evidenced in trials evaluating invasive approaches in non-ST-elevation acute coronary syndromes.
Key prognostic factors include advanced age, which independently increases mortality risk (hazard ratio approximately 1.07 per year). Comorbidities such as diabetes mellitus and chronic kidney disease (estimated glomerular filtration rate <60 mL/min/1.73 m) are associated with higher in-hospital and long-term mortality, with adjusted hazard ratios up to 2.6 for renal impairment. Reduced left ventricular function, particularly ejection fraction below 40%, roughly doubles the risk of adverse outcomes. Persistent residual ischemia following initial treatment further worsens prognosis.
According to 2025 guidelines, outcomes have improved with multivessel percutaneous coronary intervention, which reduces major adverse cardiovascular events compared to culprit-lesion-only approaches in suitable patients. Use of potent P2Y12 inhibitors (e.g., ticagrelor or prasugrel) alongside dual antiplatelet therapy further lowers ischemic risks. On dual antiplatelet therapy, the annual risk of new cardiovascular events is approximately 6%.

Prevention Strategies

Primary prevention of unstable angina focuses on lifestyle modifications and control of modifiable risk factors to reduce the incidence of coronary artery disease in at-risk populations. Smoking cessation is a cornerstone, with evidence showing that quitting reduces the risk of cardiovascular disease events by 50% within one year. Adoption of a Mediterranean diet, rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, fish, and olive oil, has been associated with a lower incidence of coronary heart disease through anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects. Regular physical activity, recommended at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise, lowers cardiovascular risk by improving endothelial function and reducing inflammation.
Control of key risk factors further mitigates the development of unstable angina. Blood pressure targets below 130/80 mm Hg are advised for individuals with hypertension to prevent atherosclerotic progression. For lipid management, low-density lipoprotein cholesterol levels should be maintained below 70 mg/dL in high-risk patients using statins. In those with diabetes, glycemic control targeting hemoglobin A1c below 7% reduces macrovascular complications, including acute coronary events.
Secondary prevention aims to prevent recurrence of unstable angina following an initial episode, emphasizing dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) with aspirin and a P2Y12 inhibitor for 12 months, as demonstrated by the PLATO trial where ticagrelor plus aspirin reduced major adverse cardiovascular events by 16% compared to clopidogrel plus aspirin. Lifelong statin therapy is recommended to sustain low-density lipoprotein cholesterol levels below 70 mg/dL, while beta-blockers and angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors are indicated for high-risk patients to reduce ischemic events. Participation in cardiac rehabilitation programs post-event achieves approximately a 30% reduction in all-cause mortality through structured exercise, education, and risk factor modification.
The 2025 ACC/AHA guidelines expand recommendations for secondary prevention in patients with acute coronary syndromes, including unstable angina, by endorsing sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 inhibitors and angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitors for those with concomitant heart failure or diabetes to further lower cardiovascular risk. Annual influenza vaccination is also advised, associated with a greater than 20% reduction in cardiovascular deaths in coronary artery disease patients. For intermediate-risk individuals without known disease, coronary artery calcium scoring aids in risk stratification, guiding intensified preventive therapies when scores indicate subclinical atherosclerosis.
