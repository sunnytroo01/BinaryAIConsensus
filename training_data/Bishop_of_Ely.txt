 The Bishop of Ely is the ordinary of the Church of England Diocese of Ely in the Province of Canterbury, serving as the principal spiritual leader responsible for pastoral oversight, clergy guidance, and church administration across the diocese. The diocese, established in 1109 by King Henry I from territory previously under the Diocese of Lincoln, primarily encompasses Cambridgeshire along with portions of western Norfolk and eastern Suffolk, covering approximately 1,507 square miles and serving a population of around 705,000 through 303 parishes. Its roots trace to the 7th-century double monastery founded by Saint Etheldreda on the Isle of Ely, which evolved into a Benedictine abbey and later the cathedral's precursor.
Ely Cathedral, the bishop's seat since the diocese's inception, originated as a Norman structure begun in 1083 and has endured as a key ecclesiastical landmark, reflecting the bishops' historical wealth derived from extensive manorial estates and monastic endowments surveyed as early as 1251. Bishops of Ely have exerted influence beyond diocesan bounds, with figures like Hugh de Balsham (12801286) founding Peterhouse, the first college at Cambridge University, and others contributing to national governance during periods such as the medieval era when episcopal roles intersected with royal administration. As of October 2025, the see remains vacant following the departure of the previous incumbent, with the Rt Revd Dr Dagmar Winter acting as bishop pending a new appointment expected later in the year.

Historical Origins

Foundation and Early Abbey Governance

The Abbey of Ely was founded in 673 by thelthryth, an East Anglian princess and daughter of King Anna, who established a double monastery for monks and nuns on the Isle of Ely, utilizing land she had inherited. She restored a pre-existing wooden church on the site and constructed a new abbey church dedicated to the Virgin Mary, serving as the first abbess and enforcing a regime of strict piety, manual labor, and scriptural study typical of seventh-century Anglo-Saxon monasticism. thelthryth's leadership emphasized perpetual virginity and asceticism, drawing on her own reputed chaste marriages, and the community numbered around 600 members by her death on 23 June 679.
Upon thelthryth's death, her sister Seaxburh, formerly queen of Kent and abbess of Minster-in-Sheppey, succeeded as abbess of Ely circa 679 and governed until approximately 699. Seaxburh oversaw the exhumation of thelthryth's body in 695, which was found incorrupt after 16 years, prompting its translation to a stone sarcophagus and affirming thelthryth's sanctity through reported miracles; this event solidified the abbey's status as a pilgrimage site. Subsequent abbesses, including possibly Eormenhild and Cynethryth, maintained governance through a hierarchical structure with a prioress for nuns and provost for monks, managing estates, alms distribution, and liturgical observance amid the abbey's growing endowments in fenland territories.
The monastery prospered under these abbesses until its destruction by Danish invaders around 870, during widespread raids that razed religious houses across East Anglia. Refounded circa 970 as a Benedictine abbey exclusively for men by royal patronage under King Edgar, it adopted the Rule of St. Benedict, with an abbot as head exercising authority over priors, obedientiaries, and obediential offices responsible for specific functions like sacristan duties and estate management. The refounded abbey received extensive lands and privileges, including jurisdictional liberties over the Isle of Ely, enabling self-governance with royal exemptions from certain taxes and fostering economic self-sufficiency through agriculture and tithes until the Norman Conquest.

Transition to Episcopal Authority

Prior to its elevation, Ely Abbey fell under the ecclesiastical jurisdiction of the Diocese of Lincoln, which encompassed a vast territory including much of eastern England, leading to administrative challenges for the abbey's governance and spiritual oversight. Efforts to secure autonomy dated back to at least the late 11th century, with Abbot Richard of Ely petitioning the papacy for elevation to bishopric status before his death in 1107, amid desires for greater independence from Lincoln's oversight following the transfer of the see of Dorchester to Lincoln in 1072. These ambitions reflected broader post-Norman Conquest reorganizations of English dioceses, driven by royal and archiepiscopal interests in streamlining church administration under centralized monarchical control.
The transition materialized under King Henry I, who collaborated with Archbishop Anselm of Canterbury to carve a new diocese from Lincoln's domain after resolving disputes over jurisdiction. Papal approval came via a brief from Pope Paschal II on November 21, 1108, formally erecting Ely as a bishopric, followed by Henry I's royal charter in October 1109 confirming the endowment and territorial bounds. Hervey le Breton, formerly Bishop of Bangor who had fled Welsh unrest for Henry I's court, was appointed as the inaugural Bishop of Ely, serving from 1109 to 1131; he had initially acted as administrator of the abbey, facilitating the shift from abbatial to episcopal leadership.
Upon elevation, the abbey church became Ely Cathedral, with the new diocese encompassing Cambridgeshire, the Isle of Ely, and adjacent areas previously under Lincoln, thereby establishing episcopal authority over pastoral care, ordinations, and synodal governance independent of the abbot. The Benedictine monastic community persisted under a prior, but revenues and properties were divided, with the bishop securing primary temporal and spiritual rights to ensure fiscal viabilityHervey prioritized safeguarding episcopal holdings against monastic claims, setting a precedent for bishop-prior tensions in monastic cathedrals. This structural change enhanced local ecclesiastical efficiency, reduced reliance on distant Lincoln oversight, and aligned with Henry I's policies of bolstering royal influence through reformed sees, though it involved protracted negotiations reflecting competing institutional interests.

Medieval Bishopric

Administrative and Ecclesiastical Role

The Bishop of Ely, upon the diocese's establishment in 1109 from the former Lincoln diocese, assumed comprehensive administrative authority over the Liberty of Ely, a semi-autonomous jurisdiction spanning the Isle of Ely and adjacent hundreds such as Witchford and Wisbech. This liberty, rooted in privileges granted by King Edgar in the late 10th century to the original abbey, conferred quasi-palatine powers, enabling the bishop to exclude royal sheriffs and bailiffs from most operations within its bounds, except in cases of default or specific royal commissions like those for drainage works in 13341338. The bishop directed franchise officers in executing arrests, imprisonments, executions, tax collection, and military levies, while retaining judicial profits, though the crown reserved rights to pardons for serious crimes.
Judicial administration centered on a hierarchy of courts under the bishop's oversight, including leet courts in key locations like Ely and Wisbech, tri-weekly hundred courts by the 13th century, and a superior curia Elyensis presided over by itinerant justices. These bodies adjudicated pleas of the crown, civil suits, and local disputes, exempting the liberty from shire courts and limiting appeals to royal benches like King's Bench or Common Pleas; specialized commissions handled jail delivery from 1286 and assizes in three annual sessions by the late 14th century. The bishop's steward served as the principal administrative officer from at least the 13th century, supported by deputies, bailiffs, coroners, and jailers, ensuring efficient governance of manorial dues, land transfers, and tenant obligations across free and bond holdings.
Ecclesiastically, the bishop held supreme spiritual authority as diocesan ordinary, responsible for ordaining clergy, disciplining priests and deacons, consecrating churches, and convening synods to enforce canon law across Cambridgeshire and the Isle of Ely. The see's conventual cathedral, staffed by a Benedictine chapter of up to 70 monks, operated under the bishop's rule, integrating monastic traditions with episcopal oversight; while the prior exercised minor local franchises like certain leet rights, ultimate ecclesiastical and temporal primacy resided with the bishop. This dual role blurred secular and sacred boundaries, as the bishop's temporal wealthderived from extensive manors and exemptions from early taxes like Danegeldbolstered diocesan maintenance and influence, though 13th-century parliamentary aids still required contributions proportionate to the liberty's resources.

Notable Achievements and Political Influence

Medieval Bishops of Ely exercised substantial political influence through their oversight of the Liberty of Ely, a semi-autonomous jurisdiction encompassing the Isle of Ely and surrounding fens, which conferred privileges akin to those of a county palatine, including rights to hold courts, levy taxes, and administer justice independently of the crown. This temporal authority, combined with the diocese's extensive landholdings, positioned Ely's prelates as key players in national governance, often serving in royal councils and financial administration. For instance, Nigel, bishop from 1133 to 1169 and nephew of the powerful Bishop Roger of Salisbury, had previously acted as Treasurer of England under Henry I and played a pivotal role in restoring the Exchequer's operations following the disruptions of Stephen's reign, introducing systematic accounting practices that strengthened royal finances under Henry II.
William de Longchamp, bishop from 1189 until his death in 1197, epitomized the fusion of ecclesiastical and secular power, being appointed Chancellor upon Richard I's accession and subsequently Chief Justiciar in 1190, thereby assuming effective regency during the king's crusade. Of modest Norman origins, Longchamp's rapid ascent enabled him to fortify royal administration, including fortifying key sites and managing diplomacy amid threats from Prince John, though his tenure ended amid baronial opposition leading to his exile in 1191.
Among notable ecclesiastical achievements, Hugh de Balsham, bishop from 1257 to 1286, founded Peterhouse at Cambridge in 1284 by securing a royal charter from Edward I to repurpose the Hospital of St. John for scholarly residence, establishing the model for endowed collegiate education that influenced Oxford and Cambridge's development. Balsham's initiative addressed monastic concerns over secular scholars' discipline while providing structured learning, with the college initially housing 16 scholars under monastic oversight. Earlier, Nigel had constructed a castle at Ely for defense and founded a hospital at Cambridge dedicated to St. John the Evangelist, enhancing the see's infrastructural presence. These endeavors underscored the bishops' roles in advancing education, fortification, and administrative resilience amid feudal turbulence.

Criticisms and Conflicts

During the medieval period, bishops of Ely frequently faced internal ecclesiastical disputes, particularly with the monastic chapter of Ely Cathedral Priory over financial control and jurisdictional rights. Nigel, bishop from 1133 to 1169, encountered opposition from the monks who accused him of retaining revenues intended for the priory's maintenance, exacerbating tensions in a see where the bishop and monks shared governance of a wealthy monastic foundation. These frictions stemmed from the unique structure of Ely as a monastic cathedral, where priors sought autonomy akin to other Benedictine houses, leading to litigation documented in contemporary chronicles like the Liber Eliensis.
Election controversies highlighted papal and royal interference in episcopal appointments. The 1256 election of Hugh de Balsham as bishop sparked a protracted dispute, with King Henry III rejecting the monks' choice in favor of his own candidate, prompting appeals to Pope Alexander IV, who confirmed Balsham's consecration in 1257 only after royal pressure subsided. Such conflicts reflected broader struggles between monastic independence, crown influence over church revenues, and papal authority, with Ely's strategic fens and liberties amplifying its political value.
Relations with the crown deteriorated under bishops perceived as defiant, as seen in the tenure of Thomas de Lisle (13451361), whose officials were accused of abuses including arson, extortion, and intimidation against lay subjects, prompting royal intervention and the 1352 confiscation of Ely's temporalities by Edward III. De Lisle himself faced charges of inciting unrest and challenging secular jurisdiction, leading to his flight to Avignon and a papal trial that underscored tensions between English royal prerogative and curial protection of clergy. These episodes, rooted in the bishop's exercise of the Liberty of Ely's extensive franchisesexemptions from royal taxes and courtsfueled criticisms of overreach, though de Lisle's defenders attributed complaints to political rivals exploiting anti-clerical sentiment.
Tensions extended to local laity, manifesting in agrarian disputes and urban violence. At the bishop's manor of Brandon in Suffolk, escalating conflicts from circa 1300 to 1381 involved peasants resisting stricter enforcement of customs like anti-poaching amercements, culminating in organized revolts demanding rent reductions and freedom from villeinage amid post-plague labor shortages. In 1326, Ely townsfolk, numbering around seventy, assaulted the bishop's bailiff and servants after ringing the town bell to muster, reflecting grievances over manorial exactions in a region where the bishop held seigneurial powers rivaling secular lords. These incidents, while not unique to Ely, arose from the diocese's vast estates and the bishops' dual role as spiritual overseers and feudal overlords, often prioritizing revenue extraction over customary leniency.

Reformation and Tudor Era

Dissolution Impacts and Reforms

The priory at Ely, functioning as the cathedral's monastic chapter since the bishopric's establishment in 1109, surrendered to the Crown on 18 November 1539 as part of Henry VIII's Dissolution of the Monasteries. Prior Robert Steward and 23 monks affixed their signatures to the deed of surrender, with minimal recorded resistance compared to other houses. The Crown seized the priory's assets, including lands and buildings, though the cathedral church itself endured with less structural devastation than many monastic sites; however, associated conventual structures were largely demolished or repurposed, leaving visible architectural scars.
Bishop Thomas Goodrich, appointed in 1534 and a proponent of royal supremacy, oversaw the implementation of reforms targeting perceived Catholic excesses. In 1535, he mandated English-language declarations during High Mass and Vespers renouncing papal authority. Under his direction, shrines to Anglo-Saxon saints, including that of St. Etheldreda, were desecrated, statues demolished, and much stained glass removed to eradicate "superstitious" elements, aligning with injunctions against idolatry. Goodrich's role extended to broader ecclesiastical restructuring, as he contributed to the 1537 Bishops' Book and served as a commissioner for revising canon law under Henry VIII.
In response to the dissolution, Henry VIII refounded Ely Cathedral in 1541 via letters patent as a secular foundation dedicated to the Holy and Undivided Trinity, replacing the Benedictine monks with a dean and chapter of canons. Former Prior Robert Steward transitioned to the inaugural dean, ensuring continuity in local governance while shifting to a non-monastic clergy structure. This refoundation preserved the bishopric's episcopal oversight and core endowments, mitigating total institutional collapse, though the transition reflected the Crown's broader reconfiguration of church assets to fund royal initiatives and Protestant-aligned administration. Many displaced monks received pensions, averting widespread destitution, but the event marked the irrevocable end of Ely's monastic tradition.

Key Bishops and Doctrinal Shifts

Thomas Goodrich served as Bishop of Ely from 1534 to 1554, playing a pivotal role in the early stages of the English Reformation within the diocese. Consecrated on 19 April 1534, Goodrich supported Henry VIII's break with Rome and the assertion of royal supremacy over the Church. He oversaw the dissolution of Ely Priory in 1539, transforming it into a cathedral under secular governance, which marked a shift from monastic to episcopal administration aligned with Henrician reforms. Goodrich enforced the 1541 royal injunctions, leading to the removal of images, relics, and shrines, including the destruction of the shrine of St. Etheldreda in Ely Cathedral, reflecting doctrinal moves away from traditional Catholic veneration practices.
Under Edward VI, Goodrich advanced further Protestant measures as a commissioner for ecclesiastical law reforms and contributor to doctrinal documents like the Bishops' Book of 1537. His tenure facilitated the introduction of evangelical preaching and supervision in parishes, harmonizing local practices with Cromwell's reform directives. The accession of Mary I in 1553 prompted a doctrinal reversal, with Protestant bishops like Goodrich deprived or sidelined; Goodrich died in May 1554 amid this Catholic restoration, during which altars, images, and masses were reinstated in the diocese, undoing prior iconoclastic changes.
Richard Cox, appointed Bishop of Ely in 1559 following Elizabeth I's accession, solidified the Protestant settlement in the diocese. A leading reformer exiled under Mary I, Cox enforced the Elizabethan religious policies, including the use of the Book of Common Prayer and suppression of Catholic recusancy. He contributed to the Bishops' Bible by translating Acts and Romans, embedding Reformed theology in scriptural dissemination. Cox's strict administration targeted both Roman Catholics and radical nonconformists, maintaining doctrinal conformity to the via media of the Church of England amid ongoing tensions. His episcopate until 1581 ensured the diocese's alignment with the 1559 Acts of Supremacy and Uniformity, marking a permanent shift from medieval Catholicism.

Post-Reformation Developments

Stuart and Georgian Periods

During the Stuart period, the Bishopric of Ely saw a succession of prelates deeply engaged in the theological and political upheavals of the era. Lancelot Andrewes, bishop from 1609 to 1619, exemplified the high-church Anglicanism favored by James I, contributing to the King James Version of the Bible and advocating ceremonial practices that bridged Catholic traditions with Protestant doctrine. His tenure emphasized scholarly rigor and liturgical order, influencing subsequent bishops like Nicholas Felton (16191628) and John Buckeridge (16281631).
Matthew Wren's episcopate from 1638 to 1667 marked intense conflict, as he enforced Archbishop William Laud's policies on altar rails, vestments, and bowing, prompting Puritan backlash and his imprisonment in the Tower of London from 1641 until the Restoration in 1660.) Wren's visitation articles in Ely diocese rigorously policed nonconformity, reflecting causal links between episcopal enforcement and civil war escalations. Post-Restoration, bishops such as Benjamin Laney (16671675), Peter Gunning (16751684), and Francis Turner (16841691) navigated the Act of Uniformity and oaths of allegiance; Turner, a non-juror, was deprived in 1690 for refusing the oath to William III and Mary II, highlighting schisms over divine right monarchy. Simon Patrick (16911707) and John Moore (17071714) shifted toward latitudinarianism, prioritizing reason and comprehension amid growing dissent.
In the Georgian period, the bishopric experienced relative stability, with prelates focusing on administrative duties and diocesan maintenance amid Enlightenment influences and declining clerical authority. William Fleetwood (17141723), Thomas Green (17231738), and Robert Butts (17381748) oversaw routine governance in a diocese spanning Cambridgeshire, Huntingdonshire, and parts of Norfolk and Suffolk. Thomas Gooch (17481754) and Matthias Mawson (17541771) supported university ties, with Mawson previously master of Corpus Christi College, Cambridge. Edmund Keene (17711781), James Yorke (17811808), and Thomas Dampier (18081812) coincided with the 18th-century restoration of Ely Cathedral, initiated under James Essex to repair structural decay from neglect since the Reformation. This effort, funded by parliamentary grants and local subscriptions totaling over 20,000 by 1770, addressed the octagon's subsidence and nave's deterioration, preserving the Gothic fabric without major doctrinal strife.

19th-Century Expansion and Challenges

In 1837, as part of the Church of England's diocesan reforms prompted by administrative inefficiencies and demographic pressures, the Diocese of Ely expanded significantly by incorporating the Archdeaconries of Bedford and Huntingdon from the Diocese of Lincoln, along with portions of western Suffolk previously under Norwich. This enlargement more than doubled the diocese's territory, extending its spiritual oversight to include Bedfordshire, Huntingdonshire, and adjacent rural areas, though these additions were reversed in part for Suffolk by 1914 with the creation of the Diocese of St Edmundsbury and Ipswich. The reforms, influenced by parliamentary acts and ecclesiastical commissions in the 1830s, aimed to redistribute episcopal workloads amid England's industrial population growth, which strained older diocesan structures.
Bishops during this period, including Joseph Allen (18341845) and Thomas Turton (18451856), navigated the integration of these territories, fostering administrative cohesion through enhanced clerical networks and ruridecanal systems revived in the mid-century to coordinate parochial activities. Later bishops like Edward Harold Browne (18641873) and William Connor Magee (18721891) oversaw a shift toward viewing the diocese as a unified ecclesiastical entity, with burgeoning organized church initiatives amid voluntary funding drives for pastoral expansion. However, challenges persisted, including financial strains on episcopal households exacerbated by fixed incomes and expectations of lavish entertaining, even as rural economies in the Fens and added counties faced agricultural depression.
A key infrastructural challenge was the deterioration of Ely Cathedral, addressed through extensive Victorian restorations directed by Sir George Gilbert Scott from the 1840s onward, which repaired medieval fabric, reinforced the octagon, and adapted spaces for contemporary liturgy amid debates over architectural authenticity. The diocese also confronted the erosion of the bishop's temporal powers, with the abolition of the Liberty of Ely's unique jurisdictional privilegesgranting quasi-palatinial authority over local courts and taxationby mid-century legislation, diminishing secular influence in favor of spiritual focus. Population increases in urbanizing fringes like Cambridge demanded new church plants and clergy, though the predominantly agrarian character limited the scale of building compared to industrial dioceses, relying on ad hoc benefactions rather than systematic endowments. These pressures underscored tensions between tradition and adaptation in a era of evangelical revivals and High Church reforms.

Modern Era

20th-Century Leadership and Events

The 20th century saw a succession of Bishops of Ely who navigated the diocese through two world wars, interwar social changes, and post-war reconstruction within the Church of England. Frederic Henry Chase, appointed in 1905 following Lord Alwyne Compton's resignation, emphasized scholarly pursuits alongside pastoral duties; a Cambridge academic and author on early Christian practices such as confirmation, Chase served until 1924, during which the diocese maintained traditional Anglican emphases on education and liturgy amid growing modernist debates in the church.
Leonard Jauncey White-Thomson (19241934) and Bernard Heywood (19341941) led during economic depression and rising European tensions, with the latter, translated from Hull, focusing on evangelical outreach in rural Cambridgeshire and Norfolk. The Second World War profoundly affected the diocese, as bishops relocated from the historic palace in Ely in 1941 due to air raid risks, shifting administrative focus to wartime pastoral care for evacuated families and service personnel. Harold Edward Wynn, appointed that year, was the first alumnus of Ely Theological College to ascend to the see; his tenure until 1957 included stabilizing church life amid rationing and rebuilding, marked by primary visitations that addressed clergy morale and community missions.
Noel Baring Hudson, a decorated First World War veteran with the DSO and MC who had served as a missionary bishop in Labuan and Sarawak, assumed office in 1957 through an unusual patronage oversight involving Archbishop Geoffrey Fisher and Prime Minister Harold Macmillan's office; he retired in 1964 after promoting ecumenical ties and temperance initiatives, reflecting broader Church of England efforts to engage post-war secularism. Subsequent leaders, including Edward Roberts (19641977) and Peter Walker (19771990), oversaw diocesan expansion into suburban growth areas and responses to declining rural attendance, with Walker advocating for conservative doctrinal stances amid 1970s liturgical reforms. Stephen Sykes, from 1990, bridged into the modern era by prioritizing theological education at Cambridge, though his full impact extended beyond the century. Throughout, Ely's bishops prioritized empirical parish metrics, such as maintaining over 400 churches serving a population growing from approximately 500,000 in 1901 to over 1 million by 1990, without major institutional upheavals.

21st-Century Appointments and Reforms

Anthony John Russell served as the Bishop of Ely from 2000 until his retirement in 2010, having previously been Bishop of Dorchester from 1990. During his tenure, Russell emphasized rural ministry, contributing to national Church of England discussions on pastoral care in countryside parishes, drawing from his earlier experience in agricultural communities. His leadership focused on sustaining church presence amid declining rural populations, promoting collaborative models between clergy and laity to address isolation and resource constraints in the diocese's extensive fenland areas.
Stephen David Conway succeeded Russell, confirmed as the 69th Bishop of Ely on 6 December 2010 and installed at Ely Cathedral on 5 March 2011. Conway, previously Bishop of Ramsbury, held the role until July 2023, when he translated to the Bishopric of Lincoln. As the Church of England's Lead Bishop for Education, he advocated for reforms enhancing teacher recruitment in deprived areas and expanding free schools under church sponsorship, arguing in parliamentary debates that such measures addressed educational disparities without relying on increased state funding alone.
Under Conway's oversight, the diocese advanced initiatives like REACH Ely (20182022), a partnership project reorienting underutilized church buildings toward community hubs for social services, economic support, and intergenerational engagement, funded partly through research collaborations with Cambridge Judge Business School to maximize asset utility amid clergy shortages. The diocese adopted the "People Fully Alive: Ely 2025" strategy, articulating a vision of "generous and visible people of Jesus Christ" through targeted evangelism, discipleship training, and financial restructuring to bolster parish sustainability amid secularization trends. These efforts included digitizing administrative processes and fostering multi-parish benefices to optimize limited ordained personnel, reflecting data-driven responses to attendance declines reported in annual diocesan reviews.

Contemporary Issues

Safeguarding Scandals and Institutional Responses

The Diocese of Ely has faced significant criticism for its handling of safeguarding failures, particularly in relation to the abuse perpetrated by John Smyth, a former Church of England lay reader whose activities spanned decades and involved over 100 victims, primarily boys and young men subjected to physical, sexual, and psychological abuse. Smyth's abuses, described in the 2024 Makin Review as "prolific, brutal, and horrific," were first systematically reported to the diocese in late 2013 by victim Graham Sturgess, who disclosed details of Smyth's crimes dating back to the 1970s and 1980s at Christian holiday camps like Iwerne. Despite this disclosure, the diocese under Bishop Stephen Conway (20102023) did not make a formal police referral until 2017, instead opting for internal correspondence and limited inquiries that failed to escalate the matter effectively, allowing Smythwho had relocated to South Africato continue evading justice until his death in 2018.
The Makin Review, commissioned by the Church of England and published on November 9, 2024, specifically faulted the Diocese of Ely for institutional shortcomings, including inadequate record-keeping, delayed risk assessments, and a reluctance to confront allegations against a figure protected by influential evangelical networks within the Church. It highlighted that Conway was informed of the 2013 disclosure but prioritized informal resolutions over statutory obligations, contributing to a broader pattern of concealment by senior clergy who were aware of Smyth's history as early as the 1980s. While the review acknowledged some diocesan efforts, such as contacting South African authorities in 2014, it concluded these were insufficient and undermined by deference to Smyth's defenders, echoing systemic issues identified in prior inquiries like the 2017 Geoffrey Robinson review into Iwerne camps. Critics, including survivors, have attributed these lapses to a culture of clericalism and evangelical insularity that prioritized reputation over victim protection, though some defenders, including retired police officers, have contested the review's interpretation of police referral timelines, arguing that Cambridgeshire Constabulary records show no formal complaint was logged until later.
In response, Bishop Conway issued a public apology on November 13, 2024, acknowledging personal and institutional failures while defending the diocese's actions as constrained by limited evidence at the time; however, this has not quelled calls for his resignation from Lincoln, where he transferred in 2023, nor disciplinary proceedings against him and up to ten other clergy named in the review for potential breaches under the Clergy Discipline Measure. The diocese has since implemented enhanced safeguarding protocols, including mandatory training, independent audits, and a dedicated National Safeguarding Team referral process, as outlined in its post-Makin policies updated in late 2024. Nationally, the scandal prompted Archbishop Justin Welby's resignation on November 12, 2024, over related oversights, and General Synod commitments in February 2025 to reform oversight, such as appointing external safeguarding leadsmeasures the Ely diocese has adopted to address ongoing vulnerabilities exposed by the review. Despite these steps, survivor groups continue to demand greater accountability, citing persistent gaps in victim support and transparency.

Current Vacancy and Acting Bishop

The see of Ely became vacant on 20 July 2023 upon the retirement of Stephen Conway, who had served as diocesan bishop since 2013. The Right Reverend Dr Dagmar Winter, Bishop of Huntingdon (a suffragan bishop within the diocese since 2019), was appointed acting diocesan bishop effective from that date to manage episcopal functions during the interregnum.
In July 2024, the Crown Nominations Commission, tasked with nominating a successor, failed to achieve consensus after multiple meetings, prompting the process to restart under Archbishop of Canterbury Justin Welby's oversight. This deadlock, attributed to differing priorities among commission members representing the diocese, the Crown, and the Church, has extended the vacancy beyond the typical 12-18 months.
As of October 2025, no new bishop has been nominated or consecrated, with Winter continuing in her acting role while the diocese advances its "Ely 2025" mission strategy focused on devolved decision-making and local ministry support. Recent announcements, such as Winter's appointments of clergy like the Revd Bethan Rodden on 13 October 2025, underscore ongoing administrative continuity amid the prolonged vacancy.

Reformation-Era Bishops (15391603)

Thomas Goodrich served as Bishop of Ely from his consecration on 19 April 1534 until his death on 10 May 1554, spanning the latter years of Henry VIII's reign and the early phase of Edward VI's Protestant advancements. A key supporter of the Henrician Reformation, Goodrich endorsed the Act of Supremacy in 1534, affirming royal headship over the English Church, and participated in the dissolution of monasteries, including the surrender of Ely's monastic cathedral priory in 1539, which transitioned the diocese to a secular foundation. Elevated to Lord Chancellor in 1552 under Edward VI, he oversaw the implementation of further reforms, such as the revised Book of Common Prayer, reflecting his alignment with evangelical doctrines while navigating the regime's demands for doctrinal uniformity.
Following Goodrich's death, Thomas Thirlby held the see from 1554 to 1559, appointed amid Queen Mary's Catholic restoration efforts. Thirlby, previously Bishop of Westminster and Norwich, advocated conservative positions, opposing radical Protestant changes and participating in trials like that of Thomas Cranmer, though he initially conformed to Edwardine reforms under necessity. Deprived in July 1559 after Elizabeth I's accession for refusing the Oath of Supremacy, his tenure marked a brief Catholic interlude in Ely's episcopal leadership, during which traditional practices were reinstated where possible.
Richard Cox succeeded Thirlby in 1559, serving until his death on 22 July 1581, as a staunch Protestant reformer appointed by Elizabeth I. Exiled during Mary's reign for his evangelical stance, Cox returned to promote the Elizabethan Settlement, enforcing the 1559 Act of Uniformity and combating recusancy in the diocese through visitations and prosecutions, as evidenced by records of pleas from 15591566. His administration emphasized scriptural preaching and clerical discipline, aligning with broader efforts to consolidate the Church of England's Protestant identity amid ongoing theological tensions.
The diocese remained vacant from Cox's death in 1581 until Martin Heton's appointment in 1600, a period of 19 years during which administrative duties fell to the dean and chapter, reflecting Elizabeth's occasional delays in episcopal appointments amid political considerations. Martin Heton, consecrated on 3 February 1600, brought scholarly credentials from Oxford and Winchester to the role, focusing on pastoral oversight and university ties until 1603. Heton's brief early tenure emphasized stability in a post-Reformation church still consolidating against Puritan and Catholic pressures.

Suffragan Bishops

Historical Suffragans

The suffragan see of Huntingdon was established by statute in 1534 (26 Hen. VIII, c. 14) as one of several intended to provide episcopal assistance in larger dioceses, but no appointment was made during the Tudor period. The title lapsed and was not revived until the mid-20th century amid post-war efforts to expand the episcopate for pastoral support in growing suburban and rural areas. Prior to formal suffragan appointments, occasional episcopal assistance occurred without the title; for instance, in 1870, Bishop Harold Browne of Ely commissioned Francis McDougall, former Bishop of Labuan, as Archdeacon of Huntingdon to provide oversight, though McDougall held no suffragan rank.
The modern succession of Bishops of Huntingdon began with Robert Martineau's consecration in 1966; he served until 1972, when he was translated to the diocesan see of Blackburn. His successor, Eric St. Quintin Wall, held the post from 1972 to 1980, focusing on chaplaincy and rural ministry before retiring to assistant duties in Gloucester diocese.

Current and Recent Suffragans

The Diocese of Ely maintains one active suffragan see, that of Huntingdon, to assist the diocesan bishop in pastoral and episcopal duties across Cambridgeshire, West Norfolk, and parts of Peterborough.
The current Bishop of Huntingdon is the Right Reverend Dr Dagmar Winter, consecrated on 3 July 2019 following her nomination approved by Queen Elizabeth II on 7 May 2019. Prior to her appointment, Winter served as Rector of Hexham Abbey in the Diocese of Newcastle. Since February 2023, she has additionally acted as Acting Bishop of Ely after the resignation of the diocesan bishop, the Right Reverend Stephen Conway, amid a review of safeguarding practices.
