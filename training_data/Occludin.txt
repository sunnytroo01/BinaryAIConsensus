Occludin
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Occludin is a ~65-kDa tetraspan integral membrane protein that functions as a key component of tight junctions (TJs) in epithelial and endothelial cells, where it contributes to the formation of selective paracellular barriers by regulating ion and solute permeability. First identified in 1993 as the inaugural transmembrane TJ protein, occludin features four transmembrane domains, two extracellular loops, and intracellular N- and C-terminal regions, including a conserved MARVEL domain that facilitates homodimerization and interactions with scaffolding proteins like ZO-1. Its discovery by Furuse et al. marked a pivotal advancement in understanding TJ architecture, revealing occludin as essential for stabilizing TJ strands and enhancing transepithelial electrical resistance.
Beyond barrier maintenance, occludin participates in intracellular signaling and TJ assembly, with its C-terminal domain binding ZO-1 to link the junctional complex to the actin cytoskeleton. Multiple isoforms arise from alternative splicing, such as occludin 1B with an extended N-terminus or variants lacking certain transmembrane regions, which may modulate its localization and function in different tissues. Regulation of occludin occurs through post-translational modifications, including phosphorylation at serine/threonine and tyrosine residues (e.g., Ser-408, Tyr-398/402), which can either reinforce or disrupt TJ integrity depending on the context, as well as ubiquitination by ligases like Itch and Nedd4-2 that promote its endocytosis and degradation.
Dysregulation of occludin is implicated in various pathologies, particularly those involving compromised epithelial barriers, such as inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), where proinflammatory cytokines like TNF- induce its phosphorylation and internalization via mechanisms involving myosin light chain kinase (MLCK) and caveolin-1, leading to increased permeability. In gastrointestinal disorders, occludin downregulation correlates with barrier loss in conditions like colitis and infections (e.g., Vibrio cholerae-induced diarrhea), highlighting its therapeutic potential through strategies targeting its phosphorylation or trafficking. While not strictly essential for basal TJ formationas evidenced by viable occludin-knockout models with compensatory mechanismsits absence alters paracellular selectivity but mitigates inflammatory responses in models such as colitis, where it reduces epithelial apoptosis.

Genetics and Expression

Gene Location and Organization

Occludin was first identified in 1993 as the inaugural integral membrane protein component of tight junctions through biochemical purification and cDNA cloning from chicken liver epithelial cells, marking a pivotal advancement in understanding epithelial barrier structures.
The human gene encoding occludin, designated OCLN (official symbol approved by the HUGO Gene Nomenclature Committee), is located on the long arm of chromosome 5 at cytogenetic band 5q13.2, spanning genomic coordinates 69,492,54769,558,104 on the GRCh38.p14 reference assembly. This region features segmental duplications and a related pseudogene (OCLNP1, also known as LOC647859) approximately 1.5 Mb downstream, contributing to genomic instability but not directly flanking the functional OCLN locus, which resides amid a complex array of nearby genes including those in the spinal muscular atrophy locus such as SMN1 and NAIP. The OCLN genomic position exhibits strong evolutionary conservation, with orthologs identified in 259 species ranging from mammals to non-vertebrates, underscoring its fundamental role in metazoan junctional biology.
The OCLN gene comprises 9 exons in its canonical structure, supplemented by an alternative first exon (exon 1A) that enables tissue-specific promoter usage, with the total transcript length for the primary isoform reaching approximately 6,181 bp. The coding sequence of the predominant isoform (OCLN-201, NM_001205254.2) consists of 1,566 base pairs, encoding a 522-amino-acid protein, while 8 of the 9 exons contribute to the open reading frame. Alternative splicing generates at least 32 transcript variants, including longer isoforms such as OCLN-1B that incorporate an additional 193-bp exon altering the N-terminal sequence, potentially influencing isoform-specific expression and subcellular targeting without disrupting core transmembrane domains. These variants arise primarily from differential exon inclusion in the 5' untranslated region and coding regions, with implications for protein isoform diversity observed across epithelial tissues.

Expression Patterns and Regulation

Occludin exhibits a tissue-specific expression pattern, with high levels predominantly in epithelial and endothelial cells that form tight junctions. Northern blot analyses have revealed elevated mRNA expression in organs such as the kidney, liver, lung, brain, and testis, reflecting its role in maintaining barrier integrity in these tissues. In contrast, occludin expression is notably low or absent in non-epithelial cells, including fibroblasts, underscoring its specialization for polarized epithelia. Within the brain, occludin is particularly enriched in endothelial cells of the blood-brain barrier, where it contributes to higher barrier selectivity compared to peripheral endothelia.
At the transcriptional level, occludin expression is regulated by transcription factors that modulate epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT). For instance, Snail represses occludin promoter activity, leading to reduced expression during EMT processes associated with cellular dedifferentiation. Similarly, ZEB1 acts as a repressor of occludin, suppressing its transcription alongside other epithelial markers to facilitate mesenchymal phenotypes. Post-transcriptional control involves microRNAs, particularly from the miR-200 family; miR-200c-3p binds to the 3' untranslated region of occludin mRNA, promoting its degradation and thereby decreasing protein levels in inflammatory contexts. These mechanisms ensure dynamic adjustment of occludin in response to developmental or pathological cues.
Post-translational modifications further fine-tune occludin stability and localization. Phosphorylation by protein kinase C (PKC) isoforms, such as PKC on threonine residues (T403 and T404), enhances occludin incorporation into tight junctions and supports barrier assembly. Conversely, ubiquitination, mediated by E3 ligases like Itch, targets occludin for proteasomal degradation, reducing its levels and increasing paracellular permeability. These modifications collectively regulate occludin's trafficking and turnover.
Recent studies up to 2025 highlight dual transcriptional and post-translational regulation of occludin in ischemic diseases, where ischemia-induced inflammation downregulates its expression via miRNA-mediated decay and enhances degradation through ubiquitination. In ischemic stroke, occludin serves as a biomarker for acute blood-brain barrier disruption, with reduced levels correlating to permeability changes and poor outcomes. Phosphorylation events, such as PKC-mediated modifications, further exacerbate occludin dysfunction during reperfusion, linking these regulatory layers to disease progression.

Molecular Structure

Primary and Secondary Structure

Occludin is a 522-amino acid integral membrane protein in humans, with a calculated molecular weight of 59 kDa based on its amino acid sequence, though it typically migrates at approximately 65 kDa on SDS-PAGE gels due to extensive post-translational modifications such as phosphorylation. The primary structure includes an N-terminal cytoplasmic domain of about 66 amino acids, four hydrophobic transmembrane segments, two extracellular loops (the first with 46 residues and the second with 48 residues), a short intracellular loop of roughly 8 residues, and a long C-terminal cytoplasmic tail spanning approximately 250 residues. This tetraspan configuration positions the extracellular loops for potential homotypic or heterotypic interactions at tight junctions.
The secondary structure of occludin features alpha-helical conformations within the four transmembrane domains, which span the lipid bilayer and are essential for membrane integration. In the C-terminal cytoplasmic tail, three alpha-helices form a coiled-coil motif that facilitates oligomerization and interactions with scaffolding proteins. These helical elements contribute to the protein's biophysical stability and its ability to assemble into tight junction strands.
Sequence motifs in occludin highlight functional specialization across domains. The extracellular loops are enriched in tyrosine and glycine residues, which support structural flexibility and cis-interactions, and contain asparagine residues but lack canonical N-linked glycosylation consensus sequences (Asn-X-Ser/Thr). The C-terminal cytoplasmic tail harbors multiple serine and threonine motifs that undergo phosphorylation by kinases such as PKC and CK2, influencing localization and tight junction dynamics.

Tertiary Structure and Functional Domains

Occludin adopts a tetraspanin-like tertiary fold, featuring four transmembrane -helices that span the plasma membrane and enable its integration into tight junction strands. The central MARVEL domain, spanning approximately residues 60270 and consisting of these helices arranged in a 1-4-3-2 topology with parallel alignment, minimizes the transmembrane width and incorporates conserved aromatic residues such as phenylalanines and tyrosines that stabilize interhelical interactions. This domain not only anchors occludin in the lipid bilayer but also promotes self-oligomerization, as evidenced by fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) and bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) assays demonstrating cis-association at the basolateral membrane.
The extracellular loops (ECL1: residues 89134; ECL2: residues 185232) project from the membrane and mediate both cis- and trans-interactions with adjacent occludin molecules or other tight junction proteins, facilitating paracellular barrier formation. ECL2, in particular, exhibits flexibility that allows conformational adaptability for homophilic adhesion. The intracellular N-terminal domain (residues 165) is short and unstructured, while the extended C-terminal cytoplasmic tail (residues 254522) folds into a compact structure comprising three -helices that form two anti-parallel coiled-coils connected by a loop, as resolved by X-ray crystallography of the distal region (residues 416522; PDB: 1XAW). This coiled-coil motif supports dimerization and recruitment of scaffold proteins, with a large positively charged surface on one helix serving as the primary binding interface for ZO-1.
At the extreme C-terminus, occludin features a class I PDZ-binding motif (residues 519522: -DSYV) that interacts with the PDZ domains of ZO-1, ZO-2, and ZO-3, anchoring occludin to the junctional plaque and regulating its localization. Computational models, including AlphaFold predictions (AF-Q16625-F1), further suggest potential dimerization interfaces involving the transmembrane helices and C-terminal coiled-coils, though with moderate confidence (pLDDT ~65) due to the challenges in modeling membrane-embedded regions. These interfaces likely contribute to the organization of occludin within tight junction networks.
Structural comparisons with tricellulin, another MARVEL domain-containing protein, highlight similarities in their coiled-coil C-terminal domains, both promoting dimerization for junctional stability; however, tricellulin's extended N-terminal region (194 residues vs. occludin's 66) and distinct polar dimer interface (PDB: 5N7K) adapt it specifically for tricellular contacts, whereas occludin's configuration supports broader bicellular tight junction roles.

Biological Functions

Role in Tight Junction Assembly and Barrier Function

Occludin plays a central role in the assembly of tight junctions (TJs) by being recruited to nascent junctions through its interaction with the scaffolding protein ZO-1, which facilitates its trafficking and incorporation into the junctional complex. This recruitment is modulated by phosphorylation events, such as at serine 408, which enhances occludin-ZO-1 binding and promotes stable TJ formation. Once localized, occludin contributes to the formation of anastomosing TJ strands in coordination with claudins, creating a paracellular seal that restricts the passage of solutes between epithelial and endothelial cells.
In terms of barrier function, occludin regulates paracellular permeability by influencing both the pore and leak pathways of TJs. Its extracellular loops, particularly the first loop, contribute to charge-selective ion permeability; for instance, deletion of specific residues in this loop has been shown to increase transepithelial electrical resistance (TER), indicating a role in modulating ion flux. Occludin also ensures macromolecular exclusion, with its depletion leading to enhanced flux of larger molecules through the leak pathway, up to a size limit of approximately 125 , while the overall pore pathway maintains selectivity for smaller ions with a diameter of 48 . Knockout studies in mice demonstrate that occludin deficiency results in increased paracellular permeability, particularly for macromolecules, without completely disrupting TJ strand integrity or abolishing TER, underscoring its modulatory rather than essential structural role.
Recent computational and experimental models, including those from 20242025 studies on acute lung injury, have linked occludin expression levels to alveolar barrier integrity during injury, showing that reduced occludin correlates with heightened permeability and impaired recovery in endothelial barriers. These findings highlight occludin's dynamic contribution to barrier maintenance under stress, where its regulation fine-tunes the selectivity and strength of the paracellular seal.

Involvement in Cell Signaling and Polarity

Occludin plays a critical role in intracellular signaling through its cytoplasmic tail, which undergoes phosphorylation to modulate key pathways. Phosphorylation of occludin at specific serine and threonine residues in response to stimuli such as hydrogen peroxide (HO) activates the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK)/extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK) pathway, leading to occludin rearrangement, dissociation from ZO-1, and tight junction (TJ) disruption. Occludin phosphorylation is regulated by activation of RhoA GTPases via p160ROCK-dependent and -independent mechanisms, which modulate cytoskeletal dynamics and TJ permeability. These signaling events are essential for occludin's non-barrier functions, as evidenced by studies showing that occludin knockdown disrupts RhoA-mediated signal transduction in response to apoptotic cues.
In apoptosis regulation, occludin acts as a transducer of external and intramembrane signals, promoting caspase-3 activation and epithelial cell death. Occludin enhances caspase-3 transcription, thereby promoting epithelial apoptosis during inflammation, and its downregulation reduces susceptibility to pro-apoptotic stimuli like UVB or TNF-related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL). This regulatory role extends to survival signaling, where occludin influences MAPK and Akt pathways to maintain cellular homeostasis in claudin-2-dependent TJ contexts.
Occludin contributes to apical-basal polarity by facilitating TJ maturation and the proper localization of polarity complexes. It interacts with polarity proteins such as atypical protein kinase C (aPKC)-Par3 and PALS1-associated tight junction protein (PATJ), directing their recruitment to the leading edge during epithelial polarization and migration. Through these mechanisms, occludin supports the establishment of distinct apical and basolateral domains, as demonstrated in occludin-deficient models where polarity defects impair junctional assembly. Furthermore, occludin links to the Hippo pathway, where co-localization with pathway components like Salvador and Hippo kinases at cell-cell contacts senses junctional integrity to control proliferation and inhibit organ overgrowth.
Recent studies up to 2025 highlight occludin's emerging roles in modulating inflammatory signaling, particularly in specialized tissues. Proinflammatory cytokines from activated glial cells downregulate endothelial occludin expression, exacerbating blood-brain barrier permeability and microglial activation in lipopolysaccharide-induced neuroinflammation models. Additionally, microRNAs (miRNAs) such as miR-193b-5p target occludin to disrupt junctional integrity, promoting barrier leakage in inflammatory conditions like viral lung injury, underscoring miRNA-mediated post-transcriptional control as a therapeutic target.

Protein Interactions

Interactions with Scaffold and Adhesion Proteins

Occludin primarily interacts with the scaffold proteins zonula occludens-1 (ZO-1) and ZO-2 through its carboxyl-terminal PDZ-binding motif, which specifically engages the first PDZ domain (PDZ1) of these multi-domain proteins. This binding anchors occludin within the cytoplasmic plaque of tight junctions, facilitating its recruitment and stabilization at cell-cell contacts. The interaction with ZO-1 and ZO-2 also positions occludin to indirectly connect with the actin cytoskeleton, as ZO-1 contains regions that bind F-actin, though detailed cytoskeletal dynamics are addressed elsewhere.
In terms of adhesion partners, occludin forms cis-interactions with claudins, such as claudin-1, within the same plasma membrane, promoting the polymerization and alignment of tight junction strands. These lateral associations help integrate occludin into the claudin-based lattice of the junctional seal. Similarly, occludin co-localizes and coordinates assembly with junctional adhesion molecules (JAMs), particularly JAM-A, at tight junctions through shared intracellular interactions. For intercellular adhesion, occludin participates in trans-interactions with occludin molecules on adjacent cells, contributing to the overall adhesion strength across the paracellular space.
Experimental evidence for these interactions includes co-immunoprecipitation (Co-IP) assays, which have confirmed direct binding of occludin's C-terminus to the PDZ domains of ZO-1 and ZO-2 in epithelial cell lysates. Frster resonance energy transfer (FRET) studies further demonstrate the dynamic nature of occludin-ZO-1 associations during tight junction maturation, showing proximity changes in response to calcium-induced junction assembly. Regarding tricellular contacts, where three epithelial cells converge, occludin co-localizes with tricellulin, and loss of occludin disrupts tricellulin's enrichment at these sites, as evidenced by immunofluorescence and knockdown experiments in cell models. Recent investigations as of 2025 reinforce this co-localization, highlighting occludin's supportive role in tricellulin positioning at tricellular tight junctions in salivary epithelial contexts.

Interactions with Cytoskeletal and Regulatory Molecules

Occludin establishes indirect connections to the actin cytoskeleton primarily through its interaction with the scaffold protein ZO-1, which directly binds F-actin to stabilize tight junction integrity and regulate epithelial polarization. This linkage allows occludin to influence cytoskeletal dynamics, as disruptions in ZO-1-F-actin binding impair occludin localization and junctional barrier function. Additionally, myosin light chain kinase (MLCK) modulates occludin-related contractility by phosphorylating myosin light chain, leading to actomyosin ring contraction that redistributes occludin and ZO-1, thereby altering paracellular permeability without directly binding occludin.
Regulatory modifications of occludin include phosphorylation by protein kinase C epsilon (PKC), which targets threonine residues to modulate occludin-ZO-1 interactions and tight junction assembly, often in response to extracellular signals like EGF. Similarly, c-Src kinase phosphorylates occludin on tyrosine residues (such as Y398 and Y402), promoting its dissociation from ZO-1 and facilitating junctional disassembly. For protein turnover, the E3 ubiquitin ligase Itch binds the N-terminal domain of occludin, inducing its ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation, a process enhanced by prior phosphorylation at sites like Ser-490. Recent 2025 research highlights PEAK1's role in stabilizing occludin alongside other tight junction proteins by inhibiting autophagy-mediated degradation, particularly through Src-mediated phosphorylation of PEAK1 that enhances its binding to junctional scaffolds.
In broader cellular contexts, occludin contributes to extracellular matrix (ECM) signaling via associations with focal adhesion kinase (FAK), a key integrator of integrin-mediated inputs, allowing occludin to relay ECM cues that influence junctional stability. Furthermore, occludin regulates tricellulin localization at tricellular contacts in anastomosing junctions, where occludin depletion disrupts tricellulin accumulation, impairing the branched tight junction network essential for enhanced barrier selectivity.

Pathophysiological Roles

Role in Cancer Progression and Metastasis

Occludin expression is frequently downregulated in various epithelial cancers, contributing to the disruption of tight junction integrity and facilitating epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT). In breast and colorectal cancers, this downregulation is often mediated by EMT inducers such as Snail, which represses occludin promoter activity through direct binding to E-box sequences, leading to reduced barrier function and enhanced tumor invasiveness. Similarly, in colorectal cancer models, Snail expression correlates with occludin loss, promoting cell motility and metastasis. In gliomas, occludin expression is frequently downregulated or delocalized from tight junctions, compromising the blood-tumor barrier and promoting tumor invasion and progression.
The loss of occludin promotes cancer cell migration and invasion by compromising tight junction barriers, allowing paracellular leakage and facilitating tumor dissemination. Studies in breast and gastric cancer models demonstrate that occludin silencing enhances cell protrusions and motility through actin cytoskeleton disorganization, while its overexpression restores barrier integrity and suppresses metastatic potential. Additionally, occludin modulates signaling pathways, including ERK/MAPK activation, which drives proliferation in transformed cells; its downregulation amplifies ERK-mediated growth signals in epithelial tumors. In angiogenesis, occludin regulates endothelial tight junctions, where its modulation by vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) increases vascular permeability, supporting tumor neovascularization in cancers such as bladder and hepatocellular carcinoma.
Clinically, occludin serves as a prognostic biomarker in several malignancies, with reduced expression linked to poorer outcomes and increased metastasis risk in colorectal and renal cell carcinomas as of 2025 analyses. Therapeutic strategies targeting occludin, such as mimetic peptides that restore tight junction assembly, show promise in preclinical models by inhibiting invasion and enhancing drug delivery to tumors, highlighting its potential for barrier-modulating interventions in cancer treatment.

Associations with Inflammatory and Neurological Disorders

Occludin plays a critical role in maintaining epithelial and endothelial barrier integrity, and its dysregulation contributes to barrier dysfunction in various inflammatory disorders. In inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), occludin expression is significantly downregulated in the intestinal epithelium, leading to increased paracellular permeability and exacerbated chronic inflammation. This reduction is cytokine-induced and correlates with limited epithelial apoptosis via suppressed caspase-3 expression, thereby promoting disease persistence. Similarly, in acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) and associated lung injuries, occludin levels are markedly decreased in alveolar epithelial cells, compromising the pulmonary barrier and facilitating inflammatory mediator influx. Immunofluorescence analyses of ARDS patient lung tissues confirm this occludin loss compared to healthy controls, underscoring its protective function during disease progression.
Emerging evidence positions circulating occludin as a biomarker for barrier leak severity in ischemia and obesity-related conditions. In ischemic stroke, blood occludin levels rise sharply within 4.5 hours post-onset, reflecting early blood-brain barrier (BBB) damage and serving as a potential diagnostic indicator. For obesity-linked metabolic disruptions, occludin downregulation accompanies BBB and intestinal barrier leaks, particularly in type II diabetes comorbidities, where it correlates with heightened permeability and systemic inflammation. A 2025 study highlights organ-specific tight junction remodeling in obese models, with occludin alterations contributing to adipose tissue barrier dysfunction and metabolic disease exacerbation.
In neurological disorders, reduced occludin expression disrupts BBB integrity and is implicated in disease pathology. Multiple sclerosis (MS) lesions exhibit marked occludin downregulation in brain capillaries, enhancing immune cell infiltration and lesion progression. Serum from MS patients in acute exacerbation further suppresses occludin and VE-cadherin in endothelial cultures, linking humoral factors to barrier compromise. Plasma occludin levels are elevated in untreated MS cohorts but decrease by approximately 25% following 12 months of disease-modifying therapy, suggesting its utility as a monitoring biomarker. In schizophrenia, BBB tight junction disruptions, including occludin alterations, contribute to neuroinflammatory imbalances and psychosis severity. Occludin changes correlate with claudin-5 dysregulation across psychiatric conditions like schizophrenia and bipolar disorder, where elevated claudin-5 levels in schizophrenia patients indicate compensatory barrier responses amid overall permeability increases.
Occludin remodeling also facilitates glial tumor progression by weakening neurovascular barriers. In gliomas, occludin abundance is dysregulated and dislocated from tight junctions, promoting invasive growth and BBB permeability that aids tumor dissemination. This TJ disruption in glial contexts correlates with broader neurological barrier failures observed in inflammatory states.
Therapeutic strategies targeting occludin restoration show promise in mitigating inflammatory and metabolic barrier defects. In IBD models, interventions like butyrate or specific agonists upregulate occludin alongside claudins and ZO-1, repairing intestinal permeability and reducing colitis severity. For ARDS, enhancing extracellular adenosine or inhibiting miR-193b restores occludin-mediated vascular integrity, attenuating lung inflammation in preclinical setups. Recent 2025 investigations into adipose tight junctions reveal that modulating occludin expression counters obesity-induced leaks, improving metabolic homeostasis in high-fat diet models. These approaches highlight occludin's potential as a therapeutic target for restoring barrier function in non-oncological disorders.
