Mil Mi-38
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Mil Mi-38 is a Russian medium-lift, twin-engine multi-role helicopter designed by the Mil Moscow Helicopter Plant and manufactured by Kazan Helicopters as a successor to the Mi-8 and Mi-17 series for transport, passenger, search-and-rescue, and utility missions.
Development of the Mi-38 originated in the late 1980s, with the first prototype achieving its maiden flight on 22 December 2003 at the Kazan Helicopters facility. The helicopter received type certification from Russia's Federal Air Transport Agency on 30 December 2015, enabling serial production to commence in 2016. Powered by two Klimov TV7-117V turboshaft engines each providing up to 3,500 shp, it offers a maximum takeoff weight of 15,600 kg, an internal payload of 5,000 kg, and capacity for up to 30 passengers or equivalent cargo. With a cruise speed of 250 km/h, maximum speed of 300 km/h, and operational range of 880 km, the Mi-38 supports diverse environments including maritime, tropical, and extreme cold conditions. Primary operators include the Russian Aerospace Forces, which has integrated the militarized Mi-38T variant, alongside civilian users such as Kolyma Aviation in Russia and a commercial entity in Zimbabwe.

Development History

Origins and Initial Design

The origins of the Mil Mi-38 trace to a mid-1981 decision by the Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union (CPSU) and the Council of Ministers to develop a new multi-role transport helicopter as a successor to the Mi-8, which had formed the backbone of Soviet medium-lift operations since the 1960s. This initiative addressed the need for modernized capabilities amid the Mi-8's expanding obsolescence in both military and civil roles, with the Mi-38 envisioned as a versatile platform for troop transport, cargo hauling, and utility missions.
Initial design work at the Mil Moscow Helicopter Plant emphasized first-principles advancements in lift, efficiency, and robustness, targeting a maximum payload of 5,000 kg internally or via external sling, a top speed of 300 km/h, and suitability for all-weather operations. The airframe drew on proven elements like the six-bladed main rotor tested in the Mi-28 attack helicopter, while prioritizing a larger cabin for up to 30 passengers or equivalent cargo to surpass Mi-8 limitations.
Post-Soviet economic pressures prompted international outreach in the early 1990s; a December 1992 agreement with Eurocopter (now Airbus Helicopters) aimed to incorporate Western avionics, flight deck systems, and passenger accommodations to align the Mi-38 with global certification standards and enhance export viability. This Euromil partnership, formalized further in 1994, sought to blend Russian rotor expertise with European electronics for improved reliability and interoperability, though it reflected pragmatic adaptation rather than core redesign.

Prototyping and Testing

The first prototype of the Mil Mi-38, designated OP-1, underwent construction leading to its maiden flight on December 22, 2003, at the Kazan Helicopters facility. This aircraft was equipped with two Pratt & Whitney Canada PW127/TS turboshaft engines, each providing approximately 2,500 shp.
Initial flight testing commenced immediately after the maiden flight and focused on validating basic aerodynamic performance, stability, and rotor system dynamics under various flight regimes. The first phase of these tests, conducted primarily in Kazan, was completed by the end of 2005, encompassing hover, low-speed maneuvers, and preliminary handling assessments.
Subsequent analysis from OP-1 trials identified requirements for airframe and systems refinements, including enhancements to hydraulic and fuel systems to improve reliability and efficiency. These findings informed modifications incorporated into the second prototype, OP-2, which achieved its first flight on December 2, 2010, also powered by PW127/TS engines. The OP-2 continued initial testing efforts, building on prior data to refine rotor dynamics and overall stability characteristics.

Engine Challenges and Delays

The initial prototypes of the Mil Mi-38 were designed to incorporate Pratt & Whitney Canada PW127TS turboshaft engines, each rated at approximately 2,500 shp, to meet performance requirements for medium-lift operations. However, in 2009, supply disruptions arose when Pratt & Whitney Canada withdrew from the program amid U.S. export controls on dual-use technologies, which Russian Helicopters attributed to political pressures following restrictions on technology transfers to Russia. This dependency on foreign components effectively halted progress toward production, postponing serial manufacturing timelines by at least two years and pushing initial deliveries beyond 2014. 
To mitigate import vulnerabilities, developers pivoted to indigenous powerplants, initially considering Klimov VK-2500 variants but ultimately prioritizing the TV7-117V turboshaft, derived from the TV7-117 turboprop family with over 90% commonality. The TV7-117V, targeted for 2,5003,000 shp output to enable hot-and-high performance, encountered its own developmental setbacks, including protracted certification testing for reliability and efficiency. Integration into the third Mi-38 prototype culminated in initial hover tests on November 13, 2013, followed by full flight trials later that month, marking a critical milestone after years of ground validations.
Subsequent engine evaluations focused on addressing technical hurdles such as sustained operation in icing conditions up to 4,000 meters altitude and optimization of fuel consumption metrics, which required multiple iterations to achieve requisite durability and power margins. These efforts, compounded by the need for domestic supply chain maturation, extended overall propulsion validation into the mid-2010s, with serial TV7-117V production commencing in 2016 under United Engine Corporation oversight. Despite progress, the transition underscored broader challenges in scaling Russian turboshaft technology to match imported benchmarks in efficiency and mean time between overhauls.

Certification, Production, and Modernization

The Mil Mi-38 obtained type certification from Russia's Federal Air Transport Agency on December 30, 2015, confirming compliance with national airworthiness standards for its baseline configuration powered by Klimov TV7-117V engines. This approval enabled progression toward serial production, though supplemental certifications for specific variants, such as the civilian VIP configuration with enhanced cabin features, followed in subsequent years, including Rosaviatsiya's endorsement of the highly comfortable interior in 2019. Military variants, including the Mi-38T, received operational approvals tailored for defense applications, facilitating integration into Russian Aerospace Forces inventories without awaiting broader international validations like EASA equivalence, which remained pending as of 2025.
Serial production of the Mi-38 commenced at Kazan Helicopters in early 2018, following the completion of prototype validations and the assembly of initial fuselages. The first Mi-38T military prototype assembly for the Russian Ministry of Defense began in August 2017 at the same facility, under a dedicated contract, marking the transition from demonstration to operational units. The inaugural serial Mi-38 was delivered in February 2020, with subsequent handovers supporting both civilian and military orders, including VIP transports equipped with specialized safety features like reinforced fuel systems.
Modernization efforts intensified in 2025, with the Russian Ministry of Industry and Trade allocating 5.7 billion rubles through 2027 for enhancements to the Ansat and Mi-38 platforms, including 1.76 billion rubles specifically for the Mi-38-2 variant's upgraded transmission units and rotor systems to improve reliability and performance. These upgrades build on prior serial production achievements, focusing on extending service life and operational envelopes for existing and future airframes, while VIP production incorporates advanced protective elements such as explosion-mitigating fuel tanks to meet executive transport demands.

Design and Engineering

Airframe and Aerodynamics

The Mil Mi-38 employs a conventional pod-and-boom fuselage configuration optimized for medium-lift utility roles, with a sweptback vertical fin and small horizontal stabilizer to enhance stability and reduce drag. The primary airframe structure utilizes high-strength composite materials extensively, including polymer composite three-layer panels and stringers, to achieve weight savings of approximately 20% compared to all-metal designs while providing superior corrosion resistance. Fixed tricycle landing gear supports operations on unprepared surfaces, contributing to the helicopter's versatility in diverse environments.
The main rotor system features six composite blades with pronounced non-linear twist and swept tips, designed to improve aerodynamic efficiency, lift distribution, and noise reduction during high-speed flight and hover maneuvers. A low-profile titanium hub with elastomeric bearings minimizes vibrations and maintenance needs, while hydraulic drag dampers ensure smooth operation. The tail rotor consists of two independent two-bladed assemblies in a narrow X-configuration on a shared shaft, balancing anti-torque forces with enhanced maneuverability and lower acoustic signature than traditional setups.
The airframe accommodates a modular cargo cabin measuring 16 m with a flat floor, rear clamshell doors, and a portside folding ramp, enabling payloads of up to 6,000 kg internally or configurations for 30 passengers. This layout supports rapid mission reconfiguration without structural alterations, optimizing the Mi-38 for transport, search-and-rescue, and other utility tasks.

Propulsion and Power Systems

The Mil Mi-38 employs two Klimov TV7-117V turboshaft engines as its primary powerplants, each capable of delivering between 2,500 and 3,000 shaft horsepower. This configuration evolved from initial considerations of foreign engines like the Pratt & Whitney Canada PW127/TS to prioritize domestic production and integration with Russian systems. The twin-engine setup provides inherent redundancy, allowing continued operation on a single engine during failures, which enhances safety in transport missions.
In the Mi-38-2 variant, transmission upgrades have been implemented to better accommodate the torque output of the TV7-117V engines, improving overall power transmission efficiency and load-handling capacity. These enhancements, supported by targeted investments in rotor and transmission components, enable sustained performance under high-demand conditions without compromising structural integrity.
The fuel system consists of independent bladder tanks positioned beneath the main cabin floor, one for each engine, equipped with automatic crossfeed provisions for balanced fuel management. Auxiliary external tanks can be added to extend operational range to up to 885 kilometers, supporting missions requiring prolonged endurance. Designed for harsh environments, the system operates reliably across temperatures from -60C to +50C, with features like explosion-proof elements in certain configurations to mitigate risks in combat or utility roles.

Avionics, Systems, and Safety Features

The Mil Mi-38 features a modern glass cockpit equipped with the IBKV-38 integrated avionics suite developed by Transas Aviation, providing pilots with data across five liquid crystal multifunction displays for enhanced situational awareness and reduced workload. This system, integrated in prototypes as early as 2010, supports automatic navigation, flight management, and digital standby autopilot functions, enabling operations in diverse conditions including autopilot and autohover modes. Collaboration with Thales for display and interface elements ensures compatibility with international standards, though core processing relies on Russian-developed software for data integration and failure monitoring displayed directly on the screens.
Flight control systems incorporate fly-by-wire architecture with hydraulic manual backups, allowing precise handling stability and redundancy against primary failures, as implemented in the baseline design. The digital autopilot operates alongside advanced electronic indication systems using color displays, contributing to low vibration levels certified by Russian authorities in 2016, which minimize pilot fatigue during extended missions.
Safety features emphasize redundancy and environmental resilience, including a ditching system for over-water operations that maintains buoyancy and control post-emergency landing. Military variants incorporate armored cockpit protections against small-arms fire, while the overall design supports operations on unprepared sites via reinforced landing gear capable of absorbing high-impact loads without structural compromise. These elements align with the helicopter's certified high flight safety profile, prioritizing causal factors like system reliability over ancillary enhancements.

Variants

Civil and Utility Variants

The baseline civil variant of the Mil Mi-38 functions as a medium-lift transport helicopter, configured for passenger or cargo operations with capacity for up to 30 passengers or a 5-ton payload in its internal cabin, supplemented by an external sling load capability of up to 5 tons.
A specialized VIP interior adaptation includes luxury seating for 11 to 12 passengers, enhanced soundproofing, and upgraded air conditioning tailored for the passenger cabin, with Russian civil aviation certification granted in 2019 and serial production initiated thereafter using domestically produced engines.
Utility configurations extend to medical evacuation and search-and-rescue roles, where the modular cabin design accommodates removable medical bays, stretchers, and equipment for patient transport, or facilitates rapid reconfiguration for rescue operations including hoist compatibility.
Commercial deployment includes the delivery of two Mi-38-4 units to Kolyma Aviation on March 4, 2024, enabling regional passenger and cargo services across Magadan Oblast's remote terrain.

Military and Specialized Variants

The Mi-38T serves as the primary military transport variant of the Mi-38, designed for troop and cargo transport missions within the Russian Ministry of Defense. Equipped with two Klimov VK-2500V turboshaft engines each rated at 2,400 kW, the Mi-38T emphasizes domestic components to enhance self-reliance amid import restrictions. The Russian Ministry of Defense received its first serial Mi-38T helicopters in December 2019, followed by a second delivery in January 2020, with initial plans for up to 15 units by the end of that year.
This variant supports multi-role operations including passenger and cargo transport suitable for contested environments, prioritizing payload capacity of up to 5 tons over internal distances or external sling loads. Potential medevac and search-and-rescue configurations have been under consideration since October 2017 to extend its utility in evacuation and utility tasks without incorporating offensive armaments. Developers have explicitly ruled out armed attack variants, focusing instead on defensive survivability enhancements such as robust airframe design and integrated systems for operations in adverse conditions.
Explorations into electronic warfare adaptations were also noted in 2017, aiming to equip the Mi-38T for specialized non-combat roles like signal intelligence or countermeasures support during troop lifts in high-threat areas. These configurations underscore a design philosophy centered on ballistic tolerance and system redundancy rather than direct engagement capabilities, aligning with broader Russian efforts to modernize medium-lift transport fleets for logistical resilience.

Operators and Deployment

Military Operators

The Russian Aerospace Forces serve as the primary military operator of the Mil Mi-38, utilizing the Mi-38T variant primarily for logistics support, troop transport, and utility missions. In August 2017, the Russian Ministry of Defense became the launch customer, initiating production of Mi-38T helicopters for joint flight tests to meet Armed Forces requirements. Deliveries commenced with the first serial Mi-38T handed over on December 2, 2019, followed by a second unit in January 2020.
Initial procurement plans outlined acquisition of up to 15 Mi-38 helicopters by 2020 for transport roles, though actual deliveries have remained limited amid development challenges and engine certification delays. The Mi-38T supports multi-mission operations, including cargo delivery and personnel evacuation, integrated into broader fleet modernization efforts for medium-lift capabilities replacing older Mi-8 series helicopters.
No confirmed foreign military operators exist as of 2025, with export efforts constrained by international sanctions and limited demonstrated interest in militarized variants beyond domestic needs. Russian authorities have expressed intent to expand the fleet for specialized applications, such as Arctic operations, amid heightened operational demands.

Civilian and Commercial Operators

The Mi-38 is manufactured by Kazan Helicopters, a subsidiary of Russian Helicopters, primarily for civilian applications including passenger transport, cargo hauling, and utility roles in Russia's remote regions. The first production civilian Mi-38 was delivered in February 2020 to Russian Helicopters Systems, a commercial operator focused on leasing and maintenance services. This marked the initial entry of the type into non-military fleets, with configurations supporting up to 30 passengers or equivalent cargo loads for regional connectivity.
In March 2024, Kolyma Aviation, a state-owned enterprise operating in Siberia's Magadan Oblast, received two Mi-38-4 variants equipped with VK-2500 engines for commercial passenger services. These helicopters facilitate flights on routes such as Yamsk to Takhtoyamsk, serving isolated communities with 30-seat interiors amid harsh Arctic conditions, enhancing logistical links where fixed-wing alternatives are impractical.
Within Russia, civilian Mi-38s support economic sectors including oil and gas exploration via utility configurations for personnel shuttling and equipment transport, as well as VIP variants certified in 2019 for up to 12 passengers over ranges exceeding 700 km. Leasing entities like Gazprombank Leasing promote the type for these roles, emphasizing its capacity to bridge gaps between lighter Mi-8 series and heavier Mi-26 models in commercial operations.
International civilian adoption has been constrained by the lack of widespread foreign type certifications beyond Russian standards, limiting deployments outside CIS countries despite compliance claims with select global norms. Zimbabwe represents the first confirmed commercial export customer, with one Mi-38 anticipated for delivery to bolster utility and transport needs in its resource extraction sectors as of 2023.

Operational Incidents and Reliability

The Mil Mi-38 has recorded no fatal accidents or hull-loss incidents in operational service as of October 2025, reflecting a low incident rate during its initial deployment phases with Russian military and civilian operators. Global aviation safety tracking, which documents over 11,000 helicopter write-offs, omits the Mi-38 from lists of significant events, underscoring its clean record relative to older designs like the Mi-8 series.
Engine reliability has been a key factor in this performance, particularly following the adoption of the fully domestic VK-2500 turboshaft, which provides 2,500 shp output with a total service life of 9,000 hours and overhaul intervals of 3,000 hours. This engine's full-authority digital control and improved fuel efficiency have minimized unscheduled maintenance, with operational feedback indicating fewer in-flight shutdowns compared to predecessors reliant on TV3-117 variants affected by supply chain dependencies.
Relative to the Mi-8, which has logged thousands of flight hours but with higher historical incident rates tied to aging airframes and less redundant systems, the Mi-38 exhibits enhanced uptime through fault-tolerant features such as rear-mounted engines for ballistic protection and automated diagnostics reducing ground time by up to 20% in fleet reports. High-altitude trials up to 3,000 meters and extreme temperature operations from -45C to 50C have confirmed structural and systems resilience without reliability compromises. Maintenance protocols emphasize on-line and periodic checks, supporting dispatch rates exceeding those of Mi-8 equivalents in similar utility roles.

Technical Specifications

General Characteristics

The Mil Mi-38 is configured with a crew of two pilots, optionally accommodating a third crew member for specific operations.
Its fuselage length measures 19.7 m, with a main rotor diameter of 21.1 m and height of 5.13 m to the rotor head.
The helicopter has an empty weight of 8,300 kg and a maximum takeoff weight of 15,600 kg.
Internal fuel capacity stands at 3,942 liters, supporting baseline range operations.
The design emphasizes modularity in the cargo cabin, which measures approximately 8.7 m in length and offers a volume of 29.5 m for standard configurations accommodating up to 30 passengers or equivalent payload.

Performance Metrics

The Mil Mi-38 attains a maximum speed of 300 km/h and a cruise speed of 275 km/h, enabling efficient medium-range transport operations. Its standard operational range measures 885 km, which extends to a ferry range of 1,300 km with auxiliary fuel tanks installed. The service ceiling reaches approximately 5,900 m, supporting missions in high-altitude environments while maintaining hover capabilities up to 2,800 m out of ground effect.
Payload capacity emphasizes versatility, with a maximum internal load of 5,000 kg in the cargo cabinaccommodating up to 30 passengers or equivalent freightand external sling loads reaching 7,000 kg for underslung transport. These figures derive from certified configurations tested under maximum takeoff weight of 15,600 kg, prioritizing balanced load distribution for stability.
Compared to the Mi-17, the Mi-38 exhibits superior operational envelopes, including 20% higher maximum speed and extended range, attributable to its more powerful TV7-117V engines and aerodynamic refinements validated in certification trials. Design enhancements yield halved maintenance intervals and component lifespans four to six times longer than the Mi-17, enhancing overall efficiency in empirical service simulations, though direct fuel consumption benchmarks remain proprietary to the manufacturer.

Challenges and Geopolitical Context

Development Hurdles and Criticisms

The development of the Mil Mi-38 originated with Soviet approval on June 30, 1981, for a successor to the Mi-8, but progressed haltingly over four decades amid funding shortfalls following the USSR's dissolution, repeated engine supplier changes, and multiple airframe redesigns including aerodynamic fuselage refinements and an X-shaped tail rotor. The first prototype achieved flight on December 22, 2003over 22 years after inceptionwith only four prototypes completed by 2018 despite earlier projections for production starting in the early 2000s. These delays stemmed from programmatic inefficiencies, such as stalled financing and the need for iterative modifications to the rotor hub and transmission systems, rendering the project economically unviable for mid-range payloads compared to established Mi-8 variants.
Early prototypes relied on Western-sourced Pratt & Whitney Canada PW127TS engines, which integrated into the initial 2003 flight but exposed vulnerabilities when partnerships faltered, prompting a switch to domestic Klimov TV7-117V powerplants amid certification hurdles and compatibility reworks. This dependence contributed to integration challenges, including prolonged testing phases and elevated development costs, as the Russian engines were criticized for underdelivering power relative to the PW127TS benchmarks, exacerbating timeline slippages.
Critics have highlighted the Mi-38's failure to substantially exceed Mi-8 performance envelopes in demanding hot-and-high environments, with test data confirming operational viability up to 3,000 meters and +45C but falling short of initial design ambitions for superior lift and range under such loads due to the TV7-117V's output limitations. Low serial production rates have compounded these issues, driving per-unit costs high enough to undermine competitiveness against proven, mass-produced alternatives.

Impact of Sanctions and Self-Reliance Efforts

Following the imposition of Western sanctions in 2014 after Russia's annexation of Crimea, the Mil Mi-38 program accelerated efforts to replace foreign components, particularly the originally planned Pratt & Whitney Canada PW127TS turboshaft engines, with domestically produced alternatives to mitigate supply chain risks. This shift prioritized the Klimov TV7-117V engine, a Russian-designed powerplant offering comparable 2,5002,800 shp output, as part of broader import substitution initiatives in the aviation sector.
Serial production of the TV7-117V commenced in June 2016 by the United Engine Corporation's Klimov subsidiary, enabling the Mi-38 to integrate a fully indigenous propulsion system and reducing reliance on Western suppliers amid ongoing sanctions. Similar localization extended to avionics and other subsystems, with Russian Helicopters achieving a predominantly domestic supply chain by the early 2020s, exemplified by certification of Mi-38 variants equipped solely with Russian components. This self-reliance enhanced strategic autonomy, insulating the program from future export restrictions and supporting sustained production rates, such as planned increases in TV7-117 family output to over 1,500 units by 2030.
Critics, including Western analysts, argue that the pivot contributed to protracted certification timelinesfull type certification for the TV7-powered Mi-38T was not granted until December 2018and potential compromises in engine maturity or integration reliability compared to established foreign options. However, proponents highlight tangible gains in resilience, as evidenced by the program's progression to serial deliveries without foreign dependency, outweighing short-term hurdles in the context of geopolitical isolation. These adaptations underscore Russia's broader aerospace indigenization drive, fostering long-term vulnerability reduction despite escalated sanctions post-2022.
