Our solar system is a cosmic neighborhood centered on a single star, the Sun, and containing eight planets, at least five dwarf planets, hundreds of moons, millions of asteroids, billions of comets, and vast quantities of dust and gas. It formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from a giant cloud of gas and dust called the solar nebula, and it occupies a relatively quiet corner of the Milky Way galaxy, approximately twenty-six thousand light-years from the galactic center.

The Sun dominates the solar system. It contains 99.86 percent of the system's total mass. Everything else, all eight planets, all their moons, all the asteroids, all the comets, everything, makes up just 0.14 percent. The Sun's gravitational influence extends approximately two light-years in every direction, roughly halfway to the nearest star. Within this sphere of influence, everything orbits the Sun.

The planets are divided into two categories based on their composition and location: the rocky inner planets and the gas giant outer planets.

Mercury is the smallest planet and the closest to the Sun, orbiting at an average distance of about thirty-six million miles. It has virtually no atmosphere, which means its surface experiences extreme temperature swings: over eight hundred degrees Fahrenheit on the sunlit side and minus two hundred and ninety degrees on the dark side. Mercury's surface is heavily cratered, resembling our Moon. Despite being the closest planet to the Sun, Mercury is not the hottest planet in the solar system. That distinction belongs to Venus.

Venus is Earth's nearest neighbor and closest match in size and mass, but the similarities end there. Venus has a thick atmosphere composed primarily of carbon dioxide with clouds of sulfuric acid, creating a runaway greenhouse effect that traps heat so efficiently that the surface temperature averages about eight hundred and sixty-four degrees Fahrenheit, hot enough to melt lead. The atmospheric pressure on the surface of Venus is about ninety times that of Earth, equivalent to being a half mile deep in the ocean. Venus rotates backward compared to most planets, and its day is longer than its year: it takes Venus two hundred and forty-three Earth days to rotate once on its axis but only two hundred and twenty-five Earth days to orbit the Sun.

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet in the universe that harbors life. It is unique among the planets in having liquid water on its surface, a substantial oxygen-rich atmosphere, a magnetic field that protects it from solar radiation, and plate tectonics that recycle its crust and regulate its carbon cycle. Earth's Moon, formed approximately 4.5 billion years ago from debris ejected by a collision between the early Earth and a Mars-sized body called Theia, stabilizes Earth's axial tilt, which in turn stabilizes the climate and makes the seasons regular enough to support the development of complex life.

Mars, the fourth planet, has captivated human imagination for centuries. It has a thin atmosphere of mostly carbon dioxide, polar ice caps of water and carbon dioxide ice, the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, which stands nearly fourteen miles high, and a canyon system, Valles Marineris, that stretches over two thousand five hundred miles long and up to six miles deep. Evidence from Mars rovers and orbiters strongly suggests that Mars once had liquid water on its surface, including rivers, lakes, and possibly an ocean covering much of its northern hemisphere. Whether Mars ever harbored life is one of the most compelling questions in planetary science, and it is a primary motivation for the ongoing robotic exploration of the planet.

The asteroid belt, located between Mars and Jupiter, contains millions of rocky objects ranging in size from small boulders to the dwarf planet Ceres, which is approximately six hundred miles in diameter. Contrary to popular depictions in science fiction, the asteroid belt is mostly empty space. If you flew through it, you would be unlikely to see a single asteroid, let alone dodge one.

Jupiter is the largest planet in the solar system, more than twice as massive as all the other planets combined. It is a gas giant composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, with no solid surface. Its most famous feature is the Great Red Spot, a storm larger than Earth that has been raging for at least four hundred years. Jupiter has at least ninety-five known moons, including the four Galilean moons discovered by Galileo in 1610: Io, the most volcanically active body in the solar system; Europa, which has a global ocean of liquid water beneath its icy surface and is considered one of the most promising places to search for extraterrestrial life; Ganymede, the largest moon in the solar system, bigger than the planet Mercury; and Callisto, heavily cratered and ancient.

Saturn is the second largest planet and is famous for its spectacular ring system, which is composed of billions of particles of ice and rock ranging in size from dust grains to house-sized boulders. The rings extend out to about one hundred and seventy-five thousand miles from Saturn's center but are remarkably thin, averaging only about thirty feet thick. Saturn has at least one hundred and forty-six known moons, including Titan, the only moon in the solar system with a substantial atmosphere. Titan's atmosphere is primarily nitrogen, like Earth's, and its surface features lakes and rivers of liquid methane and ethane, making it the only body in the solar system other than Earth known to have stable bodies of liquid on its surface. Another moon, Enceladus, has geysers of water vapor erupting from its south pole, suggesting a subsurface ocean that could potentially harbor life.

Uranus is an ice giant that rotates on its side, with an axial tilt of about ninety-eight degrees, meaning that each pole gets approximately forty-two years of continuous sunlight followed by forty-two years of darkness. Its blue-green color comes from methane in its atmosphere, which absorbs red light and reflects blue. Uranus has twenty-seven known moons and a faint ring system.

Neptune, the most distant planet, is a deep blue ice giant with the strongest sustained winds in the solar system, reaching speeds of over twelve hundred miles per hour. Its largest moon, Triton, orbits in the opposite direction to Neptune's rotation, suggesting that it was captured from the Kuiper Belt rather than forming alongside Neptune. Triton has active geysers and may have a subsurface ocean.

Beyond Neptune lies the Kuiper Belt, a region of icy bodies including Pluto, which was reclassified from planet to dwarf planet in 2006. The Kuiper Belt extends from about thirty to fifty astronomical units from the Sun and contains an estimated hundred thousand objects larger than sixty miles in diameter. Further still is the Oort Cloud, a theoretical spherical shell of icy objects extending from about two thousand to one hundred thousand astronomical units from the Sun, roughly halfway to the nearest star. Long-period comets, those with orbits lasting thousands or millions of years, originate from the Oort Cloud.

Our solar system is extraordinary in many ways, but it may not be unusual. The discovery of thousands of exoplanets around other stars has revealed that planetary systems are common in the galaxy. The Kepler space telescope alone identified over two thousand six hundred confirmed exoplanets. Some of these planets orbit in the habitable zone of their stars, where conditions might permit liquid water. Whether any of them harbor life is the most profound unanswered question in all of science.

The exploration of our solar system is one of humanity's greatest adventures. We have sent spacecraft to every planet. We have landed on the Moon, Mars, Venus, Titan, an asteroid, and a comet. We have sent probes beyond the edge of the solar system: Voyager 1, launched in 1977, entered interstellar space in 2012 and is now the most distant human-made object in existence, over fourteen billion miles from Earth, still transmitting data. The solar system is our backyard, and we have barely begun to explore it.
