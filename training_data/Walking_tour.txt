Walking tour
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 A walking tour is a form of tourism activity in which participants explore a destination on foot, typically guided by locals or experts who provide insights into its history, culture, architecture, or natural features, serving educational and recreational purposes. These tours emphasize slow-paced immersion, allowing visitors to engage directly with urban or rural environments while promoting sustainable travel practices.
The origins of walking tours trace back to the early 19th century in European cities such as Paris and Rome, where they emerged as a means for travelers to pursue cultural education and historical exploration amid the rise of modern tourism. By the mid-19th century, promoters in places like Quebec City developed prescribed itineraries and guidebooks to direct tourists through key sites, blending local advocacy with visitor experiences to highlight architectural and cultural heritage. The practice gained renewed momentum in the late 20th century, particularly from the 1960s onward with urban planning influences like Jane Jacobs' community-focused approaches, leading to organized events such as Jane's Walk, first held in 2007, which now spans over 500 cities worldwide. Popularity surged in the 1990s as demand grew for authentic, experiential travel over mass tourism, and as of 2025, walking tours continue to grow with innovations in digital guides and sustainability-focused routes.
Walking tours vary in format and focus, including guided group excursions in urban settings, self-guided routes using apps or maps, and thematic variants such as historical, architectural, or nature-based explorations. They can range from short city strolls covering a few blocks to multi-day treks in rural areas, often incorporating elements like advocacy for walkability or cultural storytelling to connect participants with local communities. In contemporary tourism, innovations like free pay-what-you-wish models, which operate on a tip-based system with average voluntary contributions of 10-20 per person, and generate additional revenue through paid add-on tours (e.g., pub crawls, themed tours), special group reservations, and commissions from recommended venues like restaurants or shops, and digital enhancements have made them more accessible, particularly in Europe and North America, while addressing issues such as equity in pedestrian infrastructure.
Beyond cultural enrichment, walking tours offer significant health advantages, including reduced blood pressure, lower body fat, improved cardiovascular fitness, and enhanced mental well-being through group activity and nature exposure. Economically, they support local businesses and artisans by directing foot traffic to neighborhoods and boosting visitor spending. As a low-impact, eco-friendly alternative to motorized travel, they align with global sustainability goals, fostering appreciation for urban equity and environmental preservation.

History

Origins and Early Practices

The origins of walking tours can be traced to ancient precedents that emphasized exploratory foot travel around significant sites. In the Greco-Roman world, periegesis referred to descriptive travel literature, such as Pausanias' 2nd-century CE Description of Greece, which provided detailed textual itineraries of cultural and historical landmarks, combining education with leisure for readers and travelers. These accounts highlighted walking as a means to engage with cultural heritage, often for elite visitors or scholars exploring sites on foot.
During the medieval period, structured group journeys on foot became prominent through pilgrimages, which served spiritual, penitential, and communal purposes across Europe. Pilgrims frequently traveled in organized groups for mutual protection and support, covering long distances to holy sites like Santiago de Compostela or Canterbury, enduring physical hardships such as weary feet and exposure to weather while fostering social bonds and shared narratives. Historical records, including chronicles and pilgrimage guides, illustrate how these foot-based expeditions evolved from individual vows into collective endeavors, emphasizing the transformative power of pedestrian travel.
In the 17th century, early examples of purposeful walking emerged within the European Grand Tour, a customary extended journey for young aristocrats that supplemented carriage travel with foot exploration to access remote or detailed sites. Travelers, primarily from Britain, would disembark to explore urban centers like Paris or Rome on foot, guided informally by locals or tutors to study art, architecture, and antiquities, as recounted in period diaries and letters. This blend of vehicular and pedestrian movement allowed for deeper immersion in landscapes and ruins inaccessible by coach.
By the 18th century, literary influences elevated walking as a form of reflective tourism, particularly through the works of Jean-Jacques Rousseau and William Wordsworth. Rousseau's Reveries of the Solitary Walker (17761778) portrayed pedestrian rambles through Swiss and French countrysides as paths to introspection and harmony with nature, inspiring a cultural appreciation for solitary or small-group walks as personal exploration. Similarly, Wordsworth's poetry and prose, drawing from his extensive foot travels in England's (/page/Lake District) during the late 18th century, promoted wandering as a source of poetic inspiration and moral renewal, influencing early tourists to seek similar experiences. Concurrently, the development of informal walking routes in British countryside estates around the 1760s reflected this shift, as landscape designers like Lancelot "Capability" Brown created meandering paths through "natural" parks at sites such as Petworth House and Blenheim Palace, inviting visitors to stroll and admire picturesque vistas on foot. These estate walks, integrated into the emerging English landscape garden style, marked a transition toward purposeful pedestrian tourism for leisure and aesthetic enjoyment.

Development in the Modern Era

The development of walking tours in the modern era began in the 19th century amid Europe's expanding tourism sector, spurred by rail networks and rising middle-class leisure. Karl Baedeker's inaugural guidebook, Rheinreise von Mainz bis Kln, published in 1828, marked a pivotal shift by offering detailed, structured itineraries that emphasized pedestrian exploration of scenic routes and historical sites, transforming informal strolls into organized tourist activities. These red-covered volumes, known for their star ratings and practical advice, sold widely across Europe, institutionalizing walking as an accessible form of cultural immersion and fueling the commercialization of guided self-tours.
In the early 20th century, walking tours evolved through formal organizations and infrastructure, reflecting broader interests in health, nature, and preservation. The Appalachian Trail in the United States, conceived by forester Benton MacKaye in 1921 as a 2,000-mile footpath from Georgia to Maine, represented an ambitious effort to create dedicated long-distance walking routes for recreation and renewal; construction began shortly after the Appalachian Trail Conference's formation in 1925, involving volunteer clubs that blazed trails through the 1920s and 1930s. In the United Kingdom, the Ramblers' Association, established in 1935 amid campaigns for public footpath access, promoted organized group walks and lobbied for trail protections, laying the groundwork for professionalized pedestrian outings in urban and rural settings. These initiatives shifted walking tours from solitary pursuits to collective, institutionalized experiences, often tied to national conservation efforts.
Post-World War II economic recovery and the advent of mass tourism dramatically accelerated the global spread of walking tours, as affordable air travel and extended paid vacations enabled broader participation. The era's prosperity, particularly in Western Europe and North America, saw tourist numbers surge from 25 million international arrivals in 1950 to 166 million by 1970, with walking tours benefiting from this influx as low-cost, immersive alternatives to motorized excursions. In the 1970s, companies like Rick Steves' Europe began offering guided walking tours across continental Europe, starting with small-group itineraries focused on historical paths and cultural sites, which popularized budget-conscious pedestrian travel among American visitors.
Key milestones further shaped this evolution, including the 1960s counterculture's embrace of pedestrian travel as a rejection of car-centric modernity and consumerism. Influenced by environmental awareness and youth movements, figures like Beat Generation writers and early hippies advocated simple, foot-powered journeyssuch as overland treks through Europe and Asiato foster personal growth and anti-materialist ideals, inspiring a surge in informal backpacker walking routes. By the 1990s, the eco-tourism boom integrated walking tours into sustainable practices, with global ecotourism growing at 20-34% annually, emphasizing low-impact hikes in natural areas like Costa Rica's rainforests and Europe's national parks to minimize environmental harm while educating participants. This period solidified walking tours' role in responsible tourism, blending historical roots with contemporary global accessibility.

Types of Walking Tours

Guided Tours

Guided walking tours are professionally led excursions where an expert guide accompanies a group of participants on foot through designated routes, delivering live narration enriched with historical, cultural, or thematic context to enhance the educational and recreational experience. These tours typically feature interactive elements such as question-and-answer sessions, allowing participants to engage directly with the guide for personalized insights. They operate on fixed schedules to ensure structured progression, accommodating groups of 10 to 20 people to balance intimacy and manageability while facilitating clear communication and group dynamics.
Tour guides for these experiences undergo specialized training to qualify, often through certified programs that emphasize knowledge dissemination, safety protocols, and audience engagement. The International Tour Management Institute (ITMI) provides comprehensive certification courses covering tour leadership, group management, and cultural sensitivity, requiring participants to complete modules on logistics, emergency response, and practical on-tour observations. Additional qualifications may include local licensing, such as exams on regional history and architecture. To captivate audiences, guides employ storytelling techniques like sensory descriptions to evoke vivid scenes, personal anecdotes for relatability, and narrative tension to build engagement, transforming factual recitations into immersive tales.
Common formats include standard historical walking tours, which offer 2-hour overviews of urban landmarks and events, providing broad contextual narratives for first-time visitors. Specialized variants, such as food tours that incorporate tastings at local eateries or architecture tours focusing on building designs and urban evolution, adapt the core structure to niche interests while maintaining the guide's interpretive role.
Economically, guided walking tours rely on per-person pricing models, typically ranging from $20 to $50, which covers guide fees, route permissions, and operational costs, making them accessible yet profitable for operators. In Europe, a prevalent tip-dependent variant is the "free walking tour" model popularized by companies like Sandeman's and GuruWalk, where tours are offered without upfront payment and revenue is generated primarily through voluntary tips at the end, typically averaging 10-20 euros per person to reward guide performance. These operators supplement income via paid tours such as pub crawls or themed experiences, special group reservations (e.g., around 120-130 euros per guide for private groups of 8-15 people), and commissions from recommended venues like restaurants or shops, ensuring the model's sustainability and directly influencing tour quality.

Self-Guided and Themed Tours

Self-guided walking tours enable participants to navigate routes independently or in small groups, relying on physical maps, printed pamphlets, digital applications, or audio devices to dictate pacing and direction without a live leader. These tours emphasize autonomy, allowing explorers to pause, detour, or extend visits as desired, often covering historical or scenic paths marked by signage or trails. A seminal example is Boston's Freedom Trail, conceived by journalist William Schofield in 1951 and officially dedicated that year by Mayor John Hynes, which uses a continuous red brick or painted lineadded in 1958to connect 16 sites pivotal to the American Revolution by 1972, facilitating self-paced discovery.
Themed variants of self-guided tours incorporate specialized narratives to enhance engagement, transforming standard walks into immersive storytelling experiences. Ghost tours, drawing from 19th-century Victorian fascination with the supernaturalsuch as cemetery visits and gothic literatureevolved into structured pedestrian explorations of haunted locales. Modern self-guided ghost tours use apps to recount eerie histories at sites like old graveyards or abandoned buildings. Literary walks similarly follow fictional itineraries, exemplified by routes tracing Sherlock Holmes's adventures in London, where participants use maps or GPS-enabled apps to visit inspirations from Arthur Conan Doyle's late-19th-century stories, such as 221B Baker Street and the Strand, promoting a blend of literature and urban geography.
These formats foster personalization through flexible timingvisitors can start anytimeand reduced costs, typically $515 for downloadable apps, pamphlets, or audio files, compared to guided alternatives.
Innovative self-guided concepts further diversify these tours, such as geocaching-integrated walks that merge global positioning system hunts with pedestrian routes. Participants follow clues to hidden "caches" along themed paths, like multi-stage historical trails in towns such as Crested Butte, Colorado, where caches align with architectural landmarks to encourage exploratory pacing and discovery. Audio drama tours, introduced in the 1980s via cassette tapes, provided narrated narratives synchronized to locations; Pathfinder Productions' 1985 New York series offered 90-minute self-paced walks through districts like Wall Street, using portable players for dramatic storytelling of urban history. Recent innovations as of 2025 include augmented reality (AR) apps that overlay historical visuals on real-time views during themed walks, enhancing immersion without live guides.

Destinations and Experiences

Urban and City Tours

Urban walking tours thrive in the dense, layered environments of metropolitan areas, where compact layouts enable short loops typically spanning 1 to 3 miles that weave through architectural highlights, vibrant markets, and public art installations. These tours emphasize the interplay of built forms and daily urban rhythms, allowing participants to navigate narrow streets and pedestrian zones while discovering evolving cityscapes. For instance, in Paris's Marais district, tours cover historic mansions, medieval facades, and lively markets like Place des Vosges, showcasing a blend of Renaissance architecture and contemporary street art within a roughly 1.5-mile circuit. Such itineraries highlight how urban density fosters immersive experiences without requiring extensive travel.
Prominent examples illustrate the appeal of these tours in transforming industrial or overlooked spaces into pedestrian pathways. The High Line in New York City, an elevated linear park opened in 2009, spans 1.45 miles and integrates wild gardens, contemporary sculptures, and panoramic views of the Hudson River and skyline, drawing millions annually for its seamless blend of nature and urban vistas. In Tokyo, shitamachi neighborhood tours, particularly in areas like Yanaka, explore hidden alleys lined with traditional wooden shops and temples over about 1 mile, revealing the preserved low-town fabric amid the modern metropolis. These routes prioritize discovery of everyday urban textures, from street vendors to quiet backstreets.
Urban walking tours often integrate with city infrastructure designed to prioritize pedestrians, enhancing accessibility and safety in high-density settings. Barcelona's superblocks initiative, which clusters nine-block areas to restrict through-traffic and create car-free zones, has boosted pedestrian activity by 10% in pilot areas, fostering environments where tours can focus on communal plazas and reduced-speed streets without vehicular interference. This model promotes sustainable mobility, with over 60% of residents in implemented superblocks reporting greater comfort for walking in narrower interior paths.
Despite these advantages, urban tours face challenges from traffic noise and high crowd densities, which can disrupt immersion and pose safety risks for participants. Road transport remains a primary noise source in cities, contributing to health strains and reduced enjoyment during daytime walks, while congested sidewalks in tourist hubs exacerbate navigation difficulties. Adaptations like nighttime tours address these by shifting to quieter hours, offering illuminated skyline viewssuch as in New York's Greenwich Village or Paris's Seine embankmentswhere reduced vehicular activity and glowing landmarks create atmospheric alternatives, though persistent evening crowds require guided pacing.

Cultural and Historical Site Tours

Cultural and historical site tours focus on heritage locations such as ancient ruins, archaeological parks, and natural landmarks imbued with interpretive elements that educate visitors about past civilizations and events. These tours typically follow designated paths through preserved sites, allowing participants to engage with tangible remnants of history while guided narratives provide context on cultural significance and historical narratives. Unlike broader urban explorations, these walks emphasize fixed monuments and their educational depth, often incorporating elements like on-site markers and expert-led interpretations to enhance understanding.
At preservation sites like Pompeii in Italy, walking tours have been integral since the site's systematic excavations began in 1748 under King Charles III of Bourbon, with guided paths tracing the Roman city's layout frozen by the eruption of Mount Vesuvius. These tours navigate streets, villas, and temples, offering insights into daily life in the 1st century AD. Similarly, at Machu Picchu in Peru, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, guided walking trails traverse the Inca citadel's terraced ruins and agricultural systems, highlighting the empire's architectural ingenuity and spiritual practices from the 15th century. Official circuits ensure visitors follow routes that protect the fragile structures while immersing them in Inca history.
Interpretive approaches in these tours often include on-site signage detailing architectural features and historical timelines, multilingual audio guides for self-paced exploration, and contextual storytelling by licensed experts. For instance, at Pompeii, audio guides in languages such as English, Italian, French, German, and Spanish narrate the catastrophic eruption of Vesuvius in 79 AD, which buried the city under ash and pumice, preserving it as a snapshot of Roman society. These methods facilitate deeper engagement, allowing visitors to visualize events like the sudden evacuation and the volcano's pyroclastic flows that claimed thousands of lives.
Global examples illustrate the diversity of these tours. In Jerusalem's Old City, a UNESCO World Heritage Site sacred to Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, walking tours traverse the four quartersJewish, Christian, Muslim, and Armeniancovering multi-faith history from ancient temples to medieval walls, with guides explaining shared holy sites like the Temple Mount. In Australia's Kakadu National Park, Indigenous-led rock art walks, such as those at Ubirr and Burrungkuy, follow trails to galleries dating back 20,000 years, where Bininj/Mungguy storytelling conveys creation myths, Dreamtime narratives, and cultural continuity through paintings of ancestral beings and contact-era motifs.
Conservation efforts are closely tied to these tours, with UNESCO World Heritage status often imposing limits to safeguard sites from overuse. At Petra in Jordan, the 1994 UNESCO Petra National Park Management Plan restricts access to sensitive areas like the Monastery (Ad Deir) to 500 visitors per day, balancing educational access with erosion prevention and structural preservation amid the site's Nabataean tombs and facades carved into rose-red cliffs. Such measures ensure that walking tours contribute to heritage protection rather than degradation, promoting sustainable visitation models worldwide.

Planning and Logistics

Route Design and Preparation

Designing a walking tour route begins with evaluating key physical and logistical factors to ensure the path is feasible and engaging for participants. Organizers typically assess the total distance, aiming for 2 to 3 miles for most urban or historical tours, as this allows coverage of multiple points of interest within 1 to 2 hours at a moderate pace of about 3 miles per hour. Elevation changes are also critical, with best practices recommending sustained grades no steeper than 5-10% (or 5-10 feet of rise per 100 feet horizontally) to accommodate varying fitness levels and minimize fatigue. Points of interest, such as historical landmarks or scenic views, are identified to structure the route, often using digital mapping tools like Plot A Route or Geographic Information Systems (GIS) software to plot paths, measure distances, and highlight features while avoiding hazards like steep slopes or wetlands.
Preparation extends to environmental and regulatory considerations to facilitate smooth execution. Weather forecasts are checked in advance, as conditions like rain or extreme heat can affect safety and enjoyment, with adjustments such as shortening the route or adding shaded areas if necessary. For tours in public spaces like parks or historic districts, permits are often required; for instance, special use permits must be acquired from entities like the National Park Service for guided walks in protected areas to prevent resource impacts. Routes are customized based on participant fitness levels, opting for flat, even paths for beginners or incorporating gentle hills for more experienced groups, ensuring the design aligns with the group's capabilities.
Participant preparation is essential for a positive experience, starting with clear guidance on what to bring and how to behave. Organizers provide packing lists emphasizing essentials like comfortable, supportive shoes to prevent blisters, reusable water bottles for hydration, weather-appropriate clothing (e.g., layers and rain gear), and sunscreen or hats for sun protection. Pre-tour briefings cover etiquette, such as remaining on designated paths in ecologically sensitive areas to protect vegetation and wildlife, maintaining group cohesion, and respecting quiet zones at cultural sites.
Incorporating best practices enhances engagement and comfort throughout the route. Rest stops are planned with level areas every 600-800 feet during sustained grades, aligning with natural breaks at points of interest or benches, to allow time for hydration, questions, and recovery while keeping the pace lively. Routes should be tested in advance by organizers to verify timing, accessibility, and appeal, with adjustments made based on real-world feedback.

Safety, Accessibility, and Group Management

Safety protocols for walking tours begin with thorough hazard identification to mitigate risks such as uneven pavement, traffic exposure, or weather-related slips, which are common in urban or natural settings. Operators conduct risk assessments by evaluating environmental, physical, and cultural factors, determining likelihood and impact, and implementing control measures like suitable routes and pre-tour briefings. Emergency plans are essential, including first-aid kits stocked with bandages, antiseptics, and electrolyte packets, knowledge of local emergency numbers, and procedures for evacuation or medical response to issues like falls or heat exhaustion. Tour operators must maintain comprehensive liability insurance covering accidents and medical emergencies, while encouraging participants to secure personal travel insurance, especially for higher-risk routes.
Accessibility features ensure walking tours are inclusive for diverse participants, incorporating wheelchair-friendly paths with smooth, level surfaces, minimum widths of 36 inches, and slopes no steeper than 1:20 to avoid barriers like stairs or protrusions. For visually impaired individuals, audio descriptions provide verbal narratives of landmarks and surroundings, often delivered via standalone devices or integrated into guided commentary, supplemented by tactile maps or Braille guides. Adaptations for elderly participants include shorter routes with frequent rest breaks every 20-30 minutes and flexible pacing to accommodate mobility limitations, alongside high-contrast signage and assistive listening devices for those with hearing impairments.
Effective group management maintains cohesion and safety during walking tours through techniques like pacing at 2.5-3 miles per hour, positioning slower walkers at the front and faster ones at the back, and assigning a "sweep" person to prevent stragglers. Regular headcounts at stops, buddy systems, and clear meeting points help track participants, while two-way radios facilitate communication in larger groups. Group cohesion is supported by proactive strategies such as clear hand signals and interactive discussions to engage participants. Optimal group sizes are limited to 8-15 participants for better control and interaction, with larger assemblies split into sub-groups of 10-12 and a maximum of 24 overall, maintaining a guide-to-participant ratio of no more than 8:1.
Legal aspects underscore the responsibilities of walking tour operators, including the use of liability waivers that clearly outline risks and shift responsibility to participants, provided they use bold, conspicuous language to meet fair notice requirements and avoid unconscionable terms. In the United States, compliance with the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), enacted in 1990, mandates accessible routes and auxiliary aids for public programs like tours, prohibiting discrimination and requiring reasonable modifications unless they impose an undue burden. Operators must ensure ongoing maintenance of accessible features and may face liability for non-compliance, such as inadequate paths leading to injuries.

Impacts and Trends

Educational and Health Benefits

Walking tours offer significant educational benefits by engaging participants in multisensory learning experiences that enhance knowledge retention and comprehension. Unlike passive methods such as reading, active exploration during walks allows individuals to interact with environments through sight, sound, touch, and even smell, fostering deeper cognitive processing. For instance, studies on nature-based experiences demonstrate that such immersive activities boost academic learning and personal development by promoting environmental stewardship and improving recall of information. Research specifically on walking while encoding new material, such as vocabulary, shows improved long-term memory performance compared to sedentary learning, with participants exhibiting better verbal recall when motorically active during the process.
The health advantages of walking tours are well-documented, encompassing both physical and mental improvements. Physically, regular walking contributes to cardiovascular health by reducing blood pressure, enhancing lipid profiles, and lowering the risk of heart disease, aligning with World Health Organization guidelines recommending at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week to prevent noncommunicable diseases. A systematic review of walking group interventions confirms benefits including decreased body fat, increased VO2 max, and improved physical functioning, which are particularly relevant to guided or group tours. Mentally, immersion in natural settings during walks, akin to forest bathing practices, significantly reduces stress levels, anxiety, and symptoms of depression, as evidenced by meta-analyses showing physiological markers of relaxation such as lowered cortisol.
Socially, walking tours promote community building and cultural empathy by facilitating interactions in group settings. Participants often report strengthened social connectedness through shared experiences in neighborhoods or natural areas, which encourages dialogue and mutual understanding across diverse backgrounds. Evidence from studies on neighborhood walking highlights its role in enhancing interpersonal relationships and a sense of belonging, contributing to overall well-being. Programs like the UK's Walking for Health initiative, launched in 2000 by Natural England, exemplify these benefits by providing structured group walks that have led to measurable improvements in mood and self-esteem among participants.

Modern Innovations and Sustainability

Modern innovations in walking tours have integrated advanced technologies to enhance navigation and immersion. GPS-enabled applications, such as AllTrails launched in 2010, provide real-time trail mapping, user reviews, and offline capabilities for hikers and urban explorers, enabling safer and more informed self-guided experiences worldwide. Augmented reality (AR) overlays further enrich tours by superimposing digital elements onto physical environments; for instance, at historical sites like Pompeii, AR reconstructs ancient cityscapes over ruins, allowing participants to visualize past urban layouts during walks. Similarly, in Rome, AR apps overlay 3D models of ancient structures onto modern landmarks, transforming standard walking paths into interactive historical journeys.
Sustainability has become a core focus, emphasizing walking tours' inherently low-carbon footprint compared to motorized travel, which aligns with broader environmental goals. Operators increasingly adopt carbon offset programs, where tour fees contribute to reforestation or renewable energy projects to neutralize emissions from related activities like transportation to trailheads. The "leave no trace" principles, established by the Center for Outdoor Ethics, guide practices such as sticking to durable surfaces, proper waste disposal, and minimizing campfire impacts to preserve natural paths for future use. Since 2019, the European Union's Green Deal has influenced tourism by promoting climate-neutral strategies, including incentives for eco-friendly infrastructure and low-impact activities like walking routes, as part of the goal to reduce transport emissions by 90% by 2050.
The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated shifts toward contactless and hybrid formats, with audio tours surging in popularity for their hands-free delivery via smartphone apps, allowing solo or small-group exploration without physical guides. Post-2020 restrictions boosted virtual hybrids, combining in-person walks with remote AR elements to maintain social distancing while connecting global audiences; for example, apps now offer live-streamed guided walks synced to participants' locations.
Looking ahead, AI-driven personalization is emerging to tailor routes based on user preferences, fitness levels, and time constraints, generating custom itineraries that adapt in real-time via apps like WalkNicely. Virtual reality (VR) previews allow prospective participants to simulate trails virtually before committing, enhancing accessibility and planning. In Singapore, 2023 initiatives like the Singapore Tourism Board's collaboration with Google's ARCore introduced AI-powered smart trails around Merlion Park, featuring immersive AR overlays for heritage sites that blend physical walks with digital enhancements.
