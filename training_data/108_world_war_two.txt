World War Two was the most destructive conflict in human history. Fought from 1939 to 1945 across six continents and every ocean, it killed an estimated seventy to eighty-five million people, including approximately six million Jews murdered in the Holocaust, the systematic genocide carried out by Nazi Germany. It involved the mobilization of over one hundred million military personnel from more than thirty countries. It introduced atomic weapons, which remain the most destructive devices ever created. It reshaped the global balance of power, ended European colonial empires, created the United Nations, and launched the Cold War between the United States and the Soviet Union that would define international relations for the next forty-five years.

The roots of World War Two lie directly in the unresolved consequences of World War One. The Treaty of Versailles left Germany humiliated, economically devastated, and seething with resentment. The Great Depression of the 1930s destroyed the fragile economic recovery of the 1920s and created mass unemployment, poverty, and desperation across Europe and the world. In this environment of economic collapse and national humiliation, Adolf Hitler and the Nazi Party rose to power in Germany, promising national renewal, racial purity, and revenge against the nations that had imposed the Versailles settlement.

Hitler's ideology combined extreme nationalism, anti-Semitism, racial supremacy, militarism, and authoritarian governance into a worldview of terrifying coherence and determination. He believed that the German race, which he called the "Aryan master race," was destined to dominate Europe and that the expansion of German territory, which he called Lebensraum or "living space," was both a right and a necessity. He blamed Germany's problems on Jews, communists, and the democratic politicians who had signed the armistice in 1918, and he promised to reverse every provision of the Versailles Treaty.

After consolidating power through a combination of democratic means and political violence, Hitler began his program of territorial expansion. He remilitarized the Rhineland in 1936, annexed Austria in March 1938, and demanded the Sudetenland, a German-speaking region of Czechoslovakia, which was ceded to Germany at the Munich Conference in September 1938. British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain returned from Munich declaring he had achieved "peace for our time." Six months later, Hitler seized the rest of Czechoslovakia. On September 1, 1939, Germany invaded Poland. Britain and France, which had guaranteed Polish independence, declared war on Germany two days later.

The early years of the war were dominated by German victories achieved through a revolutionary military doctrine called Blitzkrieg, or "lightning war," which combined fast-moving armored divisions with close air support to punch through enemy lines, encircle opposing forces, and destroy them before they could mount an effective defense. Poland fell in five weeks. Denmark and Norway fell in April 1940. Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg fell in May. France, which had been considered the strongest military power in Europe, fell in just six weeks, with Paris occupied on June 14, 1940. By the summer of 1940, Hitler controlled most of Western Europe.

Britain stood alone. Under the leadership of Winston Churchill, who had replaced Chamberlain as Prime Minister in May 1940, Britain refused to negotiate. The Battle of Britain, fought in the skies over England from July to October 1940, was the first major military campaign fought entirely in the air. The Royal Air Force, outnumbered but aided by radar and the courage of its pilots, defeated the German Luftwaffe's attempt to gain air superiority as a prerequisite for a seaborne invasion. Churchill's speeches during this period, including his famous declaration "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets, we shall fight in the hills; we shall never surrender," became some of the most powerful political oratory in the English language and rallied British morale during the darkest days of the war.

The war expanded dramatically in 1941. In June, Hitler invaded the Soviet Union in Operation Barbarossa, the largest military operation in history, deploying over three million German soldiers along a front that stretched from the Baltic to the Black Sea. Initial German advances were spectacular, with millions of Soviet soldiers killed or captured in the first months. But the Soviet Union's vast territory, harsh winters, and the determination of its people to resist created a war of attrition that Germany could not win. The Battle of Stalingrad, from August 1942 to February 1943, was the bloodiest battle in human history, with combined casualties exceeding two million. The German defeat at Stalingrad marked the turning point of the war on the Eastern Front.

On December 7, 1941, Japan attacked the American naval base at Pearl Harbor, Hawaii, killing over two thousand four hundred Americans and drawing the United States into the war. Japan, which had been expanding its empire in Asia since the 1930s, quickly conquered the Philippines, Malaya, Singapore, the Dutch East Indies, and Burma, creating a vast Pacific empire. The American island-hopping campaign, beginning with the Battle of Midway in June 1942 and continuing through brutal battles at Guadalcanal, Tarawa, Iwo Jima, and Okinawa, gradually pushed Japanese forces back across the Pacific.

The Holocaust, the systematic murder of six million Jews by Nazi Germany, is the most thoroughly documented genocide in history and one of the most horrifying chapters in the human story. The Nazis also murdered millions of Roma, disabled people, political prisoners, homosexuals, Slavic peoples, and prisoners of war. The extermination was carried out through mass shootings, starvation, forced labor, and gas chambers in death camps including Auschwitz-Birkenau, Treblinka, Sobibor, and Majdanek. The Holocaust was not an irrational outburst of violence. It was a calculated, bureaucratically organized, industrially implemented program of murder that harnessed the full resources of a modern state.

D-Day, June 6, 1944, was the largest amphibious invasion in history. Over one hundred and fifty thousand Allied troops crossed the English Channel and landed on the beaches of Normandy, France, breaking through German defenses and establishing a beachhead that would eventually lead to the liberation of France and the invasion of Germany. The landings were preceded by extensive deception operations, massive aerial bombardment, and parachute drops behind enemy lines. The cost was staggering: approximately ten thousand Allied casualties on the first day alone. But the beachhead held, and within weeks, over a million Allied soldiers had landed in France.

The war in Europe ended on May 8, 1945, when Germany surrendered unconditionally. Hitler had committed suicide in his bunker in Berlin on April 30 as Soviet forces fought their way into the city. The discovery of the concentration and death camps by advancing Allied forces revealed the full horror of the Holocaust to the world.

The war in the Pacific ended after the United States dropped atomic bombs on the Japanese cities of Hiroshima on August 6, 1945, and Nagasaki on August 9, 1945. The bombs killed approximately one hundred and forty thousand people in Hiroshima and seventy thousand in Nagasaki, most of them civilians, and many more died in subsequent months and years from radiation exposure. Japan surrendered on August 15, 1945. The use of atomic weapons remains one of the most controversial decisions in history, debated by historians, ethicists, and military strategists to this day.

The aftermath of World War Two reshaped the world completely. The United Nations was established in 1945 to prevent future conflicts. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights was adopted in 1948. The European colonial empires, weakened by the war, began to disintegrate as colonized peoples demanded independence. Germany and Japan were rebuilt with American assistance and eventually became two of the world's largest economies. The division of Europe into Western and Soviet spheres of influence launched the Cold War. The Nuremberg Trials established the principle that individuals, including heads of state and military commanders, can be held personally accountable for crimes against humanity.

World War Two is the central event of the twentieth century. It demonstrated both the depths of human cruelty and the heights of human courage. It showed what happens when democratic institutions fail, when hatred is allowed to become state policy, and when the machinery of modern technology is turned toward destruction. And it created the international order, imperfect and often strained, that has prevented a third world war for eight decades and counting. That fragile peace, maintained by institutions, treaties, alliances, and the collective memory of what happens when peace fails, is the most important legacy of the deadliest conflict in human history.
