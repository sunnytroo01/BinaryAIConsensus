Trogocytosis
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Trogocytosis is a contact-dependent cellular process in which one cell actively extracts and acquires membrane fragments, along with associated proteins and lipids, from an adjacent donor cell, often within minutes of interaction. This phenomenon, derived from the Greek word trogo meaning "to gnaw," is distinct from phagocytosis as it involves the transfer of small "bites" of plasma membrane rather than engulfment of entire cells. It is evolutionarily conserved across eukaryotes and plays a critical role in intercellular communication, particularly within the immune system.
First observed in the 1970s in protozoan predators like Naegleria fowleri and in lymphocytes, with the term trogocytosis coined in 1979, it has since been documented in diverse cell types, including immune effector cells like T cells, natural killer (NK) cells, and neutrophils, as well as non-immune contexts such as neuronal pruning and embryonic development. The process requires direct cell-cell contact, often mediated by receptor-ligand pairs such as T cell receptors (TCR) with peptide-MHC complexes or Fc receptors with antibody-opsonized targets. Mechanistically, it involves actin cytoskeleton remodeling, phosphorylation of signaling molecules like Src kinases and PI3K, and membrane scission events that enable rapid internalization of the acquired material into the recipient cell. In some cases, extensive trogocytosis can lead to donor cell death, termed trogoptosis, highlighting its potential as a cytotoxic mechanism.
In the immune system, trogocytosis facilitates antigen presentation by allowing dendritic cells to "cross-dress" with acquired MHC molecules, enhances cytotoxic responses in NK cells and CAR-T therapies, and regulates tolerance through transfer of inhibitory molecules like PD-L1. Beyond immunity, it contributes to pathogen evasion, as seen in parasites like Entamoeba histolytica acquiring host membrane proteins to disguise themselves, and to disease progression in cancer, where it may promote tumor escape from immunotherapy. Recent studies underscore its therapeutic implications, such as optimizing antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) and mitigating antigen loss in engineered T cells. As of 2025, ongoing research highlights trogocytosis's role in the tumor microenvironment and potential modulation to enhance CAR NK/T cell therapies. Overall, trogocytosis exemplifies a dynamic form of cellular exchange that influences health, infection, and immunity.

History

Early Observations

The earliest indications of trogocytosis-like membrane transfer processes emerged in microbiological studies during the early 1970s. In 1973, researchers observed the receptor-dependent transfer of lipopolysaccharide antigens from macrophages to B lymphocytes, highlighting intercellular exchange of membrane-associated molecules in immune-related cells. A related observation in 1974 involved the transfer of immunoglobulin from B cells to activated T lymphocytes, where surface Ig on T cells was found to originate from B cells, initially interpreted as possible secretion or endogenous production rather than direct membrane transfer.
A landmark report came in 1979 from T. Brown, who described how the pathogenic amoeba Naegleria fowleri acquires membrane fragments from mouse embryo cells during cytopathogenic interactions. In in vitro cultures, Brown used immunofluorescence and electron microscopy to show that amoebae form ingesting pseudopodia to detach and internalize small patches of host cell cytoplasm and membrane, without full engulfment or cytotoxic lysis, resulting in granular fluorescence indicating antigen acquisition in the amoebic cytoplasm. This process, termed a phagocytosis-like mechanism at the time, demonstrated rapid piecemeal ingestion for host cell destruction.
Throughout the late 1970s to mid-1980s, similar membrane-nibbling phenomena were documented in parasitic and microbial systems, often linked to nutrient acquisition and predation by pathogens. For instance, in 1985, Waddell and Vogel reported that the predatory slime mold Dictyostelium caveatum kills other slime molds, such as D. discoideum, by selectively nibbling membrane patches and cytoplasmic fragments, enabling efficient nutrient uptake while avoiding the energy costs of complete phagocytosis. In vitro assays in these studies revealed transfers occurring within minutes of cell contact, with labeled host components appearing on or within the predator's membrane and vacuoles, establishing membrane patch exchange as a conserved strategy in microbial pathogenesis.
These foundational microbial observations provided key insights into contact-dependent membrane transfer, paving the way for investigations in the 1980s into analogous processes among immune cells.

Development in Immunology

The development of trogocytosis research in immunology began with early observations of membrane transfer during immune interactions. In the 1970s, Hudson and Sprent reported the transfer of surface molecules, such as IgM antibodies, between antigen-presenting cells (APCs) and activated T cells in irradiated mice, highlighting a process linked to antigen recognition. These findings laid the groundwork for understanding intercellular exchange in adaptive immunity, building on brief microbial precedents from the same era involving membrane fragment transfers in eukaryotic systems.
During the 1990s, research advanced to demonstrate specific MHC molecule exchange between T cells and APCs, with evidence of functional acquisition of peptide-MHC complexes by T cells. A seminal study showed that T cells could internalize peptide-MHC complexes acquired from APCs via TCR-mediated mechanisms, enabling potential modulation of T cell responses. This exchange was antigen-specific and occurred rapidly during T cell-APC conjugation, suggesting a role in fine-tuning immune activation.
In vivo evidence further solidified trogocytosis as a relevant immune process, particularly in models of viral infection where bystander T cells acquired virus-specific T cell receptors (TCRs) from activated cytotoxic T lymphocytes. This transfer enhanced antiviral defense by allowing non-specific T cells to gain specificity and contribute to pathogen clearance. Such observations underscored trogocytosis's physiological importance beyond in vitro settings.
A pivotal contribution came in 2001 from Joly and Hudrisier, who formally described trogocytosis in CD8+ T cells acquiring MHC class I molecules from target cells, emphasizing the process's rapidity and dependence on immunological synapse formation. This work highlighted how CD8+ T cells "nibble" membrane fragments from infected or tumor targets, acquiring not only MHC but associated peptides to potentially regulate cytotoxicity. These experiments marked a shift toward recognizing trogocytosis as a distinct, dynamic feature of cytotoxic immune responses.

Terminology and Key Milestones

The term "trogocytosis" was coined in 2002 by immunologists Etienne Joly and Denis Hudrisier to describe the rapid, contact-dependent transfer of plasma membrane fragments between cells, derived from the Greek word trogo meaning "to gnaw" or "to nibble," distinguishing it from phagocytosis, which involves engulfment of entire cells. This nomenclature highlighted the process's nibbling-like nature, where recipient cells actively extract membrane patches from donor cells without cell death.
Building upon foundational observations from the 1970s and 1980s, a pivotal 2003 commentary in Nature Immunology by Joly and Hudrisier further clarified trogocytosis as a deliberate intercellular exchange with functional significance, rather than a mere experimental artifact or passive shedding. This reframing emphasized its role in modulating immune responses through antigen acquisition and receptor redistribution, solidifying trogocytosis as a purposeful biological mechanism.
In the 2010s, conceptual advancements recognized trogocytosis as capable of bidirectional transfer, where both interacting cells exchange membrane components, expanding its scope beyond unidirectional immune cell acquisition. This bidirectional dynamic was demonstrated in interactions involving monocytes, macrophages, and target cells, influencing cellular signaling and adaptation in diverse contexts.
By the 2020s, trogocytosis gained integration into cancer immunology models, where it facilitates tumor immune evasion by enabling cancer cells to acquire inhibitory receptors from immune effectors, thereby dampening anti-tumor responses. Recent 2025 reviews have underscored its underappreciated roles beyond immunity, particularly in neuronal contexts such as synaptic pruning and circuit refinement, positioning trogocytosis as a conserved process across physiological systems.

Cell Types Involved

Immune Cells

Trogocytosis plays a pivotal role in interactions among immune cells, enabling the rapid transfer of membrane fragments and associated proteins during cell-cell contacts. In lymphocytes, CD4+ and CD8+ T cells actively acquire major histocompatibility complex (MHC)-peptide complexes from antigen-presenting cells (APCs) such as dendritic cells, a process that modulates T cell activation and function. This acquisition occurs in a contact-dependent manner triggered by T cell receptor engagement, allowing T cells to incorporate foreign MHC molecules onto their surface. B cells also participate in trogocytosis, where they transfer immunoglobulins to interacting cells, as initially observed in early studies of membrane protein exchange between B and T cells. Although less extensively documented, T cells exhibit similar capabilities in mucosal environments, contributing to localized immune surveillance through membrane exchanges with epithelial-associated APCs.
Antigen-presenting cells, including dendritic cells and macrophages, engage in bidirectional trogocytosis with T cells, exchanging co-stimulatory molecules such as CD80 and CD86. Regulatory T cells, in particular, trogocytose these ligands from APCs via CTLA-4-dependent mechanisms, thereby downregulating co-stimulatory signals and suppressing effector responses. Dendritic cells transfer peptide-MHC class II complexes alongside CD86 to other immune cells, enhancing their antigen presentation capacity during immune priming. Macrophages similarly exchange membrane components with T cells, facilitating the modulation of inflammatory signals in lymphoid tissues.
Among innate effector cells, natural killer (NK) cells trogocytose MHC class I molecules from target cells during antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC), which can alter NK cell inhibitory signaling and effector function. This process is mediated by NK cell receptors like KIRs that bind MHC-I, leading to the incorporation of target-derived proteins onto the NK cell surface. Neutrophils and eosinophils acquire IgG-bound membrane fragments from opsonized targets, enabling efficient cytotoxicity through trogocytosis-mediated membrane stripping. In neutrophils, this IgG-dependent transfer supports tumor cell elimination by disrupting target integrity without full phagocytosis.
Basophils and monocytes are involved in rapid trogocytosis during allergic responses, exchanging membrane-bound cytokines and receptors to amplify Th2 polarization. Basophils acquire peptide-MHC class II and co-stimulatory molecules from dendritic cells, allowing them to present antigens and drive IL-4 production in T helper cells.

Non-Immune Cells

Trogocytosis extends beyond immune contexts to non-hematopoietic cells, where it facilitates membrane exchange for developmental remodeling, immune evasion, and pathogenesis. In these settings, the process involves rapid, contact-dependent transfer of membrane fragments and associated proteins, often mediated by actin dynamics and adhesion molecules, enabling cells to acquire functional components from neighbors without full engulfment.
In neural tissues, microglia engage in trogocytosis to prune synaptic components from neurons, regulating circuit refinement during development. Live imaging in the Xenopus laevis retinotectal system revealed microglia nibbling presynaptic terminals on retinal ganglion cell axons, a process enhanced by complement signaling and inhibited by regulators like amphibian RCA3, which is essential for proper axonal rewiring and behavioral responses to visual stimuli. Astrocytes also participate in trogocytosis-like nibbling, remodeling neuronal size and organelle composition by extracting membrane patches, contributing to synaptic maintenance in healthy brains.
Cancer cells exploit trogocytosis to acquire immune regulatory molecules from surrounding cells, promoting evasion of antitumor responses. In colon cancer models, tumor cells rapidly acquire MHC class I (e.g., HLA-B) and PD-L1 from tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes within minutes of contact, with acquired proteins that suppress Th1 activation and NK cell function by reducing cytokines like IL-2 and IFN-. Similarly, renal cancer cells obtain surface immune proteins via trogocytosis, enhancing their immunosuppressive profile in the tumor microenvironment.
Microbial pathogens, including free-living amoebae and parasites, utilize trogocytosis to acquire host membranes during infection. The brain-eating amoeba Naegleria fowleri employs trogocytosis to ingest fragments of host cells, including membrane, cytoplasm, and mitochondria, facilitating tissue invasion and cell destruction through close contact and rapid uptake. Parasites like Entamoeba histolytica similarly nibble live host cells to display acquired human membrane proteins, such as MHC I, on their surface within 5 minutes, thereby evading complement-mediated lysis by human serum and enhancing survival in invasive disease. Trypanosoma cruzi also associates trogocytosis with host membrane exchange during successful cellular invasion by extracellular amastigotes.
Among other non-immune cells, epithelial-like endodermal cells in C. elegans embryos perform trogocytosis to ingest primordial germ cell lobes, transferring membrane fragments for developmental clearance and tissue remodeling. This process underscores trogocytosis's role in non-immune cellular interactions beyond immunity, though specific examples in mammalian epithelial tissue repair remain emerging.

Mechanism of Action

Contact Formation

Trogocytosis initiates through the formation of stable cell-cell contacts mediated by specific adhesion molecules, particularly in immune cell interactions. In T cell-antigen presenting cell (APC) pairs, the integrin leukocyte function-associated antigen 1 (LFA-1) on T cells binds to intercellular adhesion molecule 1 (ICAM-1) on APCs, facilitating firm adhesion and enabling the process to proceed. This LFA-1/ICAM-1 interaction is essential for T cell trogocytosis, as disruption of LFA-1 binding impairs contact stability and subsequent membrane exchange. Similarly, in innate immune cells such as eosinophils and neutrophils, the related integrin CD11b/CD18 (Mac-1) engages ICAM-1 on target cells, promoting adhesion during anti-tumor responses.
These adhesive contacts often organize into synapse-like structures that concentrate signaling molecules and support trogocytic nibbling. In T cell-APC interactions, LFA-1/ICAM-1 binding drives the assembly of an immunological synapse, a specialized interface where the T cell receptor (TCR) engages peptide-major histocompatibility complex (MHC) ligands, stabilizing the contact within minutes. This synapse features a central supramolecular activation cluster (cSMAC) for TCR-MHC engagement surrounded by a peripheral SMAC enriched in LFA-1/ICAM-1 adhesions, which collectively maintain the structure for efficient trogocytosis. In neural contexts, microglia establish analogous synapse-like interfaces with neuronal elements, such as presynaptic boutons, where direct membrane apposition allows selective trogocytosis of synaptic components during circuit remodeling, without reliance on complement receptor 3 (CR3) signaling.
Actin cytoskeleton reorganization is critical for generating the protrusive forces that enable initial nibbling at these contacts. The Arp2/3 complex nucleates branched actin filaments at the contact site, promoting membrane protrusion and tubulation in the recipient cell, as observed in immunological synapses where F-actin polymerization links integrins like CD11b/CD18 to the cytoskeleton via adaptors such as talin-2 and kindlin-3. Inhibition of actin polymerization, including Arp2/3-dependent branching, disrupts these protrusions and halts trogocytosis in T cells, underscoring its role in contact maturation. In microglial-neuronal interfaces, similar actin dynamics facilitate filopodia-like extensions from neuronal spines upon microglial contact, potentially aiding the precision of trogocytic engulfment.
Environmental cues rapidly trigger these contacts, often within seconds to minutes. The presence of specific antigens, such as peptide-MHC complexes recognized by TCR, initiates adhesion and synapse formation in T-APC pairs, with contacts stabilizing within minutes post-engagement. Complement tagging similarly activates trogocytosis; for instance, complement receptor 2 (CR2) on marginal zone B cells binds complement-opsonized immune complexes, prompting rapid contact with presenting cells and membrane acquisition. In neural settings, developmental signals at synaptic sites cue microglial contacts, leading to trogocytosis-mediated pruning without overt inflammatory triggers.

Membrane Transfer Process

The membrane transfer process in trogocytosis is characterized by a nibbling mechanism, wherein the recipient cell actively extracts small fragments of the plasma membrane from the donor cell through localized invagination and subsequent vesiculation. This results in the transfer of membrane patches, typically around 50-100 nm in diameter, which contain embedded proteins and lipids without disrupting the donor cell's viability. The process is initiated upon stable cell-cell contact, often mediated by adhesion molecules, and proceeds via dynamic cytoskeletal rearrangements that facilitate the pinching off of these patches.
Key molecular players include myosin motors, which drive the cytoskeletal propulsion necessary for membrane deformation, and lipid rafts, which cluster receptors and lipids to enable efficient, localized transfer. These elements support either unidirectional flowfrom donor to recipientor bidirectional exchange, depending on the interacting cell types, such as between T cells and antigen-presenting cells. In the recipient cell, the acquired membrane fragments are rapidly internalized through endocytic pathways, allowing integration into the recipient's plasma membrane or processing within intracellular compartments.
The kinetics of membrane transfer are notably rapid, with the entire process typically completing within 1 to 15 minutes, distinguishing trogocytosis from slower forms of cellular uptake. Unlike phagocytosis, which involves complete engulfment of the donor cell or large particles, trogocytosis selectively captures discrete membrane portions without full internalization of the donor. This selectivity is governed by receptor-ligand affinity; for instance, high-affinity interactions promote the transfer of specific receptors like the T cell receptor (TCR) from antigen-presenting cells to T cells, or PD-1 from tumor cells to immune effectors.

Biological Roles

In Immune Regulation

Trogocytosis plays a pivotal role in immune regulation by facilitating the transfer of membrane-bound molecules between immune cells, thereby modulating activation, suppression, and effector functions. This process allows for rapid adaptation of immune responses without de novo protein synthesis, influencing both adaptive and innate immunity. In particular, trogocytosis enables antigen-presenting cells (APCs) and effector cells to exchange regulatory elements that fine-tune T cell responses and natural killer (NK) cell cytotoxicity.
A key mechanism in immune regulation involves antigen acquisition, where T cells trogocytose peptide-major histocompatibility complex (pMHC) complexes from APCs during immunological synapse formation. CD4+ T cells primarily acquire pMHC class II, while CD8+ T cells obtain pMHC class I, enhancing T cell survival, memory formation, and cross-presentation capabilities. This transfer correlates with T cell activation strength and promotes Th2 differentiation through acquisition of transcription factors like GATA3. For instance, trogocytosed pMHC sustains signaling in recipient T cells, amplifying antigen-specific responses without requiring continuous APC contact.
Trogocytosis also contributes to tolerance induction, particularly through regulatory T cells (Tregs) that utilize CTLA-4 to capture co-stimulatory molecules CD80 and CD86 from APCs, such as dendritic cells. This depletion reduces co-stimulatory signals available to conventional T cells, dampening excessive activation and promoting peripheral tolerance. CTLA-4-dependent trogocytosis is essential for Treg suppressive function, as it directly lowers B7 ligand availability on APC surfaces, preventing pro-inflammatory responses in vivo. Studies demonstrate that even cytoplasmic domain-truncated CTLA-4 retains trogocytic activity, highlighting the receptor's role in membrane stripping independent of intracellular signaling.
In effector cells like NK cells, trogocytosis enhances cytotoxicity by allowing acquisition of activating ligands from target cells, supporting serial killing and sustained immune surveillance. NK cells can trogocytose ligands such as Rae-1 via NKG2D or other activation receptors, which bolsters their responsiveness and proliferation without rendering them hyporesponsive in all contexts. For example, transfer of tyrosine kinase receptor TYRO3 from feeder cells via trogocytosis improves NK cell expansion and function, illustrating a positive regulatory loop. Recent studies as of 2024 reveal trogocytosis in the tumor microenvironment (TME), where acquisition of PD-L1 by T cells from APCs or tumor cells promotes T cell exhaustion by upregulating inhibitory markers like Tim-3 and TIGIT, thereby limiting anti-tumor immunity through fratricide and suppressed reactivity. Blocking such transfers restores effector potential, underscoring trogocytosis as a dynamic regulator of immune balance.

In Neurodevelopment

Trogocytosis plays a critical role in synaptic pruning during neurodevelopment, where microglia selectively engage presynaptic elements and induce postsynaptic changes to refine neural circuits. In this process, microglia engage in trogocytosis of presynaptic boutons, transferring membrane patches containing synaptic vesicles and other components, as observed through in vivo imaging and electron microscopy in hippocampal and retinotectal systems. Complement proteins C1q and C3 tag these synaptic elements for recognition, facilitating microglial trogocytosis via complement receptor signaling, which ensures the elimination of weaker or excess connections while preserving functional synapses.
A key example of trogocytosis in axonal refinement is seen in the developing visual system, where microglia control the pruning of retinal ganglion cell (RGC) axons through trogocytosis of presynaptic elements. A 2021 study in Xenopus laevis demonstrated that microglial interactions with RGC axons lead to the transfer of axonal membrane fragments, reducing axon arbor length and branch number; enhancing complement C3 activity further promoted this pruning, while inhibiting it via complement regulators like aRCA3 increased axonal overgrowth. This refinement is essential for establishing precise retinotectal projections, with disruptions in microglial trogocytosis leading to aberrant circuit formation.
Trogocytosis also contributes to circuit maturation by enabling the transfer of guidance molecules between neurons and glia. Specifically, bi-directional trogocytosis of Eph/ephrin complexes allows cells to exchange membrane-bound receptors and ligands, promoting cell repulsion, neurite detachment, and proper axon guidance during embryogenesis. The adaptor protein Gulp1 regulates this process by facilitating membrane scission and engulfment, ensuring efficient rearrangements in neural tissues.
Recent reviews highlight trogocytosis' broader role in neuronal pruning for behavioral adaptation, such as in visual threat detection. For instance, microglial trogocytosis-mediated pruning in the retinotectal circuit underlies the development of appropriate looming responses, where control animals exhibit rapid escape to dark stimuli, but microglial depletion reverses this to responses toward bright stimuli, impairing adaptive behaviors.

In Pathology

Trogocytosis contributes to pathological processes by enabling aberrant membrane exchanges that promote disease progression, particularly in cancer where it facilitates immune evasion in both directions. In various malignancies, including melanoma, immune cells such as T cells and NK cells acquire major histocompatibility complex class I (MHC-I) molecules and ligands like PD-L1 from tumor cells through trogocytosis, which can contribute to exhaustion but also enhance presentation in some contexts. However, in colon cancer, tumor cells acquire inhibitory molecules like CTLA-4 from tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), dampening Th1-mediated antitumor responses. This acquisition masks tumor antigens, reduces NK cell cytotoxicity, and suppresses T cell activation by mimicking host immune inhibitory signals, thereby fostering an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. For instance, in colorectal carcinoma, cancer cells trogocytose CTLA-4 and other checkpoint molecules from tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes, further dampening Th1-mediated antitumor responses.
In infectious diseases, trogocytosis facilitates pathogen survival by allowing microbes and parasites to camouflage themselves with host proteins, evading immune detection. Parasitic protozoa, such as Entamoeba histolytica, actively trogocytose host cell membrane fragments to display human proteins on their surface, protecting against complement-mediated lysis and antibody recognition during tissue invasion. This process requires direct cell-cell contact and actin remodeling, enabling the parasite to integrate acquired proteins into its own membrane for prolonged survival in the host. Similarly, in chronic bacterial infections like those caused by intracellular pathogens such as Francisella tularensis and Salmonella enterica, trogocytosis promotes cell-to-cell spread and immune escape by direct transfer of bacteria along with host membrane components, reducing phagocytic clearance and sustaining persistent infection.
Aberrant trogocytosis exacerbates autoimmunity by promoting the acquisition of self-antigens by autoreactive T cells, amplifying inflammatory responses. At sites of autoimmune inflammation, such as in rheumatoid arthritis or multiple sclerosis, CD4+ T cells exhibit heightened trogocytic activity, rapidly acquiring peptide-MHC complexes and membrane fragments from antigen-presenting cells. This transfer endows T cells with altered antigen specificity, potentially converting them into more aggressive effectors that perpetuate self-reactive responses and tissue damage.
In neurological disorders, dysregulated microglial trogocytosis disrupts synaptic pruning, contributing to neurodevelopmental pathologies like autism spectrum disorder (ASD) and schizophrenia. Microglia normally use trogocytosis, in conjunction with the complement system, to selectively nibble and refine synaptic connections during brain maturation; however, deficits in this process lead to excessive or insufficient pruning, resulting in circuit imbalances associated with social deficits in ASD and cognitive impairments in schizophrenia. For example, in ASD models, altered microglial activation leads to over-pruning of excitatory synapses via phagocytic mechanisms, linking to behavioral abnormalities.

Applications

In Antibody-Based Therapies

In monoclonal antibody (mAb) therapies, trogocytosis mediated by Fc receptors on effector cells, such as macrophages and neutrophils, leads to the stripping of bound antibodies from tumor target cells, thereby reducing the efficacy of antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC). This process involves the transfer of membrane fragments containing the mAb-antigen complex from the target cell to the effector cell, depleting the antibody coating on the tumor surface and limiting subsequent immune engagement.
A key example of trogocytosis as a resistance mechanism is observed in rituximab therapy for B-cell lymphomas, where effector cells rapidly remove rituximab-bound CD20 from lymphoma cells, correlating with diminished ADCC and complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC). In a 2015 study published in Blood, this Fc receptor-dependent trogocytosis was identified as a primary cause of antigen loss and therapeutic failure in chronic lymphocytic leukemia and non-Hodgkin lymphoma patients treated with rituximab, with in vitro assays showing up to 80% reduction in surface CD20 within hours of effector cell contact.
Trogocytosis in mAb therapies exhibits bidirectional effects, as while it removes antibodies from targets, effector cells can acquire tumor-associated antigens through membrane transfer, potentially enhancing immune recognition or, conversely, leading to inhibitory signaling if immunosuppressive molecules are co-transferred. For instance, T cells acquiring melanoma antigens like CSPG4 via trogocytosis show increased IFN- production and cytotoxicity, suggesting a possible augmentation of anti-tumor responses in antibody contexts.
To counter trogocytosis-induced resistance, strategies include engineering mAbs with glyco-modifications, such as afucosylation, to minimize membrane transfer while preserving ADCC. The afucosylated anti-CD20 antibody obinutuzumab, for example, induces significantly less trogocytosis and CD20 downmodulation on B-cell targets compared to rituximab in neutrophil co-cultures, with trogocytosis rates of approximately 50% versus 58% at early time points (3 hours), thereby sustaining antigen density for improved therapeutic outcomes.

In Cellular Immunotherapies

Trogocytosis plays a dual role in cellular immunotherapies, particularly in chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell and natural killer (NK) cell therapies, where it can both enhance effector functions and contribute to therapeutic limitations. In CAR-T cell therapy, bidirectional trogocytosis occurs between engineered T cells and target tumor cells, resulting in the transfer of CAR molecules from the T cells to the tumor surface, which reduces CAR expression on the effector cells and impairs their antigen recognition capacity. Conversely, tumor cells can acquire CARs through this process, potentially masking tumor antigens and promoting immune escape. This bidirectional exchange has been implicated in short-term resistance and long-term relapse observed in CAR-T treatments for hematologic malignancies.
A critical consequence of trogocytosis in CAR-T therapy is fratricide, where CAR-T cells acquire tumor-associated antigens from target cells, rendering the engineered T cells susceptible to self-killing by neighboring CAR-T cells. This phenomenon, detailed in studies of CD19-targeted CAR-T cells against B-cell lymphomas, leads to reduced effector cell persistence and diminished antitumor efficacy. Fratricide exacerbates antigen escape mechanisms, as trogocytosed antigens on CAR-T surfaces trigger cooperative killing among the population, ultimately limiting the durability of responses in clinical settings.
In CAR-NK cell therapies, trogocytosis can enhance serial engagement and killing efficiency, particularly when combined with bispecific antibodies that bridge NK cells to tumor targets. By transferring tumor antigens or bound antibody fragments to the NK cell surface, trogocytosis facilitates rapid disengagement from one target, allowing the effector cell to engage subsequent tumor cells more effectively. This process augments the cytotoxic potential of CAR-NK cells in solid and hematologic tumors, supporting their role in multi-targeting strategies.
Trogocytosis also contributes to relapse in CAR therapies through induction of T-cell exhaustion, as repeated antigen encounters via trogocytosed molecules on effector cells lead to upregulated inhibitory receptors and impaired proliferation. In preclinical models, inhibiting trogocytosis has been shown to mitigate exhaustion markers like PD-1 and TIM-3, improving CAR-T persistence and reducing relapse rates in antigen-positive tumors. Recent strategies include inhibiting cathepsin B to prevent trogocytosis, reducing exhaustion markers like PD-1 and TIM-3, and improving CAR-T persistence in preclinical models. These findings underscore the need for trogocytosis-modulating strategies to optimize cellular immunotherapy outcomes.

In Diagnostic and Research Tools

Trogocytosis serves as a valuable biomarker in immunomonitoring, particularly through flow cytometry to detect the transfer of major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules and T cell receptors (TCRs) from antigen-presenting cells to T cells, enabling assessment of T cell activation and exhaustion states in patient samples. In studies of post-allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplant patients, flow cytometry using dextramers has quantified tumor-associated antigen (TAA)-specific T cells, revealing that up to 90% of patients exhibit circulating exhausted T cells (marked by high inhibitory receptor expression like PD-1 and TIM-3) that retain functional TCRs identifiable via trogocytosis-based isolation. This approach exploits trogocytosis to retrieve TCR specificities from the exhausted T cell pool, providing insights into tumor-specific immune dynamics and potential for adoptive T cell therapies. Additionally, high-resolution flow cytometry in co-culture assays measures trogocytosed ligands on recipient T cells by gating on lineage markers and fluorescence intensity, allowing precise evaluation of activation thresholds in clinical cohorts.
In vitro assays leveraging fluorescent labeling have become essential for quantifying trogocytosis rates, facilitating high-throughput drug screening to identify modulators of immune cell interactions. These assays typically involve co-culturing donor cells (e.g., antigen-presenting cells labeled with PKH dyes or pH-sensitive fluorophores like pHtdGFP) with recipient immune cells, followed by flow cytometry or confocal microscopy to track membrane fragment transfer based on mean fluorescence intensity or percentage of positive events. For instance, dual-stain protocols using Hoechst for live nuclei and SYTOX green for membrane permeability enable direct measurement of trogocytosis-mediated cell killing in suspension or adherent models, with sensitivity to inhibitors like cytochalasin D that reduce events by over 95% in 60 minutes, making them adaptable for 96- or 384-well formats in pharmacological screens. Such methods have been validated for T lymphocyte trogocytosis, subtracting background noise to yield quantitative rates of ligand acquisition, aiding evaluation of compounds targeting immune synapse dynamics.
