 Alexandre de Moraes (born 13 December 1968) is a Brazilian jurist serving as a justice of the Supreme Federal Court (STF) since his appointment in February 2017 by then-President Michel Temer. A graduate of the University of So Paulo Law School (class of 1990), Moraes began his career as a public prosecutor in So Paulo, later becoming a professor of constitutional law, Secretary of Justice and Public Security for So Paulo state, and Minister of Justice and Public Security under Temer before his elevation to the STF. He has also held the presidency of the Superior Electoral Court (TSE), overseeing electoral processes including the 2022 presidential election.
As rapporteur for STF inquiries into "fake news" and "acts against democratic institutions," Moraes has ordered the suspension of numerous social media accounts, websites, and even platforms like X (formerly Twitter) for non-compliance with judicial directives aimed at curbing disinformation and threats to electoral integrity. These actions, including his role in rejecting challenges to President Lula da Silva's 2022 election victory and presiding over the 2025 trial convicting former President Jair Bolsonaro of coup plotting, have positioned him as a key defender of institutional stability amid political polarization. However, his expansive use of judicial authorityencompassing warrantless account blocks, arbitrary detentions, and extraterritorial content removal demandshas sparked accusations of overreach, censorship, and politically motivated persecution, culminating in U.S. government sanctions against him in July 2025 for serious human rights abuses, which were lifted in December 2025 following a call between U.S. President Donald Trump and Brazilian President Luiz Incio Lula da Silva.

Early life and education

Childhood and family background

Alexandre de Moraes was born on December 13, 1968, in So Paulo, Brazil. He grew up in an unostentatious middle-class family in the city.
His father, Lon Lima de Moraes (c. 19442024), worked as a lawyer. His mother was a housewife. Little additional public information exists regarding his early upbringing or siblings.

Academic and early professional training

Alexandre de Moraes graduated with a law degree from the Faculdade de Direito do Largo de So Francisco at the University of So Paulo (USP) in 1990. He later earned a doctorate in State Law (Direito do Estado) from USP in 2000, with a thesis titled Jurisdio constitucional e tribunais constitucionais: garantia suprema da Constituio. In 2001, he obtained livre-docncia (a post-doctoral qualification equivalent to associate professorship eligibility) in Constitutional Law from the same institution.
Following his undergraduate studies, Moraes began his professional career in 1991 by securing first place in the entrance examination for the Public Prosecutor's Office of the State of So Paulo (Ministrio Pblico do Estado de So Paulo), where he served as a public prosecutor (promotor de justia) until 2002. During this period, he handled cases in municipalities such as Agua and Cruzeiro, served as a criminal prosecutor in So Bernardo do Campo, and as a citizenship prosecutor in So Paulo, while also participating in specialized groups on health and consumer rights and acting as an assessor to the state attorney general. This role provided foundational experience in criminal and public interest litigation, including enforcement of constitutional protections.
In parallel with his prosecutorial work, Moraes transitioned into academia in 2002, joining USP as an associate professor (professor associado) in the Department of State Law after passing a public concurso for the position. He taught courses in constitutional and administrative law, later serving as department head from 2012 to 2014, which further honed his expertise in public law theory and practice.

Pre-Supreme Court career

Legal practice and advisory roles

Following his tenure as a public prosecutor in the Ministrio Pblico do Estado de So Paulo from 1991 to January 2002, Alexandre de Moraes transitioned into roles that included private legal practice during intervals between government positions.
In July 2010, de Moraes co-founded the law firm Alexandre de Moraes Sociedade de Advogados in So Paulo, where he practiced as a litigator and provided legal consulting services until December 31, 2015. The firm offered representation and advisory work to both individuals and corporations, drawing on his specialization in constitutional, administrative, and state law.
As a legal consultant through the firm and independently, de Moraes advised clients on matters involving public administration, regulatory compliance, and judicial strategy, consistent with his academic background in direito do estado (state law). This period of private practice occurred amid his intermittent returns to state government advisory capacities, such as informal juridical support to So Paulo authorities prior to his 2015 reappointment as Secretary of Public Security.

Government positions under various administrations

In 2002, at the age of 33, Alexandre de Moraes was appointed Secretary of Justice for the state of So Paulo under Governor Geraldo Alckmin's administration, marking him as the youngest individual to hold the post in the state's history. In this role, he focused on prison system reforms and combating organized crime, amid rising violence rates in the state.
From January 1, 2015, to May 12, 2016, Moraes served as Secretary of Public Security for So Paulo, again under Alckmin's PSDB-led government, managing the state's police forces and responding to escalating criminal activity, including operations against drug trafficking factions. His tenure emphasized aggressive policing strategies, which correlated with a reported 10% drop in homicides in So Paulo by mid-2016, though critics attributed persistent high police lethality ratesover 700 fatal shootings annuallyto his hardline approach.
On May 12, 2016, following his resignation from the state role, President Michel Temer's interim administration appointed Moraes as federal Minister of Justice and Public Security, a position he retained until February 22, 2017. In this capacity, he oversaw national law enforcement amid political instability post-Dilma Rousseff's impeachment, including coordination of federal police investigations into corruption scandals like Operation Car Wash and efforts to stabilize prisons after nationwide riots that killed over 100 inmates in early 2017. His selection drew opposition from left-leaning politicians due to his prior affiliations with center-right governance.

Appointment to the Supreme Federal Court

Nomination by Michel Temer

Following the death of Supreme Federal Court (STF) Justice Teori Zavascki in a plane crash on January 19, 2017, President Michel Temer faced the task of nominating a replacement amid Brazil's ongoing political crisis and corruption scandals.
On February 6, 2017, Temer selected Alexandre de Moraes, who was serving as Minister of Justice and Public Security in his administration, for the vacancy.
Temer highlighted Moraes's robust academic background, including a doctorate in constitutional law, extensive legal experience, and prior roles such as So Paulo State Secretary of Public Security, as qualifying him for the position.
Moraes, a longtime ally of Temer with ties to the Brazilian Democratic Movement Party (MDB), was positioned as a jurist capable of upholding judicial independence during a period of intense scrutiny over Lava Jato (Operation Car Wash) investigations, which Zavascki had overseen.
The choice drew immediate criticism from opposition figures and leftist senators, who argued it represented a strategic move to install a loyalist potentially sympathetic to Temer's government amid impeachment threats and graft probes implicating allies.

Confirmation process and initial controversies

President Michel Temer nominated Alexandre de Moraes, his Minister of Justice and Public Security, to the Supreme Federal Court (STF) on February 6, 2017, to fill the vacancy created by the death of Justice Teori Zavascki in a plane crash on January 19, 2017. The Brazilian Constitution requires Senate approval for such nominations, involving a hearing before the Commission on Constitution and Justice (CCJ) followed by a plenary vote needing at least 41 favorable votes from 81 senators.
On February 21, 2017, the CCJ conducted Moraes' sabatina, a public hearing where he defended his legal philosophy, emphasizing strict adherence to the Constitution and impartiality in judging Lava Jato (Operation Car Wash) cases, which Zavascki had overseen. The commission approved his nomination by a secret vote of 19 to 7. The following day, February 22, 2017, the Senate plenary confirmed him in another secret ballot, with 55 votes in favor and 13 against. Moraes was sworn in on March 22, 2017.
The nomination sparked immediate controversies, primarily from leftist opposition senators who viewed it as a politically motivated appointment to protect Temer's administration from ongoing corruption investigations, given Moraes' alignment with Temer's center-right coalition and his role in managing security during Dilma Rousseff's impeachment. Critics, including civil society groups, highlighted potential conflicts of interest from Moraes' prior private legal practice, such as defending the Trancooper cooperative linked to organized crime allegations, and accused him of plagiarism in his 2010 book Direito Constitucional. Additionally, his tenure as So Paulo's Secretary of Public Security (2015-2016) faced scrutiny for aggressive tactics against protesters and organized fan groups, which some human rights advocates deemed excessive. Despite these objections, supporters from legal associations praised his technical expertise and commitment to institutional stability.

Major judicial actions and inquiries

2018present inquiries into fake news and digital militias

In March 2019, Supreme Federal Court (STF) President Dias Toffoli authorized the opening of Inquiry 4781, known as the fake news inquiry, to investigate the dissemination of fraudulent news, false communications, and threats against the court and its members. Alexandre de Moraes, as the designated reporting justice, oversaw the probe, which proceeded without an initial formal complaint from the Public Ministry, relying instead on the STF's internal police force for evidence collection. The inquiry's scope encompassed online content deemed to undermine judicial authority, including coordinated campaigns labeled as "digital militias"organized networks accused of amplifying disinformation through social media bots and influencers.
Throughout 2019 and into 2020, Moraes issued orders for the suspension of accounts on platforms like Facebook and Twitter linked to investigated individuals, framing these as necessary to curb threats to democratic institutions. By July 2020, the inquiry expanded to target profiles associated with alleged digital militias, resulting in blocks of dozens of accounts accused of systematic fake news propagation. Moraes cited evidence of criminal associations formed to spread such content, including attacks on STF justices, as justifying the measures.
The inquiry faced significant criticism for procedural irregularities, including its ex officio initiation and lack of prosecutorial oversight, which opponents argued violated Brazil's accusatory legal system by allowing the court to act as investigator, accuser, and judge. In June 2020, the STF upheld the inquiry's constitutionality by a 10-1 vote, with Moraes defending it as a response to existential threats from disinformation networks, though dissenting Justice Marco Aurlio Mello highlighted risks to due process. Senators from opposition ranks condemned the validation as an assault on the rule of law, pointing to selective enforcement against conservative voices critical of the judiciary. Empirical data from the period showed the probe's focus on content challenging STF decisions, such as those related to Operation Lava Jato, amid claims that mainstream sources often downplayed similar biases in left-leaning media. The inquiry remains active as of 2025.

Handling of 8 January 2023 Braslia attacks

Following the invasion of Brazil's federal government buildings in Braslia on January 8, 2023, by supporters of former President Jair Bolsonaro protesting the election results, Supreme Federal Court (STF) Justice Alexandre de Moraes assumed a central role in the response as the rapporteur for related inquiries. The attacks damaged the STF headquarters, Congress, and Planalto Palace, prompting Moraes to authorize federal police operations targeting both participants and alleged enablers. Over 1,500 individuals were initially investigated, with Moraes issuing orders for preventive detentions and searches to prevent further unrest.

Investigations, arrests, and trials

Moraes directed the Federal Police inquiry into the events, framing them as an attempted coup d'tat under Brazil's anti-democratic crimes law, leading to probes into security lapses and coordination among military police and political figures. On January 10, 2023, he issued arrest warrants for Anderson Torres, the former Federal District public security secretary, for failing to secure the capital despite warnings. Subsequent operations, authorized by Moraes, resulted in arrests of military police commanders accused of complicity, with evidence cited including communications urging inaction during the riots. By August 2023, over 400 arrests had been made, many upheld by STF rulings under Moraes' oversight.
Trials commenced in September 2023 at the STF, with Moraes presiding over cases divided into "cores" based on involvement levels, such as direct participants and disinformation spreaders. Convictions followed for charges including coup attempt and violent abolition of the democratic order, with the "fourth core" groupaccused of propagating false narratives to incite the attacksfacing majority STF votes for guilt by October 2025. In total, dozens received sentences ranging from years in prison, often with pleas reducing terms, while investigations expanded to Bolsonaro allies suspected of planning.

Criticisms of procedural overreach

Moraes' approach drew accusations of bypassing due process, including indefinite detentions without formal charges and suspensions from public office based on preliminary evidence. Critics, including legal analysts, argued that jailing individuals solely for social media posts critical of institutions risked conflating speech with incitement, potentially eroding free expression protections. Reports highlighted cases where preventive arrests exceeded constitutional limits, with some detainees held for months pending trials, prompting concerns over judicial activism favoring institutional defense over individual rights. While supporters viewed these measures as necessary to safeguard democracy post-invasion, opponents contended they enabled selective enforcement against Bolsonaro-aligned figures, fueling perceptions of politicized justice.

Investigations, arrests, and trials

Following the 8 January 2023 attacks on Brazil's congressional, presidential, and Supreme Court buildings in Braslia, Justice Alexandre de Moraes, serving as relator for the cases before the Supreme Federal Court (STF), authorized the Federal Police to conduct widespread arrests of participants, with over 1,500 individuals detained in the initial days for suspected involvement in vandalism, invasion, and anti-democratic acts. These arrests included on-site detentions during the unrest and subsequent warrants targeting organizers, financiers, and inciters identified through video evidence, social media posts, and witness testimonies.
Moraes initiated multiple inquiries under STF Inqurito 4.875, the primary probe into the "anti-democratic acts," expanded to encompass planning, funding, and coordination of the invasions, with additional inquritos opened on 23 January 2023 specifically for alleged terrorist elements, such as coordinated destruction and calls for institutional overthrow. By March 2023, after reviewing over 1,400 custody cases, Moraes upheld preventive detention for 294 suspects deemed high-risk for flight, obstruction, or further threats to public order, while ordering releases for others pending trial. Investigations revealed patterns of interstate travel organized via messaging apps, financial contributions totaling millions of reais for logistics, and involvement by military personnel and Bolsonaro administration holdovers, leading to targeted arrests of figures like former Justice Minister Anderson Torres in January 2023.
Trials commenced in 2023 under STF's First and Second Panels, with Moraes consistently voting for convictions on charges including criminal association, qualified damage, and coup d'tat attempts under Brazil's Anti-Terrorism Law and Penal Code, resulting in sentences ranging from 3 to 17 years imprisonment plus political ineligibility. By April 2024, 13 individuals had been condemned, with 88 remaining imprisoned and 43 formal indictments accepted; this escalated to 371 convictions by January 2025 from over 2,000 investigated, encompassing 1,628 total actions, including 518 for grave offenses like ablation of democratic order. Further judgments in mid-2024 added 119 convictions in virtual sessions, prioritizing evidence of premeditation such as pre-attack encampments and public incitements. By August 2025, the STF had held 1,190 individuals accountable across these proceedings, with ongoing reviews converting some preventive detentions to house arrest amid capacity constraints in federal prisons.

Criticisms of procedural overreach

Critics have argued that Alexandre de Moraes' handling of the investigations following the 8 January 2023 attacks in Braslia involved excessive preventive arrests without sufficient individualized evidence of criminal involvement, leading to over 1,500 initial detentions, including many who were merely present at protest encampments rather than directly participating in the invasions. Supreme Federal Court Justice Nunes Marques, in a February 2023 vote, described these "large-scale arrests" as "worrisome," emphasizing the need for case-by-case evaluation to avoid collective punishment, as Brazilian law requires concrete proof for preventive detention under Article 312 of the Code of Criminal Procedure.
Procedural centralization under Moraes, who assumed rapporteurship of Inquiry 4,781 without random distribution, has been faulted for violating the principle of the natural judge and enabling unchecked authority, with detentions processed via informal "certificates" that bypassed standard timelines for judicial review. Former STF Justice Marco Aurlio Mello, a longtime colleague, publicly criticized these measures as "extreme acts" that unduly impacted citizens' lives, arguing in 2024 that Moraes' approach risked eroding judicial impartiality by combining investigative, prosecutorial, and adjudicative roles.
Further concerns include prolonged detentions exceeding legal limitssome lasting months without formal chargesand denials of habeas corpus petitions, with the STF rejecting multiple requests in early 2023 on grounds of public security threats, despite Inter-American human rights standards requiring prompt review within 24-48 hours. Critics, including U.S. officials, have labeled these as arbitrary detentions constituting human rights abuses, pointing to the release of 149 women in March 2023 amid public pressure as evidence of initial overreach. By January 2025, while 371 convictions had been secured, appeals highlighted cases where guilt was inferred solely from proximity to events, without direct evidence of crimes like ablation of liberty or coup attempt.

Regulatory actions against social media platforms

As rapporteur for Supreme Federal Court (STF) inquiries into fake news and digital militias since 2019, Alexandre de Moraes has issued numerous orders requiring social media platforms to remove content and suspend accounts deemed to promote disinformation, threats to democratic order, or anti-institutional acts, often under Brazil's Marco Civil da Internet framework and STF authority to protect elections and institutions. These directives have included multimillion-real fines for non-compliance, such as penalties imposed on platforms for failing to expeditiously block users investigated for alleged coordination in spreading election-related misinformation during the 2022 presidential vote.
De Moraes' most prominent clashes have involved X (formerly Twitter), where he mandated the suspension of dozens of accounts linked to supporters of former President Jair Bolsonaro, citing their role in alleged plots to undermine judicial independence and electoral integrity. Elon Musk, X's owner, defied these orders in April 2024 by publicly announcing resistance to what he termed unconstitutional censorship, prompting de Moraes to escalate with threats of operational shutdowns and asset freezes on non-compliant entities. The platform's refusal to appoint a local legal representativerequired under Brazilian law for foreign companiesand to pay over 28 million reais (approximately $5 million USD) in fines for prior violations intensified the standoff.
On August 30, 2024, de Moraes ordered the nationwide suspension of X after it missed a deadline to designate a representative, enforcing the block through internet service providers and prohibiting related financial transactions. A five-justice STF panel unanimously upheld the measure on September 2, 2024, rejecting X's arguments that it violated free speech protections under Brazil's constitution. De Moraes subsequently imposed daily fines of 5 million reais (about $900,000 USD) on entities aiding circumvention via VPNs or mirrors, accumulating over 11 million reais by mid-September for detected violations.
X resumed operations in Brazil on October 8, 2024, after complying by appointing a legal representative, paying fines, and submitting to ongoing content moderation mandates, though Musk continued to decry the episode as emblematic of judicial overreach. In a broader regulatory push, de Moraes supported STF rulings in June 2025 declaring portions of the prior platform liability regime unconstitutional, thereby holding social media firms directly accountable for user-generated illegal contentsuch as hate speech or defamationabsent proactive moderation, with potential civil and criminal penalties for failures. These measures, while justified by de Moraes as essential to combating organized digital threats to democracy, have drawn accusations from international observers of enabling disproportionate censorship without adequate due process for affected users.

Disputes with Twitter/X and Elon Musk

In late 2022 and early 2023, following Elon Musk's acquisition of Twitter (rebranded as X in July 2023), Justice Alexandre de Moraes issued multiple orders requiring the platform to suspend accounts accused of disseminating fake news, threats against democratic institutions, and content linked to investigations into digital militias and the January 8, 2023, Braslia attacks. These directives stemmed from de Moraes' oversight of Supreme Federal Court (STF) inquiries into anti-democratic acts, targeting profiles associated with former President Jair Bolsonaro's supporters, which X partially complied with but increasingly resisted as Musk publicly decried them as censorship. Musk argued that the orders violated Brazilian law and free speech principles, posting on X that de Moraes was acting as an "unelected judge" suppressing dissent, while de Moraes maintained the measures were necessary to curb illegal threats and disinformation campaigns.
Tensions escalated in April 2024 when de Moraes ordered X to block additional accounts spreading misinformation about Bolsonaro's 2022 election defeat, prompting Musk to label the justice a "tyrant" and refuse full compliance, citing X's global free speech commitments. By August 2024, de Moraes demanded X appoint a local legal representative to receive court notifications and ensure accountability, threatening arrest warrants for non-compliance; X responded by closing its Brazil offices on August 17 to shield employees, a move Musk justified as protecting against "criminal liability" for judicial defiance. On August 29, Musk intensified rhetoric, calling for de Moraes' impeachment and imprisonment, accusing him of destroying democracy through "totalitarian" measures, while de Moraes proceeded to suspend X nationwide on August 30, ordering internet providers to block access and imposing fines of 50,000 reais (about $9,000 USD) per VPN circumvention attempt.
The suspension lasted from August 30 to October 8, 2024, affecting X's 21.5 million Brazilian users and drawing international scrutiny over proportionality, with Musk framing it as a test of global censorship resistance and de Moraes upholding it via STF panel unanimous vote on September 2 as enforcement against "willful" evasion. X's reinstatement followed compliance, including appointing a representative and blocking ordered accounts, though Musk attributed the reversal partly to financial pressures on Starlink operations in Brazil, where de Moraes had frozen assets temporarily. Disputes persisted into 2025, with de Moraes imposing a $1.4 million fine on February 20 for prior non-compliance and ongoing content removal mandates, amid Musk's continued public criticisms portraying the actions as selective enforcement against conservative voices. Critics, including Musk, contended the orders disproportionately targeted right-leaning accounts without equivalent scrutiny of left-wing equivalents, while de Moraes' defenders cited empirical evidence of coordinated harassment campaigns documented in STF proceedings.

Temporary suspension of X in Brazil (2024)

In August 2024, Supreme Federal Court (STF) Justice Alexandre de Moraes ordered the nationwide suspension of the social media platform X (formerly Twitter) after the company failed to appoint a legal representative in Brazil by the court-imposed deadline of August 30, 2024. The decision stemmed from X's repeated non-compliance with prior judicial directives, including the removal of accounts accused of disseminating disinformation, threats to democratic institutions, and involvement in investigations related to anti-democratic activities post-2022 elections. De Moraes cited X's closure of its Brazil office in June 2024following threats of arrest against local staff for non-complianceas evidence of obstruction, arguing that without a local representative, the platform could evade accountability for content moderation orders.
The suspension took effect on August 31, 2024, with de Moraes directing Brazil's National Telecommunications Agency (Anatel) and internet service providers to block access to X across the country, imposing daily fines of up to 50,000 reais (approximately $9,000 USD) on non-compliant providers and an initial 8.1 million reais ($1.42 million USD) fine on X for prior violations. Elon Musk, X's owner, publicly denounced the order as an attack on free speech, accusing de Moraes of authoritarianism and refusing to censor content deemed protected expression, which escalated a months-long public feud. On September 2, 2024, a five-justice panel of the STF unanimously upheld de Moraes' ruling, rejecting X's appeals and imposing an additional 28.6 million reais ($5 million USD) in fines for ongoing non-compliance, including attempts to circumvent the block via VPNs or alternative access methods.
Critics, including Musk and conservative commentators, argued the suspension exemplified judicial overreach, selectively targeting platforms and accounts aligned with opposition figures like former President Jair Bolsonaro while enforcing broad content removals under vague "disinformation" criteria, potentially violating Brazil's constitutional free speech protections. De Moraes and supporters countered that the measures enforced Brazilian sovereignty over digital platforms operating within its jurisdiction, comparable to regulations in the European Union, and were proportionate responses to X's "willful" defiance amid risks to electoral integrity. The block affected over 20 million Brazilian users, prompting workarounds like VPNs, though de Moraes later fined entities aiding circumvention.
The suspension ended on October 8, 2024, when de Moraes authorized X's reinstatement after the company complied by appointing a local representative, paying accumulated fines exceeding 28 million reais, and committing to future adherence to court orders on content moderation. This resolution highlighted tensions between national regulatory authority and global tech firms' resistance to localized compliance, with ongoing appeals from X challenging the underlying content removal mandates.

Prosecutions of political opponents

Cases against Jair Bolsonaro and allies

In 2019, Supreme Federal Court Justice Alexandre de Moraes initiated an inquiry into the dissemination of fake news, threats against judicial institutions, and acts undermining democracy, which expanded to target former President Jair Bolsonaro and his supporters for alleged coordination of disinformation campaigns via social media and digital militias. This probe, authorized by the STF plenary, resulted in search warrants executed on May 27, 2020, against Bolsonaro allies including lawmakers, advisors, and influencers accused of producing and spreading content critical of the court. Several individuals faced account suspensions on platforms like Facebook and Twitter, with de Moraes ordering compliance under threat of fines, leading to convictions for crimes such as criminal association and incitement in subsequent trials.
As president of the Superior Electoral Court (TSE) from August 2022, de Moraes played a central role in a June 30, 2023, ruling that declared Bolsonaro ineligible to hold public office until 2030, citing abuse of political power and misuse of media during the 2022 election campaign. The decision stemmed from a July 2022 meeting where Bolsonaro publicly accused the electoral system of fraud without evidence, disseminating claims to foreign ambassadors, which the TSE majority deemed an intentional effort to undermine voter confidence. Bolsonaro's running mate, Walter Braga Netto, received a similar ineligibility ruling in the same proceeding.
De Moraes authorized a federal police investigation into Bolsonaro's alleged falsification of COVID-19 vaccination records, culminating in a May 3, 2023, raid on his residence and the seizure of his phone, based on evidence of manipulated entries in official systems to evade mandates. Bolsonaro was indicted on March 19, 2024, for falsification and related offenses, alongside aides accused of facilitating the scheme. However, de Moraes dismissed the probe on March 28, 2025, citing procedural issues, though related inquiries into broader misuse of public health data persisted.
In July 2025, de Moraes imposed precautionary measures on Bolsonaro, including an electronic monitoring anklet, restrictions on movement and communication, and a prohibition on granting interviews for dissemination on social media platforms, citing risks of flight and obstruction of justice in ongoing investigations. These measures were ratified by the STF's First Turma. Brazilian media outlets, including O Globo, criticized the restrictions on interviews as excessive and disproportionate, raising concerns about impacts on freedom of expression and press rights. Bolsonaro's allies, such as Flvio Bolsonaro, accused de Moraes of abuse of authority and political persecution, proposing his impeachment. Defenders of the measures maintained they were necessary to protect investigations into anti-democratic activities.
Allies prosecuted under de Moraes' oversight include former congressman Daniel Silveira, convicted in 2022 for threats against STF justices via videos linked to the fake news network, resulting in an eight-year sentence later commuted by Bolsonaro but upheld in appeals. Other cases involved military officers and communications aides, such as those targeted in 2020 operations for orchestrating attacks on electoral integrity, leading to indictments for slander and conspiracy. These actions, while defended by the STF as necessary to protect democratic institutions, drew accusations from Bolsonaro's camp of selective enforcement favoring left-leaning figures, with no equivalent probes into similar rhetoric from opponents.

Allegations of coup planning and election interference

In February 2023, Supreme Federal Court Justice Alexandre de Moraes authorized federal police to investigate former President Jair Bolsonaro and several allies for allegedly orchestrating a plot to overthrow the constitutional order after Bolsonaro's loss in the October 2022 presidential runoff election to Luiz Incio Lula da Silva. The inquiry focused on actions from November 2022 through the January 8, 2023, riots in Braslia, alleging that Bolsonaro and military commanders discussed military intervention to prevent Lula's inauguration, including proposals for a state of siege under Article 137 of Brazil's constitution to annul the election results. Prosecutors claimed Bolsonaro personally drafted a decree to declare electoral fraud and authorize federal intervention in electoral bodies, based on testimony from his former aide-de-camp Mauro Cid, who admitted in plea bargain deals to participating in efforts to fabricate evidence of irregularities in electronic voting systems.
The allegations extended to election interference, with federal police reporting that Bolsonaro's circle, including allies in the armed forces and his administration, attempted to coerce the Superior Electoral Court (TSE)chaired by Moraes at the timeby disseminating unsubstantiated claims of widespread voter fraud and hacking vulnerabilities in the voting machines, aiming to erode public trust and justify nullifying Lula's 50.9% victory margin. Specific incidents cited included a July 2022 meeting where Bolsonaro presented a video to military leaders alleging TSE manipulation, and subsequent pressure on the armed forces to produce a technical report endorsing fraud claims, which was later discredited by independent audits confirming the election's integrity. Moraes, acting as relator, expanded the probe in 2023 to encompass an "organized criminal group" involving Bolsonaro's sons, former ministers like Anderson Torres, and military figures such as Army Commander Jlio Cesar de Arruda, accusing them of coordinating disinformation campaigns and logistical planning for post-election unrest.
By November 2024, Attorney General Paulo Gonet formally indicted Bolsonaro on charges including coup d'tat under Article 359-L of the Penal Code, violent abolition of the democratic rule of law, and forming an armed criminal organization, with evidence drawn from seized documents, intercepted communications, and Cid's corroborated statements detailing assassination contingency plans targeting Lula and Moraes to facilitate the plot. The case proceeded to trial before a five-justice panel of the Supreme Court starting September 2, 2025, where Moraes presented over five hours of prosecutorial arguments, leading to convictions on September 11, 2025, for all eight defendants despite a dissent from Justice Luiz Fux who voted to acquit most of them, including a 27-year sentence for Bolsonaro on grounds of attacking democratic institutions. Bolsonaro's defense countered that the proceedings relied on coerced testimony and lacked direct evidence of intent to violently seize power, framing the charges as retaliation for his criticisms of the judiciary's electoral oversight.

International responses and sanctions

US sanctions under Global Magnitsky Act (2025)

On July 30, 2025, the U.S. Department of the Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) designated Alexandre de Moraes as a Specially Designated National under Executive Order 13818, which implements the Global Magnitsky Human Rights Accountability Act. The designation accused de Moraes of being responsible for or complicit in serious human rights abuses, including the authorization of arbitrary pre-trial detentions and the suppression of freedom of expression through his judicial role, particularly by compelling social media platforms including X to censor content amid persisting tensions with Elon Musk over such orders, linked to prior 2024 disputes. U.S. officials described these actions as de Moraes acting as "judge and jury in an unlawful witch hunt against U.S. and Brazilian citizens and companies," particularly in investigations targeting political opponents and perceived threats to Brazilian institutions.
The sanctions froze any assets de Moraes holds in U.S. jurisdiction and prohibited U.S. persons from engaging in transactions with him, effectively barring access to the U.S. financial system. This measure was framed as part of a broader U.S. response to actions by the Brazilian government perceived as threats to American interests, including censorship of U.S.-based platforms and politically motivated prosecutions. The U.S. State Department emphasized that the Global Magnitsky framework targets foreign persons involved in human rights abuses or corruption, with de Moraes's designation highlighting his alleged weaponization of judicial authority to shield allies and punish dissent.
On September 22, 2025, the sanctions expanded to de Moraes's wife, Viviane Barci de Moraes, and members of his support network, including individuals and entities accused of enabling his activities by providing material support. These additional designations aimed to disrupt financial channels sustaining the alleged abuses, with Treasury stating it would continue targeting enablers to hold de Moraes accountable. The moves intensified a diplomatic rift between the U.S. and Brazil, prompting Brazilian officials to denounce them as interference in sovereign judicial processes.
Critics of the sanctions, including human rights organizations, argued that applying Global Magnitsky in this context politicized the tool, potentially undermining its credibility for addressing genuine abuses elsewhere, as the basis relied on contested interpretations of Brazilian legal proceedings. U.S. actions under the Trump administration were also linked to prior disputes, such as de Moraes's regulatory clashes with U.S. tech firms, though official justifications centered on documented patterns of detention without due process and speech restrictions.
On December 12, 2025, the U.S. Department of the Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) revoked the sanctions by removing Alexandre de Moraes, his wife Viviane Barci de Moraes, and associated entities from the Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) List under the Global Magnitsky Act, following a phone call between U.S. President Donald Trump and Brazilian President Luiz Incio Lula da Silva amid easing bilateral tensions. No significant updates on the conflict with Elon Musk were reported in early 2026.

Reactions from other governments and organizations

On August 17, 2024, Argentine President Javier Milei publicly condemned Alexandre de Moraes on X (formerly Twitter), describing the escalation as a deepening "dictatorship" after Moraes ordered the dismissal of the CEO of a private Brazilian payment processing company for non-compliance with judicial directives.
In response to the US sanctions imposed on de Moraes on July 30, 2025, sixteen Members of the European Parliament, including Polish MEP Dominik Tarczyski, addressed a letter to the EU High Representative for Foreign Affairs and Security Policy, urging the imposition of targeted sanctions against de Moraes for allegedly weaponizing Brazil's judiciary to suppress political opponents and violate fundamental rights.
Human Rights First, a non-governmental organization focused on global human rights advocacy, issued a statement on July 31, 2025, criticizing the US sanctions as a misuse of the Global Magnitsky Act to shield political allies from accountability rather than advancing human rights, while defending the legitimacy of investigations into threats against Brazilian democracy.

Political ideology and views

Self-identification as classical liberal

Alexandre de Moraes has publicly identified as a classical liberal, emphasizing principles such as republican government and a limited role for the state in economic affairs. In an April 2025 interview with The Economist, he explicitly described himself as a "classical liberal," aligning his views with advocacy for restrained state intervention in the market while supporting robust institutional frameworks to maintain order. This self-characterization underscores his stated preference for governance that prioritizes individual liberties within a republican structure, drawing from historical liberal traditions that balance freedom with legal accountability.
Moraes has reinforced this identification through his career trajectory, noting that he has exclusively served under center-right administrations, including roles in the governments of Geraldo Alckmin as So Paulo's Secretary of Public Security from 2015 to 2016 and Michel Temer as Minister of Justice from 2016 to 2017. He has argued that classical liberalism necessitates firm measures against criminality to protect societal freedoms, positing that public perception of justice requires demonstrable enforcement, as evidenced by his endorsement of "tough repression" toward lawbreakers to ensure citizens feel secure.
This positioning contrasts with perceptions of his judicial activism, particularly in regulating digital platforms and prosecuting perceived threats to democracy, yet Moraes maintains that such actions align with liberal imperatives to safeguard institutions from subversion. His articulation of classical liberalism thus centers on a synthesis of economic restraint and authoritative defense of the rule of law, though secondary analyses from outlets like the Instituto Liberal question the coherence given expansions in state oversight under his influence.

Criticisms of authoritarian tendencies and selective enforcement

Critics, including United States government officials and Brazilian opposition figures, have accused Alexandre de Moraes of exhibiting authoritarian tendencies through the issuance of hundreds of unilateral orders to censor online content, suspend user accounts, and impose platform-wide restrictions, often without due process or transparency. These measures, primarily targeting individuals associated with former President Jair Bolsonaro, have included arbitrary pre-trial detentions, passport confiscations, and prosecutions for social media posts deemed critical of electoral processes or judicial institutions. In July 2025, the U.S. Treasury Department sanctioned de Moraes under the Global Magnitsky Act for "serious human rights abuse," citing his role in an "oppressive campaign of censorship" and "politicized prosecutions" that suppressed freedom of expression.
Allegations of selective enforcement center on de Moraes' focus on right-leaning political actors and media outlets while appearing to overlook comparable actions by left-wing groups or government-aligned entities. For instance, leaked communications from a specialized investigative unit in September 2024 revealed de Moraes' instructions for the "deliberate selection" of targets in inquiries related to anti-democratic activities, with critics pointing to a pattern where Bolsonaro allies faced swift account suspensions and arrests post-2022 elections, but similar scrutiny was not applied to leftist networks accused of disinformation or unrest. U.S. Congressman Rich McCormick described this as turning the judiciary into a "weapon to crush opposition" and "protect President Lula," arguing that de Moraes' inquiries into election denial disproportionately targeted conservatives despite historical precedents of electoral irregularities under prior administrations. Brazilian commentators have highlighted double standards, such as de Moraes' aggressive pursuit of "digital militias" linked to Bolsonaro while investigations into left-wing involvement in protests or corruption scandals progressed more slowly or were deprioritized.
These practices have drawn comparisons to judicial overreach, with de Moraes' solo decisions bypassing full Supreme Court collegiality in many cases, fostering perceptions of a "mafia-like" operational tone in target selection and enforcement. International observers, including the U.S. State Department, have condemned the approach as a "targeted and politically motivated effort" to silence dissent, particularly affecting U.S. companies like X (formerly Twitter) that resisted compliance with opaque removal orders. Defenders counter that such actions safeguard democracy against extremism, but detractors maintain that the lack of equivalent measures against pro-government narratives undermines claims of impartiality.

Personal life

Family and relationships

Alexandre de Moraes has been married to Viviane Barci de Moraes, a lawyer specializing in civil and commercial law, since early in his career. The couple met during Moraes' formative professional years and maintains a low public profile regarding their personal life.
They have three children: Gabriela Barci de Moraes, Giuliana Barci de Moraes, and Alexandre Barci de Moraes. Viviane Barci de Moraes and at least two of the children are co-owners in a family-linked law firm, reflecting shared professional interests in legal practice. No public details exist on prior relationships or separations, consistent with the family's emphasis on privacy amid Moraes' high-profile judicial role.

Public image and security concerns

Alexandre de Moraes elicits sharply divided public perceptions in Brazil, with admirers viewing him as a bulwark against political violence, corruption, and attempts to undermine democratic institutions, particularly following the January 8, 2023, invasion of government buildings in Braslia by supporters of former President Jair Bolsonaro. Critics, including Bolsonaro allies and free speech advocates, portray him as an overreaching authority figure whose judicial orderssuch as content removals from social media platforms and the 2024 nationwide suspension of X (formerly Twitter)constitute censorship and erode civil liberties. These detractors argue that Moraes has blurred lines between investigation and adjudication by leading inquiries into fake news and extremism while issuing rulings in the same cases, fostering perceptions of selective enforcement against right-wing figures. Recent revelations about his wife Viviane Barci de Moraes' law firm holding a R$129 million contract for representing Banco Mastera bank liquidated amid allegations of R$12 billion in fraudhave fueled accusations of hypocrisy in his anti-corruption efforts, with the contract continuing post-liquidation and raising potential conflicts of interest given his judicial role. Additionally, leaked messages from Daniel Vorcaro, ex-owner of Banco Master, to his then-fiance Martha Graeff in 2025 describe at least five meetings with "Alexandre" (identified as Supreme Court Justice Alexandre de Moraes), including one on April 19, 2025, near his home; these messages, recovered by federal police, were revealed in early March 2026 amid investigations into Banco Master. Federal Police analysis of Vorcaro's cellphone data further indicates WhatsApp messages exchanged with de Moraes on November 17, 2025, the day of Vorcaro's arrest in the scandal, including nine screen recordings sent to Moraes' number, which had been in Vorcaro's contacts since 2023. However, on March 6, 2026, the STF's Secretariat of Communication, at the request of Minister de Moraes' office, issued a note stating that technical analysis of Vorcaro's telematic data, made public by the CPMI do INSS, shows the single-view messages sent by him on November 17, 2025, do not match de Moraes' contacts in the seized files; the screenshots of these messages are linked to folders of other contacts in Vorcaro's list and do not appear as directed to de Moraes, being stored in folders associated with other phone numbers on his devices. De Moraes has denied that these messages were directed to him. This controversy gave rise to the pejorative meme "Careca do Master" (or variations such as "Careca Master" or "careca do master"), a nickname circulating on Brazilian social media to imply influence peddling and systemic corruption, paralleling the "Careca do INSS" figure from a major pension fraud scheme; the meme gained traction in late 2025 and early 2026 despite an investigation into the matter being archived for lack of evidence.
His confrontations with high-profile international actors, such as Elon Muskwho defied Moraes's platform orders leading to fines and temporary blocksand U.S. political figures aligned with Bolsonaro, have amplified global scrutiny, positioning him as a symbol of judicial activism in battles over digital regulation and political accountability. Brazilian media outlets like O Globo have highlighted this polarization, noting that while some hail him as a defender of the rule of law, others deride his methods as inquisitorial, contributing to waning support even among segments of the judiciary and public weary of prolonged institutional tensions.
Security concerns for Moraes have escalated due to his central role in probes targeting Bolsonaro's inner circle and far-right networks, resulting in repeated death threats from extremist elements opposed to his rulings. As a Supreme Federal Court justice, he receives protection from the Federal Police, a protocol heightened after the 2023 Braslia riots and subsequent arrests, which intensified backlash from radicalized groups. Moraes has publicly acknowledged such risks while maintaining that they stem from efforts to counter threats to democracy, though critics contend his aggressive tactics exacerbate personal and institutional vulnerabilities.
