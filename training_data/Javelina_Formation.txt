Javelina Formation
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Javelina Formation is a geologic unit of the Tornillo Group in southwestern Texas, primarily exposed in Big Bend National Park within Brewster County, dating to the middle to late Maastrichtian stage of the Late Cretaceous period, approximately 69 to 66 million years ago. It consists of fluvial sediments, including lenticular conglomeratic sandstones, varicolored mudstones, and bentonitic clays, deposited in a southeast-trending inland valley about 300400 km from the contemporary shoreline, with a thickness ranging from 120 to 200 meters. The formation is renowned for its rich vertebrate fossil assemblage, including the giant pterosaur Quetzalcoatlus northropi and other azhdarchids, titanosaurian sauropods like Alamosaurus sanjuanensis, ceratopsians such as Bravoceratops polyphemus and Torosaurus cf. T. utahensis, as well as hadrosaurians, tyrannosaurids, crocodilians, turtles, and early mammals, reflecting a diverse Late Cretaceous terrestrial ecosystem.
Stratigraphically, the Javelina Formation overlies the Aguja Formation conformably, marked by a basal chert-pebble conglomerate, and underlies the Paleocene Black Peaks Formation, with the Cretaceous-Paleogene boundary occurring near the top of the unit or in the lowermost Black Peaks. Its lithology is divided into three main facies: stream channel deposits (about 27% of the section, comprising cross-bedded sandstones and conglomerates), overbank floodplain sediments (54%, including mudstones and paleosols), and abandoned channel-lake deposits (19%, with fine-grained silts and alkaline lake indicators like gastropod shells). The depositional environment was subtropical, with mean annual temperatures of 1623C and low annual rainfall under 1 meter, supporting angiosperm-dominated woodlands interspersed with conifers, palms, and vines, as evidenced by petrified wood such as Javelinoxylon multiporosum and araucariacean conifers.
Paleontologically, the Javelina Formation is significant for yielding some of the largest known flying vertebrates, including Quetzalcoatlus northropi (with wingspans up to 10 meters) and the smaller Quetzalcoatlus lawsoni (wingspan ~5 meters), which inhabited floodplain margins and shallow lakes for foraging on small vertebrates and invertebrates. Dinosaur remains indicate a fauna transitional between earlier Campanian-Maastrichtian assemblages and the final Lancian land-mammal "age," with no evidence of the end-Cretaceous iridium anomaly within the formation itself. Plant fossils, including in situ stumps and growth-ringed logs showing responses to periodic droughts or floods, highlight a recovering ecosystem influenced by Laramide orogeny and volcanism, while invertebrate traces like termite borings underscore biotic interactions in this ancient riparian habitat. Overall, the formation provides critical insights into the final stages of non-avian dinosaurian dominance and the environmental conditions preceding the Cretaceous-Paleogene mass extinction.

Geological Setting

Location and Extent

The Javelina Formation is situated in Brewster County, southwestern Texas, where it is primarily exposed within Big Bend National Park. The formation's type locality is near Javelina Creek in the northeastern part of Tornillo Flat, and its exposures extend across the park's interior, including areas north and south of the Chisos Mountains. It overlies the Aguja Formation in the regional stratigraphic sequence.
The formation encompasses the Tornillo Basin, a Laramide foreland basin characterized by fluvial and lacustrine deposits formed during Late Cretaceous subsidence. Its outcrops form prominent belts, with major exposures along drainages such as Tornillo Creek and extending toward the Rio Grande to the south, as well as in the park's badlands and dissected slopes. These exposures create resistant cuestas and hogback ridges due to indurated sandstone layers, covering an estimated surface area of several hundred square kilometers within the basin's approximately 50100 km width.
Thickness of the Javelina Formation varies from 120 to 200 meters, generally thinning southwestward from the basin's deeper eastern sectors toward structural highs like the Grapevine anticline. This variation reflects differential subsidence rates during the Laramide orogeny, which influenced the basin's evolution and subsequent uplift, enhancing modern topographic relief and exposure through erosion of overlying units.

Stratigraphy

The Javelina Formation constitutes the middle unit of the Tornillo Group, a sequence of Upper Cretaceous to Eocene continental strata exposed in the Big Bend region of Texas. It directly overlies the Aguja Formation, with the contact marked by a basal chert-pebble conglomerate and a possible minor disconformity attributed to penecontemporaneous erosion of the underlying marine-influenced shales. This boundary reflects a shift from paralic deposits below to fluvial systems above, though the contact is locally sharp and laterally variable.
The formation underlies the Black Peaks Formation, with a transitional and locally intertonguing contact defined at the top of the highest persistent ridge-forming sandstone interval below the mudstone-dominated upper unit of the overlying formation. This gradational boundary underscores the continuity of fluvial sedimentation across the two units, with no major erosional hiatus evident. The type section for the Javelina Formation is designated along exposures near Javelina Creek, between the Big Bend National Park road and Tule Mountain in Brewster County, Texas, where the unit attains a thickness of approximately 123190 m depending on the measured locality.
Internally, the Javelina Formation lacks formal members but is characterized by two main divisions: a lower unit dominated by well-indurated sandstones interbedded with minor mudstones, and an upper unit rich in mudstones with subordinate sandstones. These divisions represent repeated progradational cycles within fluvial systems, with four major sandstone intervals that can be traced laterally across the outcrop belt in the type area. Such cyclicity highlights the vertical stacking of channel and overbank deposits, with sandstone proportions decreasing upward.
Regionally, the Javelina Formation correlates with terrestrial sequences along the southwestern margin of the Western Interior Seaway, as evidenced by reworked marine microfossils in its sandstones that link it to coeval paralic facies to the east and north. This positioning integrates the unit into broader Western Interior Basin frameworks, where it parallels nonmarine equivalents in southern Laramidia, though detailed lithostratigraphic matching remains limited by facies variations.

Lithology and Depositional Environment

Rock Composition

The Javelina Formation is primarily composed of well-indurated sandstones interbedded with bentonitic clays, mudstones, and claystones, along with nodular calcareous concretions scattered throughout the sequence. The sandstones, which form prominent lenticular bodies, are predominantly quartzose and feldspathic in composition, with grains ranging from fine- to medium-sized and often displaying cross-bedding structures. In contrast, the clays and mudstones exhibit a structureless and massive appearance, typically varicolored in shades of gray, purple, and yellow, reflecting their fine-grained, low-energy depositional nature.
Minor lithologic components include thin volcanic ash layers interpreted as tuffs, which occur as lenticular, white-weathering beds in the upper parts of the formation. Calcareous nodules, both nodular and as pebble conglomerates at sandstone bases, are abundant and signify pedogenic processes such as soil formation and carbonate precipitation. The induration of the sandstones, achieved through calcite and silica cements, enhances the durability of these rocks and aids in the preservation of embedded fossils.
The mineralogical makeup of the formation, including high quartz and feldspar content in sandstones alongside smectite-rich bentonites in clays, points to a provenance dominated by erosion of western highlands during Laramide orogeny, with additional contributions from recycled Cretaceous carbonates and volcanics.

Sedimentary Features and Paleoenvironment

The Javelina Formation represents a fluvial depositional system divided into three main facies: stream channel deposits (27% of the section), overbank floodplain sediments (54%), and abandoned channel-lake deposits (19%). The channel sandstones, typically 310 m thick and comprising the stream channel facies, exhibit trough cross-bedding and low-angle cross-stratification indicative of migrating bedforms within meandering streams, along with lateral accretion surfaces and point-bar sequences that reflect bar migration and channel avulsion. Floodplain deposits consist of varicolored mudstones with well-developed paleosols, including Inceptisols and Alfisols featuring calcic horizons, root traces, and bioturbation, which demonstrate periods of subaerial exposure and soil formation. The abandoned channel-lake facies includes drab olive green to gray calcareous claystones, nodular limestones, tan bioturbated siltstones, and fine-grained silts with alkaline lake indicators such as gastropod shells.
These sedimentary features record deposition on a broad, southeast-flowing alluvial plain within the Tornillo foreland basin, influenced by Laramide tectonics that drove subsidence and sediment supply from rising uplands to the west. The formation's channels, estimated at 3.5 m deep and 45 m wide with meander wavelengths of 500600 m, indicate ephemeral, high-velocity streams draining an inland landscape approximately 300400 km from the nearest shoreline and over 1000 km from the retreating Western Interior Seaway. Semi-arid conditions prevailed, as evidenced by pedogenic calcretes and carbonate nodules in paleosols, suggesting low precipitation (less than 1 m annually) and warm subtropical temperatures (1623C) with minimal seasonality; minor evaporite traces in associated facies further support this arid paleoclimate.
Stratigraphically, the formation shows a vertical transition from lower, sandstone-dominated meandering river channels to upper overbank fines and mudstone-rich floodplains, reflecting increased basin subsidence that promoted finer sediment aggradation and floodplain expansion toward the end of the Maastrichtian. This evolution underscores the dynamic response of the alluvial system to tectonic subsidence in the foreland setting, with vegetation such as scattered wood fragments indicating localized riparian stabilization along channels.

Age and Chronology

Radiometric Dating

Radiometric dating of the Javelina Formation primarily relies on U-Pb geochronology applied to interbedded volcanic tuffs and detrital zircons within sedimentary layers, providing absolute age constraints for its Maastrichtian deposits. A key interbedded tuff in the middle section of the formation, located approximately 90 m below the top of the unit, yielded a U-Pb age of 69.0 0.9 Ma using monazite crystals analyzed via isochron methods, establishing a middle Maastrichtian timeframe for this interval. This age aligns with broader regional correlations, though subsequent studies have emphasized zircon-based approaches for higher precision in similar Laramide foreland settings.
Detrital zircon U-Pb analyses from upper Javelina Formation samples provide maximum depositional ages (MDAs) that bracket the latest deposition near the K-Pg boundary. For instance, a sample from the uppermost Javelina at the Dawson Creek section yields a youngest single-grain (YSP) MDA of 66.3 0.5/1.6 Ma using LA-ICP-MS depth-profiling on detrital zircons, while a sample from the lowermost Black Peaks Formation at Tornillo Flat yields 67.8 0.4/1.6 Ma. These MDAs indicate deposition continued into the latest Maastrichtian, with the formation spanning approximately 6966.5 Ma overall, consistent with its overlap with Lancian biostratigraphic zones.
The Javelina Formation is entirely Maastrichtian, separated from the overlying Paleocene Black Peaks Formation by a disconformable contact that represents a hiatus of approximately 3 million years spanning the K-Pg boundary; the boundary itself is not preserved within the formation or at the contact, as constrained by integrated geochronologic and paleontologic data across multiple sections in Big Bend National Park. This placement reflects a pre-boundary Maastrichtian dominance, with no direct volcanic ash layers dated precisely at the boundary itself.
Uncertainties in these age determinations arise from the sparse distribution of datable tuff layers and potential reworking of volcanic material into detrital populations, which can inflate MDAs if recycled zircons from older eruptions are present. Potential Pb loss in young zircons further complicates the youngest age estimates, necessitating multi-grain statistical approaches like weighted mean or Bayesian modeling for robust interpretation. Despite these challenges, the combined U-Pb dataset firmly anchors the Javelina Formation to the late Maastrichtian, ~34 m.y. prior to the K-Pg mass extinction.

Biostratigraphy

The biostratigraphy of the Javelina Formation is primarily established through its vertebrate and floral assemblages, which assign the unit to the middle to late Maastrichtian stage of the Late Cretaceous, corresponding to the Edmontonian and Lancian North American Land Mammal Ages (NALMAs). The lower portion of the formation is characterized by Edmontonian-index vertebrates, including ceratopsians such as Bravoceratops polyphemus and hadrosaurs like Kritosaurus sp., alongside early appearances of multituberculate mammals of the family Cimolomyidae. These taxa indicate a relative age of approximately 7069 Ma, correlating with the upper Edmontonian subage and magnetic polarity chron C31.
In contrast, the upper Javelina Formation yields a Lancian assemblage dominated by index dinosaurs such as the sauropod Alamosaurus sanjuanensis, ceratopsians including Torosaurus cf. utahensis, hadrosaurs like ?Gryposaurus alsatei, and theropods such as Tyrannosaurus sp., which collectively mark the late Maastrichtian (ca. 6966 Ma). Floral biostratigraphy reinforces this division, with the lower formation featuring conifer-dominant assemblages rich in podocarpacean woods and subordinate dicots, shifting upward to more diverse dicot-rich floras including Javelinoxylon multiporosum alongside araucariacean conifers, signaling environmental changes across the NALMA boundary.
The Javelina Formation correlates closely with other Lancian units, such as the Hell Creek Formation of the northern Great Plains, based on shared vertebrate taxa like Alamosaurus and Tyrannosaurus, indicating contemporaneous deposition across southern and northern Laramidia. The Cretaceous-Paleogene (K-Pg) boundary lies above the Javelina, within the overlying Black Peaks Formation, inferred from the persistence of dinosaurs to the formation's top and the absence of a prominent iridium anomaly or impact horizon in Javelina strata, with only minor iridium enrichment noted in transitional sections. This places the entire Javelina below the K-Pg extinction event, with its upper Lancian zonation extending to within ~3 m.y. of the boundary.

Fossil Assemblage

Vertebrate Paleofauna

The vertebrate paleofauna of the Javelina Formation is characteristic of the Lancian North American Land Mammal Age, reflecting a diverse Late Maastrichtian assemblage dominated by large-bodied dinosaurs alongside smaller reptiles, pterosaurs, and early mammals. This fauna indicates a terrestrial ecosystem with fluvial influences, where fossils primarily represent herbivores and carnivores adapted to floodplain environments. Key discoveries include remains of titanosaurian sauropods, hadrosaurian and ceratopsian ornithischians, and tyrannosaurid theropods, with notable pterosaur and crocodyliform contributions enhancing the assemblage's diversity.
Among Dinosauria, Sauropoda is represented by the titanosaur Alamosaurus sanjuanensis, known from multiple localities yielding partial skeletons, including articulated cervical vertebrae and pelvic bones, often preserved in channel sandstones. Ornithischia includes hadrosaurians such as Kritosaurus sp., identified from cranial and postcranial elements in the lower formation, suggesting at least two sympatric species of duck-billed dinosaurs. Ceratopsians are prominent, with the chasmosaurine Bravoceratops polyphemus documented from a bonebed in the lowermost formation containing elements from approximately 37 individuals, including robust squamosals and parietals indicative of large frill-bearing herbivores. Additional ceratopsian material includes frill elements referred to Torosaurus cf. T. utahensis, further diversifying the chasmosaurine presence in the formation. Theropoda features tyrannosaurids, including a subadult maxilla and possible adult vertebrae tentatively referred to cf. Tyrannosaurus sp., marking one of the southernmost records of this group.
Pterosauria is exemplified by the azhdarchid Quetzalcoatlus northropi, the largest known flying vertebrate with an estimated wingspan exceeding 10 meters, based on wing elements and other postcranials recovered from multiple sites. Reptilia includes Crocodyliformes such as cf. Brachychampsa indet., represented by isolated osteoderms and dental fragments consistent with small-bodied alligatoroids. Testudines features baenid turtles like Baena sp., with partial skulls, shells, and postcranial elements indicating semi-aquatic habits.
Mammalia in the Javelina Formation aligns with Lancian biochronology, comprising multituberculates, marsupials, and primitive placentals recovered as microvertebrate fossils from floodplain deposits, underscoring the presence of small, insectivorous to omnivorous forms near the Cretaceous-Paleogene boundary.
Taphonomic patterns reveal a fluvial depositional regime, with most vertebrate remains occurring as disarticulated bones concentrated in channel lags and sandstone lenses, often showing abrasion and hydraulic sorting that favored preservation of robust elements from larger taxa. Bonebeds, such as those of ceratopsians and juvenile Alamosaurus, exhibit minimal weathering and rapid burial, suggesting seasonal flooding events transported and accumulated carcasses in river systems.

Flora

The flora of the Javelina Formation is primarily represented by petrified wood, which dominates the preserved plant assemblage and includes contributions from both gymnosperms and angiosperms. Coniferous woods attributable to the Araucariaceae and Cupressaceae families are prevalent, with araucarioid features such as alternate intertracheary pitting and cross-field pitting characteristic of Araucariaceae, while Cupressaceae/Podocarpaceae types exhibit narrower growth rings indicative of periodic water stress. Dicotyledonous angiosperm woods, comprising five distinct types, include those resembling modern laurels (Lauraceae) and structures suggestive of woody vines, reflecting a diverse understory component among larger trees. Fan palms, preserved as permineralized stems and leaf impressions of Sabalites, add to the woody diversity and indicate riparian palm groves.
Leaf impressions and rare fruits further highlight angiosperm diversity, with taxa showing adaptations to varying moisture levels; upward in the formation, there is a noted shift toward more hygrophilous forms, such as those in multi-aged communities on alluvial substrates under sub-humid conditions. These elements suggest increasing representation of moisture-dependent angiosperms in the latest Cretaceous landscape.
Petrified wood and impressions are most abundant in floodplain paleosols, pointing to woodland riparian zones that provided localized habitat amid broader semi-arid plains, where conifers and dicots formed canopy and understory layers suited to periodic flooding. The Araucariaceae woods from the upper Maastrichtian portion of the formation represent the youngest known record of this family in North America.

Research History

Discovery and Naming

The geological strata now comprising the Javelina Formation were first documented in 1907 as part of the Tornillo Clay by Johan A. Udden, who described exposures along Tornillo Creek in southwestern Texas and noted the presence of fossil wood and saurian bones indicative of a Mesozoic age. These early observations laid the groundwork for recognizing the continental deposits spanning the Late Cretaceous to early Paleogene in the Big Bend region.
During the 1930s, the U.S. Geological Survey conducted extensive mapping of the area's Permian and Cretaceous rocks as part of broader regional surveys, including work by Philip B. King on the adjacent Marathon Basin, which encompassed preliminary delineations of the Cretaceous continental sequences near the future Big Bend National Park. The establishment of Big Bend National Park in 1944 significantly improved access to these remote exposures, facilitating further geological and paleontological exploration. In 1940, just prior to the park's creation, paleontologist Barnum Brown discovered key fossils, including vertebrae of the sauropod dinosaur Alamosaurus sanjuanensis, in sediments later assigned to the Javelina Formation, confirming its Late Cretaceous affinity and drawing attention to the site's paleontological richness.
The Javelina Formation was formally named in 1967 by R.A. Maxwell, J.T. Lonsdale, R.T. Hazzard, and J.A. Wilson in their comprehensive study of Big Bend National Park geology, designating it for the Maastrichtian (Late Cretaceous) portion of the redefined Tornillo Group. The name derives from Javelina Creek in the northeastern Tornillo Flat area of the park, Brewster County, Texas, with the type section exposed on the south side of Dawson Creek, approximately 534 feet thick and characterized by variegated clays, sandstones, and fossiliferous horizons. This nomenclature clarified the stratigraphic separation from overlying Paleocene-Eocene units, building on decades of prior reconnaissance to establish the formation's distinct identity within the region's Tornillo sequence.

Notable Studies

During the 1970s and 1980s, vertebrate paleontological investigations led by Thomas M. Lehman significantly contributed to the understanding of the Javelina Formation's fossil record, including detailed descriptions of major taxa such as the titanosaurian sauropod Alamosaurus sanjuanensis and other large vertebrates. Lehman's analyses of skeletal remains from multiple localities revealed the diversity and ecological roles of these large-bodied vertebrates in late Maastrichtian floodplain environments, establishing the formation as a critical site for linking southern and northern North American faunal provinces.
In the 2000s, stratigraphic studies, including revisions by Lehman and collaborators, refined the internal divisions of the Tornillo Group and clarified the position of the Javelina Formation relative to the Cretaceous-Paleogene boundary. These efforts involved detailed mapping and sedimentological analysis across Big Bend National Park, resolving ambiguities in formation contacts and demonstrating the unit's conformable relationship with underlying and overlying strata.
Subsequent U-Pb geochronological work in the 2010s, building on earlier efforts, provided high-precision dates from zircon in tuff beds within the Javelina Formation, anchoring its deposition to the middle Maastrichtian. These dates, combined with magnetostratigraphic correlations, have enabled more accurate placement of fossil-bearing horizons and improved regional chronostratigraphic frameworks for Laramide foreland basins.
In the 2020s, ongoing research has further detailed fossil curation and stratigraphic correlations, including comprehensive reviews of pterosaur localities and refined placements of the Cretaceous-Paleogene boundary through integrated chemostratigraphy and paleomagnetism.
Collectively, these studies have offered profound insights into pre-K-Pg dinosaur-dominated communities, the tectonic evolution of Laramide basins, and associated end-Cretaceous climate dynamics, with fossils from key sites illustrating shifts in fluvial paleoenvironments and biotic turnover.
