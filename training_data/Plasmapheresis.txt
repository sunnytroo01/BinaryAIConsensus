Plasmapheresis
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Plasmapheresis, also known as therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE), is an extracorporeal blood purification technique that selectively removes and replaces a patient's plasma while returning cellular blood components, such as red blood cells and platelets, to treat conditions involving pathogenic plasma factors like antibodies or toxins.
The procedure originated in the mid-20th century as a method for plasma collection in blood banks but evolved into a therapeutic modality by the 1970s for managing autoimmune and neurological diseases, with modern applications expanding to intensive care settings under the guidance of specialists like nephrologists and critical care physicians. It operates via centrifugation or membrane filtration to separate plasma from whole blood, typically exchanging 1 to 1.5 plasma volumes per session (about 40-60 mL/kg body weight) over 2-3 hours, using replacement fluids such as albumin, fresh frozen plasma, or saline to maintain oncotic pressure and coagulation factors. Sessions are often repeated daily or every other day for 3-5 treatments, depending on the condition, with vascular access via peripheral veins or central catheters.
Plasmapheresis is indicated as a first-line therapy for disorders including Guillain-Barr syndrome, where it removes harmful antibodies attacking peripheral nerves to accelerate recovery and reduce symptom severity; thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura, by depleting anti-ADAMTS13 autoantibodies; and myasthenia gravis crises, targeting circulating acetylcholine receptor antibodies. It serves as second-line treatment for conditions like acute multiple sclerosis relapses unresponsive to steroids, systemic lupus erythematosus with severe organ involvement, and certain vasculitides, as categorized by the American Society for Apheresis (ASFA) guidelines, which grade evidence from randomized trials (1A) to expert opinion (1D). Emerging uses include supportive care in acute liver failure and sepsis. Experimental applications for removing pro-aging biomarkers have shown potential, with a 2025 clinical trial demonstrating an average biological age reduction of 2.6 years, though efficacy varies and requires further study.
While generally safe, plasmapheresis carries risks such as hypocalcemia from citrate anticoagulation leading to paresthesias or tetany, hypotension from volume shifts, allergic reactions to replacement fluids, and infection at access sites, necessitating careful patient selection and monitoring. Contraindications include hemodynamic instability, lack of vascular access, and recent use of angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, which heighten anaphylactoid risks. ASFA's evidence-based categories help clinicians weigh benefits against potential harms, ensuring its role as an adjunctive rather than standalone therapy in most cases.

Definition and Basics

Definition and Purpose

Plasmapheresis is an extracorporeal medical procedure that involves the separation of plasma from the cellular components of blood, with the cellular elements typically returned to the patient while the plasma is either discarded, replaced, or collected. This process utilizes specialized equipment, such as centrifuges or membrane filters, to achieve the separation outside the body, allowing for targeted manipulation of the plasma fraction. In therapeutic contexts, plasmapheresis serves to remove harmful substances from the plasma, including autoantibodies, immune complexes, toxins, or excess proteins that contribute to disease pathology. For donation purposes, it facilitates the collection of source plasma from healthy individuals, which is then used to manufacture plasma-derived therapies like immunoglobulins and clotting factors.
The primary objectives of plasmapheresis are to alleviate symptoms in patients with plasma-mediated disorders by depleting pathogenic elements and to provide a safe, efficient means of plasma procurement for biopharmaceutical production. Therapeutically, it aims to rapidly lower the concentration of deleterious plasma components that cannot be effectively addressed through other means, thereby interrupting disease processes such as autoimmune attacks or toxin accumulation. In donation settings, the procedure ensures that donors experience minimal risk, as their blood cells are reinfused, preserving essential oxygen-carrying and clotting functions. Replacement fluids, such as albumin or fresh frozen plasma, may be administered during therapeutic exchanges to maintain oncotic pressure and prevent hypovolemia.
Plasmapheresis is a specific form of apheresis, which broadly encompasses any procedure for selectively removing or collecting blood components; while apheresis can target cells like platelets or leukocytes (cytapheresis), plasmapheresis exclusively focuses on plasma separation and exchange. This distinction underscores its role in addressing conditions where plasma-borne factors are the primary culprits, rather than cellular elements.
At its core, plasmapheresis operates on the composition of whole blood, which consists of approximately 55% plasmaa straw-colored fluid comprising about 90% water along with dissolved proteins (such as albumin, globulins, and fibrinogen), electrolytes, nutrients, hormones, and antibodiesand 45% cellular components, including red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets. The plasma fraction serves critical functions like immune defense, clotting, and transport of substances, but in pathological states, it can harbor harmful agents that plasmapheresis effectively isolates and removes. By targeting this liquid portion, the procedure minimizes disruption to the vital cellular elements that sustain circulation and hemostasis.

Types and Variations

Plasmapheresis can be performed using two primary methods: centrifugation and membrane filtration. Centrifugation-based plasmapheresis separates blood components based on their density differences in a rotating centrifuge, which is the most common approach in therapeutic settings due to its efficiency in processing large volumes. Membrane filtration plasmapheresis, on the other hand, employs semipermeable membranes to filter plasma from cellular elements by size, often used in settings like hemodialysis and allowing for selective removal of specific plasma components without discarding the entire plasma volume. These variations enable adaptation to different clinical needs, such as therapeutic plasma exchange (which includes replacement) or plasma collection for donation.

Procedure and Mechanism

Principles of Operation

Plasmapheresis operates by extracting blood from the patient, anticoagulating it to prevent clotting, and separating plasma from cellular components based on differences in density and size, before returning the processed blood. Anticoagulation is typically achieved using citrate, which chelates calcium to inhibit the coagulation cascade, administered at a ratio of approximately 1:10 to 1:16 anticoagulant-to-blood volume to minimize risks like hypocalcemia. Blood is withdrawn through venipuncture with large-bore peripheral needles or central venous catheters, ensuring efficient flow rates of 50-150 mL/min to support the procedure.
The centrifugation method relies on applying high-speed rotation to exploit density gradients in blood, where plasma (density ~1.025 g/mL) forms the lightest layer atop denser red blood cells (~1.095 g/mL) and other components. Blood is spun at speeds of 1000-2000 RPM in a continuous-flow centrifuge, generating a relative centrifugal force (RCF) that stratifies components into distinct layers for selective plasma removal. The RCF is calculated using the formula:
RCF=1.118105r(RPM)2
\text{RCF} = 1.118 \times 10^{-5} \times r \times (\text{RPM})^2

where $ r $ is the rotor radius in centimeters, ensuring forces of 200-500 g sufficient for separation without excessive hemolysis.
In filtration-based plasmapheresis, plasma separation occurs across semipermeable membranes with pore sizes of 0.3-0.5 m, which retain cellular elements like red blood cells (7-8 m) and platelets (2-3 m) while permitting plasma passage. The process is driven by transmembrane pressure (TMP) gradients, typically maintained below 100-200 mm Hg to avoid clotting or membrane fouling, with blood flow rates of 100-250 mL/min and plasma filtration rates of 30-60 mL/min. This yields a filtration fraction of 15-35%, optimizing efficiency while preventing filter clogging.
Plasma volume for exchange is estimated at approximately 40 mL/kg body weight, derived from total blood volume adjusted for hematocrit (e.g., plasma volume total blood volume (1 - hematocrit), with total blood volume ~70 mL/kg). Therapeutic sessions commonly exchange 1-1.5 plasma volumes to achieve 60-80% removal of targeted plasma solutes, balancing efficacy with procedural duration.

Step-by-Step Process

The plasmapheresis procedure begins with thorough preparation to ensure patient safety and procedural efficacy. This includes a comprehensive patient assessment involving vital signs measurement, laboratory tests such as hematocrit levels, and evaluation of overall health status to determine suitability for the procedure. Vascular access is established, typically via peripheral intravenous lines using 17-19 gauge needles in patients with adequate veins, or a central venous catheter for those with poor peripheral access, with local anesthesia applied if needed. The apheresis equipment is primed with saline to remove air and prepare the system for blood processing.
Blood is then withdrawn from the patient at a controlled rate of 50-120 mL per minute through the vascular access into the apheresis machine. Inside the machine, the blood undergoes separation, most commonly via centrifugation, where plasma is isolated from cellular components like red blood cells; alternatively, membrane filtration may be used with pores of 0.3-0.5 microns. The extracted plasma volume typically corresponds to 1-1.5 times the patient's calculated plasma volume (estimated as body weight in kg 0.065 (1 - hematocrit), approximately 2,000-4,000 mL for an average adult), and is collected for discard or further processing. Anticoagulation is administered concurrently, often with citrate or heparin, to prevent clotting during the extracorporeal circuit.
Following separation, the cellular components are returned to the patient along with a replacement fluid to maintain intravascular volume and oncotic pressure. The replacement fluid, infused at an equal volume to the removed plasma (approximately 40-60 mL/kg), commonly consists of 5% albumin solution, sometimes mixed with saline or fresh frozen plasma if coagulation factors are low. This return process occurs continuously or in cycles to minimize hemodynamic shifts.
Throughout the procedure, patients are monitored continuously for vital signs including blood pressure and heart rate to detect any instability, with adjustments made as needed to the flow rates or fluids. Each session typically lasts 1-3 hours, depending on the volume processed and patient tolerance. Upon completion, post-procedure observation includes rechecking vital signs and laboratory parameters like calcium and fibrinogen levels, followed by vascular access care.
For therapeutic purposes, plasmapheresis is usually performed in 3-5 sessions spaced every 24-48 hours, tailored to the clinical response, while plasma donation contexts may involve single sessions.

Therapeutic Applications

Indications and Targeted Conditions

Plasmapheresis, or therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE), is indicated for a range of conditions involving pathogenic plasma components, such as autoantibodies, immune complexes, or toxins, where rapid removal provides clinical benefit. According to the American Society for Apheresis (ASFA) guidelines (as of 2023), indications are categorized based on evidence strength: Category I denotes first-line therapy where apheresis is essential and decision-making is unaffected by its availability; Category II indicates second-line therapy as an acceptable alternative; and Category III reflects an unclear or evolving role. Typical procedures involve exchanging 1 to 1.5 plasma volumes per session, often daily or every other day, using albumin or plasma as replacement fluid, with schedules tailored to the condition's acuity.
In autoimmune and neuromuscular disorders, plasmapheresis targets circulating autoantibodies that mediate tissue damage. For Guillain-Barr syndrome (GBS), it is a Category I indication (Grade 1A), removing anti-ganglioside antibodies responsible for nerve demyelination and conduction block, with exchanges typically performed daily or every other day for 5 sessions totaling 200-250 mL/kg. In myasthenia gravis, classified as Category I (Grade 1B), plasmapheresis depletes anti-acetylcholine receptor antibodies that impair neuromuscular transmission, providing rapid symptom relief in crises through 3-5 alternate-day exchanges of 3-5 L. In patients undergoing thymectomy, preoperative plasmapheresis rapidly reduces symptoms by removing circulating antibodies, aiding faster surgical recovery; post-operatively, it provides quick relief in flare-ups but is not routine if surgery is uncomplicated, with decisions based on antibody levels, clinical status, and thymus pathology by neurologists and surgeons. Similarly, for chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP), a Category I therapy (Grade 1B), it eliminates pathogenic demyelinating antibodies, with initial courses of 2-3 exchanges per week for 2-3 weeks.
Hematologic conditions benefit from plasmapheresis through removal of inhibitors and supplementation of deficient factors. Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP) is a Category I indication (Grade 1A), where plasmapheresis removes anti-ADAMTS13 autoantibodies that inactivate the enzyme, while replacing functional ADAMTS13 from donor plasma to prevent microvascular thrombosis; daily 1-1.5 plasma volume exchanges continue until platelet recovery exceeds 150 10^9/L. In hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS), particularly atypical forms, it holds a Category III role (Grade 2C), aiming to remove autoantibodies or dysregulated complement factors, though evidence is limited and typically involves 1-1.5 volume exchanges with plasma replacement.
Other indications include renal, neurologic, and toxicologic emergencies. For acute exacerbations of multiple sclerosis, plasmapheresis is Category II (Grade 1A), targeting inflammatory cytokines and antibodies in relapsing-remitting disease unresponsive to steroids, with 5-7 alternate-day exchanges of 1.5 plasma volumes. Goodpasture syndrome, involving anti-glomerular basement membrane antibodies, is a Category I indication (Grade 1B), with daily exchanges for 10-14 treatments to halt pulmonary-renal hemorrhage. In Waldenstrm macroglobulinemia with hyperviscosity syndrome, Category I (Grade 1B), it reduces elevated IgM levels causing retinopathy and neurologic symptoms via 1-2 sessions of 1-1.5 plasma volumes. For toxin removal, such as in Amanita phalloides mushroom poisoning, plasmapheresis serves a Category III role by extracting protein-bound amatoxins, recommended within 24-48 hours post-ingestion with 1-2 daily exchanges until toxin clearance.
The overarching mechanism of plasmapheresis in these conditions is the swift depletion of pathogenic plasma factorsfaster than endogenous clearance or immunosuppressionthereby interrupting disease progression and allowing physiologic recovery.

Clinical Efficacy and Guidelines

The efficacy of therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE) is supported by randomized controlled trials (RCTs), meta-analyses, and observational studies, as summarized in the American Society for Apheresis (ASFA) guidelines (Ninth Special Issue, 2023). For Category I indications like Guillain-Barr syndrome and thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura, RCTs demonstrate significant improvements in outcomes, such as reduced disability and faster platelet recovery, with TPE equivalent or superior to alternatives like intravenous immunoglobulin. In myasthenia gravis crises, TPE provides rapid symptom relief within days, though long-term benefits require adjunctive therapies (Grade 1B evidence). In patients undergoing thymectomy, preoperative TPE has been shown to lead to superior outcomes, including faster recovery, higher response rates, and reduced incidence of myasthenic crisis compared to no immunomodulatory treatment or IVIG.
ASFA categorizes indications from I to IV based on apheresis's role (essential to ineffective) and grades evidence from 1A (strong RCT recommendation) to 2C (weak observational). Category I (e.g., TTP, GBS) has robust Grade 1A/1B support; Category II (e.g., multiple sclerosis relapses) shows benefit as adjunct (1A); Category III (e.g., atypical HUS) has limited/weak evidence (2C). Overall, TPE accelerates recovery in antibody-mediated diseases but is adjunctive, with risks weighed per patient. No major updates to core indications as of 2025.

Plasma Donation Aspects

Donation Procedure

The donation procedure for plasmapheresis begins with rigorous donor screening to ensure safety and eligibility, adhering to guidelines set by regulatory bodies such as the FDA and EMA. Eligible donors are typically between 18 and 65 years old, weigh more than 50 kg (110 lbs), and have a hemoglobin level of at least 12.5 g/dL for females or 13.0 g/dL for males. Additional criteria exclude individuals with recent tattoos or body piercings obtained in unregulated settings (deferral period of 12 months), or 3 months in regulated facilities, active infections, or high-risk behaviors for transmissible diseases, as determined by standardized questionnaires and physical assessments, including FDA's 2023 risk-based eligibility questions for HIV and hepatitis.
Once screened and deemed eligible, plasma collection occurs via automated plasmapheresis machines, such as the Haemonetics PCS2 system, which separate plasma from whole blood through centrifugation. The process involves drawing approximately 500-800 mL of plasma per session, depending on the donor's size and plasma volume, while returning red blood cells, platelets, and saline to maintain the donor's fluid balance. For a typical 70 kg donor, plasma yield ranges from 625-800 mL, calculated based on estimated total plasma volume (about 40 mL/kg body weight) to limit removal to no more than 25-30% per donation. Anticoagulation is achieved using citrate solutions, such as acid citrate dextrose (ACD), at an anticoagulant-to-whole blood ratio of approximately 1:10 to 1:16 to prevent clotting during the procedure.
Donations are limited in frequency to protect donor health, with FDA regulations permitting collections no more than once every 48 hours and up to twice per seven-day period, resulting in a maximum of 104 donations annually. Donors require a recovery period of 24-48 hours between sessions to allow for fluid shifts and protein replenishment. Regulatory oversight includes mandatory infectious disease testing per AABB standards, screening each donation for HIV-1/2 and hepatitis B/C via nucleic acid testing and serologic assays to ensure the plasma is free of pathogens before processing.

Uses of Collected Plasma

Collected plasma from plasmapheresis donations is processed in two primary ways to preserve its therapeutic components. To produce fresh frozen plasma (FFP), the plasma is separated and frozen within 8 hours of collection to maintain the activity of clotting factors and other labile proteins; this product is stored at -18C or colder for up to one year and thawed for direct transfusion when needed. Alternatively, plasma intended for further manufacturing is pooled from multiple donors and undergoes fractionation, typically via the Cohn cold ethanol precipitation method, to isolate specific proteins such as albumin, immunoglobulins, and clotting factors.
These fractionated products serve critical therapeutic roles. Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG), derived from the immunoglobulin fraction, is used to treat primary immunodeficiencies and hypogammaglobulinemia by providing passive immunity through pooled antibodies. Albumin solutions, extracted as a major plasma protein, are administered to manage hypovolemia in shock or trauma and to support fluid resuscitation in burn patients by maintaining oncotic pressure. Cryoprecipitate, obtained by thawing FFP at controlled temperatures, is rich in factor VIII, von Willebrand factor, and fibrinogen, making it essential for treating hemophilia A and other coagulopathies.
During manufacturing, pathogen inactivation steps are integrated to enhance safety by reducing the risk of viral transmission. Common methods include solvent-detergent treatment, which disrupts lipid envelopes of viruses like HIV and hepatitis, and pasteurization (heat treatment at 60C for 10 hours), effective against a broad range of enveloped and non-enveloped pathogens. For IVIG production, fractionation yields a product concentrated at 50-100 g/L (5-10% solution), representing approximately 5% of the original plasma's protein content after purification steps that achieve over 95% immunoglobulin recovery in optimized processes.
Globally, plasma-derived products meet substantial demand, with around 19 million liters fractionated annually across reporting countries as of 2018. In the United States, over 2.2 million units of plasma are transfused yearly as of 2025, supporting surgical and emergency interventions. Demand surged during the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic, leading to shortages of convalescent plasmaharvested from recovered donorsfor experimental treatment of severe cases, highlighting supply chain vulnerabilities.
Beyond therapeutics, collected plasma supports non-clinical applications, including biomedical research to study protein interactions and drug effects, as well as vaccine development where hyperimmune plasma provides antibodies for adjuvant or reagent production.

Risks and Complications

Common Adverse Effects

Plasmapheresis, also known as therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE), is associated with an overall complication rate of 4-25% across procedures, with most adverse effects being mild to moderate and occurring in 5-12% of adult sessions according to comprehensive reviews. The American Society for Apheresis (ASFA) reports highlight that severe complications are rare, affecting less than 2% of cases, while minor issues predominate in both therapeutic and donation contexts.
Vascular access complications arise primarily from peripheral or central venous catheters used for blood draw and return. Bleeding or hematoma formation occurs in approximately 1-2% of peripheral accesses, often due to needle trauma or inadequate compression post-procedure. Infections at the access site are rare (0.1-0.4%), with higher risks in prolonged central line use. Pneumothorax, a rare but serious issue from central line insertion, has an incidence of less than 1%, particularly in subclavian approaches.
Hypovolemia and hypotension are frequent due to rapid volume shifts during plasma removal and replacement, impacting 0.4-15% of sessions overall and up to 33% in vulnerable patients such as those with neuroimmunological diseases. Citrate anticoagulation, used to prevent clotting, commonly induces hypocalcemia, leading to paresthesia or tingling in 10-20% of procedures, with symptoms resolving upon calcium supplementation. Hypotension specifically occurs in 0.4-15% of exchanges, exacerbated by inadequate fluid replacement or vasovagal responses.
Electrolyte imbalances, including hypokalemia and hypocalcemia, result from dilution with replacement fluids like albumin or saline. Albumin replacement can cause approximately 25% reduction in serum potassium concentration, potentially leading to hypokalemia and causing muscle weakness or arrhythmias if severe. Hypocalcemia is the most prevalent, affecting 9% with albumin replacement and 20% with cryoprecipitate-poor plasma, often manifesting as perioral numbness or carpopedal spasm. Allergic reactions to replacement fluids, such as rash from albumin, occur in less than 5% of cases.
Hematologic complications include anemia from repeated plasma draws in donation settings or dilutional effects in therapy, with hematocrit reductions of about 10% per session. Coagulopathy arises from dilution of clotting factors, leading to prolonged prothrombin times, though clinical bleeding is rare at 0.06%. Thrombotic events, conversely, affect 0.06-0.14% due to factor imbalances. Incidence rates are higher in pediatric or critically ill patients, reaching 15% overall per ASFA analyses. Mortality directly attributable to TPE is rare (0.03%-0.05%), usually due to underlying conditions.

Prevention and Management Strategies

To prevent complications related to vascular access during plasmapheresis, ultrasound-guided insertion of central venous catheters is recommended to improve accuracy and reduce risks such as hematoma or pneumothorax, while strict adherence to sterile techniques, including maximal barrier precautions, minimizes infection rates. For low-risk patients, alternatives like large-bore peripheral venous access can be employed to avoid central line placement, thereby decreasing the incidence of catheter-related bloodstream infections.
Citrate toxicity, a common issue due to anticoagulation, is managed through prophylactic intravenous calcium gluconate infusion, typically 1-2 g administered prior to or at the start of the session, with continuous infusion adjusted based on symptoms. Ionized calcium levels should be monitored throughout the procedure to maintain concentrations above 1.0 mmol/L, preventing symptoms such as paresthesia or arrhythmias.
For volume support, pre-hydration with 500 mL of normal saline is standard to maintain hemodynamic stability and counteract potential hypovolemia from plasma removal. Return rates of replacement fluid should be kept below 100 mL/min to avoid rapid shifts that could precipitate hypotension, and vasopressors such as norepinephrine may be initiated if blood pressure drops significantly despite fluid resuscitation.
Monitoring protocols include continuous electrocardiography (ECG) to detect arrhythmias induced by electrolyte imbalances or citrate effects, with laboratory assessments of electrolytes, including calcium and magnesium, performed every 2 hours during the session. Post-session observation for 4-6 hours is essential to identify delayed complications like hypotension or bleeding, allowing for timely intervention.
In long-term management for patients undergoing frequent plasmapheresis sessions, dose adjustmentssuch as reducing plasma volume exchanged per session based on total body water and clinical responseare implemented to mitigate cumulative risks like chronic hypocalcemia. Patient education emphasizes recognition of symptoms, such as perioral tingling or muscle cramps indicative of hypocalcemia, prompting immediate reporting and potential oral calcium supplementation between sessions.

Historical Development

Early Innovations

The origins of plasmapheresis trace back to 1914, when American pharmacologist John Jacob Abel, along with colleagues Leonard G. Rowntree and Arthur H. Turner, developed the "vividiffusion" apparatus, an early device for separating and purifying blood components in living animals. This invention, often regarded as the first artificial kidney, employed semipermeable collodion tubes to dialyze blood extracorporeally, removing diffusible waste products like urea while returning purified blood to the animal via a continuous circuit. Although primarily aimed at dialysis for renal failure models, the apparatus introduced foundational concepts of plasma separation by exploiting differences in molecular permeability, laying groundwork for later apheresis techniques.
The impetus for advancing plasma collection and fractionation accelerated during World War II in the 1940s, driven by military needs for stable blood products to treat battlefield casualties. Biochemist Edwin J. Cohn at Harvard University led efforts commissioned by the U.S. military to fractionate human plasma using cold ethanol precipitation, successfully isolating key components such as albumin for volume expansion and gamma globulin for immune support. This process enabled the production of dried plasma powders and solutions that could be stored without refrigeration, revolutionizing transfusion medicine by providing safer alternatives to whole blood for shock treatment. Cohn's methods, refined through systematic solubility manipulations, yielded fractions including fibrinogen and prothrombin, which were critical for hemostasis in wounded soldiers.
Parallel to these fractionation advances, the American Red Cross established the first nationwide blood donor service in the early 1940s to supply plasma and blood products for military transfusions, collecting millions of units under programs like "Plasma for Britain" and later direct U.S. Armed Forces support. Directed by figures such as Charles R. Drew, these initiatives focused on sourcing plasma from civilian donors for processing into dried forms, emphasizing scalability and rapid distribution to combat zones where whole blood was logistically challenging. By 1941, the Red Cross had set up donor centers and mobile units across the U.S., prioritizing plasma due to its longer shelf life and lower risk of incompatibility reactions compared to cellular components.
In the 1950s, plasmapheresis transitioned from experimental and donor-focused applications to initial therapeutic uses in humans, particularly for treating hyperproteinemia associated with conditions like multiple myeloma. Pioneering work by William S. Adams and colleagues demonstrated the procedure's efficacy in reducing plasma viscosity through selective plasma removal and red cell reinfusion, marking the first documented clinical applications to alleviate symptoms of hyperviscosity syndrome. Concurrently, the introduction of disposable plastic bags, pioneered by surgeon Carl W. Walter using plasticized polyvinyl chloride (PVC), transformed collection practices by replacing fragile glass bottles, enabling safer, sterile handling and easier separation of plasma from whole blood. These bags facilitated closed-system processing, reducing breakage risks during transport and storage.
Early plasmapheresis faced significant challenges, including reliance on manual centrifugation for plasma separation, which was labor-intensive and prone to inconsistencies in yield and purity. Open-system techniques heightened contamination risks from bacterial ingress or air exposure, limiting procedures to controlled research settings rather than routine clinical or large-scale donation use until automated systems emerged. These hurdles underscored the need for sterile, efficient methods to mitigate procedural errors and ensure product safety.

Modern Advancements and Evolution

The 1970s marked a pivotal shift in plasmapheresis technology with the introduction of continuous-flow apheresis machines, which improved efficiency and safety over manual methods by allowing simultaneous blood withdrawal and return. A key example was the Haemonetics Model 30, commercially available by 1976, which facilitated automated plasma separation and collection, enabling larger volumes to be processed in shorter times. This advancement laid the groundwork for broader therapeutic applications, as it reduced procedural risks and operator dependency.
In the 1980s, the American Society for Apheresis (ASFA) published its first guidelines in 1986, standardizing therapeutic plasma exchange protocols and categorizing indications based on evidence, which helped integrate the procedure into clinical practice worldwide. During the 1980s and 1990s, membrane filtration emerged as an alternative to centrifugation, offering comparable efficacy in plasma separation while being less mechanically complex and more suitable for certain settings; studies from the era demonstrated similar removal rates of plasma components without significant differences in patient outcomes. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved automated systems for plasma donation in the late 1970s and expanded approvals through the 1980s, enhancing collection safety and yield for both therapeutic and donation purposes. Concurrently, clinical trials in the 1980s solidified thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP) as a primary indication, with empirical use of plasma exchange showing dramatic survival improvements, culminating in the landmark 1991 Canadian Apheresis Group trial confirming its efficacy over plasma infusion.
The 2000s saw innovations in plasma safety through pathogen reduction technologies, such as the INTERCEPT Blood System, which received CE marking in Europe in 2006 for inactivating viruses and bacteria in collected plasma via amotosalen and UV light, reducing transfusion-transmitted infection risks without compromising therapeutic utility. Efforts to expand neurological applications included trials like those exploring plasma exchange for acute ischemic stroke in the 1990s, which initially showed promise in reducing viscosity but were later disproven in larger studies demonstrating no significant benefit over standard care.
From the 2010s onward, plasmapheresis evolved with portable devices, such as acoustofluidic and self-pressure-driven systems, enabling point-of-care applications in resource-limited or emergency settings by simplifying separation without heavy centrifugation equipment. Integration of artificial intelligence for real-time monitoring has further refined procedures, with AI algorithms optimizing flow rates, predicting complications, and personalizing protocols based on patient data to enhance safety and efficiency. During pandemics, convalescent plasma gained attention; 2014 trials for Ebola virus disease in West Africa, including the Ebola-Tx study, evaluated its use but yielded inconclusive results on mortality reduction due to logistical challenges and small sample sizes. Similarly, COVID-19 trials from 2020 to 2022, such as those by the RECOVERY and ACTIV-3 groups, reported mixed outcomes, with early observational benefits not consistently replicated in randomized controlled trials, leading to limited endorsement for severe cases. The ASFA updated its guidelines in 2023, refining categories for over 80 diseases with new evidence-based fact sheets to reflect these evolving applications.
Globally, accessibility has improved in developing countries through World Health Organization (WHO) initiatives like the Achilles Project, launched in the 2010s, which supports local plasma collection centers to meet international standards, enabling fractionation into therapeutics and reducing reliance on imports.
