Hayward Gallery
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Hayward Gallery is a renowned contemporary art gallery located within the Southbank Centre on London's South Bank, specializing in temporary exhibitions of innovative and international visual art without a permanent collection.
Opened on 9 July 1968 by Queen Elizabeth II, the gallery was named in honor of Sir Isaac Hayward, the former leader of the London County Council who championed its development as part of the post-war cultural regeneration of the area. Designed in the Brutalist architectural style by a team led by Norman Engleback under the Greater London Council's architecture department, its raw concrete structure and elevated walkways have made it a landmark of 1960s modernism, though it underwent significant refurbishment between 2016 and 2018 to modernize facilities while preserving its iconic form.
Since its inception, the Hayward has hosted 34 major exhibitions annually, focusing on bold, boundary-pushing works by contemporary artists from around the world, including seminal shows such as Bridget Riley's 1971 retrospective, "The Other Story" in 19891990 highlighting artists from Africa, Asia, and the Caribbean, and Antony Gormley's immersive "Blind Light" in 2007. Complementing its main program, Hayward Gallery Touring the UK's largest contemporary art touring organizationproduces and circulates exhibitions to over 45 venues annually, reaching more than half a million visitors and broadening access to cutting-edge art beyond London.

Overview

Description

The Hayward Gallery is a public gallery dedicated to contemporary visual art exhibitions, presenting the work of adventurous and influential international artists from around the world. As part of the Southbank Centre, it serves as a prominent venue in London's cultural landscape, focusing exclusively on temporary exhibitions without maintaining a permanent collection.
The gallery's year-round programme highlights diverse and boundary-pushing works across mediums such as painting, sculpture, installation, and multimedia, fostering innovative encounters with contemporary art. It comprises upper and lower galleries with multiple rooms, providing flexible environments for immersive displays.
Opened in 1968 as a landmark of Brutalist architecture, the Hayward Gallery continues to play a central role in shaping London's vibrant art scene through its commitment to bold, international programming.

Location and Facilities

The Hayward Gallery is situated on the South Bank of the River Thames in central London, at the address Southbank Centre, Belvedere Road, London SE1 8XX. It is conveniently adjacent to Waterloo station, approximately 200 meters away, and Embankment station, about 600 meters distant, providing easy access via the Northern, Bakerloo, Jubilee, District, and Circle lines.
As a core component of the Southbank Centre complex, the gallery integrates seamlessly with neighboring venues such as the Queen Elizabeth Hall and Purcell Room, forming a major hub for arts and culture on the Thames waterfront. Visitor facilities include a dedicated cafe serving sandwiches, salads, cakes, and coffee for pre- or post-visit refreshments, as well as a shop offering exhibition merchandise, books, artist-inspired items like t-shirts and tote bags, and concrete design pieces. Accessibility features encompass step-free access through ramps and lifts (with alternative step-free routes available via the adjacent Royal Festival Hall if the gallery lift is unavailable), accessible toilets in the foyer, and audio guides for exhibitions accessible via SoundCloud. Public spaces within the complex include the Queen Elizabeth Hall Roof Garden, a terrace area with views across London, providing an outdoor haven for visitors.
The gallery operates from Tuesday to Friday 10am to 6pm, Saturday 10am to 8pm, and Sunday 10am to 6pm, remaining closed on Mondays. Admission is ticketed for major exhibitions, while some events and installations are free; Southbank Centre members enjoy unlimited free entry to all Hayward Gallery exhibitions throughout the year.

History

Founding and Construction

The Hayward Gallery originated in the early 1960s as a key component of the London County Council's (LCC) ambitious redevelopment of the South Bank cultural precinct, building on the momentum from the 1951 Festival of Britain, which had established the area as a hub for public arts and entertainment. This project aimed to expand the Royal Festival Hall the Festival's enduring legacyinto a comprehensive arts centre, reflecting post-war Britain's commitment to accessible cultural infrastructure through public investment.
In December 1967, the gallery was named in honour of Sir Isaac Hayward (18841976), the former LCC leader whose advocacy for arts and urban renewal had been instrumental in advancing such initiatives during the 1950s and 1960s. The design phase, led by LCC architect Norman Engleback, began in earnest around 19601962, with significant contributions from Ron Herron and Warren Chalk of the avant-garde Archigram group, alongside other team members including John Attenborough and Alan Waterhouse. Proposals for the South Bank Arts Centre had circulated since 1955, but the 1960 LCC decision formalized the integration of the gallery with the Queen Elizabeth Hall and Purcell Room under the oversight of GLC Architect Hubert Bennett.
Construction commenced in 1963, undertaken by contractors Higgs and Hill, as part of the broader public-funded effort to create London's first major purpose-built venue for modern art exhibitions. Financed through LCC and later Greater London Council resources, the project embodied the era's emphasis on state-sponsored cultural development in the wake of World War II reconstruction, though it unfolded against growing public and critical discourse on the merits of large-scale modernist public works. The gallery reached completion in 1968, marking the culmination of this five-year build within the Southbank Centre complex.

Opening and Early Years

The Hayward Gallery officially opened on July 9, 1968, when Queen Elizabeth II inaugurated the venue as part of the Southbank Centre's cultural complex, with public access beginning two days later on July 11. The inaugural exhibition, "Henri Matisse 1869-1954: A Retrospective," showcased over 150 works by the French artist, including paintings, sculptures, and drawings, marking the gallery's debut as a space for major international retrospectives.
Under the curatorial leadership of Joanna Drew, who served as the Arts Council's exhibitions director and oversaw the gallery's early programming from its inception, the Hayward quickly established itself as a hub for international contemporary art, prioritizing bold, innovative shows that engaged global artistic currents. Drew's tenure emphasized ambitious surveys and retrospectives, such as the 1970 "Kinetics" exhibition, which featured dynamic works by artists including Jean Tinguely and Bridget Riley, exploring movement and optical effects in a major international showcase. This focus continued with the gallery's first full-scale retrospective for a contemporary painter, Bridget Riley's "Paintings and Drawings 195171" in 1971, which highlighted her op art innovations and drew significant attention to British modernism within an international context.
Through the 1970s and 1980s, the gallery's programming evolved to address underrepresented voices, culminating in the landmark 1989 exhibition "The Other Story: Afro-Asian Artists in Post-War Britain," curated by Rasheed Araeen, which presented works by over 20 artists of African, Asian, and Caribbean descent to illuminate their contributions to British modernism. Despite these artistic successes, the Hayward faced challenges, including public criticism of its Brutalist architecture, often derided in the 1970s as an eyesore amid broader backlash against concrete-heavy post-war designs. Funding constraints, tied to the Arts Council's broader budgetary pressures during economic downturns, also limited operational flexibility, though the gallery persisted in delivering influential shows that shaped contemporary art discourse.

Architecture

Design and Brutalist Features

The Hayward Gallery's original design embodies the hallmarks of Brutalist architecture, characterized by its extensive use of exposed bton brut concrete, raw and monolithic massing, and a functionalist layout that prioritizes internal spatial efficiency. Constructed between 1963 and 1968 under the leadership of architect Norman Engleback from the Greater London Council's Department of Architecture, the building features board-marked concrete surfaces created using Baltic pine moulds, which highlight the material's texture and craftsmanship, alongside precast external panels incorporating crushed Cornish granite for durability and visual interest. The galleries are stacked vertically and connected by escalators, forming a modular system that allows for adaptable exhibition spaces without fixed partitions, emphasizing the style's emphasis on honest expression of structure and utility over ornamental decoration.
Key design elements include an asymmetrical form composed of cantilevered cubic volumes and precipitous terraces that generate dynamic vistas both internally and externally. The roof incorporates approximately 60 steel-and-glass pyramids to diffuse natural light evenly across the exhibition halls, ensuring flexibility for diverse installations while minimizing glare. This asymmetrical massing, elevated on a high podium, separates pedestrian circulation from vehicular traffic and weaves the gallery seamlessly into the Southbank Centre's urban fabric, creating sheltered walkways and visual connections to the River Thames and surrounding cultural venues.
The design drew influences from the avant-garde Archigram group, particularly through contributions from members Ron Herron and Warren Chalk, who infused the podium and circulation spaces with futuristic, plug-in modularity concepts that anticipated adaptable, technology-driven environments. This emphasis on flexibility extended to the gallery's core function, enabling large-scale contemporary art installations that could transform the raw concrete interiorsfeaturing crystalline white Macedonian marble floors and cast anodized doorsinto immersive, site-specific experiences.
Critically, the Hayward Gallery has been praised for its bold sculptural presence and innovative spatial drama, often described as a "totemic example of British Brutalism" that revolutionized public art spaces through its rebellious, inward-focused geometry. However, it has faced criticism for its perceived austerity and lack of accessibility, with early detractors in 1967 voting it "Britains ugliest building" due to the stark concrete aesthetic and challenging site integration compared to more elegant contemporaries like the Royal Festival Hall. Despite heritage campaigns highlighting its architectural significance, the building was denied listed status multiple times in the 2010s, including rejections in 2010 and 2018, with authorities citing insufficient uniqueness and coherence, culminating in a Certificate of Immunity from Listing issued in 2020 (valid until February 2025). Following its expiry, heritage groups including the Twentieth Century Society renewed calls for listing in February 2025.

Renovations and Adaptations

The Hayward Gallery underwent a major renovation project from 2015 to 2018, led by Feilden Clegg Bradley Studios as part of the broader Southbank Centre Festival Wing refurbishment. This 35 million initiative focused on conserving the building's structure while enhancing its operational capabilities, with funding primarily from Arts Council England (16.7 million grant), the Heritage Lottery Fund (4.9 million), and additional contributions from trusts, foundations, individuals, and Southbank Centre supporters.
Key upgrades addressed longstanding functional limitations, including the installation of a new roof and building envelope to improve insulation and thermal performance, reducing heat loss in the concrete structure. The project restored the gallery's 66 original pyramid rooflights, introducing controllable natural daylight into the upper-level galleries for the first time since opening, which transformed previously dim exhibition spaces. Additionally, the HVAC systems were completely overhauled with new climate control and building services to ensure stable environmental conditions for artworks, while accessibility was enhanced through better provisions for disabled audiences and artists, such as improved ramps and lifts. The brutalist exterior was preserved and cleaned, maintaining its raw concrete aesthetic, while interiors were modernized with replaced terrazzo floors and updated technical infrastructure.
These adaptations emphasized flexible reuse for contemporary art practices, incorporating digital infrastructure to support installations involving media and technology, thereby accommodating evolving exhibition needs without altering the building's core spatial logic. The renovations achieved a 42% reduction in electricity use through efficient new systems and insulation, significantly boosting sustainability. The gallery reopened on January 25, 2018, coinciding with its 50th anniversary, and launched with the first major UK retrospective of photographer Andreas Gursky, showcasing the revitalized spaces.

Exhibitions

Notable Past Exhibitions

The Hayward Gallery has hosted numerous notable exhibitions since its opening, including early retrospectives such as Bridget Riley's in 1971 and "The Other Story" in 19891990, which highlighted artists from Africa, Asia, and the Caribbean.
In the 1990s, the Hayward Gallery presented "Spellbound: Art and Film," a collaborative exhibition with the British Film Institute that examined the interplay between visual art and cinema in Britain from 1900 onward, featuring installations by artists such as Damien Hirst and films by directors like Peter Greenaway. This show highlighted cinema's influence on artistic practices, blending multimedia elements to explore narrative and visual storytelling, and drew significant attention for its innovative curation of historical and contemporary works.
The gallery's 1998 solo exhibition of Anish Kapoor marked his first major public showing in the UK, showcasing over 20 large-scale sculptures from the 1990s that engaged with the venue's brutalist architecture through voids, pigments, and reflective forms. Kapoor's works, such as towering installations that distorted space and perception, emphasized themes of absence and infinity, transforming the gallery into an immersive environment that challenged viewers' sensory experiences.
Antony Gormley's 2007 exhibition "Blind Light" introduced site-specific installations, including the fog-filled glass room of the same name and the urban intervention "Event Horizon," where life-size cast-iron figures were placed on rooftops around London's South Bank. These pieces explored human isolation, visibility, and the body's relation to environment, using the gallery's architecture to provoke reflections on public space and individual presence in the city.
Marking the Hayward's 40th anniversary in 2008, "Psycho Buildings: Artists Take on Architecture" was a group show curated by Ralph Rugoff, featuring 18 international artists who reimagined built environments through surreal and dysfunctional structures, such as Do-Ho Suh's fabric house and Mike Nelson's labyrinthine installations. The exhibition delved into architecture's psychological dimensions, critiquing modernity and domesticity while underscoring the gallery's role as a unique venue for spatial experimentation.
Throughout the 1990s and 2000s, Hayward exhibitions increasingly addressed global contemporary issues, including identity through explorations of cultural hybridity and personal narrative, technology via multimedia integrations like film and digital projections, and the environment by questioning human impact on built and natural spaces. Curatorial approaches shifted toward amplifying diverse voices, incorporating feminist perspectives on gender and power in shows that highlighted women artists' reconfigurations of space and body, alongside early engagements with postcolonial themes that prefigured broader inclusivity in contemporary art discourse.

Touring and Outreach Programmes

The Hayward Gallery Touring programme, active since 1946 as part of the Southbank Centre, organizes contemporary art exhibitions that travel to galleries, museums, and other publicly funded venues across the UK, extending access to Hayward's curatorial vision beyond London.
A flagship initiative is the British Art Show, the largest touring survey of contemporary art in the UK, which has highlighted emerging trends and artists every five years since its inception. The ninth edition, curated by Irene Aristizbal and Hammad Nasar, toured to four citiesAberdeen Art Gallery, Wolverhampton Art Gallery, The Box Plymouth, and Manchester (including the Whitworth Art Gallery)from 2021 to 2023, featuring over 40 artists exploring themes of healing, togetherness, and future imaginings in response to social upheavals like the COVID-19 pandemic and racial justice movements. In August 2025, the curator Ekow Eshun and venues for the landmark 10th edition were announced, with the exhibition scheduled to open in Coventry in September 2026 and tour to Swansea, Bristol, Sheffield, and Newcastle Gateshead through 2027.
Outreach efforts complement these loans through community-engaged activities, including year-round workshops for children, families, young people, and adults that foster creativity and artist development, as well as tailored school programmes offering classroom resources, teacher training, and gallery visits to inspire student engagement with global artists. These initiatives often partner with local and national institutions to co-create events, prioritizing inclusivity by amplifying underrepresented voices, such as women artists and those from marginalized regions.
The touring programme has reached over 2.3 million people through the British Art Show alone across its first nine editions, while broader Hayward Gallery Touring projects engage more than 500,000 visitors annually in over 45 UK cities and towns, broadening cultural access in underserved areas.
A recent example is After the End of History: British Working Class Photography 19892024, curated by Johny Pitts, which tours from 2024 to 2025 across venues including Herbert Art Gallery & Museum in Coventry, Focal Point Gallery in Southend, Bonington Gallery in Nottingham, Stills in Edinburgh, and Ffotogallery in Cardiff, showcasing works by artists like Richard Billingham and Elaine Constantine to reframe narratives of working-class identity and diversity.

Recent Developments

2018 Renovation Impacts

The 2018 renovation of the Hayward Gallery significantly enhanced its operational capabilities, allowing for greater visitor throughput and improved environmental performance. The 35 million project, which included upgrades to ventilation, access, and production infrastructure, resulted in a 21% increase in overall site footfall at the Southbank Centre in the year following reopening. This boosted the gallery's capacity to handle larger audiences, contributing to the Southbank Centre's total of 4.45 million visits in 2018. Additionally, the renovation incorporated sustainable practices such as the use of recycled materials and low-VOC water-based paints, aligning with broader environmental goals like reducing carbon emissions and achieving zero-to-landfill status across the Southbank Centre. A new climate control system further supported energy-efficient operations while preserving the building's integrity.
Culturally, the renovation revitalized the Hayward as a key Brutalist landmark, enhancing the Southbank's appeal as a vibrant arts destination in London. By restoring the gallery's original pyramid-shaped rooflights and introducing natural daylight to the upper galleries for the first time since the 1960s, the project celebrated the building's architectural heritage while adapting it for contemporary use. The reopening on January 25, 2018, coincided with a major retrospective of Andreas Gursky's large-scale photographs, which effectively tied the artist's expansive works to the gallery's renewed spatial drama, underscoring the interplay between art and architecture. This not only marked the Hayward's 50th anniversary but also reinforced its role in promoting innovative visual arts programming.
Public and critical reception to the renovated gallery was overwhelmingly positive, praising its successful fusion of historical Brutalist elements with modern functionality. Critics noted how the refurbishment transformed the once-criticized "despised" structureoften derided for its stark, fortress-like appearance and poor suitability for art displayinto a "brutal beauty" that now offers generous, light-filled spaces ideal for immersive experiences. Reviews highlighted the project's sensitivity in cleaning concrete surfaces and polishing terrazzo floors without compromising the original design, effectively addressing longstanding complaints about the building's aesthetics and usability. This blend of preservation and innovation positioned the Hayward as a model for adaptive reuse in London's cultural landscape.
In the long term, the renovation has enabled the Hayward to host more ambitious and immersive exhibitions, expanding curatorial possibilities through enhanced technical capabilities and flexible spaces. The upgrades have supported a diverse program of shows, from site-specific installations to international retrospectives, fostering deeper visitor engagement and artistic experimentation. By strengthening the gallery's infrastructure and economic viabilitygenerating 30 million in gross value added for Londonthe project has bolstered the Southbank Centre's resilience as a cultural anchor amid broader challenges facing the UK's arts sector. This has helped sustain the Hayward's influence in promoting artistic excellence and community wellbeing, with its touring program alone reaching 197,000 visitors annually post-renovation.

Current and Upcoming Exhibitions

Following the 2018 reopening after extensive renovations, the Hayward Gallery presented "Space Shifters," a thematic exhibition exploring how contemporary artists manipulate perception of space and scale through immersive installations by creators such as Anish Kapoor, Yayoi Kusama, and Jeppe Hein, running from September 26, 2018, to January 6, 2019. This show marked a bold return to programming that emphasized sensory and architectural engagement, drawing over 100,000 visitors and setting a tone for innovative, site-specific works.
In 2020, amid the COVID-19 pandemic, the gallery adapted its exhibition "Among the Trees" to a one-way route with reduced capacity, featuring 37 international artists including Steve McQueen, whose photograph Lynching Tree (2013) highlighted trees as witnesses to historical violence, reopened on August 1 and on view until October 31, 2020. The installation underscored ecological and social interconnections, with McQueen's contribution evoking the brutal legacy of lynching in the American South, while adaptations like timed tickets ensured safe access during lockdowns.
The 2020s have seen a surge in solo and group shows emphasizing diverse voices and material innovation. In 2023, the group exhibition "Dear Earth: Art and Hope in a Time of Crisis" brought together 15 international artists, including Otobong Nkanga and Cornelia Parker, to explore environmental care through multimedia works like sculptures and videos addressing climate urgency, from June 21 to September 3, 2023. Haegue Yang's "Leap Year," the artist's first major UK survey, showcased over 40 works including blind sculptures and performative objects blending everyday materials with abstract forms, running October 9, 2024, to January 5, 2025. This was followed by Linder's "Danger Came Smiling," a retrospective of the British artist's provocative collages and feminist performances spanning punk-era graphics to recent animations, from November 11, 2024, to May 5, 2025.
In 2025, the gallery hosted Mickalene Thomas's "All About Love," the artist's first UK solo exhibition featuring large-scale rhinestone-encrusted paintings and collages celebrating Black female empowerment and queer intimacy, on view February 11 to May 5, 2025. Yoshitomo Nara's major retrospective presented over 150 paintings, sculptures, and drawings of rebellious child figures, exploring themes of isolation and defiance, from June 10 to August 31, 2025. As of November 2025, "Gilbert & George: 21st Century Pictures" is ongoing, displaying monumental photo-based works from their recent series that critique urban life and human folly through satirical imagery, running October 7, 2025, to January 11, 2026, alongside Val Lee's "The Presence of Solitude," a meditative installation of paintings and sculptures evoking quiet introspection.
Looking ahead, a dual exhibition by Chiharu Shiota and Yin Xiuzhen opens February 17, 2026, transforming gallery spaces with immersive thread-based and textile installations that delve into memory, migration, and cultural diasporaShiota's web-like sculptures and Xiuzhen's found-fabric assemblages highlighting personal and global narratives. This aligns with ongoing trends toward inclusivity, as seen in 2025 shows by Thomas (love and identity), Linder (gender and danger), and Gilbert & George (contemporary urban existence), alongside a continued emphasis on Afrofuturism and global perspectives in programming like the touring extensions of recent group shows.
