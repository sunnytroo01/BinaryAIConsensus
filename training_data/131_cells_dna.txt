Cells are the fundamental units of life. Every living organism on Earth, from the simplest bacterium to the most complex human being, is composed of one or more cells. A single human body contains approximately thirty-seven trillion cells, each one a miniature factory that takes in nutrients, converts them to energy, carries out specialized functions, and reproduces itself. The cell is the smallest unit of matter that can be considered alive, and understanding how cells work is the foundation of all biology, medicine, and biotechnology.

There are two fundamental types of cells. Prokaryotic cells, found in bacteria and archaea, are small, simple cells that lack a nucleus. Their DNA floats freely in the cytoplasm, the gel-like substance that fills the cell. Prokaryotes were the first life forms on Earth, appearing approximately three and a half billion years ago, and they remain the most abundant organisms on the planet. Eukaryotic cells, found in plants, animals, fungi, and protists, are larger and more complex. They have a nucleus, a membrane-bound compartment that contains the cell's DNA, and a variety of other membrane-bound organelles, specialized structures that perform specific functions within the cell.

The nucleus is the control center of the eukaryotic cell. It contains the cell's DNA, organized into structures called chromosomes. Human cells contain forty-six chromosomes, arranged in twenty-three pairs. The DNA within these chromosomes contains approximately twenty thousand genes, sequences of DNA that encode the instructions for building proteins. Proteins are the workhorses of the cell: they form structural components, catalyze chemical reactions as enzymes, transport molecules, send and receive signals, and defend against pathogens.

DNA, deoxyribonucleic acid, is the molecule that stores the genetic information of all living organisms. Its structure, discovered by James Watson and Francis Crick in 1953 with critical contributions from Rosalind Franklin and Maurice Wilkins, is a double helix: two strands twisted around each other like a spiral staircase, connected by pairs of chemical bases. There are four bases: adenine, which always pairs with thymine, and cytosine, which always pairs with guanine. The sequence of these bases along the DNA strand constitutes the genetic code, a four-letter alphabet that spells out the instructions for building every protein the organism needs.

The process by which DNA's information is used to build proteins is called gene expression. First, the relevant section of DNA is copied into a molecule called messenger RNA in a process called transcription. The messenger RNA then travels from the nucleus to structures called ribosomes in the cytoplasm, where the RNA's sequence is read and used to assemble a chain of amino acids in a process called translation. The chain of amino acids folds into a three-dimensional shape that determines the protein's function. This flow of information, from DNA to RNA to protein, is called the central dogma of molecular biology.

When a cell divides, it must copy its entire DNA so that each daughter cell receives a complete set of genetic instructions. This process, called DNA replication, is remarkably accurate: the cellular machinery copies approximately three billion base pairs with an error rate of only about one mistake per billion bases. When errors do occur, they are called mutations. Most mutations are harmless or are corrected by repair mechanisms. Some mutations are harmful and can cause disease. And rarely, a mutation provides a beneficial change that gives the organism a survival advantage, which is the raw material for evolution by natural selection.

Mitochondria are the powerhouses of the cell. These organelles convert the energy stored in glucose and other nutrients into ATP, adenosine triphosphate, the molecule that provides energy for virtually all cellular processes. A single cell can contain hundreds or thousands of mitochondria, depending on its energy needs. Muscle cells and brain cells, which have high energy demands, are particularly rich in mitochondria. Intriguingly, mitochondria have their own DNA, separate from the nuclear DNA, which supports the theory that mitochondria were originally free-living bacteria that were engulfed by an ancestral eukaryotic cell approximately two billion years ago in a process called endosymbiosis. This merger was one of the most consequential events in the history of life, enabling the evolution of complex, multicellular organisms.

Stem cells are undifferentiated cells that have the ability to develop into many different cell types. Embryonic stem cells, found in early-stage embryos, can become any cell type in the body, a property called pluripotency. Adult stem cells, found in various tissues including bone marrow, the brain, and the skin, are more limited in their differentiation potential but play a crucial role in tissue repair and regeneration throughout life. The study of stem cells holds enormous promise for regenerative medicine, offering the potential to grow replacement tissues and organs, repair damaged hearts, reverse paralysis, and treat currently incurable diseases.

Cancer is a disease of uncontrolled cell division. Normal cells divide in a regulated manner, responding to signals that tell them when to grow and when to stop. Cancer cells have acquired mutations that override these controls, allowing them to divide without restraint, invade surrounding tissues, and spread to distant parts of the body through the blood and lymphatic systems in a process called metastasis. Cancer is not a single disease but a collection of more than two hundred different diseases, each characterized by specific mutations in specific cell types. Modern cancer research is increasingly focused on understanding the specific genetic mutations that drive each type of cancer and developing targeted therapies that attack cancer cells while sparing normal ones.

The cell is the bridge between chemistry and life. Below the cell, there are only molecules: DNA, proteins, lipids, carbohydrates, and water, none of which are alive on their own. Above the cell, there are tissues, organs, organisms, and ecosystems, all of which are alive because their constituent cells are alive. The cell is where the inanimate becomes animate, where chemistry becomes biology, and where the extraordinary property we call life emerges from the ordinary properties of matter. Understanding the cell is understanding the fundamental mechanism by which the universe becomes aware of itself.
