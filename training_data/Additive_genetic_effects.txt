Additive genetic effects
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Additive genetic effects refer to the linear and independent contributions of individual alleles to an individual's phenotypic value, where the effects of alleles at different loci sum cumulatively without interactions. The additive genetic value of an individual, known as the breeding value, is defined as the sum of the average effects of all the alleles it carries, which is transmitted to offspring through Mendelian inheritance. This value represents the expected deviation of an individual's progeny from the population mean, assuming random mating.
At the population level, additive genetic effects manifest as additive genetic variance (V_A), which quantifies the variance in breeding values among individuals and arises from the segregation of alleles that influence quantitative traits. V_A is a key component of total phenotypic variance (V_P), alongside environmental variance (V_E) and non-additive genetic components like dominance (V_D) and epistasis (V_I), such that V_P = V_A + V_D + V_I + V_E. Unlike non-additive effects, which involve allele interactions within or across loci, additive effects assume no such dependencies and are modeled as the sum of allele substitution effects.
The importance of additive genetic effects lies in their role in narrow-sense heritability (h = V_A / V_P), which measures the proportion of phenotypic variance due to additive genetic variance and directly predicts a population's short-term response to natural or artificial selection (R = h S, where S is the selection differential). Empirical studies across diverse traits and species consistently show that V_A accounts for over half of the total genetic variance in many cases, underscoring its dominance in evolutionary potential and breeding programs. For instance, in behavioral traits like extraversion, additive effects explain 20-23% of variation, often interacting with environmental factors but remaining heritable across generations. This framework is foundational in quantitative genetics for understanding adaptation and trait evolution.

Fundamentals

Definition

Additive genetic effects represent the portion of phenotypic variation attributable to the independent, linear contributions of individual alleles at different loci, without considering interactions such as dominance or epistasis. These effects are modeled as the cumulative sum of small, additive increments from each allele, allowing quantitative traits to exhibit continuous variation in populations. This conceptualization underpins the analysis of inheritance for complex traits influenced by many genes of minor effect each.
The additive effect of an allele is quantified as the average change in phenotype resulting from substituting one allele for another, averaged across all possible genetic backgrounds in the populationa measure known as the average effect of allelic substitution. For instance, in a diploid locus with alleles A and a, where the homozygote AA has a genotypic value of +2, heterozygote Aa has +1, and homozygote aa has 0 (assuming no dominance), the additive effect of allele A is +1, calculated as half the difference between the homozygote values: (2 - 0)/2 = 1. This substitution effect reflects the allele's marginal contribution to the trait, independent of specific combinations with other alleles.
This framework was pioneered by Ronald A. Fisher in his seminal 1918 work on reconciling Mendelian inheritance with continuous variation in quantitative traits, where he introduced the notion of average effects to explain correlations between relatives under polygenic control. Fisher's approach emphasized that, for traits governed by numerous loci, the heritable component arises primarily from these additive allelic effects, forming the foundation for estimating evolutionary responses to selection. In one sentence, these effects are central to narrow-sense heritability, which quantifies the transmissible genetic variance relative to total phenotypic variance.

Additive versus Non-Additive Effects

Non-additive genetic effects encompass interactions that deviate from the simple summation of individual allelic contributions to the phenotype, primarily including dominance and epistasis. Dominance refers to the interaction between alleles at the same locus, where the heterozygote phenotype does not represent the average of the two homozygotes. Epistasis involves interactions between alleles at different loci, where the effect of one locus on the phenotype depends on the genotype at another locus.
A classic example of dominance is observed in a single locus with alleles A and a, where the heterozygote Aa exhibits a phenotype that deviates from the mid-parent value (the average of the AA and aa homozygotes), known as the dominance deviation. For epistasis, consider a scenario in plant breeding where the effect of a gene influencing flower color at one locus is masked or enhanced by the genotype at a second locus, altering pigmentation outcomes beyond what additive effects alone would predict.
The key distinction between additive and non-additive effects lies in their predictability and transmission: additive effects are cumulative and transmitted independently to offspring, allowing reliable prediction of progeny phenotypes based on parental breeding values, whereas non-additive effects, such as dominance and epistasis, rely on specific allelic combinations that are disrupted during sexual reproduction and thus not independently heritable across generations. This makes additive effects the foundation for narrow-sense heritability estimates in breeding programs.
To illustrate, in a two-locus model (A/a and B/b) with equal allele frequencies, additive effects contribute linearly to the phenotype by summing the average impacts of each allele across loci, yielding predictable genotypic values. However, epistasis introduces non-linear outcomes, such as overdominance (where the double heterozygote exceeds both parental types) or underdominance (where it falls below), resulting in phenotypic variances that cannot be decomposed solely into additive components.

Variance Decomposition

Components of Genetic Variance

The phenotypic variance $ V_P $ observed in a quantitative trait within a population arises from multiple sources and is fundamentally decomposed into genetic and non-genetic components. In quantitative genetics, this decomposition is expressed as $ V_P = V_G + V_E + V_{GE} $, where $ V_G $ represents the total genetic variance attributable to differences in genotypes, $ V_E $ denotes the environmental variance due to non-heritable factors affecting trait expression, and $ V_{GE} $ captures the variance stemming from interactions between genotypes and environmental conditions.
The total genetic variance $ V_G $ is itself partitioned into additive and non-additive components to reflect the different ways genes contribute to trait variation. Specifically, $ V_G = V_A + V_D + V_I $, with $ V_A $ being the additive genetic variance, $ V_D $ the dominance variance arising from interactions between alleles at the same locus, and $ V_I $ the epistatic variance resulting from interactions among alleles at different loci. This breakdown allows researchers to distinguish the heritable portions of variation that can be predicted across generations from those that cannot.
Combining these, the basic model for phenotypic variance simplifies to $ V_P = V_A + V_D + V_I + V_E $ when genotype-environment interactions are minimal or separately analyzed. Among these, the additive genetic variance $ V_A $ is particularly significant, as it quantifies the variance due to breeding valuesthe average effects of parental alleles on offspring phenotypes, assuming no dominance or epistasis. This component, rooted in the linear summation of individual gene effects, underpins predictions of response to selection in breeding programs.

Heritability Measures

Broad-Sense Heritability

Broad-sense heritability, denoted as $ H^2 $, is defined as the proportion of phenotypic variance in a population that is attributable to total genetic variance, expressed as $ H^2 = \frac{V_G}{V_P} $, where $ V_G $ represents all genetic contributions including additive, dominance, and epistatic effects, and $ V_P $ is the total phenotypic variance. This measure quantifies the overall genetic influence on a trait without distinguishing between types of genetic effects.
The value of $ H^2 $ ranges from 0 to 1, with 0 indicating no genetic contribution to phenotypic variation and 1 indicating that genetics account for all observed differences in the trait. A high $ H^2 $ implies that genetic factors predominantly explain phenotypic differences within the studied population, but this does not guarantee reliable prediction of the trait across generations, as non-additive genetic effects may not be consistently inherited.
Broad-sense heritability is commonly estimated using twin studies, which leverage the genetic similarity between monozygotic and dizygotic twins to partition variance into genetic and environmental components via structural equation modeling; this captures total genetic variance (additive plus dominance) as H = (V_A + V_D)/V_P, often assuming minimal shared environmental effects. In experimental designs, such as those in plant or animal breeding, it can also be derived from analyses of variance among clonal or full-sib families that reflect total genotypic effects.
One limitation of broad-sense heritability is that it overestimates the heritable variation that can be transmitted predictably to offspring, since non-additive components like dominance and epistasis do not follow simple Mendelian inheritance patterns and thus contribute less to response in selection programs. The total genetic variance $ V_G $ encompasses additive genetic variance as a subset, but $ H^2 $ includes these non-transmissible elements, potentially misleading applications focused on breeding value.

Theoretical Foundations

Fisher's Fundamental Theorem

Ronald Fisher introduced the Fundamental Theorem of Natural Selection in his 1930 book The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection, where he formalized the relationship between genetic variation and evolutionary change. The theorem posits that the rate of increase in the mean fitness of a population at any time ascribable to natural selection equals the additive genetic variance in fitness at that time.
Mathematically, the theorem is expressed as

This formulation highlights that the additive component of genetic variance in fitness directly determines the short-term rate of evolutionary improvement in fitness under selection.
The theorem relies on several key assumptions, including random mating within the population to ensure that genotypic frequencies align with Hardy-Weinberg expectations, and no changes in the environment that would alter the selective values of genotypes. Additionally, it treats fitness itself as the focal trait, with non-additive genetic effects such as dominance and epistasis considered part of the environmental component rather than heritable variance. These conditions allow the theorem to isolate the deterministic effects of natural selection on additive genetic variation.
At its core, the theorem underscores that evolutionary change occurs through alterations in gene frequencies, with natural selection acting primarily on the additive effects of genes that contribute to variance in fitness. This additive focus emphasizes the heritable portion of phenotypic variation that can be transmitted across generations and respond predictably to selection pressures.

Implications for Evolution

Additive genetic variance plays a central role in enabling evolutionary responses to selection, as it represents the heritable portion of phenotypic variation that can be transmitted across generations, allowing populations to adapt predictably to changing environments. The breeder's equation quantifies this relationship, stating that the response to selection (R) equals the product of narrow-sense heritability (h, or V_A / V_P) and the selection differential (S), thereby linking additive effects directly to the rate of evolutionary change. Without sufficient V_A, adaptation is limited, as non-additive components like dominance and epistasis do not contribute reliably to offspring resemblance. Over long-term selection, depletion of V_A can constrain further evolution unless replenished by mechanisms such as mutation.
Modern interpretations of Fisher's fundamental theorem extend its scope beyond fitness to any trait under selection, via the secondary theorem of natural selection, which posits that the rate of change in a trait equals its additive genetic covariance with fitness. This framework highlights how additive effects drive multivariate evolution, interacting with genetic driftwhich can erode V_A in finite populationsand mutation, which introduces new variation to sustain long-term adaptability. These interactions underscore that while selection exploits existing V_A, other forces modulate its availability, influencing overall evolutionary dynamics.
Critiques of the original theorem note its omission of non-additive effects and environmental changes, which can alter total fitness trajectories despite selection on additive variance. Extensions, such as the Price equation, address this by decomposing evolutionary change into partial effects due to selection (captured by Fisher's theorem) and transmission or environmental components, providing a more comprehensive model. For instance, Price clarified that the theorem describes only the selective component of change, resolving ambiguities in dynamic environments.
A prominent example is the adaptation in Darwin's finches (Geospiza spp.), where shifts in beak size during droughts are driven by natural selection on additive polygenic effects, with narrow-sense heritabilities for beak traits estimated at 0.650.79, enabling rapid intergenerational responses.

Estimation and Applications

Methods of Estimation

One primary method for estimating additive genetic effects involves parent-offspring regression, which assesses the resemblance between parental and offspring phenotypes to infer narrow-sense heritability, defined as twice the slope of the regression of offspring phenotype on the mid-parent value. This approach assumes random mating and no environmental covariance between parents and offspring, allowing the regression coefficient $ b $ to relate directly to the additive genetic variance component $ V_A $ through the formula $ h^2 = 2b $, where $ h^2 = V_A / V_P $ and $ V_P $ is the total phenotypic variance.
Sib analysis, particularly using half-sib designs common in animal breeding, partitions phenotypic variance by comparing covariances within and between half-sib groups, which share one parent. In this design, the between-sire (or between-dam) variance component $ \sigma^2_F $ captures a quarter of the additive genetic variance due to shared additive effects, yielding the estimate $ V_A = 4 \sigma^2_F $, often analyzed via analysis of variance (ANOVA) or mixed models to separate $ V_A $ from dominance variance $ V_D $. Full-sib analyses extend this by incorporating both additive and dominance components but require adjustments to isolate $ V_A $.
Modern genomic methods leverage single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) markers to construct a genomic relationship matrix (GRM), as in genomic best linear unbiased prediction (GBLUP), which replaces traditional pedigree-based matrices with marker-derived ones to estimate breeding values and $ V_A $. In GBLUP models, the additive genetic covariance is modeled as $ \mathbf{G} \sigma^2_g $, where $ \mathbf{G} $ is the GRM scaled by allele frequencies and $ \sigma^2_g $ approximates $ V_A $, enabling estimation even in populations with complex relatedness via mixed linear models integrated with genome-wide association studies (GWAS).
These methods face challenges, including assumptions of negligible epistasis, linkage disequilibrium, and genotype-environment interactions, which can bias $ V_A $ estimates if violated. Restricted maximum likelihood (REML) addresses some biases inherent in maximum likelihood estimation by accounting for uncertainty in fixed effects, providing more reliable variance components through iterative optimization of the restricted likelihood function.

In animal breeding, estimated breeding values (EBVs) leverage additive genetic effects to predict an individual's genetic merit for economically important traits, enabling precise selection of superior sires and dams. For instance, in dairy cattle, EBVs for milk yield are calculated from pedigree and performance data, allowing breeders to select bulls whose progeny are expected to produce 5001,000 kg more milk per lactation than the population average, thereby accelerating genetic progress without direct progeny testing.
In plant breeding, selection indices incorporate estimates of additive genetic variance to simultaneously improve multiple traits, assigning economic weights to balance gains in yield against disease resistance. These indices, such as those used in wheat or maize programs, maximize response by prioritizing genotypes with high $ V_A $-based predictions for grain yield while maintaining resistance to pathogens like rust.
Genomic selection enhances the exploitation of additive effects by using whole-genome marker data to predict breeding values across the genome, often via methods like genomic best linear unbiased prediction (GBLUP), which reduces generation intervals from years to months in crops and livestock. This approach has doubled annual genetic gains for traits like maize yield by enabling early selection of seedlings based on predicted additive contributions from thousands of loci, without waiting for field performance.
