Trichostatin A
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Trichostatin A (TSA) is a hydroxamic acid-based antifungal antibiotic originally isolated in 1976 from the secondary metabolites of the bacterium Streptomyces hygroscopicus. With the chemical formula C17H22N2O3 and a molecular weight of 302.37 g/mol, it features a (2E,4E,6R)-7--N-hydroxy-4,6-dimethyl-7-oxohepta-2,4-dienamide structure that enables zinc chelation in enzyme active sites. In 1990, TSA was identified as a potent and specific inhibitor of mammalian histone deacetylases (HDACs), particularly class I and II enzymes, by binding to their catalytic zinc ions and preventing deacetylation of histones, which leads to increased histone acetylation, chromatin relaxation, and altered gene expression.
As a research tool, TSA has been extensively used to investigate epigenetic mechanisms due to its ability to induce hyperacetylation at low nanomolar concentrations (IC50 values <10 nM for HDACs 1, 2, 3, 6, 10, and 11). It promotes cell cycle arrest in G1 and G2/M phases, differentiation, and apoptosis in various transformed cell lines, including those from breast cancer, prostate cancer, and melanoma, making it a model compound for studying antitumor effects. Beyond oncology, TSA has demonstrated potential in modulating neurological processes, such as alleviating anxiety- and depression-like behaviors in Alzheimer's disease mouse models by enhancing histone acetylation and synaptic plasticity, though its high systemic toxicity limits clinical translation. Additionally, it improves developmental outcomes in somatic cell nuclear transfer embryos by correcting aberrant gene expression patterns.
Despite its broad inhibitory profile and efficacy in preclinical models, TSA's non-selective nature and toxicity profileincluding genotoxicity and off-target effects due to non-selective inhibitionrestrict it to laboratory applications, inspiring the development of more targeted HDAC inhibitors like vorinostat for therapeutic use. Ongoing research continues to explore TSA's role in de-differentiation, angiogenesis inhibition, and regenerative medicine, underscoring its foundational impact on epigenetics.

History and Discovery

Discovery

Trichostatin A was first isolated in 1976 from the culture broth of Streptomyces hygroscopicus, a soil bacterium, during a systematic screening program for novel antimicrobial agents derived from microbial metabolites. The discovery was made by a team of Japanese researchers led by Naoki Tsuji, including Masaaki Kobayashi, Kazuo Nagashima, Yoshiharu Wakisaka, and Kenzo Koizumi, affiliated with Fujisawa Pharmaceutical Company. This effort focused on identifying compounds with potential therapeutic value against fungal pathogens, reflecting the era's emphasis on natural products from actinomycetes as sources of antibiotics. The isolation process involved fermentation of the Streptomyces strain, followed by extraction, purification via chromatography, and initial bioassays that highlighted its activity.
Initially identified as an antifungal antibiotic, trichostatin A demonstrated weak but specific inhibitory effects against Trichophyton species, such as Trichophyton mentagrophytes, which are causative agents of dermatophytosis. Its potency was modest compared to established antifungals, with minimum inhibitory concentrations in the range of 312 g/mL against select dermatophytes, but it showed limited activity against other fungi like Candida albicans or bacteria. This profile positioned it as a member of the emerging trichostatin family of compounds, named for their origin and targeted antifungal spectrum. Early studies also noted its stability under neutral conditions but sensitivity to acid and base, influencing the purification methods employed.
The initial characterization and production details were detailed in a seminal publication in The Journal of Antibiotics in 1976, which described the fermentation conditionsoptimal at 28C for 45 days in a nutrient-rich mediumand basic isolation techniques, including solvent extraction and silica gel chromatography. This work laid the groundwork for recognizing trichostatin A as a hydroxamic acid derivative. The discovery underscored the potential of Streptomyces metabolites in antifungal drug development, even if trichostatin A's immediate clinical pursuit was limited due to its narrow spectrum.

Development and Synthesis

The structure of Trichostatin A was elucidated in the late 1970s through extensive spectroscopic analyses, including nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) and mass spectrometry (MS), which confirmed its hydroxamic acid-containing framework and stereochemical features.
The first total synthesis of Trichostatin A was reported in 1983, enabling access beyond natural isolation, but a landmark enantioselective route was achieved in 1987 by Schreiber and colleagues, utilizing stereoselective aldol reactions to establish the critical (2E,4E,6R) configuration in the triene chain. This approach highlighted the compound's complex unsaturated amide structure and paved the way for scalable production in research settings.
Subsequent improvements focused on efficiency and accessibility, such as the 2010 method by Woods and coworkers, which employed a streamlined coupling of a dimethylaminophenyl ketone derivative with a chiral heptadienamide chain via hydroboration and Suzuki-Miyaura cross-coupling, yielding racemic Trichostatin A in fewer steps from commercial precursors. These synthetic advances reduced reliance on microbial fermentation and facilitated analog development for enhanced potency and selectivity.
Building on Trichostatin A's scaffold, structural analogs were developed to mitigate its toxicity while retaining histone deacetylase inhibitory activity; a prominent example is vorinostat (suberoylanilide hydroxamic acid), a simplified linear hydroxamic acid derivative approved by the FDA in 2006 for cutaneous T-cell lymphoma treatment, demonstrating reduced off-target effects in clinical use.
Key challenges in Trichostatin A synthesis include preserving the reactive hydroxamic acid functionality, which is prone to degradation during coupling reactions, and controlling stereochemistry at the C-6 position to ensure biological activity, often requiring chiral auxiliaries or asymmetric catalysis.

Physical and Chemical Characteristics

Trichostatin A appears as a white to off-white or light yellow crystalline powder.
It exhibits good solubility in organic solvents such as dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO, up to ~50 mg/mL), limited solubility in ethanol (~1-3 mg/mL) and dimethylformamide (DMF; soluble but specific concentrations not well-documented), but shows poor solubility in water (<0.1 mg/mL).
The compound is chemically stable under standard ambient conditions and neutral pH but degrades via hydrolysis of the hydroxamic acid group in strong acidic or basic environments.
Trichostatin A has a logP value of approximately 2.4-2.8, indicating moderate lipophilicity. Its melting point ranges from 140 to 143C, and the pKa of the hydroxamic acid moiety is about 9.57.
For optimal preservation, storage at -20C in a dry, tightly closed container under an inert atmosphere is recommended to minimize oxidation and degradation.

Pharmacology

Mechanism of Action

Trichostatin A (TSA) functions as a reversible inhibitor of histone deacetylases (HDACs), primarily targeting class I enzymes (HDAC1, HDAC2, HDAC3, and HDAC8) and class II enzymes (HDAC4, HDAC5, HDAC6, HDAC7, HDAC9, and HDAC10). This inhibition occurs with IC values in the low nanomolar range (typically 2-20 nM) across these isoforms, demonstrating potent nanomolar activity that disrupts the enzymatic removal of acetyl groups from lysine residues on histones and other substrates. The inhibition is competitive with respect to the acetylated substrate, following standard enzyme kinetics where TSA competes for binding at the active site.
At the molecular level, TSA binds to the HDAC active site through its hydroxamic acid moiety, which chelates the catalytic zinc ion (Zn) in a bidentate fashion, thereby blocking the deacetylation reaction. The molecule's extended aliphatic chain occupies the narrow substrate tunnel, while the terminal dimethylaminophenyl group, serving as a capping moiety, interacts with surface residues near the active site entrance, contributing to isoform selectivity by sterically influencing access. This binding mode, elucidated through crystal structures of HDAC homologues and human HDAC8 in complex with TSA, prevents the nucleophilic attack by a water molecule coordinated to Zn, essential for acetate hydrolysis.
Inhibition of HDACs by TSA leads to hyperacetylation of core histones, particularly H3 and H4 at lysine residues such as K9, K14, K18, and K27, which neutralizes their positive charge and promotes a more open chromatin structure known as euchromatin. This structural change facilitates access of transcription factors and RNA polymerase to promoter regions, resulting in enhanced expression of specific genes, including the cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor p21^{WAF1/CIP1} and the pro-apoptotic regulator Bax. Such transcriptional activation is a direct consequence of the accumulated acetyl marks, altering nucleosome positioning and epigenetic regulation without altering DNA sequence.
At higher concentrations, TSA exhibits off-target effects beyond classical HDACs, including inhibition of the NAD-dependent sirtuin deacetylase SIRT6, which shares structural similarities in its catalytic domain but operates via a distinct mechanism. Biological Effects

Trichostatin A (TSA), through its inhibition of histone deacetylases, exerts diverse effects on cellular processes, leading to cell cycle arrest that varies by cell type, often in the G1/S phase but also in G2/M phase in certain lines. This arrest is mediated by the upregulation of cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitors such as p21WAF1/CIP1, which inhibits cyclin E/CDK2 complexes and prevents progression into the S phase. In human cervix carcinoma HeLa cells, TSA treatment at concentrations of 100-300 nM induces accumulation in G1 phase within 24 hours, accompanied by increased p21 expression independent of p53 status. Similarly, in bladder cancer cells, TSA (50-200 nM) triggers G1 arrest via p21 induction, reducing Rb phosphorylation and halting proliferation.
TSA also promotes apoptosis in transformed cells by activating both intrinsic mitochondrial pathways and extrinsic death receptor signaling. In lung cancer cells, TSA (0.1-1 M) activates caspase-9 through mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization, leading to cytochrome c release and subsequent caspase-3 activation, while downregulating anti-apoptotic Bcl-2 and Bcl-XL. This dual pathway convergence results in PARP cleavage and DNA fragmentation, with apoptosis rates exceeding 50% after 48 hours of exposure. Additionally, TSA elevates mitochondrial reactive oxygen species (ROS) production, further amplifying the intrinsic apoptotic cascade in breast cancer models.
In models of leukemia and neuroblastoma, TSA induces cellular differentiation toward more mature phenotypes. Treatment of acute myeloid leukemia cells with TSA (50-100 nM) enhances erythroid or monocytic differentiation markers, such as hemoglobin or CD14 expression, by altering gene expression profiles associated with lineage commitment. In neuroblastoma cells, TSA (0.1-0.5 M) promotes neuronal differentiation, increasing neurite outgrowth and expression of markers like -III tubulin, while reducing proliferation in cell lines such as SH-SY5Y. These effects highlight TSA's role in redirecting malignant cells toward non-proliferative states.
TSA modulates immune responses by influencing cytokine production in immune cells. In macrophages, TSA suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokine expression, notably repressing inducible nitric oxide synthase (NOS2/iNOS) and reducing nitric oxide production in response to lipopolysaccharide (LPS) stimulation (100 ng/mL TSA for 24 hours). This inhibition occurs via blockade of NF-B signaling and Oct-2 transcription factor activity, limiting inflammatory mediator release. However, in certain contexts, TSA enhances anti-inflammatory profiles by promoting M2 macrophage polarization through autophagy induction. In T cells, TSA alters cytokine profiles by reducing IL-2 and IFN- secretion; for instance, in activated CD4+ T cells, TSA (200 nM) represses IL-2 gene expression, impacting T-cell proliferation and effector functions.
Beyond oncology, TSA demonstrates neuroprotective effects in non-cancer models, such as Huntington's disease. In striatal neurons from HD mouse models, TSA (1-5 M) restores brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) vesicular transport by increasing histone acetylation and compensating for mutant huntingtin-induced deficits, thereby improving neuronal survival and function. Recent studies (as of 2024) also show TSA relieving anxiety- and depression-like behaviors in Alzheimer's disease mouse models. Additionally, TSA has been reported to ameliorate combined radiation and skin wound injury in rat models (2025). In epithelial cells, TSA promotes proliferation (2025).

Research Applications

In Cancer Research

Trichostatin A (TSA), a potent histone deacetylase inhibitor, has been extensively studied in preclinical cancer research for its antitumor effects, primarily through modulation of epigenetic mechanisms that alter gene expression in malignant cells. In various cancer types, TSA demonstrates significant inhibitory activity on cell proliferation. For instance, in breast cancer cell lines such as MDA-MB-231, TSA suppresses growth in a dose-dependent manner by inhibiting histone deacetylase activity and inducing hyperacetylation of histones. Similarly, in prostate cancer cells like PC3, TSA disrupts the epidermal growth factor receptor pathway, leading to reduced proliferation. In glioma cells, TSA causes growth inhibition via cell cycle arrest at G2/M phase and increased p21WAF1 expression. Esophageal squamous cell carcinoma lines also exhibit suppressed proliferation and enhanced apoptosis following TSA exposure.
Key mechanisms underlying TSA's anticancer effects include the re-expression of silenced tumor suppressor genes and sensitization to apoptotic pathways. TSA promotes the re-expression of E-cadherin, a critical tumor suppressor often downregulated in cancers via epigenetic silencing, thereby reversing epithelial-mesenchymal transition and inhibiting invasion in colorectal and other cancer models. In p53-mutant cancer cells, TSA depletes mutant p53 protein levels, which enhances cytotoxicity and sensitizes cells to apoptosis by removing the protective effects of oncogenic p53 variants. This general induction of apoptosis is amplified in cancer contexts, where TSA upregulates pro-apoptotic genes while downregulating anti-apoptotic factors.
Preclinical in vivo models further validate TSA's potential. In xenograft models using MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells implanted in nude mice, TSA administration exhibits potent antitumor activity, reducing tumor volume through sustained HDAC inhibition. In glioblastoma models, TSA potentiates cytotoxic effects by promoting apoptosis and augmenting innate immune responses, such as natural killer cell activity against tumor cells. These findings highlight TSA's efficacy in orthotopic and subcutaneous tumor models, where it achieves significant tumor regression without immediate systemic failure.
TSA's utility extends to combination therapies, where it synergizes with standard treatments to overcome resistance. It enhances radiosensitivity in esophageal and other cancers by impairing DNA damage repair, leading to accumulation of double-strand breaks and increased cell death following irradiation. Low doses of TSA (30-100 nM) synergize with chemotherapeutics like docetaxel in lung and urothelial cancers, resensitizing HDAC inhibitor-resistant cells and amplifying apoptosis through mitochondrial pathways. These combinations often yield additive or synergistic effects in vitro, with TSA potentiating drug-induced cell cycle arrest and caspase activation.
Despite these promising preclinical outcomes, TSA's high toxicity profile has historically limited its translation to clinical use, particularly for systemic administration due to severe off-target effects at therapeutic doses. However, as of 2025, ongoing clinical trials are exploring TSA (as VTR-297) in specific contexts, including a Phase 1 dose-escalation study for relapsed or refractory hematologic malignancies (NCT03838926) and a Phase 2 trial for topical treatment of onychomycosis (NCT07065773), aiming to leverage its antifungal origins and mitigate toxicity. Research with TSA has nonetheless informed the development of safer HDAC inhibitor analogs, such as vorinostat (suberoylanilide hydroxamic acid), which retains antitumor efficacy with improved pharmacokinetics and reduced toxicity, paving the way for approved therapies in oncology.

In Epigenetics and Other Fields

Trichostatin A (TSA) serves as a valuable tool in epigenetics research, particularly for investigating histone acetylation dynamics and its influence on gene expression. As a potent histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor, TSA induces global hyperacetylation of histones, which alters chromatin structure and facilitates access to transcriptional machinery, enabling detailed profiling of gene expression changes in various cell types. For instance, treatment with TSA has been shown to robustly increase histone H3 and H4 acetylation levels, leading to enhanced transcription of specific genes such as BDNF in neuronal models, thereby providing insights into epigenetic regulation of neuroplasticity. In embryonic stem cell studies, TSA treatment improves aberrant gene expression patterns by promoting hyperacetylation at promoter regions, which has been instrumental in understanding reprogramming and differentiation processes. Recent applications include enhancing CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing efficiency in induced pluripotent stem cells by decreasing chromatin condensation, increasing editing rates up to twofold as of 2023.
In neurological research, TSA demonstrates potential in models of Alzheimer's disease (AD) and Huntington's disease (HD), where it ameliorates pathology and enhances cognitive function, albeit with short-term effects constrained by toxicity. In APP/PS1 transgenic mice modeling AD, TSA administration increases albumin expression, promotes amyloid-beta clearance, and improves spatial learning and memory in behavioral tests like the Morris water maze. Similarly, in HD mouse models such as HdhQ7/Q111, acute TSA treatment rescues memory deficits, upregulates CREB/CBP target genes, and compensates for impaired vesicular transport of brain-derived neurotrophic factor by increasing tubulin acetylation. These effects highlight TSA's role in modulating epigenetic mechanisms underlying neurodegeneration, though long-term applications are limited by its cytotoxicity at higher doses. As of 2025, studies also suggest TSA increases glutamate transporter type 1 (GLT-1) expression, potentially aiding neurodegenerative disease models.
TSA also exerts immunological effects, notably in modulating CD4+ T-cell differentiation and inhibiting glioblastoma stem cell properties. It promotes the generation and suppressive functions of regulatory T cells (Tregs) by enhancing Foxp3 expression in CD4+Foxp3+ populations, thereby augmenting their ability to inhibit effector T-cell proliferation in vitro. In CD4+ T-cell cultures, TSA treatment causes rapid declines in cytokine production, induces G1 cell cycle arrest, and shifts differentiation toward tolerogenic phenotypes, which has implications for autoimmune disease models. Regarding glioblastoma, TSA inhibits tumor sphere formation in U87-MG glioblastoma cells and tumorsphere-derived stem-like cells, reducing self-renewal capacity and promoting neuron-like differentiation through HDAC inhibition.
Beyond these areas, TSA has been explored as a geroprotector in model organisms and as a reference compound in HDAC assays for drug screening. In daf-16 mutant Caenorhabditis elegans (worms), TSA extends chronological lifespan to an average of 22.2 days compared to untreated controls, likely via epigenetic modulation of stress response pathways. Similar lifespan extension occurs in Drosophila melanogaster, where TSA upregulates heat shock protein genes like hsp22, enhancing stress resistance. In yeast, TSA serves as a standard inhibitor in HDAC activity assays, facilitating high-throughput screening of novel HDAC-targeted therapeutics by measuring fluorogenic substrate deacetylation. For cell culture studies across these applications, typical protocols involve TSA concentrations of 100-500 nM applied for 24-48 hours to achieve hyperacetylation without excessive cytotoxicity.

Toxicity and Safety

Adverse Effects

Trichostatin A exhibits acute toxicity classified under GHS as Category 4 for oral, dermal, and inhalation routes, indicating it is harmful if swallowed, in contact with skin, or inhaled. It is also classified as a skin sensitizer Category 1B and specific target organ toxicity (single exposure) Category 3, primarily affecting the respiratory system.
Observed symptoms include skin and eye irritation, with potential for serious eye irritation and allergic skin reactions upon exposure. Respiratory irritation may occur following inhalation, contributing to the overall toxicological profile.
Trichostatin A demonstrates teratogenic potential, classified under GHS as reproductive toxicity Category 1A, indicating it may damage the unborn child. In rodent models, administration at doses of 2-16 mg/kg on day 8 post-conception induces developmental defects, including abnormalities in the central nervous system and musculoskeletal system. Similarly, in chick embryos, exposure during neural tube closure stages leads to neural tube defects such as failure of posterior neuropore closure, increased apoptosis in neuroepithelial cells, and deregulation of cadherin expression.
At the cellular level, trichostatin A induces off-target effects beyond HDAC inhibition, including non-specific hyperacetylation of proteins that triggers proteotoxic stress. It also exhibits genotoxic properties, causing dose-dependent chromosomal aberrations, DNA damage, and aneuploidy in human lymphoblasts.
Dose-dependent effects are prominent at nanomolar concentrations, such as 200 nM, where trichostatin A rapidly induces cell cycle arrest in the G1 phase and apoptosis in normal cells, accompanied by elevated p21 levels and gammaH2AX phosphorylation. These cytotoxic responses occur even in non-cancerous cells, highlighting its broad cellular toxicity.

Clinical Limitations

Despite its promising preclinical antitumor activity, Trichostatin A (TSA) has not progressed to widespread clinical use primarily due to its high systemic toxicity, which severely limits safe dosing regimens. In vivo studies in rodents indicate that TSA exhibits antitumor effects at doses of approximately 0.5 mg/kg with no measurable acute toxicity up to 5 mg/kg subcutaneously, though broader concerns include potential risks such as methemoglobinemia when combined with certain drugs like ambroxol.
Another key barrier is TSA's lack of selectivity as a pan-histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor, which targets multiple classes (I and II) indiscriminately, resulting in broad off-target effects and a wide array of side effects that complicate its therapeutic index compared to more isoform-specific HDAC inhibitors. Furthermore, pharmacokinetic challenges exacerbate these issues: TSA has a very short plasma half-life of about 10 minutes in mice, necessitating intravenous or intraperitoneal administration due to its poor oral bioavailability (approximately 5% in rats), which hinders practical clinical delivery and sustained efficacy.
As a result, TSA remains primarily a research tool with no approved clinical indications. While early-phase human trials have faced toxicity and pharmacokinetic hurdles, ongoing investigations as of 2025 include a Phase 1 trial (NCT03838926) for relapsed or refractory hematologic malignancies and a Phase 2 trial (NCT07065773) for onychomycosis. However, TSA's discoveries have paved the way for more tolerable alternatives, such as vorinostat (suberoylanilide hydroxamic acid), which was FDA-approved in 2006 for cutaneous T-cell lymphoma and shares a similar HDAC inhibition mechanism but offers improved selectivity and safety profiles for clinical application.
