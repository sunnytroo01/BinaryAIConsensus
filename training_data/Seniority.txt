Seniority
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Seniority is a hierarchical principle in organizations and institutions whereby an individual's rank, privileges, benefits, and opportunities for advancement are allocated primarily according to the duration of their continuous service or tenure, rather than solely on merit, performance, or other qualifications.
In employment contexts, seniority systems are most entrenched in unionized industries, public sector jobs, and certain government roles, where they govern decisions on layoffs (last-in, first-out), promotions, shift bidding, and vacation scheduling to ensure predictability and reduce managerial discretion or favoritism. These systems promote employee loyalty, lower turnover rates, and institutional knowledge retention by rewarding long-term commitment, as evidenced by higher average tenure in firms with strong seniority wage structures.
However, seniority has faced criticism for incentivizing complacency and diminishing productivity, as empirical analyses show a negative correlation between employee tenure under such systems and creative or overall job performance, often because it subordinates ability to time served. In the United States, bona fide seniority systems receive legal protection under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, even if they inadvertently perpetuate historical disparities, provided they were not designed with discriminatory intent. Their prevalence has declined in private-sector firms favoring merit-based evaluations, reflecting a shift toward performance-driven incentives amid evidence that rigid seniority stifles innovation and mobility.

Conceptual Foundations

Definition and Core Principles

Seniority denotes the length of an individual's continuous service within an organization or group, serving as the primary basis for establishing rank, precedence, or privileges relative to those with shorter tenure. This metric, typically calculated from the date of hire and adjusted for verified interruptions such as approved leaves, creates an objective hierarchy that minimizes reliance on subjective evaluations like personal favoritism or ad hoc assessments. Unlike merit-based systems, which emphasize performance metrics, seniority prioritizes temporal investment as a proxy for experience and commitment, though it does not inherently correlate with current competence.
At its core, a seniority system formalizes the allocation of employment or organizational rightsencompassing promotions, job security, layoff sequencing (often last-in, first-out), rehiring priority, shift bidding, and benefit accrualstrictly according to relative employment duration. This principle operates on the causal logic that extended tenure accumulates institutional knowledge and loyalty, thereby justifying preferential treatment to incentivize retention and stabilize workforce dynamics. Systems are deemed bona fide when neutrally applied without discriminatory intent, as protected under U.S. federal law since the 1964 Civil Rights Act amendments, provided they accrue benefits proportionally to service length across eligible employees.
The principle extends beyond employment to institutional settings like legislatures, where continuous service determines leadership roles, such as committee chairmanships in the U.S. Congress, reflecting a similar emphasis on verifiable time-in-role over electoral popularity or ideological alignment. Fundamentally, seniority embodies a rule-based mechanism for resolving competitions over scarce resources or positions, predicated on the empirical observability of service duration to promote procedural fairness, though its rigidity can embed historical entry barriers if initial hiring practices were uneven. Variations include departmental versus enterprise-wide application, but all hinge on unbroken or adjusted service records maintained by employers or governing bodies.

Historical Origins

The principle of seniority, which accords precedence based on age, tenure, or accumulated experience, has roots in ancient societal structures where elders were presumed to possess superior wisdom and stability. Primogeniture systems, prevalent in many ancient cultures, exemplified this by granting family leadership, property ownership, and inheritance rights to the eldest male heir, thereby institutionalizing age-based hierarchy to maintain continuity and avoid fragmentation.
In East Asia, Confucian philosophy formalized seniority as a foundational element of social order, emphasizing filial piety and respect for elders to ensure hierarchical harmony; this influenced governance, family clans, and later organizational practices by prioritizing longevity in roles over merit alone. In classical antiquity, Roman institutions reflected similar dynamics, with the Senatederived etymologically from senex (elder)comprising patricians whose prestige stemmed partly from clan antiquity and seniority, reinforcing authority through generational continuity.
Medieval European developments further embedded seniority in formal organizations. Craft guilds structured membership hierarchically, requiring apprentices to serve extended periods (often 7 years) before advancing to journeyman status and eventually master, with progression tied to time served alongside skill demonstration to regulate competition and quality. Religious communities, such as Benedictine monasteries established around 529 CE, adopted strict seniority rules for leadership, ranking monks by entry date to foster discipline and deference to experience over personal ambition. These practices prefigured modern applications by linking tenure to privileges like promotion and protection from displacement.

Theoretical Evaluation

Advantages of Seniority Systems

Seniority systems provide a predictable framework for advancement, minimizing disputes over promotions by establishing objective criteria based on tenure rather than subjective evaluations. This clarity rewards employee loyalty and long-term commitment, encouraging retention as workers anticipate future benefits from continued service. In organizational settings, such systems correlate with reduced turnover rates, as evidenced by practices in unionized environments where seniority protections incentivize sustained employment.
By prioritizing time in service, seniority mitigates perceptions of favoritism, cronyism, or arbitrary decision-making, fostering workplace harmony and morale. Empirical studies indicate that seniority-based structures enhance maturity among promotees, as longer-tenured employees often possess accumulated practical knowledge and institutional expertise that contribute to effective performance in leadership roles. For instance, research on U.S. workplaces shows seniority playing a key role in promotion decisions, linking tenure to higher productivity gains over time.
In political institutions like the U.S. Congress, seniority ensures stability by assigning committee leadership to experienced members, avoiding internal conflicts and leveraging accumulated legislative knowledge for policy continuity. Similarly, in military hierarchies, tenure-based progression emphasizes respect earned through years of service, which correlates with operational reliability and command effectiveness derived from hands-on experience. Studies further suggest that seniority positively influences creative job performance in structured environments, as veteran employees apply refined skills honed over time. Overall, these systems promote causal continuity in organizations by aligning incentives with sustained contribution rather than transient talent.

Disadvantages and Criticisms

Critics argue that seniority systems undermine merit-based advancement by prioritizing tenure over competence, potentially elevating underperforming individuals to leadership roles while sidelining more capable subordinates. This approach diminishes employee motivation, as workers perceive limited rewards for exceptional performance or innovation, fostering complacency and resentment among high achievers who face blocked opportunities. Empirical analysis supports this, with one study finding a significant negative correlation between employee seniority and job performance, attributing it to reduced incentives for sustained effort as tenure increases.
Seniority rules can stifle organizational adaptability by retaining outdated expertise and discouraging risk-taking, as promotions reward longevity rather than responsiveness to changing conditions. In educational contexts, seniority-driven layoffs have been shown to impair student outcomes more severely than performance-based alternatives; for instance, districts using last-in-first-out policies lost an estimated 2-4 months of learning per affected student compared to merit evaluations. Such systems also complicate talent acquisition, as ambitious candidates avoid environments where advancement hinges on time served rather than results, exacerbating skill gaps in dynamic sectors.
In political and military applications, seniority entrenches gerontocracy, where prolonged tenure correlates with diminished vigor and fresh perspectives, leading to policy inertia. Research indicates that older leaders do not outperform younger ones in office, yet seniority privileges them, potentially hindering innovation and responsiveness to emerging threats. Overall, these mechanisms prioritize stability over efficacy, yielding suboptimal outcomes in competitive landscapes where merit correlates more strongly with success.

Institutional Applications

In Employment

Seniority in employment refers to a hierarchical ranking of workers based on the length of their continuous service with an employer, often determining priorities for job security, promotions, shift assignments, and benefit accruals. This system establishes objective criteria to allocate resources and opportunities, minimizing subjective favoritism in decisions such as layoffs, where employees with the least seniority are typically terminated first under a "last in, first out" (LIFO) principle. In the United States, seniority is frequently embedded in collective bargaining agreements negotiated between unions and employers, with protections dating back to federal labor laws like the National Labor Relations Act of 1935, which facilitate such arrangements to safeguard worker tenure.
Under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, bona fide seniority systemsthose neutral in intent and not designed to discriminate on prohibited grounds such as race or sexare permissible even if they inadvertently disadvantage protected groups, as affirmed by the U.S. Supreme Court in cases like Firefighters Local Union No. 1784 v. Stotts (1984), which upheld LIFO layoff rules over individual reinstatement claims from affirmative action hires. Bumping rights, allowing senior employees whose positions are eliminated to displace junior workers in other roles, exemplify this application and are standard in many union contracts, with seniority accrual often beginning after a probationary period of 30 to 90 days. In civil service contexts, such as U.S. federal or state government employment, seniority governs layoff order and recall rights, calculated from the date of hire and preserved during uniformed service leaves under the Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA) of 1994.
Private sector implementations vary by industry; for instance, in manufacturing and transportation unions like the United Electrical Workers, seniority dictates not only layoffs but also vacation scheduling and overtime distribution, with accumulated service credited from the hire date minus breaks exceeding specified durations. Empirical analyses of unionized workplaces indicate that seniority profiles contribute to wage premiums and job stability, though they can rigidify labor allocation by prioritizing tenure over recent performance metrics. In non-union settings, some employers voluntarily adopt hybrid models combining seniority with merit evaluations for promotions, reflecting a shift observed since the 1980s toward performance-based incentives amid competitive pressures. Government data from the U.S. Department of Labor show that as of 2023, approximately 14.4 million union-represented workersabout 10% of the workforceoperate under seniority-driven systems, predominantly in public administration and utilities sectors. These mechanisms, while promoting predictability, have faced scrutiny for potentially entrenching lower productivity among long-tenured staff, as evidenced in studies contrasting seniority with meritocratic promotions.

In Politics

In legislative bodies, seniority determines the allocation of influential positions such as committee chairmanships and leadership roles based on members' continuous years of service, prioritizing tenure over other factors like electoral success or ideological alignment. This system is most formalized in the United States Congress, where the longest-serving member of the majority party on a committee typically assumes its chairmanship, granting control over agendas, hearings, and bill referrals. In the Senate, seniority also influences office assignments, speaking order, and recognition by the presiding officer, with tiebreakers for equal tenure including prior officeholding or alphabetical order by state.
The seniority rule emerged in the Senate during the institution's formative decades in the early 19th century as a pragmatic solution to frequent disputes over precedence among equals, formalizing length of service to promote institutional stability. In the House of Representatives, it gained prominence following the 1910 revolt against Speaker Joseph Cannon, which decentralized power by empowering standing committees and establishing tenure-based leadership to curb speaker dominance and reduce partisan wrangling over posts. By the mid-20th century, both chambers adhered strictly to the norm, with senior members accumulating expertise and networks that reinforced their influence, as evidenced by data showing longer-serving representatives less frequently shifting committees or policy foci.
While dominant in the U.S., seniority's role varies internationally; in Westminster-style parliaments like the UK's House of Commons, committee chairs are elected by cross-party votes since 2010, prioritizing broader consensus over tenure and allowing newer members to lead if supported. In contrast, some systems, such as certain Latin American legislatures, blend seniority with party nominations for safe seats, where incumbents leverage tenure for renomination advantages. Reforms have occasionally tempered U.S. seniority: House Democrats in 1971 introduced secret ballots for chair selections, enabling occasional overrides, though tenure remains the default; both parties' conferences have since adjusted rules to allow leaders greater discretion, reducing absolute seniority in high-stakes panels like Rules or Appropriations. As of the 119th Congress (2025-2027), average House tenure stands at approximately 9.2 years, reflecting incumbency advantages tied to seniority perks like pork-barrel project access, though electoral pressures and party polarization have prompted sporadic challenges to veteran dominance.

In the Armed Forces

In the armed forces, seniority primarily governs the relative hierarchy among commissioned officers of the same rank, ensuring clear command authority, assignment precedence, and protocol observance. It is determined by the date of rank (DOR), defined as the date of appointment to the current grade under normal circumstances, with an earlier DOR conferring seniority over a later one. If multiple officers share the same DOR, precedence falls to the DOR of the prior grade, followed by commissioning source and date if necessary. This system applies across U.S. military branches, as codified in Department of Defense Instruction 1310.01, which standardizes rank and seniority to maintain operational cohesion, particularly in joint commands where equivalent ranks require unambiguous leadership resolution.
Promotions to higher grades incorporate seniority indirectly: selection boards evaluate officers based on performance records, but approved candidates are placed on promotion lists in order of their seniority (typically DOR in the previous grade) within competitive categories, with promotions executing sequentially from that list. The effective DOR for the new grade aligns with the promotion date unless adjusted by law, such as for wartime accelerations or federal recognition in reserve components. This merit-selection overlay on seniority prevents automatic advancement by time alone, as evidenced by "up-or-out" policies under the Defense Officer Personnel Management Act (DOPMA), which mandate separation for non-selectees to avoid grade bloat. Enlisted promotions, by contrast, emphasize time-in-service and time-in-grade thresholds alongside merit points and commander recommendations, with seniority influencing eligibility zones but not overriding board discretion.
Seniority also dictates tactical and administrative commands; for instance, in scenarios with officers of equal gradesuch as battalion-level operations or carrier strike groupsthe senior by DOR assumes control to minimize disputes and uphold the chain of command. Historical precedents include congressional recognitions of exceptional seniority, such as for five-star ranks where relative precedence was fixed by legislation to honor service longevity amid World War II exigencies. Deviations occur in temporary or acting capacities, but permanent seniority reverts to statutory DOR upon resolution, preserving institutional stability over individual assertions. This framework, while U.S.-centric in codification, mirrors principles in other professional militaries, where date-of-rank equivalents resolve equivalency in multinational operations under frameworks like NATO standardization agreements.

In Other Organizational Contexts

In labor unions, seniority systems typically prioritize members based on length of continuous service to determine layoff order, recall rights, shift bidding, and promotional opportunities, thereby reducing arbitrary favoritism and enhancing job security for veteran workers. These provisions are often enshrined in collective bargaining agreements, with seniority lists calculated from the date an employee enters the bargaining unit, excluding prior non-union service unless explicitly negotiated. For instance, the American Federation of Teachers advocates seniority as a safeguard against dismissal based on subjective criteria, linking it to sustained commitment and performance stability. Empirical analyses indicate that such rules can prolong unemployment durations for junior members during downturns by enforcing "last in, first out" policies, though they correlate with lower overall turnover in unionized settings.
Religious organizations frequently employ seniority to structure hierarchies and decision-making, often tied to ordination, vows, or tenure in leadership roles rather than merit alone. In Buddhist monastic communities, precedence is strictly determined by the date of ordination, with earlier-ordained monks holding authority over seating, rituals, and teaching privileges, fostering discipline and continuity in tradition. Similarly, in Catholic monastic orders, monks' seniority derives from the date of first vows, influencing communal roles irrespective of priestly status. Within The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles maintains a formal order of seniority based on apostolic appointment dates, which governs speaking turns in conferences and succession protocols. Case studies of religious decision-making bodies, such as Taiwanese Buddhist assemblies, reveal that deference to elders by tenure often overrides substantive debate, prioritizing harmony and experiential wisdom.
In professional associations and fraternal groups, seniority may influence committee assignments or leadership elections, though less rigidly than in unions; for example, longer-serving members gain preferential voting weights or board nominations to preserve institutional knowledge. Non-profit entities occasionally adopt hybrid approaches, where board terms limit indefinite seniority but veteran volunteers or staff receive deference in advisory capacities, as seen in governance models emphasizing continuity over rapid turnover. These applications underscore seniority's role in stabilizing non-commercial structures, yet they can entrench outdated perspectives absent performance checks.

Cultural and Societal Dimensions

Western Approaches to Seniority

In Western societies, seniority is generally approached through a meritocratic lens, where length of service or age confers limited automatic deference, subordinated to demonstrated competence, innovation, and individual performance. This reflects broader cultural values emphasizing egalitarianism, personal agency, and economic dynamism, which prioritize outcomes over tenure. For instance, organizational structures in countries like the United States and those in Western Europe often favor flatter hierarchies and performance-based evaluations, reducing reliance on rigid seniority ladders that could stifle adaptability in competitive markets. Empirical studies of cultural dimensions, such as those analyzing ascription (status by birth or age) versus achievement (status by merit), show Western nations scoring higher on achievement orientation, correlating with policies that reward skill acquisition over chronological progression.
Attitudes toward age and seniority in Western culture often exhibit pragmatism tinged with skepticism, viewing extended tenure as a potential liability if not paired with ongoing productivity. Surveys across 26 cultures indicate less positive perceptions of aging in Western samples compared to Eastern ones, with associations linking older age to decline rather than inherent wisdom. This manifests in societal norms that celebrate youth-driven innovationevident in tech sectors where founders like Mark Zuckerberg (born 1984) lead firms despite lacking decades of experiencewhile critiquing "gerontocracy" in leadership roles. Age discrimination laws, such as the U.S. Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967, codify this by prohibiting adverse actions based solely on age for workers over 40, underscoring a legal commitment to merit over seniority protections that might otherwise entrench underperformance.
Institutionally, seniority persists in targeted Western applications for stability and fairness, particularly in unionized labor and legislative bodies, but faces erosion amid meritocratic pressures. In employment, "last in, first out" (LIFO) layoff rules under collective bargaining agreementscommon in U.S. manufacturing and public sectorsgrant protections based on hire date to mitigate arbitrary dismissals, as affirmed in Equal Employment Opportunity Commission guidance since 1964. Similarly, the U.S. Congress has adhered to a seniority rule for committee chairmanships since the late 19th century, allocating power by continuous service to incentivize reelection and institutional knowledge, though reforms in the 1970s diluted party leader overrides. However, these systems are increasingly hybridized; for example, corporate promotions in Western firms integrate seniority with metrics like key performance indicators, reflecting data from organizational studies showing merit-based incentives boosting productivity by up to 20% in knowledge economies.
Contemporary Western discourse critiques unchecked seniority for fostering complacency, as seen in economic analyses linking rigid tenure protections to slower innovation in aging workforces. In Europe, policies like the EU's flexible labor directives since the 1990s encourage lifelong learning to counter seniority's disincentives, while U.S. venture capital ecosystems exemplify a near-total rejection of age-based hierarchy, funding ideas irrespective of founders' experience levels. This approach aligns with causal evidence from cross-national productivity data, where merit-heavy systems correlate with higher GDP growth rates, though seniority retains niche roles in high-stakes fields like aviation, where accumulated flight hours ensure safety without compromising evaluative rigor.

Eastern and Hierarchical Traditions

In Confucian philosophy, which profoundly shaped East Asian social structures, hierarchy is predicated on relational roles emphasizing deference to those of greater age and experience, as articulated in the Analects where Confucius stresses the moral imperative of respecting elders as bearers of accumulated wisdom. This extends beyond familial filial piety (xiao) to societal and institutional orders, where seniorityoften proxied by ageserves as a proxy for reliability and ethical maturity, fostering stability through predictable authority gradients. Empirical studies of Chinese firms link this cultural residue to persistent seniority preferences in promotions, where older executives correlate with conservative decision-making, potentially at the expense of innovation, as evidenced by analyses of patent outputs declining under seniority-driven leadership.
Japan's nenk jretsu system institutionalizes this ethos in modern employment, tying wages and promotions primarily to years of service rather than performance metrics alone, a practice that emerged post-World War II amid lifetime employment norms to incentivize loyalty and reduce turnover. Under this framework, employees advance in rank based on tenure proximity to retirement, with base pay escalating annually independent of output, which sustained economic growth through the 1980s by prioritizing harmony (wa) over individual competition but has since flattened age-wage profiles amid demographic aging, explaining over 80% of wage stagnation trends since 2000.
In historical Chinese bureaucracy, while the imperial examination system from the Tang Dynasty (618907 CE) onward prioritized scholarly merit for entry, subsequent advancement incorporated seniority alongside evaluations, as seen in Han Dynasty (206 BCE220 CE) protocols establishing 20 orders of rank where tenure influenced postings to prevent factionalism. This blend persisted into later dynasties, balancing meritocratic selection with hierarchical deference to mitigate corruption risks inherent in pure patronage, though empirical records indicate it sometimes entrenched mediocrity by insulating incumbents from displacement.
Indian hierarchical traditions, drawing from Vedic and familial norms, similarly elevate seniority through reverence for elders (pitbhakti), where age denotes authority in joint family systems and guru-shishya lineages, with decisions deferring to patriarchs or senior kin to preserve lineage continuity. This cultural valuation, observable in ethnographic accounts of rural households, underscores causality between perceived experiential wisdom and social cohesion, though it intersects with varna-based stratifications that prioritize birth over pure tenure.

Contemporary Debates and Reforms

Seniority Versus Meritocracy

Seniority-based systems prioritize length of service for promotions and rewards, aiming to ensure fairness through objective tenure metrics, whereas meritocracy emphasizes individual performance, skills, and contributions as the primary criteria. This tension arises because seniority assumes accumulated experience correlates with competence, providing stability in predictable environments like manufacturing or public administration, but it often decouples advancement from current productivity in dynamic sectors. Meritocratic approaches, by contrast, seek to align incentives with organizational outcomes, theoretically maximizing efficiency by elevating top performers regardless of hire date.
Proponents of seniority argue it minimizes subjective biases, arbitrary favoritism, and turnover by rewarding loyalty and institutional knowledge, as seen in union contracts where such rules protect workers from managerial whims. Empirical studies indicate seniority can enhance stability and certain creative engagements in teams with high synergy diversity climates, where long-tenured employees leverage experience for incremental innovations. Critics, however, contend it fosters complacency, as employees may coast on tenure without improving skills, leading to suboptimal leadership and demotivated high achievers who face blocked paths. In unionized settings, seniority-driven promotions have contributed to declining membership by rigidifying labor markets unfit for service or tech economies, where adaptability trumps rote experience.
Meritocracy counters these issues by tying advancement to measurable outputs, such as sales targets or project successes, which studies link to higher employee engagement and firm-level productivity when promotions reward performance over time served. For instance, research on promotion strategies shows merit-based systems boost satisfaction and motivation more than seniority alone, as individuals perceive rewards as causally linked to effort. Yet, implementation challenges persist: subjective evaluations can introduce biases, and in some organizational cultures espousing merit, managers paradoxically favor in-groups, undermining the system's intent. Broader evidence ties meritocratic beliefs to economic growth, albeit sometimes via increased inequality that incentivizes competition.
Contemporary reforms increasingly favor hybrid models blending seniority safeguards with merit thresholds, as pure seniority correlates with lower innovation in fast-evolving industries, while unchecked meritocracy risks burnout without tenure protections. In corporations, case analyses highlight merit's edge for leadership roles, enabling rapid adaptation over rigid hierarchies. Government and union debates underscore efficiency losses from seniority dominance, prompting calls for performance-linked overhauls to counter stagnation, though entrenched interests resist change. Ultimately, causal analysis favors merit where tasks demand novel problem-solving, as tenure alone fails to guarantee relevance in knowledge-driven contexts.

Recent Developments and Policy Shifts

In the United States military, a notable policy shift occurred in early 2025 when Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth announced the formation of a task force to prioritize merit-based promotions and retention, explicitly moving away from diversity quotas and toward evaluations centered on combat effectiveness and performance. This reform addressed prior emphases on demographic factors over traditional blends of seniority and merit, with Hegseth stating that promotions would focus on "lethality and merit, not on political correctness and diversity." By September 2025, implementation emphasized loyalty to mission objectives rather than tenure alone, reflecting broader critiques of seniority-driven systems that had allegedly prioritized non-performance metrics.
Federal civilian employment saw parallel changes through Executive Order 14170 in January 2025, which established the Merit Hiring Plan enforced by the Office of Personnel Management (OPM). This policy banned the use of diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) criteria or racial quotas in hiring and promotions, mandating assessments based solely on qualifications and performance to reduce time-to-hire from an average of 100 days. The reforms aimed to counteract perceived politicization in prior systems, where seniority often intersected with affirmative action preferences, though implementation faced internal resistance from agencies accustomed to tenure protections.
In corporate contexts, seniority's role has eroded amid declining union membership, which fell to 10% of the U.S. workforce by 2020 and continued downward, diminishing collective bargaining clauses that once enshrined time-in-service for layoffs and promotions. Surveys from 2025 indicate 61% of workers view traditional career laddersoften seniority-reliantas outdated, with tech and knowledge-based firms accelerating performance-based evaluations over tenure, as seen in post-2020 restructurings at companies like Meta and Amazon that prioritized agility during economic volatility.
Politically, debates over congressional seniority intensified with renewed pushes for term limits, bolstered by a 2023 Pew survey showing 87% public support for capping House and Senate terms to disrupt entrenched tenure-based leadership and committee assignments. In 2025, bipartisan resolutions and state-level campaigns, including those tied to U.S. Term Limits, gained traction amid criticisms of aging incumbents, though no federal enactment occurred; proponents argued limits would foster merit over incumbency advantages, while opponents contended they ignore voter choice without addressing underlying incentives like campaign finance. Age limit discussions escalated globally, with U.S. examples highlighting seniority's role in blocking reforms, as evidenced by stalled bills in the 118th Congress.
