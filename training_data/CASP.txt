The Critical Assessment of protein Structure Prediction (CASP) is a biennial blind experiment that evaluates computational methods for predicting three-dimensional protein structures from amino acid sequences, providing an independent benchmark of progress in the field. Organized by the Protein Structure Prediction Center, CASP selects target sequences from proteins whose experimental structures are imminent but unpublished, inviting global research teams to submit predictions before revealing the structures for rigorous assessment using metrics like global distance test (GDT) scores.
Initiated in 1994 to accelerate advancements amid slow empirical structure determination, CASP has run every two years, fostering competition and collaboration that has driven improvements from template-based modeling to physics-based simulations and, more recently, deep learning approaches. A landmark achievement occurred in CASP14 (2020), where DeepMind's AlphaFold2 system achieved median GDT scores exceeding 80 for many targets, effectively solving the long-standing protein folding problem for single chains and enabling near-experimental accuracy predictions. Subsequent iterations, including CASP15 (2022) and CASP16 (2024), have extended evaluations to multimers, RNA-protein complexes, and function annotations, maintaining relevance despite AI breakthroughs by addressing unsolved challenges like dynamics and assemblies.
While CASP's empirical evaluations have been pivotal in validating methods against causal biophysical realities rather than self-reported claims, the experiment faced a funding hiatus in 2025 when U.S. National Institutes of Health support ended after three decades, prompting Google DeepMindcreators of AlphaFoldto provide new sponsorship to sustain its role as the gold standard for structural bioinformatics assessment. No major methodological controversies have marred the experiment, though its shift toward AI dominance has sparked discussions on whether traditional physics-informed approaches retain value amid data-driven successes.

History

Inception and Founding (1994)

The Critical Assessment of Structure Prediction (CASP) experiment was initiated in 1994 by John Moult of the University of Maryland Biotechnology Institute, with co-founder Krzysztof Fidelis of the University of California, Davis, to provide an empirical, community-wide benchmark for computational protein structure prediction methods. The effort responded to stagnant advances in ab initio modeling, where theoretical approaches from first principles had yielded limited reliable results despite optimistic reports, necessitating blind testing to objectively distinguish effective techniques from unsubstantiated claims. Organizing teams, including Jan Pedersen and Richard Judson, publicized the challenge to gather unpublished targets from experimental structural biologists, ensuring predictions preceded structure determination.
CASP1, the first round, featured 33 targets categorized into comparative modeling (homology-based), fold recognition or threading (template matching without sequence similarity), and ab initio folding (physics-based without templates). Thirty-five groups submitted over 100 predictions in total, with the blind protocol preventing access to solved structures and thus avoiding overfitting to known data. Assessments were conducted by specialized independent teamsMichael James for comparative modeling, Shoshana Wodak for fold recognition, and Fred Cohen for ab initioemploying quantitative measures like coordinate root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) to compare predicted atomic positions against experimental references post-release. This structure highlighted early limitations, particularly in novel folds, while establishing CASP as a rigorous standard for tracking methodological progress.

Early Assessments and Incremental Progress (CASP2CASP10, 19962012)

Following the inaugural CASP1 in 1994, the experiment established a biennial cadence, with CASP2 held in 1996 and subsequent rounds through CASP10 in 2012. Prediction targets were systematically categorized by difficulty levels, distinguishing template-based modeling (TBM) for sequences with identifiable homologs in the Protein Data Bank from free modeling (FM) for novel folds lacking evident templates. This classification enabled focused assessments of method efficacy across varying challenges, emphasizing empirical blind testing against experimentally determined structures released post-prediction.
Predictors in these early rounds predominantly employed homology modeling for TBM targets, leveraging sequence alignments to thread queries onto known structures, alongside physics-based approaches like fragment assembly for FM targets. Refinements in fragment assembly, particularly through Monte Carlo sampling of short peptide fragments derived from the PDB, marked incremental gains; David Baker's group advanced this via the Rosetta protocol, which prioritized low-energy conformations to build plausible folds. The automated Robetta server, integrating Rosetta's de novo and comparative modeling, demonstrated improved coverage and accuracy in CASP5 (2002), producing full-chain models by partitioning sequences into domains and applying hybrid strategies. 
Template recognition saw successes, with enhanced threading algorithms better identifying distant homologs, yielding GDT-TS scores often exceeding 50 for straightforward TBM cases by CASP10. However, FM targets exposed persistent limitations, as physics-driven sampling struggled with search space vastness and energy function inaccuracies, frequently resulting in models with incorrect secondary structure packing. Overall, top GDT-TS scores for FM domains progressed modestly from around 20-30 in CASP2-CASP4 to approximately 40 by CASP10, reflecting gradual algorithmic tweaks but underscoring fundamental computational barriers absent machine learning paradigms. 

Transition to Machine Learning Dominance (CASP11CASP12, 20142016)

In CASP11, conducted from summer to fall 2014, residue-residue contact prediction advanced markedly through coevolutionary methods analyzing multiple sequence alignments (MSAs) to infer direct couplings between evolutionarily conserved residues. Techniques such as direct-coupling analysis (DCA) enabled top predictors like CONSIP2 (using MetaPSICOV) to achieve 27% precision for the top L/5 long-range contacts across target proteins, surpassing the 21% average from CASP10 and outperforming non-coevolutionary approaches. At least eight participating groups adopted these evolutionary coupling strategies, marking a shift from prior reliance on secondary structure or solvent accessibility predictions.
These coevolution-derived contacts served as geometric restraints in hybrid modeling pipelines for free-modeling targetsproteins without detectable templateswhere they were integrated with physics-based tools like Rosetta for fragment assembly and energy minimization. For instance, augmenting Rosetta with such restraints improved de novo folding accuracy in blind tests, yielding models closer to native structures than simulations using only theoretical potentials. Hybrid methods thus demonstrated empirical superiority over pure physics-based ab initio approaches, which struggled with sampling vast conformational spaces and optimizing complex energy landscapes without data-guided constraints; CASP11 assessments confirmed this edge via global distance test (GDT) scores in template-free categories.
CASP12, held in 2016, accelerated this transition by incorporating early machine learning enhancements, including neural networks, to process coevolutionary signals and predict contacts with greater precision. Best predictors averaged 47% precision for top long-range contacts, nearly doubling CASP11's top performance and reflecting gains from deeper MSAs and supervised learning on protein databases. Servers such as RaptorX-Contact applied convolutional neural networks to MSA features, outperforming coevolution-alone methods in blind evaluations and boosting free-modeling topologies. Blind tests validated machine learning's pattern-recognition advantagesdrawing from empirical distributions in the Protein Data Bankover traditional physics simulations, which lagged in resolving nonlocal interactions despite refinements in force fields. This era's data-driven hybrids laid groundwork for ML's expanded role, with over 100 groups submitting tens of thousands of models emphasizing contact-guided assembly.

Methodology

Target Protein Selection

Target proteins for CASP assessments are sourced exclusively from experimental structural biologists who have determined structures via techniques including X-ray crystallography, nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy, and cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM), but have withheld deposition in the Protein Data Bank (PDB) or any public disclosure prior to the prediction deadline. These experimentalists provide only the target sequences to CASP organizers, who disseminate them to participating prediction groups during the active competition phase, ensuring a blind evaluation that precludes access to ground-truth coordinates and mitigates risks of overfitting to leaked data. This process, coordinated biennially since CASP's inception in 1994, relies on voluntary submissions from over 36 experimental groups across multiple countries in recent rounds, such as CASP13.
Selection criteria prioritize empirical rigor and methodological challenge: targets must exhibit structural novelty, with free-modeling (FM) cases specifically chosen to lack close homologs in the PDB (typically sequence identity below 30% to known structures), thereby testing predictors' capacity for de novo folding without template reliance. Template-based modeling (TBM) targets permit detectable homologs but require coverage of most secondary structure elements, while overall emphasis falls on difficult proteinssuch as those with rare folds or low homologyto probe fundamental limits in sequence-to-structure mapping rather than incremental homology exploitation. Organizers reject targets with prior public sequences or excessive similarity to PDB entries to maintain unbiased assessment of causal prediction accuracy from primary sequence data alone.
Targets are classified into specialized categories to dissect performance across prediction modalities: TBM for homology-aided cases, FM for template-free challenges, human-origin proteins for biomedical relevance (e.g., disease-related structures), oligomers for multi-chain assemblies requiring interface prediction, and refinement targets involving all-atom adjustments from initial partial models. Post-CASP14, CASP15 introduced RNA single structures, protein-RNA complexes, and ligand-bound proteins, expanding beyond monomeric proteins to evaluate emerging multimodal predictions, with 8 RNA targets among 93 evaluated structures that year.
Over the course of 16 CASP rounds through 2024, more than 1,100 targets have been released, with annual totals varying from approximately 50 to 130, consistently favoring hard cases to drive empirical advancements in structure prediction fidelity. This cumulative dataset underscores the competition's role in validating methods against withheld experimental realities, free from retrospective bias.

Prediction Submission Process

Participating groups, exceeding 100 in recent rounds such as CASP15 where nearly 100 groups contributed, submit up to five ranked models per target to predict the three-dimensional structure from the provided amino acid sequence. These models are uploaded via a dedicated web-based submission form on the Prediction Center website or, alternatively, emailed in a standardized text format that includes target identifier, prediction category, group details, and atomic coordinates.
Deadlines for submissions align with the experimental determination timelines of targets, typically granting three days for automated server predictions and up to three weeks for human-expert groups to allow differentiation between fully automated pipelines and methods incorporating human intervention or refinement. This temporal distinction facilitates separate evaluations of computational efficiency versus predictive accuracy enhanced by expertise, while the overall process collected over 53,000 models across 127 targets in CASP15 from May to mid-September 2022.
To maintain blind testing conditions that replicate unsolved real-world structure prediction scenarios, participants are prohibited from incorporating any target-specific experimental data, leaked coordinates, or information beyond the released sequence; violations undermine the experiment's validity by introducing non-causal biases. The enforced standardization of submission protocols, including format compliance and category-specific guidelines, ensures transparency in methodology disclosure post-assessment and enables rigorous, apples-to-apples comparability of diverse approaches from global predictors.

Evaluation Criteria and Metrics

The evaluation of predictions in CASP relies on standardized, quantitative metrics that quantify structural similarity between models and experimentally determined reference structures, typically obtained via X-ray crystallography or cryo-electron microscopy. These metrics emphasize empirical deviations in atomic positions, prioritizing superposition-based global alignment for topology assessment and local measures for precision, with scores computed independently by dedicated assessor teams after target structures are released to prevent bias.
For core protein tertiary structure prediction, the primary metric is the Global Distance Test Total Score (GDT-TS), which evaluates topological similarity by calculating the percentage of aligned C atoms within distance cutoffs of 1 , 2 , 4 , and 8 after optimal superposition, then averaging these fractions; scores range from 0 to 100, with values above 60 indicating moderate similarity and over 80 reflecting high accuracy. Complementing GDT-TS, Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) measures local backbone accuracy in angstroms after superposition, sensitive to deviations in residue positions but less robust for global folds due to alignment sensitivity. In more recent assessments, the Local Distance Difference Test (lDDT) provides a superposition-independent, all-atom evaluation by scoring pairwise interatomic distance differences binned into categories (0-1 preserved, etc.), yielding a value from 0 to 1 that captures local structural fidelity without penalizing rigid-body rotations.
Category-specific metrics address specialized targets; for protein complexes, DockQ integrates ligand CAPRI criteria, interface RMSD, and orientation metrics to classify predictions as high-quality (DockQ > 0.80 ), medium (0.49-0.80 ), or acceptable (0.23-0.49 ), focusing on binding interface accuracy. RNA predictions employ adapted scores like lDDT for local geometry and GDT for backbone topology, often combined into composite Z-scores (e.g., ZRNA) weighting multiple deviations, with deformation indices assessing base-pairing fidelity. For ambiguous cases, such as novel folds or disordered regions where automated metrics yield inconclusive results, human expert assessors incorporate qualitative judgments alongside quantitative data to refine rankings. Overall rankings, derived from these metrics across multiple models per group, are published transparently to provide objective benchmarks, countering unsubstantiated claims of predictive success.

Major Breakthroughs

AlphaFold's Emergence in CASP13 (2018)

In the 2018 CASP13 competition, DeepMind's AlphaFold system disrupted established protein structure prediction paradigms by topping the free-modeling (FM) category, dedicated to targets lacking close structural homologs in existing databases. This category tests de novo folding capabilities, where traditional methods like fragment assembly and physics-based simulations had plateaued at modest accuracies. AlphaFold's success stemmed from its use of deep neural networks trained on evolutionary data from multiple sequence alignments (MSAs) and structural templates to predict full probability distributions of inter-residue distances and orientations, forming a protein-specific potential subsequently refined through gradient descent optimization.
Unlike prior approaches reliant on Rosetta-style fragment piecing or contact map inference followed by Monte Carlo sampling, AlphaFold enabled end-to-end differentiable prediction of atomic coordinates, directly leveraging co-evolutionary signals for novel topologies without homologous templates. For 43 FM domains, it attained TM-scores 0.7indicative of correct fold predictionfor 24 cases, surpassing the runner-up's 14, while mean GDT-TS scores advanced to approximately 60 for hard targets, a roughly 20-point gain over the prior CASP12 FM leaders' ~40 range and exceeding historical progress rates by 1.8-fold.
This empirical leap underscored deep learning's capacity to model causal geometric constraints from sequence covariation, validating a shift from knowledge-based heuristics to scalable, data-centric inference and enabling reliable structure recovery for previously intractable proteins. Observers praised the method for injecting momentum into the field, as it outperformed ensembles of human-designed pipelines on blind benchmarks, though some noted its heavy dependence on deep MSAs limited utility for orphan sequences. Initially, DeepMind withheld full code release despite utilizing public databases for training, prompting critiques of reproducibility barriers in an open-science community; code became available only with the 2020 methodology publication.

AlphaFold2 Dominance in CASP14 (2020)

In the Critical Assessment of Structure Prediction (CASP14), held from May to November 2020, DeepMind's AlphaFold2 system demonstrated unprecedented dominance, achieving a median Global Distance Test-Total Score (GDT-TS) of 92.4 across all evaluated domains, far surpassing the next-best group's score of approximately 70. This performance marked a qualitative leap, with AlphaFold2 delivering atomic-level accuracymedian C root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) below 1 for many targetseffectively solving the folding of single-domain proteins without relying on experimental structures or explicit physics-based simulations. Human expert predictors and other automated servers lagged significantly, with summed Z-scores for AlphaFold2 at 244.0 compared to 90.8 for the runner-up, underscoring its superiority in blind prediction challenges.
Central to AlphaFold2's success was the Evoformer architecture, a stack of transformer blocks that iteratively refines multiple sequence alignments (MSAs) and pairwise residue representations through axial attention mechanisms, enabling emergent capture of evolutionary and spatial constraints. This fed into an invariant point attention-based structure module that directly outputs 3D coordinates, incorporating geometric priors implicitly rather than through iterative energy minimization. For easy, single-domain targetstypically those with rich homologsmedian GDT-TS scores approached or exceeded 90, reflecting near-perfect alignment with experimentally determined structures and validating the end-to-end differentiable pipeline's ability to generalize folding principles from training data.
AlphaFold2's CASP14 release of predicted models for all submitted targets accelerated access to high-fidelity structures, paving the way for broader dissemination via the AlphaFold Protein Structure Database, which later encompassed over 200 million entries. However, its scope remained confined to static monomer conformations, yielding suboptimal results for oligomeric complexes (GDT-TS often below 70) and failing to model conformational dynamics or ligand-induced changes, as it predicts a single representative state per sequence. These constraints highlighted that while single-domain folding was effectively "solved" for practical purposes, broader protein behavior involving interactions or flexibility persisted as unresolved challenges.

Subsequent Predictors and Methodological Advances

In 2021, the Baker Laboratory introduced RoseTTAFold, a deep learning framework utilizing a three-track neural network that processes one-dimensional sequence, two-dimensional distance map, and three-dimensional coordinate representations to predict protein structures with accuracy approaching that of leading methods on CASP benchmarks. This approach facilitated open-source implementations and extensions, such as RosettaFold, which integrated RoseTTAFold outputs with physics-based Rosetta energy minimization for refinement in CASP15 complex modeling.
The Zhang Laboratory advanced hybrid methodologies with D-I-TASSER, combining deep learning-based distance predictions, iterative threading alignments, and fragment assembly to achieve superior performance in high-resolution structure prediction during CASP15 and subsequent benchmarks, particularly for targets lacking deep evolutionary homologs. These methods emphasized template-free modeling augmented by structural templates derived from threading, yielding median GDT-TS scores exceeding 70 for monomeric targets in CASP15 evaluations.
Meta AI's ESMFold, released in 2022, represented a shift toward sequence-only prediction by leveraging large language models trained on evolutionary-scale sequence data, enabling up to 60-fold inference speedup without multiple sequence alignments and generating structures for 772 million metagenomic proteins in the ESM Metagenomic Atlas. Knowledge distillation techniques further enhanced efficiency across predictors, distilling complex models into lighter variants for scalable applications while preserving accuracy on ordered domains.
CASP15 results demonstrated methodological convergence, with top predictors attaining GDT-TS scores above 80 for over 70% of assessed targets involving ordered regions, reflecting shared reliance on attention-based architectures. However, intrinsic disorder posed persistent challenges, as predictors exhibited reduced confidence (pLDDT scores below 50) and higher RMSD errors in unstructured segments due to sparse evolutionary signals and training biases toward globular proteins. Hybrid physics-ML refinements mitigated some inaccuracies in flexible loops but highlighted the need for disorder-specific datasets.

Impact and Achievements

Acceleration of Structure Prediction Accuracy

Prior to the inception of CASP in 1994, protein structure prediction methods predominantly relied on homology modeling, achieving reliable results only for targets with detectable structural templates in databases like the PDB, which covered a minority of cases; for novel folds lacking homologs, predictions often yielded accuracies comparable to random coil models, with scores around 20-30% on scales like GDT-TS. CASP's inaugural rounds documented this baseline, with top predictions for difficult targets averaging GDT-TS scores below 40, underscoring the field's nascent state where ab initio and threading approaches frequently failed to identify correct topologies. Over successive iterations, CASP tracked incremental gains through the 2000s and early 2010s, with average GDT-TS for hard targets rising modestly to 40-60 by CASP12, driven by refinements in template detection and fragment assembly, yet plateauing due to persistent challenges in de novo folding.
The blind prediction protocol central to CASPrequiring submissions before experimental structures are releasedimposed rigorous, unbiased testing that causalized verifiable progress by eliminating overfitting to known structures and debunking unsubstantiated claims from non-blind benchmarks. This format compelled methodological evolution, as groups iteratively addressed failures exposed in assessments, fostering hybrid approaches integrating physics-based simulations with statistical potentials that incrementally boosted accuracy without reliance on hindsight. By CASP14 in 2020, this cumulative rigor yielded transformative leaps, with top models achieving GDT-TS scores exceeding 90 for monomeric domains across diverse targets, marking a shift from sporadic successes to routine high-fidelity predictions.
CASP's target selection, encompassing all protein domains including underrepresented or template-poor ones from structural genomics initiatives, comprehensively revealed systemic biases in template-centric methods, which dominated early rounds but faltered on novel scaffolds lacking database matches. Assessments highlighted how over-dependence on threading inflated perceived capabilities in non-blind tests, while CASP's diverse, blind targetssourced from unpublished experimental dataexposed these gaps, spurring innovations in contact prediction and deep learning that generalized across fold space.
These accuracy gains, empirically validated through CASP, have accelerated applications in drug design by supplying atomic-level models for orphan targets, enabling structure-based virtual screening and rational ligand optimization where experimental determination was prohibitive. Similarly, for genetic variant analysis, predicted structures now permit causal inference on mutation effects, such as stability disruptions in disease-associated proteins, supporting precision medicine workflows previously bottlenecked by low-confidence models.

Influence on AI-Driven Biology Research

The successes of AI predictors validated through CASP have profoundly shaped AI-driven biology by embedding structure prediction into experimental workflows, allowing researchers to prioritize hypothesis testing over de novo determination. The AlphaFold Protein Structure Database, released in 2021 and expanded thereafter, provides open access to over 214 million predicted structures covering nearly all cataloged proteins, enabling biologists to integrate these models directly into studies of function, interactions, and evolution without awaiting experimental resolution. This resource has democratized access, with tools like ColabFoldintroduced in 2022further accelerating adoption by enabling fast, GPU-accelerated predictions via web-based interfaces like Google Colab, bypassing the need for high-end hardware or proprietary codebases.
In protein engineering, CASP-honed methods have streamlined variant design and validation; for instance, AlphaFold predictions have been used to assess structural stability of engineered proteins for applications such as solar energy capture, guiding iterative improvements that align sequence changes with desired folds. For SARS-CoV-2, predictions of receptor-binding domain interactions with ACE2 have supported variant tracking and epitope analysis, potentially informing vaccine updates by highlighting mutational impacts on binding affinity, though empirical validation remains essential due to the method's focus on static monomers over dynamic assemblies. These integrations have shortened design cycles, with reports of AlphaFold aiding de novo protein creation by estimating designability and stability from sequence alone.
Despite these advances, critiques highlight overreliance on predictions for challenging cases, particularly non-soluble proteins like membrane-embedded ones, where AlphaFold underperforms due to insensitivity to lipid environments or atypical folds, often yielding models that diverge from experimental data in thickness-constrained or novel contexts. Such limitations underscore that while CASP-driven AI excels for soluble, homologous targets, its utility wanes for therapeutic targets involving disorder or complexes, tempering claims of universal workflow transformation. The private sector's lead, exemplified by DeepMind's AlphaFold iterations triumphing in CASP over academic entrants, illustrates how concentrated resources in for-profit entities propelled breakthroughs where distributed institutional efforts lagged, shifting innovation dynamics away from traditional public funding models.

Recognition and Broader Scientific Legacy

The 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry was awarded jointly to David Baker for computational protein design and to Demis Hassabis and John Jumper for developing AlphaFold, an AI system that achieved breakthrough accuracy in protein structure prediction, as validated through its dominance in the CASP14 experiment. This recognition underscores CASP's role as a pivotal benchmark, having tracked empirical advances in the field since its establishment in 1994 as a blind, community-wide assessment of prediction methods. Over 15 iterations spanning more than three decades, CASP has catalyzed measurable improvements by rigorously evaluating models against experimentally determined structures prior to public release.
CASP's enduring legacy lies in standardizing evaluation protocols, including metrics like global distance test (GDT) scores and template-free modeling categories, which provide objective, reproducible benchmarks for comparing predictor performance across diverse protein targets. By mandating disclosure of methods, predictions, and code post-assessment, it has fostered open data sharing and methodological transparency, enabling iterative refinements and widespread adoption of successful techniques. This framework has indirectly supported the expansion of structural biology resources, such as the Protein Data Bank (PDB), by validating predictive tools that accelerate annotation of unsolved structures and aid experimental validation.
The experiment's emphasis on empirical blind testing has been lauded for prioritizing verifiable accuracy over theoretical claims, driving the field toward causal understanding of folding principles through data-driven competition. However, the competitive structure has drawn commentary for potentially incentivizing proprietary developments within groups, occasionally at the expense of immediate cross-lab synergies, though participants routinely share insights in post-CASP analyses. Overall, CASP's contributions extend beyond structure prediction to foundational advancements in AI-biology integration and high-throughput modeling, establishing it as a model for community-driven scientific validation.

Criticisms and Challenges

Limitations in Evaluating Complex Structures

Historically, the Critical Assessment of Structure Prediction (CASP) competitions prioritized monomeric protein structures, with primary assessments centered on single-chain or domain-level modeling, limiting the evaluation of multimers until later rounds. This undersampling meant that complex structures, involving protein-protein interfaces, received inconsistent or minimal targets in early iterations like CASP13 and CASP14, where fewer than 10% of assessed models addressed quaternary assemblies. Consequently, methodological advances in monomer folding did not fully extend to complexes, as interface formation introduces additional variables such as relative subunit orientations and binding affinities not captured in isolated chain predictions.
CASP15 in 2022 marked a shift with expanded multimer targets47 complexes assessed versus 81 monomersintroducing dedicated categories for oligomeric interfaces in collaboration with CAPRI, yet prediction accuracy lagged behind monomers. Top-performing groups achieved average TM-scores of approximately 0.76 for multimer assemblies, compared to 0.850.95 for monomers, reflecting roughly 7080% relative success in structural fidelity. Interface contact scores (ICS), the primary metric for multimer quality, highlighted persistent deficiencies, with over 30% of targets classified as difficult even in subsequent CASP16 assessments, underscoring that monomer breakthroughs like AlphaFold2's do not extrapolate seamlessly to quaternary structures.
Challenges in complex evaluation stem from the inherent difficulty of interface prediction, where conformational entropy losses upon binding and subtle energetic penalties for misalignment reduce model reliability compared to rigid monomer folding. DockQ scores, combining native contact fraction, ligand RMSD, and interface RMSD into a 01 scale, reveal these gaps: while DockQ >0.49 indicates medium accuracy, many CASP multimer models scored below this threshold, with success rates for acceptable docking (DockQ 0.23) hovering around 3040% for challenging dimers. This necessitates specialized metrics and categories beyond monomer-focused global distance tests, as achievements in single-chain accuracy fail to address the combinatorial search space of subunit assembly.

Debates on the "Solved" Status of Protein Folding

Following the success of AlphaFold2 in CASP14, where it achieved median global distance test (GDT) scores above 90 for about two-thirds of target proteins, organizers declared the protein folding problem solved for practical purposes in predicting static monomer structures. This view was echoed by DeepMind leaders, with CEO Demis Hassabis stating in 2022 that AlphaFold had addressed a 50-year-old challenge by predicting structures for nearly all known proteins with atomic accuracy. The 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, awarded to Hassabis and John Jumper, reinforced this perspective, recognizing their AI method for enabling routine structure prediction comparable to experimental methods.
However, skeptics contend that these advances do not equate to a complete solution, as AlphaFold relies on empirical pattern recognition from existing structural data rather than deriving folding principles from physical laws, failing to explain why proteins fold as they do. In CASP16 assessments from 2024, while single-domain monomer folds were nearly fully predicted without misses, challenges persisted in cases involving novel topologies, dynamic conformational changes, and interactions with ligands or cellular environments, underscoring that static prediction alone omits the full in vivo folding process. Critics, including computational biologists, argue this leaves approximately 10-30% of difficult targets inadequately handled, depending on metrics like TM-score, and does not advance understanding of folding kinetics or thermodynamics.
Media portrayals often overstated the achievement as a total resolution, prompting pushback from researchers who emphasize that true solvability requires generalizable physics-based models applicable beyond trained datasets, a benchmark AlphaFold has not met. As noted in 2025 analyses, post-AlphaFold competitions like CASP16 reveal ongoing gaps, with experts like CASP participant Namita Dube asserting the problem remains unsolved for multifaceted biological contexts. This debate highlights empirical progress in accuracyevident in CASP14's leap to experimental-level predictionsbut insists on distinguishing tool efficacy from foundational insight into causal folding mechanisms.

Funding Transitions and Institutional Dependencies

The Critical Assessment of Structure Prediction (CASP) has relied on National Institutes of Health (NIH) grants for funding since its inception in 1994, supporting biennial competitions that evaluate protein structure prediction methods. In 2025, the latest NIH grant expired without renewal amid broader federal budget constraints, prompting the core organizing team to face potential layoffs and threatening the continuity of CASP16 results and future iterations. This transition underscores CASP's historical institutional dependency on government support, which has enabled independent, community-wide assessments but also exposed vulnerabilities to fluctuating public budgets, as evidenced by the non-renewal despite the competition's role in advancing structural biology benchmarks like AlphaFold's breakthroughs.
Google DeepMind, developer of the AlphaFold systems that dominated CASP13 and CASP14, announced interim bridge funding in July 2025 to sustain operations for up to six months, averting immediate discontinuation and allowing time for long-term arrangements. This private infusion highlights a shift toward corporate sponsorship in scientific benchmarks, potentially accelerating innovation through resources unburdened by public procurement delays, as private entities like DeepMind have demonstrated via AlphaFold's open-sourced models. However, it raises concerns over sustainability, as temporary measures may not guarantee perpetual neutrality; reliance on a single for-profit entity could introduce subtle priorities aligned with commercial AI applications, diverging from CASP's original emphasis on diverse, academic-led methodological breadth.
The funding pivot thus reveals systemic risks in publicly anchored scientific infrastructures: while government grants fostered impartiality and broad participation over three decades, their abrupt constraintsunrelated to CASP's performancenecessitate diversified private streams, yet without diversified backers, events could skew toward proprietary advancements over comprehensive evaluation. Organizers, including co-founder John Moult, have emphasized seeking multi-stakeholder support to mitigate over-dependence, ensuring CASP's role in causal validation of prediction accuracies remains insulated from singular institutional influences. This episode parallels broader trends in computational biology, where empirical progress hinges on stable funding ecosystems balancing public oversight with private agility.

Recent and Future Developments

CASP15 Advances in Protein Complexes (2022)

CASP15 introduced a focused emphasis on protein assembly prediction, evaluating quaternary structures for multimolecular complexes alongside traditional categories. Nearly 100 groups worldwide submitted over 53,000 models across 127 targets, with 40 dedicated assembly targets ranging from obligate dimers to large complexes involving up to 11 polypeptide chains. These targets tested predictors' ability to model inter-subunit interfaces, building on prior monomer successes by incorporating experimental data like stoichiometry and interaction partners where available.
Leading methods, such as AlphaFold-Multimer, dominated assembly predictions by exploiting coevolutionary signals in multiple sequence alignments to capture protein-protein interactions, achieving interface root-mean-square deviations (RMSDs) below 2 for many targets and overall high accuracy in both global fold and local interface regions. This represented enormous empirical progress in quaternary modeling, with top models recovering approximately 80% of native interface contacts in benchmark assessments, underscoring machine learning's efficacy for interaction inference over traditional docking approaches reliant on rigid-body transformations.
Relative to CASP14, improvements were moderate yet consistent, particularly in interface precision for non-obligate and transient complexes, as iterative refinement protocolssuch as recycling MSAs and structural samplingenhanced model convergence without overhauling core architectures. Assessments confirmed that 87 predictors generated around 11,000 assembly models, with community-wide metrics like median MM-scores exceeding 0.7 for high-confidence predictions, validating coevolution-driven assembly as a scalable extension of single-chain folding paradigms.

CASP16 Results and Refinements (2024)

The CASP16 experiment, spanning mid-2024, involved blind predictions for 156 protein tertiary structure targets, including monomers and multimers, with results evaluated and discussed at the conference in Punta Cana, Dominican Republic, from December 1 to 4, 2024. Nearly 100 research groups participated, submitting over 80,000 models across categories such as protein monomers, multimers, and complexes involving nucleic acids or ligands. Assessments confirmed high accuracy in monomer predictions, with top methods yielding average GDT-TS scores exceeding 90 for numerous single-domain targets, indicating that de novo monomer structure prediction remains largely resolved since CASP14, with only marginal gains attributable to refined deep learning architectures like AlphaFold 3 (AF3). 
In protein multimer and complex predictions, AF3-based approaches delivered moderate improvements over AlphaFold 2 baselines, particularly for interfaces in antibody-antigen and heteromeric assemblies, driven by better handling of atomic interactions and expanded training data. However, top performances often required extensive samplinggenerating thousands of models per targetto capture optimal conformations, underscoring ongoing challenges in efficiently exploring conformational ensembles for dynamic complexes without experimental data. Leading groups, such as those from the Yang and Cheng labs, excelled by integrating AF3 outputs with custom ranking and exception-handling protocols, achieving superior interface metrics like DockQ scores above 0.5 for select targets. 
Refinements in all-atom modeling highlighted progress in incorporating ligands and cofactors, where human-expert groups outperformed automated servers by iteratively adjusting side-chain placements and hydrogen bonding networks, as measured by refined RMSD and clash scores in evaluation pipelines. Metrics for human-assisted categories revealed targeted enhancements in quaternary accuracy for challenging multimers, though overall advancements were incremental, relying on hybrid strategies rather than novel algorithmic breakthroughs. Independent assessments noted that while AF3 addressed some multimer sampling gaps, systemic limitations in predicting transient or lowly populated states persist, tempering claims of comprehensive resolution in complex structure prediction. 

Ongoing Evolution and New Assessment Frontiers

CASP competitions have expanded beyond static structure prediction to evaluate protein dynamics, recognizing that functional behaviors often arise from conformational ensembles rather than single snapshots. This shift addresses critiques that tools like AlphaFold prioritize equilibrium states, potentially overlooking transient intermediates critical for catalysis and allostery. Integration of molecular dynamics (MD) simulations into assessment pipelines enables testing of predicted trajectories against experimental data, such as NMR ensembles or time-resolved spectroscopy, to validate models of folding pathways and ligand-induced motions.
Emerging categories now include protein-ligand interactions, with CASP16 introducing blind challenges for docking drug-like compounds provided by pharmaceutical partners, assessing both pose accuracy and binding affinity via metrics like root-mean-square deviation and interaction energy estimates. Reverse-engineering tasks probe the invertibility of design processes, requiring predictors to reconstruct plausible sequences from engineered structures or infer functional motifs, thereby testing causal links between sequence, fold, and utility in biotech applications.
The experiment continues on a biennial schedule, bolstered by Google DeepMind's one-time funding in July 2025 to bridge gaps from expiring NIH grants, ensuring sustained evaluation of predictions' verifiable impact on fields like drug design and enzyme engineering. Future frontiers emphasize empirical validation of dynamic and interactive models, prioritizing outcomes that demonstrate causal realism in biological mechanisms over mere structural fidelity.
